This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models, leveraging Gaussian processes (GPs) for the state transition dynamics and a particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) sampler for efficient inference. The core idea is to marginalize over the transition dynamics function, allowing direct inference of the smoothing distribution. This work builds upon a rich history of SSMs and GP-based modeling, referencing key contributions from Rasmussen & Williams (2006), Turner et al. (2013), and Deisenroth et al. (2015, 2016, among others). The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, particularly highlighting how their approach addresses limitations of existing methods that rely on parametric approximations of the GP dynamics.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the key contributions. The mathematical derivations are presented logically and are easy to follow, and the inclusion of Figure 1 (although not explicitly mentioned in the provided excerpt) is helpful for visualizing the model. The authors also provide clear explanations of the implementation details, including the computational optimizations for both the vanilla GP and the sparse GP variant utilizing a fully independent conditional (FIC) prior. The experimental setup and results are also well presented, demonstrating the efficacy of their approach on both a synthetic benchmark and a cart-pole system.

The originality of this work lies in the application of a tailored PMCMC scheme—specifically, particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) as described in Lindsten et al. (2014)—to this GP-SSM setting, combined with the analytical marginalization of the transition dynamics. This is a significant contribution, as it allows for a fully nonparametric treatment of the dynamics while still enabling efficient Bayesian inference. The development of the sparse GP-SSM variant further enhances the practicality of the approach, particularly for high-dimensional state spaces.

The significance of the work is substantial. The ability to perform fully Bayesian inference in nonlinear SSMs is crucial for a wide range of applications, including time series analysis, control, and robotics. The authors demonstrate the potential of their approach through compelling experimental results, showcasing its ability to learn complex dynamics and provide accurate predictive distributions. The combination of nonparametric flexibility with efficient inference makes this a potentially impactful contribution to the field.

However, a notable weakness is the lack of comparative evaluation against other particle MCMC schemes commonly used for state-space models. Specifically, the paper does not compare performance to particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) or particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as developed by Whiteley et al.). Implementing these comparisons would be relatively straightforward and would provide a crucial benchmark for assessing the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed PGAS-based approach. The reviewer believes that PMMH, while potentially requiring a particle count that scales quadratically with the time horizon (as discussed by Whiteley et al.), could provide a useful baseline for comparison.

Furthermore, the experimental results would benefit from more detailed diagnostic analysis. The current presentation lacks informative graphics demonstrating the scaling of the method with respect to the number of particles (N) and the time horizon (T). Plots of autocorrelation functions (ACFs) or effective sample size (ESS) as functions of N and T would be invaluable for understanding the mixing properties of the sampler and for determining whether the computational complexity scales linearly or sublinearly with these parameters. Such analysis would provide further insight into the practical limitations and applicability of the proposed method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs, enabling fully nonparametric inference.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of a complex methodology.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through compelling experimental results.
*   Development of a sparse GP-SSM variant for improved computational efficiency.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of comparative evaluation against other relevant particle MCMC schemes (PMMH, particle Gibbs with backward sampling).
*   Limited diagnostic analysis of the sampler's performance (e.g., ACFs, ESS plots).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian state-space modeling. The methodology is well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by promising experimental results. However, the lack of comparison to alternative particle MCMC schemes and the limited diagnostic analysis are significant weaknesses. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the paper. Despite these drawbacks, the originality and potential impact of the work are substantial.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should be encouraged to include a comparative evaluation against PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, as well as to provide more detailed diagnostic analysis of the sampler's performance. These additions would significantly enhance the impact and credibility of the work.
