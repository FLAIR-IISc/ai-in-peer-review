

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models by placing Gaussian process priors over transition dynamics and using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo with ancestor sampling (PGAS) for efficient joint state and parameter estimation. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by marginalizing out the transition dynamics function while preserving the full nonparametric expressivity of the model. This approach differs fundamentally from prior work that either assumes parametric transition models or uses finite approximations of the GP [2,5,6,8], and it enables accurate smoothing distributions without relying on fixed transition models.

The paper is technically sound with a clear presentation of the novel marginalization technique through ancestor sampling, which effectively handles the non-Markovian dependencies introduced by marginalizing the GP. The computational efficiency considerations, particularly the use of sparse GPs with FIC and low-rank Cholesky updates, are well-justified and implemented with careful attention to complexity reduction from O(T^4) to O(M^2T). The experimental results demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world systems, though the evaluation could be strengthened by including out-of-sample forecasting metrics as suggested in Table 1.

Strengths include the significant theoretical novelty in online marginalization via ancestor sampling, the thorough treatment of computational considerations, and the clear exposition of the method. The paper is well-positioned within the literature, building on recent advances in PMCMC [3,4] while addressing an important gap in Bayesian nonparametric state-space modeling.

Weaknesses include the lack of explicit discussion comparing parametric models used as GP means versus full GP modeling (with implications for forecasting), and the ambiguity in Table 1 regarding whether RMSE values are in-sample or out-of-sample. The abbreviation CPF-AS appears before definition, and the empirical results, while sufficient, could be expanded with more detailed analysis of the sparse GP's performance trade-offs.

Overall, this is a strong, novel contribution that advances Bayesian inference for non-Markovian state-space models. The paper addresses a significant challenge in the field with technically sound methods and clear presentation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the suggested improvements.
