

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics. The authors develop a novel method to marginalize over the transition dynamics function using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling, enabling joint state and parameter estimation without resorting to parametric approximations of the transition function. This approach contrasts with previous work that typically requires fixing the transition dynamics model first (e.g., [2, 8, 5]).

The paper builds upon recent advances in nonparametric Bayesian methods for state-space models, particularly extending the work on Gaussian process dynamical systems [5, 6, 8]. While previous approaches focused on filtering and smoothing given a pre-learned GP model, this paper provides the crucial component for learning the GP dynamics itself in a fully Bayesian manner. The authors also leverage the recently developed Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm [4], which is particularly well-suited for non-Markovian latent variable models like their GP-SSM.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with clear, accessible presentation. The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for marginalizing the transition dynamics and derive efficient computational methods for the resulting non-Markovian state-space model. The implementation details regarding sparse GP constructions and Cholesky updates are particularly strong, addressing practical computational concerns that often limit the applicability of GP methods. The empirical results demonstrate the model's capability to capture complex dynamics even when the mean function is misspecified, as shown in the nonlinear benchmark system (Figure 2). However, the experimental section is somewhat brief, with limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity or comparison with alternative approaches.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear, and the authors effectively explain the key innovation of online marginalization via ancestor sampling. The only minor issue is that the abbreviation "CPF-AS" is used before being defined in Section 3.3.1.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing online marginalization of transition dynamics via ancestor sampling, which is novel in the context of state-space models. This approach preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the GP model while enabling efficient inference. The authors clearly articulate how their method differs from previous approaches that either use parametric approximations of the transition function [2, 8] or require pre-learned dynamics [5, 6].

**Significance**: The work addresses an important gap in the literature by providing a fully Bayesian approach to learning both states and dynamics in nonparametric state-space models. This has implications for applications requiring uncertainty quantification in dynamical systems, such as robotics and control. The method could stimulate further research on inference for non-Markovian state-space models, as noted by the authors. However, the paper could strengthen its significance by adding a discussion comparing the implications of using a parametric transition model a priori versus using it as the GP mean (for forecasting/out-of-sample performance), which would help practitioners understand when to use this approach versus more traditional methods.

**Strengths**: 
- Novel and technically sound approach to joint state and parameter estimation
- Strong computational considerations (sparsity and low-rank updates)
- Clear presentation of the method and its theoretical foundations
- Demonstrated capability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions

**Weaknesses**:
- Empirical results are brief and could be expanded (e.g., with more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity)
- Unclear whether RMSE in Table 1 is in-sample or out-of-sample; should add out-of-sample/forecast RMSE column
- Minor issue with abbreviation CPF-AS used before definition

Overall, this is a strong, novel paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for state-space models. It presents a technically sound approach with clear potential for impact in applications requiring flexible, nonparametric modeling of dynamical systems. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in time series modeling with a novel and rigorous solution, advancing the state of the art in Bayesian inference for dynamical systems.

I recommend acceptance as it addresses an important problem with a technically sound and novel approach, and the paper is clearly written with strong theoretical foundations. The minor weaknesses identified are easily addressable in a revision.
