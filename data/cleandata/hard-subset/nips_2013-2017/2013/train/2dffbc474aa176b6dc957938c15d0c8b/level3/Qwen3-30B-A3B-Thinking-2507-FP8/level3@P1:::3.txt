

This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models by placing a Gaussian process prior over the state transition dynamics and using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) to marginalize over the transition function while inferring the joint smoothing distribution. The authors build upon recent advances in PMCMC (Andrieu et al., 2010; Lindsten et al., 2012) and Gaussian process state-space models (Deisenroth et al., 2011; Turner et al., 2010), addressing a gap in the literature regarding learning the GP model for dynamics rather than using parametric approximations.

The primary strength of this work is its theoretically sound approach to preserving the full nonparametric expressivity of the model while enabling efficient Bayesian inference. The marginalization of the transition dynamics function is a clever technical solution that allows the authors to bypass the strong dependencies between states and the dynamics function. The use of sparse Gaussian processes to reduce computational complexity from O(T^4) to O(M^2T) is well-motivated and practically significant. The experimental results on a nonlinear benchmark system and a cart-pole system demonstrate the method's ability to capture complex dynamics and provide uncertainty estimates, with the smoothing distribution correctly accounting for multimodality in the state trajectory.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution. The application of PMCMC to this problem is largely a straightforward extension of existing methodology rather than a novel development of the PMCMC framework itself. The authors fail to provide a detailed, critical evaluation of the method's strengths and weaknesses, particularly regarding practical implementation challenges. There is no discussion of the sensitivity of the PMCMC sampler to parameter choices, convergence diagnostics, or the computational cost of the algorithm beyond the theoretical complexity bounds.

The experimental section demonstrates the approach but lacks rigorous empirical evaluation. The comparison with parametric models in Table 1 is limited to RMSE on a single benchmark, without statistical significance tests or analysis of when the GP-SSM outperforms parametric alternatives. The paper does not address important practical questions such as how the method scales with dimensionality, how many particles are required for convergence, or how the method performs when the assumed mean function is substantially different from the true dynamics. The sparse GP implementation details are also insufficiently described for reproducibility.

The paper's contribution is primarily methodological rather than theoretical or empirical. While the approach is technically sound, it doesn't advance the state of the art in PMCMC methodology or Gaussian process modeling. The authors claim to "retain the full nonparametric expressivity of the model," but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from previous work in terms of practical performance. The connection to previous NIPS papers on GP-SSMs (Deisenroth et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2005) is acknowledged but not sufficiently differentiated.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections and appropriate mathematical notation. However, the lack of detailed discussion about practical implementation challenges and limitations makes it difficult for readers to assess the method's real-world applicability. The experimental section could be strengthened by including diagnostic plots of the Markov chain convergence, detailed comparisons with alternative approaches, and analysis of when the method fails.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in state-space modeling, but the incremental nature of the contribution means it's unlikely to have substantial impact on the field. The method could be useful for practitioners, but the lack of rigorous evaluation and practical implementation guidance limits its immediate applicability.

Pro: Theoretical soundness of the marginalization approach and efficient implementation using sparse GPs.
Pro: Demonstrates ability to capture complex dynamics and multimodal smoothing distributions.
Pro: Provides a fully Bayesian framework for learning GP-SSMs.
Con: Application of PMCMC is a straightforward extension rather than a novel contribution.
Con: Lacks detailed evaluation of practical challenges and implementation difficulties.
Con: Experimental section lacks rigorous empirical comparison and statistical analysis.
Con: Insufficient critical evaluation of the method's limitations and failure cases.

Overall, while the paper presents a technically sound approach to a relevant problem, the lack of rigorous empirical evaluation, insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges, and incremental nature of the contribution make it a marginal submission for NIPS. The method would be stronger with more detailed analysis of the PMCMC sampler's performance, a more thorough comparison with alternative approaches, and clearer articulation of how this work advances beyond previous GP-SSM literature.
