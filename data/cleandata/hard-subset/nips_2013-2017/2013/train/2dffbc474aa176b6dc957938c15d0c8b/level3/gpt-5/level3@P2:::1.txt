Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents a fully Bayesian method for inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior on the transition function f and marginalizing f out analytically. This yields a non-Markovian prior over latent state trajectories, expressed as a product of GP one-step conditionals. To sample from the resulting joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T, θ | y0:T), the authors use particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), which maintains good mixing even for highly correlated state trajectories. Hyperparameters are updated conditional on a sampled state trajectory via standard GP machinery (e.g., slice sampling), and the predictive distribution over f is then obtained as a mixture of GP posteriors across MCMC samples. The paper also introduces a sparse GP variant using the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and details efficient factorization reuse (rank-1 updates/downdates) for the full GP case.

This work connects to and advances a line of GP-based dynamical system models: unlike EM/MAP approaches that parameterize f (Ghahramani & Roweis; Turner et al.; Wang et al.), the method preserves nonparametric expressivity by collapsing f. It complements work on filtering/smoothing with learned GPs (Deisenroth et al.) by providing the missing piece of fully Bayesian learning of the GP-SSM itself. The use of PGAS (Lindsten et al.) to address path degeneracy in non-Markovian SSMs is technically well-motivated and appropriate.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: collapsing the GP transition and sampling states via PGAS yields a coherent fully Bayesian solution that retains the nonparametric nature of the model.
- Technical soundness: derivations for the collapsed prior and the PGAS invariance are correct and well-cited; hyperparameter updates are consistent with GP regression conditional on x0:T.
- Practicality: clear attention to computational efficiency, including Cholesky updates/downdates and a sparse FIC construction with O(M^2 T) complexity.
- Clarity and accessibility: the exposition makes the non-Markovian SSM inference problem understandable and the algorithmic choices transparent.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical scope: experiments are relatively brief. Adding comparisons to EM/variational GP-SSM baselines (e.g., GPDM, EP-based methods) and to parametric particle smoothers would strengthen claims.
- Evaluation metrics: Table 1 and text should clearly separate in-sample versus out-of-sample predictive performance; add a column for forecast RMSE (and ideally predictive log-likelihood and calibration/coverage).
- Role of the mean function: discuss the implications of using a parametric transition model a priori versus as the GP mean (extrapolation, misspecification, identifiability) and show an ablation where the mean is zero/linear.
- Algorithmic details: define CPF-AS on first use; report sensitivity to N (particles), L (MCMC samples), and M (inducing points); include runtime and memory scaling with T, and mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS).
- Modeling choices: briefly discuss kernel choices and priors on Q and kernel hyperparameters, and potential issues if both transition and measurement models were made highly flexible.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and careful. The collapsed GP trajectory prior is correctly derived; the PGAS kernel is appropriate and its invariance property is leveraged correctly. The sparse GP construction is standard but well-integrated. Claims are supported by experiments, although broader baselines and more thorough predictive evaluation would improve confidence.

Clarity
Generally very clear and well organized. Minor issues: CPF-AS/PGAS abbreviations appear before full definitions in places; Table 1 needs explicit labeling of in-sample vs out-of-sample metrics and data splits. The implementation section is useful; a small algorithm box for CPF-AS would further aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. Prior GP-SSM learning approaches typically parametrize f and optimize via EM or MAP; here, f is marginalized and the non-Markovian posterior over x is sampled with PGAS. Applying ancestor sampling to this collapsed GP-SSM is, to my knowledge, novel for NIPS. The integration of sparse GP structure into the collapsed trajectory prior is also a valuable contribution.

Significance
Significant. A practical, fully Bayesian learning and smoothing method for GP-SSMs should be of interest to the time series, control, and Bayesian inference communities. The ability to preserve nonparametric flexibility while enabling efficient inference can influence subsequent work on non-Markovian latent variable models, and the implementation insights are likely to be reused.

Pros
- Fully Bayesian, nonparametric GP-SSM learning via marginalization of f.
- Effective use of PGAS to address path degeneracy in a non-Markovian setting.
- Computationally thoughtful (sparse GP option; factorization reuse).
- Clear exposition and useful implementation guidance.

Cons
- Limited empirical comparisons and evaluation metrics; unclear in/out-of-sample RMSE reporting.
- Minor presentation issues (early use of abbreviations).
- Limited discussion of sensitivity to mean function, kernel, and hyperpriors; no runtime/memory scaling plots.

Questions for the authors
- Is Table 1 RMSE computed in-sample or out-of-sample? Please add a held-out predictive metric (RMSE and log-likelihood) and calibration (e.g., coverage).
- How sensitive are results to the choice of mean function and kernel? Can you report ablations (zero/linear mean; RBF vs ARD kernels)?
- What are the practical limits on T, state dimension, and M given your implementation? Please provide wall-clock and memory scaling with T, N, and M.
- How do results compare to GPDM/EM and EP/variational GP-SSMs on the benchmark?

Minor comments
- Define CPF-AS upon first use and keep terminology consistent.
- Consider adding mixing diagnostics and acceptance stats for hyperparameter updates.
- Briefly discuss extensions to missing observations and non-Gaussian measurement noise.

Recommendation
Lean accept. Strong, technically sound paper with a novel and useful contribution to Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs; empirical evaluation could be expanded but is sufficient to demonstrate feasibility and benefits.

Reviewer confidence
High.
