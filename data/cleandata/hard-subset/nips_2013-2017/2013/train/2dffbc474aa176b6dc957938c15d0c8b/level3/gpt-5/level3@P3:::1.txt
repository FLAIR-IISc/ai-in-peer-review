Summary and relation to prior work
This paper presents a fully Bayesian method for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) with Gaussian process (GP) priors over the transition dynamics. The key idea is to marginalize the unknown transition function f analytically, which induces non-Markovian dependencies over the latent states, and then to perform inference over the joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T, θ | y0:T) using Particle MCMC—in particular, particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) implemented via a conditional particle filter with ancestor sampling (CPF-AS). This removes the need to sample/functionally represent f during inference and avoids strong x–f coupling in MCMC. The paper also develops an efficient sparse GP variant (FIC) and shows how to realize substantial computational savings using Cholesky updates/downdates and low-rank structure.

Relative to prior GP-SSM work (e.g., GPDM and EM/MAP approaches of Wang et al.; Turner et al.; Deisenroth et al.’s GPDS filtering/smoothing given learned dynamics), the novelty is a collapsed, fully Bayesian treatment of the dynamics coupled with PGAS to handle the resulting non-Markovian latent process. The approach leverages recent PMCMC theory (Andrieu et al.) and ancestor sampling for particle Gibbs (Lindsten et al.) to obtain good mixing without backward passes. The paper’s contribution thus complements earlier work that assumed a learned transition model and focuses instead on learning it while inferring states.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The derivation of the collapsed marginal prior over trajectories and its use within CPF-AS/PGAS is sound and well rooted in existing PMCMC theory. The use of GP predictive identities to form the non-Markovian state prior is correct. The sparse-GP (FIC) construction is standard and the complexity analyses (vanilla O(T^3), FIC O(M^2T)) are credible. The implementation details on rank-1 Cholesky updates/downdates and reusing factorizations are thoughtful and practically important.
- Empirics: The experiments (nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing; cart-and-pole) demonstrate that the method learns dynamics and provides calibrated uncertainty. Results are brief but adequate; adding more thorough comparisons and out-of-sample forecasting metrics would strengthen the empirical case.
- Reproducibility: The algorithmic description is sufficiently clear for an expert to reimplement, given the cited PGAS/CPF-AS literature.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized; the exposition of collapsing and the CPF-AS kernel for non-Markovian models is accessible.
- Minor clarity issues:
  - The abbreviation CPF-AS is used before being defined; define it at first use.
  - Table 1 and the preceding text are unclear about whether RMSE is in-sample or out-of-sample. Recommend adding an explicit out-of-sample/forecast RMSE column and clarifying dataset splits.
  - A concise schematic of computational reuse (which quantities are updated vs. recomputed) would help readers implement the rank-one updates.

Originality
- The main novelty is marginalizing the transition dynamics online within the forward CPF-AS recursion and using ancestor sampling to maintain mixing in a non-Markovian latent model. While PGAS itself is known, applying it to a collapsed GP-SSM with careful complexity control and a sparse (FIC) prior is a new and useful synthesis. The paper differentiates clearly from EM/MAP approaches and from post-learning filtering/smoothing work.

Significance
- This is a solid and timely contribution. A fully Bayesian, scalable inference scheme for GP-SSMs that does not require fixing a parametric transition model and that handles non-Markovian dependencies cleanly is valuable. The computational insights (sparsity and low-rank updates) increase practical impact. The work is likely to stimulate further research on inference for non-Markovian SSMs and to be adopted in applications requiring flexible dynamics with uncertainty quantification.

Strengths
- Elegant collapsed Bayesian treatment with GP priors over dynamics.
- Use of PGAS with ancestor sampling to restore mixing in non-Markovian latent models.
- Strong attention to computational efficiency: O(T^3) with reuse; O(M^2T) with FIC; practical Cholesky updates/downdates.
- Clear, accessible presentation with relevant connections to prior work.
- Empirical demonstrations are concise but illustrative; uncertainty is well characterized.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical scope is limited. Suggestions:
  - Add an explicit out-of-sample forecasting evaluation. In Table 1, clearly separate in-sample reconstruction error from out-of-sample 1-step (and possibly k-step) forecast RMSE. The current description of RMSE between predicted f(xt, ut) and ground truth should state whether predictions are conditioned on the training trajectory or evaluated on held-out sequences.
  - Include ablations: with/without sparse GP; with/without ancestor sampling; sensitivity to N (particles) and PGAS iterations; wall-clock timings vs. baselines.
- Modeling discussion:
  - Expand the discussion comparing (i) using a parametric transition model a priori (i.e., committing to it) versus (ii) using it as the GP mean function. Clarify implications for extrapolation/forecasting, bias-variance tradeoffs, and uncertainty calibration out of sample. A small experiment contrasting these choices would be very informative.
  - Comment on scalability to higher-dimensional states and long horizons; discuss memory costs and whether structured kernels or inducing-point placement strategies (e.g., learned vs. greedy) can help.
- Minor clarity/notation:
  - Define CPF-AS upon first mention in Algorithm 1.
  - In Section 3.1, remind readers that Q may be non-diagonal and clarify the block-diagonalization in Λ.
  - Briefly discuss how inducing inputs are selected and whether they are fixed or adapted across PGAS iterations.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is mixing to N and to the degree of nonlinearity/multimodality? Any diagnostics you recommend?
- For forecasting, do you propagate uncertainty through the mixture-of-Gaussians predictive transition or approximate with a single GP surrogate? What is the runtime tradeoff?
- How does performance degrade as state dimension increases (e.g., d ≥ 10)? Any practical tips (kernel choices, sparsity levels)?

Pros for acceptance
- Technically strong, novel, and well-executed combination of collapsed GP-SSM inference with PGAS and efficient linear algebra.
- Addresses an important problem with clear practical relevance.
- Likely to spark follow-up work on non-Markovian SSM inference.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical breadth; forecasting evaluation and ablations are missing.
- A few clarity issues (acronyms, evaluation protocol) that can be easily fixed.

Overall assessment
A strong, novel paper that advances Bayesian inference for GP-based dynamics by collapsing the transition function and leveraging ancestor-sampling PMCMC with careful computational engineering. With modest additions to the empirical evaluation and clarification of evaluation protocols and acronyms, this would be a very solid NIPS contribution. I recommend acceptance.
