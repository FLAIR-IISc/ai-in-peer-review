This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models where the transition dynamics are modeled by a Gaussian process (GP-SSM). The core idea is to marginalize out the infinite-dimensional state transition function, which induces long-range temporal dependencies between the latent states, breaking the Markov property. To perform inference in this resulting non-Markovian model, the authors employ a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This allows for sampling from the joint posterior distribution of the latent state trajectory and the model hyper-parameters. Once samples from this smoothing distribution are obtained, the posterior predictive distribution for the transition function is available as a mixture of GPs. The paper also proposes a sparse variant using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) approximation to reduce the computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). This work builds upon a rich literature of GP-SSMs but distinguishes itself by providing a complete, non-parametric Bayesian learning framework that avoids common approximations like MAP estimation or parametric representations of the GP.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution by applying a sophisticated and recent inference technique (PGAS) to the challenging problem of fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs. This is a novel combination of techniques that elegantly sidesteps the difficulty of direct inference on the function space by marginalizing the GP. This provides a principled and powerful method for system identification in a nonparametric setting, which is a problem of broad interest. The ability to learn the dynamics and infer the latent states simultaneously, while fully accounting for uncertainty, is a notable advance over prior work that often relied on point estimates or parametric approximations.

2.  **Quality:** The proposed methodology is technically sound. The derivation of the marginal state-transition prior and the subsequent application of PMCMC are correct and well-motivated. The paper is a complete piece of work, taking the reader from the model definition through to the inference algorithm and its sparse extension. The development of an efficient sparse GP-SSM variant to address the O(T^3) complexity of the full model is a strong and practical contribution that greatly enhances the potential applicability of the method.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the model, the motivation for marginalizing the transition function, and the resulting inference challenges. The description of the PGAS algorithm and its adaptation to this specific model is lucid. The experiments, while limited in some respects (see below), successfully illustrate the key capabilities of the model, such as handling multi-modality in the smoothing distribution and leveraging prior knowledge through the GP mean function.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its empirical evaluation and comparison to closely related alternative methods.

1.  **Lack of Comparison to other PMCMC methods:** The paper proposes using Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), but provides no justification for this choice over other standard PMCMC algorithms. For a paper whose main contribution is the application of a specific MCMC methodology, a comparison to strong baselines is essential. Specifically, comparisons to Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and Particle Gibbs with backward sampling would be highly informative. Both are well-established techniques for the same class of problems, and implementing them in this context appears straightforward. Without such comparisons, it is impossible to assess the relative efficiency and mixing performance of the proposed PGAS approach.

2.  **Insufficient Performance Diagnostics:** The experimental results demonstrate that the sampler works, but they lack the quantitative diagnostics necessary to understand its performance characteristics. The efficiency of PMCMC samplers is critically dependent on the number of particles (N) and the length of the time series (T). The authors should include plots of performance metrics like the Effective Sample Size (ESS) or the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) of the states or hyper-parameters. Analyzing these metrics as a function of N and T is crucial for understanding the algorithm's scalability and for providing practical guidance on setting N. For instance, it is known that for some models, the number of particles required by PMMH may grow quadratically with T to maintain a constant acceptance rate, whereas Particle Gibbs variants often exhibit more favorable sublinear scaling. An empirical investigation of this scaling behavior for the GP-SSM would have significantly strengthened the paper's contribution.

**Detailed Comments**

*   The work is a very interesting and elegant application of PMCMC to a fundamental problem. The idea of marginalizing the GP function and tackling the resulting non-Markovian latent variable model is compelling.
*   Section 4.2 provides a good high-level overview of the implementation, correctly identifying the need for efficient updates to the Cholesky factorizations. This attention to computational detail is commendable.
*   The experiment in Section 6.1 successfully highlights the model's ability to capture multimodal posteriors, a key advantage of particle-based methods over methods that rely on Gaussian approximations.
*   To significantly improve the paper, I strongly recommend that the authors conduct experiments comparing their chosen PGAS sampler to PMMH and Particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as proposed by Whiteley et al.). The key metrics for comparison should be the ESS achieved per unit of computational time for different values of N and T. This would provide a much clearer picture of the practical utility and efficiency of the proposed method and would constitute a more complete scientific contribution.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and important contribution to the literature on state-space models. It is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant problem. Its main shortcoming is the lack of a rigorous empirical comparison to alternative, state-of-the-art inference schemes. While the current results are promising, including such a comparison and more detailed performance diagnostics would make this an outstanding paper. Even without these additions, the conceptual contribution is strong enough to warrant serious consideration.
