This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs). The core idea is to place a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the unknown state transition function, resulting in a flexible, nonparametric model termed the GP-SSM. In a significant departure from previous work on GP-SSMs (e.g., Turner et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2008), which often resorts to parametric approximations or point estimates (MAP/ML) of the GP, this paper proposes to marginalize out the latent transition function entirely. This marginalization induces long-range temporal dependencies, rendering the state sequence non-Markovian. The authors skillfully tackle this challenging inference problem by employing a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This allows them to sample from the joint posterior distribution of the state trajectory and model hyperparameters without making parametric approximations to the GP, thus preserving its full nonparametric expressivity. The paper also provides a detailed treatment of computational efficiency, proposing the use of sparse GPs and efficient matrix factorization updates to reduce the complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2T).

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—marginalizing the GP dynamics function and performing inference directly on the resulting non-Markovian state trajectory—is highly novel and represents a significant conceptual advance in this area. It provides a principled, fully Bayesian framework for learning in GP-SSMs that avoids the approximations and potential for overfitting inherent in methods that optimize a finite set of inducing points or seek MAP estimates. This work is likely to be influential and stimulate further research on inference techniques for non-Markovian models and other complex probabilistic systems.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The application of PGAS to this specific problem is well-motivated and elegant, providing a practical tool to navigate the complex posterior landscape. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of both GP models and advanced sequential Monte Carlo methods. Furthermore, the detailed discussion in Section 4 on computational complexity, including the use of FIC sparse priors and efficient Cholesky updates/downdates, shows a commendable commitment to making the method practical. This is a complete and well-executed piece of research.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the model is introduced logically, and the inference algorithm is developed in a step-by-step manner that is accessible to readers familiar with GPs and MCMC. The derivation of the marginal prior and the subsequent application of SMC and PGAS are explained with precision.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments effectively demonstrate the method's core capabilities, particularly in handling multimodal posteriors, the evaluation is somewhat brief. The benchmark in Section 6.1 is illustrative, but the paper would be stronger with experiments on more challenging, higher-dimensional problems or a real-world dataset to better showcase the benefits of the sparse implementation and the overall robustness of the approach.

*   **Clarity of Experimental Results:** In Section 6.1, the text describes generating a new dataset of 10,000 time steps to measure predictive capability for the results in Table 1. However, it is not explicitly stated whether the reported RMSE is for one-step-ahead prediction on this new out-of-sample data or for smoothing the original 200-step training trajectory (in-sample). This ambiguity makes it difficult to fully assess the model's generalization performance. I strongly recommend the authors clarify this point and consider adding a column to Table 1 for multi-step-ahead forecasting RMSE, which would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the learned dynamics.

*   **Discussion on the GP Mean Function:** The paper astutely notes that prior knowledge can be encoded in the GP mean function, as demonstrated with "model B." This is a powerful feature. However, the paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of the trade-offs involved. For example, how does using a misspecified parametric model as the GP mean compare to using a fully parametric model alone? What are the implications for long-term forecasting, where the GP predictions revert to the prior mean? A brief discussion on this would add further depth and practical guidance for users of the model.

*   **Minor Issue:** The abbreviation "CPF-AS" is used in Algorithm 1 before it is formally defined in Section 3.3.1. This should be a simple correction.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and principled fully Bayesian inference method for GP-SSMs.
    *   The technique of marginalizing the dynamics function and applying PGAS is a significant contribution to the field.
    *   The paper is technically excellent, clearly written, and considers practical computational aspects.
    *   The work is likely to have a high impact and inspire follow-up research.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The experimental validation is somewhat limited.
    *   Reporting of key results in Table 1 is ambiguous.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong and impressive paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the literature on learning in dynamical systems. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily related to the scope and clarity of the experimental section; they do not detract from the importance of the core methodological contribution. The proposed approach is elegant, technically sound, and well-presented. I recommend acceptance.
