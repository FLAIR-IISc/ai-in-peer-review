This paper presents a compelling approach to Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), utilizing particle MCMC methods, specifically building upon Lindsten et al.’s ancestral-sampling particle Gibbs. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of prior work – the need for a fixed model of the transition dynamics – and elegantly address it by marginalizing over the transition function. The proposal is clear, well-motivated, and represents an original application of particle MCMC techniques to this challenging problem. A significant methodological contribution is the development of a sparse GP-SSM variant, aiming to improve computational efficiency. The writing is exceptionally clear, and the paper is well-organized, making the complex methodology accessible.

However, despite these strengths, the paper is noticeably lacking in comparative evaluations. A major omission is the absence of comparisons to other prominent particle MCMC schemes, most notably particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (Whiteley et al.). Given the authors’ focus on particle MCMC, these comparisons are crucial for contextualizing their method’s performance and highlighting its potential advantages. Implementing these schemes would be relatively straightforward, building on the existing PMCMC framework, and their inclusion would significantly strengthen the paper's claims. 

Furthermore, the results section lacks informative diagnostics for assessing the behavior of the proposed method. While the presented experiments demonstrate the method's applicability to benchmark problems, graphs displaying performance metrics such as autocorrelation functions (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS) as functions of both the particle count (N) and the time horizon (T) are essential. These diagnostics would reveal the scaling properties of the algorithm, indicating whether performance degrades linearly or sublinearly with increasing problem complexity.  My belief, and that supported by prior work (specifically Whiteley et al. regarding PMMH), is that PMMH’s particle count is likely to grow quadratically with T, whereas particle Gibbs variants, including the one presented here, should exhibit sublinear growth. Demonstrating this scaling difference empirically would be a strong argument in favor of the proposed approach. 

The experiments themselves, while illustrative, could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the approximation quality.  For example, quantifying the accuracy of the learned dynamics beyond RMSE would be valuable.  Exploring sensitivity to hyperparameters and varying the complexity of the true underlying system would further demonstrate the robustness of the method.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of marginalizing out the transition function in GP-SSMs and utilizing a tailored particle MCMC sampler is novel and addresses a significant limitation in existing literature.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand, despite the technical complexity.
*   **Soundness:** The methodology appears theoretically sound and builds upon established concepts in Bayesian nonparametrics and particle MCMC.
*   **Potential Impact:** The method has the potential to be widely applicable in various fields where GP-SSMs are used, such as robotics, finance, and environmental modeling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Missing Comparisons:** The lack of comparison to PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling is a significant weakness, hindering a fair assessment of the method’s performance.
*   **Insufficient Diagnostics:** The absence of performance diagnostics (ACF, ESS vs. N and T) makes it difficult to evaluate the algorithm's scaling and convergence properties.
*   **Limited Empirical Analysis:** While the experiments are illustrative, a more comprehensive analysis of the method's performance and sensitivity would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and technically sound paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of GP-SSMs and particle MCMC. However, the lack of comparative analysis and performance diagnostics significantly limits its impact. Addressing these weaknesses would substantially improve the paper and make a stronger case for its acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions contingent upon the inclusion of the requested comparisons and diagnostics. Specifically, the authors should include results comparing their method to PMMH and Whiteley et al.'s particle Gibbs with backward sampling, and provide plots of ACF or ESS as functions of N and T to demonstrate the scaling behavior of their algorithm.
