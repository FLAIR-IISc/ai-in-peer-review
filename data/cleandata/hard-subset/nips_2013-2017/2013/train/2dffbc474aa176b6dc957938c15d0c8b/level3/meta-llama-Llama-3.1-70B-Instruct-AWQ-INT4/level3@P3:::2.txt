This paper proposes an application of particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) to perform Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The authors employ the particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) algorithm, which is a variant of PMCMC that has been shown to be effective in certain scenarios. The paper is well-written, clear, and provides an original application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs.

The authors make a significant methodological contribution by developing a sparse GP-SSM variant, which reduces the computational complexity of the algorithm. This is an important contribution, as GP-SSMs can be computationally expensive to evaluate.

One major omission in the paper is the lack of comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes, such as particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling. These comparisons would be straightforward to implement and are necessary to assess the relative performance of the proposed method.

Furthermore, the results presented in the paper lack informative diagnostics. For example, it would be useful to see graphs of performance metrics, such as the autocorrelation function (ACF) or the effective sample size (ESS), as functions of the particle count N and the time horizon T. These diagnostics would help to determine the scaling of the algorithm (linear vs. sublinear).

The reviewer believes that PMMH likely requires the particle count to grow quadratically with T (as shown in Whiteley et al.), whereas particle Gibbs variants require sublinear growth. However, this is not verified in the paper, and the authors should consider including such comparisons in future work.

In conclusion, the paper is well-written and provides an original application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs. However, it would be significantly improved by including comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes and providing more informative diagnostics.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides an original application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs.
* The authors make a significant methodological contribution by developing a sparse GP-SSM variant.
* The paper is well-written and clear.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes.
* The results presented in the paper lack informative diagnostics.
* The authors do not verify the scaling of the algorithm.
