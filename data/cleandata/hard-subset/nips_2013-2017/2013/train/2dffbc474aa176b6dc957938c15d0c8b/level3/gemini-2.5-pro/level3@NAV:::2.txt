This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The core idea is to marginalize the GP transition function and apply Particle MCMC (PMCMC) methods to sample from the joint posterior of the latent states and hyperparameters. The authors focus on the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm, building upon work by Lindsten et al. (2012). This method extends the PMCMC framework to a non-parametric and non-Markovian setting, providing an elegant alternative to previous methods based on parametric approximations or point estimates. A sparse variant using the FIC prior is also introduced to improve computational scaling.

The paper’s strengths lie in its originality, clarity, and significance.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of PGAS for fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs is a novel and valuable contribution. It allows for the full expressivity of the non-parametric model to be retained, which is a significant advance over prior art that often relies on restrictive approximations. The development of a sparse version of the model is also a strong methodological addition.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The technical exposition is excellent, guiding the reader effectively through the complex model and inference scheme.

Despite these strengths, the paper has a major omission in its experimental evaluation.

*   **Quality:** The primary weakness is the lack of comparison to other standard PMCMC algorithms. To properly assess the performance of the chosen PGAS sampler, it is crucial to benchmark it against Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and Particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PGBS). These alternatives are directly applicable and would provide essential context for the sampler's efficiency.
*   Furthermore, the results section lacks informative diagnostics on sampler performance. The authors should provide an analysis of how performance metrics like Effective Sample Size (ESS) or the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) scale with the number of particles (N) and the time horizon (T). This is critical for understanding the algorithm's practical limits. For instance, theory suggests that PMMH may require N to grow quadratically with T, whereas PG variants often exhibit much better, sublinear scaling. An empirical validation of this would greatly strengthen the paper's claims.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper with a strong core idea. However, the lack of comparative experiments and scaling analysis makes it difficult to fully evaluate the contribution. The paper would be significantly improved and would constitute a much stronger submission if it included a thorough empirical comparison against PMMH and PGBS, along with a quantitative analysis of the sampler's mixing performance as a function of N and T.
