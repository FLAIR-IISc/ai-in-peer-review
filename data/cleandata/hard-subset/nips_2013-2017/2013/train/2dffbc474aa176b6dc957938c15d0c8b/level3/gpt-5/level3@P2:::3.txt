Summary
The paper addresses fully Bayesian inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior on the transition function f and using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) to sample from the joint smoothing distribution while marginalizing out f. The key technical move is to collapse the model by analytically integrating out the GP transition, which induces a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories that can be written as a product of GP one-step predictions. The authors then use Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) to sample x0:T and slice sampling for hyperparameters, leveraging the analogy to GP regression given a state trajectory. They also present a sparse variant using the FIC approximation to reduce computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2T), and describe Cholesky updates/downdates for efficient CPF-AS implementation. Empirically, they demonstrate smoothing and one-step prediction on a 1D nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole system, and discuss obtaining predictive mixtures and single-GP surrogates.

The work relates to prior GP-SSM literature that has typically used EM/MAP estimates or fixed dynamics for subsequent filtering/smoothing (e.g., Ghahramani & Roweis 1999; Wang, Fleet & Hertzmann 2006 GPDM; Turner et al. 2010 EM with GP structures; Deisenroth et al. on EP-based inference in learned GPDS). On the inference side, it builds directly on PMCMC (Andrieu, Doucet & Holenstein 2010) and PGAS (Lindsten et al., NIPS 2012) to handle the non-Markovian collapsed model.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean formulation: collapsing the GP transition preserves nonparametric flexibility and leads to a principled target p(x0:T, θ | y0:T).
- Appropriate and modern inference machinery: PGAS is a good fit for non-Markovian smoothing and often mixes better than earlier PMCMC variants.
- Clear derivation of the collapsed prior and efficient implementation details, including rank-1 Cholesky updates and a sparse FIC construction with complexity analysis.
- The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive view is well-motivated and allows uncertainty-aware predictions; the surrogate single-GP option is practical for control.
- Demonstrations on multimodal smoothing (1D benchmark) and a 4D mechanical system illustrate feasibility.

Weaknesses
- Methodological contribution is largely an application of existing PMCMC/PGAS ideas to a GP-SSM; the novelty beyond careful engineering is modest.
- Limited empirical evaluation: small-scale case studies without rigorous baselines, quantitative comparisons, or ablations. No comparisons to EM/MAP GPDM, EP/variational GPDS, or alternative PMCMC variants (e.g., PMMH, backward simulation).
- Practical challenges are not adequately discussed or evaluated: sensitivity to priors and kernel choice, particle number N, effect of T and state dimensionality, path degeneracy in long series, mixing diagnostics, and runtime/memory scaling.
- No systematic assessment of sparse vs. dense GP trade-offs (M, inducing input selection), or of the impact of the mean function specification on identifiability and learning.
- Evaluation focuses on one-step prediction; no multi-step forecasting, predictive log-likelihoods, calibration metrics, or robustness to model misspecification.

Quality
Technically sound in its derivations and use of PGAS for the collapsed non-Markovian model. The implementation details appear correct, and the use of slice sampling for θ given x0:T is standard. However, the paper does not provide the level of empirical evidence needed to substantiate robustness and practical performance: no mixing diagnostics (ESS/AUTOCOR, trace plots), no sensitivity studies (N, M, priors), and limited comparisons. The claims about “very good mixing” are qualitative and unquantified.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The collapsing derivation and CPF-AS with ancestor weights are clearly presented, and the complexity discussion is helpful. However:
- More guidance on choosing N, the number and placement of inducing points, and hyperpriors would improve reproducibility.
- A concise algorithmic summary with practical defaults and complexity/memory budgets would be useful.
- Empirical sections should report metrics, computational costs, and diagnostics.

Originality
Moderate. Collapsing GPs in time-series models is known, and PMCMC (including PGAS) is established. The contribution is the synthesis and careful implementation for GP-SSMs with a sparse option. The invariance and non-Markovian handling stem from PGAS rather than a new inference principle.

Significance
Potentially useful to practitioners who need fully Bayesian GP-SSMs and want to avoid parametric approximations. That said, the limited empirical scope and absence of strong baselines make it hard to assess state-of-the-art advancement. The sparse construction and efficient updates may be of practical interest, but broader impact depends on demonstrated scalability and robustness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous empirical evaluation:
  - Compare to EM/MAP GPDM (Wang et al., NIPS 2006), EP-based GPDS (Deisenroth & Mohamed, NIPS 2012), and PMMH/backward-simulation PMCMC variants on the same datasets.
  - Report predictive log-likelihoods, multi-step forecast errors, calibration (PIT/histograms), and smoothing RMSE with confidence intervals.
  - Include mixing/efficiency diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS/sec) and wall-clock/runtime vs. N, T, and state dimension.
  - Sensitivity analyses over priors, kernel choices, Q, and mean function misspecification; ablate ancestor sampling and the sparse approximation (vary M, inducing placement).
- Discuss practical challenges: path degeneracy for large T, initialization strategies, identifiability when g has unknown parameters, memory requirements, and numerical stability.
- Explore higher-dimensional benchmarks and a real dataset; include robustness to non-Gaussian measurement models.
- Clarify guidance for selecting inducing inputs (even if using simple heuristics), and quantify the accuracy–speed trade-off.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, fully Bayesian nonparametric SSM inference with a clear, coherent algorithmic pipeline.
- Appropriate and well-motivated use of PGAS for a challenging non-Markovian posterior.
- Useful engineering contributions (sparse variant, efficient factorizations) that could make the approach practical.

Arguments against acceptance
- Primarily an application of known PMCMC methods; limited novelty.
- Empirical evidence is not rigorous enough to establish effectiveness and robustness relative to strong baselines.
- Missing discussion and evaluation of practical difficulties that are central to PMCMC in this setting.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is technically sound and potentially useful, but it needs substantially stronger and more critical empirical evaluation, plus a deeper discussion of practical considerations, to justify publication at NIPS.
