Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles fully Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing the transition function f and sampling directly from the joint smoothing distribution using particle MCMC. The core inference engine is particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS; Lindsten et al.), implemented via CPF-AS, with a careful treatment of computational reuse. A sparse variant based on FIC reduces cost from O(T^3) to O(M^2T) while preserving a nonparametric prior. This sits squarely within a line of NIPS work on GP-based dynamical systems (e.g., Wang et al. GPDM; Deisenroth et al. EP/robust smoothing) and PMCMC (Andrieu et al.), and is, to my knowledge, the first application of PGAS to GP-SSMs with the dynamics collapsed.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound, with clear derivations of the collapsed prior and practical implementation details (rank-1 updates, reuse of Cholesky factors). The FIC construction is well-motivated. However, the experimental evaluation misses essential baselines among PMCMC schemes: particle marginal MH (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PG-BS; Whiteley et al.). These are straightforward to implement on the same collapsed target and are necessary to assess relative mixing and computational efficiency. The paper also lacks informative MCMC diagnostics: autocorrelation functions and effective sample size (ESS) as functions of particle count N and time horizon T, ideally normalized by wall-clock time. Such plots would substantiate scaling claims (e.g., PMMH often requires N growing as O(T^2) whereas particle Gibbs variants can be sublinear).

Clarity: The paper is very clearly written and well organized; algorithmic details are sufficient for reproduction. Please cite and discuss PG-BS explicitly and clarify what claims are empirical versus theoretical.

Originality: While the sampler itself is not new, applying PGAS to collapsed GP-SSMs and introducing a sparse GP-SSM variant is an original and interesting combination.

Significance: A robust fully Bayesian alternative to EM/MAP approaches for GP-SSMs is valuable, and the sparse construction increases applicability. The impact would be considerably strengthened by rigorous PMCMC comparisons and convergence diagnostics.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, careful, and practically useful integration of PGAS with GP-SSMs.
- Sparse GP-SSM variant with compelling complexity reduction.
- Applications beyond toy problems (cart-pole).

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing PMMH and PG-BS baselines.
- No ACF/ESS-vs-(N,T) or runtime-per-ESS scaling diagnostics.
- Limited sensitivity analyses (e.g., inducing point number/placement).

Overall: Well-written and promising; inclusion of PMMH/PG-BS comparisons and diagnostic scaling plots would substantially improve the paper.
