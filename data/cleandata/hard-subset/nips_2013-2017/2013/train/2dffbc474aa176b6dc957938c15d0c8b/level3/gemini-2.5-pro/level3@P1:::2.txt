This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models where the transition dynamics are governed by a Gaussian Process (GP-SSM). The core idea is to marginalize out the unknown dynamics function, which induces long-range temporal dependencies in the state trajectory, making the resulting latent process non-Markovian. To perform inference in this non-Markovian model, the authors propose using Particle MCMC (PMCMC) methods, specifically the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm developed by Lindsten et al. (2012). This allows for jointly sampling the latent state trajectories and model hyper-parameters. Once posterior samples of the state trajectories are obtained, the posterior predictive distribution over the dynamics function can be constructed as a mixture of GPs. The authors also develop a sparse variant using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) GP prior to reduce the computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2T).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper is well-written, clear, and addresses an important and challenging problem in machine learning and time-series modeling. The application of recent PMCMC techniques to the GP-SSM setting is novel and constitutes a significant contribution. Placing a GP prior on the dynamics is a natural way to model complex systems without committing to a specific parametric form, and the proposed fully Bayesian treatment is principled and elegant. The development of a sparse GP-SSM variant to improve computational scalability is also a valuable methodological contribution. The experiments, particularly on the nonlinear system benchmark, effectively demonstrate the model's ability to capture complex, multi-modal posterior distributions over the state trajectories.

However, the paper has two significant weaknesses in its experimental evaluation and comparison to related work.

First, the primary omission is the lack of any comparison to other state-of-the-art PMCMC samplers. The authors choose to use PGAS, but provide no justification for this choice over other prominent alternatives. Specifically, a comparison against Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and Particle Gibbs with backward sampling would be highly informative. Both are standard techniques in the PMCMC literature and would be straightforward to implement for this model. Without such comparisons, it is impossible to assess the relative performance of the chosen sampler. Does PGAS offer better mixing, lower variance, or a more favorable trade-off between particle count and accuracy compared to these alternatives? This is a critical question that the paper leaves unanswered.

Second, the experimental results lack informative diagnostics about the performance of the sampler itself. The paper shows that the method can produce reasonable smoothing distributions, but it does not analyze the efficiency of the MCMC algorithm. To properly assess the quality of an MCMC-based method, it is crucial to report metrics like the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) of the samples or the Effective Sample Size (ESS). Furthermore, it would be extremely valuable to see how these metrics scale with the number of particles (N) and the length of the time series (T). For instance, work by Whiteley et al. suggests that for certain models, PMMH may require a particle count that grows quadratically with T to maintain a constant acceptance rate, whereas Particle Gibbs variants might require only sublinear growth. An empirical investigation of these scaling properties for the GP-SSM would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and provide practical guidance for users of the method. The current experiments demonstrate feasibility but not efficiency or scalability.

In summary, this is a very good paper with a strong central idea. The application of PGAS to a marginalized GP-SSM is an elegant and powerful combination. However, the work in its current form feels incomplete due to the lack of experimental comparison with other PMCMC methods and the absence of quantitative diagnostics regarding sampler performance. Including these elements would elevate the paper from a good application paper to a more complete and impactful contribution to the literature on inference in nonparametric time-series models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and difficult problem of fully Bayesian learning in nonparametric state-space models.
*   The core idea of marginalizing the GP dynamics and using PMCMC for inference is elegant, principled, and novel in this context.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
*   Includes a practical sparse variant to address computational complexity, increasing the potential impact of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete. There is a major omission in not comparing the chosen PGAS sampler against key alternatives like PMMH or PG with backward sampling.
*   The paper lacks standard MCMC performance diagnostics (e.g., ACF, ESS), making it impossible to judge the efficiency or scaling properties of the proposed sampler.
*   Without these comparisons and diagnostics, the central claims about the efficiency and utility of the proposed inference scheme are not fully substantiated. The reader is left wondering if a simpler or alternative PMCMC method would have performed as well or better.
