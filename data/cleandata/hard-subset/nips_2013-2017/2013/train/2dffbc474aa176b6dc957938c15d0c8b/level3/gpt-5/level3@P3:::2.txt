Summary and relation to prior work
This paper applies particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) to fully Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior on the transition function f, then marginalize f out to target p(θ, x0:T | y0:T) directly. This produces a non-Markovian latent process, for which the authors use Lindsten et al.’s particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) to sample full state trajectories efficiently, alternating with updates of hyperparameters. Methodologically, the paper also develops and implements a sparse GP-SSM variant based on the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). Experiments include a nonlinear scalar benchmark and a 4D cart-pole system.

Relative to prior NIPS/PMCMC work, the paper leverages PGAS (Lindsten et al., NIPS 2012) as the main inference engine, contrasting with earlier GP-SSM learning approaches based on MAP/EM or parametric surrogates (e.g., Turner et al., Deisenroth et al., Wang et al.). Compared to conventional smoothing conditioned on a fixed transition model, the proposed collapsed formulation makes smoothing feasible without explicitly instantiating f, after which posterior over f can be recovered analytically given sampled trajectories.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The marginalization of f and the resulting Gaussian product structure, the CPF-AS kernel invariance, and the hyperparameter sampling strategy are all presented correctly and with appropriate references. The complexity analysis and the sparse FIC construction are sound and practically relevant.
- Empirics: The paper convincingly demonstrates that the method mixes on nontrivial examples and that the learned dynamics are predictive. However, the experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to alternative PMCMC schemes and informative MCMC diagnostics. This omission materially limits conclusions about relative efficiency and scaling.
- Reproducibility: Algorithmic details are sufficient for an expert to reproduce the method; the sparse implementation section is particularly helpful.

Clarity
The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The presentation of the collapsed prior, the CPF-AS-based sampler, and the sparse variant is careful and accessible. Figures are informative, but the analysis would benefit from standard MCMC diagnostics and scaling plots (see below).

Originality
- Original application: Using PGAS for fully Bayesian GP-SSMs with f marginalized is, to my knowledge, an original and interesting application of particle MCMC.
- Methodological addition: The FIC-based sparse GP-SSM within this collapsed PMCMC framework is a worthwhile contribution.
- Relation to prior work is adequately cited, though empirical comparisons to other PMCMC variants are missing.

Significance
Learning nonparametric nonlinear dynamics with full uncertainty quantification is important across time-series and control. The proposed approach maintains nonparametric expressivity, offers a scalable sparse option, and can be a building block for downstream tasks (e.g., robust filtering/control). With stronger empirical comparisons and diagnostics, the paper could have substantial impact.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous exposition; careful algorithmic design.
- Appropriate and powerful use of PGAS to address non-Markovianity induced by collapsing f.
- Sparse FIC variant meaningfully reduces computational cost without sacrificing the nonparametric model class.
- Demonstrations on both a challenging multimodal scalar system and a 4D dynamical system.

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) Missing baselines among PMCMC schemes. The paper does not compare to:
- Particle marginal Metropolis–Hastings (PMMH; Andrieu et al.).
- Particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PG-BS; e.g., Whiteley et al., Lindsten & Schön).
These are natural, straightforward baselines to implement given the existing codebase (same SMC core; different MCMC wrapper). Such comparisons are necessary to assess relative performance and mixing.

2) Lack of informative diagnostics and scaling analyses. Please add:
- Autocorrelation functions (ACF) for representative state coordinates and key hyperparameters, and effective sample size (ESS) per second.
- Systematic studies as functions of particle count N and time horizon T: ACF/ESS vs N for fixed T; ACF/ESS vs T with N tuned.
- Acceptance rates (for PMMH) or integrated autocorrelation times (for PG variants), and CPU-time–normalized ESS.
These will reveal whether the method scales linearly or sublinearly and whether path degeneracy is adequately mitigated.

3) Expected scaling behavior to test. My prior is that PMMH’s mixing deteriorates more severely with T (e.g., needing rapidly growing N, plausibly quadratic in T as suggested in discussions by Whiteley et al.), whereas particle Gibbs variants (including PGAS/PG-BS) can achieve sublinear growth in N to maintain mixing. Please empirically probe this with the proposed diagnostics and report the observed scaling laws.

4) Ablations helpful to practitioners:
- PGAS vs PG-BS vs PMMH on both tasks, across N and T, with wall-clock budgets.
- Effect of the sparse FIC approximation (vary M) on mixing and predictive accuracy.
- Sensitivity to mean function specification.

Questions for the authors
- Given your CPF-AS implementation, how much extra code/compute would be needed to run PMMH and PG-BS? Any obstacles?
- Do you observe different sensitivity to N between PGAS and PMMH on your benchmarks?
- Can you report ESS/sec for θx (kernel length-scales/variances) and for representative xt at early vs late times to quantify path degeneracy effects?

Minor comments
- Please include references to the specific PG-BS implementations you consider most comparable (e.g., Whiteley et al.; Lindsten & Schön).
- If space permits, add runtime breakdowns for O(T^3) vs O(M^2 T), and a small study showing near-linear scaling in T for fixed M, N.

Pro/con list
Pros:
- Clear, technically solid paper with an original and useful application of PGAS to GP-SSMs.
- Maintains full nonparametric expressivity; elegant collapsed formulation.
- Practical sparse variant with careful implementation details.
- Promising results on challenging systems.

Cons:
- No comparisons to PMMH and PG-BS; hard to judge relative efficiency.
- Missing MCMC diagnostics and scaling studies vs N and T.
- Limited guidance for practitioners on tuning and trade-offs.

Verdict
A well-written and interesting paper that advances fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs and contributes a practical sparse variant. However, it would be significantly strengthened by including straightforward comparisons to PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, along with standard diagnostics and scaling analyses (ACF/ESS vs N and T). With those additions, the work would make a compelling case for PGAS in this setting.
