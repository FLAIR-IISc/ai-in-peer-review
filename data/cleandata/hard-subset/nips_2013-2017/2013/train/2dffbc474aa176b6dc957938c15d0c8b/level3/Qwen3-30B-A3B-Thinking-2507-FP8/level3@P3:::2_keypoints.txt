- Presents application of PGAS (ancestral-sampling particle Gibbs) to inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), marginalizing the transition function f to infer p(x₀:T | y₀:T, θ).  
- Introduces a sparse GP-SSM using the FIC approximation, reducing complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T) with M ≪ T.  
- Builds on prior GP-SSM and PMCMC work (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al., Lindsten et al.) and adapts PGAS for non-Markovian latent models.  
- Strong technical quality and solid theoretical foundation with clear derivations of the marginal prior and implementation details.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and reproducible; sparse GP implementation details are particularly clear.  
- Novel combination of PGAS with GP-SSMs provides a fully Bayesian, nonparametric approach that fills a gap in the literature.  
- Methodologically significant: marginalizing the transition function preserves nonparametric expressivity while enabling Bayesian inference.  
- Experimental validation on synthetic and cart-pole benchmarks shows practical utility but lacks comparative depth.  
- Major omission: no comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes (PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling), which are necessary to assess relative performance.  
- Experimental diagnostics are insufficient: missing ACF/ESS plots or metrics as functions of particle count N and time horizon T to assess scaling behavior.  
- Reviewer suspects PMMH may need particle counts growing ~O(T²) while PGAS likely requires sublinear growth—this comparison should be empirically evaluated.  
- Limited discussion and empirical evidence for computational complexity claims relative to alternative methods.  
- Overall recommendation: technically sound and valuable contribution, but requires major revision to include PMCMC comparisons and scaling diagnostics before final acceptance.
