This paper studies fully Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing the transition function and sampling latent states and hyperparameters with Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). The authors show how collapsing the GP transition induces a non-Markovian prior over trajectories, propose efficient CPF-AS-based updates, and discuss a sparse FIC variant to reduce complexity to O(M^2 T). Experiments on a nonlinear synthetic benchmark and a 4D cart-pole system illustrate smoothing and one-step prediction performance.

Relation to prior work: The modeling setup builds on GP-SSMs and GP-based filtering/smoothing (e.g., Deisenroth et al.; Turner et al.; Wang et al.). The inference machinery follows PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.). The paper’s main conceptual move—collapsing f and sampling x0:T, θ with PGAS—is consistent with known PMCMC methodology; the contribution is its application to GP-SSMs with practical implementation details.

Quality: The high-level formulation is sound, and the collapsed representation is correct. However, the paper understates practical challenges in getting PMCMC to work: there is no systematic assessment of path degeneracy, particle number N, mixing (ACF, ESS), or sensitivity to priors, kernel choice, and initialization. The sparse variant raises nontrivial issues (inducing input selection, stability of downdates) that are not empirically probed. Empirically, results are demonstrative but not rigorous: no comparisons to strong alternatives (e.g., EM/MAP GP-SSM, variational GP-SSM, SMC^2 or other PMCMC kernels), no predictive log-likelihood or calibration (coverage/PIT), and limited runtime/efficiency reporting.

Clarity: The exposition of the model and collapsed prior is clear, and the link to GP regression for θ updates is helpful. Reproducibility would benefit from explicit priors, initialization, convergence diagnostics, kernel/mean choices, and ablations over N, M, T with wall-clock time.

Originality: Moderate. The combination of GP-SSMs with collapsed PGAS is natural and largely a straightforward application of existing PMCMC tools. Implementation tricks are useful but incremental.

Significance: Potentially useful for practitioners needing Bayesian GP-SSM learning, but the current evidence does not convincingly establish advantages over existing methods or robustness in realistic regimes.

Pros (for acceptance):
- Clear, coherent presentation of a collapsed PMCMC approach for GP-SSMs.
- Practical complexity discussion and a sparse variant.
- Demonstrations on nonlinear and 4D tasks.

Cons (against acceptance):
- Limited novelty beyond applying established PMCMC to this setting.
- Insufficient analysis of practical difficulties and algorithmic tuning.
- Lacks rigorous empirical evaluation, baselines, calibration, and efficiency metrics.

Recommendation: Weak reject; strengthen with thorough diagnostics, comparative baselines, sensitivity studies, and calibration/likelihood evaluations.
