This paper presents a novel Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), tackling the challenging problem of state and parameter estimation in nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to marginalize over the transition dynamics function, enabling efficient inference via a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) sampler with ancestor sampling. This work builds upon a substantial body of research in GP-SSMs, notably extending the methods of Turner et al. [2] and Deisenroth et al. [5, 6] by providing a principled way to *learn* the GP dynamics, rather than assuming them known. The approach is technically sound, well-presented, and addresses a key limitation of existing GP-SSM methods – the need for a pre-specified transition model.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The technical contribution of online marginalization of the transition dynamics via ancestor sampling is significant. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges involved in inference for non-Markovian state-space models and propose a well-motivated and effective solution. The consideration of computational efficiencies, particularly through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates, is commendable and crucial for practical applicability. The presentation is generally very clear and accessible, facilitating understanding of the complex methodology. The connections to related work are well-established, accurately placing the contribution within the broader research landscape.

However, the empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. While the results on the nonlinear system benchmark and the cart-pole example are encouraging, they are brief. Expanding the experimental section with additional datasets and comparisons to alternative methods would strengthen the paper considerably. Specifically, the discussion on the learned dynamics in the cart-pole example could be expanded with a visualization of the learned function.

A crucial point to address is the comparison between using a parametric transition model a priori versus as the GP mean, as the authors briefly mention. A more detailed discussion of the implications for forecasting and out-of-sample performance would be valuable. In what scenarios is one approach preferred over the other? What are the trade-offs in terms of flexibility, computational cost, and predictive accuracy?

The clarity of Table 1 is also a concern. The text preceding the table is unclear regarding whether the reported RMSE values are in-sample or out-of-sample. Adding a column for out-of-sample/forecast RMSE would significantly improve the interpretability of the results and demonstrate the model's generalization ability. 

A minor issue exists regarding the abbreviation "CPF-AS," which is used before it is defined. This should be rectified by introducing the abbreviation when it is first mentioned in Section 3.3.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to GP-SSM inference.
*   Efficient marginalization of the transition dynamics via ancestor sampling.
*   Strong consideration of computational complexity.
*   Clear and accessible presentation.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
*   Likely to stimulate further research in non-Markovian state-space modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed discussion regarding the choice between parametric prior and GP mean.
*   Unclear reporting of in-sample/out-of-sample performance in Table 1.
*   Minor notational issue with “CPF-AS”.

**Overall:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong and original paper. The proposed method offers a significant advancement in the field of GP-SSMs and has the potential to impact a wide range of applications. The authors have demonstrated a deep understanding of the underlying theory and a commitment to practical implementation. Addressing the concerns regarding the empirical evaluation and clarity of results would further enhance the paper's contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
