This paper presents a fully Bayesian framework for inference and learning in state-space models where the transition dynamics are governed by a Gaussian Process (GP-SSM). The authors' key contribution is an elegant method to marginalize out the unknown dynamics function. This marginalization induces temporal dependencies across the entire state trajectory, breaking the Markov assumption. To perform inference in this resulting non-Markovian model, the paper employs a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This approach is a notable departure from previous work in GP-SSMs, which often relies on parametric approximations of the GP or point estimates (e.g., MAP or EM), thereby circumventing the challenge of inferring the full posterior over the state trajectories.

The paper is technically excellent, with a clear and accessible presentation of a sophisticated inference scheme. Its primary novelty is the application of PGAS to enable online marginalization of the dynamics, a method that uniquely maintains the full nonparametric expressivity of the model. This is a significant advance for system identification. A major strength is the careful consideration of computational complexity; the authors not only identify the O(T^3) bottleneck but also provide practical solutions through efficient low-rank Cholesky updates and the integration of sparse GP approximations. The work is of high quality and is likely to be influential, stimulating further research on inference for nonparametric and non-Markovian dynamical systems.

The main weakness is the brevity of the empirical evaluation. While the experiments on the nonlinear benchmark and cart-pole system are sufficient to demonstrate the method's validity, they could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive assessment. In particular, the text preceding Table 1 and the table itself are unclear as to whether the reported RMSE is for in-sample reconstruction or out-of-sample prediction. I strongly recommend the authors clarify this and add a column for multi-step ahead forecast RMSE on a held-out dataset to better evaluate generalization. Furthermore, a discussion comparing the use of a parametric transition model *a priori* versus its use as the GP mean function would be insightful, particularly regarding implications for out-of-sample forecasting and model misspecification. A minor issue is the use of the abbreviation 'CPF-AS' in Algorithm 1 before its formal definition.

In summary, this is a strong and novel paper presenting a technically sound and important contribution. The weaknesses are minor and addressable. The proposed method is elegant, well-motivated, and computationally robust, and I recommend acceptance.
