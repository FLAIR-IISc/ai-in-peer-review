## Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper presents a novel Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models, leveraging Gaussian process priors over transition dynamics and a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method with ancestor sampling for efficient computation. The work addresses a challenging problem – fully Bayesian inference in non-Markovian systems – and proposes a technically sound solution. The authors effectively demonstrate how marginalizing over the transition dynamics enables learning without pre-committing to a specific parametric form. 

The paper builds upon existing work in GP-SSMs, notably distinguishing itself from approaches that rely on finite approximations to the GP (e.g., Turner et al., 2012) by fully marginalizing out the transition function. It also offers a critical component for methods requiring learned GP dynamics (Deisenroth et al., 2011, 2013), namely, a method for *learning* those dynamics in the first place. The technical contributions, particularly the online marginalization via ancestor sampling and the attention to computational efficiencies through sparsity and Cholesky updates, are significant. The presentation is remarkably clear and accessible, making a complex topic understandable. 

However, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited. While the results on the benchmark system demonstrate the method’s capability, expanding these experiments – perhaps with more complex datasets or comparisons to a wider range of baseline methods – would strengthen the claims. The discussion would benefit from a more in-depth comparison of using a parametric transition model *a priori* versus using it as the GP mean, as suggested by the authors themselves. This would clarify the implications for forecasting and out-of-sample performance. Furthermore, Table 1 currently lacks an out-of-sample/forecast RMSE column, hindering a direct assessment of predictive ability. The abbreviation CPF-AS is used before being fully defined, a minor stylistic issue.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The online marginalization of transition dynamics via ancestor sampling is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-grounded in Bayesian principles and leverages efficient computational techniques.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and explains complex concepts effectively.
*   **Potential Significance:** This work offers a powerful framework for inference in non-Markovian systems, with potential impact on various fields.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical results, while promising, could be more extensive.
*   **Missing Out-of-Sample Evaluation:** The lack of out-of-sample RMSE in Table 1 limits the assessment of generalization performance.



Overall, this is a strong, novel paper that is likely to stimulate discussion and further research in inference for non-Markovian state-space models. Despite the minor weaknesses in the empirical evaluation, the technical contributions and clarity of presentation justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
