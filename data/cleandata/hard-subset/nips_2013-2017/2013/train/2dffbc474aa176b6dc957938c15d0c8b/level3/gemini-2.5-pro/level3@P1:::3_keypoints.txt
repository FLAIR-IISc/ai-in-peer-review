- Paper proposes a fully Bayesian, nonparametric GP-SSM by placing a GP prior on the transition and marginalizing it to infer states and hyperparameters.  
- Marginalizing the transition induces a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories, enabling a true Bayesian treatment without parametric GP approximations.  
- Uses Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to jointly sample entire state trajectories and hyperparameters.  
- Recovers the posterior over the transition function as a mixture of GPs after sampling.  
- Introduces a sparse GP (FIC) variant to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T).  
- Strength: elegant, conceptually sound formulation preserving nonparametric Bayesian character.  
- Strength: appropriate use of modern inference tools (PMCMC / PGAS) for the non-Markovian latent model.  
- Strength: clear writing, organized derivations, and practical consideration of sparse approximation.  
- Weakness: limited algorithmic novelty—mainly a combination of existing GP-SSM and PGAS components.  
- Weakness: lacks critical evaluation of PMCMC practicalities and tuning challenges for good mixing.  
- Weakness: no empirical diagnostics for mixing (trace plots, ESS, autocorrelations) despite claiming “very good mixing.”  
- Weakness: uses N=20 particles without justification or sensitivity analysis on number of particles.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of computational limits and run times for varying T and M.  
- Weakness: experimental validation is limited—only low-dimensional synthetic benchmark and qualitative cart-pole results.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to one-step-ahead RMSE; missing longer-term forecasting and quantitative baselines.  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a principled, fully Bayesian approach that may inspire further work.  
- Argument for acceptance: offers a novel application of marginalized GP-SSM combined with PMCMC as an alternative to MAP/variational methods.  
- Argument against acceptance: methodological contribution mainly an application of existing methods rather than new inference techniques.  
- Argument against acceptance: omits critical analysis of sampler performance and scalability, undermining practical claims.  
- Argument against acceptance: weak experimental comparisons and benchmarks fail to demonstrate clear advantages over alternatives.  
- Recommendation: promising proof-of-concept but needs substantial revision—add detailed PMCMC performance analysis, sensitivity studies, runtime/complexity discussion, and stronger comparative experiments.
