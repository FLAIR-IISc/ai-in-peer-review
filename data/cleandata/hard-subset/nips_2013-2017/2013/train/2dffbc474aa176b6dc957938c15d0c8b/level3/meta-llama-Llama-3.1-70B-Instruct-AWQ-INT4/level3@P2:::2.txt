The paper "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC" presents a novel approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models using particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. The authors propose a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), which is a generalization of the standard parametric state-space model. The main contribution of the paper is the development of a tailored particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) algorithm for efficient inference in GP-SSMs.

The paper is well-written, clear, and interesting, and the application of particle MCMC to GP-SSMs is original and timely. The authors provide a thorough introduction to GP-SSMs and the challenges associated with inference and learning in these models. They also present a detailed description of the PGAS algorithm and its implementation.

One of the major strengths of the paper is the development of a sparse GP-SSM variant, which reduces the computational complexity of the algorithm. The authors also present experimental results on two benchmark systems, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

However, there are some omissions in the paper that need to be addressed. Firstly, the authors do not provide comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes, such as particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling. These comparisons are necessary to assess the relative performance of the proposed algorithm and would be straightforward to implement.

Secondly, the results presented in the paper lack informative diagnostics. The authors should provide graphs of performance metrics, such as the autocorrelation function (ACF) or the effective sample size (ESS), as functions of the particle count N and the time horizon T. These diagnostics would help to determine the scaling of the algorithm (linear vs sublinear) and provide insights into its performance.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to GP-SSMs and the challenges associated with inference and learning in these models. The presentation of the PGAS algorithm and its implementation is also clear and easy to follow.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel application of particle MCMC to GP-SSMs, which is a significant contribution to the field. The development of a sparse GP-SSM variant is also an original contribution.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of machine learning and signal processing, and the proposed approach has the potential to impact a wide range of applications.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with the condition that the authors address the omissions mentioned above and provide more informative diagnostics.
