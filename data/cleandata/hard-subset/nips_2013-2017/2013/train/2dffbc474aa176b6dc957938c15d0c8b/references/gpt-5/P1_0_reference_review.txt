Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) where the transition function f is given a Gaussian process (GP) prior and the observation model g is known (possibly parametric). The key idea is to fully respect the nonparametric nature of the GP by marginalizing f exactly, yielding a non-Markovian prior over the latent states x0:T. This collapsing removes strong posterior dependence between x and f but induces long-range temporal dependencies in x. The authors then perform Bayesian inference over states and hyperparameters via Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), to sample x0:T | θ, y and then θ | x0:T, y. Given sampled trajectories, the posterior over f and predictive distributions follow from standard GP regression formulas. They also present a sparse variant using the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T) and provide careful implementation details that reuse Cholesky factorizations and rank-1 updates for efficiency.

This work sits at the intersection of GP-based dynamical models and advanced SMC/MCMC methodology. Prior GP-SSM work often resorts to parametric or MAP approximations (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al. GPDM) or handles inference after learning the dynamics (Deisenroth et al.). PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.) provide the algorithmic backbone for sampling latent trajectories in non-Markov models; here, they are adapted to the collapsed GP-SSM where f is integrated out. The sparse GP treatment follows Snelson & Ghahramani and Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen (FIC), integrated into the state-space setting.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and elegance: Collapsing f to retain full nonparametric expressivity and then sampling x with PGAS is a clean solution to the strong x–f dependency problem.
- Sound use of modern inference: Appropriate deployment of PGAS for non-Markov latent structures, with correct ancestor-sampling weights based on the marginalized prior p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1).
- Practical contributions: Detailed complexity analysis and implementation strategies (Cholesky updates, caching) that make the approach viable; sparse GP (FIC) variant with O(M^2 T) cost.
- Flexibility: Handles general observation models (not necessarily Gaussian), and separates θx (GP/transition) from θy (measurement) with plausible sampling strategies (slice sampling for θx).
- Empirical demonstration on a multimodal 1D system and a 4D cart-pole, showing the method can learn dynamics and quantify uncertainty.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope and comparisons: Empirical section is modest. The 1D benchmark and the limited cart-pole example are informative but do not provide comprehensive comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., EM/RBF, GPDM, EP-based GPDS learning, SMC^2, Rao-Blackwellized variants). Reported RMSE table is not fully shown in the text; runtimes and wall-clock comparisons are absent.
- Sensitivity and diagnostics: No analysis of chain mixing (autocorrelation, ESS), sensitivity to particle number N, T, and hyperpriors. The confounding between process noise Q and GP flexibility is acknowledged but not empirically examined.
- Clarity gaps in experiments: Important implementation details (kernel choice, priors on θ, initialization, inducing input selection in FIC, number of inducing points beyond one setting) are not fully specified; figures/tables referenced but not reproduced in the text hinder assessment.
- Scalability limits: While complexity reductions are addressed, the per-iteration cost (O(T^3) or O(M^2 T) times N and number of PGAS iterations) may be substantial for long sequences; discussion of memory/computation trade-offs and parallelization is limited.

Quality
- The methodology is technically sound. The collapsed prior over trajectories (Eq. 3–4) follows from GP conditioning with process noise Q; the non-Gaussian nature of p(x1:t | x0) is correctly noted. The PGAS kernel invariance to p(x0:T | θ, y) is supported by prior work. The ancestor sampling weights using p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) are consistent with PGAS in non-Markov models, and the paper outlines how to compute these efficiently.
- However, claims about “very good mixing” and efficiency would be stronger with quantitative diagnostics and broader benchmarks. The sparse FIC derivation is standard but the practical recipe to truly achieve O(M^2 T) rests on careful caching; more empirical evidence would help.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and clear on the modeling and algorithmic ideas, with valuable implementation notes. The derivations are compact; some notation is heavy but standard for this audience.
- Suggestions: explicitly state kernel choices and prior distributions used in experiments; provide algorithm pseudo-code for CPF-AS with the non-Markov prior; include a small appendix (even as supplemental) detailing the rank-1 update/downdate scheme and stability considerations; show full tables/figures referenced.

Originality
- The combination of exact marginalization of f in a GP-SSM with PGAS for state inference appears novel and nontrivial. Prior works either parametrize or approximate f, or perform separate learning and inference. The sparse variant in this SSM context is also a meaningful addition. Related work is reasonably cited.

Significance
- Addresses a challenging and practical problem: learning flexible dynamics with uncertainty while performing smoothing without committing to a parametric transition model. The approach should interest both GP and SSM communities and could be a building block for control, RL, and time-series modeling.
- Greater empirical breadth would strengthen the case for widespread adoption.

Questions and suggestions to authors
- Provide quantitative mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS) versus N and T, and wall-clock times for vanilla vs. FIC.
- Compare against at least one strong learning baseline (e.g., EM with RBF, GPDM, EP-based GPDS learning) on the nonlinear benchmark and cart-pole, including multi-step predictive performance.
- Discuss identifiability between Q and GP amplitude; report posterior over Q; examine sensitivity to priors.
- Clarify kernel choice and hyperpriors; detail slice sampling settings; specify inducing input selection strategy and sensitivity to M and placement.
- Consider adaptive proposals for x (beyond one-step predictive) to reduce variance; discuss potential of backward simulation alternatives.

Reasons to accept
- Conceptually strong, principled Bayesian treatment that preserves nonparametric flexibility.
- Solid algorithmic integration of PGAS with collapsed GP-SSMs, plus sparse variant and concrete implementation guidance.
- Promising empirical results and applicability to real systems.

Reasons to reject
- Limited and partially reported experiments; lack of comparisons to strong baselines and performance diagnostics.
- Scalability and robustness not convincingly demonstrated; some practical details omitted.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The methodological contribution is compelling and timely, and the paper is technically solid and generally clear. Strengthening the experimental section with fuller results and comparisons would make this an easy accept.
