This paper presents a compelling approach to Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by employing Particle MCMC (PMCMC) to marginalize out the state transition function. This is a significant contribution as it avoids the parametric restrictions imposed by previous methods, offering a truly nonparametric approach. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and addresses a challenging problem with a sophisticated solution. It builds upon a rich literature in GP-SSMs (Deisenroth et al. [5,6], Turner et al. [2], Wang et al. [8]) and provides a crucial component for existing inference methods by enabling learning of the GP model itself. The use of recent advancements in PMCMC samplers [4] and the analytical marginalization of the transition dynamics are key strengths.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound. The derivation of the marginal prior and the application of PMCMC are well-explained. The efficient implementation details leveraging Cholesky factorization for computational speed are a strong point.
* **Novelty:** The approach of fully marginalizing out the state transition function in a GP-SSM is novel and overcomes limitations of prior work. The combination of particle methods with GP-SSMs in this way is a significant advance.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are intuitive. The inclusion of Figure 1b is helpful for visualizing the model.
* **Significance:** The ability to learn the dynamics of a system in a fully Bayesian, nonparametric fashion has broad implications for time series analysis, control, and robotics. The experiments demonstrate the method’s applicability to both a synthetic benchmark and a more complex cart-pole system.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors acknowledge limitations and potential improvements (e.g., thinning the Markov chain for computational efficiency). The inclusion of implementation details, like the FIC prior optimization, shows attention to practical considerations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address computational complexity with optimizations, the method remains computationally intensive, especially for high-dimensional state spaces or long time series. The scaling with *T* (even with *M* < *T* for sparse GPs) could be a barrier to wider adoption.  More detailed analysis of the computational cost for various settings would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of GP-SSMs is known to be sensitive to hyperparameter selection.  While the authors address this through Bayesian inference, further discussion of hyperparameter tuning strategies (beyond log-normal priors) and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The experiments are a good start, but a more comprehensive comparison to alternative GP-SSM inference methods (e.g., those based on Expectation Propagation [6]) would be valuable. Also, while comparisons to parametric models are made, these models are relatively simple.  A comparison to more sophisticated parametric state-space models might provide more insight.
* **Scalability of Sparse GP:** While the FIC prior is presented, the choice of 40 inducing points seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of the trade-off between inducing point count, accuracy, and computational cost would be helpful.  How does performance degrade as the state dimension increases, even with the sparse GP?
* **Algorithm 1 Clarity:** While generally well-written, Algorithm 1 could benefit from slightly more detailed comments explaining the purpose of each step, particularly for readers unfamiliar with PGAS.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in probabilistic modeling.
* The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in both synthetic and real-world scenarios.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of time series analysis and dynamical systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost remains a significant concern, limiting scalability.
* The comparative analysis could be more comprehensive.
* Further investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity is needed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated. While the computational cost and comparative analysis could be improved, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I believe this paper is **accept**, with the suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above in a revision. Specifically, I recommend including a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability, a more comprehensive comparative analysis, and a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity.  Including a brief discussion of how the choice of *M* impacts performance (e.g., a small ablation study) would also be valuable.
