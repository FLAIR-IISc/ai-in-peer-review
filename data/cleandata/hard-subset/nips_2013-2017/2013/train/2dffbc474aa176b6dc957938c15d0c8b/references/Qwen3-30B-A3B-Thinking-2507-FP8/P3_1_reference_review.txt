

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the problem of fully Bayesian learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), where the state transition function is modeled nonparametrically using a Gaussian process prior. The key innovation is marginalizing out the state transition function f from the model, which allows for fully nonparametric inference while still enabling efficient sampling from the smoothing distribution p(x₀:T | y₀:T, u₀:T). The authors tackle the resulting non-Markovian state process using a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm, which they show provides efficient mixing and accurate inference. The paper also introduces a sparse GP implementation using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to reduce computational complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T), where M ≪ T.

This work builds on prior literature in GP-SSMs (e.g., Deisenroth et al. [5, 6], Turner et al. [2]) but addresses a key gap: previous approaches typically use parametric representations of f or require fixed models for the dynamics, whereas this paper provides a fully Bayesian approach that maintains the nonparametric expressivity of the GP model. The paper differs from Wang et al. [8] who use MAP estimation, and from Turner et al. [2] who apply EM to learn parametric GP models.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper presents a novel approach to marginalizing out the nonparametric state transition function f, which is a significant methodological advance. The derivation of the marginal prior (Equation 4) is elegant and well-justified, showing how the state process becomes non-Markovian but still tractable with SMC methods.

2. **Algorithmic Innovation**: The adaptation of PGAS for non-Markovian latent processes is well-executed and clearly explained. The authors provide a detailed algorithm (Algorithm 1) and carefully explain the ancestor sampling mechanism (Section 3.3.1), which is crucial for the efficiency of their approach.

3. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper makes a strong contribution to the computational scalability of GP-SSMs by introducing the sparse FIC prior implementation. The complexity analysis (O(T³) for vanilla GP, O(M²T) for sparse GP) is thorough and well-supported by the implementation details in Section 4.2.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments demonstrate the method's ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions (Figure 2), which is a significant advantage over parametric approaches that would fail in such cases. The cart-pole example (Figure 3) effectively shows how the model captures uncertainty in different regions of the state space.

5. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical derivations. The motivation for each step is well-explained, and the relation to previous work is appropriately contextualized.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper provides computational complexity results, it lacks theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the PGAS sampler in this non-Markovian setting. It's mentioned that the kernel is ergodic, but the paper doesn't establish rates of convergence or provide any theoretical bounds on the mixing time, which would be valuable for understanding the practical limitations of the method.

2. **Limited Experimental Comparison**: The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. The comparison in Table 1 only includes two parametric models and the ground truth, but doesn't compare against other Bayesian nonparametric approaches to SSMs (e.g., [16, 21]). The paper could have better demonstrated the advantage of their fully Bayesian approach versus approximate Bayesian methods.

3. **Sparse GP Implementation Details**: The paper mentions using greedy methods for inducing point selection but doesn't evaluate how this choice affects performance. The comparison between vanilla and sparse GP (Table 1) shows minimal difference, but doesn't explore how the number of inducing points affects the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost.

4. **Scalability Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach for very long time series (T >> 1000) or high-dimensional state spaces. While they claim O(T³) complexity, this can become prohibitively expensive for large T, and the paper doesn't discuss potential extensions to handle such cases.

5. **Lack of Discussion on Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The experiments use log-normal priors for hyperparameters, but the paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to the choice of prior. This is particularly important for the GP prior, where hyperparameter choice can significantly affect performance.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical derivations are correct, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the computational complexity) and compare their approach to relevant baselines. However, the lack of theoretical guarantees for the PGAS sampler and limited experimental comparison slightly reduce the strength of the claims.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is clearly written with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The key contributions are well-articulated, and the relation to previous work is appropriately contextualized. The main weakness in clarity is in the more technical derivations (Section 3.1), which might be challenging for readers without strong GP background. The authors could improve by adding a brief intuitive explanation of the marginal prior before diving into the mathematical details.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a fully Bayesian approach to learning GP-SSMs that maintains the nonparametric expressivity of the model. While the use of PMCMC for state-space models is not entirely novel, their adaptation to handle the marginalization of the nonparametric dynamics function is a clear advance. The sparse GP implementation using FIC is a good extension but not entirely novel (building on [16, 17]). The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly Turner et al. [2] and Wang et al. [8], who use parametric or MAP approaches.

## Significance Assessment

This work addresses an important problem in time series modeling: learning flexible, nonparametric dynamics models in a fully Bayesian framework. The ability to capture multimodal smoothing distributions (as demonstrated in Figure 2) is a significant practical advantage over parametric approaches. The computational efficiency improvements make the approach applicable to realistic problems. The results could be useful to researchers and practitioners working with time series data where dynamics are complex and uncertain.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian time series modeling. The marginalization approach to handle nonparametric dynamics is elegant and well-executed, and the PGAS adaptation for non-Markovian processes is a nice technical contribution. The computational efficiency improvements through sparse GP make the approach more practical. The paper is well-written and well-motivated.

The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical guarantees for the sampler's convergence properties and the limited experimental comparison with other Bayesian approaches. However, these do not significantly undermine the technical contribution of the paper.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian time series modeling with clear technical advances and good experimental validation. While there are some limitations in the theoretical analysis and experimental comparison, these are outweighed by the novelty and quality of the methodological contribution.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs that maintains nonparametric expressivity
- Effective solution to the non-Markovian state process problem via tailored PGAS
- Good computational complexity analysis with efficient implementation details
- Demonstrated capability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions
- Well-written and clear presentation of technical content

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of the PGAS sampler's convergence properties
- Experimental comparison could be more comprehensive (lacks comparison to other Bayesian nonparametric approaches)
- Sparse GP implementation details could be more thoroughly evaluated
- Limited discussion of scalability limitations for very long time series

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief discussion of the convergence properties of the PGAS sampler in the non-Markovian setting, even if only as a limitation.

2. Expand the experimental section to include a comparison with at least one other Bayesian nonparametric approach to SSMs (e.g., [16, 21]).

3. Include a sensitivity analysis of the inducing point selection method for the sparse GP implementation.

4. Discuss the limitations of the approach for very long time series (T > 10,000) and potential future directions for addressing these limitations.

5. Consider adding a brief intuitive explanation of the marginal prior (Equation 4) before the formal derivation to aid reader comprehension.

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian time series modeling and will likely be of interest to researchers working on nonparametric dynamics learning and state-space models.
