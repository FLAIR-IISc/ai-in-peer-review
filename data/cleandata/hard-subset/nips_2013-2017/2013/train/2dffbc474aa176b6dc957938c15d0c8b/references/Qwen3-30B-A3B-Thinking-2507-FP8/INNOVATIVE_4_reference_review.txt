

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper presents a novel approach to learning nonlinear state-space models using Gaussian process priors, with the key innovation being the marginalization of the state transition function f from the model, enabling a fully Bayesian treatment of the GP-SSM. Previous work on GP-SSMs (cited as [2, 5, 6, 8]) typically resorts to parametric approximations of the GP or fixes the dynamics to a specific functional form before learning, which contradicts the nonparametric nature of the approach. The authors instead marginalize out f, resulting in a non-Markovian state process that they efficiently handle using Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), a recent PMCMC method. This represents a fundamental shift from conventional approaches, as it allows for Bayesian inference over the dynamics without requiring a fixed model of the transition function.

The paper's main contribution lies in its elegant solution to the challenging problem of learning GP-SSMs in a fully Bayesian manner. By marginalizing f, the authors avoid the common pitfall of parametric approximations while maintaining the nonparametric expressivity of the GP. The marginalization introduces dependencies across time, but the authors cleverly leverage recent advances in PMCMC to efficiently sample from the resulting smoothing distribution. The paper also provides a sparse GP-SSM implementation using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior, making the approach computationally feasible for larger datasets.

The theoretical development is sound, with clear derivations of the marginal prior over the state trajectory (equations 3-4) and the CPF-AS algorithm. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, showing how the naive implementation can be optimized to O(T³) for the vanilla GP and O(M²T) for the sparse variant. The experimental results on both a synthetic nonlinear system and a cart-pole system demonstrate the approach's ability to capture multimodal smoothing distributions and learn complex dynamics, even when the mean function is misspecified (Figure 2).

**Strengths:**
- **High novelty**: The marginalization approach represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work, which has largely relied on parametric approximations of the GP dynamics.
- **Theoretical soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous derivation of the marginalization procedure and the PGAS sampler, showing how it retains the nonparametric expressivity of GP-SSMs.
- **Practical implementation**: The sparse GP-SSM construction using FIC makes the approach computationally feasible for real-world applications.
- **Strong experimental validation**: The results on both synthetic and physical systems demonstrate the approach's ability to handle multimodal smoothing and learn complex dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental comparison against state-of-the-art methods is somewhat limited. While the paper compares against parametric models and the ground truth, it would be stronger to include a comparison with recent GP-SSM approaches that use different inference techniques.
- The sparse implementation details could be more thorough. The paper mentions the computational complexity reduction to O(M²T) but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how the intermediate matrix-vector multiplications are efficiently reused.
- The paper doesn't fully explore the limits of the approach. For example, it would be valuable to understand how the method scales with very long time series (T > 1000) or high-dimensional state spaces.

Despite these minor weaknesses, the paper's core contribution is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental problem in state-space modeling. The marginalization approach is a bold and elegant solution that fundamentally changes how we think about learning GP-SSMs. The authors have successfully demonstrated that it's possible to maintain the full nonparametric expressivity of the model while performing efficient Bayesian inference.

The paper advances the state of the art in GP-SSM learning by providing a method that doesn't require fixing the dynamics to a specific parametric form before inference. This is a significant step forward from previous work that has struggled with the tension between model flexibility and computational tractability. The approach could have broad applications in robotics, control, and time series analysis where accurate modeling of complex nonlinear dynamics is crucial.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field. The marginalization technique is a creative solution to a long-standing problem, and the authors have provided a theoretically sound and practically implementable method. While the experimental section could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons, the core idea is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Pro:**
- Fundamentally new approach to learning GP-SSMs by marginalizing out the state transition function
- Retains full nonparametric expressivity of the model
- Elegant solution to a challenging inference problem using PGAS
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear derivations
- Demonstrated on meaningful benchmark problems

**Con:**
- Limited comparison against recent state-of-the-art methods
- Sparse implementation details could be more thorough
- Limited exploration of the method's scalability limits

This paper represents a significant step forward in Bayesian learning of nonlinear state-space models and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. The core idea is novel, well-executed, and has clear potential for broad impact.
