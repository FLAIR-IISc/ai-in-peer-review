## Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to learning nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) by combining Gaussian Processes (GPs) for modeling the dynamics with a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method for efficient Bayesian inference. The key contribution lies in *marginalizing out* the state transition function, enabling a fully nonparametric approach that avoids the limitations of parametric GP-SSM methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method with both a synthetic benchmark and a cart-pole control problem, showcasing its ability to learn complex dynamics and provide uncertainty quantification.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on GP-SSMs. It clearly distinguishes its approach from methods relying on finite approximations of the GP (e.g., [2, 7]) and MAP estimation [8], highlighting the benefits of full Bayesian inference and marginalization. The connection to existing PMCMC techniques [3] and recent advances in particle filtering [4] is also well-articulated. The authors accurately acknowledge relevant works like Deisenroth et al. [5, 6] on filtering and smoothing with learned GP dynamics, framing their work as a crucial component for *learning* the GP dynamics needed for those methods. The reference to Wang et al. [8] and its potential for overfitting in high-dimensional state spaces is a particularly insightful point.

**Strengths:**

* **Principled Nonparametric Approach:** The core idea of marginalizing out the state transition function is a significant strength. This allows for a truly flexible model that adapts complexity without the limitations of fixed functional forms. 
* **Efficient Inference:** The tailored PMCMC algorithm, leveraging recent advancements in particle sampling [4], appears to be effective in tackling the complex inference problem. The reported good mixing is encouraging.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The derivation of the marginal prior and the explanation of the inference steps are clear and mathematically sound. The connection to Gaussian process regression is well-explained.
* **Practical Demonstration:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on a challenging benchmark and a realistic control problem. The visualisations in Figure 2 effectively illustrate the multimodal nature of the smoothing distribution and the ability to capture complex dynamics. The results in Table 1 are compelling, demonstrating performance comparable to (and in some cases exceeding) existing methods.
* **Implementation Details:** The discussion of computational complexity and the sparse GP implementation (FIC prior) in Section 4 show a strong awareness of practical considerations. The strategies for efficient computation (reusing Cholesky factorizations) are valuable.
* **Uncertainty Quantification:** The cart-pole example illustrates the valuable capability of the method to provide uncertainty estimates, crucial for applications like reinforcement learning and control.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address computational complexity, the PMCMC method remains computationally demanding, particularly for long time series or high-dimensional state spaces. The practical scalability of the approach beyond the demonstrated examples is a potential concern.  A more detailed discussion of the computational cost for different problem sizes would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper doesn’t detail the hyperparameter tuning process (e.g., priors used, optimization strategies). The sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters should be discussed.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison in the benchmark experiment is limited. While the authors compare against several baselines, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art GP-SSM methods (e.g., those based on Expectation Propagation [6]) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **FIC Prior Selection:**  The choice of the FIC prior with 40 inducing points seems somewhat arbitrary.  A more systematic exploration of different sparse GP approximations and a justification for the chosen FIC configuration would be beneficial.
* **Clarity on Algorithm 1:** The description of Algorithm 1 is relatively concise. While the references to [4] help, a slightly more detailed explanation of the steps within the algorithm itself would improve readability.




**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem (learning nonlinear SSMs).
* The method offers a significant advantage over existing approaches by avoiding the limitations of parametric GP-SSMs.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and potential of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost remains a significant concern.
* The limited comparison with other state-of-the-art methods weakens the evaluation.
* The hyperparameter tuning process is not adequately discussed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and learning in dynamical systems. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically well-grounded, and demonstrated to be effective in practice. While the computational cost and limited comparison are potential weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is well-suited for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Rating:** 8.5/10

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the method.
* Expand the comparison with other state-of-the-art GP-SSM methods.
* Elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process and sensitivity analysis.
* Provide a more detailed explanation of the steps in Algorithm 1.
* Justify the choice of the FIC prior and explore alternative sparse GP approximations.
