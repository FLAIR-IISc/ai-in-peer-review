

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach for learning Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function and using a tailored particle MCMC (PMCMC) algorithm for inference. The authors address the challenge of learning nonlinear SSMs without parametric assumptions on the dynamics, using a GP prior for the transition function and a particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) algorithm for inference. The paper makes a valuable contribution by enabling nonparametric modeling of state transitions while maintaining computational tractability.

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of marginalizing the GP dynamics to enable fully Bayesian inference is well-motivated and addresses a clear gap in the literature.
- The authors provide a detailed theoretical derivation of the marginal prior over state trajectories, which is technically sound.
- The sparse GP extension (FIC prior) is well-motivated and the computational complexity analysis is thorough.
- The experimental results on synthetic and physical systems demonstrate the method's ability to capture multimodal smoothing distributions and complex dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Notational inconsistencies and typos:** The paper contains multiple formatting issues that undermine technical precision. Equation (3a) has a typographical error ("T∏" should be "∏_{t=1}^T"). Equation (3b) uses inconsistent spacing in "K̃ −1 0:t−2" and the paper alternates between "x0:T" and "x0:T−1" without clear justification. The notation "N ( xt | µt(x0:t−1),Σt(x0:t−1) )" should follow standard formatting as "N(x_t | μ_t(x_0:t-1), Σ_t(x_0:t-1))".

2. **Technical oversight in Equation (3):** The paper claims "p(x1:T | θ,x0) = T∏ t=1 p(xt | θ,x0:t−1)" but this would only hold for Markov processes, which the model is not due to the GP dynamics. The authors should clarify that this factorization is approximate due to the non-Markovian nature of the marginal state prior.

3. **Insufficient empirical evidence:** The claim "we report very good mixing" lacks quantitative evidence. No mixing diagnostics (e.g., autocorrelation plots, effective sample size) are provided to support this assertion. The paper mentions "very good mixing enabled by the use of recently developed PMCMC samplers [4]" but fails to compare with alternative samplers.

4. **Incomplete explanation of computational complexity:** The paper states "the computational complexity involved in computing the above mean and covariance is O(M^2t)" but provides no derivation or justification for this claim, making it difficult to verify.

5. **Missing experimental details:** The paper references "Table 1" but fails to include it, preventing evaluation of the quantitative results. The claim "the smoothing density p(x0:T | y0:T) is multimodal" is not substantiated with evidence beyond Figure 2, which is not provided in the submission.

6. **Inconsistent treatment of hyperparameters:** In Section 3.3.2, the paper assumes independence between θx and θy ("p(θ | x0:T,y0:T) ∝ p(θy|x0:T,y0:T)p(θx|x0:T)") but does not justify this assumption or discuss potential consequences of violating it.

7. **Citation issues:** The seminal PMCMC paper by Andrieu et al. [3] is cited but not properly introduced as the foundational work on the method. The reference to "Gaussian Processes for Machine Learning" lacks the publication year, which is standard practice.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach to Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies, lack of quantitative evidence for key claims, and incomplete experimental reporting significantly undermine the paper's quality. These issues are particularly problematic for a NIPS submission, where technical precision and reproducibility are paramount.

The paper's main contribution is sound, but the execution falls short in critical areas. The authors have addressed a meaningful problem, but the presentation and supporting evidence are insufficient for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend rejection due to these technical shortcomings, which overshadow the paper's conceptual contributions.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** Strong theoretical foundation, novel application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs, and promising experimental results.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** Multiple notational inconsistencies, lack of quantitative evidence for key claims, incomplete experimental details, and insufficient technical justification for computational complexity claims.
