

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper presents a novel approach to fully Bayesian inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function f. The authors develop a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to efficiently sample from the smoothing distribution while marginalizing over the nonparametric transition dynamics. This contrasts with existing approaches that typically require parametric approximations of f.

The work builds on the rich literature of GP-SSMs, including Deisenroth et al.'s filtering/smoothing methods for learned GP dynamics [5,6] and Turner et al.'s EM-based parametric GP learning [2]. The authors correctly identify that their approach provides a crucial component for learning the GP model itself, which previous methods assumed were already learned. The paper also relates to the broader literature on nonparametric state-space modeling, including Wang et al.'s MAP-based learning [8] and Ghahramani and Roweis's EM approach for radial basis functions [7].

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound methodology with clear theoretical foundations. The marginalization of the transition function is well-motivated and the resulting inference problem is properly addressed. The derivation of the marginal prior (equations 3-4) is rigorous, and the PGAS algorithm is well-explained with a clear algorithm description (Algorithm 1). The experiments demonstrate practical utility on both synthetic and real-world systems, with clear comparisons to baselines. The sparse GP-SSM construction using FIC prior [17] is well-integrated and provides a significant computational advantage.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured. The mathematical derivations are clear and well-explained, making the complex inference procedure accessible. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented in a way that effectively demonstrates the method's capabilities.

**Originality:** The core contribution of marginalizing out the transition function f while maintaining the nonparametric nature of the model is genuinely novel. Previous approaches (e.g., [2,8]) require parametric representations of f, whereas this work enables fully Bayesian inference in the nonparametric setting. The tailored PGAS algorithm for this non-Markovian inference problem represents a significant methodological advance.

**Significance:** The approach addresses a fundamental limitation in existing GP-SSM methods, enabling truly nonparametric modeling of state transitions without restrictive parametric assumptions. The ability to learn the dynamics while maintaining full Bayesian treatment of uncertainty is particularly valuable for applications where uncertainty quantification is critical. The successful application to the cart-pole system demonstrates practical utility in a real-world RL context.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a clear algorithmic description, it lacks a thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the PGAS algorithm in this specific context. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the theoretical guarantees (e.g., ergodicity, mixing rates) of the proposed sampler.

**Comparison with Baselines:** The experimental comparison (Table 1) focuses primarily on RMSE, which is a common metric but insufficient for a Bayesian method. The paper should include a more comprehensive comparison of uncertainty quantification (e.g., coverage rates, predictive variance) to demonstrate the Bayesian advantages of the approach. The sparse GP-SSM comparison should also include a computational time analysis to better demonstrate the tradeoff between accuracy and efficiency.

**Scalability:** The computational complexity (O(T^3) for vanilla GP, O(M^2T) for sparse GP) may limit applicability to very long time series. The paper mentions that M << T for the sparse implementation, but it would be helpful to include more discussion about the practical limits of the approach in terms of time series length.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a theoretical section analyzing the convergence properties of the PGAS algorithm for this specific non-Markovian model.

2. Extend the experimental section to include uncertainty quantification metrics (e.g., coverage rates, predictive variance) in addition to RMSE, to better demonstrate the Bayesian advantages of the approach.

3. Include a computational time analysis comparing the vanilla GP-SSM, sparse GP-SSM, and baseline methods to better illustrate the computational tradeoffs.

4. Consider adding a discussion of the sensitivity of the method to the choice of inducing points in the sparse GP-SSM, as this is a crucial practical consideration.

5. Provide more detailed implementation details in Section 4.2 to aid reproducibility, particularly regarding the Cholesky factorization updates.

## Recommendation

This is a technically sound and well-written paper that makes a genuine contribution to the field of Bayesian state-space modeling. The marginalization approach to GP-SSM learning is novel and addresses a significant limitation in existing methods. The paper is well-motivated, the methodology is clearly presented, and the experiments demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in theoretical analysis and experimental comparison, these are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's significant contributions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical and experimental gaps identified above.

The work represents an important step forward in Bayesian nonparametric modeling of dynamical systems, and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. The paper's clear writing and solid technical foundation make it particularly suitable for the NIPS audience.
