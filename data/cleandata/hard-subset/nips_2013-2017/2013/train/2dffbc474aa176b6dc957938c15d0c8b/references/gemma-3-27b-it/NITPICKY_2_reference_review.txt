This paper presents a Bayesian nonparametric approach to learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) via Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC). The core idea – marginalizing out the state transition function to retain full nonparametric flexibility – is a valuable contribution, addressing a key limitation of existing GP-SSM methods. The authors rightly point out the trade-offs with parametric approaches, and the reliance on a known observation model is a reasonable simplification. However, while the conceptual contributions are strong, the execution and presentation suffer from numerous, often minor, but cumulatively significant flaws.

The paper's strength lies in its theoretical grounding. The derivation of the marginal prior (Equation 4) and the subsequent adaptation of PMCMC for this setting are well-motivated. The connection to existing work (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al., Wang et al.) is correctly positioned, and the explanation of how this work complements prior efforts is clear. The introduction of the sparse GP variant (Section 4.1) is also a valuable addition, addressing a practical computational concern.

However, the devil is in the details, and these details are, unfortunately, often lacking or poorly explained. First, the notation is inconsistent and occasionally confusing. For example, `ft` in Equation (2a) is used without clear definition, and the conditioning on inputs `ut` is inconsistently handled throughout. The use of boldface for vectors is not always adhered to. While seemingly minor, these inconsistencies impede comprehension. 

Second, the implementation details (Section 4.2) are presented at a very high level. Claims of computational efficiency (O(T^3) or O(M^2T)) are made without sufficient justification. While the authors mention reusing Cholesky factorizations, the specific steps involved are glossed over, leaving the reader uncertain about the practical feasibility of these optimizations. The reference to “rank one updates and downdates” is insufficient; a more detailed algorithmic sketch would be beneficial.

Third, the experimental section (Section 6) feels underdeveloped. The “Nonlinear System Benchmark” is a good starting point, but the presentation of results is superficial. Table 1 presents RMSE values without providing confidence intervals or standard deviations, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the improvements. The choice of hyperparameter settings is not discussed, nor is the sensitivity of the results to these choices analyzed. The claim of "very good mixing" in the PMCMC sampler (Introduction) is not substantiated with mixing diagnostics (e.g., trace plots, autocorrelation plots).

Fourth, the paper occasionally suffers from imprecise language. Statements like "This contrasts with conventional approaches…" are used frequently without specific citations to support the contrast. This weakens the argument and leaves the reader wondering exactly what is being contrasted.

Fifth, the discussion of the FIC prior lacks sufficient detail. While the equation (7) is provided, the implications for the covariance function and the practical considerations for choosing inducing points are not thoroughly addressed. The claim that the FIC prior "sometimes outperforms" the non-sparse version is weak; a more detailed comparison, including computational costs and accuracy metrics, would be more persuasive.

Finally, several minor typographical errors and grammatical mistakes are present throughout the paper, detracting from its overall polish.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to GP-SSM learning via marginalization.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and derivation of key equations.
*   Introduction of a computationally efficient sparse GP variant.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Inconsistent and occasionally confusing notation.
*   Insufficient detail in the implementation section.
*   Underdeveloped experimental evaluation with lacking statistical rigor.
*   Imprecise language and unsubstantiated claims.
*   Numerous minor typographical and grammatical errors. 
*   Overall lack of polish hinders comprehension.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the underlying ideas presented in this paper are promising, the numerous flaws in execution and presentation raise serious concerns. The paper *feels* incomplete. The lack of detailed implementation explanations, thorough experimental validation, and careful attention to notation and language detract significantly from its impact. Although the theoretical contributions are noteworthy, I believe these are outweighed by the shortcomings. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. A major revision, addressing the issues outlined above, would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper needs significantly more detailed explanations, a more rigorous experimental evaluation, and a careful review for consistency and clarity.
