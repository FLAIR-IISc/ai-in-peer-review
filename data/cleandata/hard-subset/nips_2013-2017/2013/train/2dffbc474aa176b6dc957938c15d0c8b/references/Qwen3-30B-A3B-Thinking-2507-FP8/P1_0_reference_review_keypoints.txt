- Paper presents a fully Bayesian GP-SSM that models the state transition function nonparametrically and uses PGAS to marginalize the GP for inference.  
- Derives the marginal prior for the state trajectory enabling non-Markovian inference.  
- Implements Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) tailored to this GP-SSM.  
- Proposes a sparse GP-SSM using the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^4) to O(M^2 T).  
- Empirical validation on synthetic multimodal smoothing and a cart-and-pole system shows ability to learn complex dynamics and robustness to mean-function misspecification.  
- Technically novel and sound: marginalizing the transition GP and using PGAS differs fundamentally from prior parametric/finite-target approaches.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: clear derivations of marginal prior, CPF-AS algorithm, sparse construction, and complexity analysis.  
- Experiments are well-designed and effectively demonstrate key capabilities.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized with helpful figures and logical derivations.  
- Sparse implementation provides a practical solution to computational challenges for longer time series.  
- Lacks comparison with other nonparametric dynamics-learning methods (e.g., probabilistic neural network approaches).  
- Scalability concerns remain: limited experiments (T=200, M=40) leave performance on much longer series unclear.  
- Insufficient exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the sparse GP-SSM.  
- Limited discussion of theoretical convergence guarantees of PGAS in the non-Markovian setting.  
- Suggested improvements: add comparisons to other nonparametric methods, perform scalability and sensitivity analyses, clarify PGAS convergence guarantees, and discuss extensions to unknown observation functions.  
- Acceptance pros: addresses a fundamental problem, fully Bayesian nonparametric solution, practical sparse implementation, strong empirical results, and clear presentation.  
- Acceptance cons: limited benchmark comparisons, unanswered scalability questions, and insufficient theoretical convergence discussion.  
- Final recommendation: accept — significant, technically sound contribution with some addressable weaknesses.
