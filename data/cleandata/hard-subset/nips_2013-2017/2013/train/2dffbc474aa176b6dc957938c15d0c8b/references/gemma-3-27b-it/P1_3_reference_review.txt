## Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to learning and inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by fully marginalizing out the state transition function *f*. This is a significant departure from existing methods which often rely on parametric approximations of *f*. The authors achieve this through a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, leveraging recent advances in PMCMC sampling techniques and demonstrating excellent mixing.  The method allows for a fully Bayesian treatment, providing a posterior distribution over all latent entities including the function *f* itself, the state trajectory, and hyperparameters.  The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach on both a synthetic nonlinear system and a cart-pole control problem.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors correctly situate their work within the existing landscape of GP-SSMs. They acknowledge and contrast their approach with methods like those of Deisenroth et al. [5,6] focusing on filtering/smoothing *after* learning *f*, and Turner et al. [2] utilizing EM for parametric GP-SSM estimation. The connection to Ghahramani & Roweis [7] and Wang et al. [8] is also well drawn, highlighting the benefit of avoiding MAP estimation and the potential for overfitting in high-dimensional state spaces. The core contribution is to move beyond parametric approximations for the transition function, a limitation highlighted in previous work.

**Strengths:**

* **Nonparametric Flexibility:** The key strength is the ability to perform fully Bayesian inference without imposing strong parametric assumptions on the state transition dynamics. This allows the model to adaptively increase complexity with more data.
* **Principled Bayesian Treatment:** The full marginalization of *f* is a significant theoretical contribution. This leads to a more complete and robust uncertainty quantification compared to methods relying on point estimates.
* **Efficient Inference:** The tailored PMCMC algorithm, utilizing recent advances like [4], appears to be efficient and exhibits good mixing – a common challenge in these types of models. The clever reuse of computations in the implementation is also commendable.
* **Demonstrated Empirical Performance:**  The experiments, especially the demonstration on the cart-pole system, show that the method can learn complex dynamics and provide useful predictive distributions, even with limited data.  The demonstration of capturing multi-modality in the synthetic data is also a strength.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are presented in a logical manner. The mathematical derivations are largely accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address computational efficiency with sparse GPs, the overall cost, even with optimization, remains a concern.  A more detailed discussion of scalability to higher-dimensional state spaces and longer time series would be helpful. Though O(M^2 T) is better than O(T^3) for large T, the memory requirements for M can still be substantial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance is likely sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters (kernel parameters, prior distributions etc.). While the paper mentions priors, a more comprehensive discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis is needed.
* **FIC Prior Choice:** The justification for choosing the FIC prior specifically is somewhat limited. A brief comparison with other sparse GP approximations might be beneficial.
* **Limited Baselines:**  The comparison with other state-of-the-art GP-SSM methods (especially those that attempt to learn *f* in a Bayesian manner, even if parametrically) feels limited. A stronger comparison with methods like those in [5, 6] with different learning schemes would solidify the contribution.
* **Implementation Details:** While implementation details are discussed, providing code or more specific details regarding the implementation of the PMCMC sampler could enhance reproducibility.



**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Represents a significant theoretical advance in GP-SSM inference.
* Provides a principled and flexible approach to modeling nonlinear dynamical systems.
* Empirically demonstrates strong performance on challenging benchmarks.
* Offers a well-written and clear presentation of complex ideas.

**Con:**

* Computational cost remains a potential barrier to wider adoption.
* Limited comparison to alternative learning schemes for *f*.
* Further discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness is needed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of GP-SSMs. The authors address a key limitation of previous approaches by providing a principled method for fully Bayesian inference with nonparametric dynamics. The theoretical justification is solid, the implementation seems well-optimized, and the empirical results are promising. While the computational cost and limited baseline comparisons are valid concerns, they do not overshadow the novelty and potential impact of the work. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  **Scalability Discussion:**  Expand the discussion of computational scalability, particularly for higher-dimensional state spaces and longer time series.
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Include a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and a discussion of strategies for tuning them.
3. **Baseline Comparison:**  Add a more thorough comparison with other recent GP-SSM learning approaches.
4. **Code Availability (Optional):** Consider releasing code to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
