

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

## Summary
This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach for learning nonlinear state-space models using Gaussian processes for the dynamics and Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) for inference. The key contribution is marginalizing out the state transition function to maintain the nonparametric nature of the model, enabling efficient sampling from the smoothing distribution. The authors introduce a tailored PGAS sampler and explore sparse GP approximations to improve computational efficiency. The work builds on previous GP-SSM literature (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al.) but differs by maintaining full nonparametric expressivity rather than resorting to parametric approximations.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a significant gap in GP-SSM literature by providing a fully Bayesian approach that maintains nonparametric expressivity rather than approximating the GP as parametric.
2. The marginalization approach is theoretically sound and the PGAS sampler is well-motivated for the non-Markovian nature of the marginalized state process.
3. The sparse GP implementation using FIC prior is well-explained and provides a practical computational improvement.
4. The experiments demonstrate the model's ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions and capture complex dynamics in both synthetic and real-world systems.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Notational Issues
1. **Equation (4) contains a critical notational error**: The expression should be $p(x_{1:t} | \theta, x_0) = |(2\pi)^{n_x t} \tilde{K}_{0:t-1}|^{-1/2} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}(x_{1:t} - m_{0:t-1})^T \tilde{K}_{0:t-1}^{-1}(x_{1:t} - m_{0:t-1})\right)$. The current version has $|(2\pi)^{n_x t} \tilde{K}_{0:t-1}|^{-1/2}$ which incorrectly places the $2\pi$ factor inside the determinant. This is a fundamental error in the probability density expression.

2. **Equation (6) contains a mathematical inconsistency**: The equality $p(\{x_{i0:t-1}, \tilde{x}_{t:T}\}, y_{0:T}) / p(x_{i0:t-1}, y_{0:t-1}) = p(\tilde{x}_{t:T} | x_{i0:t-1})$ is incorrect. The correct expression is $p(\{x_{i0:t-1}, \tilde{x}_{t:T}\}, y_{0:T}) / p(x_{i0:t-1}, y_{0:t-1}) = p(\tilde{x}_{t:T} | x_{i0:t-1}, y_{0:T})$, which is not equivalent to $p(\tilde{x}_{t:T} | x_{i0:t-1})$ without the conditional independence assumption (which is not explicitly stated).

3. **Computational complexity claims are misleading**: Section 4.2 states "all the operations at a specific time step can be done in O(T^2) computations, leading to a total computational complexity of O(T^3)". This is incorrect. Per time step, the complexity is O(T^2), but the total complexity for all T time steps is O(T^3). The phrasing suggests O(T^2) per time step is cumulative, which is not the case.

4. **Sparse GP complexity claim is inaccurate**: The paper claims "a naive implementation will give rise to O(M^2T^2) computational complexity. This can be reduced to O(M^2T) by keeping track of a factorization for the matrix P." The correct complexity for sparse GP with FIC is O(M^2T) for the entire algorithm, not per time step.

### Implementation and Experimental Details
1. **Lack of MCMC convergence details**: The experiments use "50 iterations" of PGAS without mentioning burn-in period or convergence diagnostics, which is critical for MCMC-based methods. The paper should report on convergence assessment (e.g., effective sample size, trace plots).

2. **Insufficient experimental setup**: In Section 6.1, the authors state "log-normal priors for the hyper-parameters" but don't specify the hyperparameters of these priors. For a Bayesian method, this is crucial information for reproducibility.

3. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses both "x0:T" and "x0:T-1" interchangeably in the model definition, causing confusion about the indexing of the state trajectory.

### Minor but Significant Issues
1. **Title inconsistency**: The title uses "MCMC" but the text consistently uses "MCMC" (with capital M and C). The title should match the consistent usage in the paper.

2. **Citation issues**: The reference to "Deisenroth et al. [5,6]" should cite specific papers (e.g., "Deisenroth et al. (2011) for filtering/smoothing, Deisenroth et al. (2012) for GP-SSM"). The current citation format is too vague.

3. **Theoretical gap**: The claim "Our only approximation is that of the sampling algorithm" is misleading. The marginalization of f is an exact operation, but the smoothing distribution approximation via PMCMC is indeed the only approximation. However, the marginalization itself is not an approximation - the paper should clarify this distinction.

## Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach to GP-SSM learning, with a clear contribution to maintaining the nonparametric nature of the model. However, the paper contains several critical technical errors (particularly in equations 4 and 6) and misleading claims about computational complexity that undermine the technical rigor of the work. The lack of details on MCMC convergence and prior specification also raises concerns about reproducibility.

While the core idea is sound and the experiments demonstrate reasonable performance, the technical errors and inconsistencies are substantial enough to warrant rejection. A revised version addressing these issues would be suitable for consideration, but as currently written, the paper does not meet the technical standards expected at NIPS.

The paper's significance is limited by its incremental nature (building on existing PMCMC and GP-SSM work) and the presence of fundamental technical errors that affect the paper's credibility. The strengths of the approach are overshadowed by these technical shortcomings.
