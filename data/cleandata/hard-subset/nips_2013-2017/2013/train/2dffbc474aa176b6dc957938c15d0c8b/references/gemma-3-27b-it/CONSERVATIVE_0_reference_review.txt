This paper presents a Bayesian approach to learning and inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) to marginalize out the state transition function. While the premise of achieving full Bayesian inference in this setting is appealing, and builds upon a rich history of GP-SSMs [5, 6, 2], I find the paper lacking in substantial novelty and rigorous evaluation, particularly given the complexity of the proposed method. 

The core idea – marginalizing out the GP dynamics – isn’t entirely new; the authors correctly point to the challenges others have faced in learning GP dynamics alongside the states [2, 8]. However, simply stating that one has *marginalized* out the function doesn't automatically constitute a significant advancement. The authors highlight the benefit of avoiding parametric representations, yet their implementation utilizes a sparse GP (Section 4.1), effectively reintroducing a parametric component via the inducing points. The advantage over existing sparse GP-SSM methods isn’t clearly articulated.

My primary concern lies with the experimental validation. The “Nonlinear System Benchmark” (Section 6.1) presents results that are difficult to interpret.  Table 1 compares the proposed method against a range of baselines, but the description of these baselines is insufficient.  What constitutes the "true model" and the "linear model"? Without clear details (e.g., parameter settings, training procedures), the RMSE values are difficult to assess.  The claim that the GP-SSM "accurately portrays the underlying system" based on Figure 2 is subjective. The visual inspection of state trajectories, while helpful, isn't a substitute for quantitative comparison.  Moreover, the impact of the chosen prior (a parametric form mirroring the true system, albeit with different parameters) is not adequately discussed – this seems to heavily influence the result. The cart-pole example (Section 6.2) is similarly light on quantitative evaluation.

The paper’s methodological contribution – the efficient PMCMC implementation using CPF-AS and the optimizations related to the sparse GP – is presented in substantial detail (Sections 3.3 and 4.2). This is a strength. However, the computational complexity analysis feels somewhat optimistic; the practical benefits of these optimizations, specifically in a high-dimensional state space, are not demonstrated. The implementation details, though thorough, do not address concerns about the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter settings, particularly those governing the PMCMC sampler.

The clarity of the writing is generally good, but the paper suffers from a degree of self-reference. The constant emphasis on how this work "contrasts with conventional approaches" feels repetitive and doesn't always justify the claims being made.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging problem – full Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs – and proposes a technically sound approach based on PMCMC.
*   The detailed description of the implementation and optimizations is valuable.
*   The method demonstrates the ability to learn dynamics even with a misspecified parametric prior.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of marginalizing out the GP dynamics is questionable, particularly given the use of sparse GPs.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method over existing approaches. The baselines lack sufficient detail for meaningful comparison.
*   The computational advantages of the proposed optimizations aren’t convincingly demonstrated.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter settings.



**Overall Assessment:** I lean towards **reject**. While the paper presents a technically competent approach, it doesn't offer a substantial advance over existing methods and lacks the rigorous experimental validation necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The claims of improved performance and flexibility are not sufficiently supported by evidence. The paper feels more like a detailed implementation study than a significant scientific contribution. A major revision with a more comprehensive and rigorous experimental evaluation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art GP-SSM methods, is required before reconsideration.
