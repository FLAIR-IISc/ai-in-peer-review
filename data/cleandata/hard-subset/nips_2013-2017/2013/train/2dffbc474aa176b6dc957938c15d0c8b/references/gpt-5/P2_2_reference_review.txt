Summary
This paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and smoothing in nonlinear Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior over the unknown transition function f, analytically marginalize f (collapsing), and then sample the latent state trajectory x0:T and hyperparameters θ using particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). Collapsing avoids strong posterior couplings between f and x and yields a non-Markovian prior over trajectories that can still be handled by PGAS. Given samples of x0:T and θ, posterior inference over f at test points reduces to standard GP regression, yielding a mixture of Gaussians predictive distribution. The paper also develops an efficient implementation and a sparse variant based on the FIC approximation, reducing complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). Experiments on a nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a cart-pole system demonstrate feasibility.

Relation to prior work
- GP dynamical systems have been explored via MAP/EM with parametric GP surrogates or pseudo-inputs (e.g., GPDM; Wang et al. 2005; Turner et al. 2010) and via approximate filtering/smoothing given learned GP dynamics (Deisenroth et al.). This work differs by maintaining a nonparametric prior over f throughout, marginalizing f exactly, and using PMCMC/PGAS [Andrieu et al.; Lindsten et al.] to sample x under the induced non-Markovian prior. The use of ancestor sampling is well-motivated for the non-Markovian setting.
- The FIC sparse GP prior [Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen] is adapted to the GP-SSM trajectory prior and combined with efficient rank-1 updates, which is a practical contribution.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: collapsing f in a GP-SSM and using PGAS to target p(x0:T | y0:T, θ) addresses strong x–f dependence cleanly and preserves nonparametric expressivity.
- Sound algorithmic design: correct use of PGAS kernels that leave the smoothing distribution invariant; sensible hyperparameter sampling via GP marginal likelihood with slice sampling; thorough discussion of complexity and efficient Cholesky updates; extension to FIC with clear computational benefits.
- Practical value: mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution over f captures multimodality; sparse variant broadens applicability.
- Experiments illustrate the framework’s ability to handle multimodal smoothing and to quantify epistemic uncertainty in regions away from data.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical evaluation: only one synthetic benchmark (T=200, N=20, 50 PGAS iterations) and one real system with 100 observations. No runtime, ESS, or autocorrelation diagnostics; no sensitivity to N, T, or hyperparameters; no comparison to strong baselines such as EM/MAP GPDM, variational GP-SSMs, or PMCMC without collapsing f.
- Metrics are narrow: mainly RMSE of f(xt, ut); no predictive log-likelihood, long-horizon rollout accuracy, or calibration metrics; cart-pole results are qualitative one-step predictions without baselines.
- Some technical details are deferred: the proposal p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t) used in SMC is described but not explicitly derived when the likelihood is non-Gaussian; reproducibility would benefit from pseudocode and clarifications on numerical stabilization.
- Inducing input selection is external; interaction between inducing set, mixing, and bias is not analyzed. The claim that sparse can sometimes outperform non-sparse is plausible but unsubstantiated in this setting.
- Scalability and high-dimensional states: complexity and memory scaling with nx are not fully discussed; the approach may struggle as nx and T grow, and the assumption of known g limits generality.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The collapsing derivation for p(x1:T | x0, θ) via GP one-step conditionals is correct, and the observation that the resulting prior is non-Gaussian because inputs are the states themselves is accurate. The PGAS kernel invariance and ancestor sampling weights based on the trajectory prior are consistent with [Lindsten et al.]. The complexity analysis and use of rank-1 updates are reasonable. However, the empirical section is too light to substantiate claims of “very good mixing” and efficiency; quantitative diagnostics and comparisons are needed.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable. The distinction between the Gaussian-looking density in (4) and its non-Gaussian nature is helpful. The CPF-AS description is concise but may be terse for readers unfamiliar with non-Markovian latent variable PGAS; a short pseudocode or schematic would help. Some notational density in Section 4 (e.g., indices and block structures) could be eased with a figure. References appear incomplete in formatting; figures and table details are not fully visible in the text.

Originality
The combination of fully collapsed GP-SSM inference over x with PGAS is, to my knowledge, novel relative to EM/MAP and variational approaches and to filtering/smoothing with learned GPs. Leveraging FIC within this trajectory-prior perspective and detailing efficient updates is a practical innovation rather than a theoretical one. The contribution is a new and principled inference scheme for GP-SSMs.

Significance
Potentially significant for researchers working on Bayesian nonlinear state-space models, providing a blueprint to do fully Bayesian inference without parametric surrogates for f. If empirically validated at larger scale and against strong baselines, it could influence practice. Current experiments, however, limit the strength of the empirical case.

Questions and suggestions
- Please report mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS) and wall-clock time vs. T, N, and M. How does performance degrade with state dimension?
- Compare against EM/MAP GPDM and a variational GP-SSM baseline, both in accuracy and runtime.
- Clarify the proposal used in (5): when p(yt | xt, θy) is non-Gaussian, do you default to the bootstrap proposal? Any auxiliary/adapted proposals tried?
- Analyze sensitivity to mean function choice and to inducing input selection; include ablations on M and active set strategies.
- Provide predictive log-likelihoods and multi-step rollout accuracy and calibration.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Principled, elegant inference method that preserves nonparametric modeling.
- Solid use of PMCMC/PGAS for a challenging non-Markovian prior.
- Useful sparse variant and implementation insights.

Cons:
- Empirical validation is limited; lacks strong baselines, diagnostics, and scalability evidence.
- Some practical details (proposals, robustness, inducing set selection) underexplored.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The methodological contribution is clear and potentially impactful, but the paper would be substantially strengthened by more comprehensive and comparative experiments, mixing/runtime diagnostics, and clarifications noted above.
