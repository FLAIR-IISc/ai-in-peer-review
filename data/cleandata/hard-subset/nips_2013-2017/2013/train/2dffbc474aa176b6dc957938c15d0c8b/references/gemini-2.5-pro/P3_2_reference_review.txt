### **Summary**

This paper presents a fully Bayesian, nonparametric approach for learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The core problem is to infer the latent state trajectory, model hyperparameters, and the unknown nonlinear state transition dynamics from observation data. The central contribution is to marginalize out the unknown state transition function, `f`, rather than approximating it parametrically or seeking a point estimate. This marginalization respects the nonparametric nature of the GP prior but introduces long-range temporal dependencies, breaking the Markov property of the state process.

The authors tackle this challenging inference problem by employing a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This allows for efficient joint sampling from the posterior distribution of the latent state trajectory and the model hyperparameters. The transition function `f` can then be recovered analytically a posteriori from the samples of states and hyperparameters. The paper also addresses the cubic computational complexity inherent in GPs by proposing a sparse variant using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) approximation, reducing complexity to O(M²T). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic nonlinear system with a multimodal smoothing posterior and on the classic cart-pole control problem.

This work builds upon the rich literature of GP-SSMs but distinguishes itself from prior work. Unlike methods that require a pre-learned model for filtering/smoothing (e.g., Deisenroth et al.), this paper focuses on learning the model itself. In contrast to EM-based or MAP-based approaches (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.), which can underestimate uncertainty and lead to overfitting, this work provides a fully Bayesian treatment, capturing a posterior distribution over all latent variables. The use of advanced PMCMC techniques for this specific model structure is a novel and powerful combination.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Principled Bayesian Framework:** The marginalization (collapsing) of the dynamics function `f` is the main strength of this paper. It is an elegant and theoretically sound way to handle the infinite-dimensional nature of the GP, avoiding the pitfalls of MAP estimation or parametric approximations. This allows the model to properly account for uncertainty in the dynamics when inferring the state trajectory.

2.  **Sophisticated Inference Methodology:** The application of Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) is well-motivated and perfectly suited for the resulting non-Markovian model. This is a state-of-the-art inference technique, and its use here to enable the full marginalization of `f` is a significant contribution. The paper clearly explains how PGAS works in this context, including the crucial ancestor sampling step which improves MCMC mixing.

3.  **Demonstrated Ability to Handle Uncertainty:** The experiment in Section 6.1 is excellent. It clearly shows the model's ability to capture the bimodal posterior over the latent states, a scenario where methods based on Gaussian approximations or point estimates would fail. This provides strong empirical evidence for the value of the proposed fully Bayesian approach.

4.  **Practicality and Scalability:** The authors proactively address the O(T³) computational complexity of the standard GP formulation by incorporating a sparse GP (FIC) model. This makes the approach viable for longer time series and demonstrates a strong grasp of the practicalities of GP modeling. The discussion of efficient implementation via Cholesky updates further strengthens the paper's practical relevance.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** While the experiments are illustrative, they lack a direct comparison to other contemporary GP-SSM learning methods. The baseline models in Table 1 (parametric models) are arguably weak competitors. It would be significantly more convincing to compare against modern variational inference (VI) based GP-SSM methods, which also aim to learn the dynamics and are often more scalable. This would help position the contribution of this MCMC-based approach in terms of accuracy versus computational cost.

2.  **Understated Difficulty of Hyperparameter Sampling:** Section 3.3.2 suggests that sampling the GP hyperparameters `θx` is "straightforward" with slice sampling once the trajectory is given. However, the posterior landscape for GP hyperparameters can be highly multimodal and exhibit strong correlations, making MCMC sampling notoriously slow to mix. The paper does not provide any diagnostics on the mixing of the hyperparameter chains (e.g., trace plots, effective sample size), which is a crucial aspect of the overall algorithm's performance.

3.  **Inducing Point Selection for Sparse GP:** The paper introduces the sparse FIC model but explicitly states, "We do not address the problem of choosing the inducing inputs." This is a critical detail. The performance of sparse GPs is highly sensitive to the placement of inducing points. Were they placed on a grid, selected from the data, or optimized? This lack of detail makes the sparse results difficult to interpret and reproduce. A brief discussion of the strategy used and its potential impact would be beneficial.

4.  **Clarity of Implementation Details:** While Section 4.2 provides a high-level overview of the efficient implementation, the description is quite dense. For instance, the claim that all operations can be done in O(T²) per time step via rank-2nx updates on the Cholesky factor is non-trivial and could benefit from a clearer, perhaps more algorithmic, explanation or a reference to a specific algorithm for this update.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the marginalized prior (Eq. 4) is correct, and the application of PGAS is appropriate. The claims about the method's ability to handle multimodality are well-supported by the experiment in 6.1. The work is a complete and valuable contribution.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, and the progression from the model definition to the inference algorithm is easy to follow. Figure 2 is particularly effective. However, as noted above, some implementation and hyperparameter sampling details could be clarified.

*   **Originality:** The paper's originality lies in the combination of ideas. While GP-SSMs and PMCMC are not new in themselves, the use of PMCMC to perform fully Bayesian inference in a GP-SSM *after* marginalizing the dynamics function is novel and powerful. This provides a new and compelling alternative to existing VI or MAP-based methods for this model class.

*   **Significance:** The work is significant. It presents a robust and principled method for a fundamental problem: learning nonlinear dynamics from data. The ability to obtain a full posterior over trajectories without first committing to a point estimate of the dynamics is a key advantage for applications where uncertainty quantification is critical (e.g., robotics, finance, control). Researchers are likely to build upon this work, perhaps by exploring more scalable MCMC variants or by applying the framework to more complex problems. It advances the state of the art in Bayesian inference for dynamical systems.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a strong methodological contribution. Its main weakness is a somewhat limited experimental comparison, but its theoretical soundness and the novelty of its approach make it a clear candidate for acceptance.
