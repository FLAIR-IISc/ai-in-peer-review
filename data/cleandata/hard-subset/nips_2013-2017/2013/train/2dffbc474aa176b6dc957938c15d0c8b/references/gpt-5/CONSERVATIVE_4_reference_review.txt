Summary
The paper studies fully Bayesian learning and inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior on the unknown transition function f, marginalize f analytically to obtain a non-Markovian prior over trajectories x0:T, and then perform posterior inference over states and hyperparameters using Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). The authors give expressions for the collapsed trajectory prior, discuss SMC/PGAS adaptations to non-Markovian latent processes, present implementation details (Cholesky updates), and propose a sparse alternative using the FIC GP to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). Experiments include a 1D nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole example with qualitative one-step predictions.

Relation to prior work
There is extensive prior work on GP-based dynamical systems: GPDM (Wang et al.), EM-based parametric GP transition models (Turner et al.), and approximate inference for learned GP dynamics (Deisenroth et al., EP and moment-matching). Particle MCMC is a mature tool (Andrieu et al.), and PGAS is well-established (Lindsten et al.). The paper’s main difference from earlier GP-SSM work is the exact marginalization of the transition function within a fully Bayesian state/hyperparameter inference scheme using PGAS, plus implementation for both full and sparse GPs. Conceptually, the collapsed formulation for GP-SSMs is natural; the main novelty lies in combining it with PGAS and engineering an efficient implementation.

Strengths
- Clear Bayesian formulation that respects the nonparametric GP prior by integrating out f.
- Uses established, principled inference machinery (PGAS) that is appropriate for non-Markovian latent processes; ancestor sampling is a good fit here.
- Derivation of the collapsed trajectory prior and use of Cholesky updates are technically sound and practically useful.
- Sparse FIC variant reduces cost to O(M^2 T) and keeps the method nonparametric.
- The approach can handle non-Gaussian observation models and multimodal smoothing distributions; the benchmark illustrates this.
- The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive representation is expressive; discussion of reducing it to a single GP for downstream use is pragmatic.

Weaknesses and concerns
Empirical validation
- The experiments are limited in scope and scale. The 1D toy benchmark (T=200) and a qualitative 4D cart-pole example (100 points) are not sufficient to demonstrate robustness or competitiveness.
- Baselines are weak. Comparing to two parametric models and a sparse variant is not enough; missing are GPDM, variational GP-SSMs, EM-based GP transition models, and standard SSM baselines (EKF/UKF/particle smoothers with parametric f), especially for predictive accuracy and smoothing quality.
- No quantitative mixing/efficiency diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, acceptance rates) for PGAS or hyperparameter sampling; “very good mixing” is asserted without evidence.
- No runtime or scaling study versus T, state dimension nx, or number of particles N; no analysis of the impact of the sparse approximation (M) on accuracy/speed.

Methodological clarity
- The proposal in the SMC step is stated as sampling from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t). For general observation models this conditional is not tractable; the paper does not explain how this is implemented (e.g., prior proposal with importance weighting, local Laplace/unscented proposals, or MH-within-SMC). This is a crucial missing detail affecting correctness and performance.
- Hyperparameter sampling: while slice sampling is mentioned, the parameterization, priors, blocking strategy (especially Q), and computational tricks used to compute the marginal likelihood and its updates are not specified. No diagnostics on mixing or sensitivity to priors.
- Inducing input selection for FIC is left open. Given that M critically affects performance, a concrete strategy (and ablation) is needed.
- The collapsed prior derivations are correct but terse; reproducibility would benefit from more explicit algorithmic details (kernel choice, mean function options, initialization, resampling scheme, ancestor sampling weight computation in practice).

Assumptions and scope
- The approach assumes a known observation model g (up to finite-dimensional parameters). Many practical problems have unknown g, and the method’s performance under model mismatch is not assessed.
- Scalability to higher-dimensional states is unclear. The complexity and numerical stability with nx > 5–10, and the effect on PGAS mixing, are not evaluated.

Quality
Technically competent and grounded in established methods. However, key implementation choices (proposals, hyperparameter sampling) are under-specified, and the empirical section does not rigorously support the performance claims.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with useful intuition. Important algorithmic details are missing (proposal distribution, practical computation of ancestor weights, priors/blocks for θ), hindering reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Collapsing the GP transition and using PGAS for the resulting non-Markovian prior is a reasonable synthesis rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic idea. The sparse variant and engineering details are incremental but useful.

Significance
Potentially useful for Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs, but the present experimental evidence is insufficient to establish a clear advantage over strong baselines or to demonstrate robustness and scalability.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely how do you sample xt in (5)? If you use the optimal proposal, how is it computed for generic p(yt | xt)? If not, what is the proposal, and how does it affect variance?
- What kernels and mean functions were used in experiments? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- How are inducing inputs selected, and how does performance vary with M?
- Provide mixing diagnostics for PGAS and θ updates, and runtimes versus T, N, nx, and M.
- Can you report multi-step predictive performance and compare to GPDM/variational GP-SSMs and parametric SSM baselines?

Pros
- Conceptually clean, fully Bayesian treatment with principled inference.
- Handles multimodal smoothing; sparse variant included; solid implementation ideas.

Cons
- Limited and weak empirical validation; missing strong baselines and diagnostics.
- Key algorithmic details under-specified; unclear proposal for SMC step.
- Unclear scalability and robustness; assumptions on known g narrow applicability.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is promising and methodologically grounded, but it falls short on empirical rigor and essential implementation detail. Strengthening the experimental section with strong baselines, scalability studies, and mixing/runtimes, and clarifying the proposal/hyperparameter sampling, would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
