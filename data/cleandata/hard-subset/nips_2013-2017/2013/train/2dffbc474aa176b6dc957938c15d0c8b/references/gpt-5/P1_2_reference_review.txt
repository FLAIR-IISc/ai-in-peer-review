Summary
This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to learning and inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior on the unknown nonlinear transition function f in xt+1 = f(xt, ut) + vt, assume a known observation model yt | xt, and marginalize f analytically. This yields a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x1:T | θ) expressible via a sequence of GP one-step conditionals. The authors then perform joint smoothing and hyperparameter inference using Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), which is well-suited for non-Markovian latent variable models. They detail efficient implementations: (i) reusing Cholesky factorizations to reduce vanilla GP complexity from naive O(T4) to O(T3); and (ii) a sparse GP construction (FIC) that yields O(M2 T) complexity with inducing variables M ≪ T. After sampling the state trajectories and hyperparameters, the posterior over f at test points is recovered as a mixture of GP predictors conditioned on the sampled trajectories. Experiments on a standard nonlinear SSM benchmark and a 4D cart-and-pole system show good smoothing quality and competitive predictive performance, with calibrated uncertainty.

Relation to previous work
The paper connects to several lines of work: (i) GP models for dynamics and smoothing (Deisenroth et al.) typically assume learned GP models and focus on approximate filtering/smoothing given f; (ii) GP-SSM learning via EM/MAP with parametric surrogates or pseudo-observations (Turner et al., Wang et al.) sacrifices full nonparametric treatment and can overfit; (iii) PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.) provide MCMC kernels for state inference in complex latent models. The novelty here is to collapse the (infinite-dimensional) transition function exactly under the GP prior and then perform fully Bayesian inference over states and hyperparameters with PGAS, preserving nonparametric expressivity while addressing the induced non-Markovianity. The sparse FIC prior follows Quinonero-Candela & Rasmussen/Snelson & Ghahramani.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Exact marginalization of f in a GP-SSM and use of PGAS to sample from the smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) in a non-Markovian setting is elegant and principled.
- Practical algorithms: Careful attention to computational cost (rank-1 Cholesky updates, ancestor-sampling weights computation, FIC speedups) brings the method into a usable regime.
- Bayesian learning of f: Post hoc recovery of p(f* | x*, y) as a mixture of Gaussians is well-motivated and captures multimodality from state uncertainty.
- Experiments: The nonlinear benchmark highlights multimodal smoothing and robustness to a misspecified mean function; cart-pole shows calibrated uncertainty and plausible one-step predictions.
- Clarity of the modeling choice: Keeping g known addresses identifiability issues and mirrors practical sensor modeling.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited experimental comparison: The benchmark compares mainly to parametric baselines and a sparse variant. Please compare against prior GP-SSM learning approaches (e.g., EM-based GPDM/GPSSM variants, variational methods if applicable) and strong nonparametric baselines (e.g., NARX) on prediction and smoothing.
- Diagnostics and ablations: Only 50 PGAS iterations with N = 20 particles are used; provide mixing diagnostics (ESS/ACF, between-chain variance), sensitivity to N, trajectory length T, and inducing points M. Report runtime scaling empirically vs. theory (O(T3) and O(M2 T)).
- Identifiability and priors: There is potential non-identifiability between process noise Q and GP output scale. Please discuss priors/regularization used (you mention log-normal priors) and show posteriors (e.g., amplitude vs. Q) or constraints that mitigate confounding.
- Proposal distribution in SMC: Eq. (5) uses p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t). If this is the “optimal” proposal proportional to p(yt | xt) p(xt | θ, x0:t−1), please clarify how it is computed in the non-Markovian prior and observation model, and whether closed forms are available for the cases considered. If instead the prior proposal p(xt | θ, x0:t−1) is used, correct the notation.
- Multi-output structure: f maps Rnx to Rnx. Clarify whether outputs are modeled independently or with a multi-output GP; this affects covariance structure, complexity, and performance in higher-dimensional systems.
- Inducing inputs: For FIC, inducing inputs are fixed. Provide a selection strategy, sensitivity analysis, or a discussion on learning them (or jointly sampling/optimizing per MCMC iteration) and how that affects posterior uncertainty.
- Observation model scope: While fixing g aids identifiability, many applications have unknown g. A brief discussion on extending to partially unknown g (e.g., parametric g with priors) and the impact on inference would broaden the paper’s reach.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the collapsed prior over trajectories, PGAS kernel invariance, and complexity arguments are standard and appropriately adapted. Some derivations (e.g., efficient computation of ancestor weights via (4)) are sketched; an appendix with explicit formulas and numerics (stability of downdates, handling near-singular K̃, jitter) would improve reproducibility. Experimental evidence is positive but somewhat limited in scope and baselines; more comprehensive comparisons and diagnostics would strengthen claims.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. Notational conventions around K-submatrices are clear, though dense. Minor issues: clarify Eq. (5) (proposal), define nx early, and specify whether Q is diagonal per dimension. A brief pseudo-code or schematic for CPF-AS within PGAS would help readers unfamiliar with [4]. Consider adding a small illustrative example for computing w̃t−1|T.

Originality
The combination of exact marginalization of f with PGAS for GP-SSMs is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. The sparse GP-SSM variant is a principled alternative prior rather than an approximation to the same model, which is a useful perspective.

Significance
Addressing fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs is important. The approach is general, leverages modern PMCMC theory, and could impact practice in time-series modeling and control where model uncertainty matters. With stronger empirical comparisons and scaling evidence, this could be a reference method.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, elegant treatment of GP-SSMs with exact marginalization of f
- Solid inference machinery (PGAS) adapted to non-Markovian priors
- Attention to computational efficiency (Cholesky updates, FIC)
- Demonstrated handling of multimodal smoothing and calibrated prediction

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to prior GP-SSM learning methods and broader baselines
- Limited diagnostics and sensitivity analyses
- Some clarity gaps (proposal distribution, multi-output structure)

Questions for authors
1) Do you model output dimensions of f independently? If so, any loss observed relative to multi-output kernels? 2) How sensitive is performance to the choice of mean function? 3) Can you provide mixing diagnostics and wall-clock scaling? 4) How are inducing inputs chosen; do results vary substantially with different selections?

Minor comments
- Cite related variational GP-SSM/GPDM work if available and compare qualitatively. 
- Include numerical stabilization details (jitter, damping during downdates).
- Consider HMC/NUTS for θx sampling; slice sampling can be robust but slow in highly correlated hyperparameters.

Overall recommendation
A strong, technically sound contribution with clear novelty and potential impact. I recommend acceptance after addressing the empirical comparison and clarity points above.
