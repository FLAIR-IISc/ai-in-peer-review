Summary
The paper tackles learning in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) where the transition function f is modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP), while the observation model g is known (possibly with finite-dimensional parameters). The core idea is to marginalize the latent transition function f analytically, which induces a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories, and then perform fully Bayesian inference over the latent state trajectory x0:T and hyperparameters θ using particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). This collapsing alleviates strong posterior coupling between x and f and avoids parametric approximations to f used in much prior work. Hyperparameters are sampled with slice sampling, leveraging the GP marginal likelihood conditional on a proposed state trajectory. To address computational cost, the authors provide (i) an O(T^3) implementation with careful Cholesky rank-1 updates/downdates, and (ii) a sparse variant using the FIC prior with complexity O(M^2 T). After posterior sampling of x and θ, prediction for f at test points is obtained by Monte Carlo mixtures of the GP predictive distributions, or optionally by fitting a single sparse GP to the posterior sample “cloud.” Experiments on a challenging 1D nonlinear benchmark (multimodal smoothing) and a 4D cart-pole system demonstrate accurate smoothing and competitive one-step dynamics prediction, with uncertainty quantification.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well situated with respect to GP dynamical models: GPDM (Wang et al., 2005) and Turner/Deisenroth’s EM and EP-based methods typically introduce finite parameterizations/MAP steps and optimize latent trajectories. Deisenroth et al. have addressed filtering/smoothing for a learned GP model but not full Bayesian learning of the GP SSM itself. The proposed approach differs by (i) fully marginalizing f to retain a genuinely nonparametric model, and (ii) using PMCMC—specifically PGAS—to sample the smoothing distribution under the induced non-Markov prior, addressing path degeneracy. The sparse FIC construction follows standard sparse GP methodology but is thoughtfully integrated into the SSM setting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The marginalization of f leading to p(x1:T|x0,θ) as a product of GP conditionals is correct and clearly justified. The CPF-AS/PGAS machinery is appropriate for the non-Markov latent model; the ancestor sampling weights are derived using the collapsed trajectory prior. The hyperparameter sampling via GP marginal likelihood given x is standard and reasonable.
- Algorithmic details: The O(T^3) and O(M^2 T) implementations with careful reuse of Cholesky factorizations and rank-1 updates/downdates are plausible, though numerically delicate; the paper gives sufficient high-level guidance but would benefit from more explicit pseudocode or stability discussion.
- Empirical support: The 1D benchmark convincingly showcases multi-modality handling and predictive accuracy; the cart-pole results qualitatively illustrate calibrated uncertainty. However, quantitative comparisons are limited: there is no head-to-head against EM/MAP-based GP-SSM learners (e.g., GPDM, Turner et al.), no runtime/complexity scaling plots, and limited ablations (e.g., effect of N, M, kernel choice, mean function misspecification). Claims about “very good mixing” are plausible with PGAS but not quantified (ESS, autocorrelation, acceptance).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The derivations for the collapsed prior and the ancestor sampling weights are clear. Notation is heavy but mostly consistent; K̃(·), block indices, and the concatenated-trajectory covariance could be explained with a small illustrative example. Implementation Section 4.2 is dense and could be clearer with a short algorithmic schematic. Experimental setup details (priors, slice sampling settings, kernel hyperpriors) could be expanded for reproducibility.

Originality
The combination of collapsing the GP transition function with PGAS for state and hyperparameter inference in GP-SSMs appears novel and addresses known limitations of parametric/MAP approaches. The sparse FIC extension is an expected but useful addition. The main novelty resides in the exact marginalization of f within a PMCMC framework for SSM learning.

Significance
This is a meaningful advance for Bayesian learning in GP-based dynamical systems: it provides a principled path to sample latent trajectories under a nonparametric prior over dynamics, alleviating bias from parametric approximations and reducing posterior coupling. The approach is likely to be adopted by researchers working on probabilistic time-series, control, and RL, and complements existing GP filtering/smoothing work by learning the model itself. Practical significance is somewhat tempered by computational cost and the assumption of a known observation model, but the sparse variant and implementation guidance mitigate this.

Strengths
- Fully Bayesian treatment that retains the nonparametric GP prior by marginalizing f.
- Elegant use of PGAS to address non-Markov smoothing with good expected mixing.
- Solid implementation insights (Cholesky updates; FIC integration) and complexity analysis.
- Clear handling of multimodal smoothing and principled uncertainty quantification.
- Thoughtful discussion of using informative mean functions and identifiability considerations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited experimental breadth: add comparisons to GPDM/EM/MAP learners on the benchmark and cart-pole; report state RMSE/NLL, mixing diagnostics, and runtimes vs N, T, M.
- Scalability: discuss memory/time scaling in higher-dimensional states; report behavior for longer T where path degeneracy is severe even with PGAS.
- Observation model restriction: demonstrate learning unknown θy or simple parametric g to illustrate generality; discuss potential extensions to GP observation models with identifiability constraints.
- Sparse model: inducing input selection is treated as fixed; discuss selection strategies and their interplay with posterior uncertainty and θ updates.
- Numerical stability: comment on downdate stability and jitter strategies; clarify kernel/likelihood priors used.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of mean function? Do mis-specified mean functions slow mixing or bias θ estimates?
- What are typical autocorrelation times for x and θ under PGAS for different N? Any guidance on choosing N?
- How does the method perform when both f and g are unknown and modeled with GPs (with constraints to avoid non-identifiability)?
- Can you provide ablations on M and inducing input placement for FIC?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a technically sound and novel approach that advances Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs. While experiments could be more comprehensive and some implementation details expanded, the contribution is significant and likely to impact subsequent work.
