This paper presents a compelling, fully Bayesian approach to learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) via Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC). The core idea – marginalizing out the state transition function *f* to retain the nonparametric nature of the GP – is genuinely novel and addresses a significant limitation of existing GP-SSM literature which typically relies on parametric approximations. The combination of PMCMC with recent advances in particle sampling techniques appears effective, enabling accurate smoothing even without a pre-defined transition model. The authors correctly identify the connection to prior work and clearly articulate how their method builds upon it, particularly referencing the need for their approach to enable effective learning for filtering/smoothing methods like those in Deisenroth et al. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The marginalization of *f* is a significant contribution, allowing for a truly nonparametric GP-SSM. This is a bold approach that sets it apart from much of the existing literature.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the marginal prior and the use of PMCMC are presented with sufficient detail to suggest technical correctness. The connections to existing SMC and MCMC methods are clearly explained.
*   **Potential Significance:** The ability to learn a flexible, nonparametric SSM has broad implications for time series modeling, control, and reinforcement learning. The demonstration on the cart-pole system highlights this potential.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from introduction to experimental validation. The authors do a good job of explaining complex concepts.
*   **Practical Implementation:** The discussion of computational optimizations (sparse GPs, efficient implementation details) demonstrates an awareness of practical concerns.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Validation:** While the experiments demonstrate the method's functionality, they feel somewhat limited. The “nonlinear system benchmark” is a good start, but more diverse and challenging datasets would strengthen the evaluation. Specifically, the RMSE improvement isn't dramatically reported, requiring readers to scrutinize the table to understand the gain.
*   **Scalability:** The computational complexity, even with optimizations, remains a concern. While the authors address it, the practical scalability to very high-dimensional state spaces or long time series isn't fully explored. A more detailed discussion of scalability limitations would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of hyperparameter choices (e.g., the number of particles, the choice of inducing points in the sparse GP) on the performance is not thoroughly investigated. A sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Sparse GP Selection:** The use of 40 inducing points in the FIC prior seems arbitrary. A justification for this choice or a discussion of how the number of inducing points impacts performance would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to GP-SSM learning.
*   The theoretical foundation is solid, and the proposed PMCMC algorithm appears well-designed.
*   The experimental results, while limited, are encouraging and demonstrate the feasibility of the method.
*   The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature and clearly articulate the contributions of their work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is not as extensive as it could be.
*   The scalability of the method is not fully addressed.
*   The sensitivity to hyperparameters is not thoroughly investigated.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and potential significance of the work outweigh the limitations. The authors have tackled a challenging problem with a creative and technically sound approach. While further experimental validation and a more detailed discussion of scalability would be desirable, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field and warrants publication at NIPS. The approach of marginalizing *f* is a conceptually important step forward, and I expect this work to inspire further research in nonparametric SSMs. The willingness to address a difficult inference problem and embrace a fully Bayesian framework is commendable. I believe this work has the potential to be highly influential.
