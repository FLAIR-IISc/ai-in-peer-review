

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper introduces a fundamentally novel approach to Bayesian inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the nonparametric state transition function f while still enabling efficient sampling from the smoothing distribution. The key insight is that by marginalizing f, the authors can maintain the full nonparametric expressivity of the GP model while avoiding the common pitfall of reducing the model to a parametric form (as done in previous work by Turner et al. [2] and Wang et al. [8]). Instead of requiring a fixed model for f, they develop a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm that handles the resulting non-Markovian dependencies in the state trajectory.

The paper's main contribution lies in its conceptual shift from previous approaches that either use parametric representations of the GP dynamics or require the dynamics model to be fixed during inference. By marginalizing f and using PGAS to sample from the smoothing distribution p(x₀:T | θ, y₀:T), the authors enable a fully Bayesian treatment of both the state trajectory and the nonparametric dynamics function. This is a significant conceptual advance over the work of Deisenroth et al. [5, 6], which focused on inference for already-learned GP dynamics, and over Turner et al. [2], which used EM to learn parametric GP representations.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for their approach, showing how the marginalization of f leads to a non-Gaussian prior over the state trajectory, and how the PGAS algorithm can efficiently sample from this complex distribution. The implementation details for both vanilla GP-SSM and sparse GP-SSM using the FIC prior are well-explained, with careful attention to computational complexity (O(T³) for vanilla GP-SSM and O(M²T) for sparse GP-SSM). The experiments on a nonlinear benchmark system and a cart-pole system demonstrate the method's ability to capture multimodal smoothing distributions and provide meaningful uncertainty estimates, which is particularly valuable for applications like reinforcement learning.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While there is a rich literature on GP-based SSMs, this work represents the first to fully marginalize the dynamics function while maintaining the nonparametric nature of the model during inference. Previous approaches either required a parametric representation of the dynamics (Turner et al. [2]) or focused on inference for fixed dynamics (Deisenroth et al. [5, 6]). The authors clearly articulate this distinction in their introduction, which strengthens the novelty claim.

The significance of this work is high. By enabling fully Bayesian inference in nonparametric SSMs, the approach opens up new possibilities for uncertainty-aware modeling of complex dynamical systems. The ability to maintain nonparametric expressivity while still efficiently sampling the smoothing distribution is particularly valuable for applications where uncertainty quantification is critical, such as in control systems or reinforcement learning.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient mathematical detail to allow reproduction of the method. The experiments are appropriate for the problem and demonstrate the strengths of the approach, particularly in handling multimodal smoothing distributions where parametric models fail.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The paper could provide a more thorough comparison with other nonparametric approaches to learning SSMs (e.g., using neural networks with Bayesian methods).
2. The computational complexity of O(T³) may limit scalability to very long time series, though the sparse GP-SSM version mitigates this issue.

However, these weaknesses are minor compared to the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The core idea of marginalizing out the nonparametric dynamics function while still enabling efficient smoothing is bold, novel, and addresses a fundamental limitation in the field.

In summary, this paper presents a significant conceptual advance in Bayesian inference for state-space models. The marginalization approach is both novel and elegant, solving a problem that previous methods either ignored or addressed through restrictive assumptions. The paper's main contribution—enabling fully Bayesian treatment of nonparametric dynamics while maintaining the ability to sample from the smoothing distribution—is a substantial step forward in the field.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on novel and impactful contributions. While the methodological details are sound, the true strength lies in the conceptual breakthrough of marginalizing the GP dynamics while still enabling efficient inference. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values over incremental improvements.

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian time series modeling and state-space inference, with the potential to influence future work in nonparametric dynamical systems.
