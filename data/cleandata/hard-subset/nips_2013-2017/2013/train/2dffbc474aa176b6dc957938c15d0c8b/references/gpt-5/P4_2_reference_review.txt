Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies fully Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior over the unknown state transition function f in xt+1 = f(xt, ut) + vt while assuming a known (possibly parametric) observation model yt | xt. Rather than introducing a finite parameterization of f, the paper marginalizes f exactly, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ). Smoothing and hyperparameter inference are then performed with Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), which is well-suited for non-Markovian latent variable models. Given samples from p(x0:T, θ | y0:T), the posterior over f is available in closed form (as in GP regression conditioned on the sampled state trajectory). The paper also presents a sparse variant using the FIC prior to reduce computational cost from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and implementation details exploiting rank-one Cholesky updates within CPF-AS. Experiments on a nonlinear scalar system with bimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole system show accurate smoothing and predictive performance, with uncertainty estimates reflecting distance to training data.

Relative to prior work: Deisenroth et al. [5,6] developed filtering/smoothing for known GP dynamics; Turner et al. [2] used EM with parametric GP-like models; Wang et al. [8] used MAP for GP dynamical models. The present contribution performs fully Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs by marginalizing f and using PMCMC [3] with PGAS [4] to sample the states and hyperparameters, retaining the nonparametric character of f and addressing path degeneracy in SMC.

Strengths
- Methodologically principled: exact marginalization of f removes strong state–function dependencies and avoids parametric approximations common in GP-SSMs.
- Appropriate inference engine: PGAS targets the exact smoothing distribution in non-Markovian models and mitigates path degeneracy.
- Computational contributions: clear derivation of the collapsed trajectory prior; complexity analysis; efficient Cholesky update/downdate strategy; sparse FIC construction yielding O(M^2 T).
- Flexibility: supports informative GP mean functions to encode prior dynamical knowledge while letting the GP capture deviations.
- Empirical evidence: Handles multimodal smoothing; demonstrates uncertainty-aware predictions in a 4D control setting.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Proposal distribution in SMC/CPF-AS: Equation (5) uses the optimal proposal p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t), which is generally intractable for non-Gaussian measurement models and the non-Gaussian prior in (4). Later text suggests using the one-step predictive prior p(xt | θ, x0:t−1). The paper should clarify which proposal is actually used in CPF-AS and how sampling is performed (e.g., prior proposal, local MCMC move, or numerical approximations).
- Computational complexity statements omit dependence on the number of particles N. The total cost per PGAS sweep scales at least linearly in N; making this explicit would help practitioners.
- Ancestor sampling weights require p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1). While the paper outlines reuse of Cholesky factors to compute these efficiently, the exact cost and numerical stability (e.g., handling downdates) would benefit from more detail, especially for long T and higher-dimensional states.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: only 50 PGAS iterations with N = 20 on T = 200; no diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, R-hat) or sensitivity to N, T, M, or hyperpriors; limited comparative baselines on cart-pole; no timing or scaling results. Adding convergence diagnostics, ablations over N/M/iterations, and runtime/memory reports would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Scope: The observation function g is treated as known. Many applications require learning g; discussing identifiability and whether the approach extends to partially unknown g (e.g., parametric with priors) would increase applicability.
- Inducing input selection: The sparse FIC section mentions selection strategies but does not specify the method used in experiments. Providing details (and robustness to M/selection) would improve reproducibility.

Quality
- The derivations for the collapsed trajectory prior (equations (3)–(4)) are consistent with standard GP predictive formulas and appear correct. Use of PGAS for non-Markovian models is supported by [4]. The implementation strategy via rank-one updates is sound in principle.
- The main technical gap is the unclarity around proposal sampling in (5). Without a tractable optimal proposal, performance can degrade; explicit description of the implemented proposal and any rejuvenation steps is needed.
- Claims about mixing (“very good mixing”) are not quantitatively substantiated. Including diagnostics would improve the evidential basis.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and clearly motivates the collapsed approach and the use of PGAS. Notation is heavy but standard for GP/SSM contexts.
- Specific clarifications recommended: the actual proposal used in CPF-AS; explicit computational complexity including N; memory requirements for storing per-particle factorizations; details of hyperparameter priors and slice sampling settings; inducing point selection procedure.

Originality
- Combining exact marginalization of f in GP-SSMs with PGAS for fully Bayesian smoothing and hyperparameter learning is a meaningful advance relative to EM/MAP-based and fixed-GP approaches. The sparse FIC trajectory prior within this PMCMC framework is also a useful contribution. The work is distinct from prior NIPS/AISTATS contributions [2,5,6,8].

Significance
- The approach addresses an important problem: principled, uncertainty-aware learning of nonlinear dynamics without committing to a parametric form. The methodology should be of interest to researchers in Bayesian time series, control, and reinforcement learning, and provides a building block for pipelines that require learned GP-SSMs. Practical impact will depend on clarifying/proving scalability and providing stronger empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance
- Fully Bayesian, nonparametric treatment of GP-SSMs via collapsing and PGAS.
- Solid integration of recent PMCMC advances for non-Markovian models.
- Thoughtful computational engineering, including a sparse variant.
- Empirical demonstrations illustrating multimodal smoothing and calibrated predictive uncertainty.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete specification of the proposal distribution and sampling steps in CPF-AS.
- Limited quantitative evaluation, diagnostics, and scalability studies.
- Complexity and memory costs may be high; dependence on N/T not fully characterized.
- Assumption of known observation model limits scope; extension not evaluated.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and novel framework with promising results. Addressing the noted clarity and empirical validation issues would significantly strengthen the submission.
