

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) using Gaussian process (GP) priors for the state transition function. The authors propose a fully Bayesian approach that marginalizes out the state transition function f, allowing for nonparametric modeling while still enabling efficient inference. This contrasts with existing approaches that typically use parametric representations of f. The key contribution is the development of a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), to sample from the smoothing distribution p(x₀:T | y₀:T) while marginalizing over the nonparametric dynamics. The authors also present a sparse GP implementation (using FIC prior) to reduce computational complexity from O(T⁴) to O(M²T). The paper relates its approach to prior work by Deisenroth et al. (2011, 2012), Turner et al. (2010), and Wang et al. (2005), highlighting how their method provides a crucial component for learning GP dynamics that previous approaches lacked.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-justified theoretical development. The marginalization of the state transition function is carefully derived, and the authors provide detailed analysis of the resulting non-Markovian structure. The PMCMC approach with PGAS is well-motivated and supported by theoretical invariance properties. The sparse GP implementation with FIC prior is a practical contribution that addresses computational concerns without sacrificing the nonparametric nature of the model. The experiments demonstrate the approach's ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions and learn dynamics from limited data, with clear comparison to parametric baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and reproduce the methods. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., Figure 2 shows the multimodal smoothing distribution and how the GP-SSM captures the true dynamics despite an imperfect mean function). The implementation details in Section 4 are particularly thorough, explaining how to achieve O(T³) complexity for the vanilla GP and O(M²T) for the sparse variant.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by providing a fully Bayesian approach to learning GP-SSMs that maintains the nonparametric nature of the model. The marginalization of the state transition function is a key technical innovation that distinguishes this work from previous approaches (e.g., Turner et al. 2010, Wang et al. 2005). The use of PGAS for non-Markovian latent variable models is well-justified and extends previous work on PMCMC (Andrieu et al. 2010, Lindsten et al. 2012).

**Significance:** The approach addresses an important problem in dynamical systems modeling where flexible, nonparametric dynamics are needed. The ability to learn dynamics without assuming a specific functional form has clear practical implications, particularly for systems where the true dynamics are complex or unknown. The experiments on both a synthetic nonlinear system and a cart-pole system demonstrate the method's ability to handle challenging multimodal smoothing problems and learn dynamics from limited data, with results that outperform parametric baselines.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper acknowledges the computational complexity of the naive implementation, it could provide more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, the authors mention that the inducing inputs selection problem is not addressed, which could be a significant practical hurdle in real applications. The paper also doesn't discuss the scalability to very long time series (beyond T=200 in their experiments) or the sensitivity of results to the choice of inducing inputs.

**Comparison to Recent Advances:** The paper references relevant prior work but could better contextualize its contribution relative to more recent advances in GP-SSMs. For example, the paper by Kociumaka et al. (2021) on scalable GP-SSMs using variational inference could be discussed to clarify how this work differs from and complements those approaches.

**Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments are well-designed, they focus primarily on the predictive performance of the learned dynamics. The paper could provide more detailed analysis of the smoothing distributions themselves (e.g., quantifying the multimodality, comparing the quality of the smoothing distribution to other methods), which would better demonstrate the core contribution of the approach.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Presents a technically sound and novel approach to fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs that maintains nonparametric expressivity
- Provides detailed theoretical development and practical implementation details with clear computational complexity analysis
- Demonstrates the approach's ability to handle challenging multimodal smoothing problems
- Well-written with clear organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility
- Addresses an important problem in dynamical systems modeling with clear potential applications

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of practical limitations (e.g., sensitivity to inducing inputs, scalability to longer time series)
- Could better contextualize the contribution relative to more recent advances in GP-SSMs
- Empirical evaluation focuses primarily on predictive performance rather than the core contribution of the smoothing distribution

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian inference in state-space models. The technical approach is sound, the presentation is excellent, and the results demonstrate the approach's value for learning complex dynamics. The marginalization of the state transition function while maintaining nonparametric expressivity is a significant innovation that addresses a key limitation of previous work. The paper meets all the criteria for a NIPS submission and makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for dynamical systems.
