### **Summary**

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The central problem is the joint inference of the latent state trajectory, the unknown state transition function, and model hyperparameters. The key contribution is a novel inference strategy that marginalizes out the unknown transition function, thereby retaining the full nonparametric nature of the GP prior. This marginalization induces a non-Markovian dependency in the latent state sequence. The authors tackle this challenging inference problem by employing a tailored Particle MCMC (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), which is well-suited for such non-Markovian structures. The method allows for sampling from the joint posterior of the states and hyperparameters. The posterior over the transition function is then recovered as a mixture of GPs. The paper also addresses the cubic computational complexity of the standard approach by introducing a sparse GP variant based on the FIC approximation, reducing complexity to O(M²T). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a challenging synthetic benchmark with a multimodal posterior and on learning the dynamics of a cart-pole system.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methodology is sound, and the theoretical justifications are clear.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Principled Bayesian Treatment:** The core idea of marginalizing the GP transition function `f` is elegant and allows for a more principled Bayesian treatment compared to methods that rely on point estimates (MAP) or parametric approximations of the GP. This avoids potential overfitting issues associated with MAP estimation of latent states and maintains the model's nonparametric flexibility.
2.  **Appropriate Choice of Inference Algorithm:** The resulting non-Markovian posterior over state trajectories is a significant challenge. The authors correctly identify that PMCMC methods are a suitable tool, and their choice of PGAS is particularly well-justified, as it has been shown to improve mixing in models with strong temporal dependencies.
3.  **Thoroughness in Implementation:** The authors demonstrate a strong grasp of the practical challenges by addressing the computational complexity. The discussion of the O(T³) complexity and the proposed reduction to O(M²T) via a sparse FIC-based GP prior is crucial for the method's applicability beyond short time series. The mention of efficient Cholesky updates shows careful implementation considerations.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Experimental Details:** In the first experiment (Section 6.1), the PGAS sampler is run for only 50 iterations. This is an exceptionally low number for an MCMC algorithm, which raises concerns about whether the sampler has converged to the stationary distribution. While PGAS can have good mixing properties, 50 samples are typically insufficient to characterize a posterior, especially in a multimodal setting. The authors should provide convergence diagnostics or a justification for this choice.
2.  **Hyperparameter Sampling:** The paper states that sampling the GP hyperparameters `θx` is "straightforward" using slice sampling. However, sampling GP hyperparameters within an MCMC scheme is often challenging, characterized by strong correlations and slow mixing. A more detailed discussion of the practical performance of this step would strengthen the paper's claims of providing a complete and efficient inference solution.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The logical flow from model definition to inference and experiments is easy to follow for a reader familiar with GPs and MCMC.

**Strengths:**
1.  The problem statement is articulated clearly in the introduction.
2.  The derivation of the non-Markovian prior over state trajectories in Section 3.1 is the technical core of the paper and is explained with sufficient detail.
3.  The use of Algorithm 1 to outline the PGAS sampler is helpful. The distinction between a standard particle filter and the conditional version used within PGAS is also well-explained.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  The explanation of the ancestor sampling weights in equation (6) could be slightly more intuitive. While formally correct, a sentence explaining *why* the term `p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1)` is the crucial factor for re-weighting—that it measures the compatibility of the past trajectory `i` with the future conditioned trajectory—might aid understanding.

#### **Originality**

The paper makes a novel and significant contribution to the literature on learning in dynamical systems.

**Strengths:**
1.  While the components—GPs, SSMs, and PMCMC—are established, their combination in this manner is novel. Specifically, the strategy of marginalizing the GP function `f` to perform smoothing and then reconstructing the posterior over `f` is a departure from previous approaches.
2.  This work correctly positions itself with respect to prior art. It contrasts its fully Bayesian approach with the EM-based methods of Turner et al. (2009) and the MAP-based approach of Wang et al. (2008), clearly articulating the advantages of avoiding point estimates in the presence of significant posterior uncertainty. It also appropriately frames itself as a method for *learning* the GP model, complementing inference methods like those of Deisenroth et al. (2012) that assume a known model.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The paper could benefit from a discussion of how this work relates to modern variational inference (VI) techniques for GP-SSMs. While VI methods provide an approximation, they often scale better, and a comparison (even a qualitative one) discussing the trade-offs between the exactness of PMCMC and the scalability of VI would be valuable context for the reader.

#### **Significance**

The results presented are important and likely to have an impact on the field.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** Learning the structure of unknown nonlinear dynamics from data is a fundamental problem in many fields. This paper provides a powerful, flexible, and principled tool for this task.
2.  **Enables Better Uncertainty Quantification:** By providing samples from the true posterior (up to MCMC error), the method offers a much richer characterization of uncertainty than methods based on MAP or variational approximations. The bimodal posterior example in Figure 2 is a perfect illustration of a scenario where other methods would fail, but this approach succeeds. This is critical for applications in control and reinforcement learning where robust decision-making under uncertainty is key.
3.  **Potential for Broader Impact:** The methodology is general and could be built upon. The idea of using advanced MCMC to tackle the non-Markovian models that arise from marginalizing GPs could be applied in other contexts beyond standard SSMs.

### **Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, technically sound, and significant contribution to the problem of learning in nonlinear state-space models. The core idea of marginalizing the GP dynamics function and then applying a sophisticated sampler (PGAS) is both elegant and effective. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the approach, particularly in handling posterior uncertainty.

While there are minor points that could be clarified (e.g., the low number of MCMC iterations in the experiment, details on hyperparameter sampling), these do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian modeling of dynamical systems.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
