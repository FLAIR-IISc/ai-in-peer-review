### Summary

This paper presents a novel method for fully Bayesian inference and learning in state-space models where the state transition dynamics are governed by a Gaussian Process (GP-SSM). The central idea is to analytically marginalize the infinite-dimensional state transition function, thereby retaining the full nonparametric nature of the GP prior. This marginalization induces complex, long-range dependencies in the latent state sequence, breaking the Markov property and rendering standard inference methods inapplicable. The authors elegantly solve this challenging inference problem by employing a tailored Particle MCMC (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This allows them to sample from the joint posterior distribution of the state trajectories and model hyperparameters. Given these samples, the posterior over the transition function is recovered as a mixture of Gaussians. The paper also addresses the O(T^3) computational complexity of the method by proposing a sparse variant based on the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) approximation, which reduces the complexity to O(M^2T). The approach is validated on a synthetic nonlinear system with a bimodal posterior, demonstrating its ability to handle complex uncertainty structures, and on a cart-pole system benchmark.

This work builds upon a rich literature of GP-SSMs (e.g., Wang et al., 2005; Deisenroth et al., 2012) but distinguishes itself by providing a complete, fully Bayesian learning framework that avoids both parametric approximations of the GP (cf. Turner et al., 2011) and MAP estimation. It is one of the first works to successfully apply modern PMCMC techniques (Andrieu et al., 2010; Lindsten et al., 2014) to this important class of models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methodology is sound, well-motivated, and represents a complete piece of work, spanning from model formulation to a sophisticated inference algorithm and experimental validation. The application of PGAS to the non-Markovian model that results from marginalizing the GP function is a clever and appropriate choice. The derivations appear correct. The experiments are well-chosen to highlight the strengths of the method; in particular, the benchmark with a bimodal posterior is a compelling demonstration of the value of a sampling-based approach over methods that might collapse to a single mode. The inclusion of a sparse variant and a detailed discussion of computational complexity show a thorough consideration of the practical aspects of the algorithm.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for marginalizing the GP function and the resulting inference challenges are explained very well. The overall structure guides the reader logically from the model definition to the inference algorithm and its results. However, Section 4.2 ("Implementation Details") is very dense. While the claims of reducing complexity to O(T^3) and O(M^2T) are believable, the description of how this is achieved (e.g., via rank-2nx updates/downdates of Cholesky factors) is too brief to be easily reproducible for a non-expert in this specific area. Providing more detail, perhaps in an appendix, would significantly strengthen the paper's practical value.

**Originality:** The primary contribution is a novel and principled combination of existing concepts. While GP-SSMs and PMCMC are not new in themselves, their application in this manner to create a fully Bayesian, nonparametric learning algorithm for SSMs is highly original. The conceptual shift—from jointly inferring the function `f` and the state trajectory `x` to first inferring `x` while marginalizing `f`—is elegant and powerful. This approach stands in contrast to the majority of previous work and opens up a new avenue for inference in this model class.

**Significance:** The paper addresses the fundamental and widely important problem of system identification for nonlinear dynamical systems. The proposed method could have a significant impact in many fields, including robotics, control, signal processing, and econometrics. It provides what could be considered a "gold standard" inference procedure for GP-SSMs, offering a benchmark against which faster but more approximate methods (e.g., based on variational inference) can be measured. The ability to correctly capture complex posterior uncertainty, as demonstrated experimentally, is crucial for applications where robust decision-making is required.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a novel, fully Bayesian inference framework for GP-SSMs that sidesteps common simplifying assumptions.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The application of PGAS to the resulting non-Markovian system is technically sophisticated and well-executed.
3.  **Significance:** Addresses a fundamental problem with broad applicability and provides a high-quality benchmark method for learning nonlinear dynamics.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments, especially the bimodal example, effectively demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.
5.  **Completeness:** The work considers practical aspects such as computational complexity and scalability via a sparse GP construction.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Computational Cost:** The method is computationally intensive (O(T^3) for the full model), which may limit its application to long time series. This is an inherent trade-off, but a weakness nonetheless.
2.  **Reproducibility:** The implementation details in Section 4.2 are condensed, which may pose a barrier to reproduction without significant effort from the reader.

### Suggestions for Improvement

Overall, this is an excellent paper and I recommend acceptance. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed. My suggestions are intended to further improve the manuscript:

*   **Elaborate on Implementation Details:** The most important suggestion is to expand upon Section 4.2. An appendix detailing the sequential updates to the Cholesky factorization for the O(T^3) algorithm and the specific matrix operations required to achieve the O(M^2T) complexity for the FIC version would be invaluable for practitioners and would greatly enhance the paper's impact and reproducibility.
*   **Sensitivity to `N`:** The performance of PMCMC depends on the number of particles, `N`. While the paper mentions that a moderate `N` is often sufficient, providing a brief empirical analysis (e.g., a plot of the autocorrelation of a hyperparameter vs. `N` for the benchmark experiment) would offer useful practical guidance to users of the method.
*   **Inducing Point Selection:** The paper defers the problem of inducing point selection for the sparse model to existing literature. While this is acceptable, a short discussion of potential challenges or interactions when using these methods within the PGAS loop (e.g., whether inducing points should be adapted between Gibbs steps) would be a welcome addition to the future work discussion.
*   **Comparison to Variational Inference:** While not essential for acceptance, a discussion or experimental comparison against a modern variational inference (VI) approach for GP-SSMs would help to better contextualize the performance-vs-computation trade-off of this PMCMC-based method. This could highlight cases where the computational expense of PGAS is justified by superior accuracy, particularly in capturing multi-modality which VI methods often struggle with.
