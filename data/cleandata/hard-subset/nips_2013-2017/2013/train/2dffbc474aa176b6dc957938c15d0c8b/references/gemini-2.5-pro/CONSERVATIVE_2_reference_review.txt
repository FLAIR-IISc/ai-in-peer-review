**Summary:**
This paper proposes a fully Bayesian inference and learning method for Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The central idea is to marginalize out the unknown state transition function, which is modeled with a GP prior. This marginalization renders the state sequence non-Markovian. To perform inference in this resulting model, the authors employ a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm. This allows for jointly sampling the latent state trajectories and model hyperparameters. The posterior over the transition function can then be recovered as a mixture of GPs. The authors also propose a sparse GP variant to reduce the computational complexity from cubic to linear in the time series length. The method is demonstrated on a synthetic nonlinear system and a cart-pole system.

**Strengths:**
*   **Methodological Soundness:** The paper presents a principled, fully Bayesian treatment of the GP-SSM learning problem. By marginalizing the transition function `f` rather than resorting to point estimates (like MAP) or finite-dimensional approximations, the approach correctly accounts for the uncertainty in the dynamics. This is a clear strength from a theoretical standpoint.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. It clearly explains the motivation for marginalizing the GP, the resulting non-Markovian structure of the state model, and how the PGAS algorithm is adapted to perform inference in this setting. The derivations and algorithmic descriptions are coherent.
*   **Appropriate use of Advanced Inference:** The choice of PGAS is well-suited for the inference problem at hand. It is a state-of-the-art technique for sampling from the smoothing distribution in non-Markovian latent variable models, and its application here is technically sound.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Limited Experimental Validation and Baselines:** The primary weakness of this paper lies in its empirical evaluation. While the method is technically elegant, its practical advantages over existing, established methods are not convincingly demonstrated.
    *   The main experiment in Section 6.1 compares the proposed method against two simple parametric models. This is insufficient. A rigorous evaluation would require direct comparison against the key GP-SSM learning methods mentioned in the introduction, such as the EM-based approach of Turner et al. [2] or the MAP-based method of Wang et al. [8]. The paper claims these methods are parametric or risk overfitting, but provides no empirical evidence to show that the proposed method yields better predictive accuracy, more robust uncertainty estimates, or superior performance on a downstream task. Without these comparisons, the significance of the contribution is difficult to assess.
    *   The cart-pole experiment is illustrative at best. It shows one-step-ahead predictions but does not evaluate the model's ability to generate long-term rollouts or its usefulness for tasks like planning or control, which are common applications for such learned dynamics models.
*   **Originality:** The contribution of this work is primarily the combination and application of several existing, powerful techniques (GPs, SSMs, PMCMC/PGAS). While the application to GP-SSMs in this fully Bayesian manner is novel, the core components themselves are well-established. This makes the work more of an incremental, though non-trivial, integration of existing ideas rather than a fundamental breakthrough.
*   **Scalability and Practical Concerns:** The paper acknowledges the O(T^3) complexity of the full model and proposes a sparse O(M^2 T) alternative. However, the experiments are conducted on relatively short time series (T=200). The performance and computational cost for longer sequences, where such a method would be most needed, are not explored. Furthermore, practical details that would be crucial for reproduction and adoption are sparse. For instance, the wall-clock time for the experiments is not reported, the sensitivity to the number of particles (N) is only anecdotally addressed, and the strategy for selecting inducing points in the sparse model is mentioned but not evaluated. The PMCMC framework itself is known to be computationally intensive and can have mixing issues, which are not thoroughly investigated here beyond a statement of "very good mixing".

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a methodologically sound and elegant fully Bayesian approach for a challenging and important problem.
2.  The work is clearly written and provides a good example of applying advanced MCMC techniques to a complex non-parametric model.
3.  The ability to correctly capture multi-modality in the smoothing distribution (Figure 2) is a notable feature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The experimental evaluation is critically lacking. The absence of comparisons to relevant and established GP-SSM learning baselines makes it impossible to judge the practical utility and significance of the proposed method.
2.  The contribution is more of a novel combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamental advance.
3.  The experiments are limited to small-scale problems, and the practical aspects of scalability, computational cost, and hyperparameter sensitivity are not adequately addressed.

**Recommendation:**
While the proposed method is theoretically appealing and technically sound, the lack of a rigorous empirical comparison to established baselines is a major flaw. The claims of superiority over prior work are based on theoretical arguments rather than empirical demonstration. For a field like NeurIPS, where practical performance is highly valued, this is a significant shortcoming. The experiments presented are more of a proof-of-concept than a thorough validation. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The paper would be much stronger if it included direct, quantitative comparisons against the state-of-the-art methods it aims to supersede, on a more challenging set of benchmark problems.
