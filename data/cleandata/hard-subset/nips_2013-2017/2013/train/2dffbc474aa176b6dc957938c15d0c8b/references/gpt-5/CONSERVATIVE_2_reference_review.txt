Summary and relation to prior work
The paper considers nonlinear state-space models where the transition function f is given a Gaussian process (GP) prior and the observation model g is assumed known (possibly parametric). The key idea is to marginalize f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories. Posterior inference over states x0:T and hyperparameters θ is then performed using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), which is well-suited to non-Markovian latent-variable models. Hyperparameters for the GP prior and observation model are sampled conditional on x0:T (slice sampling for GP hyperparameters). To address computational cost, the authors derive implementations with O(T^3) scaling for a “vanilla” GP and O(M^2 T) using the FIC sparse GP with M inducing points, with careful reuse of Cholesky factorizations and rank-one updates/downdates. Finally, given samples from p(x0:T, θ | y), posterior predictive inference over f follows via a mixture of GP posteriors.

This work sits at the intersection of GP dynamical models (e.g., Wang et al., GPDM; Deisenroth et al. on GP filtering/smoothing with learned GPs; Turner et al. on EM-like treatments of GP-SSMs) and Particle MCMC (Andrieu et al.) with PGAS (Lindsten et al.). The principal novelty claimed is to perform fully Bayesian inference in a GP-SSM by collapsing f and using PGAS directly on the non-Markovian trajectory posterior, rather than introducing a finite parametric representation or relying on MAP/EM-type procedures.

Strengths
- Methodological soundness: Collapsing f is an elegant way to remove strong dependencies between x and f and retain the nonparametric nature of the model. Using PGAS for the resulting non-Markovian latent process is principled and consistent with established theory.
- Clear connection to standard GP regression: Conditional on x0:T, θ, the updates for f and predictive distributions follow familiar GP formulae, which increases transparency.
- Attention to computational issues: The paper discusses complexities, reuse of Cholesky factors, rank-one updates/downdates, and a sparse FIC variant to reduce cost.
- Expressive predictive representation: The mixture of GP conditionals across MCMC samples can capture multimodality in dynamics induced by ambiguous observations.
- Experiments demonstrate qualitative capabilities: The 1D nonlinear benchmark shows multimodal smoothing; the cart-pole example illustrates heteroscedastic predictive uncertainty localized to regions supported by data.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited empirical validation and baselines: The experimental section is quite thin for a method of this complexity. The main synthetic example is 1D with T=200, and the cart-pole is 4D with 100 observations. There is no comparison to strong alternatives such as EM/MAP GP-SSM (Turner et al.), GPDM (Wang et al.), GP-UKF/EP filtering/smoothing after learning (Deisenroth et al.), or parametric state-space PMCMC. Reported RMSEs (Table 1) are only summarized narratively; detailed numbers, standard deviations, and statistical significance are not provided. No negative log-likelihood or multi-step rollout metrics are reported. Without rigorous benchmarking, claims of “very good mixing” and “efficient learning” are not well supported.
- Scalability and complexity claims: The O(T^3) and O(M^2 T) statements omit important factors (number of particles N, state dimension nx, and constants from repeated rank updates). The method maintains per-particle factorizations and updates them at each time step; the paper should provide precise overall complexity per PGAS iteration (as a function of N, T, nx, M) and wall-clock timings. The 10,000-step prediction evaluation appears to bypass full inference and does not demonstrate that the proposed sampler scales to long sequences.
- Assumption of known observation model g: While sometimes reasonable, this materially limits applicability. Many practical GP-SSM settings require learning both f and g; the paper only briefly motivates identifiability concerns but does not empirically study the impact of misspecification in g or explore partially unknown g with priors.
- Sparse GP implementation details: The FIC section outlines formulas but leaves open how inducing inputs are chosen and how sensitive performance is to M and selection strategy. The claim that FIC can outperform “vanilla” GP-SSM should be substantiated empirically.
- Diagnostics and robustness: There is no analysis of MCMC diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS, R-hat), sensitivity to N (particles), L (MCMC iterations), priors, or the mean function choice. Given the heavy-tailed posterior geometries and non-Markovian structure, such diagnostics are essential.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Several derivations are dense and leave out intermediate steps (e.g., efficient computation of ancestor weights in Eq. (6) under the collapsed prior for each particle/time). Pseudocode for the CPF-AS in the non-Markovian setting would help. Many references are incomplete (missing years/venues), which is unusual for a NeurIPS submission.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide precise overall computational complexity per PGAS iteration, including dependence on N, T, nx, M, and the cost of ancestor sampling computations. Also report wall-clock times and memory usage for your experiments.
2. How sensitive is performance/mixing to the choice of mean function? What happens if the mean is misspecified more strongly, or if a zero-mean GP is used?
3. Can you compare against EM/MAP GP-SSM (Turner et al.), GPDM (Wang et al.), and filtering/smoothing with a learned GP (Deisenroth et al.) on your benchmarks, using both one-step and multi-step predictive metrics and NLL?
4. Please report MCMC diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, acceptance in slice sampling) and ablate N and L to demonstrate robustness.
5. For the FIC variant, how are inducing inputs selected? Show results varying M and selection strategy, and compare accuracy-vs-time to the full method.
6. Can the approach be extended to partially unknown g, and what identifiability constraints would you adopt?

Minor comments
- Equation (4) is not Gaussian in x1:t; consider clarifying this more directly in the main text with a short derivation in the appendix.
- Include pseudocode for the non-Markovian CPF-AS highlighting how you form and update the needed factorizations for Eq. (6).
- Fix incomplete references and ensure reproducibility details (priors, kernels, hyperparameters) are fully specified.

Pros
- Principled Bayesian treatment with exact marginalization of f and theoretically justified PGAS.
- Solid connection to GP regression and clear computational strategy.
- Addresses multimodal smoothing; flexible predictive representation.

Cons
- Empirical validation is limited, with weak baselines and no diagnostic analyses.
- Scalability and complexity not convincingly demonstrated.
- Restrictive assumption of known g; sparse GP variant lacks empirical support.
- Presentation omits important practical details; references are incomplete.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper combines established components in a coherent way, and the collapsed PGAS treatment for GP-SSMs is conceptually appealing. However, given the modest empirical validation, lack of rigorous comparisons and diagnostics, and unclear scalability, I am not convinced the contribution is sufficiently substantiated for NeurIPS at this stage. Strengthening the experiments, clarifying complexities, and providing thorough comparisons would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
