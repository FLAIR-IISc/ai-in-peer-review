

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper introduces a fundamentally novel approach to Bayesian learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function f entirely, rather than approximating it parametrically as in all prior work. The authors propose a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to efficiently sample from the smoothing distribution while marginalizing over the nonparametric dynamics model.

The key innovation lies in recognizing that direct learning of f from unobserved states is challenging, and instead of reverting to parametric representations as previous approaches did, the authors marginalize f from the model. This preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the GP while addressing the core challenge of learning dynamics from incomplete state observations. The paper carefully develops the marginal prior over state trajectories, which introduces non-Markovian dependencies, and then proposes a PGAS-based inference method to handle this non-Markovian structure. The authors also address computational complexity through a sparse GP-SSM construction using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior, reducing complexity from O(T^4) to O(M^2T).

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on GP-based SSMs, which has largely focused on inference for already-learned models (Deisenroth et al. [5,6]) or on parametric approximations of the GP (Turner et al. [2]). Previous work by Wang et al. [8] used MAP estimation, which risks overfitting, while the current paper provides a fully Bayesian solution. The paper clearly articulates how their approach differs from these prior methods and provides a compelling theoretical foundation for their marginalization strategy.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The core idea of marginalizing out the state transition function is bold and novel, representing a significant conceptual advance over existing approaches. The theoretical development is sound, with clear derivations of the marginal prior and the PGAS algorithm. The experimental results on both a synthetic nonlinear system (where the smoothing distribution is multimodal) and a cart and pole system demonstrate the approach's practical utility. The sparse GP-SSM implementation is particularly impressive, addressing computational concerns while maintaining the nonparametric nature of the model.

I note that the experimental section could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods for learning nonlinear SSMs, though the current comparisons with parametric models are sufficient to demonstrate the approach's value. The paper does not extensively discuss sensitivity to inducing point selection, but this is a minor limitation given the overall strength of the contribution.

From a novelty perspective, this paper stands out as a significant conceptual advance. The authors have identified a fundamental limitation in previous GP-SSM approaches (parametric approximation of the GP) and proposed a fundamentally different solution that maintains the full nonparametric expressivity. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values.

The significance of this work is high. Learning nonlinear state-space models is a fundamental problem in time series analysis, and this paper provides a fully Bayesian solution that properly handles the nonparametric nature of the dynamics model. The approach enables accurate smoothing distributions even when the parametric baseline model is misspecified, as demonstrated in the experiments. The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution also offers a more expressive representation of uncertainty than single-GP approximations.

While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational trade-offs compared to alternative methods. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's strong conceptual contribution.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel core idea of marginalizing out the state transition function
- Bold approach that fundamentally differs from prior work
- Theoretical soundness with clear derivations
- Demonstrated effectiveness on challenging synthetic and real-world problems
- Addresses a significant limitation in existing GP-SSM approaches

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental comparison to state-of-the-art methods
- Some computational details could be more thoroughly explained
- Experimental section is somewhat brief

This paper represents a significant advance in the field of Bayesian learning for state-space models. The authors have successfully developed a fully Bayesian approach that preserves the nonparametric expressivity of GP models while solving the key challenge of learning dynamics from incomplete observations. The marginalization strategy is conceptually elegant and technically well-executed, and the sparse GP-SSM implementation makes the approach computationally feasible for practical applications.

The paper's contribution is not incremental but represents a new direction for learning GP-SSMs, which aligns perfectly with NIPS's emphasis on novel ideas. While the experimental section could be expanded, the core conceptual contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance. I strongly recommend accepting this paper for NIPS.
