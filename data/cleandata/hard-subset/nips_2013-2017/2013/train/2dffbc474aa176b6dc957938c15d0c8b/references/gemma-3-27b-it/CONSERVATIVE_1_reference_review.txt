This paper presents a novel approach to learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function and employing a particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method for inference. While the idea of fully Bayesian treatment and nonparametric dynamics is appealing, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a significant advantage over established methods, and the experimental validation feels somewhat limited given the methodological complexity.

The core contribution – collapsing the GP dynamics – addresses a genuine challenge in GP-SSM learning. Existing work often relies on parametric approximations, which can limit flexibility. The proposed PMCMC scheme, leveraging recent advances in particle sampling, is presented as a solution to efficiently handle the resulting inference problem. The theoretical grounding appears solid, with a clear derivation of the marginal likelihood and a detailed explanation of the inference algorithm.  The connection to existing SMC and MCMC literature is well-established.

However, the paper’s primary weakness lies in its reliance on a single, somewhat artificial benchmark problem (the nonlinear system with the specific dynamic equation) and a single real-world application (cart-pole).  While the results on the benchmark are positive, the improvement over simpler baselines isn’t decisively demonstrated. The table comparing RMSE values includes comparisons to a "ground truth model," which isn’t a standard baseline; a comparison to established GP-SSM methods like those by Deisenroth et al. [5, 6] – which, notably, *assume* a learned GP dynamics, making this work directly comparable – is missing.  Furthermore, the impact of the sparse GP formulation (FIC prior) is not clearly quantified; the RMSE improvement is modest. 

The cart-pole example is more convincing in demonstrating the capability of the learned model to capture uncertainty, but again lacks a rigorous comparison to existing reinforcement learning or control methods using GP-SSMs. Showing performance gains in a control task would substantially strengthen the claims. The statement that the learned model "reports different amounts of uncertainty in different regions of the state-space" is somewhat expected of a GP-based method and doesn't, on its own, represent a major breakthrough. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, though the mathematical notation can be dense. Implementation details are provided, which is helpful, but the computational complexity analysis feels a little buried and could be presented more prominently.  The discussion of related work is adequate, but a more thorough comparison to the broader GP-SSM literature (beyond the cited references) would be beneficial. The claim that this provides a “vital component” for the filtering/smoothing methods of Deisenroth et al. [5, 6] seems overstated. Those methods can, and are, used *without* a fully Bayesian learning scheme for the GP.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to GP-SSM learning, addressing a key limitation of existing methods.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and detailed algorithmic description.
*   Demonstrated capability of learning complex dynamics and capturing uncertainty.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental validation; lacks strong comparisons to established baselines (especially GP-SSM methods by Deisenroth et al.).
*   Benchmark problem is somewhat artificial.
*   Impact of the sparse GP formulation isn't convincingly demonstrated.
*   Claims of significance feel somewhat overstated given the evidence presented.



**Overall Assessment:**

I view this paper as a potentially interesting contribution, but one that requires substantial revision before it would be suitable for NIPS. The core idea is sound, but the paper needs to be strengthened with more rigorous experimental validation, a more thorough comparison to existing work, and a more nuanced discussion of its limitations. Currently, the evidence presented doesn't justify the methodological complexity. I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form, but encouraging the authors to address the concerns outlined above and resubmit to a future conference.
