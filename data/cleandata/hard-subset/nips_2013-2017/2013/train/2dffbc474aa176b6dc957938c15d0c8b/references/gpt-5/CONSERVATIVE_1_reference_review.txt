Summary
This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing the unknown transition function f and performing inference over the latent states x0:T and hyperparameters θ using Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). Marginalization renders the latent process non-Markovian; the authors construct SMC kernels that operate on this non-Markovian prior, derive computational strategies to keep complexity at O(T^3) (or O(M^2T) with a sparse FIC prior), and then learn f post hoc via GP regression conditioned on sampled state trajectories. Experiments include a 1D nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole example.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on established ideas: GP priors for dynamics [2,5,6,8], collapsing/“marginalizing out” latent functions, and PMCMC [3], with PGAS [4] for better mixing. In contrast to EM/MAP approaches to GPSSMs [2,8] and GPDM [8], the authors target a fully Bayesian posterior over states and hyperparameters while retaining a nonparametric prior over f. The sparse FIC prior [16,17] is standard. Overall, the paper combines well-known components; the main novelty is the specific collapsing-plus-PGAS construction and associated implementation.

Strengths
- Sound high-level idea: marginalize f to avoid strong x–f coupling and use PGAS to sample non-Markovian latent states. This is principled and leverages solid literature.
- Methodological care: clear derivation of the non-Markovian prior over trajectories, and thoughtful discussion of computational tricks (Cholesky rank updates; FIC to reduce complexity).
- Practical benefits: mixture-of-Gaussians predictive representation; ability to encode prior mean functions; clear articulation of identifiability issues if both f and g are too flexible.
- The multimodal benchmark illustrates a case where collapsing f can be beneficial; the framework naturally propagates model uncertainty.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited empirical validation. Only a small 1D synthetic benchmark and a qualitative cart-pole example are presented. There is no comparison to strong baselines widely used for GP-based dynamical systems (e.g., EM with RBF/GP transitions [2,7,8], GPDM [8], or filtering/smoothing with learned GP dynamics [5,6]). Claims of “very good mixing” are unsupported by quantitative diagnostics (e.g., autocorrelation, ESS, R-hat), sensitivity to N and T, or runtime.
- Proposal inconsistency. In Sec. 3.2, Eq. (5) states sampling from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t), but the subsequent text says “sampling from the one-step predictive density is a simple choice,” and the weighting wit ∝ p(yt | θ, xit) matches the bootstrap filter. If the proposal ignores yt (bootstrap), that should be stated; otherwise the form of the optimal proposal with general p(yt|xt) needs specification. This matters for efficiency in the non-Markovian setting.
- Scalability remains speculative. While the O(T^3)/O(M^2T) narratives are plausible, there are no wall-clock results, memory profiles, or scaling curves vs T, M, N. The sparse FIC variant omits how inducing inputs are selected; the paper acknowledges this but offers no empirical study of its impact.
- Evaluation criteria are narrow. The main metric is RMSE on f(xt, ut) rather than predictive accuracy on x or y, multi-step forecasting, or calibration of uncertainty (e.g., coverage). The cart-pole results are qualitative; no comparison to physics-based baselines or learned parametric models is provided.
- Assumptions reduce generality. The measurement model g is assumed known (up to parameters), which simplifies identifiability but limits applicability. The paper does not explore robustness when g is misspecified.
- No ablations. It would be important to test: effect of the GP mean function (e.g., with/without informative mean), sensitivity to Q and kernel choices, and comparison to a MAP/EM variant under the same GP prior to justify the additional complexity of full Bayesian PMCMC.

Quality
- The core methodology is principled and builds on rigorous foundations. However, important practical details (proposal specification; diagnostics; inducing-point selection) are underdeveloped, and empirical evidence is not yet sufficient to substantiate the claimed advantages.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with useful intuition. Some notation is heavy, and Section 3.2’s proposal/wighting inconsistency should be corrected. More pseudo-code or a concise algorithmic summary of CPF-AS for this non-Markovian setting would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The components (GP prior, collapsing, PMCMC/PGAS, FIC) are established. The contribution is mainly in their combination and the derived non-Markovian SMC/PGAS machinery with computational refinements. Moderate originality.

Significance
- Potentially impactful if shown to outperform EM/MAP or variational alternatives in accuracy, calibration, and robustness. As is, the empirical section does not convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art performance or scalability.

Pros
- Principled fully Bayesian treatment that preserves nonparametric flexibility.
- Thoughtful computational treatment and a sparse alternative.
- Handles multimodal smoothing naturally.

Cons
- Sparse and limited experiments; lacks strong baselines and quantitative diagnostics.
- Ambiguity about the particle proposal and practical efficiency.
- No scalability evidence; inducing-point selection not addressed empirically.
- Assumes known observation model; limited exploration of robustness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and, if needed, correct the proposal distribution and weighting; consider comparing bootstrap vs partially/fully adapted proposals in this non-Markovian setting.
- Add comprehensive baselines (EM/MAP GPSSM/GPDM; learned parametric SSMs) with multi-step prediction, smoothing error, calibration, and runtime.
- Provide mixing diagnostics, sensitivity analyses (N, T, M), and scaling plots; include wall-clock and memory usage.
- Evaluate on a larger real-world time-series dataset and quantify benefits over simpler established methods.
- Study impact of mean-function choice and inducing-point selection.

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the approach is principled and potentially valuable, the current empirical validation and clarity on key implementation choices are insufficient to justify acceptance. Strengthening the experiments, clarifying the proposal, and providing scalability evidence would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Reviewer confidence: High on methodological/PMCMC aspects; moderate on specific implementation details due to limited exposition.
