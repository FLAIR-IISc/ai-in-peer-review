This paper presents a novel approach to learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by performing fully Bayesian inference and marginalizing out the state transition function. This is achieved using a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically a tailored PGAS sampler. The work builds upon a significant body of research in GP-SSMs (Deisenroth et al. [5, 6], Turner et al. [2], Wang et al. [8]) and particle methods (Andrieu et al. [3], Lindsten et al. [4]). A key distinction from prior work is the avoidance of parametric approximations to the GP, retaining its nonparametric flexibility. The paper also explores sparse GP methods (Snelson & Ghahramani [16]) for computational efficiency.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations appear correct, and the proposed PMCMC algorithm is well-motivated and described. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the underlying theory. The use of marginalization is a principled approach to address the challenges of learning nonlinear SSMs. The experimental results provide evidence for the effectiveness of the method, although the complexity of the system makes it challenging to definitively assess the contributions. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential avenues for further improvement, demonstrating careful consideration of the work’s scope. The inclusion of a sparse GP variant is a pragmatic step to address computational concerns.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are mostly clear. However, some sections (e.g., section 3.1) require careful reading due to the density of equations. Figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts. The presentation of the algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clear. A minor improvement could be to provide more intuitive explanations of the mathematical steps, perhaps with a running example. The connections between different sections, such as the motivation for marginalizing out the function and the implications for the smoothing distribution, are adequately explained.

**Originality:** The core idea of fully Bayesian inference with marginalization in GP-SSMs is novel. While PMCMC has been used in related contexts, its application to this specific problem with the tailored PGAS sampler is a significant contribution. The authors clearly position their work with respect to existing literature, and they highlight how their approach differs from previous methods. The combination of non-parametric GP modeling with a sophisticated sampling technique represents a novel approach in the field.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate the potential of the proposed method for learning complex dynamical systems. The ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions is particularly valuable. The application to a cart and pole system highlights the practical relevance of the work. The method could be impactful in areas such as robotics, control, and time series analysis. The ability to obtain uncertainty estimates (as demonstrated in the cart-pole example) is crucial for many real-world applications.  The potential to use a parametric prior as a baseline within the GP framework is a valuable addition.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach offers a new perspective on learning GP-SSMs, overcoming limitations of previous methods.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper is rigorous and well-supported by theoretical analysis.
*   **Potential Impact:** The method has the potential to be applied to a wide range of problems in dynamical systems modeling.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The method is complex and may be challenging to implement and apply.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors address this with sparse GPs, the method remains computationally intensive.
*   **Limited Scalability:**  Further analysis of scalability to very high-dimensional state spaces would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Validation:** While the experiments are promising, more extensive validation on diverse datasets would strengthen the claims.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in the field of GP-SSMs. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and has the potential to make a significant impact. While the complexity and computational cost are concerns, the authors have made efforts to address these issues. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors could improve clarity in a few dense sections, and expanding the experimental evaluation would be beneficial.
