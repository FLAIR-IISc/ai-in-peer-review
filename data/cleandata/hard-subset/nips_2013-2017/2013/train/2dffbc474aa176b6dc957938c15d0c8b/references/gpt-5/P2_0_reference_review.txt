Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models where the transition function is unknown and modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP). The key idea is to marginalize the transition function f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over latent trajectories, and then to perform fully Bayesian inference over the latent states and hyperparameters using Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This avoids the common workaround of turning the GP into a finite-dimensional parametric model. The paper also presents an efficient implementation with Cholesky updates (O(T^3)) and a sparse variant using FIC inducing variables to reduce complexity to O(M^2 T). Experiments include a 1D nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole system.

The work relates to: GP filtering/smoothing with known dynamics (Deisenroth et al.), GPDM/MAP/EM-style approaches that treat the GP in a parametric manner (Wang et al., Turner et al.), and PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) with the recent PGAS kernel (Lindsten et al.). The novelty here is the collapsed treatment of the GP dynamics in a GP-SSM and the tailored use of PGAS for the induced non-Markovian latent process, combined with computationally efficient implementations and a sparse prior option.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Methodological soundness: Strong. Collapsing f removes strong x–f dependencies and is statistically principled. The use of PGAS provides a Markov kernel invariant to the exact smoothing distribution for any N ≥ 2; this addresses path degeneracy and is well-justified by prior theory. The derivations for the collapsed prior over trajectories (product of GP one-step conditionals) are correct, and the complexity analysis is reasonable. Slice sampling for θx after collapsing f is appropriate and standard in GP settings.
- Algorithmic detail: The paper gives enough detail to implement the main steps, including how to compute ancestor sampling weights in the non-Markovian setting and how to maintain factorizations to achieve O(T^3) or O(M^2 T).
- Empirical support: Positive but limited. The scalar benchmark convincingly illustrates multimodal smoothing and robustness to a misspecified mean function. The cart-pole example shows sensible one-step predictive uncertainty. However, quantitative comparisons are sparse: no head-to-head against EM/MAP GP-SSM variants or variational/EP methods, limited ablations on particle number N, chain length, or sparse-GP settings, and no runtime/memory reports. The claim of “very good mixing” would benefit from ESS/autocorrelation diagnostics and sensitivity to N and T.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. The exposition of the collapsed prior (Eq. 3–4) and the PGAS kernel is careful, and the implementation section usefully explains how to reuse computations.
- Areas for improvement: Some notation is dense; a brief pseudo-code of the CPF-AS step in the appendix or a schematic for the matrix updates would help reproducibility. Figures and tables are referenced but not fully shown here; ensure they communicate quantitative performance clearly. Some references are incomplete; provide full bibliographic details.

Originality
- The combination of: (i) exact marginalization of the GP transition function within a GP-SSM, (ii) inference over the resulting non-Markovian latent process with PGAS, and (iii) an efficient sparse-GP prior option is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior GP-SSM work that used EM/MAP or assumed learned dynamics. The nonparametric, fully Bayesian treatment distinguishes it from GPDM and related approaches.

Significance
- Scientifically meaningful: Provides a principled way to learn GP-SSMs without resorting to parametric surrogates or point estimates, handling multimodal posteriors over trajectories and properly propagating uncertainty into learned dynamics.
- Practical impact: Potentially valuable for time-series modeling and control where model uncertainty matters. However, scalability may limit adoption to small/medium T and low/moderate state dimensions, even with FIC; demonstrating performance for larger T/d and reporting wall-clock times would strengthen the case.

Pros
- Conceptually clean collapsed inference that preserves the nonparametric nature of the GP dynamics.
- Use of PGAS is well-motivated; correct invariant target and typically good mixing.
- Solid implementation insights (Cholesky updates; O(T^3) and O(M^2 T) with FIC).
- Handles multimodal smoothing; demonstrates robustness to mean-function misspecification.
- Clear route to posterior predictive dynamics as a mixture of Gaussians; discussion of single-GP approximation for downstream use.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is limited: few baselines, no direct comparison to EM/MAP/variational GP-SSM or GPDM, no ablations/mixing diagnostics, and no runtime/memory scaling.
- Practical guidance on choosing M and inducing inputs is minimal; no analysis of sensitivity to inducing set selection.
- Focuses on known observation model g; scope is narrower when g is unknown or misspecified.
- Some notational density may hinder reproducibility for nonspecialists.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please add quantitative comparisons to MAP/EM-based GP-SSM (e.g., Turner et al.) and GPDM, and include runtime and mixing diagnostics (ESS, autocorr) vs. N, T, and dimension.
- Provide multi-step predictive performance and long-horizon rollout behavior, not only one-step RMSE.
- Report sensitivity to the mean function choice, Q prior, and to the number/placement of inducing inputs; consider an automatic inducing-input selection procedure.
- Include memory and time complexity constants and practical limits (e.g., max T for a given N, M).
- Complete references and ensure figures/tables include error bars and clear baselines.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a principled and well-executed Bayesian approach to GP-SSMs that preserves nonparametric flexibility via collapsing and leverages PGAS for tractable inference. While the current experiments are not comprehensive, the methodology is solid, and the contribution is novel and potentially impactful. Strengthening the empirical section as suggested would make a clear accept.
