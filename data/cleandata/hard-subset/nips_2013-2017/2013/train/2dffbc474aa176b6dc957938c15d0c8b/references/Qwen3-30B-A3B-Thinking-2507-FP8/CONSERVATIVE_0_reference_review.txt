

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) methods, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). The authors argue that their approach retains the nonparametric expressivity of GP-SSMs while avoiding the need for parametric approximations of the dynamics, which they claim is superior to existing methods that either fix the dynamics model or use parametric approximations of the GP.

The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by presenting a marginalization approach that avoids the Markovian structure loss when marginalizing the dynamics function. The computational complexity analysis for both vanilla and sparse GP implementations is sound, with the authors demonstrating O(T³) complexity for the vanilla GP and O(M²T) for the sparse implementation using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior. The experimental section includes two synthetic benchmarks and a cart-pole system, showing the method's ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions and capture uncertainty in state-space.

The paper demonstrates good technical understanding of Bayesian inference in state-space models and presents a well-structured mathematical formulation. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from previous work by Deisenroth, Turner, and Wang, who used parametric approximations of the GP dynamics.

However, the paper falls short in several critical areas. First, the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claim of methodological superiority. The authors compare their method only to simple parametric models (true model structure, linear model) and their own baseline model B, but do not compare against state-of-the-art methods for GP-SSM learning. For instance, Deisenroth et al. have developed sophisticated approximate inference methods for GP-SSMs that could serve as more relevant baselines. The paper's claim that "our approach provides a vital component needed for these inference methods" lacks evidence of actual performance improvements over existing approaches.

Second, the paper's computational complexity claim for the sparse implementation appears questionable. The authors state that the sparse GP implementation achieves O(M²T) complexity, but the detailed derivation in Section 4.2 suggests this might be overly optimistic without addressing the additional computational burden of maintaining the FIC approximation across time steps.

Third, the paper's experimental results are limited to two synthetic benchmarks and a single physical system (cart-pole), which is insufficient to demonstrate the method's general applicability. The authors report RMSE values but fail to provide statistical significance testing or error bars for their comparisons, making it difficult to assess the reliability of their claims.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the method's limitations. For example, the computational complexity of the PGAS algorithm grows with the number of particles N, and while the authors note that "a moderate N is enough," they provide no empirical evidence of how the algorithm scales with increasing time horizon T or state dimensionality.

Regarding originality, the core idea of marginalizing out the GP dynamics is not entirely novel. The authors cite Andrieu et al. on PMCMC, but the specific application to GP-SSMs with marginalization is not as groundbreaking as claimed. The use of FIC for sparse GP is a standard technique (as referenced in the paper's own citations to Snelson and Ghahramani), and the paper does not demonstrate how their specific implementation differs meaningfully from existing sparse GP approaches.

The significance of the contribution is limited by the lack of compelling evidence that the proposed method provides substantial improvements over established approaches. While the ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions is interesting, the paper does not demonstrate how this translates to better predictive performance in practical applications, which is the ultimate goal of such modeling approaches.

In summary, while the paper presents a technically sound approach with solid theoretical foundations, the lack of rigorous experimental validation against relevant baselines, limited scope of experiments, and overstatement of the method's novelty and significance prevent it from meeting NeurIPS standards. The authors would need to substantially expand their experimental section to include comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, provide more detailed analysis of computational complexity, and demonstrate the practical advantages of their approach across a wider range of benchmarks.

Strengths: Solid theoretical foundation, clear mathematical exposition, good discussion of computational complexity, well-organized presentation.

Weaknesses: Insufficient experimental validation, limited comparison to relevant baselines, questionable computational complexity claims for sparse implementation, lack of discussion of method limitations.

Recommendation: Reject. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of methodological advancement over existing approaches. While the technical content is sound, the lack of rigorous validation against established baselines and the limited scope of experiments fail to demonstrate the significance of the contribution for NeurIPS.
