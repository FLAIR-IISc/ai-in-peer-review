This paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function using a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm. Existing methods typically learn the GP dynamics with a fixed model, while this work aims for a fully nonparametric approach. This builds upon a rich literature in GP-SSMs, referencing work by Deisenroth et al. [5, 6], Turner et al. [2], and Wang et al. [8], positioning itself as a vital component for learning the GP model *before* applying filtering and smoothing techniques. The approach addresses challenges associated with non-Markovian dependencies arising from marginalization, utilizing recent advancements in PMCMC samplers [3, 4]. The inclusion of a sparse GP variant for computational efficiency (using the FIC prior [17]) and demonstration on a nonlinear system benchmark and a cart-pole system adds practical relevance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a mathematically well-defined framework. The derivation of the marginalization process and the associated equations (3-4) are clear. The use of PMCMC, specifically PGAS [4], is appropriate for the problem and justified. The implementation details regarding computational efficiency (Cholesky factorization reuse) are well-considered and demonstrate a strong understanding of the practical challenges.
*   **Novelty:**  Marginalizing out the state transition function in GP-SSMs is a significant contribution. Most prior work focuses on learning the GP *after* fixing the model structure. This work tackles the more general and challenging problem of learning the GP dynamics themselves in a fully Bayesian manner. The use of PGAS for this specific problem is also novel.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation and context. The mathematical notation, while dense, is consistent and well-explained. The description of the algorithms is detailed enough to understand the key steps.
*   **Significance:** The approach has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in GP-SSM learning. The ability to handle nonlinearity and uncertainty effectively, as demonstrated in the experiments, is valuable. The method’s applicability to reinforcement learning, as shown with the cart-pole example, further increases its significance.  The framework allows for incorporation of prior knowledge through the GP mean function.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the paper addresses computational complexity with the FIC prior, the description of its implementation is somewhat brief. The benefits and tradeoffs of the FIC approach (e.g., choosing the number and placement of inducing points) could be discussed more thoroughly.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are limited in scope. The "nonlinear system benchmark" uses a single set of parameters, and while informative, it doesn't provide a comprehensive assessment of the method’s robustness across different system dynamics. A comparison to other state-of-the-art SSM learning methods (beyond the listed parametric approaches) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices, such as the number of particles (N) in the PMCMC sampler. A brief sensitivity analysis would improve the reproducibility and understanding of the method.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of a known observation model (g) is stated, but its impact on the overall performance isn't fully explored.  A discussion of scenarios where the observation model is also unknown would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging and important problem in SSM learning.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and novel.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited.
*   The computational complexity, while addressed, could benefit from a more detailed discussion.
*   The sensitivity to hyperparameter choices isn't analyzed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of GP-SSM learning. The approach is technically sound, novel, and well-motivated. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the method. The clarity of the writing and the thoroughness of the technical details are commendable.  I recommend acceptance.
