This paper presents a compelling, and genuinely novel, approach to learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by fully marginalizing out the transition dynamics. This is a significant departure from existing literature, which largely relies on parametric approximations once the GP is involved, and unlocks the full nonparametric potential of the GP-SSM framework. The core idea – leveraging recent advances in Particle MCMC to efficiently sample the smoothing distribution *without* a fixed dynamical model – is both elegant and powerful. The experimental results, while not exhaustive, are promising and demonstrate the capability of the approach to handle challenging multimodal scenarios and outperform existing methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core contribution—fully Bayesian inference and learning in GP-SSMs via marginalization of the transition function—is highly novel. This distinguishes it from the existing GP-SSM literature, which typically focuses on inference *given* a learned or fixed GP model.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the marginal prior and the adaptation of PMCMC for this specific problem appear theoretically sound. The connection to existing work on particle filtering and smoothing is clearly articulated.
*   **Potential Impact:** Enabling fully nonparametric GP-SSMs has the potential to significantly advance the field of time-series modeling and dynamical systems. It provides a flexible framework that can adapt to complex, unknown dynamics without imposing strong parametric assumptions.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The paper provides a concrete algorithmic framework (PGAS) with detailed implementation considerations for efficiency (sparse GPs).
*   **Demonstrated Capability:** The experiments, despite being limited in scope, showcase the ability of the approach to handle a challenging multimodal system and learn from limited data. The cart-pole example further illustrates applicability to a more complex real-world problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section, while promising, is somewhat limited. The comparison is primarily against a single baseline model (Model B) and lacks a more thorough evaluation against state-of-the-art GP-SSM methods that *do* use parametric approximations. The performance gains are not overwhelmingly demonstrated.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors discuss optimization strategies for the computational cost, the algorithm remains computationally demanding, particularly for long time series or high-dimensional state spaces. The scalability to larger, more realistic problems needs to be further investigated.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of PMCMC algorithms is often sensitive to hyperparameter settings. The paper doesn’t fully address the sensitivity of the method to choices of particle number, proposal distributions, and other PMCMC-related parameters.
*   **FIC Prior Detail:** While the introduction of a sparse GP (FIC prior) is good, there's little discussion of how the inducing points were selected/optimized. This choice can heavily impact performance.
*   **Lack of Real-World Datasets**: The presented experiments are primarily synthetic. Applying this method to real-world time-series data would strengthen the paper considerably.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is truly novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in existing GP-SSM approaches.
*   The theoretical framework is well-developed and provides a principled way to perform fully Bayesian inference in these models.
*   The authors demonstrate the feasibility of the approach with a working implementation and promising experimental results.
*   The paper opens up new avenues for research in nonparametric dynamical systems modeling.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The empirical evaluation is limited and doesn't provide strong evidence of superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   The computational complexity of the algorithm may limit its applicability to practical problems.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity could make the method difficult to use in practice without careful tuning.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a highly original and promising contribution to the field of GP-SSMs. While the empirical evaluation is somewhat limited and the computational complexity is a concern, the novelty and theoretical soundness of the approach are compelling. I believe the benefits of unlocking the full nonparametric potential of GP-SSMs outweigh the current limitations. I lean towards **acceptance**, as the ideas presented are genuinely innovative and could have a significant impact on the field. I would strongly encourage the authors to expand the experimental evaluation and address the concerns regarding computational complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity in future work. Minor revisions addressing these points would further strengthen the paper.
