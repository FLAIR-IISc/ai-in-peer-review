Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to learning nonlinear Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) where the state transition function f is given a GP prior and is analytically marginalized. This induces a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories. The authors then perform inference over states and hyperparameters using Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), which they argue mixes well despite the induced long-range dependencies. They further describe implementation details to reduce computational complexity from O(T^3) (via Cholesky updates) and a sparse variant using the FIC prior to get O(M^2 T). After smoothing, they obtain a posterior over f (a mixture of GP predictions over PGAS samples) and suggest a pragmatic single-GP approximation for downstream tasks. Experiments include a 1D nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole system.

This work is positioned against (i) EM/MAP approaches to GP-based dynamical models (e.g., Turner et al.; Wang et al.’s GPDM) which introduce parametric approximations or point estimates, (ii) GP-based filtering/smoothing given learned dynamics (Deisenroth et al.), and (iii) standard SMC/MCMC for parametric SSMs. Using PGAS in a collapsed GP-SSM appears to be the novel combination; the work also leverages established sparse GP approximations (FIC).

Strengths:
- Methodologically principled: collapsing f is sound and avoids parametric approximations; using PGAS is a careful choice for non-Markovian latents.
- Clear connection to standard GP regression once x0:T is sampled, and use of conjugate structure where possible.
- Useful implementation insights: Cholesky rank-1 updates/downdates; expressing ancestor sampling weights using the collapsed prior; FIC-based complexity reduction and caching strategies.
- The model can incorporate prior knowledge through the GP mean function, mitigating non-identifiability to some extent.
- Demonstrations show the method can represent multimodal smoothing and heteroscedastic predictive uncertainty.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Limited empirical validation. Only two datasets are reported (a 1D toy system and cart-pole). There is no quantitative comparison to established baselines for GP-SSMs such as GPDM (Wang et al.), variational GP-SSMs, EM-based methods (Turner et al.), or more recent approximate inference methods (e.g., EP/variational approaches by Deisenroth/Mohamed). The cart-pole experiment shows qualitative one-step-ahead performance without quantitative metrics, rollout evaluation, or control implications.
- Claims about “very good mixing” are not substantiated with diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, R-hat, multiple chains, trace plots). For T=200 and N=20 particles, PGAS mixing can be delicate; evidence is needed. For the sparse variant, there is no study of the effect of M or inducing input placement on accuracy and mixing.
- Scalability remains unclear. Although the paper argues O(T^3) or O(M^2 T) complexity, wall-clock timings, memory usage, and scaling with state dimension nx, sequence length T, and particle number N are not reported. Non-Markovian CPF-AS with ancestor sampling weights p(x̃t:T | x0:t−1) can be expensive in practice; the constants may be large.
- Proposal distribution in SMC is underspecified. The text suggests sampling from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t) (the optimal proposal), but no derivation/implementation details are provided; if instead the prior predictive p(xt | θ, x0:t−1) is used, this should be made explicit and the potential loss in efficiency discussed. This is important for both correctness and performance.
- Assumption that g is known. While common in some engineering settings, many applications require learning both f and g. The authors mention non-identifiability but do not empirically explore sensitivity to misspecification of g or the impact of known-but-noisy sensor parameters.
- Hyperparameter inference lacks detail. Priors, slice sampling settings, convergence checks, and sensitivity analyses are not reported. Given the strong influence of kernel hyperparameters and Q on both the smoothing and the inferred f, this is a notable omission.
- Inducing input selection is left unaddressed. In sparse GP-SSMs, performance and calibration can depend heavily on inducing input placement. A simple greedy scheme is mentioned but not evaluated; no ablation between dense vs sparse is reported beyond a single FIC setting.
- Evaluation metrics are narrow. The 1D benchmark reports RMSE against a long test trajectory for f(xt, ut), but the table is not reproduced here; it is unclear how uncertainty calibration, long-horizon predictive performance, or robustness to multimodality are assessed. For cart-pole, results are qualitative; no comparisons to physics-based models or black-box baselines are given.

Quality:
The core methodology is technically sound and consistent with GP and PMCMC theory. However, critical empirical and algorithmic details are missing or ambiguous (proposal choice; diagnostics; runtime), and the experimental evidence is not sufficient to support the strong claims about efficiency and mixing.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but some derivations are dense, and key algorithmic choices are left implicit (proposal distribution; exact computations of ancestor weights with y0:T). The experiments section lacks sufficient quantitative detail for reproducibility (hyperparameters, priors, kernel forms, N, M sensitivity).

Originality:
Combining PGAS with a collapsed GP-SSM is a novel and interesting synthesis relative to EM/MAP approaches and GPDM. The use of FIC within this collapsed, non-Markovian PGAS framework is also a useful engineering contribution.

Significance:
Potentially significant if the approach can be shown to scale and outperform established baselines. As presented, the empirical support is too limited to conclude that this is a step forward in practice.

Questions for the authors:
- What proposal distribution is actually used in the CPF-AS? If the optimal proposal p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t) is used, please provide derivations and computational cost. If not, report performance differences.
- Provide mixing diagnostics (ACF, ESS per iteration, R-hat across multiple chains) and timing vs N, T, nx. How sensitive is mixing to multimodality in the 1D task?
- How are inducing inputs chosen for FIC? Please report M-sensitivity and a comparison to the dense model.
- How sensitive are results to the GP mean function specification (e.g., model B vs less informative mean)? Does the prior dominate for small T?
- Can you compare to GPDM/variational GP-SSM/EM-based methods on the 1D and cart-pole tasks, including long-horizon rollouts and uncertainty calibration?

Pros:
- Principled Bayesian treatment; avoids parametric approximations of f.
- Clever use of PGAS and exact GP conditionals; solid implementation insights.
- Handles multimodal smoothing and yields uncertainty-aware predictions.

Cons:
- Insufficient experimental validation and baseline comparisons.
- Missing mixing diagnostics and runtime scaling.
- Underspecified SMC proposal and practical details.
- Sparse variant not adequately evaluated; inducing input selection left open.
- Assumes known g; sensitivity to misspecification not studied.

Recommendation:
Reject. The methodological idea is solid and potentially impactful, but the paper lacks the rigorous empirical validation, diagnostic evidence, and comparative analysis against established baselines expected for NeurIPS. Strengthening the experiments, clarifying algorithmic details (proposal choice), and providing thorough scaling/mixing studies would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence: High.
