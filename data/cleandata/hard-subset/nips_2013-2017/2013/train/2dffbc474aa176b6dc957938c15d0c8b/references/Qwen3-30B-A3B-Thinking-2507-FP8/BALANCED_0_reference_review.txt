

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of fully Bayesian learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), where the state transition function is modeled as a Gaussian process. The authors propose marginalizing out the transition function f from the model, enabling fully Bayesian inference over the state trajectory and hyperparameters while preserving the nonparametric nature of the GP. This contrasts with prior work that often resorts to parametric approximations of the GP (e.g., [2, 8]) or requires fixed dynamics for smoothing [5, 6]. The authors develop a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to efficiently sample from the smoothing distribution, addressing the non-Markovian structure introduced by marginalizing f. They also present computational improvements using sparse GP approximations with the FIC prior to achieve O(M²T) complexity.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical foundation. The derivation of the marginal prior p(x₀:T | θ) (Equation 3-4) is well-motivated and clearly presented. The authors effectively address the computational challenges through careful implementation details (Section 4.2) and provide a thorough complexity analysis. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world systems (cart-pole) demonstrates the method's effectiveness, with the GP-SSM outperforming parametric baselines in capturing complex dynamics.

**Originality:** The core contribution of marginalizing the state transition function to maintain full nonparametric expressivity is novel and well-positioned against prior work. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods (e.g., [2, 5, 6, 8]), particularly noting that their work provides a "vital component needed for these inference methods" (Section 1). The adaptation of PGAS to this specific problem is also well-justified.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a significant limitation in the GP-SSM literature, where most approaches require parametric approximations. The ability to perform fully Bayesian learning while preserving the GP's flexibility is valuable for applications requiring uncertainty quantification in dynamical systems. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate advantages over parametric alternatives, particularly in handling multimodal smoothing distributions (Figure 2).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and logically structured. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1) are clear. Figures 2 and 3 effectively illustrate the method's capabilities in capturing complex dynamics and uncertainty.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Complexity Claims:** While the paper claims O(T³) complexity for the vanilla GP and O(M²T) for the sparse version, the justification is somewhat hand-wavy. The paper states that "reusing computations across time" achieves these complexities, but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how this is implemented in practice to justify the claimed complexity improvements.

**Limited Comparison to Bayesian Baselines:** The paper compares primarily to parametric models and the ground truth, but doesn't include a comparison to other Bayesian approaches for GP-SSM learning. For instance, it would be valuable to compare against the Bayesian approach in [5] (which focuses on inference for already-learned dynamics) to highlight the advantage of their learning framework.

**Sparse GP Implementation Details:** While the paper introduces the FIC prior, it doesn't sufficiently address the practical challenge of choosing inducing points. The authors mention "greedy methods" but don't discuss how this interacts with the MCMC sampling or provide guidance on selecting M for different problem sizes.

**High-Dimensional State Spaces:** The paper doesn't adequately address how the method scales to high-dimensional state spaces, which is a common challenge in real-world applications. The computational complexity claims assume the state dimension is fixed, but this becomes increasingly problematic as the dimension increases.

## Recommendations

1. **Strengthen Computational Complexity Analysis:** Provide more concrete details about how the O(T³) complexity is achieved in practice, particularly regarding the Cholesky factorization updates. A brief complexity analysis for the sparse version would be valuable.

2. **Expand Experimental Comparison:** Include a comparison with a Bayesian baseline method (e.g., the approach from [5]) to better demonstrate the value of their learning framework. This would strengthen the significance claim.

3. **Address Inducing Point Selection:** Discuss practical strategies for selecting inducing points, including how this might interact with the MCMC sampling process. Consider adding a small experiment showing sensitivity to M.

4. **Discuss High-Dimensional Limitations:** Add a brief discussion on how the method scales to high-dimensional state spaces, and perhaps include a simple experiment demonstrating this (e.g., a 2D state space versus 4D).

## Conclusion

This is a technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on GP-SSMs by enabling fully Bayesian learning while preserving the model's nonparametric expressivity. The marginalization approach is novel and well-justified, and the computational improvements are valuable for practical application. While the paper has some minor weaknesses in the complexity analysis and experimental comparison, these can be addressed with reasonable revisions. The work is of high quality, original, and significant for the field, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points above.
