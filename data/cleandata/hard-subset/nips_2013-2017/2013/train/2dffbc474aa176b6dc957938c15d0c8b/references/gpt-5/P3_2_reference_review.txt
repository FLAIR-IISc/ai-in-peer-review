Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior over the transition function f, marginalize f analytically to obtain a non-Markovian prior over the latent trajectory x0:T, and then perform inference over states and hyperparameters with a Particle MCMC scheme, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This collapses the highly correlated function-state posterior and exploits PGAS to mitigate path degeneracy and enable efficient smoothing. The authors also introduce a sparse variant using the FIC prior to reduce computational cost from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and provide practical implementation details (Cholesky rank-1 updates/downdates) to keep CPF-AS efficient. Learning of f is obtained post hoc by mixing standard GP posteriors over draws from p(x0:T, θ | y).

This work sits at the intersection of GP-based dynamical models and PMCMC. It differs from previous GP-SSM approaches such as GPDM [Wang et al. 2005] and EM/MAP-based methods [Turner et al., Ghahramani & Roweis] by maintaining the nonparametric character of f via analytic marginalization rather than parametrization or point estimates. It complements GPDS filtering/smoothing work [Deisenroth et al.] by providing the missing Bayesian learning component. On the PMCMC side, it leverages PGAS [Lindsten et al.] to handle the induced non-Markovian dependencies after marginalizing f. The paper is closely related to contemporaneous efforts on collapsing GP dynamics within PMCMC for SSMs; please make explicit how the present contributions go beyond those (e.g., new complexity reductions, specific CPF-AS construction for GP-SSMs, or theoretical guarantees).

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean and principled: collapsing f eliminates strong posterior dependencies and respects the nonparametric model.
- Methodologically sound use of PGAS for non-Markovian latent-variable models; correct invariance and ergodicity properties.
- Practical algorithmic contributions: detailed discussion of computational bottlenecks and how to reuse Cholesky factorizations; extension to sparse GP (FIC) with clear complexity scaling.
- Sensible modeling choices: using informative mean functions for f and acknowledging identifiability issues with overly flexible g.
- Experiments demonstrate handling of multimodal smoothing (sign ambiguity) and a 4D cart-pole example with state-dependent uncertainty.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Proposal distribution in SMC: Eq. (5) states sampling from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t). Unless the measurement is Gaussian with simple g, sampling from the “locally optimal” proposal is nontrivial. In practice, did you use the prior proposal p(xt | θ, x0:t−1) with weights p(yt | xt), or an approximate optimal proposal? Please clarify the exact proposal used in both vanilla and CPF-AS variants, and its computational impact.
- Computational complexity and constants: The big-O counts elide dependence on the number of particles N, state dimension nx, and hyperparameter updates. Maintaining per-particle factorizations suggests at least O(N T^2) per time step without further sharing; please clarify the dependence on N and nx, and how memory scales. For the FIC method, how are costs affected when θ is resampled (necessitating updates of P and related caches)?
- Inducing inputs: The paper postpones inducing input selection and suggests greedy selection with hyperparameters fixed “for a given run of the particle filter.” But θ is updated within PGAS iterations; how do you avoid reselecting inducing points or degrading approximation when θ changes? Please describe the schedule you used and the sensitivity to M and inducing locations.
- Identifiability and prior specification: There is a well-known trade-off between GP amplitude/lengthscale and process noise Q. What priors were used (beyond “log-normal”) and how sensitive are posterior inferences to these priors? Some discussion or a sensitivity study would strengthen the Bayesian story.
- Convergence diagnostics: The claim of “very good mixing” is not substantiated. The experiments use N=20 and 50 iterations; for a highly multimodal smoother this is likely insufficient. Please report trace plots, effective sample sizes, autocorrelation, and wall-clock times. An ablation varying N, T, and PGAS components (with/without ancestor sampling) would be informative.
- Experimental evaluation: The 1D benchmark is appropriate for illustrating multimodality, but the predictive RMSE table is referenced without numbers; include it and ensure fair baselines (e.g., GPDM, EM/MAP GP-SSMs, variational GP-SSMs). For the cart-pole, provide quantitative one-step and multi-step roll-out errors, and comparisons to learned parametric SSMs or GP-based baselines; visual means/variances alone are insufficient. Also evaluate the sparse FIC variant on cart-pole to demonstrate scalability benefits.
- Scope limitation: Assuming known g is often reasonable, but many applications require learning g as well. Discuss identifiability constraints (e.g., structural priors, informative sensors) and whether your collapsing strategy can be extended to marginalize both f and g (perhaps with complementary priors) or whether PGAS remains effective when both are unknown.
- Notational clarity: Eq. (3)–(4) is dense. A compact pseudo-code for the CPF-AS specialized to the GP-SSM, explicitly showing what is cached and how updates are computed, would help reproducibility. Clearly state dimensions, indexing conventions for K, and how inputs ut are included in kernels.

Quality:
Technically solid overall; the use of collapsing and PGAS is appropriate and derivations are consistent. The complexity arguments are plausible but need clearer dependence on N and nx, and more details on proposals and numerical stability of ancestor weights p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1). Experimental support is promising but currently insufficiently detailed to back claims of efficiency and mixing.

Clarity:
Well written and organized. The implementation section is valuable. However, explicit algorithm boxes (CPF-AS instantiation) and additional experimental details (priors, proposals, diagnostics) would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Originality:
The combination—analytically marginalizing GP dynamics in SSMs and using PGAS for smoothing and learning—is impactful. Please clarify novelty relative to closely related contemporaneous work applying PMCMC to GP-SSMs and identify what is new here (e.g., the sparse FIC construction in the collapsed setting and the specific factorization/reuse strategy).

Significance:
If properly validated, the approach advances Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs by removing the need for parametric surrogates or point estimates, and enabling credible smoothing under model uncertainty. The sparse variant and implementation guidance increase practical impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, fully Bayesian treatment preserving nonparametric expressivity.
- Effective use of PGAS for non-Markovian smoothing; sound theory.
- Practical computational contributions; sparse alternative.
- Addresses multimodality; clear modeling narrative.

Cons for acceptance:
- Ambiguity about proposal distributions and complexity scaling with N/nx.
- Limited and partially reported experiments; lack of convergence diagnostics and strong baselines.
- Unclear novelty relative to closely related prior work; missing citations may exist.
- Inducing point policy and hyperparameter–sparsity interaction underspecified.

Overall recommendation:
Leaning toward accept if the authors strengthen the empirical section (complete tables, diagnostics, baselines), clarify algorithmic choices (proposals, complexity, inducing points), and position the contribution clearly against closely related prior work. Otherwise, this is a strong and promising submission that could significantly influence GP-SSM learning practice.
