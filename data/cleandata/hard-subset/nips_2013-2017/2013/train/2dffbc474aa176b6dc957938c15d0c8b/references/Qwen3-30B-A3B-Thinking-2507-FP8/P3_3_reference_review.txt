

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

## Summary
This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to learning nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) using Gaussian process (GP) priors for the state transition function. The key innovation is marginalizing out the GP function f from the model, allowing for nonparametric inference while enabling sampling from the smoothing distribution p(x₀:T | y₀:T) without requiring a fixed model of the dynamics. The authors develop a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to handle the non-Markovian structure introduced by marginalization, and introduce a sparse GP-SSM construction using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to reduce computational complexity from O(T⁴) to O(M²T). The work builds on the PMCMC framework [3,4] and extends the GP-SSM literature [5,6,8], providing a crucial component for learning GP dynamics in SSMs.

## Strengths

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, the core idea of marginalizing out the state transition function f is theoretically sound and addresses a significant gap in the literature. Previous approaches [2,8] typically require parametric approximations of the GP, while this work maintains the full nonparametric expressivity of the GP model. The derivation of the marginal prior p(x₀:T | θ) in Section 3.1 is particularly elegant, showing how the GP's predictive distribution can be expressed as a product of Gaussians despite the non-Gaussian nature of the overall distribution.

The computational efficiency claims are well-supported. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the O(T³) complexity for the vanilla GP and O(M²T) for the sparse GP, with detailed implementation insights in Section 4.2 that demonstrate how to efficiently reuse Cholesky factorizations across time steps. This is a significant practical contribution that addresses a major barrier to applying GP-SSMs at scale.

The experimental evaluation is strong, particularly in the synthetic benchmark (Section 6.1) where the method successfully handles a multimodal smoothing distribution. The comparison with a misspecified parametric mean function (model B) demonstrates the flexibility of the GP-SSM, and the sparse GP-SSM results show good performance with only 40 inducing points. The cart-pole application (Section 6.2) convincingly demonstrates the method's ability to capture uncertainty in different regions of the state space.

## Weaknesses

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the claim about "very good mixing" enabled by PMCMC samplers lacks quantitative evidence. The paper mentions good mixing but provides no comparison of mixing rates with alternative samplers or a demonstration of the number of iterations required for convergence. This is a significant omission for a method relying on MCMC.

The sparse GP-SSM evaluation is limited. While they use 40 inducing points, they don't explore how the choice of M affects the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy. A more thorough analysis of this trade-off, including a plot of RMSE vs. M, would strengthen the sparse GP claims.

The comparison with related work is insufficient. The paper doesn't compare with other Bayesian nonparametric approaches for SSMs, such as those in [5,6,15], which would better establish the novelty and advantages of their method. The comparison with parametric models is useful, but the paper doesn't address how their approach compares to other Bayesian methods for learning dynamics.

The paper mentions the risk of overfitting in high-dimensional state spaces but doesn't provide evidence that their method avoids this issue. For the 4-dimensional cart-pole system, they don't discuss how the method would scale to higher dimensions, which is a critical concern for real-world applications.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric state-space modeling. The core idea of marginalizing out the GP function is novel and well-executed, and the computational efficiency claims are well-supported. However, the paper would be significantly stronger with more quantitative evidence about the mixing properties of the PGAS sampler, a more thorough analysis of the sparse GP trade-offs, and a more comprehensive comparison with related Bayesian nonparametric approaches.

The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, but the paper needs to address these concerns before acceptance. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a significant problem in the field. It represents a meaningful advance in the Bayesian learning of nonlinear dynamical systems.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with major revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above)

The paper has strong potential to be an important contribution to the field, but the current version lacks sufficient empirical evidence to fully support its claims about mixing properties and the sparse GP trade-offs. Addressing these issues would make this a compelling contribution to NeurIPS.
