

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper proposes a Bayesian approach for learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC). The key innovation is marginalizing out the state transition function f, allowing for fully Bayesian inference without resorting to parametric approximations. The authors present a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to handle the resulting non-Markovian state process.

The paper builds on existing work in GP-SSMs (e.g., Deisenroth et al. [5, 6], Turner et al. [2]) but makes a significant contribution by addressing the challenge of learning the state transition function f without assuming a parametric form. The approach differs from previous methods that use finite target points for the GP (Turner et al. [2]) or MAP estimation (Wang et al. [8]).

**Strengths:**
1. The marginalization approach elegantly preserves the nonparametric nature of the GP model.
2. The PGAS implementation is well-motivated and addresses the non-Markovian structure introduced by marginalizing f.
3. The sparse GP extension using FIC prior provides a computationally efficient alternative.
4. The experimental results demonstrate the model's ability to capture multimodal smoothing distributions and learn complex dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper suffers from severe notational inconsistencies throughout. For example:
   - In equation (2a), the covariance function notation uses "x't" instead of "x'_t" or "x_t'" consistently.
   - Equations (3b) and (3c) have missing subscripts: "K̃ −1 0:t−2" should be "K̃^{-1}_{0:t-2}".
   - Equation (4) uses inconsistent spacing in "K̃0:t−1" which should be "K̃_{0:t-1}".
   - The notation "x i t" (equation 5) and "µ`(x∗)" (equation 10) lacks proper superscripts/subscripts, making equations difficult to parse.

2. **Computational Complexity Claims:** The paper states that "a naive implementation of the CPF-AS algorithm will give rise to O(T^4) computational complexity" but then claims to reduce it to O(T^3) through "reusing computations." The explanation is insufficiently detailed to verify this claim. The paper should provide a clear complexity analysis of the matrix factorization updates.

3. **Algorithmic Details:** Algorithm 1 contains inconsistent notation: "x1:T [`] = xk1:T" should be "x^{[l]}_{1:T} = x^k_{1:T}". Additionally, the description of ancestor sampling (equation 6) lacks proper mathematical notation for the path concatenation.

4. **Experimental Verification:** The paper references "Figure 2" and "Table 1" but provides neither in the text. Without these, it's impossible to assess the experimental results or validate the RMSE claims. The paper should either include these elements or provide sufficient textual description.

5. **Technical Depth in Key Claims:** The paper claims "the marginal prior p(θ,x0:T) can be represented as a product of Gaussians" but equation (3a) is presented without sufficient justification for why this holds despite the nonlinear dependencies. The claim that "this expression looks very much like a multivariate Gaussian density function" is misleading since the mean and covariance depend nonlinearly on x1:t.

6. **Comparison to Previous Work:** While the paper references Turner et al. [2], it doesn't sufficiently clarify how their approach differs from the one proposed here. The paper states that "Turner et al. [2] applied the EM algorithm to obtain a maximum likelihood estimate," but doesn't explicitly contrast their parametric approach with the fully Bayesian approach presented here.

7. **Reference Format:** Several references are incomplete or improperly formatted (e.g., "Gaussian Processes for Machine Learning, C.E. Rasmussen, C.K.I. Williams, , , ").

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs, the numerous notational inconsistencies, insufficient technical details regarding computational complexity, and missing experimental results significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The paper's core idea is promising, but the execution lacks the rigor expected at NIPS. The technical claims require more precise mathematical formulation to be properly evaluated.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address the notational issues, provide a more detailed complexity analysis, and include the referenced figures and tables. Without these corrections, the paper's contribution cannot be properly assessed.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper has significant technical flaws and presentation issues that prevent proper evaluation of its contribution. While the problem addressed is important, the paper fails to present a complete and rigorous solution. The notational inconsistencies alone make it difficult to assess the technical merit of the proposed methods.
