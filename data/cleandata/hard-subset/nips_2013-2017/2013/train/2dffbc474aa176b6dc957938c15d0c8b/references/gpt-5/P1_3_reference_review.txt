Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models where the transition function f is given a Gaussian process (GP) prior while the measurement model g is known (possibly parametric). The key idea is to collapse/marginalize f exactly, yielding a non-Markovian prior over the entire state trajectory p(x0:T | θ) that factorizes as a product of GP one-step predictive Gaussians. Posterior inference over states x0:T and hyperparameters θ is then performed with Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), which can handle non-Markovian latent models and provides an ergodic Markov kernel invariant to the exact smoothing distribution. Hyperparameters are sampled given a fixed trajectory using standard GP regression machinery; a sparse variant (FIC) is proposed to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). Implementation details focus on reusing Cholesky factorizations and rank-one updates/downdates, and on maintaining factorizations needed for ancestor sampling. After obtaining samples from p(x0:T, θ | y), the posterior over f at test inputs is a mixture of Gaussians via GP regression conditional on sampled trajectories. Experiments include a nonlinear scalar benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole system, demonstrating uncertainty-aware predictions.

This work is closely related to GP dynamical models (e.g., Wang et al., GPDM), EM/MAP-based learning of GP-SSMs (Turner et al.), and EP-based filtering/smoothing for learned GP models (Deisenroth et al.). The main difference is a fully Bayesian treatment that avoids finite parameterizations of f by collapsing it, coupled with PMCMC/PGAS to sample state trajectories under the induced non-Markovian prior. The ancestor-sampling PGAS kernel for non-Markovian models follows Lindsten et al., but its application to collapsed GP-SSMs with an explicit sparse prior and detailed implementation is new.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and principled: exact marginalization of f retains nonparametric expressivity while removing strong posterior coupling between f and x, enabling efficient sampling of x via PGAS.
- Solid algorithmic design: appropriate use of PGAS for non-Markovian latent structures, with clear invariance arguments and practical implementation details (Cholesky reuse, rank-one updates, ancestor weights).
- Sparse GP prior (FIC) integrated into the state trajectory prior, with complexity reductions and guidance for efficient implementation.
- The approach naturally handles multimodal smoothing distributions and yields a rich mixture-of-Gaussians posterior over f, reflecting epistemic uncertainty.
- The method leverages prior mean functions, facilitating incorporation of first-principles models.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is limited. There is no quantitative comparison of smoothing accuracy or predictive performance against strong baselines (EM/MAP GPDM, EP-based GP-SSM, parametric PMCMC smoothers, UKF/EKS), nor wall-clock/runtime scaling analyses. Claims about “very good mixing” are not backed by ESS/autocorrelation plots.
- Scalability concerns remain: per-iteration costs for PGAS with N particles and T steps are not fully quantified; the impact of state dimension nx on complexity and mixing is not explored; only a 4D real system is shown.
- Inducing point selection is left unspecified; performance sensitivity to M and inducing locations is not studied.
- The assumption of known g limits generality; discussion of identifiability or extension to unknown g (e.g., GP observation models) is brief.
- Some derivations (e.g., Eq. 3) are compactly presented; numerical stability of repeated downdates is not discussed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The collapsing derivation and non-Markovian prior construction are standard, and use of PGAS with ancestor sampling for non-Markovian models is appropriate. The sparse FIC construction is consistent with prior work. The approach is credible.
- Empirical support: Qualitative demonstrations are convincing, but quantitative evidence against baselines and scalability metrics are insufficient for a strong empirical claim at NeurIPS.
- Carefulness: The paper is generally careful in claims, but additional diagnostics and comparisons would strengthen it.

Clarity
- The paper is well-written overall, with clear problem setup and algorithmic description. However, some notational density (K, K̃, block indices) could be eased; a brief derivation of (3b–c) and an explicit statement of per-iteration complexity in terms of N, T, nx, M would help. A small algorithmic pseudocode box for CPF-AS specialized to this model would improve readability.

Originality
- Collapsing f in GP-SSMs and using PGAS to sample states under the resulting non-Markovian prior appears novel and is a meaningful advance beyond EM/MAP or fixed-model filtering/smoothing. Integrating an explicit sparse prior and detailing efficient updates also adds novelty.

Significance
- The method addresses an important problem—fully Bayesian learning and smoothing in nonlinear GP-SSMs—relevant to time-series modeling, control, and RL. If shown to scale and to outperform/match strong baselines, the contribution would be impactful. As-is, significance is promising but would benefit from stronger empirical validation.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide per-iteration complexity for a PGAS sweep: big-O in N, T, nx, and for the FIC case in M, and wall-clock times for your experiments.
- How are inducing locations chosen in practice, and how sensitive are results to M and location selection?
- Do you observe mixing issues on highly multimodal posteriors? Provide autocorrelation/ESS vs N and T.
- What kernel and hyperpriors are used? Please report them and the slice sampler settings.
- Can your framework accommodate an unknown g modeled by a GP? If so, how do you mitigate identifiability?
- How robust are results to mean-function misspecification? Provide an ablation sweeping mean functions.

Minor comments and suggestions
- Clarify that Σt includes Q via K̃; ensure positive definiteness under rank downdates and discuss numerical stabilization (e.g., jitter).
- Explain computation of p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) cost and how the proposed factor reuse reduces it.
- Provide more quantitative metrics: smoothing RMSE vs ground truth, predictive log likelihood, ablation on N/M/T.
- Consider including a real-world dataset beyond simulated cart-pole.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, fully Bayesian GP-SSM learning via collapsing and PGAS; novel and relevant.
- Handles multimodal smoothing and yields uncertainty-aware dynamics models.
- Solid algorithmic details and sparse variant for efficiency.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is relatively thin, with limited comparisons and no runtime/mixing diagnostics.
- Scalability to higher-dimensional states and longer sequences not convincingly demonstrated.
- Inducing point selection and sensitivity not addressed.

Recommendation: borderline accept (would be a clear accept with stronger empirical comparisons and scaling evidence).

Reviewer confidence: medium.
