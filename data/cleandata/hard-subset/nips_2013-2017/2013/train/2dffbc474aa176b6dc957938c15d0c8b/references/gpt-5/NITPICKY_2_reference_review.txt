Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) where the transition function f is given a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The key idea is to collapse out f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over the state trajectory p(x0:T | θ), and to target the smoothing posterior p(x0:T, θ | y0:T) using particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). Once samples of x0:T and θ are obtained, the posterior over f at test points is obtained via standard GP regression conditioned on each sample, yielding a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive density. To reduce cubic complexity, the paper also discusses a sparse GP prior (FIC) over f and implementation tricks (rank-one Cholesky updates/downdates) to achieve O(T^3) or O(M^2T) scaling. Experiments include a nonlinear scalar benchmark with sign ambiguity in observations and a 4D cart-pole system.

The work relates to GP-SSMs and GP-based dynamical models (e.g., Turner/Deisenroth/Rasmussen; Wang/Fleet/Hertzmann; Deisenroth et al. on GP filtering/smoothing). It departs from EM/MAP learning by fully marginalizing f and using PMCMC (Andrieu-Doucet-Holenstein), and leverages PGAS (Lindsten-Jordan-Schön) to mitigate path degeneracy in non-Markovian settings. The sparse construction follows the inducing-point literature (Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen; Snelson & Ghahramani).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and principled: collapsing f respects the nonparametric prior and avoids strong x–f posterior couplings that plague joint sampling.
- Sound use of PGAS to sample x0:T in a non-Markovian latent model with only a forward pass; correct invariance arguments via conditional PF with ancestor sampling.
- Useful implementation guidance (reuse of Cholesky factors; rank-1 updates; FIC complexity reductions).
- Sensible discussion on leveraging informative mean functions and multimodal smoothing.
- The scalar benchmark illustrates the ability to handle multimodality; the cart-pole example is a relevant RL system.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Inference details glossed over where tractability is nontrivial: in SMC step (5), sampling xt ∼ p(xt | θ, xai0:t−1, y0:t) is generally intractable for nonconjugate likelihoods p(yt | xt, θy). The “simple choice” of sampling from the one-step predictive density is underspecified; if the optimal proposal is used, how is it sampled? If instead they sample from p(xt | θ, xai0:t−1) and weight by p(yt | xt), say so. As written, the method appears to rely on an intractable proposal in the general case.
- Computational complexity accounting omits factors of N and nx: per-iteration cost of PGAS is O(N T^3) (vanilla GP) or O(N M^2 T) (FIC), plus costs for θ-updates; memory to store per-particle Cholesky factors seems O(N T^2), which can be prohibitive. These considerations and wall-clock times are absent.
- Numerical stability: repeated Cholesky downdates/updates can be unstable; no discussion of jitter, re-factorization triggers, or conditioning for K̃ when xt proposals change rapidly.
- “Only approximation is the sampling algorithm” is inaccurate when using FIC; FIC changes the prior and is an approximation to the full GP prior. This should be stated clearly (the text waffles between “not an approximation” and “different prior”).
- Slice sampling for θx is called “straightforward,” but the collapsed marginal likelihood requires repeated log|K̃| and solves; no discussion of priors, step-out/tuning, autocorrelation, or mixing diagnostics; θy sampling is “straightforward” only for conjugate likelihoods, which the paper does not guarantee.
- Predictive mixture cost: Eq. (10) implies O(L T^3) work (per θ) for GP predictions; the claim that cost “increases linearly with L” overlooks cubic dependence on T unless factors are reused across ` (they cannot be if θ varies). Proposed thinning/herding is a palliative, not a solution.
- Experimental evaluation is weak: the benchmark lacks the promised Table 1 values; there are no quantitative baselines for cart-pole; no runtime/complexity comparisons; no diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, R-hat) to substantiate “very good mixing”; no ablations (vanilla vs FIC, effect of N, T, M).
- Identifiability and multimodality: while the sign ambiguity is acknowledged, the paper does not analyze conditions under which the posterior over x is well-behaved nor provide robustness checks (e.g., sensitivity to mean function misspecification beyond “model B”).
- Notation/indexing inconsistencies: y0:T vs y1:T; u0:T vs u0:T−1; Algorithm 1 sets x1:T but sometimes discusses x0:T; µ1(x0) = m0 (is this m(x0)?); K̃0:t−1 depends on x1:t (argument mismatch); minor but pervasive.
- Missing derivations: Eq. (4) “Gaussian-looking” density over x1:t is pivotal; the supplement (not provided) should include the derivation and gradient expressions for θ updates.
- Reference issues: many entries lack years/venues/pages; numbering appears inconsistent (FIC is attributed to [17], but list suggests otherwise); slice sampling cites Agarwal & Gelfand instead of standard Neal (2003); multiple entries have duplicated commas and inconsistent formatting.

Quality
Technically promising but underspecified in crucial places (proposal distribution; complexity and stability), with insufficient empirical validation and missing quantitative results.

Clarity
Readable high-level narrative, but numerous notation inconsistencies, missing algorithmic details, and incomplete references. Reproducibility is limited.

Originality
Collapsing f in GP-SSMs and combining with PGAS is a neat synthesis; using ancestor sampling in this setting is appropriate and, to my knowledge, not widely explored in earlier GP-SSM works. The sparse construction is standard.

Significance
Potentially significant for Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs and for smoothing with unknown dynamics, but current empirical evidence is too thin to assess impact.

Pros
- Principled collapsed formulation with PGAS; addresses path degeneracy in non-Markovian models.
- Practical implementation ideas and discussion of sparse priors.
- Clear articulation of advantages of informative mean functions.

Cons
- Incomplete/intractable proposal specification; missing algorithmic details.
- Weak, partially missing experiments; no diagnostics or runtime.
- Complexity/memory and numerical stability not rigorously treated.
- Notation and reference formatting issues; several inconsistencies.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The core idea is strong and could be impactful, but the paper needs: (i) a precise, implementable proposal (or explicit statement that a simple prior proposal is used) with resulting weight formulas; (ii) quantitative experiments with baselines, diagnostics, and runtimes; (iii) corrected complexity/memory analysis and numerical stability discussion; (iv) cleaned-up notation and references; (v) inclusion of derivations and implementation details (possibly in an appendix). I would be enthusiastic to reconsider after these revisions.

Confidence: high.
