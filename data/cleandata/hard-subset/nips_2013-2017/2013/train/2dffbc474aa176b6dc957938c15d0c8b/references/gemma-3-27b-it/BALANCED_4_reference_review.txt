This paper presents a compelling approach to Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) using Particle MCMC (PMCMC). The core idea of marginalizing out the state transition function *f* to retain the nonparametric nature of the model is a significant contribution, addressing a key limitation of existing GP-SSM methods. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature, clearly positioning their work within the field and highlighting its advantages over previous approaches like those by Turner et al. [2] and Wang et al. [8]. The detailed description of the PMCMC algorithm, particularly the use of CPF-AS [4] and the efficient implementation leveraging Cholesky factorization, showcases technical depth.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically rigorous. The derivations appear correct, and the algorithm builds upon well-established methods (GP, SMC, MCMC) in a novel way. The efficient implementation details (reuse of Cholesky factorizations, sparse GP with FIC prior) demonstrate a commitment to practicality.
* **Novelty & Originality:** Marginalizing out the state transition function *f* is a key innovation, offering a genuinely nonparametric approach to GP-SSMs. This distinguishes the work from existing methods that often rely on parametric approximations.  The combination of PMCMC with a tailored CPF-AS algorithm specifically designed for this non-Markovian setting is also novel.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is largely clear, and the flow from model description to inference algorithm to experimental validation is logical. Figure 1 provides a helpful visual overview of the model.
* **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental challenge in learning dynamical systems.  A fully Bayesian, nonparametric approach has the potential to provide more robust and accurate models, particularly in situations with limited data or complex, unknown dynamics. The application to a cart and pole system demonstrates practical relevance.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors address computational complexity concerns and provide detailed implementation considerations. The experiments showcase the method’s ability to handle multimodal distributions (learning a nonlinear system benchmark) and learn from limited data (cart and pole system).
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, especially in scenarios where the parametric model of the dynamics is misspecified. The comparison with other methods (ground truth, parametric models, sparse GP-SSM) is informative.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address computational efficiency, the PMCMC algorithm remains computationally expensive, particularly for high-dimensional state spaces or long time series.  A more detailed analysis of the scalability with respect to the state dimension (n<sub>x</sub>) and the time horizon (T) would be beneficial.  
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of GP models is often sensitive to hyperparameter settings. The paper does not thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter choices (e.g., kernel parameters, particle number). A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Complexity of Implementation:**  The implementation details, although thorough, are complex.  Reproducing the results might be challenging for researchers without a strong background in particle filtering and Gaussian processes.  Making the code publicly available would greatly enhance the impact of the work.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made with several baseline methods, the comparison to recent advances in variational inference for GP-SSMs (e.g., those leveraging automatic differentiation and amortized inference) is lacking.  These methods offer different trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost and should be considered in a comparative evaluation.
* **Discussion of FIC Prior:** While the FIC prior is mentioned to improve computational efficiency, the trade-offs between accuracy and efficiency with respect to the number of inducing points are not extensively discussed.  A more detailed analysis of the impact of the number of inducing points on the performance of the sparse GP-SSM would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in Bayesian nonparametrics with a novel and technically sound approach.
* The method demonstrates clear advantages over existing GP-SSM techniques, particularly in scenarios where model misspecification is a concern.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method.
* The authors provide a thorough and detailed description of the algorithm and implementation considerations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the PMCMC algorithm may limit its applicability to large-scale problems.
* The paper lacks a thorough sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters.
* The comparison with recent advances in variational inference for GP-SSMs is missing.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametrics and dynamical systems. The novelty of the approach, combined with the technical rigor and experimental validation, justifies acceptance. However, addressing the weaknesses related to computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity, and comparison to recent variational methods would further strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address these points. Specifically, the authors should consider adding a discussion of the computational scalability, a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and a comparison to relevant variational inference methods.  Making the code publicly available would also be highly encouraged.
