Summary
The paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models where the transition function f is modeled nonparametrically via a Gaussian process (GP). The key idea is to marginalize f exactly, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories. This “collapsed” formulation removes strong posterior dependencies between f and the latent states but introduces long-range temporal dependencies. The authors address the resulting inference challenge with Particle Markov chain Monte Carlo, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), to sample from the exact smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) while iterating with sampling of hyperparameters θ. Given posterior samples of x0:T and θ, the posterior over f at test points is obtained in closed form, resulting in a mixture of Gaussian predictive densities. The paper also presents a sparse variant using the FIC GP prior for computational gains (from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T)), and careful implementation details (rank-1 updates/downdates of Cholesky factors) to make CPF-AS efficient. Experiments on a 1D nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole system illustrate the method’s ability to learn dynamics and to quantify predictive uncertainty.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on and complements a substantial literature on GP-based dynamical systems. Unlike approaches relying on MAP/EM with parametric GP representations or pseudo-inputs (e.g., Ghahramani and Roweis; Turner et al.; Wang et al.), the paper retains the nonparametric nature of the GP by integrating out f. It provides a learning component missing in methods that assume a pre-learned GP dynamics and focus on approximate filtering/smoothing (e.g., Deisenroth et al.). On the inference side, the paper leverages PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and the recent PGAS kernel (Lindsten et al.) to sample state trajectories efficiently in non-Markovian models. The sparse FIC construction follows Quinonero-Candela and Rasmussen and related sparse GP literature. Overall, the combination of collapsing f, exact smoothing via PGAS, and a scalable FIC construction is novel in this context.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Collapsing the GP transition function to obtain exact smoothing in a non-Markovian latent model and combining this with PGAS is an elegant and principled Bayesian solution that retains full nonparametric expressivity.
- Technical care: Clear derivation of the collapsed prior over trajectories, how PGAS can be applied with ancestor sampling weights computable from the collapsed prior, and efficient implementation using Cholesky rank updates. The sparse FIC extension and complexity analysis are valuable.
- Practicality: The approach directly yields a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution for f that can represent multimodality and uncertainty from partial observability.
- Empirical illustrations: The 1D benchmark convincingly shows multimodal smoothing and robustness when the GP mean is misspecified; the cart-pole case demonstrates nontrivial 4D dynamics with meaningful uncertainty quantification.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical comparison: The experiments do not quantitatively compare against strong baselines for learning GP-SSMs (e.g., EM/MAP approaches, variational GPDS/GPDM) beyond the parametric baselines on the 1D example. The cart-pole section lacks quantitative metrics and runtime comparisons.
- Scalability evidence: While the paper provides complexity analysis and implementation tricks, there is little empirical evidence on computational cost, memory footprint, scaling with T, N (particles), nx, and with/without FIC. Reporting wall-clock times and sensitivity to N would strengthen the case.
- Hyperparameter inference details: Slice sampling for θx is mentioned but without enough detail on priors, mixing, and diagnostics. Similarly, diagnostics for PGAS mixing (autocorrelations, effective sample sizes) are not reported.
- Inducing input selection: The FIC section defers selection to heuristics (e.g., greedy forward selection) but does not evaluate the effect of M or selection strategy on accuracy and runtime.
- Assumptions: The observation model g is assumed known (up to low-dimensional parameters); while reasonable in many settings, this restricts applicability and the paper would benefit from a discussion of identifiability and possible extensions when g is also unknown.

Quality
Technically sound, with correct use of GP marginalization, PMCMC/PGAS, and sparse GP constructions. The derivations and algorithmic steps are plausible and consistent. However, the empirical section is somewhat thin for NeurIPS: broader, more rigorous comparisons and runtime/mixing diagnostics are needed to fully support the claims.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized. The derivation of the collapsed prior and the CPF-AS/ancestor sampling weights is informative. Implementation details are helpful. Suggestions:
- Provide a concise algorithm box for the CPF-AS steps used inside PGAS, with explicit computational steps and reused factors.
- Clarify memory requirements per particle (e.g., whether per-particle Cholesky factors of size O(T^2) are stored).
- Include a brief pseudo-code for the FIC implementation to show how O(M^2 T) is achieved in practice.

Originality
High. The collapsed GP-SSM with PGAS for exact smoothing and subsequent GP posterior over dynamics is, to my knowledge, new relative to prior EM/MAP/variational lines and to GPDS work focused on inference given learned models. The synthesis of PGAS with GP collapsing is particularly neat.

Significance
Potentially significant. A principled, fully Bayesian, nonparametric approach to learning nonlinear SSMs is impactful. If the method scales as argued, it can influence both research and applications (e.g., robotics, control). The sparse extension and implementation guidance improve practical relevance. The current experimental scope limits demonstrated impact; stronger empirical validation would improve significance.

Questions and suggestions
- Please report ESS or autocorrelation for x and θ, and wall-clock times for PGAS with varying N, T, and M (FIC), including memory usage.
- Compare against EM/MAP GPDM/GPSSM baselines and variational GPDS on the 1D benchmark and a mid-size synthetic system; include cart-pole quantitative one-step RMSE and multi-step predictive metrics.
- Discuss robustness to misspecification of Q and priors on θx, and show sensitivity analyses.
- Elaborate on strategies for inducing input placement and choosing M; consider adaptive placement or marginalizing inducing inputs if feasible.
- Briefly discuss extensions when g is also unknown and identifiability considerations.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, well-motivated Bayesian formulation; technically solid
- Leverages state-of-the-art PMCMC (PGAS) appropriately
- Provides scalable sparse alternative and concrete implementation insights
- Clear benefits in multimodal smoothing and uncertainty-aware predictions

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental validation limited; lacks strong comparative baselines and runtime/mixing analysis
- Some practical choices (inducing inputs, θ sampling) under-specified
- Applicability narrower due to known observation model assumption

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on strengthening empirical comparisons and reporting computational/mixing diagnostics.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
