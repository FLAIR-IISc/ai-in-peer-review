Summary and context
The paper tackles learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models where the transition function f is unknown and modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process prior. The key methodological choice is to marginalize f exactly, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x1:T|θ,x0) that factorizes into GP one-step conditionals. This removes strong posterior coupling between f and x0:T and preserves the nonparametric nature of the model. Posterior inference over states and hyperparameters is then performed with particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), a PMCMC method that is well-suited to non-Markovian latent variable models. The paper further proposes a sparse variant using the FIC prior to reduce computational cost from O(T^3) to O(M^2T), and describes careful linear-algebraic implementations (rank-one Cholesky updates and reuse across time). Experiments on a scalar nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole system demonstrate credible smoothing and one-step predictive performance; the method yields a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive posterior over f by averaging standard GP posteriors across samples of x0:T and θ.

Relation to prior work is well addressed: EM and MAP approaches for GP-SSMs (Turner et al.; Wang et al./GPDM) optimize parametric surrogates and do not fully integrate over f or x. Deisenroth et al. focus on filtering/smoothing given learned dynamics; the proposed method aims to provide fully Bayesian learning of those dynamics. The paper leverages PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.) to address the induced non-Markovianity.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Collapsing the GP transition function is a principled and elegant way to maintain nonparametric expressivity while reducing posterior correlation; the resulting non-Markovian state prior is exploited effectively with PGAS.
- Methodological soundness: The construction of p(x1:T|θ,x0) via GP one-step conditionals with process noise is correct; using ancestor sampling with weights proportional to p(x̃t:T|xi0:t−1) is the right PGAS machinery for non-Markovian models.
- Implementation detail: The paper demonstrates awareness of computational pitfalls and provides practically useful strategies (rank-1 Cholesky updates/downdates, reuse across time; FIC-based O(M^2T) scaling).
- Empirical value: The scalar benchmark emphasizes multimodality in smoothing and shows the benefit of a GP prior correcting misspecified mean functions; the cart-pole example illustrates state-dependent uncertainty quantification.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Experimental evaluation is limited. The scalar example uses T=200 and N=20 with 50 PGAS iterations; no diagnostics (autocorrelation, effective sample sizes, acceptance) are provided to substantiate “very good mixing.” For cart-pole, only one-step predictive means and variances are shown without quantitative metrics (NLL/RMSE), multi-step rollouts, or comparisons to baselines (e.g., GPDM, EM-based GP-SSMs, variational GP-SSMs, or particle MCMC variants that parameterize f).
- Baseline comparisons: In the scalar experiment, results are said to be summarized in Table 1, but the table is not included in the text provided. Even if present in the full paper, comparisons should include methods that (i) learn GP dynamics but do not collapse f (e.g., pseudo-input GP with MH over f), (ii) variational GP-SSMs, and (iii) standard SSM baselines (EKF/UKF smoothing with parametric f).
- Scalability and robustness: While the complexity analysis is convincing, empirical scaling plots (runtime vs T and vs M; accuracy vs M) and sensitivity analyses (to kernel hyperpriors, Q priors, and number of particles N) are missing. High-dimensional states are common in practice; discussion or experiments beyond nx=1 and nx=4 would strengthen claims.
- Proposal distribution in SMC: Section 3.2 alternates between proposing from p(xt|θ, x0:t−1, y0:t) and from the prior p(xt|θ, x0:t−1). For general non-Gaussian observation models p(yt|xt,θy), sampling from the “optimal” proposal may be intractable; if the prior proposal is used, this should be stated clearly along with any MH-within-SMC used when needed.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, but some notational choices burden the reader (e.g., K̃ depending on x1:t both as argument and as matrix being inverted). It would help to separate clearly the Gaussian-looking density in (4) from an actual Gaussian, and to give a concise recipe for evaluating ancestor weights using the maintained Cholesky factors.
- The CPF-AS step is only sketched; a concise algorithmic box for the conditional PF with ancestor sampling tailored to the non-Markovian prior would be very helpful.
- Minor inconsistencies: y0:T vs y1:T indexing varies; clarify whether x0 is included in the Gibbs state and how x0 is sampled/treated.

Originality
- The combination of collapsing GP dynamics and using PGAS for state sampling in the resulting non-Markovian model is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. The use of FIC inside this collapsed PMCMC framework is also a useful contribution. The paper clearly differentiates from EM/MAP approaches that make the model parametric in practice.

Significance
- Fully Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs with correct handling of multimodal smoothing is important. If backed by broader experiments and comparisons, this could be a reference method for uncertainty-aware modeling of unknown dynamics. The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive representation is practically valuable (with a reasonable discussion of thinning/herding).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative comparisons against GPDM/EM-based GP-SSMs, variational GP-SSMs, and parametric SSM baselines on both the scalar benchmark and cart-pole, reporting RMSE and predictive log-likelihood, including multi-step forecasting.
- Provide mixing diagnostics and sensitivity to N, T, M, and choice of mean function; include runtime scaling plots.
- Clarify the SMC proposal used in practice under non-Gaussian likelihoods; if using the prior proposal, state this and discuss its impact on variance/degeneracy.
- Include an appendix with explicit formulas for computing p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) efficiently, and pseudo-code for the CPF-AS kernel in this non-Markovian setting.
- Discuss identifiability when both f and g are flexible; the paper assumes known g, but practical scenarios might require partial learning of g.

Minor issues
- Reference for slice sampling would typically be Neal (2003); the current citation is less standard.
- Proofreading: unify notation for time indexing of y; ensure Table 1 and figures are present and legible; define nx and dimensions where first used.

Pros
- Principled, fully Bayesian treatment preserving GP nonparametrics by marginalizing f.
- Appropriate and state-of-the-art inference engine (PGAS) for non-Markovian latent models.
- Solid implementation insights; sparse variant enabling scalability.
- Convincing qualitative handling of multimodality.

Cons
- Limited and partly qualitative empirical evaluation; weak baseline coverage.
- Missing diagnostics and scaling studies.
- Some ambiguity in SMC proposal and clarity gaps in the ancestor sampling implementation.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The core idea is strong and technically sound, and with strengthened experiments and clarity, this could be a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community.
