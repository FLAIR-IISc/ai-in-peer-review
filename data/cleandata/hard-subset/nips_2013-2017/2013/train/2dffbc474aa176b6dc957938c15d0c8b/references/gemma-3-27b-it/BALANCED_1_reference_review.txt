This paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function using a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm. This is a significant contribution as it avoids the limitations of previous methods that rely on parametric approximations of the GP dynamics. The authors tackle a challenging problem and propose a technically sound solution with clear theoretical grounding. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology, building upon existing work in GP-SSMs and PMCMC.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The core idea of marginalizing out the state transition function is well-motivated and mathematically rigorous. The derivation of the marginal prior and the application of PMCMC appear logically consistent and well-explained. The use of recent advances in PMCMC sampling (specifically PGAS) is a good choice and demonstrably improves mixing.
* **Novelty:** The approach of fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs without parametric assumptions on the dynamics is novel and addresses a key limitation of existing methods.  The sparse GP extension further enhances scalability.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. Complex concepts are explained clearly, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a digestible manner. The organization is logical, and the use of equations and figures aids understanding.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both a synthetic nonlinear system and a cart-pole system provide compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison with other approaches (parametric models, ground truth, sparse GP-SSM) is informative.  The visualization of the smoothing distribution and the learned dynamics is particularly helpful.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper addresses potential computational bottlenecks (O(T^3) complexity) and presents a sparse GP implementation to mitigate them. The discussion of implementation details and the connection to related work is comprehensive.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address the computational cost with a sparse GP implementation, the runtime remains a significant concern. Even with the optimization, the method may not be practical for very high-dimensional state spaces or long time series without further improvements or specialized hardware. A more detailed discussion of the computational demands (e.g., wall clock time for the presented experiments) would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of GP models is known to be sensitive to hyperparameter settings.  The paper mentions priors on hyperparameters but does not fully discuss the sensitivity of the results to these choices. A sensitivity analysis on the hyperparameters would strengthen the paper.
* **Scalability of Sparse GP:** While the sparse GP implementation reduces computational cost, the choice of the number and placement of inducing points (M) can significantly impact performance.  The paper briefly mentions greedy methods, but a more detailed discussion of strategies for choosing M and their potential limitations would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited. While the authors compare to several baselines, including other GP-based methods, a more extensive comparison to state-of-the-art SSM inference techniques (e.g., variational inference, extended Kalman filter) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in Bayesian inference.
* The method addresses a significant limitation of existing GP-SSM approaches by avoiding parametric assumptions on the dynamics.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on relevant benchmark problems.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the methodology.
* The contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to researchers in the fields of machine learning, statistics, and dynamical systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the method, even with the sparse GP implementation, may limit its practical applicability.
* The paper lacks a thorough sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters.
* The comparison to other SSM inference techniques is somewhat limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of GP-SSMs. The novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the methodology, and the clarity of the presentation are all commendable. While the computational cost and limited comparison are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the paper.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Provide more detailed information on the computational cost of the experiments (e.g., wall clock time).
2.  Include a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters.
3.  Expand the comparison to include a wider range of state-of-the-art SSM inference techniques.
4.  Provide a more detailed discussion of strategies for choosing the number and placement of inducing points in the sparse GP implementation.
