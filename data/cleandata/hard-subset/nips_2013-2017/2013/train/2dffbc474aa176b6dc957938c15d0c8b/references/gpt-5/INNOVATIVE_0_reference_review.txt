Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) without collapsing to a parametric surrogate. The key idea is to marginalize the unknown transition function f under a GP prior, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories that can be written as a product of conditionals with GP predictive means/covariances. This breaks Markov structure, so the authors employ Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to sample from the exact smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) while alternating Gibbs-like updates of states and hyperparameters. The approach retains the nonparametric nature of f, avoids MAP/EM over a finite set of pseudo-targets, and uses slice sampling for GP hyperparameters conditioned on sampled state trajectories. To address computational cost, the paper gives careful implementation details (rank-1 Cholesky updates/downdates) and a sparse alternative via the FIC prior, reducing complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T) with M inducing points. Learning of f then follows by averaging GP predictive distributions over posterior samples of (x, θ), yielding a mixture of Gaussians predictive for f(x).

Compared to prior work: Deisenroth et al. focused on filtering/smoothing given learned GPs; Turner et al. and Wang et al. relied on EM/MAP with parametric GP surrogates; GPDMs similarly optimize latent states/parameters. This paper’s central novelty is collapsing f and using PMCMC to sample states and θ under the exact nonparametric model, leveraging PGAS for non-Markovian latent structures—an approach not present in the cited literature. The sparse FIC variant and efficient CPF-AS implementation further distinguish the work.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Collapsing the GP transition and casting the resulting non-Markovian inference as PGAS is a bold, clean idea that preserves full nonparametric expressivity while enabling exact (up to Monte Carlo) smoothing.
- Principled inference: The only approximation is Monte Carlo; conditional updates for θ and x are well-motivated, and the relation to GP regression when conditioning on x0:T is clearly used for θ sampling.
- Technical depth: Derivations for the collapsed prior over trajectories, ancestor sampling weights in the non-Markovian setting, and complexity-aware implementations (Cholesky updates; FIC with O(M^2 T)) are detailed and reusable.
- Practical flexibility: Incorporation of parametric mean functions allows injecting domain knowledge while letting the GP capture residuals; predictive mixture over f captures multimodality from ambiguous observations.
- Empirical illustrations: The scalar nonlinear benchmark demonstrates handling multimodal smoothing and robustness to misspecified mean; the 4D cart-pole shows reasonable one-step predictive uncertainty that adapts to data coverage.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: Experiments are modest in scale and breadth. Comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., variational GP-SSMs, GPDM with EM, PILCO-style models, or recent PG variants) are missing. Quantitative results are summarized but not deeply analyzed (e.g., no runtime/memory scaling vs T, N, M; no sensitivity analyses).
- Scalability and dimensionality: While complexity reductions are discussed, practical performance for long sequences and higher-dimensional states remains uncertain. The per-iteration cost and dependence on particle number N are not profiled empirically; mixing/ESS diagnostics are not reported.
- Inducing inputs selection: The sparse FIC variant leaves inducing input placement as future work; for state trajectories, automatic schemes or joint sampling could matter substantially.
- Assumptions on g: The measurement model is assumed known (up to finite-dimensional θy). While motivated, many applications seek jointly flexible f and g; discussion of identifiability/partial flexibility would be helpful.
- Clarity/organization: Though generally clear, some heavy algebra could be shifted to an appendix; a compact pseudocode for CPF-AS in the non-Markovian setting would improve readability. A clearer statement of per-PGAS-iteration complexity (accounting for N) would aid practitioners.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The collapsed prior derivation and mapping to PGAS are consistent with GP and PMCMC theory; implementation insights are thoughtful. Claims about mixing are plausible but would benefit from empirical diagnostics. The sparse construction is standard FIC applied carefully in this context.

Clarity
Well-written and organized. Notation for block matrices is dense; a small schematic for ancestor sampling weights in the collapsed model would help. Including an algorithm box for the CPF-AS update in the GP-SSM would make it more self-contained.

Originality
High. Collapsing f in GP-SSMs and using PGAS for non-Markovian latent variable inference, while retaining full GP nonparametrics and detailing a sparse variant, is a substantial conceptual advance beyond EM/MAP parametric surrogates and post hoc filtering/smoothing.

Significance
High potential impact. A general recipe for fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs can influence time-series modeling, system ID, and RL. The mixture predictive for f(x) is practically useful, and the sparse pathway increases applicability.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Add comparative baselines (e.g., variational GP-SSM, GPDM-EM/MAP) with accuracy and runtime; include mixing diagnostics (ACF, ESS) and scaling plots vs T, N, M, nx.
- Discuss extensions when g is partially unknown and how identifiability is handled.
- Explore adaptive/learned proposals within CPF-AS to reduce variance.
- Elaborate on strategies for inducing input placement in state spaces (e.g., tempering, k-DPPs, or online selection).
- Report memory costs and stability of rank-1 updates for long T.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel, and principled approach to a longstanding hard problem.
- Maintains full nonparametric GP semantics by analytically marginalizing f.
- Solid algorithmic contributions and practicality (sparse variant, efficient updates).
- Potentially broad impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical section is limited; lack of strong baselines and scalability evidence.
- Some practical components (inducing point selection, diagnostics) are underdeveloped.

Recommendation
Accept. The conceptual advance outweighs the relatively light experimentation, and the paper is likely to stimulate follow-on work and practical adoption.

Confidence
4/5.
