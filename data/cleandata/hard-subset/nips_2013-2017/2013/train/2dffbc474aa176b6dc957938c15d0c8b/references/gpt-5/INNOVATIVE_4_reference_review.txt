Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning in nonlinear state-space models where the transition function f is unknown and modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP). The core idea is to marginalize f exactly, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories, and then perform fully Bayesian inference over the latent states and hyperparameters using particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). After obtaining samples from the smoothing distribution with f marginalized out, the posterior over f is recovered analytically (as in standard GP regression conditioned on sampled state trajectories). The authors also develop a sparse variant using the FIC prior with inducing inputs to reduce complexity to O(M^2 T), and provide careful implementation details (rank-one Cholesky updates) to keep costs tractable in the vanilla case.

This differs from much prior GP-SSM work that either: (i) fixes a GP learned in a parametric or MAP fashion and then filters/smooths (e.g., Deisenroth et al.), or (ii) uses EM/MAP to estimate a parametric finite GP representation along with states (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.). Here, the nonparametric nature of the GP is preserved end-to-end by collapsing f and sampling states/hyperparameters with an invariant PMCMC kernel that targets the exact smoothing distribution. The paper builds on PGAS [Lindsten et al.] and standard GP regression, adapting them to this non-Markovian setting.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual contribution: Collapsing the transition function in a GP-SSM to derive an explicit non-Markovian prior over states and then using PGAS to sample from the exact smoothing distribution is a bold and elegant idea. It preserves the nonparametric nature of f and leverages recent PMCMC advances in a way that, to my knowledge, has not been done in prior GP-SSM learning work.
- Soundness: The marginal prior over trajectories (Eq. 3–4) and the invariance of the CPF-AS kernel are well grounded. The connection to standard GP regression for hyperparameter sampling is clean, and the use of slice sampling is reasonable.
- Practicality: The paper goes beyond “proof-of-concept” by giving nontrivial implementation details (rank-one/downdate Cholesky updates to maintain O(T^3); FIC-based O(M^2 T) with attention to factor reuse). This is valuable for practitioners.
- Flexibility: The framework allows prior knowledge in the GP mean function and can incorporate known observation models; the predictive distribution over f is a Gaussian mixture that captures multimodality induced by observation ambiguity.
- Empirical illustrations: On a nonlinear 1D benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole system, results qualitatively demonstrate the method’s ability to learn dynamics even when the GP mean is misspecified and to express calibrated uncertainty.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited experimental validation: Beyond qualitative plots and an RMSE table (details elided in this version), the experiments are relatively small-scale (T=200, N=20 particles, 50 PGAS iterations) with few baselines. For NeurIPS, a stronger empirical section would compare to (i) MAP/EM-based GP-SSMs (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.), (ii) GP-SSM smoothing with learned GP but without collapsing f, and (iii) particle MCMC on parametric nonlinear SSMs to show the value of collapsing. Ablations on particle number, chain length, and inducing points (M) would help.
- Scalability concerns: Although O(T^3) and O(M^2 T) are standard, constants and memory can be large; no wall-clock or memory profiling is given. It would be useful to report scaling past T~1–5k, discuss nx dependence explicitly, and show high-dimensional state cases beyond 4D.
- Inducing input selection: The sparse construction relies on inducing inputs, but selection is deferred; some auto-selection (greedy, gradient-based, or herding) and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the sparse story.
- Assumption of known measurement model g: While sensible in many engineering settings, the requirement that g be known limits applicability. A short discussion or experiment with a flexible observation model (or identifiable subset) would be useful, as would comments on identifiability when both f and g are flexible.
- Mixing and diagnostics: The paper claims good mixing with PGAS but provides no quantitative diagnostics (autocorrelations, ESS, R-hat, marginal likelihood stability). Including these would bolster the quality claims.
- Clarity/organization: The CPF-AS/ancestor sampling probabilities for the non-Markovian case (Eq. 6) are compactly presented; providing a small boxed algorithm for the CPF-AS step specialized to this model and a short derivation sketch would aid reproducibility. A table summarizing computational and storage complexity for vanilla vs. FIC across steps (proposal, weighting, ancestor sampling) would help.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically solid with careful derivations and correct use of PGAS; the main gap is empirical breadth and diagnostics rather than methodology.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and self-contained, though dense. Minor improvements as noted would aid readers.
- Originality: High. The exact marginalization of f in a GP-SSM coupled with PGAS for smoothing/learning is a meaningful advance over prior EM/MAP or fixed-GP pipelines.
- Significance: High potential impact. Provides a principled, fully Bayesian route to learning GP-SSMs that others can build upon, and a sparse path to scalability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative comparisons to EM/MAP GP-SSMs and to learned-GP smoothing without collapsing f; include runtime and memory metrics.
- Provide mixing diagnostics and sensitivity to N, iterations, M, and priors.
- Discuss/experiment with partial unknown g or mild flexibility in the observation model.
- Expand on inducing input selection strategies and report sensitivity.
- Include an appendix with explicit CPF-AS steps for this non-Markovian setting and a complexity/memory summary.

Minor nits
- Clarify how Q interacts with GP covariance in K̃ and its effect on stability and identifiability.
- Specify the priors used in experiments and their influence on results.
- Ensure figures show uncertainty intervals clearly and axes are labeled.

Recommendation
- Overall score: 8 (accept)
- Confidence: 4/5
- Justification: The paper introduces a conceptually strong and novel approach—collapsing GP transition dynamics and leveraging PGAS for exact smoothing and learning—that opens a promising direction for fully Bayesian GP-SSMs. Despite a relatively light empirical section, the methodological contribution and practical implementation insights warrant acceptance. Strengthening experiments and diagnostics would make this even more compelling.
