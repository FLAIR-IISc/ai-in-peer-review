Summary
The paper studies Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The main idea is to place a GP prior over the unknown transition function f while assuming a known observation model g (possibly with finite-dimensional parameters). Rather than parameterizing f, the authors collapse it out analytically, which induces a non-Markovian prior over the latent states x0:T. They then target p(θ, x0:T | y0:T) with Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to sample from the smoothing distribution and the hyperparameters. Given samples of x0:T and θ, the posterior over f at test points is obtained via standard GP regression formulas, yielding a mixture of Gaussians predictive distribution. To reduce computational cost, they discuss an implementation reusing Cholesky factorizations (O(T^3)) and a sparse GP variant (FIC) to reach O(M^2 T). Experiments include a 1D nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole system, with qualitative and some quantitative results; an RMSE comparison is mentioned, and sparse and dense GP-SSMs are compared.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on GP-SSMs/GPDynamics (e.g., Wang et al., 2005; Turner et al., 2010; Ghahramani & Roweis, 1999) and PMCMC (Andrieu et al., 2010) and PGAS (Lindsten et al., 2012). Deisenroth et al. provide approximate filtering/smoothing for learned GP models; here, the authors address learning by marginalizing f and using PMCMC. The FIC sparse prior follows Snelson & Ghahramani and Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean: collapsing f respects the nonparametric prior and avoids bias from finite parameterizations.
- Methodologically grounded: the use of PGAS for non-Markovian latent models is appropriate and theoretically justified (invariance, ergodicity).
- Practical implementation details: reuse of Cholesky factors and rank-1 updates, and the FIC construction to control complexity, are useful to practitioners.
- The approach handles multimodal smoothing distributions and yields uncertainty-aware predictions (mixture of Gaussians).

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: small-scale experiments (T=200, 1D; a brief 4D example with 100 observations). No rigorous comparison to strong baselines beyond brief mentions (no numbers shown in the review text), e.g., EM-based GP-SSMs, variational GP-SSMs, EP-based smoothing on learned models, or parametric SSMs learned with PMCMC.
- Computational costs and scaling are not convincingly characterized. Claims of O(T^3) or O(M^2 T) need to be contextualized with constants, nx dependence, and PMCMC overhead (number of particles N, iterations L). No wall-clock times, ESS, autocorrelation plots, or scaling with T, nx, M, N.
- Assumption that g is known: this is a strong restriction. Many applications require learning g (even if linear). The paper does not investigate robustness to mild misspecification of g.
- Identifiability and prior dependence: inference may be sensitive to the choice of GP mean, kernel, and priors on Q; the benchmark uses an informative mean that resembles the true structure. No ablation on mean function choice or hyperprior sensitivity.
- Sparse GP details: inducing input selection is deferred; using a fixed set undermines the “fully Bayesian” stance and can materially affect performance. No comparisons to variational inducing-point methods which are more principled than FIC.
- Convergence diagnostics are weak: 50 PGAS iterations with N=20 are reported as “very good mixing” without quantitative diagnostics. For multimodal posteriors this is unconvincing.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the collapsing derivation and use of PGAS for the resulting non-Markovian prior are consistent with cited theory. However, key practical aspects are insufficiently validated: mixing, sensitivity, and computational behavior.
- The empirical section is too thin to support strong claims. The absence of numerical tables/curves (RMSEs, runtime, calibration metrics) and lack of strong baselines leaves open whether this advances the state of the art in practice.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well written and organized. The description of CPF-AS for non-Markovian models and the FIC formulas is clear, albeit dense.
- Implementation details are appreciated, but complexity statements should be per PGAS sweep and include dependence on nx, N, and L. Clarify how ancestor sampling weights in Eq. (6) are evaluated efficiently for long T.

Originality
- The combination of exact marginalization of f with PGAS for GP-SSMs is a thoughtful synthesis rather than a wholly new algorithmic primitive. PGAS itself and sparse GP ideas are known. The paper’s main novelty is the application and careful collapsing for GP-SSMs with accompanying implementation tricks.

Significance
- Potentially valuable, but current evidence is limited. Without stronger empirical comparisons and scaling studies, it is hard to judge whether this will be adopted over existing learning procedures for GP-SSMs or parametric SSMs.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Provide quantitative results for the benchmark: RMSEs with means/variance, and comparisons to (i) EM-based GP-SSM (Turner et al.), (ii) variational inducing-point GP-SSMs, (iii) parametric SSMs learned with PMCMC, (iv) Deisenroth-style EP smoothing on learned models.
- Report computational metrics: wall-clock per PGAS iteration and total training time, scaling with T, nx, N, M; autocorrelation/ESS for x and θ; sensitivity to N and number of iterations.
- Analyze robustness to misspecified g and to different mean functions for f (e.g., zero mean vs linear vs mis-specified parametric form).
- For the sparse variant, compare FIC to variational inducing points (Titsias 2009) and discuss inducing input selection strategy; show performance vs M.
- Provide uncertainty calibration (e.g., CRPS, PIT histograms) for one-step predictions.
- Clarify identifiability between Q and kernel amplitude; what priors are used and how sensitive are results?

Pros
- Solid probabilistic formulation respecting the GP prior via collapsing.
- Uses established, principled inference (PGAS) with guarantees.
- Practical insights on implementation and sparsity.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is too limited; lack of strong baselines and diagnostics.
- Unclear computational viability for larger T/nx; no runtime evidence.
- Assumption of known g limits applicability; no robustness study.
- Sparse GP choice not fully justified; inducing input selection undeveloped.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The methodological synthesis is sound and potentially impactful, but the paper, as presented, lacks the rigorous empirical validation and scaling evidence needed to justify acceptance at NeurIPS. Strengthening the experiments with strong baselines, thorough diagnostics, scaling studies, and robustness analyses would substantially improve the case.

Reviewer confidence: medium-high.
