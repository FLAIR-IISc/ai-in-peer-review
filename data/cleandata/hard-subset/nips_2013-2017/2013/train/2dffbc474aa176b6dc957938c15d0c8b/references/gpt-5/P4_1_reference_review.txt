Summary and relation to prior work
The paper considers nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) with unknown transition function f and known observation model g, and places a Gaussian process (GP) prior on f. The central idea is to perform fully Bayesian learning by marginalizing out the latent transition function f exactly, and then sampling the latent state trajectory x0:T and hyperparameters θ using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) [3,4]. This avoids the parametric approximations commonly used when applying GPs to SSMs and preserves the nonparametric expressivity. Once samples of x0:T and θ are available, the posterior over f at test inputs is obtained in closed form via standard GP regression, yielding a mixture of Gaussians predictive distribution.

Key contributions include: (i) derivation of the collapsed prior over state trajectories induced by marginalizing f, which yields a non-Markovian model where p(x1:t|x0) can be evaluated using GP conditioning; (ii) a tailored PGAS algorithm for sampling from p(x0:T, θ | y0:T), exploiting forward-only recursions and ancestor sampling weights computable from the collapsed prior; (iii) computational strategies to reduce complexity from naive O(T^4) to O(T^3) via Cholesky updates, and further to O(M^2 T) by adopting a sparse GP prior (FIC) [17], with details on efficient factor reuse; (iv) empirical demonstrations on a nonlinear 1D benchmark and a 4D cart–pole system.

Related work includes GP-based filtering/smoothing methods that assume learned dynamics [5,6], EM/MAP approaches that parametrize the GP with finite targets [2,8], and earlier RBF-based EM learning [7]. The present paper differs by marginalizing f and using PMCMC for joint state and hyperparameter inference, providing a fully Bayesian alternative to EM/MAP and preserving nonparametric flexibility.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The collapsing of f is standard GP conditioning applied temporally, leading to a product-of-Gaussians form for p(x1:T | θ, x0) where the mean and covariance depend nonlinearly on x0:T−1 (eqs. 3–4). This is technically sound.
- Use of PGAS for non-Markovian latent variable models is appropriate, and the invariance properties of CPF-AS kernels [4] justify correct targeting of the smoothing distribution for any N ≥ 2. The ancestor sampling weights proportional to p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) are computable under the collapsed prior.
- The hyperparameter updates leverage the GP marginal likelihood p(x1:T | x0, θx, Q) and slice sampling; this is a standard and valid approach when f has been marginalized.
- Complexity analysis and implementation details appear careful: reusing Cholesky factors via rank-one updates/downdates for O(T^3), and the FIC prior enabling O(M^2 T) with attention to reusing factorizations and matrix-vector products [16,17].
- One minor clarity/consistency issue in Section 3.2: the proposal distribution for xt is written as p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t), but the subsequent weighting wit ∝ p(yt | θ, xit) corresponds to proposing from the prior p(xt | θ, x0:t−1). If the proposal incorporates yt (fully adapted filter), the weights should differ. This should be clarified to avoid confusion about the actual proposal and weights used.
- The paper does not discuss identifiability and posterior coupling between the GP kernel amplitude and the process noise covariance Q, which can be problematic in practice without informative priors. While a Bayesian framework can mitigate this, the lack of sensitivity analysis is a gap.
- Empirical evaluation shows plausibility and qualitative fit, but quantitative evidence is limited. For the 1D benchmark, RMSE is reported (Table 1), but mixing diagnostics, predictive log-likelihood, calibration, and runtime scaling (N, T, M) are not provided. The cart–pole example shows one-step predictions with uncertainty but lacks baselines or quantitative metrics.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with helpful explanations of the collapsed prior, PGAS, and computational strategies. The use of Figures (smoothing samples, dynamics surfaces) aids understanding.
- Notation is heavy but consistent. The distinction between prior p(xt | x0:t−1) and proposal q(xt | ·) should be made explicit in Section 3.2, with correct weight formulas.
- Algorithm 1 is concise; a brief pseudocode for CPF-AS specialized to the collapsed GP-SSM would further aid reproducibility.
- The FIC section is clear about the change of prior (not an approximation of the full GP-SSM) and the resulting computational benefits.

Originality
- Combining exact marginalization of the GP transition function with PMCMC/PGAS for full Bayesian inference over states and hyperparameters in GP-SSMs is, to the best of the cited context, novel relative to EM/MAP-based GPDM [2,8] and to works focusing on filtering/smoothing with already learned GPs [5,6]. The explicit use of the collapsed non-Markovian prior within PGAS and the tailored computational scheme are original and technically interesting.

Significance
- The approach addresses a recognized bottleneck—learning GP-SSMs without parametric surrogates—offering a principled Bayesian solution with correct uncertainty propagation. This can be impactful for time series modeling and control where model uncertainty matters.
- The sparse GP prior option increases practical relevance by improving scalability.
- The empirical section demonstrates feasibility but falls short of a thorough comparison against strong baselines (e.g., variational GP-SSM learning, EM/MAP with careful regularization, parametric PMCMC), limiting conclusions about state-of-the-art performance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear and principled formulation of fully Bayesian GP-SSM learning via collapsing and PGAS [3,4].
- Sound derivations and careful attention to computational efficiency, including sparse prior integration [17].
- Maintains nonparametric flexibility; mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution reflects multimodality from partial observability.
- Useful contribution that complements existing GP inference methods for dynamical systems [5,6].

Arguments for rejection
- Limited empirical evaluation: few datasets, modest T, no comprehensive baselines, no predictive likelihood or calibration metrics, and limited runtime/scaling evidence.
- Some ambiguity in the SMC proposal/weighting description (Section 3.2), and no analysis of identifiability between kernel scale and Q.
- No study of mixing behavior vs. number of particles/iterations; claims of “very good mixing” are not substantiated quantitatively.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the proposal distribution and weight computation in Section 3.2; if a fully adapted proposal is used, provide the exact form and resulting weights.
- Provide quantitative diagnostics: effective sample size, autocorrelation, acceptance/mixing of θ, and sensitivity to N, T, and M.
- Include predictive log-likelihood and calibration (e.g., PIT, coverage) and runtime comparisons to EM/MAP and variational methods; add ablations for ancestor sampling vs. vanilla particle Gibbs.
- Discuss identifiability and prior choices for Q and kernel amplitude; include sensitivity analysis.
- For FIC, report performance vs. M and a comparison to the full GP prior on at least one dataset.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and original approach with promising potential, but would benefit from stronger empirical validation and minor clarifications.
