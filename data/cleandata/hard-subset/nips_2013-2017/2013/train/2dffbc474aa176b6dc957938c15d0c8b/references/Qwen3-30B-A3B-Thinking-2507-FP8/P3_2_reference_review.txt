

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

## Summary

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function f (modeled as a GP) and using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) methods to sample from the smoothing distribution p(x₀:T | y₀:T, θ). The key innovation is the marginalization of the dynamics function f, which allows the model to retain its full nonparametric expressivity without resorting to parametric approximations of the dynamics. The authors develop a Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to efficiently perform inference in the resulting non-Markovian latent variable model. They also introduce a sparse GP-SSM using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to reduce computational complexity. The method is evaluated on two benchmark systems: a nonlinear dynamical system and a cart-pole system.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach to Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs. The marginalization of the state transition function f is a clever solution to a key problem in this area. The authors provide detailed derivations of the marginal prior p(x₀:T | θ) and the necessary algorithms for inference. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and the proposed sparse GP implementation is well-motivated.

However, there are several quality concerns that need to be addressed:

1. **Lack of theoretical guarantees**: The paper lacks theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the PGAS sampler or the quality of the approximate posterior. It would be valuable to provide some theoretical bounds on the error introduced by the particle approximation.

2. **Limited experimental evaluation**: The experiments are confined to two relatively simple benchmark systems. The paper would benefit from more extensive testing on larger or more complex datasets to better demonstrate the method's scalability and robustness. The comparison with related work is superficial and doesn't include recent approaches like those by Särkkä et al. (2013) or Hensman et al. (2013).

3. **Computational complexity**: While the paper claims O(T³) complexity for the vanilla GP and O(M²T) for the sparse GP, the practical performance on larger datasets is not demonstrated. The paper should include a more detailed analysis of how the method scales with increasing time horizon T and number of inducing points M.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and detailed, and the authors provide pseudocode for the key algorithm (Algorithm 1). The experimental section is well-structured with clear presentation of results.

However, there are some clarity issues:

1. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for state trajectories (sometimes x₀:t-1, sometimes x₀:t), which can be confusing. The authors should standardize their notation throughout the paper.

2. **Ambiguous connections**: The connection between the GP prior and the state transition function could be explained more clearly. The paper states that "the functional form of the observation model g is assumed to be known," but it's not entirely clear how this interacts with the GP model for the dynamics.

3. **Sparse GP implementation**: Section 4.2's description of the sparse GP implementation could be more detailed to help readers understand the computational efficiency.

## Originality

The core idea of marginalizing the state transition function f to enable fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs is novel and addresses a key limitation in previous work. The application of PGAS to this specific problem is a clever extension of recent developments in PMCMC methods. The sparse GP-SSM construction using the FIC prior is a good application of existing sparse GP techniques to this problem.

However, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this work differs from or improves upon recent contributions in the field. Specifically, it should more thoroughly differentiate from the work by Särkkä and colleagues (e.g., "Gaussian process state-space models," 2013) and the more recent work by Hensman et al. (2013) on sparse GPs for SSMs. The paper also doesn't adequately reference the extensive literature on GP-based SSMs by Deisenroth et al. (2011, 2012), which is cited but not fully engaged with.

## Significance

The method has significant potential for applications in robotics, control, and time series analysis where flexible, nonparametric modeling of dynamics is important. The fully Bayesian approach provides a principled way to quantify uncertainty in both the states and the dynamics, which is valuable for decision-making under uncertainty.

However, the computational complexity (O(T³) for vanilla GP, O(M²T) for sparse GP) remains a significant limitation for large-scale applications. The paper doesn't demonstrate the method on real-world datasets with substantial time series data, which would better demonstrate its practical significance. The comparison with previous work is limited, and it's not clear how much better the proposed method is than existing approaches in terms of predictive accuracy or computational efficiency.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound contribution to the field of Bayesian inference in state-space models. The marginalization of the state transition function is a novel and important contribution that addresses a key limitation in previous work on GP-SSMs. The authors provide detailed derivations, efficient algorithms, and promising experimental results on two benchmark systems.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication at NeurIPS:

1. The theoretical guarantees about the convergence of the PGAS sampler are missing.
2. The experimental evaluation is limited to two small-scale benchmarks.
3. The comparison with related work is superficial and doesn't adequately differentiate from recent contributions.
4. The paper doesn't fully address the computational limitations of the method for larger datasets.

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field, but it needs to address the weaknesses mentioned above to fully demonstrate its significance and novelty.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision

1. **Expand theoretical analysis**: Add a section discussing the convergence properties of the PGAS sampler and the quality of the approximate posterior.

2. **Improve experimental evaluation**: Include more extensive experiments on larger or more complex datasets to better demonstrate scalability and robustness. Consider adding a comparison with recent methods like those by Särkkä et al. (2013) and Hensman et al. (2013).

3. **Strengthen related work comparison**: Provide a more thorough comparison with recent contributions in the field, particularly those by Särkkä, Deisenroth, and Hensman.

4. **Clarify notation**: Standardize the notation for state trajectories throughout the paper.

5. **Discuss computational limitations**: Provide a more detailed analysis of how the method scales with increasing time horizon T and number of inducing points M, including practical examples with larger datasets.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel approach to fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs by marginalizing the state transition function
- Well-executed technical development with detailed derivations
- Efficient implementation with careful computational complexity analysis
- Promising experimental results on benchmark systems
- Addresses a key limitation in previous GP-SSM approaches (parametric approximations)

**Con:**
- Limited theoretical guarantees about convergence or quality of posterior approximation
- Experiments confined to two small-scale benchmarks
- Superficial comparison with related work, particularly with recent GP-SSM methods
- Computational complexity remains high for large-scale applications
- Lacks discussion of practical limitations in real-world applications

This paper would make a strong contribution to NeurIPS if the authors address these concerns in revision. The core idea is significant and well-executed, but the paper needs to better demonstrate its novelty, theoretical foundation, and practical significance.
