

# Peer Review: "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

## Summary

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), where the state transition function is modeled as a nonparametric Gaussian process rather than a fixed parametric form. The key contribution is the development of a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), that efficiently samples from the smoothing distribution while marginalizing over the state transition function. The authors address the computational challenges of this approach by introducing a sparse GP-SSM construction using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior, reducing complexity from O(T⁴) to O(M²T). The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on a synthetic nonlinear system benchmark and a cart-pole system, showing its ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions and learn complex dynamics with uncertainty quantification.

This work builds upon and extends previous research on GP-SSMs by Deisenroth et al. [5,6], Wang et al. [8], and Turner et al. [2]. It specifically addresses a limitation in existing approaches that either use parametric approximations of the GP (as in [2,8]) or rely on fixed model structures for inference (as in [5,6]). The paper also leverages recent advances in PMCMC methods [3,4] to solve the non-Markovian inference problem that arises when marginalizing out the state transition function.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant technical contribution by providing a method to fully marginalize the state transition function while maintaining the nonparametric nature of the GP prior. This is a substantial improvement over previous approaches that either use parametric approximations of the GP or require a fixed model for the dynamics.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The mathematical derivations are thorough and well-justified. The authors carefully address the non-Markovian nature of the state process after marginalization and provide a rigorous treatment of the inference problem.

3. **Computational Efficiency**: The introduction of the FIC sparse GP model and the efficient implementation details (Section 4.2) are well-motivated and significantly improve the practical applicability of the method.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on both synthetic and real-world systems demonstrate the method's ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions and learn complex dynamics. The comparison with parametric models and the sparse variant provides strong empirical support for the approach.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the complex methodology accessible to readers. The algorithmic descriptions are precise, and the experimental results are well-presented.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Complexity**: While the paper achieves O(M²T) complexity with the FIC prior, this is still higher than some alternative approaches for learning state-space models. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed comparison of computational costs with other state-of-the-art methods, especially those using variational inference.

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate how sensitive the results are to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the number of inducing points M, the number of particles N). This is particularly important for the sparse GP-SSM, where M is a critical parameter.

3. **Comparison with Variational Methods**: The paper focuses on MCMC-based inference but doesn't compare with variational inference approaches, which are commonly used in Bayesian state-space modeling. A brief comparison would strengthen the paper's position.

4. **Scalability to Larger Systems**: The experiments are limited to small-scale problems (T=200 for the synthetic example, 100 observations for the cart-pole system). It would be valuable to discuss how the method scales to larger systems with higher-dimensional state spaces.

5. **Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper provides empirical evidence of good mixing properties, it lacks theoretical guarantees about the convergence properties of the PGAS sampler for this specific problem.

## Specific Comments

1. **Section 3.3.1**: The explanation of the ancestor sampling probabilities (Equation 6) is slightly confusing. It would be helpful to provide a more intuitive explanation of why these weights are proportional to p(x̃t:T | xi0:t-1).

2. **Section 4.1**: The paper mentions that the inducing inputs can be chosen using greedy methods, but it doesn't discuss how this interacts with the hyperparameter learning process. It would be valuable to clarify whether the inducing inputs are fixed before hyperparameter learning or if they are updated during the MCMC process.

3. **Section 6.1**: The comparison with the parametric model B is interesting, but it would be more convincing if the authors also included a comparison with a standard GP regression approach (treating the state transitions as observations) to demonstrate the advantage of the full Bayesian approach.

4. **Figure 2**: The visualization of the smoothing distribution in Figure 2 (right) could be enhanced by showing the uncertainty intervals around the predictive mean.

5. **Table 1**: The paper reports RMSE values, but it would be helpful to include standard deviations across the 10 independent runs to better understand the variability of the results.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The technical contributions are significant, the methodology is sound, and the experiments provide compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The paper makes a clear and meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for state-space models.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

### Pro-Acceptance:
- Provides a fully Bayesian nonparametric approach to learning state-space models, which is a significant advancement over existing methods that use parametric approximations.
- Solves a fundamental problem in GP-SSM learning by marginalizing out the state transition function while maintaining the nonparametric nature of the model.
- The sparse GP-SSM construction and efficient implementation details make the approach practical for real-world applications.
- The experimental results demonstrate the method's ability to handle challenging multimodal smoothing distributions and complex dynamics.
- The paper is well-written, well-structured, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

### Con-Acceptance:
- The computational complexity (O(M²T)) is still relatively high for large-scale problems, though this is acknowledged by the authors.
- The paper could provide more detailed comparisons with alternative inference methods, particularly variational approaches.
- The scalability to high-dimensional state spaces is not thoroughly demonstrated.
- The sensitivity to hyperparameters (particularly M for the sparse GP) is not fully explored.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed contribution to the field of Bayesian inference in state-space models. The method addresses a significant limitation in existing GP-SSM approaches by providing a fully Bayesian nonparametric learning framework that retains the full expressivity of the GP prior. The paper's strengths in theoretical contribution, methodological innovation, and experimental validation outweigh its minor weaknesses. The work advances the state of the art in Bayesian learning for dynamical systems and will likely be of interest to researchers working on time series analysis, robotics, and reinforcement learning. I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS and should be accepted.
