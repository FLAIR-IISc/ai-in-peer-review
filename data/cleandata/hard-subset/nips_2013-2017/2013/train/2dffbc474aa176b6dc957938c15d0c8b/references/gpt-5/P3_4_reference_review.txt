Summary
The paper addresses fully Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior over the unknown transition function f while assuming a known (possibly parametric) observation model g. Instead of introducing a finite set of parameters for f, the authors marginalize f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ). They then perform posterior inference over states and hyperparameters using Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), which combats path degeneracy and enables joint state/parameter sampling. The paper derives the marginal prior over x1:T as a product of GP predictive conditionals, discusses efficient SMC/PGAS implementations with O(T^3) complexity via Cholesky rank-1 updates, and further reduces cost to O(M^2 T) using a sparse FIC GP prior. Once samples from p(x0:T, θ | y) are obtained, the posterior over f is recovered in closed form at test inputs, resulting in a mixture of GP predictions. Experiments on a nonlinear 1D benchmark and a 4D cart-pole system demonstrate multimodal smoothing handling and predictive uncertainty calibration.

Relation to prior work
- Differs from EM/MAP approaches to GP-SSMs (e.g., Turner et al.; GPDM by Wang et al.) by fully marginalizing f and sampling states and hyperparameters; avoids parametric approximation to f.
- Complementary to Deisenroth et al.’s GP filtering/smoothing with learned models: this paper supplies the learning component.
- Builds on PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.) to tackle non-Markovian latent variable models arising from GP marginalization.
- Adopts FIC sparse GPs (Snelson & Ghahramani; Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen) for scalability, with practical factorization reuse.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Exact marginalization of the transition function f within GP-SSMs and casting the resulting non-Markovian smoothing as a target for PGAS is elegant and addresses strong state–function coupling that plagues joint sampling.
- Methodological soundness: Uses well-founded building blocks (GP regression, PMCMC/PGAS). The conditional particle filter with ancestor sampling is an appropriate tool for the induced non-Markovian structure.
- Computational engineering: Clear attention to complexity with rank-one updates/downdates, careful handling of ancestor weights via concatenated trajectory covariance, and a sparse GP option with O(M^2 T) scaling.
- Practical utility: Produces state smoothing even without a fixed transition model; posterior over f summarized as a mixture of GPs allows multimodality and uncertainty-aware prediction. The use of a mean function to encode domain prior is a pragmatic and flexible modeling feature.
- Experiments show desirable qualitative behavior (multimodal smoothing, sensible uncertainty on cart-pole).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Proposal distribution in SMC (Eq. 5) is unclear. The text states x_t is drawn from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t), which in general is intractable; later comments suggest sampling from the prior p(xt | θ, x0:t−1) and weighting by p(yt | xt). Please clarify the actual proposal used, and whether any auxiliary lookahead or locally optimal proposals are employed.
- Scalability and complexity accounting: While O(T^3) and O(M^2 T) are given, the dependence on number of particles N is not explicit. Maintaining a Cholesky per particle suggests overall costs scale with N; a precise per-iteration complexity in T, N, nx, and output dimension would help practitioners.
- Inducing inputs: The sparse FIC variant is promising, but the choice/selection of inducing inputs is not integrated into inference. Fixing them across PGAS may limit performance; discuss strategies (e.g., optimizing inducing locations between PGAS sweeps or using variational inducing variables) and their computational impact. Empirical comparisons between full and sparse variants are limited.
- Experimental evaluation breadth: Benchmarks are narrow (1D nonlinear system and a 4D cart-pole with 100 observations). No quantitative comparisons against strong baselines such as EM/GPDM, variational GP-SSMs, or alternative PMCMC variants (e.g., particle Gibbs with backward simulation) are presented. Runtime, ESS, mixing diagnostics, and sensitivity to N, M, and the mean function are not reported.
- High-dimensional states: The approach inherits the curse of dimensionality in GP inputs. Discuss kernel choices (e.g., ARD), priors, and structure (e.g., additive/structured kernels) to handle nx > 10. The method’s performance in higher dimensions is unclear.
- Identifiability and priors: While the paper reasonably fixes g to avoid non-identifiability, the interaction among f, Q, and x can still create multi-modality and weak identifiability. A discussion of prior choices and robustness would be valuable.
- Theoretical guarantees: PGAS invariance is cited, but there is no analysis of mixing in the non-Markovian setting induced by GP marginalization or guidance on particle numbers beyond qualitative statements.

Quality
Technically sound with correct use of GP predictive conditionals and PMCMC. However, key implementation details (proposal, complexity in N) and broader empirical validation are missing, which weakens the empirical support for generality and scalability claims.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with helpful implementation insights. Some notational overload and the proposal distribution ambiguity (Eq. 5) hinder reproducibility. Providing pseudocode for the CPF-AS step with explicit proposals and costs would improve clarity.

Originality
The combination of exact marginalization of GP transition dynamics with PGAS for GP-SSMs appears novel and timely. The sparse FIC instantiation within this framework and the efficient ancestor-weight computation are useful contributions.

Significance
Addresses an important problem—learning flexible nonlinear dynamics with uncertainty quantification. The approach is likely to influence subsequent work on Bayesian nonparametric state-space modeling and will be of interest to both ML and control communities. Broader empirical evidence is needed to fully establish impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and justify the proposal distribution; if using prior proposals, discuss alternatives and their effect on variance.
- Provide explicit complexity in terms of T, N, M, nx, output dimension, and discuss memory costs.
- Expand experiments: include comparisons to EM/variational GP-SSMs and PG variants; report ESS, autocorrelations, runtimes; ablate N and M; compare full vs FIC accuracy and speed; test higher-dimensional systems.
- Discuss inducing input selection strategies and possibly include a simple adaptive scheme.
- Analyze sensitivity to mean function misspecification and priors on Q and kernel hyperparameters.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, principled Bayesian treatment that preserves nonparametric flexibility by marginalizing f.
- Sound use of PGAS tailored to the non-Markovian model, with thoughtful computational techniques.
- Conceptually important contribution that fills a gap between GP-based filtering/smoothing and learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Ambiguity in key algorithmic details (proposal in SMC), incomplete complexity accounting.
- Limited experimental validation and baseline comparisons; scalability and robustness not convincingly demonstrated.
- Sparse GP integration is underdeveloped empirically and methodologically.

Overall, this is a strong and interesting paper with notable methodological contributions, but it would benefit from clarifying core algorithmic choices and bolstering empirical validation.
