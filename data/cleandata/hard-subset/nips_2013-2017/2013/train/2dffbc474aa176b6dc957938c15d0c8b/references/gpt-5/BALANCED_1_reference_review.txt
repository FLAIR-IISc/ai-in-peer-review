Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and smoothing in nonlinear Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior on the transition function f while keeping the observation model g known (possibly parametric). Rather than parameterizing f, the method analytically marginalizes f, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ) that factors into GP predictive conditionals. Posterior inference over states and hyperparameters is then performed with particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), using a conditional particle filter adapted to the non-Markovian structure. Hyperparameters are sampled given x0:T, leveraging GP regression and slice sampling. To address computational costs, the paper develops an efficient implementation reusing Cholesky factorizations across time and proposes a sparse GP (FIC) version reducing complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). Experiments on a nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole system illustrate the approach. The method also provides a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution for f(·), with the option to distill to a single GP for downstream use.

This work connects to a line of GP-based dynamical systems: GPDM (Wang, Fleet, Hertzmann, NIPS 2005), EM/MAP-based learning with GP-like parametric forms (Turner et al., AISTATS 2010; Ghahramani & Roweis, NIPS 1998), and approximate filtering/smoothing with learned GPs (Deisenroth et al., NIPS 2012; TAC 2012). The distinctive contribution here is to retain the nonparametric GP prior by collapsing f and performing state/hyperparameter inference with modern PMCMC (Andrieu et al., JRSSB 2010; Lindsten et al., NIPS 2012). Relative to MAP/EM and variational approaches to GP-SSMs (e.g., Frigola et al., NIPS 2013/2014), this paper offers an exact-in-principle Bayesian treatment modulo Monte Carlo error, with a novel use of PGAS for non-Markovian latent models.

Evaluation by criteria

Quality
- Soundness: The collapsing derivation is correct; the resulting non-Markovian prior over trajectories is properly handled with PGAS. The ancestor sampling weights are derived from the ratio of joint priors and are computable via the GP trajectory prior. Hyperparameter updates reduce to GP regression with Q as likelihood covariance; slice sampling is appropriate.
- Algorithmic details: The paper thoughtfully addresses computational issues: per-particle Cholesky maintenance with rank-1 updates, and an FIC prior to reduce scaling. However, overall complexity per PGAS iteration likely scales as O(N T^3) (or O(N M^2 T) with FIC); this could be stated more explicitly, including memory costs.
- Empirics: Experiments convincingly show handling of multimodal smoothing and calibrated predictive uncertainty. That said, comparisons to strong GP-SSM baselines (e.g., variational GP-SSM) and more comprehensive metrics (predictive log-likelihood, state RMSE vs. ground truth, runtime/mixing diagnostics) are limited.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, with clear motivation and a concise derivation of the collapsed prior. The CPF-AS description is precise; implementation details are helpful.
- Some notation is dense (e.g., block indexing of kernel matrices). A small worked example or pseudocode for CPF-AS in the non-Markovian setting would help reproducibility.
- Complexity statements would benefit from a summary table and explicit per-iteration costs as a function of T, N, nx, and M.

Originality
- Marginalizing f and targeting p(x0:T, θ | y) with PGAS in GP-SSMs is, to my knowledge, novel at the time and distinct from EM/MAP or variational approaches. The ancestor sampling adaptation to non-Markovian latent structure is a nice contribution. The sparse FIC formulation within this collapsed PGAS framework is also a useful design.

Significance
- The ability to perform fully Bayesian smoothing and learning in GP-SSMs without parameterizing f is important. This provides calibrated uncertainty in both states and dynamics and can serve as a core component for downstream tasks (planning, control, filtering with learned models).
- The approach is computationally heavy; practical adoption hinges on sparse approximations and careful engineering. Demonstrating scalability and robustness across higher-dimensional systems would strengthen the case.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and principled Bayesian treatment preserving nonparametric flexibility.
- Clever use of PGAS to address path degeneracy and non-Markovian structure.
- Solid implementation insights (factorization reuse, rank-1 updates; FIC-based complexity reduction).
- Experiments demonstrate advantages in multimodal settings and uncertainty calibration.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited empirical comparisons. Please add baselines: variational GP-SSM (e.g., Frigola et al., NIPS 2013/2014), GPDM, and MAP/EM methods (Turner et al., Wang et al.). Report predictive log-likelihoods, state RMSE, and uncertainty calibration (e.g., PIT histograms).
- Scalability not fully quantified. Provide wall-clock/runtime vs. T, N, M; memory usage; ablation over N and chain length; autocorrelation plots; effective sample sizes. Compare PGAS to PG with backward simulation (Lindsten & Schön, 2012) and to PMMH for this model.
- Identifiability and priors. Discuss scale ambiguities between kernel amplitude and Q, and the impact of mean-function misspecification. Recommend priors or constraints to mitigate degeneracies.
- High-dimensional states. Clarify how costs scale with nx and whether structured kernels (separable/additive) or Kronecker structure could be exploited. A higher-dimensional benchmark would help.
- Observation model. While fixing g is reasonable, please discuss extensions to partially unknown g, identifiability issues, and how the framework would change (e.g., joint GP priors with constraints).
- Sparse GP details. How are inducing inputs selected during inference? Show sensitivity to M and active set selection strategies; consider variational inducing-point learning for stability.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, novel inference framework for GP-SSMs preserving nonparametric character.
- Theoretical correctness and careful algorithmic design.
- Practical contributions (PGAS adaptation, sparse construction, implementation tricks).
- Addresses an important problem with potential impact in time-series modeling and control.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is somewhat narrow; missing comparisons to strongest contemporary baselines and detailed scalability/mixing analyses.
- Computational burden remains high; limited evidence of performance on larger T/nx.
- Some clarity gaps around complexity counts and reproducibility details.

Recommendation
Lean accept. This paper advances Bayesian learning and inference in GP-SSMs in a substantive and principled way. Strengthening the experimental section with stronger baselines, scalability/mixing diagnostics, and an additional higher-dimensional dataset would make it a clear accept.
