

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper proposes a Bayesian approach to learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) methods. The authors present a method to marginalize out the state transition function f, enabling fully Bayesian inference over both the latent states and the GP model parameters. The key innovation is the use of Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) to handle the non-Markovian dependencies introduced by marginalizing f, and they develop a sparse GP-SSM construction to reduce computational complexity.

The paper builds upon established work in GP regression (Rasmussen & Williams), state-space models (Deisenroth et al.), and PMCMC methods (Andrieu et al.). The marginalization approach is conceptually related to "collapsing" techniques in MCMC, and the sparse GP implementation follows standard approaches (e.g., FIC prior, Snelson & Ghahramani). The theoretical development is technically sound, with clear derivations of the marginal prior and the PGAS algorithm.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper presents a theoretically rigorous approach to Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs, avoiding the common practice of approximating the GP with a parametric model.
2. The marginalization approach properly handles the non-Markovian structure introduced by the GP dynamics.
3. The sparse GP implementation is well-explained and follows standard practices.
4. The paper correctly acknowledges related work in the literature, including Deisenroth et al. and Turner et al.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of comprehensive experimental validation:** The paper provides only two synthetic experiments (a nonlinear benchmark and a cart-pole system) without thorough comparison to established baselines. The comparison in Table 1 includes only two parametric models and the ground truth, omitting more relevant baselines like the methods of Deisenroth et al. (2011, 2012) who developed refined approximation methods for GP-based SSMs. The RMSE comparisons are limited to a single metric without showing predictive distributions or uncertainty quantification, which are key advantages of Bayesian approaches.

2. **Insufficient evidence for claimed advantages:** The paper claims that "the smoothing distribution accurately portrays the underlying system" and that "the GP allows for accurate sampling from the smoothing distribution even when the parametric model fails." However, the figures (Figure 2) show qualitative comparisons without quantitative evidence of improvement over existing methods.

3. **Computational complexity claims lack empirical support:** The paper claims computational complexity improvements (from O(T^4) to O(T^3) and O(M^2T) for sparse GP), but provides no runtime measurements to validate these claims. Without empirical evidence of computational gains, the practical utility of the sparse implementation remains questionable.

4. **Overstated novelty:** The paper's main contribution—marginalizing the GP dynamics and using PMCMC for inference—is not sufficiently distinguished from prior work. The approach builds directly on established PMCMC methods (Andrieu et al., 2010; Lindsten et al., 2012), and the sparse GP implementation is a standard technique (Snelson & Ghahramani, 2005; Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen, 2005). The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how their approach differs from or improves upon existing Bayesian approaches to GP-SSMs.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper does not adequately address the limitations of their approach. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the method scales with increasing state dimension, the sensitivity to the choice of inducing points in the sparse implementation, or the computational burden of the PGAS sampler for long time series.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a technically sound approach to Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs, it falls short of demonstrating sufficient novelty or practical significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The method builds on well-established techniques without providing compelling evidence that it outperforms existing approaches in meaningful ways. The experimental validation is inadequate, lacking comprehensive comparisons against relevant baselines and failing to demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method.

The paper's greatest weakness is the absence of thorough empirical validation. The authors claim their approach "retains the full nonparametric expressivity of the model" and provides "accurate sampling from the smoothing distribution," but they don't provide sufficient evidence to support these claims. Without rigorous comparison to established methods and clear demonstration of advantages, the paper reads more like a methodological description than a significant contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper is technically correct and builds on solid foundations, it does not meet the high standards of NIPS for novelty, significance, and thorough empirical validation. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their experimental section with comprehensive comparisons to relevant baselines, detailed analysis of computational costs, and clear demonstration of practical advantages before this work could be considered for publication at NIPS.
