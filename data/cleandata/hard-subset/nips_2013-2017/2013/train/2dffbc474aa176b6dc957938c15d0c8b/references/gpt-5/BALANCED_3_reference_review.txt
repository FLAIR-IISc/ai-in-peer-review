Summary
This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing the unknown transition function f and performing inference over latent states x0:T and hyperparameters θ using Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). The key idea is to collapse f to obtain a non-Markovian prior over trajectories p(x1:T|x0,θ) represented as a product of GP one-step conditionals, and then use PGAS to draw samples from the exact smoothing distribution p(x0:T|θ,y0:T). Hyperparameters are sampled given x0:T, with θy handled via conjugacy and θx (GP covariance and Q) via slice sampling using the GP marginal likelihood with inputs x0:T−1 and outputs x1:T. The paper also proposes a sparse GP-SSM using the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2T), and provides implementation details (rank-one Cholesky updates/downdates per time step) to make PGAS efficient for the non-Markovian model. Experiments on a nonlinear benchmark (with multimodal smoothing) and a 4D cart-pole system demonstrate that the method can recover dynamics and provide calibrated uncertainty.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself relative to GPDS/GP-SSM work that typically relies on MAP/EM or uses learned GPs for inference (e.g., Turner et al., Deisenroth et al., Wang et al.). It builds on PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.) by applying these to a collapsed GP-SSM and extending ancestor sampling to the non-Markovian trajectory prior induced by marginalizing f. The sparse GP construction follows Snelson & Ghahramani and Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The collapsing of f is principled and leads to a coherent non-Markovian prior. The use of PGAS is appropriate and yields an exact (up to Monte Carlo error) smoothing kernel invariant to p(x0:T|θ,y0:T). The derivation of ancestor sampling weights for the non-Markovian case and their efficient computation using updated Cholesky factors are well motivated. Sampling θx via slice sampling on the GP marginal likelihood conditioned on x is standard.
- Empirics: The experiments are illustrative (multimodality captured; calibrated uncertainty on cart-pole). However, the empirical evaluation is limited in breadth and lacks comparisons to strong baselines and thorough scaling analysis. Reported iteration counts (50) and particle numbers (N=20) are modest; mixing diagnostics and wall-clock are missing.

Clarity
- Overall, the paper is clearly written and well organized. The notation around K, K̃, block indices, and the equivalence between (3) and (4) is dense but largely correct. The practical implementation section is useful but could benefit from pseudo-code and clearer guidance on reusing factorizations. The assumption that g is known (possibly up to parameters) is clearly stated.

Originality
- The main novelty is the fully collapsed treatment of the GP transition within a PMCMC framework, using PGAS for non-Markovian trajectory priors and delivering exact smoothing while retaining the nonparametric nature of the dynamics. The efficient ancestor sampling and factorization reuse tailored to this non-Markovian setting, together with the FIC-as-prior construction, are original and useful contributions.

Significance
- The method fills an important gap: principled Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs without parametric surrogates for f and with proper uncertainty over states and dynamics. This is valuable for time-series modeling and control where uncertainty quantification and multimodality matter. Practical impact will depend on scalability; the sparse variant and implementation tricks are promising, but more evidence is needed for higher-dimensional states and longer sequences.

Pros
- Principled, fully Bayesian formulation that preserves GP nonparametric flexibility by marginalizing f.
- Use of PGAS yields exact smoothing kernels for a challenging non-Markovian latent process.
- Careful algorithmic engineering (rank-one updates; FIC prior) with complexity analyses.
- Expressive predictive distribution as a mixture of Gaussians; ability to encode prior dynamics in the GP mean.
- Convincing qualitative results on multimodal smoothing and uncertainty calibration.

Cons
- Limited empirical evaluation: small number of datasets and no comparison against strong baselines (EM/MAP GP-SSMs, GPDM, variational methods, learned parametric SSMs with particle smoothing).
- No ablations on number of particles, chain length, inducing points M, or dependence on priors and mean functions. Missing mixing diagnostics and runtime benchmarks.
- Assumption of known measurement function g reduces generality; identifiability and robustness when g is misspecified are not explored.
- Selection of inducing inputs I is not addressed experimentally; potential sensitivity is not discussed.
- Some dense derivations and implementation details would benefit from pseudo-code and more explicit guidance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative comparisons on standard benchmarks to (i) EM/MAP-based GP-SSM learning (e.g., Turner et al.; Ghahramani & Roweis), (ii) GPDM (Wang et al.), and (iii) particle smoothers conditioned on learned parametric models. Include predictive RMSE, log-likelihood, and smoothing error metrics.
- Provide mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS), runtime vs N, T, nx, and wall-clock per iteration. Show how performance scales and where the sparse FIC prior makes a difference; include ablations on M and inducing input selection strategies.
- Explore sensitivity to the GP mean function: zero mean vs informed parametric mean; quantify gains. Report priors for θ and their impact.
- Consider estimating (parts of) g or at least unknown parameters θy to demonstrate robustness to measurement model misspecification; discuss identifiability issues.
- Clarify the computation of ancestor sampling weights in practice (numerical stability, reuse of sub-factors). Include pseudo-code for the CPF-AS with the non-Markovian prior.
- Discuss memory complexity and practical limits for long sequences; consider strategies such as blocking or windowed PGAS.
- Release code to facilitate adoption.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a principled, technically solid contribution that advances Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs by combining collapsing with PGAS, and it provides practical algorithmic insights. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying certain implementation aspects would substantially improve the paper’s impact.
