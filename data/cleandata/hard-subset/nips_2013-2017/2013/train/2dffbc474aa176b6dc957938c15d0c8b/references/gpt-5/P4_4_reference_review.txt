Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) where the unknown transition function f is modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP), yielding a GP-SSM. The observation function g is assumed known (possibly with finite-dimensional parameters). The key methodological step is to marginalize out the transition function f exactly, resulting in a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ) that can be written as a product of GP one-step predictive Gaussians. Posterior inference is then performed over states x0:T and hyperparameters θ using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). This allows sampling from the exact smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T), despite the loss of Markov structure after marginalizing f, while avoiding the strong dependencies that plague joint sampling of (x0:T, f).

Given samples from the smoothing distribution, the posterior over f becomes standard GP regression with inputs x0:T−1, outputs x1:T, and likelihood covariance Q, enabling closed-form conditionals and predictive densities. The paper also develops computational strategies: efficient Cholesky rank-1 updates/downdates to reduce the naive O(T^4) cost to O(T^3), and a sparse construction using the FIC inducing-point prior to reach O(M^2 T) with M inducing variables. Hyperparameters are sampled via slice sampling conditioned on x0:T, leveraging the GP marginal likelihood.

Experiments include: (i) a 1D nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing due to sign ambiguity, showing that PGAS with the GP prior recovers accurate smoothing and transition dynamics even when the GP mean function is misspecified; and (ii) a 4D cart-pole system, demonstrating one-step predictive performance and calibrated uncertainty from a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution. Results are compared to parametric baselines in the 1D case, with an additional sparse FIC variant.

This work connects to GP dynamical systems and GP-SSM literature such as EM/MAP learning with parametric GP representations [2, 7, 8], and to GP-based filtering/smoothing with learned dynamics [5, 6]. On the inference side, it builds on PMCMC [3] and PGAS [4], adapting these to the non-Markovian structure induced by marginalizing f. The sparse GP component follows the FIC framework [17, 16].

Quality (technical soundness)
- The marginalization of f and resulting expressions (Eq. 3–4) are consistent with GP predictive conditionals; the paper correctly notes that the induced trajectory prior is not Gaussian because the kernel and mean depend on the unknown x’s.
- Using PGAS to target p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) in a non-Markovian model is appropriate; the ancestor sampling weights (Eq. 6) exploit ratios of unnormalized targets and can be computed from the prior over trajectories.
- The hyperparameter update conditioned on a trajectory reduces to GP regression marginal likelihood; slice sampling is a reasonable generic choice.
- Complexity analysis and implementation details (rank-1 Cholesky updates, reuse of factors) are plausible; the FIC derivations align with standard inducing-point algebra and the use of Woodbury identities.
- Open technical details that would benefit from clarification:
  - The proposal in Eq. (5) uses p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t). For the non-Markovian prior, the “optimal” proposal including yt is generally nontrivial; later sections rely on CPF-AS with a prior-like proposal. Clarifying the exact proposal used within CPF-AS and the resulting weight expressions would improve reproducibility.
  - The stated O(T^3) (and O(M^2 T)) costs appear to omit the dependence on the number of particles N; clarifying total computational and memory complexity as functions of (N, T, M) would be useful, especially since per-particle Cholesky factors are maintained.
  - Numerical stability of repeated rank-one downdates and memory costs for storing factors across particles merit discussion.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well-structured, and careful in notation. The derivation of the non-Markovian prior and the PGAS kernel invariance are explained with appropriate references. Implementation guidance is valuable, though certain choices (proposal distribution for CPF-AS, details of hyperparameter priors and slice sampling settings, and inducing point selection strategy) could be described more concretely. Experimental descriptions are understandable, but the absence of reported numerical values (e.g., Table 1 is referenced but not shown here) limits assessment.

Originality
The combination of (i) exact marginalization of the GP transition to retain nonparametric expressivity, (ii) inference via PGAS in the resulting non-Markovian latent model, and (iii) a sparse FIC variant with tailored computational updates, appears novel relative to EM/MAP approaches [2, 7, 8] and to prior GPDS work focused on filtering/smoothing with learned dynamics [5, 6]. Applying PGAS with ancestor sampling specifically to GP-SSMs and leveraging the exact GP conditionals for hyperparameter sampling are meaningful contributions.

Significance
The methodology advances fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs, enabling smoothing without committing to a parametric transition. This is impactful for time-series modeling and control where model uncertainty matters. The approach offers a principled alternative to EM/MAP methods and could serve as a foundation for downstream tasks (e.g., robust controllers using mixtures of GP predictions). Computational scaling (O(N T^3) or O(N M^2 T)) may limit very long sequences, but the sparse variant and efficient updates mitigate costs. The experimental section demonstrates feasibility and benefits (multimodal smoothing, calibrated uncertainty), though broader benchmarks and state-of-the-art comparisons would strengthen claims.

Pros
- Fully Bayesian, nonparametric treatment by marginalizing f; closed-form conditionals for f given trajectories.
- Appropriate and principled use of PGAS to handle non-Markovian dependencies; good mixing expected from ancestor sampling.
- Thoughtful computational treatment (Cholesky updates; FIC prior) to reduce complexity.
- Demonstrates handling of multimodal smoothing and uncertainty quantification.
- Clear connections to and differentiation from prior GP-SSM learning methods.

Cons
- Limited experimental evaluation: few datasets, limited baselines (especially in the 4D cart-pole), and missing quantitative details (e.g., runtime, ESS, sensitivity to N, M, hyperpriors).
- Complexity analysis omits explicit N dependence and memory considerations; practical scalability bounds are not quantified.
- Some algorithmic details under-specified (exact CPF-AS proposal and weights in the non-Markovian setting; slice sampling configuration; inducing point selection).
- No ablation on the impact of the mean function choice or on sparse vs. full GP trade-offs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide complete quantitative results (e.g., Table 1 numbers), runtime, and mixing diagnostics; include comparisons to EM/MAP GP-SSM learning [2, 8] and to GPDS inference baselines [5, 6] where applicable.
- Detail the CPF-AS proposal and weight computations used in practice and report the total computational/memory complexity as functions of N, T, M.
- Add sensitivity analyses (N, M, inducing set selection) and robustness to mean function misspecification.
- Discuss numerical stability of factor updates and any regularization used.

Overall assessment
The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and proposes a novel and significant approach to Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs by marginalizing the transition function and leveraging PGAS. With stronger empirical evaluation and clarified implementation details, it would be a solid contribution.
