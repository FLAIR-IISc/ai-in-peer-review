Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The core idea is to place a GP prior over the unknown state transition function f, keep a known/parametric observation model g, and marginalize f analytically to avoid the strong posterior coupling between f and the latent state trajectory x0:T. This marginalization yields a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories (expressible as a product of Gaussian conditionals, eqs. (3)–(4)). The authors then target p(θ, x0:T | y0:T) using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), to sample the smoothing distribution efficiently despite the non-Markovian dependence. Given samples of x0:T and θ, the posterior over f is obtained in closed form (standard GP regression conditionals), and predictive densities are approximated as mixtures of Gaussians across posterior draws. The paper also presents a sparse GP-SSM construction based on the FIC approximation to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and practical implementation details (incremental Cholesky updates, ancestor sampling weights).

Relative to prior work: Deisenroth et al. [5,6] developed filtering/smoothing for learned GP dynamics; this paper supplies the “learning” component in a fully Bayesian manner. Turner et al. [2] and Ghahramani & Roweis [7] optimize parametric GP-like models (EM/MAP) rather than marginalizing f; Wang et al. [8] also use MAP for GPDM. The PMCMC framework [3] and PGAS [4] are the inferential engines; the paper adapts them to the non-Markovian prior induced by marginalizing a GP. Sparse GP ideas (FIC, pseudo-inputs) [16,15] are used to further reduce complexity.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Collapsing the GP transition function in a GP-SSM is elegant and principled, directly addressing the x–f coupling that hinders MCMC. Using PGAS to sample in a non-Markovian latent model is a good fit.
- Methodological soundness: The target distribution is exact; PGAS leaves the smoothing distribution invariant. The sampler alternates between CPF-AS for x0:T and slice sampling for θ; the conditional structure is well-motivated.
- Practicality: Clear computational analysis, with rank-one updates and a sparse FIC variant to reduce cost. The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive view is informative and flexible; the paper discusses thinning/herding for efficiency.
- Empirical: Demonstrates handling of multimodal smoothing (benchmark with sign ambiguity) and a 4D cart-pole example, with uncertainty quantification and a sparse-GP variant.

Weaknesses
- Experimental depth: Limited quantitative evaluation. The benchmark uses T=200 and a single RMSE table (values not shown here); cart-pole results are qualitative and based on 100 observations. There is no systematic comparison to strong baselines (e.g., GPDM [8], EM-based GP-SSMs [2], nonparametric particle smoothers) in terms of accuracy and runtime.
- Scalability and complexity: While O(M^2 T) is an improvement, the constant factors for CPF-AS (ancestor sampling weights over long horizons; maintaining per-particle Cholesky factors) and the dependence on state dimension nx are not empirically characterized. Guidance on choosing N, M, and their effect on mixing, ESS, and wall-clock time is missing.
- Modeling assumptions: The observation model g is assumed known (up to θy), which limits applicability; identifiability issues are acknowledged but not empirically probed. Selection of inducing inputs is left unspecified; sensitivity analyses are absent. Potential confounding between Q and GP amplitude hyperparameters is not discussed.
- Clarity and reproducibility: While the derivations are generally clear, some implementation-heavy details (e.g., computing ancestor weights via eq. (6) efficiently in high dimensions) could be elaborated, and experimental settings (priors, initializations, run times) should be more explicit.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound and coherent. The use of collapsing and PGAS is appropriate; the invariance properties and conditional structure are correct. The main limitation is the modest empirical evaluation and absence of diagnostic analyses (autocorrelation, ESS, sensitivity).
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. Equations (3)–(4) and Algorithm 1 are helpful. Some notation is dense (omitting inputs ut), and more detail on practical choices (N, M, θ priors) would improve reproducibility.
- Originality: High. Marginalizing the GP transition in an SSM and sampling states via PGAS in a non-Markovian setting is novel compared to EM/MAP alternatives; combining this with a sparse FIC prior is a useful twist.
- Significance: Potentially strong. A fully Bayesian, nonparametric treatment of SSM dynamics is of broad interest in time series, control, and RL. However, the limited empirical validation tempers the immediate impact.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative comparisons (accuracy and runtime) against EM/MAP GP-SSMs [2,8] and filtering/smoothing pipelines that learn f first [5,6]. Include higher-dimensional benchmarks if possible.
- Report mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS) for x0:T and θ across N∈{10,20,50}, and show sensitivity to M and inducing input selection strategy. Include wall-clock timings and memory usage.
- Clarify inducing input selection in the FIC experiments (greedy vs. fixed grid), and analyze sensitivity to M.
- Discuss identifiability and posterior coupling between Q and GP kernel amplitude/lengthscales; show posterior marginals or priors that mitigate degeneracy.
- Explore partial learning of g (e.g., low-dimensional parametric uncertainty) to demonstrate robustness when g is misspecified.
- For cart-pole, add quantitative metrics (1-step/rollout RMSE, NLL) and comparisons to physics-informed or learned baselines.
- Provide code or pseudocode for the efficient ancestor weight computation and Cholesky updates, and specify all priors used.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, elegant Bayesian treatment; exact marginalization and PGAS are combined in a novel way.
- Solid methodological contribution likely to be used by others.
- Clear path to practical use via sparse variant and implementation insights.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and depth, with few rigorous comparisons and no runtime/mixing diagnostics.
- Applicability constrained by assuming known observation model; scalability in higher dimensions not convincingly demonstrated.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers a solid and original methodological advance with careful algorithmic design. Strengthening the empirical section as suggested would make the case unequivocal.
