

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach for learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function f, enabling nonparametric learning of dynamics while still allowing sampling from the smoothing distribution. The authors address a key limitation in existing GP-SSM approaches, which typically use parametric representations of the dynamics. Their core contribution is the use of Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) to efficiently sample from the smoothing distribution while marginalizing over the state transition function. This contrasts with conventional approaches that require a fixed model of the transition dynamics.

The paper builds upon and extends prior work in several important ways. It relates to Deisenroth et al.'s work on GP-based SSMs for filtering and smoothing, but addresses the missing component of learning the GP dynamics in a fully Bayesian manner. It also distinguishes itself from Turner et al.'s EM-based approach and Wang et al.'s MAP estimation method by maintaining the full nonparametric nature of the GP prior. The authors clearly position their work within the NIPS literature, citing relevant prior work and explicitly stating how their approach differs.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations. The marginalization of the state transition function is well-justified, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the resulting non-Markovian structure. The PGAS algorithm is properly tailored to the problem, with a clear explanation of how it handles the marginalization. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, with careful consideration of both vanilla and sparse GP implementations. The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the method's ability to learn dynamics even when the parametric mean function is a poor approximation of the true dynamics. The authors correctly acknowledge that their approach is limited to the specific PMCMC method (PGAS) they employ, rather than claiming generality to all PMCMC algorithms.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent, and the flow from problem statement to solution is logical. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the key algorithms. The experimental section effectively illustrates the method's capabilities through both synthetic and real-world examples (nonlinear system benchmark and cart-pole system). The figures clearly convey the multimodal nature of the smoothing distribution and the accuracy of the learned dynamics.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution—marginalizing out the state transition function in GP-SSMs and using PGAS for inference—is novel. While there is prior work on GP-SSMs (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al., Wang et al.), the authors' approach of fully marginalizing the dynamics function while maintaining nonparametric expressivity represents a significant advancement. The paper clearly articulates how this work differs from previous contributions, and the technical details of the marginalization and PGAS adaptation appear to be new. The sparse GP implementation using FIC prior is a useful extension but not the main novelty.

**Significance:** The problem of learning nonlinear state-space models is highly relevant to many applications in robotics, control, and time series analysis. The authors' method provides a principled Bayesian approach to this problem that avoids the limitations of parametric approximations. The experimental results demonstrate that their approach can accurately model complex dynamics even when the parametric baseline is poor, which has significant practical implications. The method advances the state of the art by enabling fully nonparametric learning of dynamics while still providing accurate smoothing distributions. The mixture-of-Gaussians representation of the predictive distribution is particularly valuable for capturing multimodality in the dynamics.

**Strengths:**
1. Clear and rigorous mathematical formulation of the marginalization approach
2. Effective use of PGAS for inference in the non-Markovian setting
3. Comprehensive analysis of computational complexity and efficient implementation
4. Thorough experimental validation showing advantages over parametric baselines
5. Clear distinction from prior work and precise articulation of contributions

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited comparison to other nonparametric approaches for SSM learning beyond GP-based methods
2. No explicit discussion of scalability limitations for very long time series
3. The sparse GP implementation is mentioned but not thoroughly evaluated in terms of practical computational benefits
4. The paper could provide more concrete timing comparisons between their method and baselines
5. The paper doesn't explore the impact of different kernel choices on performance

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to Bayesian inference for state-space models. The method addresses a meaningful problem in a principled way, with clear advantages demonstrated through experiments. The writing is excellent, and the technical details are well-explained. While there are some minor limitations in the experimental comparison and scalability discussion, these do not detract from the paper's core contribution. The work aligns well with NIPS's focus on theoretical and methodological advances in machine learning.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** Novel marginalization approach for GP-SSMs, rigorous theoretical foundation, effective PMCMC adaptation, thorough experimental validation, clear writing, significant potential impact on Bayesian time series modeling.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** Limited comparison to non-GP nonparametric methods, no explicit scalability analysis for very long sequences, sparse GP implementation lacks practical performance evaluation.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian time series modeling and would be valuable to the NIPS community. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches.
