Summary
The paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) with unknown nonlinear transition dynamics f and known observation model g. The key idea is to marginalize the latent transition function f analytically under a GP prior, which induces a non-Markovian prior p(x0:T | θ) over state trajectories. This “collapsed” model is then handled by a Particle MCMC scheme, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to sample x0:T and hyperparameters θ, while preserving the nonparametric character of the dynamics. The authors further propose a sparse construction via the FIC prior to reduce computational cost from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and give implementation details to reuse Cholesky factorizations and perform efficient rank-1 updates. They demonstrate the method on a nonlinear scalar benchmark (with sign ambiguity and multimodal smoothing) and a 4D cart-pole system, showing that the learned predictive distribution (a mixture of GPs) captures uncertainty and multimodality; they also illustrate that informative GP mean functions can incorporate prior structural knowledge.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on GP-SSMs and GP dynamical models (e.g., Wang, Fleet & Hertzmann; Turner et al.; Deisenroth et al.) but differs by maintaining a nonparametric GP over f and collapsing f out rather than optimizing over a finite set of pseudo-targets or using EM/MAP. On the inference side, it leverages recent PMCMC advances—Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (Lindsten et al.)—to address path degeneracy and efficiently sample global state trajectories in a non-Markovian latent model. The sparse GP prior (FIC; Snelson & Ghahramani; Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen) is used to further reduce cost.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The marginalization of f and the resulting expressions for the one-step predictive factors and the joint prior p(x1:t | x0, θ) are correct. Using ancestor sampling weights proportional to p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) is appropriate given conditional independence of observations. The PGAS kernel invariance is well grounded.
- Algorithmic detail: The paper provides concrete CPF-AS implementation details, including how to reuse Cholesky factors across time and perform rank-one updates/downdates, and an FIC-based construction yielding O(M^2 T) arithmetic. However, the asymptotic complexity omits the dependence on the number of particles N, state dimension nx, and memory requirements; a more complete accounting would help.
- Empirical support: The scalar benchmark convincingly illustrates multimodal smoothing and robustness to a misspecified mean function. The cart-pole example demonstrates scalability to 4D states and sensible uncertainty. That said, quantitative baselines on cart-pole are missing, and mixing diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation) and runtime scaling are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and clear, especially the exposition of marginalization and CPF-AS. Some notational density remains (e.g., K̃({xi0:t−1, x̃t:T−1})) and could benefit from a schematic to show how concatenated trajectories map to kernel blocks. The complexity discussion should explicitly include N, nx, and memory. Sampling details for θx (priors, parameterization of Q, slice sampling schedule) are only briefly mentioned and could be expanded.

Originality
The combination of collapsing the GP transition function with PGAS for joint smoothing/hyperparameter learning in GP-SSMs is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. Prior GP-SSM work often resorted to EM/MAP or approximate variational methods with parametric finite representations; here the method preserves nonparametric expressivity and leverages exact MCMC kernels for the collapsed model. Using FIC as a prior (rather than as a post-hoc approximation) is a thoughtful perspective.

Significance
Learning flexible nonlinear dynamics with calibrated uncertainty is important in time series, control, and RL. The method provides a principled Bayesian answer to “learn f while smoothing x,” and the collapsed PGAS approach is potentially impactful. If computational aspects scale as claimed with sparse priors, this could be a practical alternative to EM/variational GP-SSMs. The ideas should be of interest to the NeurIPS community.

Strengths
- Elegant collapsing of f leading to principled non-Markovian inference.
- Use of PGAS addresses path degeneracy and yields an exact (invariance) kernel for smoothing.
- Clear route to hyperparameter inference; separation of θx and θy is sensible.
- Thoughtful implementation details (factor reuse, rank updates) and a sparse (FIC) construction to reduce cost.
- Experiments demonstrate multimodal smoothing and uncertainty-aware prediction.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Complexity and memory: The paper states O(T^3) (vanilla) and O(M^2 T) (FIC) but does not account for N, nx, and memory. In CPF-AS, maintaining a Cholesky per particle over a Tnx-by-Tnx matrix suggests O(N T^2 n^2x) memory and O(N T^3 n^3x) time unless more sharing is possible; please clarify actual scaling and limits (e.g., T=200 works; what about T=1000?).
- Hyperparameter sampling: Slice sampling over θx and Q can be challenging in high dimensions and with strong posterior correlations with x0:T. No diagnostics are shown; acceptance rates, number of evaluations, and sensitivity to priors would help. Are ARD kernels used? Is Q diagonal or full?
- Sparse prior: The choice and placement of inducing inputs I are not inferred; they are selected heuristically and fixed during PF. This may bias results. A discussion of sensitivity to M and inducing input selection would be useful. Comparing FIC to other sparse priors (FITC/PITC) would strengthen the section.
- Baselines and metrics: The cart-pole example lacks quantitative comparisons (e.g., one-step NLL/RMSE vs parametric models, GP-NARX, or EM-based GP-SSMs) and runtime. For the scalar system, more details on the RMSE computation, confidence intervals, and predictive likelihoods would be welcome.
- Assumption of known g: Many practical problems require learning g. While the paper reasonably argues identifiability issues, a brief discussion of when joint GP f and g is feasible (and how PMCMC would extend) would broaden applicability.
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond kernel invariance, there is no discussion of mixing rates as a function of N, T, and model mismatch, or of bias introduced by FIC. Even empirical autocorrelations/ESS would be informative.

Questions for the authors
- What is the precise computational and memory complexity including N and nx? How large can T and nx be in practice?
- How sensitive is performance to the GP mean function? If the mean is severely misspecified, can the GP compensate without excessive data?
- How are Q and θx parameterized and priors chosen? Is Q diagonal or full in experiments?
- How are inducing inputs chosen for FIC, and how sensitive are results to that choice and to M?
- Can you report mixing diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation) for x0:T and θ, and runtime per iteration?

Suggestions
- Add quantitative baselines on cart-pole and report NLL/RMSE and runtime.
- Include complexity tables with N, nx, T, M, and memory.
- Provide mixing diagnostics and sensitivity analyses for θ priors and M.
- Consider simple diagnostics for the impact of the mean function choice.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and technically sound collapsed Bayesian approach to GP-SSMs.
- Leverages state-of-the-art PMCMC (PGAS) appropriately.
- Practical implementation guidance and sparse construction.
- Demonstrates value on challenging multimodal smoothing.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete complexity and memory accounting; scalability unclear.
- Limited empirical comparisons and diagnostics on larger systems.
- Sparse prior/inducing point selection treated heuristically.

Overall, I find the contribution original and promising; with strengthened empirical evaluation and clarified scalability, it would be a strong addition.
