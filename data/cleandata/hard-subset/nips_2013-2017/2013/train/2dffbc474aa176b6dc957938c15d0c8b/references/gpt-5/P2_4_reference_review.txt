Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) where the transition function f is unknown and modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP). The key idea is to marginalize f exactly, which induces a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories. Inference is then performed over the latent states x0:T and hyperparameters θ using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), which is well-suited to non-Markovian latent-variable models. Once samples from the smoothing distribution are obtained, the posterior over f at test points is available in closed form (mixture of GP posteriors conditioned on sampled trajectories). The paper further introduces a sparse construction via a fully independent conditional (FIC) GP prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and details an implementation based on incremental Cholesky updates to evaluate proposal and ancestor-sampling weights efficiently.

Relative to GP-based SSM literature, prior approaches often make the model parametric (e.g., EM/MAP over pseudo-outputs or radial basis expansions; Turner et al.; Wang et al.’s GPDM) or assume learned GP transition and observation models to focus on filtering/smoothing (Deisenroth et al.). The present paper fills the gap of fully Bayesian learning of the GP-SSM while respecting the nonparametric nature of f through exact marginalization. It leverages recent PMCMC developments (Andrieu et al.; Lindsten et al.) and adapts them to the non-Markovian prior induced by f-marginalization.

Quality
Technical soundness: The collapsing of f is correct and yields a product-of-Gaussians representation for p(x1:T | x0, θ) with GP predictive mean and covariance (Eqs. 3–4). The use of PGAS to leave p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) invariant in non-Markovian settings is appropriate and supported by cited theory. Ancestor-sampling weights p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) are computable under the marginalized prior; the implementation details around caching/factoring K̃ over concatenated paths and using rank-1 updates/downdates are sensible.

The sparse FIC prior is well-motivated; the resulting predictive forms (Eq. 8) and complexity claims are consistent with sparse GP regression. Sampling θy and θx given x0:T is standard; using slice sampling over GP hyperparameters conditioned on latent inputs/outputs is reasonable.

Empirics: The nonlinear 1D benchmark convincingly demonstrates handling of multimodal smoothing and improvement over mismatched parametric baselines; the cart-pole example illustrates uncertainty-aware one-step predictions in 4D. However, the experimental section is relatively thin for a NeurIPS-level paper: limited baselines, no quantitative results for cart-pole, and no runtime/ESS/mixing diagnostics or ablations.

Concerns/clarifications:
- Complexity dependence on the number of particles N and state dimension nx is under-specified. The text states O(T^3) (or O(M^2 T)) but appears to ignore N; with per-particle factorizations and rank updates, the cost and memory are typically O(N T^2) per step and O(N T^2) storage unless sharing is possible. Please clarify total time and memory complexity in N, T, nx, and M.
- Numerical stability of repeated rank-1 downdates can be delicate; a discussion of conditioning and jitter would help.
- For ancestor sampling in the non-Markovian model, computing p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) is heavy; are truncated lookahead approximations (as in PGAS variants) necessary/practical for large T?
- Inducing inputs in FIC are treated as fixed; this is a different prior, not an approximation, but performance can depend strongly on inducing locations. A brief empirical sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and clearly written. The derivations of the marginalized prior and how that feeds into the CPF-AS/PGAS machinery are clear. The discussion of implementation tricks is useful. That said:
- Provide explicit pseudo-code for the CPF-AS step specialized to the marginalized GP prior (with the required matrix updates) to aid reproducibility.
- Make the dimensionalities and computational/memory scaling explicit, including dependence on N, nx and the cost of evaluating p(yt | xt).
- Some references are incomplete; please standardize citations.

Originality
The combination of exact marginalization of the GP transition with PGAS for state/hyperparameter inference in GP-SSMs is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. Prior GP-SSM learning methods either parametrize f or optimize MAP estimates; here the nonparametric model is retained and exploited to improve mixing by avoiding sampling f. The tailored ancestor sampling for the non-Markovian prior and the careful complexity-reduction details add original value. Using an FIC prior as an alternative GP-SSM prior is known in regression but its integration into this inference pipeline is a practical contribution.

Significance
Learning flexible, calibrated dynamical models is important for time series, control, and RL. A fully Bayesian, nonparametric treatment that can capture multimodality and propagate model uncertainty is valuable. The approach is likely to be used or extended by researchers working on probabilistic dynamical models; the mixture-of-Gaussians predictive summarizes uncertainty well and can seed downstream filters/controllers. The main limitation is empirical validation and scalability: stronger quantitative comparisons (predictive log-likelihood, long-horizon forecasting, runtime) and larger-scale experiments would better establish state-of-the-art status.

Pros
- Conceptually clean and technically sound marginalization of f with PGAS inference.
- Retains full nonparametric expressivity; good mixing expected by collapsing f.
- Practical sparse variant and detailed implementation guidance.
- Handles multimodal smoothing; uncertainty-aware predictions.

Cons
- Limited empirical evaluation and baselines; no quantitative results on cart-pole.
- Complexity and memory scaling w.r.t. N, nx not fully specified; no runtime/ESS.
- Sensitivity to inducing locations not explored; no comparisons to PMMH/SMC^2 or EM/MAP GP-SSM baselines.
- Some numerical concerns (downdates) left unaddressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative metrics (test log-likelihood/RMSE) and baselines (GPDM/EM-MAP, PMMH) on both tasks; include runtime and ESS/mixing diagnostics, and ablations over N, M, and T.
- Clarify and empirically validate complexity and memory scaling with N, T, nx, M.
- Discuss numerical stability and regularization in matrix updates.
- Include a brief sensitivity study for inducing input selection or a simple adaptive strategy.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The methodology is solid and meaningfully advances fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs; the paper is clear and the contributions are likely to influence follow-up work. Strengthening the empirical section as suggested would make the case unequivocal.
