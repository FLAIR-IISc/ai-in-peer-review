

# Review: Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC

This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach for learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function and using Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) for inference. The authors argue that their method preserves the nonparametric expressivity of GP-SSMs, unlike previous approaches that rely on parametric approximations of the GP dynamics. They present theoretical derivations for marginalizing the GP and implement a PGAS-based sampler to handle the resulting non-Markovian state process.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with detailed mathematical derivations. The experimental results on a nonlinear benchmark system and a cart-pole system demonstrate reasonable performance, particularly in capturing multimodal smoothing distributions. The sparse GP extension using FIC prior is a practical contribution that addresses computational concerns. The authors correctly cite relevant prior work on GP-SSMs (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al.) and particle methods.

**Weaknesses:** While the paper claims to provide a "fully Bayesian" approach, it fails to adequately demonstrate how this differs substantially from existing methods. The marginalization technique they employ is conceptually similar to approaches described in Deisenroth et al. (2011) and Wang et al. (2005), which also use GP priors for dynamics but require parametric approximations for inference. The paper's strongest claim—that their method "retains the full nonparametric expressivity of the model"—is not convincingly supported by experimental evidence against established baselines. The authors do not compare against the state-of-the-art methods they cite, such as the work by Deisenroth and Turner, which already provides sophisticated inference for GP-SSMs.

The computational complexity of O(T³) (or O(M²T) with sparse approximation) is not sufficiently justified. For time series with T > 1000, this approach becomes computationally prohibitive, yet the paper only demonstrates results on T = 200. The authors claim "very good mixing" enabled by PGAS, but provide no convergence diagnostics or mixing time analysis to support this claim. The paper lacks a clear discussion of when this approach would be preferable to simpler alternatives like sparse GP regression or variational inference methods.

The experimental section is particularly weak. Table 1 reports RMSE results but only compares against two parametric models and a sparse GP variant, not against the more relevant baseline methods they cite (e.g., Deisenroth's GP-SSM inference). The "ground truth" model they compare against is not clearly identified in the experiments. The paper does not address the critical question of whether the marginalization approach actually leads to better predictive performance than simpler alternatives, despite claiming to provide "a vital component needed for these inference methods."

**Originality and Significance:** The paper's core innovation—marginalizing the GP dynamics in SSMs—is not sufficiently novel relative to the existing literature. The authors cite several relevant prior works but do not clearly articulate how their approach differs in a meaningful way. The use of PGAS for this specific problem is not new (the paper cites Lindsten et al., 2012, which introduced PGAS). The paper does not demonstrate that this approach provides substantial improvements in either accuracy or computational efficiency over existing methods, which is required for a significant contribution to NIPS.

**Quality Concerns:** The paper makes a strong claim that "our only approximation is that of the sampling algorithm," but fails to provide evidence that the PGAS sampler converges to the true posterior. The experimental results are limited to small-scale problems (T=200) without analysis of scalability. The paper does not address the practical challenges of implementing this method for real-world time series with longer lengths or higher dimensions.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound method, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that this approach offers significant advantages over established methods. The claims about "fully Bayesian" learning and "retaining the full nonparametric expressivity" are not adequately supported by experimental results against relevant baselines. The computational complexity and lack of scalability analysis further undermine the paper's contribution. The work represents a reasonable incremental improvement rather than a significant advance that would warrant publication at NIPS. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their experimental validation against state-of-the-art baselines and provide clearer justification for why this approach is necessary when alternative methods exist.
