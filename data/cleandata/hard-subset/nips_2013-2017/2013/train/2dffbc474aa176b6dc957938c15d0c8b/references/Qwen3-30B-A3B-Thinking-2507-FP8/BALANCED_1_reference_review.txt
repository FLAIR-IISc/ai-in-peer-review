

# Review: Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC

This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function, avoiding the common practice of using parametric approximations for the dynamics. The authors develop a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to handle the non-Markovian structure introduced by marginalization, and provide efficient implementations including a sparse GP-SSM variant.

The work builds on previous NIPS contributions in GP-based SSMs (e.g., Deisenroth et al. [5,6]) but addresses a key limitation in the field: most existing methods require fixing the dynamics model before performing inference, whereas this paper maintains the full nonparametric expressivity of the GP model. The paper relates well to the broader literature on GP regression and state-space models, citing relevant work including Turner et al. [2] and Wang et al. [8], though it could better connect to more recent advances in scalable GP inference.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical development. The derivation of the marginal prior (Section 3.1) and the PGAS algorithm (Section 3.3) are well-justified. The authors carefully analyze computational complexity (O(T^3) for vanilla GP, O(M^2T) for sparse GP) and provide an efficient implementation strategy. The experiments demonstrate the method's effectiveness, particularly on the multimodal benchmark where parametric models fail. However, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of convergence properties of the PGAS sampler, which would strengthen the quality assessment. The sparse implementation's performance depends on inducing point selection, but the paper doesn't adequately address this dependency.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem formulation to solution to experiments. The mathematical derivations are presented carefully, and figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., Figure 2 showing multimodal smoothing). The sparse GP implementation details (Section 4.2) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. Some readers unfamiliar with advanced particle methods may struggle with Sections 3.3.1-3.3.2, but the paper provides adequate background.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by developing a method that maintains the full nonparametric nature of GP-SSMs through marginalization of the dynamics function. This contrasts with most prior work that uses parametric approximations. The application of PGAS to this non-Markovian setting is novel, and the sparse GP-SSM construction (Section 4.1) extends the approach to larger datasets. The paper properly distinguishes itself from previous work, though it could better highlight how this approach differs from more recent NIPS papers on GP-SSMs.

**Significance**: The approach enables fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs, which is valuable for uncertainty quantification in dynamical systems. The ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions (as demonstrated in the synthetic benchmark) represents a meaningful advance over parametric approaches. The sparse implementation makes the method applicable to larger problems, and the cart and pole experiments demonstrate real-world applicability. However, the significance would be stronger with more challenging real-world applications beyond the cart and pole system.

**Strengths**: 
- Principled solution to the problem of learning GP-SSMs without parametric approximations
- Rigorous theoretical development with careful computational complexity analysis
- Effective handling of multimodal smoothing distributions
- Well-executed experiments demonstrating clear advantages over parametric alternatives
- Practical sparse implementation for larger datasets

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods for GP-SSMs
- Insufficient discussion of inducing point selection's impact on sparse GP-SSM performance
- No detailed analysis of PGAS convergence properties
- Limited real-world applications beyond the cart and pole system
- Lacks scalability analysis with respect to state-space dimensionality

**Pro/Con for Acceptance**:
*Pro*: This is a technically sound contribution that solves a meaningful problem in the intersection of Gaussian processes and state-space modeling. The approach maintains the full nonparametric expressivity of GP-SSMs, which is a significant advancement over existing methods. The theoretical development is rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages in challenging scenarios.

*Con*: The paper's significance would be strengthened with more challenging real-world applications, and the sparse implementation's dependency on inducing point selection warrants deeper analysis. The lack of comprehensive comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods is a notable limitation.

**Suggestions for Improvement**:
1. Add a more comprehensive comparison with recent methods for GP-SSMs, particularly those addressing scalability.
2. Include a detailed analysis of how inducing point selection affects sparse GP-SSM performance.
3. Provide convergence diagnostics for the PGAS sampler across different problem instances.
4. Extend the experiments to a more challenging real-world application (e.g., robotics or environmental monitoring).
5. Include a discussion of the method's scalability with respect to state-space dimensionality.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances the field of Bayesian inference for state-space models. While the current version has some limitations in demonstrating significance, the core methodology is sound, well-executed, and represents a meaningful step forward. With the suggested improvements, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
