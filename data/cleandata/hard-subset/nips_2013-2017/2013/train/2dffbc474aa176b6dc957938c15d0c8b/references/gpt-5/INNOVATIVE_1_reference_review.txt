Summary
This paper tackles learning in nonparametric Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by collapsing out the unknown transition function f and performing fully Bayesian inference over latent states x0:T and hyperparameters θ using Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). The key idea is to retain the full nonparametric expressivity of the GP prior over dynamics while avoiding the common parametric approximations. Collapsing f induces non-Markovian dependencies in the latent trajectory; the authors derive the resulting trajectory prior as a product of GP predictive Gaussians and show how to use CPF-AS to sample from the exact smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T). Hyperparameters are sampled via standard GP machinery (slice sampling given x0:T). They also develop a sparse variant using the FIC approximation, reducing complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and describe efficient rank-1 update/downdate schemes to reuse Cholesky factorizations across time. Finally, they outline how to reconstruct the posterior over the dynamics f as a mixture of Gaussians given samples from the smoothing distribution, or approximate it with a single sparse GP for downstream tasks.

Relation to prior work
Previous GP-SSM work either assumes learned dynamics and focuses on approximate filtering/smoothing (e.g., Deisenroth et al., EP/robust smoothing), or learns parametric surrogates for f via EM or MAP (Turner et al.; Wang et al.; GPDM). The present work differs by (i) collapsing the GP over f to respect nonparametric modeling throughout, (ii) using PMCMC (PGAS) to sample entire latent trajectories under the induced non-Markovian prior, and (iii) integrating sparse GP ideas into the state-space setting in a principled way. The use of ancestor sampling weights computed from the collapsed GP trajectory prior is, to my knowledge, novel in this context and addresses the path degeneracy issues typical in SMC.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: Collapsing the GP transition and performing exact-in-target PGAS on the non-Markovian latent model is an elegant and powerful idea that avoids the parametric crutches common in GP-SSM learning.
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the collapsed trajectory prior, the CPF-AS kernel invariance argument (via [4]), and the hyperparameter sampling steps are consistent with GP regression theory and PMCMC foundations.
- Algorithmic practicality: Clear attention to computational efficiency—rank-one updates for the Cholesky factors and an FIC-based O(M^2 T) variant—makes the approach more than a purely theoretical construct.
- Bayesian learning of dynamics: The mixture-of-Gaussians posterior over f* captures uncertainty and multimodality in a way that parametric or MAP approaches cannot.
- Experiments illustrate key advantages: The nonlinear scalar benchmark highlights the ability to resolve multi-modality and correct for a misspecified mean function; the cart-pole example demonstrates calibrated uncertainty away from data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical scope and depth: Experiments are relatively small-scale (T=200; 4D cart-pole with 100 observations). There is no wall-clock/runtime analysis, nor a sensitivity study versus number of particles N, PGAS iterations, or inducing points M. The mixing claims would be stronger with trace plots/ESS/autocorrelation diagnostics and performance vs N.
- Baselines and comparisons: Comparisons to GPDM/MAP (Wang et al.), EM-parametric (Turner et al.), and variational GP-SSMs (if contemporary) are limited or absent. Since one contribution is “learning the GP model in the first place,” head-to-head comparisons on the same tasks would strengthen significance claims.
- Scalability: While O(T^3) and O(M^2 T) are analyzed, practical scaling to long sequences and higher-dimensional states is unclear. For nx > 1, constants matter; discussion of memory footprint and numerical stability of repeated downdates would help.
- Inducing input selection: The FIC variant defers selection to heuristics (e.g., fast forward selection). Some empirical evidence that results are not overly sensitive to inducing locations would be reassuring.
- Identifiability/assumptions: The approach requires a known observation model g (up to finite parameters). This is reasonable in many settings, but limits generality; a discussion of identifiability and potential extension to learning g (e.g., weakly parametric priors) would broaden impact.
- Predictive deployment: The mixture-of-Gaussians posterior over f* is conceptually attractive but can be expensive. The suggested single-GP refit is pragmatic yet breaks the fully Bayesian story; some guidance on when this approximation is safe would be useful.

Clarity
Overall well-written and organized. The notation around K blocks and indices is dense but standard for GP experts. Including a small pseudo-code block for CPF-AS specialized to the collapsed GP prior (with the required quantities for ancestor weights) would improve readability. The implementation section is helpful; a figure illustrating the rank-1 update/downdate schedule would be welcome.

Significance
Addressing fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs without reverting to parametric approximations is impactful. The collapsed-GP + PGAS construction is a compelling direction likely to influence subsequent work on nonparametric dynamical systems and to serve as a component in decision-making pipelines that need realistic uncertainty.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel conceptual contribution (collapsed GP dynamics with PGAS) that opens a new direction.
- Solid algorithmic development with attention to computational concerns.
- Clear empirical illustrations of advantages (multimodality, calibration).
- Relevance to a core NeurIPS community (probabilistic modeling, SMC, GP).

Cons against acceptance
- Limited experimental scale and ablations; missing strong baselines.
- Incomplete discussion of scalability and practical deployment trade-offs.
- Dependence on known observation model reduces generality.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons with EM/MAP/variational GP-SSM baselines on the presented tasks.
- Provide runtime/memory scaling, mixing diagnostics, and sensitivity analyses (N, M, iterations).
- Elaborate on inducing set selection and its effect.
- Clarify numerical stability and constants in the rank-1 update scheme.
- Discuss extensions to partially unknown g and identifiability.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant and original idea with the potential to shift how we learn GP-SSMs, and the technical development is careful. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the conceptual advance outweighs these limitations.

Confidence: High.
