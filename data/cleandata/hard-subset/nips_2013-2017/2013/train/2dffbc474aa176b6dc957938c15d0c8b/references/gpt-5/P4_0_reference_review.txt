Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses fully Bayesian inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) where the state transition function f is modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP), while the observation model g is known up to parameters. The key idea is to marginalize out the unknown transition function f exactly under the GP prior, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ) that can be expressed via chained GP one-step predictive Gaussians. This “collapsed” representation avoids a finite-dimensional parameterization of f and preserves the nonparametric nature of the model.

Because the induced state prior is non-Markovian, the paper employs particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) [Lindsten et al., NIPS 2012], to sample from the joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) and to alternate with sampling of hyperparameters θ. Sampling θy is straightforward (often conjugate), while sampling θx (GP kernel and process noise) uses slice sampling on the analytically collapsed marginal likelihood, analogous to GP regression with inputs x0:T−1 and outputs x1:T.

The authors provide implementation details to make the method computationally feasible: efficient rank-one Cholesky updates/downdates to evaluate one-step priors and ancestor weights, leading to O(T^3) cost for the “vanilla” GP-SSM, and a sparse variant using the FIC prior [Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen, JMLR 2005], reducing complexity to O(M^2 T) with M inducing points (M ≪ T). After inference, predictions over f(x∗) are obtained by averaging GP conditionals over posterior samples of (x0:T, θ), yielding a mixture of Gaussians.

Empirically, the paper demonstrates: (i) a nonlinear 1D benchmark with multimodal smoothing due to sign ambiguity in observations; the method recovers sensible smoothing distributions and predictive dynamics even when the GP mean function is a misspecified parametric baseline; (ii) a 4D cart-pole system, where one-step predictions reflect uncertainty that varies with proximity to training data. Comparisons include parametric baselines and a sparse GP-SSM; results suggest good predictive accuracy.

This work connects to prior GP-SSM research that typically learns a parametric approximation to f via EM or MAP (e.g., Turner et al., AISTATS 2010; Wang et al., NIPS 2005), and to GP-based filtering/smoothing assuming learned GP dynamics (e.g., Deisenroth et al.). The contribution here is a fully Bayesian treatment that marginalizes f and uses PGAS to handle the induced non-Markovian structure.

Strengths
- Methodological soundness: Collapsing f under the GP prior is principled and eliminates strong posterior dependencies between x and f. Using PGAS to target the exact smoothing distribution for non-Markovian latent models is appropriate and supported by cited invariance properties.
- Retains nonparametric expressivity: Unlike approaches that discretize or parametrize f via a finite set of targets, the model remains a true GP prior over functions throughout.
- Implementation detail and computational advances: Clear derivations for the collapsed prior, efficient Cholesky update strategies, and a sparse FIC variant reducing complexity to O(M^2 T) are valuable and make the approach practical for moderate T.
- Handling multimodality: The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive over f naturally captures multimodal smoothing arising from ambiguous observations.
- Clarity of the inference pipeline: The division into sampling x via PGAS and sampling θ via slice sampling is well explained; Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical evaluation breadth: Comparisons to prior GP-SSM learning methods (e.g., EM/MAP approaches such as [2], [8]) are not reported. Including quantitative comparisons on shared benchmarks would strengthen claims about accuracy and robustness.
- Scalability and complexity clarity: While O(T^3) and O(M^2 T) are discussed, the dependence on number of particles N, state dimension nx, and constants is not explicit. Clarifying overall complexity as a function of (T, N, nx, M) and memory footprint would aid practitioners. Reporting runtimes and sensitivity to N, number of PGAS iterations, and M would be informative.
- Mixing diagnostics: The claim of “very good mixing” would benefit from autocorrelation/effective sample size plots for representative parameters and latent states. This is particularly important given the strong coupling in SSMs and the use of slice sampling for θx.
- Numerical stability: Rank-one updates/downdates for Cholesky and ancestor weight evaluation can be numerically delicate. The manuscript would benefit from advice on stabilization (e.g., jitter, conditioning strategies) and failure modes.
- Assumption on known observation model g: While motivated by sensor models, this restricts applicability. A discussion of identifiability and whether/when g could also be learned (e.g., partially parametric) would improve scope.
- Large-scale or real data: Beyond the cart-pole example, including a real-world dataset or higher-dimensional/time-horizon experiments would better demonstrate significance and robustness.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears solid. The collapsing derivations and use of PGAS in non-Markovian settings are consistent with cited literature. The sparse FIC extension follows standard GP methodology. More empirical diagnostics and broader comparisons would solidify the evaluation.

Clarity
- Generally clearly written with precise notation and helpful algorithmic and implementation details. Suggestions: explicitly state complexity in N and nx; include pseudo-code or references for CPF-AS details; summarize prior choices for θ and slice sampling settings.

Originality
- The combination of exact marginalization of f with PGAS for GP-SSMs is, to the best of my knowledge based on the cited literature, novel at the time referenced. Using a sparse FIC prior within this collapsed PMCMC framework also appears original.

Significance
- The approach advances Bayesian learning for nonlinear SSMs by enabling fully Bayesian inference without parametric approximation of f. It should be of interest to researchers in Bayesian nonparametrics and sequential inference. Practical impact will depend on scalability; the sparse variant and implementation tips help, but larger-scale demonstrations would strengthen significance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, fully Bayesian treatment of GP-SSMs that preserves nonparametric flexibility.
- Appropriate and elegant use of PGAS to handle non-Markovian latent priors induced by marginalizing f.
- Useful implementation insights and a sparse variant that reduces computational cost.
- Empirical results indicate the method can handle multimodal smoothing and provide calibrated uncertainty.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical comparisons to established GP-SSM learning methods (EM/MAP/variational).
- Scalability and runtime not systematically evaluated; lack of mixing diagnostics and sensitivity analyses.
- Assumption of known observation model may limit applicability; discussion of identifiability and extensions is brief.

Scope
- The work is in scope for NeurIPS (Bayesian inference, time series, nonparametric modeling, Monte Carlo methods).

Overall, this is a technically sound and potentially impactful contribution; strengthening the empirical validation and clarifying scalability would further improve the paper.
