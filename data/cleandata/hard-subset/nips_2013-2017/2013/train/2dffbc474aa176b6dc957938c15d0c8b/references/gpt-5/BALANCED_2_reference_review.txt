Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models where the state transition function f(·) is unknown and modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP). The key idea is to marginalize the GP mapping f out of the model, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ), and then to perform smoothing and hyperparameter inference using a particle Gibbs sampler with ancestor sampling (PGAS). This “collapsed” approach avoids introducing a finite parameterization of f and directly samples from p(x0:T, θ | y0:T). Given samples of x0:T and θ, the posterior over f is obtained in closed form (as in GP regression), producing a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution over f(·). To address computational cost, the paper presents efficient updates based on Cholesky rank-1 updates (O(T^3) overall) and a sparse variant using FIC inducing variables (O(M^2 T)). Experiments are shown on a nonlinear benchmark system (with multimodal smoothing due to sign ambiguity) and on a 4D cart-pole system.

This work builds on: GP-SSMs and GPDMs (e.g., Wang, Fleet & Hertzmann, NIPS’05; Turner et al., AISTATS’10), GP-based filtering/smoothing with learned GPs (Deisenroth et al., NIPS/IEEE TAC), and Particle MCMC (Andrieu, Doucet & Holenstein, JRSSB’10; Lindsten et al., NIPS’12). The novelty here lies in marginalizing f to retain full nonparametric expressivity and in using PGAS to sample the non-Markovian state trajectory efficiently, alongside a sparse GP prior and careful computational engineering.

Strengths:
- Methodological novelty and rigor: Collapsing the GP transition function and sampling x0:T via PGAS is principled and addresses strong state–function coupling that plagues joint MCMC. The invariance properties of CPF-AS are used appropriately.
- Retains full GP nonparametric modeling: Unlike EM/MAP or finite-parametric approximations, f is integrated out; posterior over f is recovered after smoothing.
- Practical computational contributions: O(T^3) scheme with reuse of Cholesky factors and rank-1 updates; FIC-based O(M^2 T) variant; clear discussion of implementation pitfalls.
- Handles general measurement models (beyond Gaussian), and the GP mean function encodes prior mechanistic knowledge while allowing complex departures.
- Predictive distribution as a mixture of Gaussians naturally captures multimodality in smoothing/prediction, demonstrated on the sign-ambiguous benchmark.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Only one synthetic benchmark and one cart-pole example are shown; the latter is qualitative without comparisons or predictive likelihoods. Quantitative comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., GPDM, variational GP-SSMs, PMMH with parametric f, EP-based GP-SSMs after learning) are missing.
- Scalability and high-dimensionality: Claims of O(T^3)/O(M^2 T) are valuable, but no runtime/memory plots, scaling with T, N (particles), nx, or M are reported. For nx > 1, cross-output structure and kernel choices are not discussed (independent GPs vs multioutput/correlated outputs).
- Some algorithmic clarity issues: Eq. (5) suggests proposing from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t) but the described weight update is p(yt | xt), which would be correct for the optimal proposal; details of how the predictive density with yt is computed in the non-Markovian setting should be clarified. The ancestor sampling weight (6) relies on p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1); more guidance on its efficient evaluation (and numerical stability) would improve reproducibility.
- Assumptions/identifiability: The observation model is assumed known (possibly up to low-dimensional parameters). While practical in some domains, this limits generality; identifiability issues when g is also unknown are acknowledged but not explored or bounded.
- Inducing inputs are not learned. The choice of inducing locations is critical for sparse GPs; using fixed or greedy choices without sensitivity analysis may bias results.

Quality:
Overall technically sound. The collapsed prior over trajectories (Eq. 3–4) and the PGAS construction are correct and well-motivated. The computational section is thoughtful. However, more detail on proposal distributions, weight formulas, and numerical conditioning would help ensure the method is fully reproducible. Experimental claims of “very good mixing” should be supported with ESS/autocorrelation diagnostics and acceptance statistics for hyperparameters.

Clarity:
Generally clear and well organized, with helpful intuition about the GP mean. Suggestions:
- Clarify the proposal in Sec. 3.2: is it the optimal one-step predictive p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, yt) or the prior p(xt | θ, x0:t−1)? Provide the exact weight formula for the chosen proposal in the non-Markovian setting.
- Provide pseudo-code for CPF-AS specialized to the collapsed GP-SSM, including how p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) is computed and stabilized.
- Specify kernel choices, priors over θ, and state/output dimensionality handling (independent vs correlated outputs).

Originality:
High. Collapsing f in a GP-SSM and leveraging PGAS to address non-Markovian smoothing is a novel and nontrivial contribution, distinct from prior EM/MAP or variational approximations and from methods that assume a fixed, learned GP.

Significance:
Potentially significant for Bayesian learning of nonlinear dynamics where model uncertainty matters. If scalability and robustness are demonstrated, this can become a go-to baseline for GP-SSM inference.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, elegant inference framework with nonparametric expressivity.
- Solid algorithmic innovations and complexity reductions.
- Addresses multimodal smoothing and provides calibrated predictive uncertainty.
- Clear connection to and extension of influential PMCMC/PGAS ideas.

Cons for acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is too narrow; lacks strong baselines and quantitative metrics.
- Limited discussion of scalability to longer sequences/high dimensions and of multioutput GP structure.
- Some ambiguities in the SMC proposal/weighting and practical implementation details.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add quantitative comparisons on benchmarks against GPDM (Wang et al.), variational GP-SSM, EP-based smoothing after learning, and parametric PMMH. Report RMSE and predictive log-likelihood, with runtime/memory.
- Provide mixing diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation) vs number of particles N and sequence length T; include sensitivity to priors and mean-function misspecification.
- Evaluate sparse vs full GP, effects of M and inducing input selection; consider learning inducing locations within the PGAS loop or via interleaved optimization.
- Discuss/experiment with multioutput kernels for coupled state dimensions.
- Clarify proposal/weights in Sec. 3.2 and provide pseudo-code for CPF-AS in the collapsed model.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The methodological contribution is strong and of clear interest to NeurIPS, but the empirical section needs strengthening and some clarity issues should be addressed.
