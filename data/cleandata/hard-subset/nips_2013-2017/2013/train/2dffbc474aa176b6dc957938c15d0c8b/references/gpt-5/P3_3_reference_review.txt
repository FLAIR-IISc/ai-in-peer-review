Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles fully Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models where the transition function f is modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP), while the observation model g is known up to parameters. The central methodological choice is to collapse f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ) that is expressed as a product of Gaussian conditionals derived from GP regression. Because marginalizing f induces long-range temporal dependencies and destroys Markov structure, the authors employ particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to sample from the smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T). Hyperparameters are updated via Gibbs-within-PGAS, with a slice sampler for GP covariance parameters and Q, leveraging the collapsed marginal likelihood. To address computational cost, they introduce a sparse prior using fully independent conditional (FIC) inducing variables, reducing complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and describe careful Cholesky/low-rank updates across time and particles. They illustrate on a nonlinear scalar benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole, showing predictive performance and uncertainty quantification.

This work relates to: GP-based SSM inference methods that assume learned dynamics [Deisenroth et al.], EM/MAP approaches to GP-SSMs/GPDM [Turner et al.; Wang et al.], and particle MCMC [Andrieu et al.], including ancestor sampling [Lindsten et al.]. The novelty here is collapsing the GP transition function to perform fully Bayesian learning over states and hyperparameters via PGAS, maintaining the nonparametric nature of f. The sparse FIC prior is used as an alternative GP prior for scalability.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity and technical soundness: Collapsing f is well-motivated (reduces strong x–f coupling) and leads to an exact marginal prior over trajectories, albeit non-Markovian. Using PGAS is a principled and effective choice for such non-Markovian latent models; the invariance arguments and use of ancestor sampling weights based on the collapsed prior are appropriate.
- Retaining nonparametric expressivity: Unlike EM/MAP with finite bases or pseudo-targets, the posterior over f is recovered post hoc via standard GP conditioning on sampled trajectories; predictive distributions are mixtures of Gaussians reflecting smoothing uncertainty.
- Computational insights: Derivations for the collapsed prior (eqs. 3–4), ancestor sampling weights, and careful O(T^3) implementation via rank-1 updates to Cholesky factors per particle/time are valuable. The FIC construction and the complexity reduction to O(M^2 T) are practically important.
- Practical modeling aspects: Clear role for mean functions to encode prior knowledge/physics, while the GP captures residuals; discussion of hyperparameter sampling conditioned on trajectories (akin to GP regression with inputs x0:T−1, outputs x1:T).
- Experiments show the method handling multimodal smoothing and reporting calibrated uncertainty; the cart-pole case demonstrates applicability in moderate dimensions.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Experimental evaluation is limited. The scalar benchmark (T=200, N=20, 50 PGAS iterations) is small and lacks comparisons to strong baselines designed for GP-SSMs, e.g., EM/MAP approaches (Turner et al.; GPDM), variational GP-SSMs, or PMMH/PGBS variants. For cart-pole, only one-step predictive moments are shown; no multi-step rollouts, predictive log likelihoods, or control-relevant metrics are reported. Runtime and scaling with T, N, and M are not quantified.
- Proposal distribution ambiguity in SMC step (Sec. 3.2): Eq. (5) denotes sampling from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t), but later text says “sampling from the one-step predictive density is simple,” which reads like p(xt | θ, x0:t−1). If the prior predictive is used, performance can degrade under sharp likelihoods; if the locally optimal proposal is used, how is it computed efficiently given non-Markovian structure? Clarification and an ablation on proposal choice would help.
- Scalability and memory: Maintaining per-particle Cholesky factors over concatenated paths implies significant memory/cost for large T and N; the paper would benefit from a concrete memory/cost analysis and limits in practice. For FIC, the O(M^2 T) claim requires reusing not only factorizations but intermediate matrix-vector products; implementation details are terse.
- Identifiability and prior specification: There is potential confounding between GP amplitude/length-scales and process noise Q. Without informative priors or constraints, the model may attribute dynamics variability to either kernel variance or Q. The mean function can also induce bias if misspecified; an analysis of sensitivity to mean/kernels and priors over Q would strengthen the paper.
- Inducing inputs: Treating FIC as a different prior is defensible, but in practice performance hinges on inducing input placement. The paper defers selection to a greedy method and fixes inducing sets during PGAS runs; the impact of M and inducing placement on inference/mixing is not evaluated.
- Theory: While PGAS invariance guarantees are cited, there is no discussion of overall convergence/mixing when alternating θ-updates with CPF-AS in the collapsed model. Diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation) would be reassuring, especially under multimodal smoothing.

Quality:
- The methodology is technically sound and well-grounded in PMCMC theory. Some implementation aspects and the proposal distribution need clarification. Empirical support is promising but insufficiently comprehensive for a definitive performance claim.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized, with helpful derivations and algorithmic descriptions. It would benefit from clarifying Sec. 3.2’s proposal, expanding Sec. 4.2 with more concrete pseudo-code or complexity/memory tables, and adding details on hyperparameter priors and slice sampling setup.

Originality:
- The combination of collapsing the GP transition function with PGAS for fully Bayesian GP-SSMs is novel and valuable. The FIC-based prior adaptation for scalability in this context is also a useful contribution.

Significance:
- Addresses a long-standing challenge in learning GP-SSMs without resorting to parametric approximations; likely to influence subsequent work on nonparametric dynamical models and PMCMC for non-Markovian latents. With stronger experiments and scalability demonstrations, impact could be high.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines (EM/MAP GP-SSM/GPDM, PMMH, variational GP-SSMs) with metrics like predictive log-likelihood, multi-step prediction, and calibration (PIT, CRPS).
- Provide runtime, memory, and scaling curves versus T, N, and M; ablate proposal choices and ancestor sampling; report mixing diagnostics.
- Analyze sensitivity to mean function, kernel choice, and priors on Q; consider weak identifiability mitigation (e.g., hierarchical priors, constraints).
- Detail inducing input selection strategy and its effect; consider adaptive inducing placement.
- Clarify the proposal in (5) and how p(xt | x0:t−1, y0:t) is computed/approximated if used.

Pros:
- Principled, fully Bayesian treatment retaining nonparametric f.
- Clever use of PGAS for non-Markovian smoothing; solid theoretical grounding.
- Practical scalability via FIC and careful linear algebra.
- Clear modeling story leveraging mean functions and uncertainty propagation.

Cons:
- Limited experimental validation and comparisons.
- Some ambiguity in proposal mechanics; sparse implementation details.
- Scalability and memory trade-offs not empirically characterized.
- Sensitivity/identifiability issues not analyzed.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The methodological contribution is strong and relevant for NeurIPS; with improved empirical evaluation and clarifications, this could be a solid paper.
