Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models where the state transition function is modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP). The key idea is to marginalize the unknown transition function f exactly, which induces non-Markovian dependencies across the latent states, and to perform posterior inference over states x0:T and hyperparameters θ using Particle MCMC—in particular, particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). Given samples from the smoothing distribution p(x0:T, θ | y0:T), the posterior over f at test points is obtained in closed form (mixture of GP predictions). The paper further proposes an efficient implementation with Cholesky rank-1 updates yielding O(T^3) complexity for a vanilla GP prior, and introduces a sparse GP-SSM based on the FIC prior to reduce complexity to O(M^2 T) with M inducing inputs. Experiments include a nonlinear 1D benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole system.

This work is positioned against: (i) GP-SSM learning via EM/MAP with parametric surrogates or pseudo-targets (e.g., Turner et al.; Wang et al.; Ghahramani & Roweis), which sacrifice full Bayesian nonparametric treatment; and (ii) GP-based filtering/smoothing assuming learned dynamics (e.g., Deisenroth et al.), for which this paper provides a Bayesian learning counterpart. Methodologically it builds on particle MCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.), tailoring them to the collapsed GP-SSM setting.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and coherence: Collapsing f to retain the nonparametric prior while sampling states with PGAS is elegant and addresses the strong x–f coupling that hinders joint sampling. This is a clear advance over MAP/EM approaches that parametrize or fix f.
- Soundness: The use of PGAS provides Markov kernels invariant to the true smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T). The derivation of the collapsed prior p(x1:T | x0, θ) and the use of GP predictive conditionals are correct and well-motivated.
- Practical efficiency: Careful algorithmic engineering (Cholesky updates, ancestor sampling weights via the collapsed prior) and the FIC variant to reach O(M^2 T) are valuable contributions for scalability.
- Flexibility: Incorporation of prior structure via the GP mean function and support for general observation models p(yt | xt, θy).
- Empirical illustrations: The nonlinear benchmark demonstrates handling of multimodal smoothing; the cart-pole example shows uncertainty-aware predictions and nontrivial 4D dynamics.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Limited empirical evaluation: Only two datasets are shown, with modest ablation. There is no comprehensive comparison to strong baselines such as GPDM/EM, variational GP-SSMs, or particle MCMC variants (e.g., PG with backward simulation). Predictive performance is summarized mainly via RMSE; probabilistic metrics (log predictive density, calibration) are missing.
- Scaling study is insufficient: While complexity is analyzed, experiments do not systematically assess runtime, memory, or accuracy as functions of T, state dimension, N (particles), and M (inducing points). Mixing/ESS diagnostics for PGAS (autocorrelation, acceptance of θ steps) are not reported.
- Sparse prior choice: The FIC construction is treated as a different prior rather than an approximation, which is reasonable, but empirical evidence comparing vanilla GP-SSM vs FIC in accuracy vs cost trade-offs is minimal; inducing-input selection is underspecified and not learned.
- Assumptions: The observation model g is assumed known up to parameters, limiting applicability. Identifiability concerns are noted but not empirically explored (e.g., with overly flexible g).

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized; however, some derivations are dense. Implementation details around maintaining and reusing factorizations for ancestor weights could benefit from pseudocode and explicit memory cost. The description of how Q and kernel hyperparameters are sampled (priors, parameterization, slice-sampling schedules) is brief.

Originality
- The integration of exact marginalization of f with PGAS for GP-SSMs, plus the non-Markovian SMC treatment and efficient updates, is novel and nontrivial. The sparse FIC variant in the SSM context is a useful addition.

Significance
- The approach advances Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs by avoiding parametric surrogates and enabling principled smoothing with unknown dynamics. Potential impact is high for researchers working on Bayesian time series, control, and RL. However, the limited empirical scope and lack of head-to-head comparisons temper the immediate practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant and principled methodology combining collapsed GP priors with PGAS.
- Solid algorithmic contributions with careful complexity considerations.
- Addresses an important gap: fully Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs without parametricization of f.
- Clear relation to and extension of established methods (particle MCMC, GP inference).

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is relatively narrow; lacks strong baselines, calibration metrics, and scalability studies.
- Missing diagnostics of PGAS mixing and sensitivity analyses (N, M, kernel choices, mean functions).
- Inducing-point selection and its effect on inference are not thoroughly treated.
- Applicability constrained by assuming known observation function.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comprehensive comparisons: EM/GPDM (Turner et al.), variational GP-SSMs, PMMH/PG-BS alternatives, and GP-based RL models (e.g., PILCO-style learned dynamics) on shared benchmarks with predictive log-likelihood and calibration metrics.
- Provide mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS per iteration), sensitivity to N and M, and runtime/memory scaling. Report wall-clock times for T up to several thousand and for higher-dimensional states.
- Explore robustness to misspecified mean functions and kernel choices; include an ablation with zero-mean and different priors over Q and kernel hyperparameters.
- For the FIC model, either jointly optimize or sample inducing inputs, or at least provide a principled selection and sensitivity analysis; compare accuracy–cost trade-offs versus the full GP prior.
- Consider extensions where g is also learned (e.g., parametric g with priors or GP g), and discuss identifiability remedies (regularization, priors, experiment design).
- Improve implementation clarity with pseudocode for factorization updates and explicit computational/memory costs.

Decision
Borderline accept. The methodological contribution is strong, principled, and of clear interest to the NeurIPS community. However, the empirical section is thin relative to the ambition of the paper. If strengthened along the lines above, this work would merit clear acceptance; as is, I lean slightly positive based on novelty and technical quality.
