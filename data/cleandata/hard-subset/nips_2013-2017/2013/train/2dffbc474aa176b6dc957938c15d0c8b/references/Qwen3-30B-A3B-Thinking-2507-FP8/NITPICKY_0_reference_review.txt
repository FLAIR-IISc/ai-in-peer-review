

# Review: Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC

## Summary
This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) methods. The key contribution is marginalizing out the state transition function f to maintain the nonparametric nature of the model, enabling efficient inference over the state trajectory and hyperparameters. The authors present a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm that handles the non-Markovian structure introduced by marginalizing f. The paper also introduces a sparse GP-SSM construction using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) approximation to reduce computational complexity.

## Strengths
The paper presents a technically sound approach to a challenging problem in state-space modeling. The marginalization of the transition function is a novel contribution that addresses a key limitation in previous GP-SSM approaches. The theoretical development is well-motivated, and the experimental results on both synthetic and real-world systems demonstrate the practical utility of the method. The authors provide clear connections to related work, particularly to Deisenroth et al. and Turner et al., and situate their contribution within the broader literature on GP-based SSMs.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Notational and Formatting Inconsistencies
The paper contains multiple formatting and notational inconsistencies that undermine readability and technical precision. For example:
- In equation (3a), the product notation "T∏" should be "∏_{t=1}^T" or similar.
- In equation (3b), "K̃ −1 0:t−2" should be "K̃^{-1}_{0:t-2}".
- In equation (4), "|(2π)nxtK̃0:t−1|− 1 2" should be "|(2π)^{n_x t} K̃_{0:t-1}|^{-1/2}".
- In equation (5), "xit" is inconsistently written as "x i t" in some places.
- In equation (8a), "Kt−1,IPKI,0:t−2Λ −1 0:t−2" should be "K_{t-1,I} P K_{I,0:t-2} Λ^{-1}_{0:t-2}".

These minor formatting issues accumulate to create a significant distraction from the technical content.

### 2. Technical Ambiguities
The paper contains several technical ambiguities that would make the method difficult to reproduce:
- Section 3.1 states "the marginal prior p(θ,x0:T ) = p(x0:T | θ)p(θ)", but this is not a marginal prior; it's the joint prior. The marginal prior would be p(x0:T) = ∫ p(x0:T | θ)p(θ) dθ. This is a critical notational error that could confuse readers.
- In section 3.3.2, the paper claims p(θ | x0:T ,y0:T ) ∝ p(θy | x0:T ,y0:T ) p(θx | x0:T ) without explicitly stating the assumption of independence between θx and θy. This assumption should be clearly stated.
- The paper states that "the marginal prior p(θ,x0:T ) = p(x0:T | θ)p(θ)" (section 3.1), but this is not a marginal prior as defined in the paper. This is a fundamental confusion in probability notation.

### 3. Incomplete Explanation of Key Concepts
The paper claims that "marginalization of f will introduce dependencies across time for the state variables that lead to the loss of the Markovian structure of the state-process" (section 3.1), but it does not adequately explain how the non-Markovian structure is handled in the inference process. The authors reference the PGAS algorithm but do not provide a clear explanation of how it specifically addresses the loss of Markovian structure.

### 4. Experimental Details
The paper reports results for the cart and pole system in section 6.2 but does not specify the number of inducing points used in the sparse GP-SSM for this experiment, which is critical for reproducibility. Additionally, while the paper claims that "the mixture of Gaussians can correctly take into account multimodality," it does not provide specific examples or analysis demonstrating this advantage over a single GP.

### 5. Related Work
The paper cites Turner et al. [2] and Wang et al. [8] as previous approaches, but fails to adequately distinguish the current work from these in terms of specific technical contributions. The paper states that "Turner et al. [2] applied the EM algorithm to obtain a maximum likelihood estimate of parametric models which had the form of GPs where both inputs and outputs were parameters to be optimized," but doesn't explain how the current work differs from this in a specific, technical way.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to GP-SSM learning with some promising experimental results, the accumulation of minor but significant technical and notational issues substantially undermines the paper's quality. The notational inconsistencies and technical ambiguities would make the paper difficult to reproduce and understand for readers, which is a fundamental requirement for a NeurIPS paper. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues, particularly the critical notational errors in section 3.1.

Given the stringent quality requirements of NeurIPS and the paper's failure to meet basic standards of technical precision and clarity, I recommend rejection. The authors' technical contribution is interesting, but the paper as written does not meet the standards expected for publication at NeurIPS.
