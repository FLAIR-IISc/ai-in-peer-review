Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses Bayesian learning and inference in nonlinear state-space models where the state transition function f is modeled nonparametrically with a Gaussian process (GP). The key idea is to marginalize f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x1:T | x0, θ) expressed via GP conditionals, and then perform joint inference over the latent state trajectory and hyper-parameters using particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). Sampling the states from the exact smoothing distribution (up to Monte Carlo error) is combined with slice sampling of GP hyperparameters conditioned on a state trajectory, leveraging the GP regression likelihood. The paper also provides an efficient implementation with O(T^3) complexity via rank-one Cholesky updates, and a sparse variant using the FIC prior to reduce complexity to O(M^2 T). Experiments on a 1D nonlinear benchmark and a 4D cart-pole system illustrate that the approach can learn accurate dynamics and quantify uncertainty.

Relative to prior work: GP-SSMs and GP-based dynamical models have been considered for filtering/smoothing with learned GPs [5,6], and for learning with EM/MAP or parametric GP surrogates [2,7,8]. Particle MCMC provides a general framework for state/hyperparameter inference [3], and PGAS improves mixing for path space [4]. The novelty here lies in fully collapsing the GP transition function within a PMCMC scheme to sample from the smoothing distribution without committing to a parametric representation of f, and in deriving practical algorithms and complexity reductions for this non-Markov setting.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The derivation of the collapsed trajectory prior p(x1:T | x0, θ) as a product of Gaussian conditionals leveraging GP predictions is standard and correct. The non-Gaussian nature of the induced trajectory density is clearly noted.
- Using SMC/PGAS to handle non-Markov latent models is appropriate, and the ancestor sampling weights w̃t−1|T ∝ w_{t−1} p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) are derived from target density ratios in a way consistent with [4]; evaluating these terms via the collapsed prior is feasible.
- The Gibbs scheme alternating between state trajectory and hyper-parameters is standard for PMCMC. Sampling θx given x is equivalent to GP regression; slice sampling is a reasonable choice.
- Complexity analysis is plausible: O(T^3) with Cholesky rank-one updates per particle trajectory, and O(M^2 T) under FIC with careful reuse of factorizations. Implementation details on maintaining factorizations and rank updates are helpful.
- Assumptions and limitations are acknowledged (e.g., known measurement model form, potential identifiability issues if both f and g are fully nonparametric).
- One clarification would improve rigor: in Section 3.2, the notation p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t) as a proposal could be read as requiring likelihood-informed sampling; in practice the implementation appears to use the prior predictive p(xt | θ, x0:t−1) with importance weighting by p(yt | xt, θy), consistent with an auxiliary particle filter. Making the chosen proposal explicit would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written, with a clear high-level narrative: collapse f, use PGAS for states, sample hyperparameters given states, and optionally use a sparse prior for scalability. The notation for block matrices and indices is precise. Algorithm 1 and the CPF-AS description are concise; referencing [4] for details is appropriate. The complexity discussion and implementation details are valuable. Minor clarity suggestions:
- Explicitly state the proposal distribution used in SMC/CPF-AS and how sampling from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t) is realized.
- Summarize the priors used for θ (especially for kernel amplitude vs process noise Q) and discuss potential identifiability.
- Provide pseudo-code for the Cholesky update/downdate scheme per particle (even in appendix).

Originality
The combination of:
- exact marginalization of the GP transition function within a state-space model,
- PGAS-based sampling of the smoothing distribution in the resulting non-Markov latent model, and
- detailed scalable implementations (including a sparse FIC prior) 
appears novel relative to earlier GP-SSM learning methods that rely on EM, MAP, or parametric approximations [2,8], and to prior GP filtering/smoothing that assumes a learned GP [5,6]. The work leverages established components (GPs, PMCMC/PGAS) but contributes a cohesive, fully Bayesian, nonparametric learning framework and practical algorithms.

Significance
The method advances Bayesian learning for nonlinear dynamical systems by avoiding parametric surrogates or point estimates for f, potentially improving uncertainty quantification and mitigating overfitting. The approach can serve as a learning backend for GP-based filtering/smoothing and model-based RL. The sparse construction increases applicability. However, empirical evidence is somewhat limited in scope (one synthetic benchmark and a qualitative cart-pole example). Stronger quantitative comparisons to prior learning approaches on standard datasets would strengthen claims of impact.

Pros
- Conceptually clean and principled: fully Bayesian, nonparametric treatment of SSM dynamics by collapsing the GP.
- Technically sound use of PGAS for non-Markov latent trajectories; clear invariance argument and practical ancestor sampling computation.
- Thoughtful computational treatment with Cholesky updates and a sparse FIC variant; concrete complexity reductions.
- Demonstrates robustness to misspecified mean functions and ability to represent multimodal smoothing posteriors.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited: few baselines and metrics, no quantitative results on cart-pole, no runtime/memory scaling plots, and modest T and N.
- Some practical aspects need clarification (choice of proposal in SMC, memory complexity per particle, sensitivity to hyperpriors/identifiability between kernel variance and Q).
- Assumes known observation function structure; learning both f and g nonparametrically is out of scope, which may limit applicability in some domains.
- The method may remain computationally heavy for long sequences without inducing points; guidance on choosing M and inducing locations is brief.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide quantitative comparisons against EM/MAP GP-SSM learning [2,8] and GPDM [Wang et al., 2005] on shared benchmarks, including multi-step predictive performance and calibration.
- Report computational cost (time/memory) vs T, N, and M, and effective sample size or autocorrelation of PGAS to support mixing claims.
- Clarify the proposal distribution used in CPF-AS and whether any likelihood-informed proposals were employed.
- Discuss identifiability and prior choices for kernel amplitude vs process noise Q; include a sensitivity analysis.
- For the cart-pole, add baselines (e.g., learned parametric dynamics or GP learned from approximate smoothed states) and quantitative metrics.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-motivated contribution that brings fully Bayesian, nonparametric learning to GP-SSMs via PGAS with careful algorithmic design. The approach is technically sound and potentially impactful, though empirical validation could be broadened and some practical details clarified.
