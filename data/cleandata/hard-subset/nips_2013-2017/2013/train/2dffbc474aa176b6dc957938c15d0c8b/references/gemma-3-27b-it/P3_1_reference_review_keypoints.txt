- Proposes a fully Bayesian, non-parametric GP-SSM that marginalizes the transition function f using a tailored PMCMC algorithm.  
- Leverages analytic GP posterior tractability once the state trajectory is known to implement efficient marginalization.  
- Demonstrates the method on synthetic nonlinear benchmarks and a cart-pole control problem.  
- Situates the work well in prior literature, highlighting differences from parametric/MAP approaches and connections to EM- and particle-based methods.  
- Builds on particle MCMC foundations (Andrieu et al., Lindsten et al.) and introduces FIC inducing-point priors for computational efficiency.  
- Core strength: flexible modeling of highly non-linear dynamics without strong parametric constraints.  
- Marginalizing f avoids identifiability issues and allows the model to adapt complexity in a principled way.  
- Tailored PMCMC implementation reuses computations and makes the approach practical for moderate problem sizes.  
- Theoretical derivations (marginal prior, GP theory) are clear and technically sound.  
- Empirical results show effective learning and appropriate uncertainty quantification on challenging benchmarks.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, facilitating understanding of technical details.  
- Major weakness: PMCMC remains computationally expensive, especially for long time series and high-dimensional states.  
- Lacks concrete timing/scaling results to clarify cost vs. number of particles and problem size.  
- Hyperparameter tuning for both GP and PMCMC sampler is not sufficiently addressed; sensitivity and guidance are missing.  
- FIC prior scalability depends on inducing-point choice, but analysis of selection strategies is limited.  
- Experimental validation is restricted to synthetic datasets; real-world evaluations are absent.  
- Some notation is dense (notably Sections 3.1 and 4.1) and would benefit from clearer variable/dimension explanations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, advances over prior methods, strong empirical performance, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: high computational cost, limited experimental scope, unresolved hyperparameter and inducing-point issues.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper worthy of acceptance with minor revisions to address hyperparameter tuning, FIC scalability, timing results, and a discussion of limitations and future scaling approaches.
