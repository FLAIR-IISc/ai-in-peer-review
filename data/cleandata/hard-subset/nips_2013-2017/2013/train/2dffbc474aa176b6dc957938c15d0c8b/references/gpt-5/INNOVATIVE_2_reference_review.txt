Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning and inference in nonlinear Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) by placing a GP prior over the unknown transition function f while assuming a known (possibly parametric) measurement model g. The central idea is to fully marginalize f, which induces non-Markovian dependencies over the latent states, and then perform posterior inference over the latent trajectory and hyperparameters using particle MCMC—specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). This yields a sampler for the exact smoothing distribution p(x0:T | y), despite unknown transition dynamics, and subsequently a closed-form GP posterior for f conditional on sampled trajectories. The paper also proposes a sparse FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and provides careful implementation details (Cholesky rank-1 updates, reuse across time steps).

Relative to prior GP-SSM work, the contribution is to avoid parametric finite target-point approximations (Turner et al., GPDM) and MAP/EM treatments (Wang et al.; Ghahramani & Roweis), and to provide a fully Bayesian nonparametric treatment that samples from the joint posterior over states and hyperparameters with f integrated out. The approach complements GP-based filtering/smoothing for known dynamics (Deisenroth et al.) by enabling learning of the dynamics. Methodologically it builds on PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.) but adapts them to the non-Markovian structure induced by the collapsed GP prior, including efficient evaluation of ancestor weights.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Collapsing the transition function in a GP-SSM and sampling latent trajectories with PGAS is a bold and elegant idea that preserves the nonparametric nature of the model while addressing the strong state–function coupling that plagues joint sampling.
- Sound methodology: The derivations for the marginal prior over trajectories (product of GP one-step predictions), the CPF-AS kernel, and ancestor weights are correct and well-motivated. The use of conjugacy to recover f posterior after smoothing is clean.
- Practical contributions: Clear complexity analysis; efficient implementation via incremental Cholesky updates; sparse FIC variant with O(M^2 T) complexity. These details matter for making the method usable.
- Expressive predictive representation: The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive density over f∗ captures multimodality induced by observation ambiguity, a clear advantage over point-estimate approaches.
- Experiments illustrate key behaviors: The nonlinear 1D benchmark highlights multimodal smoothing and robustness to a misspecified mean function; cart-pole shows sensible uncertainty quantification in a 4D system.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical scope is limited: Only two datasets, primarily qualitative for cart-pole. No runtime/memory reports, scaling with T/M/N, or sensitivity analyses (e.g., mixing vs number of particles, chain length).
- Baselines and metrics: The 1D benchmark has reasonable baselines, but cart-pole lacks comparisons to learned parametric models, variational GP-SSMs, or alternative PMCMC baselines. Multi-step prediction and control-relevant metrics are missing.
- High implementation complexity: While the paper provides guidance, computing ancestor weights with reusing Cholesky factors and rank-one updates is nontrivial; more algorithmic pseudocode or release of code would help reproducibility.
- Scalability and dimensionality: The approach may struggle in higher-dimensional state spaces and long sequences; the paper does not empirically probe these limits. Inducing point selection is deferred and could interact with hyperparameter sampling and mixing.
- Assumptions: The known measurement model g (possibly with a few parameters) may be restrictive in domains where sensor models are also complex; identifiability issues are acknowledged but left unexplored.

Quality
The technical development is careful and largely sound, with correct use of PMCMC theory. The marginal trajectory prior and CPF-AS adaptation are well executed. The experimental section supports claims about mixing and expressivity qualitatively, but more thorough quantitative validation (scaling, ablations, diagnostics) would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The derivations for equations (3)–(4) and (6)–(8) are clear. The implementation section is useful but could benefit from a concise algorithmic summary (e.g., a boxed procedure for efficient ancestor weight computation and Cholesky updates) to enhance reproducibility.

Originality
High. While it leverages known PMCMC machinery, the decision to collapse the GP transition function and perform exact smoothing in a non-Markovian latent model via PGAS is, to my knowledge, novel and impactful for GP-SSMs. The sparse FIC integration and practical guidance add value.

Significance
High. A fully Bayesian, nonparametric treatment of GP-SSMs that avoids parametric approximations fills a gap and can influence future work in system identification, time-series modeling, and RL model learning. Despite computational costs, the framework is a strong conceptual advance, and the sparse variant improves practicality.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide runtime and memory benchmarks versus T, N, and M, and basic mixing diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation) to quantify the claimed good mixing.
- Include multi-step predictive performance on cart-pole and at least one competitive learned baseline (e.g., parametric SSM, GPDM/MAP, or a variational GP-SSM).
- Discuss robustness to kernel choice and the role of the mean function prior; can the method recover when the mean is poor and data are scarce?
- Clarify hyperparameter priors and slice sampling settings; report acceptance/mixing behavior.
- Consider adding a brief pseudocode block for the efficient ancestor weight computation and Cholesky updates to ease implementation.

Pros
- Strong, novel idea: fully marginalize f and sample trajectories with PGAS.
- Theoretically principled and practically informed.
- Retains nonparametric expressivity; handles multimodal smoothing.
- Sparse variant addresses computational burden.

Cons
- Limited experimental breadth and ablations.
- No quantitative scalability or mixing diagnostics.
- Implementation complexity high; reproducibility could be improved.
- Scalability to high-dimensional states and very long T remains uncertain.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold and novel contribution that advances Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs by a clear conceptual step—collapsing the transition function and leveraging PGAS for exact smoothing in a non-Markovian latent model. While the experiments could be expanded, the methodological contribution is significant and likely to spur follow-up work and practical adoption.
