

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper proposes a Bayesian approach for learning Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing out the state transition function f and using Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) for inference. The authors claim to maintain the full nonparametric expressivity of GP-SSMs while enabling fully Bayesian inference over the state trajectory and hyperparameters. The work builds on established literature in GP-SSMs (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al.) and PMCMC methods (Andrieu et al., Lindsten et al.), presenting a marginalization technique that addresses the challenge of learning the dynamics when states are unobserved.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a technically sound approach to marginalizing the state transition function in GP-SSMs. The theoretical derivations for the marginal prior (equations 3-4) and the PGAS algorithm are well-motivated and clearly presented. The sparse GP-SSM construction using the FIC prior is a reasonable extension of established sparse GP methods (Snelson et al., Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen). The experimental results on the nonlinear system benchmark and cart-pole system demonstrate the model's ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions and capture uncertainty in different regions of state space, which is valuable for dynamical systems modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the solid theoretical foundation, the paper falls short on several critical aspects of scientific rigor expected at NIPS. The most significant deficiency is the lack of comprehensive experimental validation against established baselines. The authors compare to only two parametric models (true model structure and linear model) and a single alternative GP-based model (model B), but fail to include comparisons to recent state-of-the-art methods for GP-SSM learning. For instance, they do not compare to the GP-SSM learning approaches by Wang et al. (2006), Deisenroth et al. (2011), or the more recent Bayesian inference methods in the literature.

The paper's claim of "very good mixing" (Section 1) is unsubstantiated. No convergence diagnostics are provided, no mixing rates are reported, and there's no comparison to alternative samplers for the same problem. The computational complexity analysis (O(T^3) for vanilla GP, O(M^2T) for sparse GP) is presented but not validated with timing experiments on increasingly large datasets to demonstrate scalability.

The experimental section (Section 6) is particularly weak. The nonlinear system benchmark uses T=200 time steps, which is relatively small for a time series application. The cart-pole system is trained on only 100 observations, raising questions about the model's ability to generalize to more complex or larger-scale systems. The results in Table 1 are presented without statistical significance testing, making it difficult to assess whether the reported improvements are meaningful.

The paper also fails to adequately address limitations. For instance, it doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the method to the choice of inducing points in the sparse GP approach, nor does it compare the performance of the sparse versus full GP-SSM on larger datasets where computational efficiency would be crucial. The authors claim to "retain the full nonparametric expressivity," but provide no evidence that this leads to significantly better predictive performance compared to simpler parametric models.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a technically interesting extension of existing GP-SSM learning methods, but the lack of comprehensive experimental validation against established baselines significantly undermines its contribution. While the marginalization approach is conceptually sound, the paper fails to demonstrate that it provides meaningful advantages over simpler, more established methods. The claims about "very good mixing" and the superiority of the approach are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by: (1) a more thorough comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods for GP-SSM learning, (2) experiments on larger datasets to demonstrate scalability, (3) detailed convergence diagnostics for the PGAS sampler, and (4) a more critical discussion of limitations and when this approach might be preferable to simpler alternatives.

As a skeptical reviewer who values methodological rigor and thorough experimental validation, I find the current submission lacking in the key areas required for acceptance at NIPS. While the methodological approach is sound, the insufficient empirical evidence prevents me from concluding that this represents a significant advance over established approaches to GP-SSM learning. I recommend rejection unless the authors can substantially strengthen the experimental section with more comprehensive comparisons and validation.
