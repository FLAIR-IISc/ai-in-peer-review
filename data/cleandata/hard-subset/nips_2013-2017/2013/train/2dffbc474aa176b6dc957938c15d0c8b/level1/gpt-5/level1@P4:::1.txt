Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses fully Bayesian inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior on the transition dynamics f, while assuming a known or parametric measurement model g. The key idea is to marginalize out the unknown transition function f exactly, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories, and then perform inference over the latent states x0:T and hyperparameters θ with Particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). Once samples from the smoothing distribution p(x0:T, θ | y0:T) are obtained, the posterior predictive for f at any test point can be computed in closed form, giving a mixture of Gaussian predictive models over f. The method maintains the nonparametric character of the model and supports sparse GPs (FIC) to reduce computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T).

This work builds on: GP-based dynamical systems (e.g., Deisenroth et al. on filtering/smoothing for fixed GPs), EM and MAP approaches to GP dynamical models (Turner et al., Wang et al.), and PMCMC (Andrieu et al.)/PGAS (Lindsten et al.). The novelty here is to collapse the GP function within an SSM and perform smoothing and hyperparameter learning via PGAS, without resorting to parametric approximations of f.

Quality (technical soundness and empirical support):
- The marginalization of f leads to closed-form one-step predictive Gaussians for p(xt | x0:t−1, θ) (equations 3a–3c) and a non-Gaussian joint prior over trajectories (equation 4). The derivations are consistent with standard GP regression conditional formulas augmented with process noise Q.
- The CPF-AS/PGAS construction is appropriate for non-Markovian latent variable models. The ancestor sampling weight computation w̃ ∝ w p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) correctly exploits cancellation of likelihood terms and uses the collapsed prior over trajectories.
- The computational analysis is credible: naive O(T^4) reduced to O(T^3) via rank-1 updates/downdates of Cholesky factors across time; the FIC prior yields O(M^2 T) when caching factorization of P and reusing intermediate products.
- Hyperparameter sampling leverages the collapsed GP regression view given fixed states; slice sampling is a reasonable generic choice.
- Experiments demonstrate: (i) a nonlinear scalar benchmark with multimodal smoothing due to symmetric observations; the sampler captures multimodality and produces competitive predictive RMSEs compared with parametric baselines; (ii) a 4D cart-pole example illustrating calibrated one-step predictive uncertainty in regions near training data; and a sparse variant with 40 inducing points.

While the algorithms are well-motivated and derivations are sound, the empirical evaluation is limited in scope: comparisons to strong GPDM/EM/EP baselines are qualitative or absent; scalability to long sequences/high-dimensional states is not extensively tested; predictive metrics are limited (primarily one-step RMSE).

Clarity (organization and presentation):
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The exposition of the collapsed prior (Section 3.1) and the PGAS-based sampler (Section 3.3) is precise. Implementation details (Section 4) are valuable, especially the Cholesky update strategy and the explicit FIC formulas. Areas that could be clarified:
- Explicit treatment of multi-output GPs for vector-valued f, including assumptions (e.g., independent dimensions vs. correlated outputs) and implications for Q and kernel structure.
- More detail on the choice and optimization/selection of inducing inputs in the sparse model and how this interacts with the PMCMC loop.
- A concise pseudocode for CPF-AS with the required matrix updates for reproducibility.

Originality:
The combination of (i) exact marginalization of the nonparametric transition function, (ii) PMCMC/PGAS to sample from the resulting non-Markovian smoothing distribution, and (iii) subsequent closed-form posterior over f given state samples appears novel relative to prior GPDM and EM/MAP approaches. Prior NIPS work focused on learning parametric representations or approximate inference with fixed GP dynamics; this paper enables fully Bayesian learning with preserved nonparametric expressivity.

Significance:
The contribution is significant for Bayesian time-series and dynamical systems: it provides a principled route to fully Bayesian GP-SSMs, mitigating overfitting issues associated with MAP/EM and enabling uncertainty-aware learning of dynamics. The ability to incorporate sparse GPs broadens applicability. Potential impact spans system identification, control, and reinforcement learning. Practical significance would be strengthened by more extensive empirical evidence on scalability and comparisons to established baselines.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Conceptually clean and technically sound collapsing of GP dynamics within an SSM.
- Appropriate and state-of-the-art inference machinery (PGAS) tailored to the non-Markovian setting, with careful computational considerations.
- Maintains full nonparametric expressivity; clear path to sparse approximations with rigorous complexity reductions.
- Addresses multimodal smoothing effectively; produces mixture-of-Gaussian predictive dynamics capturing ambiguity.
- Well-situated within and advancing the GP dynamical systems literature.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Empirical evaluation is relatively modest: limited datasets, primarily one-step predictions, and few quantitative comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., GPDM/EP or EM-based methods).
- Scalability remains a concern: O(T^3) (vanilla) and O(M^2 T) (sparse) may still be heavy for long sequences; high-dimensional state settings are not thoroughly evaluated.
- Some implementation choices (multi-output GP assumptions, inducing-input selection) are not fully specified or validated.
- The assumption of known/parametric measurement function g reduces generality; the approach to jointly learning g is not explored.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include quantitative comparisons with GPDM (Wang et al.), EP-based GPDS (Deisenroth et al.), and EM/variational baselines on shared benchmarks, reporting smoothing accuracy and multi-step predictive performance.
- Evaluate scalability on longer sequences and higher-dimensional state spaces; report runtime and mixing diagnostics versus number of particles N and T.
- Clarify multi-output GP modeling choices and their computational consequences; consider discussing coregionalization vs. independent outputs.
- Provide methodology for selecting inducing inputs (e.g., fast forward selection) within the PMCMC workflow and sensitivity analyses to M and inducing locations.
- Explore extensions to learning g (with appropriate identifiability constraints) or discuss scenarios where this assumption holds.

Overall assessment:
A solid and novel contribution that advances fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs by collapsing the transition function and leveraging PGAS. The theory and algorithmic design are strong and clearly presented. The main limitation lies in the breadth and depth of empirical validation. With strengthened experiments, this work is likely to have substantial impact.
