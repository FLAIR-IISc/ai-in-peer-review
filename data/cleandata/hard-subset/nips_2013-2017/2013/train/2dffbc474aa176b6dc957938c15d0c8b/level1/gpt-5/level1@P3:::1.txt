Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). A GP prior is placed on the unknown transition function f, while the observation model g is known up to parameters. The key methodological choice is to marginalize f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ). Inference over the latent state path and hyperparameters is performed with Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), using a conditional particle filter (CPF-AS) tailored to the non-Markovian trajectory prior. After sampling from the smoothing distribution, the posterior over f and predictive distributions follow from standard GP regression conditioned on the sampled trajectories. The paper also develops a sparse variant using the FIC prior with inducing points to reduce computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and provides implementation details (rank-1 Cholesky updates; efficient ancestor sampling weights).

This work connects to a line of research on GP-based dynamical systems (e.g., Wang et al., NIPS 2005; Turner et al., AISTATS 2010; Deisenroth et al., NIPS/IEEE TAC). Prior methods typically fit a parametric approximation of the GP or use EM/EP for MAP/approximate Bayesian learning. In contrast, this paper performs fully Bayesian learning with the GP transition marginalized and uses PMCMC for exact (up to Monte Carlo error) joint smoothing and hyperparameter inference. The use of PGAS for non-Markovian latent variable models leverages recent advances in PMCMC (Andrieu et al., 2010; Lindsten et al., NIPS 2012) and is well-motivated here.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Collapsing the transition function and performing PMCMC over trajectories/hyperparameters is a clean, fully Bayesian alternative to EM/MAP approaches. It preserves the nonparametric character of the dynamics.
- Technical soundness: The construction of the non-Markovian trajectory prior (Eq. 3–4), the PGAS kernel for smoothing, and the ancestor sampling weights specialized to the GP trajectory prior are correct and well-cited.
- Practical contributions: Solid implementation guidance (rank-one Cholesky updates/downdates; efficient use of concatenated paths for ancestor weights) and a sparse FIC formulation that reduces complexity to O(M^2 T) are valuable for practitioners.
- Empirical demonstrations: The nonlinear benchmark illustrates multimodal smoothing and the ability to correct misspecified mean functions; the cart-pole example shows calibrated uncertainty and one-step predictive performance in 4D.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Computational scaling: Even with careful updates, the vanilla method is O(T^3) per PGAS sweep and requires per-particle matrix updates and ancestor computations. The sparse variant is promising, but the paper downplays implementation difficulty: achieving O(M^2 T) requires avoiding T-scaling intermediates everywhere. Please report actual wall-clock times, N, M, and memory usage as T grows, and provide sensitivity plots (RMSE/ESS vs. N, M).
- Experimental evaluation: Baselines are limited. A fair comparison to (i) GPDM/GP-DS learned with EM/MAP (Wang et al. 2005; Turner et al. 2010), (ii) EP-based GP-DS smoothing/filtering when the GP is learned from data, and (iii) PMCMC without collapsing f would strengthen the case for both accuracy and mixing. Include multi-step predictive performance and long-horizon rollouts, not only one-step RMSE.
- Hyperparameter sampling: θx is sampled via slice sampling conditioned on x0:T; mixing can be challenging with strong x–θ coupling. Diagnostics (autocorrelation, trace plots) and acceptance/mixing stats for θ would be useful. Consider elliptical slice sampling for GP hyperparameters or blocked updates if feasible.
- Proposal choice in SMC: Eq. (5) suggests sampling from p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t), but later the “simple choice” appears to be the prior p(xt | θ, x0:t−1). Clarify the actual proposal used and whether auxiliary/lookahead proposals leveraging yt were employed, as these substantially impact weight degeneracy.
- Inducing inputs: The sparse model section notes selection methods (e.g., fast forward selection), but the experiments do not specify how inducing inputs were chosen/optimized. Please detail this and assess sensitivity to inducing point placement and M.
- Identifiability and priors: While the paper motivates fixing/parametrizing g to avoid non-identifiability, discuss identifiability between GP amplitude/lengthscales and process noise Q. Prior choices for θ (e.g., log-normal) may bias Q; sensitivity analyses would help.

Quality:
- The methodological core is sound, with correct use of PGAS for non-Markovian smoothing and a well-justified collapsing strategy. Implementation details are thoughtful. The main caveat is computational cost and the need for more comprehensive empirical validation and diagnostics.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized. The notation around K̃ for concatenated paths and the ancestor weight computation is dense; a schematic and pseudocode for CPF-AS in the non-Markovian case would improve reproducibility. Explicitly state the proposal used in SMC and provide an algorithmic complexity table for vanilla vs. FIC implementations.

Originality:
- High. To my knowledge at the time, fully Bayesian GP-SSM with the transition GP marginalized and inference via PGAS is novel relative to EM/MAP or variational/EP approaches.

Significance:
- Potentially high for Bayesian time-series modeling: enables exact (up to Monte Carlo error) smoothing and system identification in flexible nonparametric dynamics. Practical impact will hinge on computational scalability; the sparse variant and implementation guidance are important steps.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, principled Bayesian treatment of GP-SSMs using modern PMCMC.
- Strong methodological contribution (collapsed non-Markovian PGAS) with solid derivations.
- Practical sparse formulation and implementation techniques.
- Clear demonstrations on synthetic and a standard control system.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical comparison to strong baselines; lack of runtime/scaling studies.
- Computational demands may limit applicability to longer series/high dimensions.
- Some ambiguities in proposal choice and inducing point selection; limited diagnostics on mixing.

Minor comments:
- Clarify whether inputs ut are included in the GP kernel (they are omitted for notational simplicity) and how they are scaled/concatenated with xt.
- When recommending compressing the MoG predictive to a single GP, note the loss of multimodality and provide a heuristic for when this is acceptable.
- Typographical: ensure consistent indexing (e.g., 0:t−1 vs. 1:t) and define all matrices when first introduced.

Overall recommendation: Leaning toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the experimental section with runtime/scaling, stronger baselines (including EM/EP-based GP-DS), hyperparameter mixing diagnostics, and clarifications on the proposal and inducing point selection.
