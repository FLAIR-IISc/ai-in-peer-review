This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior on the transition function f, then collapse/marginalize f to obtain a non-Markovian prior over trajectories p(x0:T | θ), and perform joint smoothing and hyperparameter inference with Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). Given samples from the smoothing distribution, the posterior over f is available in closed form as in GP regression. The authors also introduce a sparse construction using the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). Empirically, they demonstrate accurate smoothing under multimodal posteriors and competitive transition prediction on a nonlinear benchmark, as well as calibrated one-step predictive uncertainty on a 4D cart-pole system. The work is well positioned relative to prior GP-SSM learning via EM/MAP [2, 7, 8] and approximate inference with learned GPs [5, 6], and leverages recent PMCMC/PGAS advances [3, 4].

Quality: The methodological choices are sound. Collapsing f reduces strong state–function coupling, and the use of CPF-AS is appropriate for non-Markovian latents. The derivations for the collapsed prior (Eq. 3–4) and FIC-based updates (Eq. 8) are careful, with clear complexity analysis and implementation guidance (rank-one Cholesky updates). However, the empirical section is relatively modest: mixing diagnostics, sensitivity to N/L, robustness to kernel mis-specification, and runtime versus baselines are not fully explored. The measurement model generality is claimed but only Gaussian likelihoods are evaluated.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized; sections 3–4 provide actionable implementation details. Notation is heavy in places (e.g., block indices in Eq. 3), and a brief algorithmic schematic for CPF-AS would help readability. The discussion of inducing-point selection could be more prescriptive.

Originality: Collapsing the GP transition and performing PGAS-based smoothing in GP-SSMs is, to my knowledge, novel and distinct from prior EM/MAP approaches and from filtering/smoothing with fixed GP dynamics [2, 5, 6]. The integration with sparse GPs is a practical contribution.

Significance: The framework enables fully Bayesian learning in flexible dynamical models and can serve as a building block for downstream filtering/control. Scalability to long horizons and high-dimensional states remains a concern but the sparse variant is promising.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean, fully Bayesian treatment; novel use of PGAS with collapsed GP dynamics.
- Solid derivations and practical implementation insights; clear path to sparse scaling.
- Demonstrates handling of multimodal smoothing and calibrated uncertainty.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited empirical breadth and ablations; few baselines and runtime/mixing diagnostics.
- Assumes known observation model; identifiability and joint f–g learning not addressed.
- Scalability beyond moderate T and higher-dimensional states not convincingly demonstrated.
