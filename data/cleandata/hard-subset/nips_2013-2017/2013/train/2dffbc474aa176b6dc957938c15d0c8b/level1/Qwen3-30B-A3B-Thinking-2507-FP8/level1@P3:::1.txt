

# Peer Review: Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC

This paper presents a novel fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models by placing a Gaussian process prior over the state transition dynamics. The key innovation is marginalizing out the transition function and using tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) samplers to infer the joint smoothing distribution. This approach differs significantly from previous work that typically resorts to parametric approximations of the GP dynamics (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.).

The authors correctly identify a fundamental limitation in existing GP-SSM approaches: most previous methods either fix the GP model structure (Turner et al.) or use finite representations (Wang et al.), sacrificing the nonparametric expressivity of the model. By marginalizing out the transition function and using the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm, the authors preserve the full nonparametric flexibility while enabling efficient inference. The paper makes a particularly strong contribution by addressing the non-Markovian nature of the marginalized state process through tailored SMC-MCMC methods.

**Strengths:**
1. The marginalization approach is elegant and well-motivated, providing a principled way to maintain the nonparametric expressivity of the model while avoiding the need for parametric approximations.
2. The theoretical development is thorough, with clear derivations of the marginal prior and the PGAS algorithm. The connection to existing work (e.g., Lindsten et al.'s CPF-AS) is well-established.
3. The sparse GP extension using FIC (Fully Independent Conditional) is well-justified and implemented efficiently, reducing computational complexity from O(T^4) to O(M^2T) where M << T.
4. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the approach's ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions (Figure 2) and learn complex dynamics in the cart-pole system (Figure 3).
5. The paper clearly differentiates from related work (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al.) and properly addresses the non-identifiability concerns when modeling both dynamics and observation functions nonparametrically.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The computational complexity analysis, while presented, lacks concrete details about the practical scaling of the algorithm. The paper claims O(T^3) complexity for the vanilla GP implementation, but the analysis of the Cholesky factorization updates could be more rigorous.
2. The paper doesn't provide sufficient comparison with alternative nonparametric approaches to SSMs. For instance, how does this method compare to the GP-based methods using expectation propagation (Deisenroth & Mohamed, 2012) or the sparse GP methods for SSMs (Wang et al., 2005)?
3. The theoretical guarantees are limited to the mixing properties of the PGAS sampler (as in Lindsten et al.), but the paper doesn't provide any bounds on the approximation error introduced by the particle approximation.
4. The sparse GP implementation details could be more concrete. The paper states that "intermediate matrix-vector multiplications" need to be reused to achieve O(M^2T) complexity, but doesn't explain how this is implemented in practice.
5. The scalability to very long time series (T > 1000) isn't addressed, which is a significant limitation for many real-world applications.

**Significance and Originality:**
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a fully Bayesian approach to learning nonparametric state-space models that preserves the model's expressivity. Previous work (Deisenroth et al., 2012; Turner et al., 2011) focused on inference given a learned GP model, while this paper provides the crucial component for learning the GP model itself. The authors demonstrate that their approach can handle multimodal smoothing distributions that would be problematic for parametric approaches, which is a key advantage in real-world applications.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in time series modeling with a technically sound solution that builds upon recent advances in Bayesian inference (PMCMC, sparse GPs). The work is clearly original and advances the state of the art in GP-based state-space modeling.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The marginalization approach is elegant, the theoretical development is sound, and the experiments demonstrate clear advantages over parametric alternatives. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and addresses a meaningful problem.

While the weaknesses noted above are important, they don't undermine the paper's core contribution. The computational complexity analysis could be strengthened, and more thorough comparisons with alternative nonparametric methods would be valuable, but these are refinements rather than fundamental flaws.

I recommend acceptance. This paper would be of interest to the broader NIPS community working on Bayesian methods, time series modeling, and nonparametric statistics. The approach has clear potential for applications in robotics, control, and other fields requiring flexible modeling of dynamical systems.
