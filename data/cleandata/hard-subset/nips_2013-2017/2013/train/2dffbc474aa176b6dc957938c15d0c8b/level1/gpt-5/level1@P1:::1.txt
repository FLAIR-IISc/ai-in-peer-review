Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian method for inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) where the state transition function f is modeled by a Gaussian process (GP). The key idea is to marginalize f exactly, which induces a non-Markovian prior over trajectories x0:T expressed as a product of Gaussian conditionals where the mean and covariance are given by standard GP predictive formulas. This eliminates strong posterior coupling between f and x, and enables sampling directly from the smoothing distribution p(x0:T | y0:T, θ) using particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS), a recent PMCMC method that mitigates path degeneracy. Given samples of x and θ, the posterior over f and its predictive distribution at test inputs are available in closed form, yielding a mixture of Gaussians. The authors also develop a sparse variant based on the FIC approximation to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and provide careful implementation details (rank-1 Cholesky updates and reuse across time).

The work connects to a rich literature on GP-based dynamical models. In contrast to EM/MAP approaches that parameterize the GP via a finite set of targets (e.g., GPDM; Wang, Fleet, Hertzmann, NIPS 18; Turner et al., AISTATS), the present approach remains fully Bayesian and nonparametric by integrating out f. It complements methods like EP-based filtering/smoothing for GPs (Deisenroth et al., NIPS/IEEE TAC) by addressing the learning step. On the inference side, the paper leverages recent advances in PMCMC, specifically PGAS (Lindsten et al., NIPS 25), applying them to a non-Markovian latent model induced by GP marginalization—this is novel and technically interesting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The collapsing of f and the induced prior over trajectories are derived correctly, with clear expressions for the GP-predicted means and covariances even though the overall prior is non-Gaussian due to dependence on x. The use of PGAS is appropriate; the invariance of the CPF-AS kernel to the exact smoothing distribution is correctly stated, and the ancestor sampling weights are derived using the non-Markovian prior. Sampling θy and θx is well motivated; given x, learning θx reduces to GP regression with likelihood covariance Q, and slice sampling is a reasonable choice.
- Computational aspects: The O(T^3) naive cost and the reuse of Cholesky factorizations via rank-1 updates/downdates are well explained. The FIC variant and its O(M^2 T) complexity are valuable and practically important; the derivations of the FIC predictive moments are detailed.
- Experiments: The synthetic 1D nonlinear benchmark with bimodal smoothing demonstrates the core claim (learning under multimodal posteriors, robustness to mean function misspecification). The cart-pole (4D) one-step predictions with uncertainty illustrate applicability beyond toy settings. However, quantitative comparisons are limited: while RMSEs are reported against parametric baselines and a sparse variant, there is no comparison to strong GP-SSM learning baselines (e.g., EM/MAP, GPDM, variational GP state-space models) or to PMCMC on parametric SSMs, and no runtime/scaling analysis, ESS, or autocorrelation diagnostics.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear notation for the collapsed prior and thorough implementation guidance. Some parts are terse and could be expanded for reproducibility:
- Provide pseudocode or a summarized CPF-AS algorithm inline (rather than relying solely on [4]), including how ancestor sampling probabilities are computed efficiently in practice for the non-Markovian case.
- Clarify treatment of vector-valued outputs: are output dimensions modeled with independent GPs with shared kernel and additive Q, or is a multioutput kernel considered? This affects both modeling fidelity and complexity.
- Explicitly state priors and sampling steps for Q and θy (e.g., conjugate forms), and how identifiability is handled when g is known but θy is unknown.
- Include a small complexity table and memory footprint for vanilla vs FIC implementations.

Originality
The combination of exact marginalization of f with PGAS to sample from the induced non-Markovian smoothing distribution appears novel. Prior GP-SSM works either fix f and do approximate inference, or learn via EM/MAP with parametric surrogates. The paper preserves nonparametric expressivity and provides a principled Bayesian alternative with solid algorithmic contributions (including a sparse prior formulation for the state trajectory).

Significance
The approach addresses a central obstacle in GP-SSMs—joint inference over latent states and an unknown nonparametric transition—offering a method with asymptotically exact inference (up to Monte Carlo error) and improved mixing via ancestor sampling. If scaled, it could impact learning of dynamics for control, RL, and time-series modeling, and serve as a foundation for subsequent approximate filtering/smoothing methods that assume a learned GP. Practical significance would be strengthened by more comprehensive empirical evaluation (scaling in T and state dimension, comparison to variational/EM baselines) and release of code.

Pros
- Conceptually clean and principled fully Bayesian framework; f marginalized analytically.
- Clever use of PGAS to tackle the induced non-Markovian smoothing problem.
- Sparse FIC construction for substantial computational savings with clear implementation guidance.
- Demonstrates robustness to misspecified mean functions and handles multimodal smoothing.
- Produces rich predictive distributions (mixtures) and discusses pragmatic single-GP surrogates.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is somewhat limited: few datasets, limited baselines, no timing or mixing diagnostics, and no ablation on N, M, T.
- Assumes known observation model g; applicability when g is partially unknown is not investigated.
- Multioutput GP structure and correlations across state dimensions are not discussed.
- Potential computational burden for long sequences and higher-dimensional states; mitigation strategies (blocking, windowed smoothing, backward simulation variants) are not empirically explored.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to GPDM/EM-MAP methods, variational GP-SSMs, and parametric PMCMC baselines on the same datasets, including accuracy and runtime.
- Report autocorrelation/ESS vs number of particles and the impact of ancestor sampling on mixing; provide wall-clock scaling with T and nx for vanilla vs FIC.
- Clarify multioutput modeling choice and consider cross-output kernels or discuss trade-offs.
- Detail priors and sampling updates for Q and θy; discuss identifiability and sensitivity to the GP mean function prior.
- Include an additional real-world dataset (e.g., motion capture, robotics) with longer sequences.

Recommendation
Leaning toward acceptance. This is a solid, technically sound contribution that advances Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs via an elegant combination of ideas (collapsing + PGAS) and provides practical sparse implementations. Strengthening the empirical section as suggested would further increase its impact.
