This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models, leveraging Gaussian processes (GPs) for the state transition dynamics and Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) for efficient inference. The core idea is to marginalize over the transition dynamics function, enabling direct inference of the joint smoothing distribution. This is a significant contribution as it moves beyond parametric GP-SSMs and retains full nonparametric expressivity. The work builds upon existing GP-SSM literature (e.g., Deisenroth et al., Turner et al., Wang et al.) by providing a method for *learning* the GP model itself, rather than assuming a pre-learned dynamics. Related work in Bayesian nonparametric modeling and sequential Monte Carlo is also appropriately acknowledged.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical justifications for marginalizing out the dynamics and the resulting expressions for the prior are clearly presented. The use of PMCMC, specifically PGAS, is well-motivated and appropriate for the problem. The explanation of the computational optimizations (reuse of Cholesky factors, sparse GP implementation with FIC) is detailed and convincing. The experimental validation on both a synthetic benchmark and a cart-pole system demonstrates the practical viability of the approach. The authors acknowledge the approximation inherent in the sampling algorithm. The discussion of limitations (e.g., computational cost, need for thinning) is reasonable. However, more extensive empirical validation with comparisons to alternative methods (e.g., variational inference approaches to GP-SSMs) would strengthen the assessment. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of potential identifiability issues when both the state transition *and* observation model are flexible.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, though dense in places. The motivation for the approach is clearly articulated. The use of figures (e.g., Figure 1, Figure 2, Figure 3) helps to illustrate the key concepts and results. The section on implementation details is particularly valuable, providing concrete guidance for practical application. The explanations of the algorithms (CPF-AS, PGAS) are clear enough to follow, though familiarity with SMC and MCMC is assumed. A more explicit discussion of the hyperparameters used in the experiments, and their sensitivity analysis, could enhance clarity.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel combination of techniques. Marginalizing out the state transition function in a GP-SSM is a key contribution, differentiating this work from previous approaches that typically fix the dynamics during inference. The use of PGAS to efficiently sample from the resulting smoothing distribution is also a significant innovation. While the individual components (GPs, SMC, MCMC) are well-established, their integration in this specific context represents a substantial advance. The application of FIC sparse GPs to further reduce computational complexity adds to the novelty.

**Significance:** The results are important. The ability to learn nonlinear state-space models in a fully Bayesian and nonparametric manner has broad applications in various fields, including time series analysis, robotics, and control. The demonstration of successful learning on both a synthetic benchmark and a cart-pole system is encouraging. The method provides a flexible and principled framework for modeling complex dynamical systems with uncertainty. The potential for using the learned model for prediction and control is significant. The preservation of full nonparametric expressivity and the ability to incorporate prior knowledge through the GP mean function are valuable features. The method addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrably better way than previous research focused on parametric GP-SSMs.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to GP-SSM learning through marginalization.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and technical implementation.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on relevant benchmark problems.
*   Addresses a challenging problem with a significant impact.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparative evaluation against alternative methods.
*   Potential identifiability concerns are not fully addressed.
*   Computational complexity could be a barrier for high-dimensional problems.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is lacking.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Gaussian process state-space models. The technical soundness, originality, and potential impact of the work justify acceptance. Addressing the minor weaknesses noted above (e.g., more comprehensive evaluation, discussion of identifiability) would further strengthen the manuscript.
