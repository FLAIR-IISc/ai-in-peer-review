This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), tackling a challenging problem in time series analysis. The key contribution lies in marginalizing over the state transition dynamics, allowing for a truly nonparametric model and enabling efficient inference via a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) sampler. This builds upon a growing body of work leveraging GPs for SSMs, notably [5, 6, 2], but crucially addresses the learning of the GP dynamics itself, a component often missing or approximated in prior approaches. The method is theoretically sound, with clear connections to existing techniques like particle filtering, SMC, and MCMC, and it’s backed by illustrative experiments on both a synthetic benchmark and a cart-pole system.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper demonstrates a strong grasp of the underlying theory. The derivation of the marginalization of the transition function (Section 3.1) and the formulation of the PMCMC sampler (Section 3.3) appear correct and well-motivated. The connection to existing PMCMC literature [3, 4] is clearly established.
* **Novelty:** Marginalizing out the transition function *fully* within a Bayesian nonparametric framework is a significant contribution. Previous approaches often rely on parametric approximations of the GP dynamics, limiting the model's expressiveness. The use of recent advances in PMCMC, particularly [4], to handle the resulting challenges is also novel and smart.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The equations are clearly presented, and the notation, while dense, is consistent. The flow from problem formulation to solution and experiments is logical. The motivations behind the design choices are well explained.
* **Significance:** The work addresses a core challenge in modelling complex dynamical systems. The ability to learn a nonparametric model of the dynamics without making strong assumptions about its functional form is highly valuable. The experimental results demonstrate the method’s ability to handle multimodal distributions and provide uncertainty estimates, crucial for applications like control and reinforcement learning.
* **Implementation Details:** The inclusion of detailed implementation considerations (Section 4.2) regarding computational complexity and optimization strategies (e.g., reusing Cholesky factorizations) is a strong point.  This demonstrates the authors’ awareness of practical challenges and facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address computational complexity, it remains a significant concern, especially for high-dimensional state spaces. The O(M<sup>2</sup>T) complexity for the sparse GP version is still considerable.  A more detailed discussion on the scalability of the method to truly large-scale problems would be beneficial. The experiments, while illustrative, are relatively small-scale.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter selection. The priors used (log-normal) are mentioned, but the sensitivity of the results to different prior choices and the methods used for setting hyperparameters are not elaborated upon. This is a crucial practical consideration.
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** While the comparisons to parametric models are insightful, a comparison to other recent Bayesian nonparametric approaches for SSMs (e.g., those based on Dirichlet Process mixtures or other flexible priors) would strengthen the paper.  How does this method perform *relative* to these alternatives in terms of accuracy, computational cost, and ease of implementation?
* **FIC Prior Justification:** The choice of the FIC prior for the sparse GP version is not fully justified.  A brief discussion on the advantages and disadvantages of FIC compared to other sparse GP methods (e.g., variational sparse GPs) would be helpful.
* **Experiment Scope:** The cart-pole experiment, while interesting, could be expanded. Presenting results on more complex control tasks or comparing performance to established reinforcement learning algorithms would further demonstrate the method's practical utility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and technically sound contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric SSMs.
* The marginalization of the transition function is a significant advance, enabling a truly flexible model.
* The method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and backed by illustrative experimental results.
* The inclusion of implementation details demonstrates a practical awareness of the challenges involved.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost remains a significant limitation.
* The lack of a detailed discussion on hyperparameter tuning and comparison to alternative methods weakens the paper.
* The experiments, while promising, are relatively small-scale and lack a comprehensive evaluation on more complex problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. The technical depth, novelty, and clarity of presentation are commendable. While the computational cost and the lack of extensive experimentation are legitimate concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the points raised regarding hyperparameter tuning, comparison to alternatives, and scalability in a revised version.  A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs involved in choosing different sparse GP methods would also be valuable.
