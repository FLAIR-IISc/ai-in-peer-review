- Proposes a novel, fully Bayesian GP-SSM that analytically marginalizes the GP transition function, producing a non-Markovian latent model sampled via PMCMC (PGAS).  
- Posterior predictive for the transition becomes a mixture of GPs; a sparse FIC variant reduces complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T).  
- Core idea is original and significant: marginalizing the GP dynamics and applying PMCMC is a major conceptual advance for nonparametric dynamical-system learning.  
- Methodological derivation is sound and elegant, correctly accounting for uncertainty in states, hyperparameters, and the function.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and situates itself appropriately relative to prior GP-SSM and SMC literature.  
- Presents a complete pipeline for inference and learning, and includes a sparse GP formulation to address scalability.  
- Computational cost is a major concern: complexity applies per particle per MCMC iteration, leading to potentially prohibitive runtimes for large T or many iterations.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited: comparisons are against parametric models rather than state-of-the-art GP-SSM methods (variational/EM), weakening empirical claims.  
- Cart-pole experiment is qualitative and lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., multi-step prediction or long-term simulation) to validate performance.  
- Sparse FIC practicalities are underdeveloped: selecting and updating inducing inputs inside the MCMC loop is non-trivial and not addressed.  
- Reproducibility could be improved: Section 4.2 is dense and needs more detailed pseudo-code or explanation of the Cholesky rank-2nx updates.  
- Posterior as a mixture of GPs is expressive but may be cumbersome for downstream tasks; the proposed distillation to a single sparse GP raises questions about information loss.  
- Overall recommendation: strong methodological contribution deserving acceptance, tempered by the need for stronger empirical validation and more practical guidance on computational and sparse-model issues.
