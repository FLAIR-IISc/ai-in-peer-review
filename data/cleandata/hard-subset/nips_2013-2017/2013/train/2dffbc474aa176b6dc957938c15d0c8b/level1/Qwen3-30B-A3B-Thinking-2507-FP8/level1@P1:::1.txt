

# Peer Review

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), addressing the challenge of learning nonlinear dynamics without assuming a fixed functional form. The authors place a Gaussian process prior over the state transition function, marginalizing it out during inference and using a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to sample from the joint smoothing distribution. This approach preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the model while enabling efficient learning. The paper also introduces a sparse GP-SSM construction using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to reduce computational complexity, making the approach scalable.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions such as Deisenroth et al. (2011, 2012) who developed approximation methods for filtering and smoothing in GP-SSMs but assumed the dynamics were already learned. Turner et al. (2009) and Wang et al. (2005) used EM algorithms to learn parametric representations of GP dynamics, which the authors correctly identify as less flexible than their nonparametric approach. The paper properly situates itself within this literature by highlighting how their method provides the crucial component for learning the GP model that previous methods lacked.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical foundation for marginalizing the transition dynamics function is solid, and the derivation of the joint smoothing distribution is carefully presented. The PGAS approach is well-motivated and supported by theoretical arguments. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, showing how the approach can be made efficient using sparse GP approximations. The experimental results on benchmark nonlinear systems demonstrate the approach's effectiveness, particularly in handling multimodal smoothing distributions where parametric models fail. However, the paper could have provided more details on the sensitivity of the approach to the choice of hyperparameters and the number of particles, which would strengthen the empirical validation.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical derivations. The key algorithm (PGAS) is presented in a readable format. However, some sections, particularly the theoretical background on marginalizing the dynamics function, could benefit from more intuitive explanations to make the contribution accessible to readers without deep prior knowledge of GP-SSMs. The connection between the marginalization step and the loss of Markovian structure could be explained more clearly. The figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively illustrate the method's capability to handle multimodal distributions.

**Originality**: This work makes a clear contribution by providing a fully Bayesian nonparametric approach to learning GP-SSMs, which significantly differs from previous methods that typically used parametric approximations of the dynamics. The marginalization of the transition dynamics function is a novel technical contribution. The use of PGAS for inference in this context is also novel. While sparse GP methods for SSMs have been explored, the FIC-based construction presented here appears to be a well-motivated adaptation of existing sparse GP techniques to the specific challenges of SSMs. The authors properly reference related work, though a more explicit comparison with specific prior methods (e.g., how their approach differs from Deisenroth's filtering methods) would strengthen the originality claim.

**Significance**: The results are significant because they enable fully Bayesian inference in nonparametric state-space models, which is a challenging problem with broad applicability. The ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions (demonstrated in the experiments) is particularly valuable for real-world applications where standard parametric approaches may fail. The computational efficiency achieved through sparse GP approximations makes the approach applicable to larger datasets, potentially expanding its use in robotics, control systems, and time series analysis. The paper successfully demonstrates the approach's superiority over parametric alternatives on benchmark problems.

**Strengths**:
- The fully Bayesian approach preserves the nonparametric expressivity of the model
- The marginalization of the dynamics function is a novel and technically sound contribution
- The PGAS inference algorithm provides efficient sampling
- The sparse GP-SSM construction enables practical scalability
- The experiments demonstrate clear advantages over parametric alternatives, especially in handling multimodal distributions

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited discussion of sensitivity to hyperparameters and number of particles
- Some theoretical details could be made more accessible to a broader audience
- The comparison with related work could be more thorough, particularly regarding how the approach differs from specific prior methods
- The paper doesn't fully address limitations (e.g., when inducing points are insufficient)

**Arguments for acceptance**: This paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference in state-space models. The fully Bayesian approach to learning GP-SSMs addresses a clear gap in the literature and provides a practical solution with demonstrated effectiveness on challenging benchmark problems. The computational improvements through sparse GP approximations make the approach applicable to real-world problems. The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance**: The main concerns are that the paper could provide more thorough comparisons with related work and address the sensitivity of the approach to key parameters. However, these are minor weaknesses that could be addressed in a revised version.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to NIPS that advances the state of the art in Bayesian inference for nonlinear dynamical systems. The approach is theoretically well-founded, practically implemented, and empirically validated. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the comparison with related work and address the sensitivity analysis.
