Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian method for inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). A GP prior is placed on the state transition function f, while the observation model g is assumed known (possibly parametric). The key idea is to marginalize f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories, and then sample from the joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T, θ | y0:T) using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). Given samples of x0:T and θ, the predictive distribution over f at new inputs follows from standard GP regression, resulting in a mixture of Gaussians representation. The paper also introduces a sparse variant using the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2T) and provides implementation details (rank-1 updates/downdates of Cholesky factors and reuse of matrix products) to make the method practical. Experiments on a nonlinear benchmark (with multimodal smoothing) and a 4D cart-pole system demonstrate state smoothing and one-step prediction performance.

Relative to prior GP-SSM work, this paper differs from EM/MAP-based learning with parametric approximations to the GP (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.) and from works that assume the GP dynamics are already learned and focus on filtering/smoothing (Deisenroth et al.). The main novelty is collapsing the GP dynamics and using PGAS to sample the state trajectory and hyperparameters jointly, preserving full nonparametric expressivity. On the inference side, it leverages PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and the PGAS kernel (Lindsten et al.) in a new way for GP-SSMs.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the collapsed prior over states (Eq. 3–4) and its use within SMC/PGAS is careful and consistent with GP regression identities. The invariance property of PGAS is correctly cited, and the ancestor sampling weights are adapted to the non-Markovian prior using Eq. 6. The sparse FIC construction is standard and the complexity claims O(M^2T) are plausible given the outlined reuse of factorizations.
- Algorithmic practicality: The authors address the main computational bottlenecks with detailed discussion of matrix factor updates and how to avoid recomputation. This is valuable and often missing in similar papers.
- Empirical support: The nonlinear benchmark convincingly shows handling of multimodal smoothing and improved prediction over mismatched parametric baselines; the cart-pole example shows calibrated uncertainty and reasonable one-step predictions. However, empirical evaluation is limited in breadth (two datasets), and quantitative comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., GPDS EM/MAP learning, particle MCMC without collapsing f, or alternative nonparametric SSMs) are sparse. Claims of “very good mixing” are not quantified (e.g., autocorrelation, ESS, acceptance rates), and using only 50 PGAS iterations with N=20 particles may be on the low side for difficult posteriors.
- Assumptions and limitations: The method accommodates general likelihoods p(yt | xt, θy) in principle, but experiments focus on Gaussian observation noise. Scalability with state dimension and long horizons is discussed theoretically but not tested extensively; memory/time scaling plots would strengthen the paper.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and provides sufficient mathematical detail to reproduce the method, including explicit expressions for the collapsed prior and the FIC variant. Implementation details are unusually thorough and helpful. Notational density is high and some steps (CPF-AS pseudocode, practical slice sampling settings, initialization and diagnostics) could be expanded slightly. Still, overall clarity is strong.

Originality:
- The combination of exact marginalization of the GP transition function with PGAS for smoothing and hyperparameter sampling in GP-SSMs is, to my knowledge, novel at the time. Prior work either fixes a parametric form, uses MAP/EM over a finite representation, or assumes dynamics are already learned. The paper’s collapsed PMCMC approach is a meaningful advance.

Significance:
- Conceptually, the paper advances fully Bayesian learning in nonlinear, nonparametric SSMs and provides a practical inference algorithm that preserves GP flexibility. This is likely to be used and extended (e.g., for control, reinforcement learning, and time-series modeling). The sparse variant and implementation guidance increase practical impact. The main caveat is computational cost and limited empirical breadth: convincing scaling results and comparisons to more baselines would enhance demonstrated significance.

Pros:
- Clear conceptual advance: collapsing GP dynamics and using PGAS to sample states/hyperparameters.
- Maintains full nonparametric expressivity; no need to parametrize f.
- Solid, careful derivations and implementation details; sparse GP variant with complexity reduction.
- Handles multimodal smoothing; produces mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution for f.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation is limited (two datasets), with few quantitative comparisons to prior GP-SSM learning methods and no ablations (e.g., effect of collapsing f, number of particles, number of PGAS iterations).
- No explicit mixing diagnostics or computational runtime/memory benchmarks; N=20, 50 iterations may be insufficient in harder settings.
- Experiments focus on Gaussian observation models; the generality to non-Gaussian likelihoods is not empirically demonstrated.
- Details on inducing point selection and sensitivity are minimal.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add quantitative comparisons to EM/MAP-based GP-SSM learning (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.), and to PMCMC without collapsing f, if feasible.
- Report autocorrelation/ESS plots for x and θ, as well as runtime and memory scaling with T, N, and state dimension.
- Include at least one non-Gaussian observation experiment or missing-data case to substantiate generality.
- Provide ablation on number of particles/iterations and effect of sparse FIC vs. full GP on accuracy and speed.
- Briefly discuss identifiability issues when both f and g are flexible, and practical guidance on choosing the mean function and priors.

Assessment against criteria:
- Quality: High, with minor empirical limitations. Theoretical framing and algorithmic details are strong; more quantitative evaluation would help.
- Clarity: High; well-organized and explicit, with a few dense notational sections.
- Originality: High; the collapsed PGAS approach for GP-SSMs is novel.
- Significance: High potential; practical impact would be clearer with broader experiments and benchmarks.

Overall recommendation: Strongly positive. The paper presents a principled, practically implementable, and novel method for fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs, with clear potential to influence subsequent work. Expanding empirical evaluation and diagnostics would further strengthen the paper.
