

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models with Gaussian process priors over state transitions. The key innovation is marginalizing out the state transition function f and using tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) samplers, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to infer the joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T | y0:T, u0:T). This approach preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the model while enabling efficient inference, contrasting with previous approaches that typically required parametric representations of f.

The paper makes a strong case for its contribution by clearly identifying the limitations of existing GP-SSM methods (e.g., Turner et al. [2], Wang et al. [8]) which often treat the GP as parametric or risk overfitting. The marginalization approach, combined with the PGAS sampler, elegantly addresses these limitations. The theoretical development is sound, with appropriate mathematical derivations for the non-Gaussian state trajectory prior and the implementation details for both vanilla and sparse GP-SSMs. The sparse GP extension using the FIC prior is particularly valuable for practical applications, and the computational complexity analysis (O(T^3) for vanilla GP, O(M^2T) for sparse GP) is well-justified.

The experimental section demonstrates the approach's effectiveness on two challenging benchmarks: a nonlinear system with multimodal smoothing distribution and a cart-pole system. The results show that the GP-SSM can accurately capture complex dynamics even when the mean function is misspecified, and the sparse variant maintains performance with significantly reduced computational cost. The comparison against parametric models and the ground truth model is appropriate, and the mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution effectively captures multimodality.

Strengths:
1. The paper successfully addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by enabling fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs while preserving nonparametric expressivity.
2. The theoretical development is rigorous and well-justified, with clear explanations of the marginalization approach and PGAS implementation.
3. The sparse GP extension provides a practical solution for larger datasets, with appropriate computational complexity analysis.
4. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the method's advantages over existing approaches.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks a thorough comparison of computational complexity with alternative methods. While it states O(T^3) complexity, it doesn't compare this to the computational cost of other Bayesian SSM approaches (e.g., those using variational inference or other MCMC methods), making it difficult to assess practical scalability.
2. The experimental section could be strengthened by including more detailed comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods for nonlinear SSMs, particularly those that also use nonparametric approaches (e.g., Deisenroth et al.'s work on GP-SSMs).
3. The limitations of the approach aren't sufficiently discussed. For example, how does the method scale to very long time series (T > 1000)? What are the sensitivity considerations for the number of particles N in the PGAS algorithm?
4. The paper claims "very good mixing" but doesn't provide concrete evidence (e.g., autocorrelation plots or effective sample size metrics) to support this claim.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with appropriate citations to prior work. The main contribution is original and significant for the field of probabilistic modeling of dynamical systems. The approach represents a meaningful advancement over previous GP-SSM methods by properly preserving the nonparametric nature of the model while enabling efficient Bayesian inference.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a detailed computational complexity comparison with relevant alternatives in the experimental section
2. Including more thorough experimental comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods
3. Providing evidence for the claimed "very good mixing" (e.g., autocorrelation plots)
4. Discussing the practical limitations and scalability of the approach more thoroughly
5. Adding a brief discussion of when this approach would be preferable to other methods (e.g., when the state transition is highly nonlinear but smooth)

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The technical content is sound, the approach is novel, and the experiments demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The minor weaknesses identified above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's main contribution. I recommend acceptance.
