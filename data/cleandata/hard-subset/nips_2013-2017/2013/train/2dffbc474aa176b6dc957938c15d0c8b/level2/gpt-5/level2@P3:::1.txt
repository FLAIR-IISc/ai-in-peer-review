Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses fully Bayesian inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior on the transition function f and marginalizing it out. This yields a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories p(x0:T | θ) expressible as a product of GP one-step conditionals, enabling inference over states and hyperparameters with Particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC). The core algorithmic contribution is a tailored particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) scheme that samples from the exact smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) while keeping the nonparametric function fully integrated out. Given samples of x0:T and θ, a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution for f(x∗) is obtained analytically; an optional sparse GP construction (FIC) reduces the per-iteration complexity to O(M^2 T). Implementation details show how to reuse Cholesky factorizations to achieve O(T^3) for full GPs and O(M^2 T) for FIC.

This work differs from prior GP-SSM approaches that either assume learned dynamics and focus on approximate filtering/smoothing (e.g., Deisenroth et al., TAC 2012; NIPS 2012) or optimize latent states and hyperparameters via EM/MAP with parametric representations (e.g., Turner et al., AISTATS 2010; Wang et al., NIPS 2006). By collapsing f and using PGAS (building on Andrieu et al., JRSS-B 2010 and Lindsten et al., NIPS 2013), the paper provides a fully Bayesian alternative that avoids parametric surrogates for the dynamics.

Strengths:
- Conceptual advance: fully Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs with the transition function marginalized, preserving nonparametric flexibility.
- Sound inference: PGAS kernels leave the exact smoothing distribution invariant; the use of ancestor sampling is well-motivated for non-Markovian priors.
- Solid derivations: explicit expressions for the collapsed trajectory prior and the PGAS weights for non-Markovian models; clear connection to GP regression for hyperparameter sampling.
- Practicality: computational strategies (rank-1 updates/downdates of Cholesky factors) and a principled sparse alternative (FIC) with complexity analysis.
- Expressive predictions: mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution captures multimodality in dynamics induced by ambiguous measurements.
- Experiments demonstrate handling of multimodal smoothing and report predictive accuracy; the method leverages informative GP mean functions while correcting model mismatch.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope. Comparisons to closely related learning baselines (EM for GP-SSMs, GPDM, MAP with variational approximations) are absent, making it hard to assess relative accuracy and computational cost.
- Limited scalability analysis. Results lack runtime, memory, and effective sample size (ESS) curves as a function of T, state dimension, number of particles N, and mixture size L; no ablation on the impact of ancestor sampling versus standard particle Gibbs/backward simulation.
- Sensitivity and identifiability considerations are under-discussed. The interplay between process noise Q and GP kernel amplitude can cause non-identifiability; guidance on priors and reparameterizations would be helpful.
- Sparse GP details: inducing input selection is not examined empirically; the effect of FIC vs full GP on posterior accuracy is not quantified.
- Some notation and exposition are dense. Equation (4) “Gaussian-looking but not Gaussian” could be better explained; more high-level intuition and a compact pseudocode for CPF-AS in the non-Markovian setting would aid clarity.

Quality:
The methodology is technically sound and builds on established PMCMC theory. The collapsed GP-SSM prior, PGAS construction, and complexity reductions are carefully described. Claims about invariance and mixture predictions are justified. The main limitation is the breadth of empirical validation and lack of head-to-head baselines.

Clarity:
Overall clearly written, but sections on non-Gaussian “Gaussian-looking” priors and CPF-AS mechanics could be made more accessible. Implementation details are valuable; adding schematic pseudocode and summarizing computational/memory costs per PGAS iteration would improve readability.

Originality:
High. Marginalizing the GP transition function and coupling with PGAS for exact smoothing in GP-SSMs appears novel relative to EM/MAP and post-hoc inference approaches.

Significance:
Potentially significant. Provides a principled, practical route to fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs, with implications for time-series modeling, system identification, and model-based RL where uncertainty calibration and multimodality matter.

Suggestions for improvement:
Essential:
- Add comparisons with EM-based GP-SSM learning (e.g., Turner et al.) and GPDM (Wang et al.), including predictive RMSE, state estimation error, and runtime/ESS. Include a PMMH or particle Gibbs without ancestor sampling baseline to quantify PGAS benefits.
- Provide scaling results versus T, N, and state dimension, including wall-clock per iteration and ESS per second; include autocorrelation plots of key hyperparameters and state components.
- Discuss identifiability between Q and kernel variance; report priors used and whether constraints or reparameterizations (e.g., fixing scales or using informative priors) are necessary.

Optional:
- Evaluate the sparse FIC variant against full GP in terms of accuracy vs M and runtime; include an inducing-input selection strategy and sensitivity analysis.
- Include a non-Gaussian likelihood example to demonstrate generality.
- Provide numerical stability notes on rank-one downdates and jitter strategies; report failure rates if any.
- Release code or pseudo-code for CPF-AS with the collapsed GP prior.

Pros:
- Fully Bayesian, nonparametric learning with collapsed dynamics
- Correct, efficient PGAS inference for non-Markovian priors
- Mixture predictive distribution capturing multimodality
- Complexity-aware implementation and sparse GP variant
- Demonstrated ability to correct misspecified mean functions

Cons:
- Limited empirical comparisons and scalability analysis
- Sparse GP choices and effects not empirically characterized
- Identifiability and prior sensitivity not fully addressed
- Exposition dense in parts; more intuition/pseudocode would help

Recommendation: Accept (strong). The methodological contribution and correctness justify inclusion; strengthening the empirical section with the suggested baselines and scaling analyses would further enhance impact.

Confidence: High in the assessment of methodology; moderate regarding empirical performance due to limited comparisons.
