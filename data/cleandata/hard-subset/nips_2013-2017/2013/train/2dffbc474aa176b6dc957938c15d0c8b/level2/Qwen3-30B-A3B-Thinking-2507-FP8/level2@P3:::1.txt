

The paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), addressing the challenge of nonparametric dynamics modeling without fixing the transition function upfront. The core contribution lies in marginalizing the state transition function \(f\) from the model, enabling direct inference of the smoothing distribution \(p(x_{0:T} | y_{0:T})\) via a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm. This approach preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the GP prior while avoiding the parametric approximations common in prior work (e.g., Turner et al. [2], Wang et al. [8]). The authors further introduce a sparse GP variant using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to reduce computational complexity to \(O(M^2T)\), where \(M \ll T\), making the method scalable.

The work demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical foundation is well-grounded, leveraging recent advances in PMCMC (Andrieu et al. [3], Lindsten et al. [4]) to handle the non-Markovian dependencies introduced by marginalizing \(f\). The marginalization strategy (Section 3.1) correctly avoids the pitfalls of parametric approximations, and the PGAS implementation (Algorithm 1) is rigorously justified with convergence guarantees. Computational analysis (Section 4) is thorough, showing how Cholesky updates and sparse approximations achieve practical complexity. The experiments validate the approach: on the nonlinear benchmark (Section 6.1), the method accurately recovers multimodal smoothing distributions despite a misspecified mean function, and the sparse variant achieves comparable predictive performance to the full GP. The cart-pole experiment (Section 6.2) demonstrates meaningful uncertainty quantification across the state space, a critical feature for control applications.

Originality is well-established. While GP-SSMs have been explored (Deisenroth et al. [5,6]), this work uniquely focuses on *fully Bayesian learning* of \(f\) without reducing it to a parametric form, contrasting with EM-based approaches (Turner et al. [2]) or MAP estimation (Wang et al. [8]). The marginalization technique combined with PGAS is a novel application to GP-SSMs, and the sparse implementation (FIC prior) extends existing sparse GP methods [15,16] to this non-Markovian setting. The paper adequately contextualizes its contribution against prior NIPS work (e.g., references to Deisenroth, Turner, Ghahramani).

Significance is compelling. The method enables principled Bayesian uncertainty quantification for dynamics learning, which is essential for safety-critical applications like reinforcement learning (as shown in the cart-pole example). The ability to learn complex dynamics without restrictive parametric assumptions addresses a long-standing challenge in SSMs. The computational efficiency via sparse GP makes the approach practically viable, advancing the state of the art beyond prior nonparametric SSMs that struggled with scalability.

Two weaknesses warrant attention. First, the experimental scope is limited to two synthetic systems. Adding a third benchmark—such as a high-dimensional robotics task or a real-world sensor dataset—would better demonstrate generalizability and scalability. Second, while the paper contrasts with parametric approaches (e.g., Table 1), it omits comparison to recent non-Bayesian GP-SSM methods, such as Deisenroth et al.’s (2014) expectation propagation-based filtering. Including such a comparison would strengthen the significance claim by positioning the work within the current landscape of GP-SSM inference.

The clarity is generally excellent. The model (Section 2) and inference (Section 3) are well-explained, with precise equations and algorithmic details. Minor improvements could be made: the description of Figure 2 (Section 6.1) would benefit from explicitly stating how the green mean function differs from the true dynamics, and the sparse GP implementation (Section 4.2) could briefly note the impact of inducing point selection on runtime (e.g., via the greedy method [18]).

The paper is a strong technical contribution that advances Bayesian inference for nonparametric dynamical systems. It resolves a key limitation in prior GP-SSM work by avoiding parametric approximations while maintaining computational feasibility. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and experimentally validated. The suggested improvements—expanding experimental benchmarks and adding comparative analysis against non-Bayesian GP-SSM methods—are reasonable and would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. The work aligns squarely with NIPS’s focus on principled probabilistic modeling and algorithmic innovation.
