

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) methods. The main claims are: (1) marginalizing over the transition dynamics function enables fully Bayesian inference while preserving the nonparametric expressivity of the model, (2) tailored PMCMC samplers (specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling) efficiently infer the joint smoothing distribution, and (3) sparse GP implementations significantly reduce computational complexity.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations with well-motivated marginalization techniques and carefully designed PMCMC samplers. The experimental validation on a nonlinear system benchmark and cart-and-pole system shows clear advantages over parametric models in capturing complex dynamics, with the sparse GP implementation effectively reducing computational complexity. The method addresses a key limitation in existing GP-SSM approaches that typically require fixed transition dynamics or parametric representations.

The paper reflects strong field knowledge, with appropriate citations to seminal works by Rasmussen, Deisenroth, and Turner, and demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature. The methodological details are sufficiently complete for reproducibility, including implementation specifics for both vanilla and sparse GP variants.

While the paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian time series modeling, it would benefit from more extensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly other Bayesian approaches to nonlinear state-space modeling. The experimental section is somewhat limited to two benchmark problems, and a more thorough analysis of computational efficiency across different problem sizes would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the discussion of limitations could be expanded to include scalability challenges with high-dimensional state spaces.

The paper's technical soundness, clear presentation, and practical significance make it a valuable contribution to the field. The fully Bayesian approach to nonparametric state-space modeling represents a meaningful advancement over previous work that often required parametric approximations. The sparse GP implementation further enhances its practical applicability.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian time series modeling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited experimental scope and provide more detailed discussion of scalability limitations.
