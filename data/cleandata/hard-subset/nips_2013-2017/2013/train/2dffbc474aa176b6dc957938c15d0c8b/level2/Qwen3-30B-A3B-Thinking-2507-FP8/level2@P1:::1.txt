

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics. The authors address a key limitation in previous GP-based SSM approaches, which typically require a parametric representation of the dynamics, by marginalizing out the transition function and inferring the joint smoothing distribution using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) samplers. The approach preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the model while enabling efficient inference, with a sparse GP construction to reduce computational complexity.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a thorough mathematical development of the marginal prior and the PMCMC inference algorithm. The authors effectively relate their work to previous contributions (e.g., Deisenroth et al., Turner et al.) while clearly articulating their novel contributions. The use of Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) to handle the non-Markovian dependencies introduced by marginalizing the transition function is particularly well-motivated and technically sound. The derivation of the marginal prior (equation 4) is carefully presented, and the authors provide a detailed explanation of the computational challenges and their solutions.

The quality of the paper is strong. The authors provide rigorous mathematical derivations of the key components, including the marginal prior (equation 4) and the efficient implementation details for both vanilla and sparse GP models. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and the experimental results on both a synthetic nonlinear system benchmark and a cart-and-pole system demonstrate the method's effectiveness, particularly in capturing multimodal smoothing distributions where parametric models fail. The results show that the GP-SSM approach outperforms parametric models in terms of predictive accuracy, as evidenced by the RMSE comparisons in Table 1.

The paper's originality is significant. While there has been prior work on GP-based SSMs (e.g., Deisenroth et al. [5,6], Turner et al. [2]), these approaches typically assume a parametric model for the dynamics or use finite approximations. This paper's fully Bayesian approach that preserves the nonparametric expressivity of the GP model is novel and addresses an important gap in the literature. The marginalization of the transition function while inferring the smoothing distribution represents a fundamental shift from conventional SSM inference approaches.

The significance of the work is high. The ability to learn complex, nonlinear dynamics without restrictive parametric assumptions has important implications for time series analysis, dynamical systems modeling, and control applications. The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness in both synthetic and real-world scenarios, and the sparse GP construction makes the approach computationally feasible for larger problems. The authors effectively show how their approach can capture multimodal smoothing distributions, which is crucial for systems where observations don't uniquely determine the state.

A few minor suggestions for improvement:
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with the most closely related work (e.g., Wang et al. [8]), particularly regarding how the authors' approach differs in terms of assumptions and computational complexity.
2. The discussion of the sparse GP implementation (Section 4.2) could be slightly expanded to clarify how the computational complexity is reduced to O(M²T) rather than O(M²T²).
3. While the experiments are promising, a more extensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods for nonparametric SSM inference would strengthen the significance claim.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference in dynamical systems. The approach is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrated to be effective in experiments. The paper is clearly written and addresses an important problem in a novel way. I recommend acceptance.
