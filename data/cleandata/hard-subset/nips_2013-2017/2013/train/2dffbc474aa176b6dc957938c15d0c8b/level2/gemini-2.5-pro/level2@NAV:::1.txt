This paper presents a novel, fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in state-space models where the transition dynamics are governed by a Gaussian Process (GP). The central contribution is a method that avoids parametric approximations of the GP. Instead, the authors analytically marginalize out the unknown transition function, which induces a non-Markovian dependency structure on the latent state trajectory. To perform inference in this challenging model, they employ a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to sample from the joint smoothing distribution of the states. This work provides a principled alternative to previous methods for GP-SSMs, which often rely on point estimates (MAP/ML) via EM or variational approximations.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The combination of marginalizing the GP dynamics and applying advanced PMCMC techniques for inference is both elegant and novel. It allows for a fully nonparametric treatment that respects the model's expressivity, a significant step forward from methods that require finite-dimensional parameterizations. This approach is highly relevant for learning complex, unknown dynamics in fields like robotics and control.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation for the marginal prior over state trajectories is clear, and the application of PGAS is well-motivated and appropriate for the resulting non-Markovian structure. The inclusion of a sparse GP variant to reduce computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M²T) is a crucial and well-executed practical consideration.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly explains the model, the inference challenges introduced by marginalization, and how the PMCMC framework provides a solution.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments effectively illustrate the method's capabilities, particularly in handling multimodal posteriors, they could be strengthened. The nonlinear system benchmark would benefit from a direct quantitative comparison against other state-of-the-art GP-SSM learning methods (e.g., variational approaches). For the cart-pole system, demonstrating multi-step-ahead prediction or long-term simulation from the learned model would provide a more compelling case for its utility beyond one-step forecasts.
*   **Scalability Discussion:** The paper correctly identifies the computational complexity, but the scalability to long time series remains a practical challenge, even with the sparse variant. A brief discussion on the limitations regarding sequence length (T) and the practicalities of inducing point selection for the sparse model would make the work more complete.

**Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality paper with a strong, novel technical contribution. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to the breadth of the experimental evaluation rather than the core methodology. I recommend acceptance.
