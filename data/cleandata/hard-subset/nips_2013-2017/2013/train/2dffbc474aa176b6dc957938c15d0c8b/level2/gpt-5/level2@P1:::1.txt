Summary
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian method for inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the transition function f and marginalizing f out analytically. This induces a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories, for which the authors develop efficient inference using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). The approach samples from the exact joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T | y0:T, θ) while alternating with updates of hyperparameters θ; given a state trajectory, the GP hyperparameters can be sampled as in standard GP regression, and the predictive distribution over f at test points is a mixture of Gaussians obtained by averaging GP posteriors over MCMC samples. The paper provides implementation details to reduce computational complexity from naive O(T^4) to O(T^3), and further to O(M^2T) using a sparse FIC GP prior. Experiments demonstrate learning on a nonlinear 1D benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole system, along with ablations using sparse GP.

Relation to prior work: Unlike EM/MAP-based GP dynamical models (e.g., Wang et al., Ghahramani & Roweis; Turner et al.) that effectively parametrize the GP or optimize latent states, this work maintains a fully Bayesian, nonparametric treatment by integrating out f and sampling the joint smoothing distribution. It complements prior GPDS filtering/smoothing methods (Deisenroth et al.) by providing a learning mechanism. On the inference side it builds on PMCMC foundations (Andrieu et al.) and leverages PGAS (Lindsten et al.) to tackle the non-Markovian latent model arising from marginalizing f. The sparse GP construction follows FITC/FIC-type approximations (Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen; Snelson & Ghahramani).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean: exact marginalization of the GP transition function and direct targeting of the joint smoothing distribution preserves nonparametric expressivity.
- Solid inference design: using PGAS to address path degeneracy and non-Markovian dependencies is appropriate and state-of-the-art; the invariance guarantees are sound.
- Practicality: clear implementation advice (rank-1 Cholesky updates/downdates; reuse of factorizations), and a sparse GP variant to reduce complexity to O(M^2T).
- Flexibility: can incorporate informative mean functions while letting GP capture residual nonlinearities; can handle multimodal smoothing.
- Empirical demonstrations show sensible posterior smoothing and predictive behavior; sparse GP-SSM variant included.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope: only one synthetic 1D benchmark and a cart-pole example with one-step predictions; no long-horizon rollouts or real-world datasets. Lack of quantitative comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., EM/MAP GPDM, EP-based GPDS learning, SMC^2, PMMH) in terms of predictive error, NLL, and runtime.
- Scaling and resource transparency: while asymptotic complexity is discussed, there is no report of wall-clock times, memory usage, or sensitivity to particle number N, chain length L, or inducing points M. Ancestor sampling weight computation in non-Markovian models can be expensive; practical costs are not quantified.
- Assumptions on the observation model g: the method assumes known g (or low-dimensional θy), and the experiments appear to use simple forms; it is unclear how robust the approach is when g is also flexible or misspecified.
- Inducing input selection: FIC performance depends on inducing locations; selection is mentioned but not evaluated; robustness to M and placement is not studied.
- Predictive mixture over f: while elegant, evaluation only uses RMSE of f(xt, ut) with limited reporting; calibration tests (NLL, PIT) or uncertainty-aware metrics are absent.

Quality
- The methodology is technically sound, with correct use of GP marginalization, PGAS invariance, and hyperparameter sampling analogous to GP regression. The implementation details are credible. Empirical validation is adequate for a proof-of-concept but could be strengthened with broader comparisons and diagnostics.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The derivation of the non-Markovian prior over trajectories and the CPF-AS mechanics are clear, though some index-heavy notation (e.g., Kt−1,0:t−2, K̃) could be streamlined. Providing a brief pseudo-code for CPF-AS with cost annotations would help.

Originality
- Applying PGAS to GP-SSMs with the transition function integrated out is a novel and meaningful combination at the time, distinct from prior EM/MAP or fixed-dynamics GPDS work. The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive for f conditioned on sampled trajectories is a neat byproduct.

Significance
- Offers a principled Bayesian learning procedure that may become a reference for GP-SSMs. Potential impact is high for research on probabilistic dynamical systems and Bayesian nonparametrics, though practical adoption depends on demonstrated scalability and comparative performance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative baselines: compare against EM-based GPDM, EP-based GPDS learning, PMMH/PG without ancestor sampling, and SMC^2, reporting RMSE and NLL for one-step and multi-step predictions, plus smoothing accuracy where ground truth is available.
- Report computational metrics: wall-clock time, memory, and sensitivity to N, L, M, T, and state dimension; show autocorrelation/ESS of PGAS chains.
- Evaluate long-horizon rollouts and uncertainty calibration (NLL, CRPS, calibration plots).
- Provide an ablation for mean-function choices and robustness to misspecified g; if g has unknown parameters, include a learning example with θy.
- For the sparse GP, evaluate inducing point selection strategies (greedy vs random) and sensitivity to M; discuss numerical stability of updates.
- Release implementation details or code pointers to facilitate reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, fully Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs by integrating out f; methodologically strong and well-grounded.
- Appropriate and innovative use of PGAS for non-Markovian smoothing with practical efficiency tricks.
- Clear potential to influence subsequent work on probabilistic modeling of dynamical systems.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is relatively narrow, with limited baselines and scaling evidence.
- Practical costs and robustness (e.g., to observation model misspecification, high-dimensional states) are not convincingly demonstrated.

Overall
A strong methodological contribution with good clarity and promising results. Strengthening the empirical section with broader comparisons and scalability analysis would substantially increase the paper’s impact. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address some of the empirical concerns.
