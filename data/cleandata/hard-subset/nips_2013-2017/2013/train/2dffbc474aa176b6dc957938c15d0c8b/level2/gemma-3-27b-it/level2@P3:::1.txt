This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models, leveraging Gaussian processes (GPs) to model the state transition dynamics. The core contribution is a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method tailored for efficient inference by marginalizing over the transition dynamics function, thereby preserving the full nonparametric expressivity of the model. The work builds on a rich literature of GP-based SSMs, notably addressing a critical component often missing – learning the GP model itself – which is foundational for methods like those proposed by Deisenroth et al. [5, 6]. The paper also appropriately references earlier work using EM algorithms [2, 7] and MAP estimation [8] in GP-SSMs.

The strengths of the paper lie in its principled Bayesian treatment of the problem, the elegant marginalization of the transition function, and the use of a modern PMCMC sampler to tackle the resulting inference challenges. The theoretical justification for the approach is clearly presented, and the authors demonstrate the feasibility of the method through two experimental evaluations: a nonlinear system benchmark and a cart-pole control problem. The ability to learn a useful model even with a deliberately inaccurate parametric prior is a compelling demonstration of the GP’s flexibility. The use of sparse GPs (FIC prior) to address computational complexity is also a significant addition. The inclusion of implementation details and discussion of computational complexity is particularly valuable.

However, the paper could be significantly improved with several revisions. Firstly, while the experiments are illustrative, they lack a comprehensive quantitative comparison to other state-of-the-art methods for SSM learning. The RMSE results presented in Table 1 are informative but don't clearly establish a performance advantage over existing techniques. A more thorough comparison, ideally including both synthetic and real-world datasets, is needed to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. Secondly, the description of the PMCMC sampler, while referencing [3, 4], could benefit from more detailed explanations of the key steps, especially the CPF-AS algorithm. A clearer walkthrough of Algorithm 1 and the reasoning behind the choices made would enhance understanding.  Thirdly, the discussion on hyperparameter learning lacks depth. The authors mention slice sampling but don’t elaborate on the practical considerations, such as step size selection or convergence diagnostics. Fourthly, the notation, while generally clear, can be dense and occasionally difficult to follow.  More liberal use of intermediate variable definitions and a clearer organization of equations would improve readability. Finally, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as its scalability to very high-dimensional state spaces or its sensitivity to the choice of GP kernel.

Arguments for acceptance:

*   Novel approach to GP-SSM learning through marginalization and PMCMC.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and clear mathematical derivations.
*   Demonstrated feasibility through experiments, showcasing the method's flexibility.
*   Addresses a key challenge in GP-SSM literature - learning the transition dynamics.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   Limited quantitative comparison to existing methods.
*   Insufficient detail in the description of the PMCMC sampler.
*   Lack of in-depth discussion of hyperparameter learning.
*   Limited discussion of the method’s limitations and scalability.



Overall, this is a promising contribution to the field of GP-SSM learning. While the paper has some weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, its theoretical soundness, and the potential for significant impact warrant consideration. Addressing the suggested revisions, particularly the more thorough experimental evaluation and clearer explanation of the PMCMC algorithm, would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its likelihood of acceptance.  The essential revisions would be the more extensive benchmarking and clarification of the PMCMC algorithm. The remaining suggestions would be considered helpful refinements.
