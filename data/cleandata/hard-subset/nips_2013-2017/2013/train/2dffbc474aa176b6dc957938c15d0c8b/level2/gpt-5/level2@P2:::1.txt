Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the transition dynamics f and marginalizing f out. This yields a non-Markovian prior over the state trajectory x0:T that is expressed as a product of conditionals obtained via GP one-step predictions. The main algorithmic tool is Particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to sample from the exact smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) while integrating out f. Given state trajectory samples, the posterior over f is then available in closed form (standard GP regression), allowing computation of predictive distributions as mixtures of Gaussians. The paper further introduces a sparse GP-SSM variant using the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and outlines efficient implementations based on Cholesky rank-one updates and careful reuse of computations.

This work connects to several strands: GP-based dynamical models (Wang et al., GPDM), GP filtering/smoothing with learned models (Deisenroth et al.), and identification of nonlinear SSMs via EM or MAP (Ghahramani & Roweis; Turner et al.). The key novelty is collapsing the GP transition function in a fully Bayesian setting and using PGAS to address the resulting non-Markovian smoothing problem, thereby avoiding parametric approximations to f.

Quality:
The methodology is sound and technically careful. Collapsing f is appropriate to reduce posterior dependencies and avoid high-dimensional sampling; the resulting non-Markovian structure is correctly handled by PGAS with ancestor sampling. The derivation of the prior over trajectories and the use of GP predictive formulas appear correct. The implementation details (rank-one update/downdate of Cholesky factors per particle; efficient evaluation of ancestor weights using the collapsed prior) are valuable and nontrivial. The FIC-based complexity reduction and discussion of inducing inputs are consistent with the GP literature.

Empirical evaluation demonstrates: (i) accurate smoothing under multimodal posteriors in a 1D nonlinear benchmark where the observation model is sign-ambiguous; (ii) competitive prediction of transition dynamics compared to parametric baselines; (iii) a 4D cart-pole example highlighting uncertainty-aware predictions and data-dependent confidence. However, the experiments are somewhat limited in scope and ablations (e.g., sensitivity to number of particles, chain length, kernel/hyperprior choices, and inducing set size/placement) are not reported. Comparisons to strong GPDM/EM/MAP baselines are sparse; runtime and scaling with T, N, and state dimension are not quantified.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The notation for the collapsed trajectory prior is heavy but precise; equations (3)–(4) and the discussion of ancestor sampling weights are helpful. Implementation insights are appreciated. A few places could be clearer: the proposal notation p(xt | θ, x0:t−1, y0:t) suggests a likelihood-informed proposal, but the described default uses the one-step prior predictive; clarifying this would avoid confusion. More explicit pseudocode or complexity accounting for CPF-AS with the collapsed prior (including N and state dimension dependence) would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
High. Collapsing the GP transition function in SSMs and sampling the exact smoothing distribution with PGAS is, to my knowledge, new in the NIPS literature circa 2013. The combination of exact marginalization (preserving nonparametric expressivity) with PMCMC for non-Markovian latent models is a notable advance over EM/MAP and fixed-model GP filtering/smoothing.

Significance:
High. The approach fills an important gap: learning GP-SSMs in a fully Bayesian way without resorting to parametric approximations or variational surrogates, and enabling smoothing without a fixed transition model. It should be useful both for system identification and as a component in GP-based control and RL pipelines. The sparse variant and implementation guidance increase practical impact.

Strengths:
- Fully Bayesian, nonparametric treatment of SSMs by marginalizing f; principled handling of non-Markovian smoothing with PGAS.
- Solid algorithmic design with invariance guarantees and careful implementation details.
- Sparse GP-SSM (FIC) variant reducing complexity and maintaining coherence as a different prior rather than an ad-hoc approximation.
- Clear pathway from smoothing samples to transition function posterior and predictive distributions (mixture-of-Gaussians).
- Demonstrated ability to represent multimodal smoothing distributions and uncertainty-aware predictions.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Experimental scope is narrow. Please:
  - Include comparisons to GPDM/EM (Wang et al.) and MAP approaches (Turner et al.), and, if feasible, to particle MCMC on parametric nonlinear SSMs to isolate the benefit of collapsing f.
  - Report wall-clock times and scaling with T, state dimension, number of particles N, and chain length; provide autocorrelation/effective sample size to substantiate “very good mixing.”
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for N, number of iterations, kernel choice, and hyperpriors.
  - For FIC, study the effect of M and inducing input selection (e.g., compare greedy vs random; show accuracy vs speed trade-offs).
- Clarify the proposal distribution in the SMC step. If the default is the prior predictive p(xt | θ, x0:t−1), state so explicitly; consider likelihood-informed proposals (e.g., auxiliary/optimal proposals) and assess their effect on variance and mixing.
- Discuss limitations: O(T^3) complexity and memory per particle for the full GP; practical limits on T and nx; whether Q must be diagonal in practice for efficient implementation (Section 4.2 hints at block diagonalization).
- Provide more details on computing p(x̃t:T | xi0:t−1) efficiently within ancestor sampling, possibly with a brief derivation or appendix.
- Reproducibility: include pseudocode for CPF-AS in the collapsed model and specify all priors and kernel hyperparameters; releasing code would strengthen impact.

Reasons to accept:
- Novel and principled inference strategy that advances GP-SSMs beyond EM/MAP and fixed-model smoothing.
- Solid theoretical grounding and careful algorithmic engineering.
- Practical sparse extension and insights likely to influence subsequent work.

Reasons to reject:
- Limited empirical comparison and analysis; lack of runtime/mixing diagnostics.
- Scalability to long sequences and higher-dimensional states not convincingly demonstrated.

Overall recommendation:
Leaning accept. The methodological contribution is substantial and timely for NIPS, and the paper is technically sound and clearly presented. Strengthening the experimental section as suggested would make the case unequivocal.
