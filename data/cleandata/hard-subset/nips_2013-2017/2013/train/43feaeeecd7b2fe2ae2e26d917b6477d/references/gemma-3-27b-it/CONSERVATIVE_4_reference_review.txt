This paper tackles the challenging problem of modeling multivariate count data, a common scenario in modern data analysis. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches like log-linear models and extensions of the Poisson distribution (PGM) due to either parameter explosion or restrictions on dependencies (specifically, the inability to model positive correlations in the standard PGM). They critique the Winsorization approach proposed by Kaiser & Cressie, correctly demonstrating its inconsistency. The core contribution is the development of three new graphical model extensions – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – each addressing the drawbacks of prior work in different ways.

The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, and the mathematical derivations appear sound. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the existing literature in graphical models and count data analysis. However, despite the theoretical contributions, I have significant reservations regarding the empirical validation and overall significance of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods for modeling multivariate count data.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivations and proofs (specifically Theorem 1 and the subsequent developments) seem mathematically sound. The analysis of the normalizability conditions is particularly insightful.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuinely new approaches to modeling count data within a graphical model framework.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper does a good job of connecting its contributions to previous work in graphical models, spatial statistics, and count data analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments are relatively limited in scope. Comparing against several established baselines (e.g., non-paranormal, graphical lasso adapted for count data) is good, but the data sets are small and the results aren't overwhelmingly convincing. The simulation study only considers a limited set of network structures. Furthermore, the comparison relies on ROC curves without reporting specific performance metrics (e.g., precision, recall, F1-score).
*   **Computational Considerations:** The paper lacks discussion about the computational complexity of learning these models, particularly the QPGM and SPGM. The SPGM's reliance on approximating the partition function with a Poisson distribution feels like a significant simplification that needs more justification.
*   **Practical Applicability:** While the breast cancer miRNA case study is interesting, it doesn't provide strong evidence of the practical advantages of the proposed models over existing approaches. The claims about identifying biologically relevant miRNAs are not sufficiently substantiated.
*   **Lack of Discussion on Model Selection:** The paper mentions using penalized likelihood for parameter estimation but doesn't delve into the specifics of model selection criteria (e.g., BIC, AIC) or cross-validation strategies. The choice of regularization parameter appears arbitrary.
*   **Overly Cautious Approach:** The authors frequently highlight the limitations of their own methods, which, while commendable, sometimes overshadows the potential benefits.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in statistical modeling.
*   The analysis of the normalizability conditions provides valuable insights into the limitations of existing models and the requirements for developing new ones.
*   The proposed models offer the potential to capture more complex dependencies in count data than existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is insufficient to demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed models.
*   The computational complexity and scalability of the models are not adequately addressed.
*   The lack of a thorough discussion of model selection and hyperparameter tuning weakens the empirical findings.
*   The paper’s significance is overstated given the limited empirical evidence.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid theoretical contribution, but its empirical validation is lacking. While the ideas are interesting and potentially valuable, I am not convinced that they represent a significant advance over existing methods in practice. The paper feels incomplete; it lays the groundwork for future research but doesn't fully deliver on its promise. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation and address the computational concerns before resubmission. The authors should focus on larger-scale simulations and real-world datasets, provide a more thorough comparison to established baselines, and discuss the computational feasibility of their proposed models. They also need to address the practical aspects of model selection and hyperparameter tuning.
