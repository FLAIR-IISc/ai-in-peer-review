## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the limitations of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) for modeling multivariate count data. The original PGM, while a natural extension of the univariate Poisson distribution, suffers from a restriction to only modeling negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors systematically explore modifications to overcome this limitation. They first demonstrate the failure of a truncation approach proposed in prior work (Kaiser & Cressie, 2020). They then introduce a Truncated PGM (TPGM) using a novel truncation strategy, followed by a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) based on modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution. The paper concludes with illustrative results on simulated data and a breast cancer miRNA expression dataset.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the foundational work of Besag (1974) and the extensions by Yang et al. (2013, 2015) establishing the PGM framework. It acknowledges and critically analyzes attempts to address the negative dependency issue, particularly the Winsorization approach of Kaiser & Cressie (2020). It situates itself within the broader context of multivariate count data modeling, referencing log-linear models, copula transforms, and hierarchical models.  Importantly, it connects to the Gaussian graphical model literature (Meinshausen & Bühlmann, 2006; Yuan & Lin, 2007; Friedman et al., 2008) and draws parallels in terms of graphical model construction and sparsistency properties.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing PGMs and provides a strong motivation for exploring alternative modeling approaches.
* **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:**  The authors provide mathematical proofs (Theorems 1-4) to justify their proposed models and establish their normalizability. The analysis of the base measure requirements for positive dependencies is particularly insightful.
* **Novel Model Development:** The introduction of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represents a significant contribution to the field.  Each model builds on the previous one, addressing limitations and offering different trade-offs.
* **Practical Demonstration:** The inclusion of numerical experiments on both simulated data and a real-world dataset (breast cancer miRNA expression) provides empirical evidence supporting the utility of the proposed models.  The comparison with existing methods (Gaussian GGM, Nonparanormal) is valuable.
* **Connections to Gaussian Graphical Models:** The analogy drawn to Gaussian Graphical Models, particularly regarding the variational upper bound in the QPGM section, is helpful for understanding the behavior of the proposed models and potentially leveraging existing inference algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Accessibility:** While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, it is also dense and potentially inaccessible to readers without a strong background in graphical models and statistical theory.  More intuitive explanations of the key concepts would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation, while a good starting point, is somewhat limited. The simulations only explore two network structures. A more comprehensive evaluation with different network topologies and varying levels of noise would strengthen the conclusions. The miRNA dataset analysis focuses on network structure but lacks detailed biological validation of the findings.
* **Computational Challenges:** The paper briefly mentions using proximal gradient ascent for parameter estimation. However, it doesn’t thoroughly discuss the computational challenges associated with these models, particularly the log-partition function calculation and scalability to high-dimensional datasets.
* **SPGM Justification:** The motivation for the sublinear statistic feels less compelling than the quadratic base measure. While mathematically sound, the intuition behind manipulating the sufficient statistic is not as clear.
* **Truncation Parameter Selection:** The paper doesn’t delve into methods for selecting the truncation parameter *R* in the TPGM. This parameter selection is likely crucial for performance and deserves further discussion.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation in a relatively unexplored area – multivariate count data modeling.
* The proposed models are novel, theoretically well-founded, and offer a promising path forward.
* The empirical results, while limited, provide initial evidence of the utility of the proposed methods.
* The connection to established concepts in Gaussian graphical models facilitates adoption and extension of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical complexity may limit the accessibility of the paper.
* The empirical evaluation is not comprehensive enough to fully demonstrate the benefits of the proposed models.
* Computational challenges and parameter selection issues are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models. The authors identify a critical limitation of existing PGMs and propose a series of innovative solutions. While the theoretical complexity and limited empirical evaluation are concerns, the potential impact of this work is significant.  I believe the paper is **acceptably strong** for NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing some of the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more accessible explanations and expanding the empirical evaluation.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.



**Rating:** Accept with Minor Revisions
