- Identifies a key limitation: existing Poisson graphical models can only represent negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints.  
- Proves the Winsorized Poisson approach is inconsistent and cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1).  
- Proposes three new models—Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that allow both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Establishes a fundamental theoretical result (Theorem 3) that a quadratic base measure is necessary for normalizability with linear sufficient statistics.  
- SPGM is highlighted as the most flexible model, offering heavier tails than QPGM.  
- Empirical results show advantages over Gaussian-based methods on count data.  
- Real-data case study (breast cancer microRNA) yields biologically meaningful findings (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b).  
- Authors transparently acknowledge model limitations (TPGM’s bounded domain; QPGM’s Gaussian-like tail).  
- Major computational concern: SPGM’s log-partition involves infinite summation and practical complexity is not addressed.  
- Lacks practical guidance on model selection, specifically choosing truncation/scale parameters (R, R0).  
- Missing theoretical learning guarantees: sparsistency and network recovery analysis are deferred to future work.  
- Comparison to related recent work is insufficient, particularly non-parametric extensions, hierarchical models, and copula-based approaches.  
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and well-designed experiments; claims are well-supported.  
- Exceptionally clear writing and organization; mathematical notation and critique of prior work are well presented.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept, with suggested future work on computational methods, parameter selection, and contextualization with related models.
