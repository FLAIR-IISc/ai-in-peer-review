

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper proposes three novel graphical model extensions for count data: Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and Sub-Linear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM). The authors address the limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that only allow negative edge weights, which restricts their applicability to count data with positive dependencies. The main technical contribution is showing how modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics can yield models that permit both positive and negative dependencies while remaining normalizable.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a genuine limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (only negative dependencies) and proposes a thoughtful approach to overcoming it. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) provide clear characterizations of the constraints needed for normalizability. The numerical experiments demonstrate that the proposed models outperform Gaussian-based methods on count data, which is a valuable practical contribution. The case study on breast cancer miRNA data shows the potential real-world utility of the proposed models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Errors and Ambiguities**: 
   - The paper claims the sum of independent Poisson variables is Poisson, but this is only true when variables have the same rate parameter. This critical detail is omitted, potentially misleading readers.
   - In Section 3.2, the sub-linear sufficient statistic's functional form is inconsistent with its description. The paper states "after R0 its slope decreases linearly" but the formula provided is quadratic, not linear. This is a significant technical error.
   - Theorem 3 states "C(Z) = Ω(Z^2)" but doesn't specify whether this refers to the node-conditional or joint distribution, creating critical ambiguity.

2. **Misinterpretation of Prior Work**:
   - The paper incorrectly cites [20] as introducing "a Winsorized Poisson" but [20] is about "Modeling Poisson variables with positive spatial dependence," not truncation. This misattribution undermines the paper's foundation.
   - The claim that "Kaiser and Cressie [20] first introduced an approach to truncate the Poisson distribution" is factually incorrect based on the cited reference.

3. **Inconsistencies and Ambiguities**:
   - The paper uses inconsistent terminology: "Poisson MRF" (Section 1), "PGM" (Section 2), and "Poisson graphical model" (Section 2.1) without clear definitions.
   - The paper states "the domain X of each Xs is {0, 1, 2, ...}" but in Section 2.1.2 refers to "truncated positive integer set, {0, 1, ..., R}" - this is inconsistent as {0, 1, ..., R} is not positive integers.
   - The paper claims "the normalization constant involves a summation over finitely many terms" for TPGM but doesn't specify how the sum is computed or its computational complexity.

4. **Theoretical and Experimental Shortcomings**:
   - The paper states "no restrictions are imposed on the parameters for the normalizability" of TPGM but this is misleading. Parameters are still constrained by the need to maintain valid probabilities over the truncated domain.
   - The numerical experiments claim to use Gibbs sampling for TPGM data generation but don't explain why Gibbs sampling is appropriate for a bounded domain model.
   - The case study uses R=11 as "the maximum count" without justification for this specific value, making the results appear arbitrary.

5. **Formatting and Presentation**:
   - The references are inconsistently formatted (some with journal names, some with conference names, some with no venue).
   - The paper uses "R > 3" in Theorem 1 without explaining why this specific threshold is chosen, making it seem arbitrary.
   - Equation (6) contains confusing notation: "∑ s∈V θssX^2 s" - this appears to refer to a node-specific quadratic term but is not clearly defined.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses a genuine limitation of existing Poisson graphical models and proposes theoretically interesting extensions, the numerous technical errors, inconsistencies, and misinterpretations of prior work significantly undermine its credibility. The fundamental error in the sub-linear sufficient statistic definition (Section 3.2) is particularly concerning, as it suggests a lack of careful mathematical verification. The paper's claims about the relationship between base measures and normalizability are not sufficiently supported by the presented theoretical analysis.

The numerical experiments are promising but lack sufficient detail to assess their validity. The case study's arbitrary choice of R=11 and lack of explanation for this parameter selection further weaken the practical contribution.

Given the paper's technical shortcomings and the fact that it's not clear how the proposed models advance the state-of-the-art beyond what could be achieved with more careful application of existing exponential family graphical model theory, I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting direction but requires substantial technical correction before it can be considered a valid scientific contribution to NIPS.
