- Main contribution: Identifies and overcomes the PGM’s limitation of only allowing non-positive pairwise parameters, enabling both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Contribution 1: Shows Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals cannot form a consistent MRF (Theorem 1), and proposes a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) via exponential-family truncation.  
- Contribution 2: Proves necessity that the base measure must grow at least quadratically for linear sufficient-statistic Poisson variants to allow both sign edges while ensuring normalizability (Theorem 3).  
- Contribution 3: Proposes Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with saturated sufficient statistics, both permitting positive and negative edges with established normalizability conditions.  
- Practical methods: Provides neighborhood-selection estimation with l1 penalties and variational upper bounds to simplify nodewise regressions.  
- Experiments: Simulated (hub, scale-free) and miRNA sequencing data show TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-based baselines in edge recovery and produce biologically plausible hubs.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Besag-style auto-models and Yang et al., corrects spatial-statistics misclaims about Winsorized Poisson, and contrasts with multivariate Poisson sum constructions and Gaussian copulas.  
- Strength: Clear diagnosis of PGM negativity constraint and rigorous impossibility result for naive truncation.  
- Strength: Constructive, novel alternative models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with accompanying theoretical normalizability results.  
- Strength: Theorem 3 and other theoretical results give fundamental necessary conditions that guide model design.  
- Strength: Provides practical estimation strategy and empirical evidence that Gaussian methods underperform on counts.  
- Weakness: Central theorems (1 and 3) need full proofs and clearer assumptions; supplement must be carefully vetted.  
- Weakness: Estimation for QPGM/SPGM relies on heuristic variational bounds without analysis of approximation error, bias, or impact on sparsistency.  
- Weakness: Hyperparameter selection (R, R0) lacks guidance; risk of data leakage and degeneracy if set to observed max; robustness analysis missing.  
- Weakness: Experiments omit simulations from SPGM/QPGM, lack runtime/convergence diagnostics, and don't report held-out likelihood or calibration on real data.  
- Weakness: Comparators miss negative-binomial MRFs and count copula models; discussion on these alternatives is brief.  
- Quality: Technically ambitious and largely sound but with partly heuristic estimation and insufficient empirical ablations.  
- Clarity: Well written overall but needs precise statements of key definitions and more intuition/algorithmic details (convergence, proximal steps).  
- Originality: High — novel impossibility result and new valid MRF constructions addressing a real modeling gap.  
- Significance: High — valid multivariate count MRFs that permit both dependency signs are broadly useful if estimation and hyperparameter guidance are strengthened.  
- Suggestions: Include full proofs/sketches for Theorems 1 and 3, analyze convexity/Lipschitz and proximal convergence, quantify variational approximation error, and add simulations for SPGM/QPGM plus robustness/timing studies.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — strong conceptual/theoretical work with promising empirical results but needs stronger estimation guarantees and expanded validation.
