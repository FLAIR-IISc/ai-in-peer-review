## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of modeling multivariate count data, a problem increasingly relevant in diverse fields. The authors identify the limitations of existing approaches like log-linear models, multivariate Poisson distributions, and the original Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) proposed by Besag and extended by Yang et al., which suffers from requiring non-positive edge weights restricting its ability to model realistic dependencies.  The paper proposes three novel extensions: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each with varying trade-offs in terms of model complexity and expressiveness.  The authors demonstrate the potential of these models through simulations and a case study applying them to miRNA expression data.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Modeling multivariate count data is a crucial and growing area, and the paper tackles a significant limitation of existing tools.
* **Clear Motivation & Thorough Literature Review:** The introduction provides a well-structured overview of the problem and a comprehensive survey of related work, clearly identifying the gaps this paper aims to fill.
* **Novel Contributions:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuinely novel approaches to extending PGMs for count data, with sound theoretical justification.  The analysis demonstrating the necessity of quadratic or sub-linear base measures is particularly insightful.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes important theoretical results (Theorems 1-4, Propositions 1-3) which underly the model development and provide justification for the proposed methods.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and miRNA expression analysis provide initial evidence of the utility of the proposed models, demonstrating their ability to learn network structures.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper thoughtfully relates its contributions to existing frameworks (e.g., Gaussian graphical models, variational bounds) and builds upon previous research (Besag, Yang et al.).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Practicality:** While theoretically appealing, the SPGM, in particular, appears quite complex.  The computational challenges associated with inference and parameter estimation are not fully addressed beyond mentioning proximal gradient ascent.  A more detailed discussion of computational scalability and practical implementation is needed.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation studies are a good start, but could be more extensive. Specifically, it would be beneficial to assess the models' performance on a wider range of network structures and with varying degrees of sparsity.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The selection of parameters (e.g., R, R0, c) in the TPGM, SPGM and QPGM is not discussed in detail.  Sensitivity analysis of these parameters and guidelines for their selection would be valuable.
* **Comparison with Alternatives:** While the paper compares with some relevant methods, it would be helpful to include a comparison with other recent advances in modeling count data, such as those based on negative binomial distributions or other copula-based approaches.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:** The paper assumes count data. While this is the stated goal, a brief discussion of the implications of deviations from this assumption (e.g., continuous approximations) would strengthen the analysis.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete, well-reasoned contribution. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results, while limited, are encouraging.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, some sections, particularly those describing the SPGM, could benefit from further clarification and simplification.
* **Originality:** The proposed models are novel and offer a significant advancement over existing methods for modeling multivariate count data. The theoretical insights regarding base measures are particularly original.
* **Significance:**  The results have the potential to be highly significant. The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies in count data has broad applicability across various domains.  The miRNA case study illustrates the practical relevance of the proposed methods.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem.
* Presents novel and theoretically grounded solutions.
* Demonstrates potential through simulations and a real-world case study.
* Significant contribution to the field of graphical models.

**Con:**

* Computational complexity and scalability are not fully addressed.
* Empirical evaluation could be more extensive and rigorous.
* Parameter tuning and sensitivity analysis require further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong paper** with significant potential. While some aspects of the computational complexity and empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and potential impact of the work are compelling.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest the authors expand the simulation studies, provide more detail on parameter selection and computational aspects, and consider including a broader comparison with alternative methods.  This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
