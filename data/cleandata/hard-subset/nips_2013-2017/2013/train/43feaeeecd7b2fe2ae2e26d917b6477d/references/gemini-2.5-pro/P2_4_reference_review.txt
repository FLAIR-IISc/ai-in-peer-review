### Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), namely their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints that require non-positive edge weights (θ_st ≤ 0). The authors begin by providing a clear overview of this problem and then systematically debunk a previously proposed solution based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution, proving in Theorem 1 that such a construction can never lead to a valid joint distribution.

The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of three novel classes of graphical models for count data that overcome this limitation.
1.  **Truncated PGM (TPGM):** A new truncation scheme is proposed that, unlike the Winsorized version, maintains the exponential family form, thus allowing for a valid joint MRF. This model permits both positive and negative dependencies but requires a pre-specified truncation level R, which introduces trade-offs between model flexibility and adherence to Poisson-like behavior.
2.  **Quadratic PGM (QPGM):** This model modifies the base measure of the Poisson distribution from log(X!) to a quadratic term (X^2). This ensures normalizability for a broader class of parameters, including positive dependencies, provided the parameter matrix is negative-definite. The resulting model has Gaussian-esque thin tails.
3.  **Sublinear PGM (SPGM):** To allow for heavier tails than the QPGM, this model retains the original log(X!) base measure but modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear. This clever construction ensures normalizability for all real-valued pairwise parameters.

The paper concludes with simulation studies and a real-world application to microRNA expression data, demonstrating the superior performance of the proposed models over Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM for network recovery.

**Evaluation**

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical contributions are significant. Theorem 1, which proves the invalidity of the Winsorized PGM approach, is a valuable correction to the literature. The constructions of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are well-motivated and their validity is supported by formal theorems (Theorems 2 and 4) and propositions (Proposition 2). The authors are careful to discuss the strengths and weaknesses of each proposed model (e.g., the bounded domain of TPGM, the thin tails of QPGM), which demonstrates a nuanced understanding of their contributions. The experimental section, while brief, provides solid empirical support for the utility of the proposed models, particularly in the context of biological network inference. The use of a neighborhood selection framework for learning is appropriate and standard for this class of models.

A minor weakness is that the theoretical analysis of the learning procedure (e.g., sparsistency guarantees) is mentioned as future work. While understandable given space constraints, including even a high-level argument for why existing proofs might extend to these models would have further strengthened the paper.

**Clarity: Excellent**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is established clearly in the introduction, making the problem accessible to a broad audience in machine learning. The authors guide the reader through a logical progression: identifying the core problem with PGMs, showing why a prior solution fails, and then systematically introducing their three novel solutions. Each new model is presented with clear mathematical formulation and intuition. The distinction between modifying the domain (TPGM), the base measure (QPGM), and the sufficient statistic (SPGM) is a particularly effective way to frame the contributions. The experimental results are presented clearly with ROC curves and network visualizations that effectively communicate the key findings.

**Originality: High**
The work is highly original. While graphical models for count data are not new, existing methods suffer from severe limitations that have hindered their practical application. This paper provides the first principled and viable solutions for building tractable MRFs for count data that can capture rich, mixed-sign dependency structures. The refutation of the Kaiser and Cressie [20] approach is an original and important contribution in its own right. The designs of the QPGM and especially the SPGM are novel and clever modifications to the exponential family framework, demonstrating a deep understanding of the underlying statistical theory. The work is well-situated within the literature, clearly building upon the framework of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19] while making fundamental advances.

**Significance: High**
The results presented in this paper are very important and likely to have a significant impact. Count data is ubiquitous in modern data science (e.g., genomics, neuroscience, social network analysis), yet the toolkit for modeling conditional dependencies in such data is underdeveloped compared to continuous or binary data. This paper fills a critical gap. The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are not merely theoretical curiosities; they are presented with a practical learning framework and demonstrated on real-world data, suggesting they can be readily adopted by practitioners. Researchers working on graphical models and applications in biology, social sciences, and other fields are likely to use and build upon these ideas. The work significantly advances the state of the art in probabilistic modeling for non-Gaussian data.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** Solves the long-standing issue of PGMs being restricted to negative dependencies, which has severely limited their applicability.
*   **Novel and Sound Contributions:** Introduces three well-motivated, theoretically-grounded, and novel graphical models for count data.
*   **Correction of Prior Work:** Formally proves that a previously proposed method (Winsorized PGM) is invalid, which is a valuable service to the research community.
*   **High Potential Impact:** Provides a much-needed tool for a common data type, with clear applications in several high-impact domains like genomics.
*   **Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The paper is written and organized to a very high standard, making complex ideas easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Learning:** The paper focuses on model specification and leaves the formal consistency analysis of the learning algorithm for future work.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The TPGM and SPGM models introduce hyperparameters (R, R0) that may require careful tuning in practice, a point that could be discussed in more detail.

**Conclusion**
This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of probabilistic graphical models. It elegantly solves a critical problem, introduces several novel and practical models, and demonstrates their effectiveness empirically. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
