**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a critical and well-known limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs): their inability to model positive conditional dependencies. Standard PGMs, a natural extension of the Poisson distribution for multivariate count data, are only normalizable when the pairwise interaction parameters are non-positive, severely restricting their practical applicability. The authors systematically tackle this problem by proposing three novel classes of graphical models for count data that permit rich dependence structures.

First, the paper examines truncation-based approaches. It makes a significant negative finding by proving that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional distribution (Kaiser and Cressie, 1997) cannot form a consistent joint distribution, thereby correcting a flaw in the existing literature. The authors then propose their own valid truncation method, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), which properly belongs to the exponential family and allows for both positive and negative dependencies, albeit with limitations related to the choice of the truncation level.

Recognizing the drawbacks of truncation, the authors then propose two more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution over an infinite domain. They first show that to allow positive dependencies with linear sufficient statistics (`XsXt`), the base measure must grow at least quadratically. This insight leads to the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which has a Gaussian-like quadratic penalty term and is normalizable under conditions similar to a Gaussian graphical model (e.g., a negative-definite parameter matrix). Finally, to address the thin tails of the QPGM, they propose the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which reverts to the original `log(X!)` base measure but employs a sublinear sufficient statistic that flattens for large count values. This construction cleverly ensures normalizability for any real-valued interaction parameters. The paper validates the practical utility of the TPGM and SPGM on simulated data and a real-world application of learning a breast cancer microRNA expression network from sequencing count data.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions are sound, well-motivated, and elegant.
*   **Strengths:**
    1.  The disproof of the Winsorized PGM's validity (Theorem 1) is a strong and important contribution that clarifies and corrects the literature.
    2.  The derivation of the three new models is principled and systematic. The connection made between the growth rate of the base measure and the sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) is particularly insightful and provides a clear justification for the QPGM and SPGM.
    3.  The experimental evaluation is solid. The proposed models convincingly outperform standard methods like Gaussian graphical models on count data. The case study on microRNA data demonstrates the practical significance of the work by identifying biologically relevant interactions missed by other methods.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  The theoretical analysis for learning the model structure (sparsistency) is deferred to future work. The paper states that extensions of existing analysis are expected to hold, but a more formal discussion of the required conditions or a brief proof sketch would have made the contribution more complete.
    2.  The QPGM is a central theoretical contribution but is notably absent from the experimental section. The paper speculates it would behave like a Gaussian model, but providing empirical evidence would have been valuable to fully characterize the proposed family of models.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:**
    1.  The motivation is established clearly in the introduction, framing the problem and the paper's contributions effectively.
    2.  Each section builds logically on the previous one, from identifying the core problem with PGMs, to debunking a prior approach, to introducing the new models as sequential solutions to the identified limitations (truncation -> quadratic base measure -> sublinear statistic). This narrative structure makes the paper very easy to follow.
    3.  The trade-offs between the proposed models (TPGM's bounded domain, QPGM's thin tails, SPGM's hyperparameters) are discussed honestly.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  A minor point on clarity would be the selection of hyperparameters for TPGM (`R`) and SPGM (`R0`, `R`). The paper uses a reasonable heuristic (`R` = max count), but a brief discussion on more data-driven selection methods like cross-validation would be helpful for practitioners.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on the framework of Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2012), it provides novel solutions to a long-standing open problem.
*   **Strengths:**
    1.  The problem of modeling positive dependencies in PGMs is not new, but the proposed solutions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are entirely novel and well-conceived.
    2.  The idea of modifying the base measure or the sufficient statistic to ensure normalizability is a creative and powerful approach that moves beyond simple truncation.
    3.  The refutation of the Winsorized PGM approach is an original and important finding.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant.
*   **Strengths:**
    1.  Multivariate count data is ubiquitous in machine learning, particularly in fields like genomics, neuroscience, and social sciences. This work provides a flexible and powerful toolkit for modeling such data, which was previously lacking.
    2.  The models are likely to be widely adopted by both researchers and practitioners. The connection to the generalized linear model framework and the use of standard neighborhood selection for learning makes them relatively easy to implement and apply.
    3.  The work opens up several avenues for future research, including more detailed theoretical analysis of estimation consistency, development of more scalable inference algorithms, and extensions to more complex dependency structures.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a fundamental, important, and long-standing limitation in an established class of models (PGMs).
2.  Provides not one, but three principled and novel model classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with clear theoretical justification and well-explained trade-offs.
3.  Includes a significant negative result that corrects an error in prior literature, which is a valuable scientific contribution in itself.
4.  The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and easy to follow.
5.  Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the practical utility and superiority of the proposed methods for modeling count data.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The theoretical guarantees for the learning procedure (neighborhood selection) are asserted but not formally proven, with the authors deferring this to future work.
2.  One of the three proposed models, the QPGM, is not empirically evaluated, leaving a gap in the comparison.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a substantial contribution to the literature on graphical models. It cleanly solves a well-known problem with elegant theory, proposes a suite of practical new models, and demonstrates their effectiveness empirically. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength and significance of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
