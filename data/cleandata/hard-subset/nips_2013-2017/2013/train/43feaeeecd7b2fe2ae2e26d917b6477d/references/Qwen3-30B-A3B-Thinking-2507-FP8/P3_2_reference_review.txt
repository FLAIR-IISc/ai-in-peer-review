

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in graphical modeling: the inability of existing methods to model multivariate count data with both positive and negative dependencies. The authors identify that the Poisson graphical model (PGM) proposed by Yang et al. (2013) only permits negative edge weights due to normalizability constraints. They systematically examine previous attempts to address this, including the Winsorized Poisson approach (Kaiser & Cressie, 2005), which they rigorously prove cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1). The authors then propose three novel approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). Each addresses the normalizability constraint through distinct modifications to the Poisson distribution's base measure or sufficient statistics, enabling both positive and negative conditional dependencies. The paper demonstrates these models' efficacy on synthetic data and a breast cancer microRNA expression network.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper makes strong theoretical contributions. The proof that Winsorized Poisson cannot yield a valid graphical model (Theorem 1) corrects a significant error in prior work. The characterization of normalizability conditions (Theorems 3-4) provides crucial insights into the relationship between base measures and sufficient statistics for graphical models over count data.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The authors effectively establish the need for a proper graphical model for count data with flexible dependency structures, contrasting with existing approaches that either have exponential parameter growth (log-linear models), rely on restrictive assumptions (sums of independent Poissons), or cannot capture positive dependencies (PGM).

3. **Practical Validation**: The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed models over Gaussian-based methods for count data. The breast cancer microRNA network case study provides compelling evidence of the models' practical utility, identifying biologically relevant hubs (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b) missed by both PGM and Gaussian graphical models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Considerations**: While the paper focuses on model specification, it lacks discussion of computational challenges. The SPGM involves infinite sums in its log-partition function (Proposition 3), requiring approximation. The authors note this but don't address how to efficiently compute these quantities in practice, which is critical for high-dimensional applications.

2. **Limited Experimental Comparison**: The simulation studies compare TPGM and SPGM against Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal, and non-paranormal SKEPTIC. However, they omit comparison with the only other count-data graphical model approach that permits positive dependencies: the "multivariate Poisson" via sums of independent Poissons (Bhattacharya & Dunson, 2012). This omission weakens the comparative assessment.

3. **Theoretical Nuance**: The QPGM's Gaussian-esque tail behavior (Section 3.1) is noted but not fully explored. The authors state that the QPGM has "Gaussian-esque thin tail," but don't analyze how this affects modeling of heavy-tailed count data (common in many applications like genomics), where SPGM's heavier tail might be more appropriate.

## Critical Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results. The proofs are well-constructed and address a fundamental limitation in existing work. The experimental validation, while limited, is relevant and demonstrates practical value. However, the lack of computational discussion slightly weakens the completeness of the contribution.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation is logical. Some technical details (e.g., the precise relationship between base measures and sufficient statistics) could be clarified for broader accessibility.

**Originality**: This work makes significant original contributions. The identification of the Winsorized Poisson approach's invalidity and the three novel model classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent a substantial advancement beyond the state of the art. The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous approaches, including Yang et al. (2013) and Kaiser & Cressie (2005).

**Significance**: The problem addressed is highly significant for fields dealing with count data (genomics, spatial statistics, etc.). The proposed models fill a critical gap, enabling more realistic modeling of complex count data dependencies. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, particularly in identifying biologically meaningful network structures.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a strong contribution to graphical modeling for count data. The theoretical insights are novel and well-justified, and the empirical validation demonstrates practical utility. While the computational aspects and comparative experiments could be strengthened, these are minor concerns given the paper's primary focus on model specification.

The work is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental problem in statistical modeling with important applications. It advances the state of the art by providing the first graphical models for count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies without restrictive assumptions.

**Pro Acceptance**: Theoretical rigor, clear problem statement, significant contribution to a well-defined gap in the literature, strong empirical validation.

**Con Acceptance**: Limited discussion of computational challenges, omission of comparison with alternative count-data graphical models.

Overall, this paper makes a significant and timely contribution to the field of graphical models, with clear theoretical advances and practical implications. It deserves publication at NIPS.
