This paper proposes several extensions to Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) to overcome the limitations of the original formulation, namely the restriction to negative dependencies. The authors first demonstrate the failure of a natural truncation approach suggested by Kaiser & Cressie (20), then introduce a Truncated PGM (TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper concludes with empirical validation on simulated and real datasets.

The motivation for extending PGMs is clear and the problem well-defined. The initial demonstration that the Winsorized Poisson approach of Kaiser & Cressie doesn’t lead to a valid graphical model is a valuable contribution. The subsequent derivations of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM demonstrate a good understanding of exponential families and graphical model theory. The authors correctly identify the role of the base measure in achieving positive dependencies. The inclusion of simulation studies and a case study adds practical relevance.

However, despite the novelty of the proposed models, a significant number of issues detract from the paper's overall quality.

**Technical Issues & Concerns:**

1. **Mathematical Rigor:** While the authors present theorems, the proofs often lack sufficient detail. For example, the proof of Theorem 1 is cited as existing work, but the reader isn't given enough context to assess its validity. Similarly, the proof of Theorem 3 is crucial, yet feels incomplete. What exactly constitutes “Ω(Z^2)”? A precise definition would be helpful.
2. **SPGM Parameterization:** The sublinear sufficient statistic B(x) introduced for the SPGM seems arbitrarily defined. The choice of R0 and R influences the shape of the statistic, but the paper lacks a theoretical justification for their selection beyond preventing B(x) from becoming negative.  Furthermore, the behavior of B(x) near R seems discontinuous, a property that may have unforeseen consequences.
3. **Variational Bounds:** The reliance on variational upper bounds (particularly for the SPGM) is concerning. The paper does not adequately discuss the tightness of these bounds or the potential impact of the approximation on the inference process. Asserting similarity to Gaussian graphical models based on these bounds requires stronger justification. The Gaussian assumption may be quite poor, especially in the tails.
4. **Computational Complexity:** The paper mentions using proximal gradient ascent for learning the network structure. However, it offers no discussion of the computational cost of this procedure, especially for large-scale datasets. The QPGM, in particular, introduces quadratic terms, which can significantly increase computational complexity.
5. **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation studies are relatively limited.  The results show the TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian methods, but a more thorough comparison against other relevant count data models (e.g., log-linear models, copula-based approaches) is needed. The case study, while demonstrating applicability, lacks a rigorous statistical comparison. Simply stating that the proposed methods find more edges than other methods is insufficient; a quantitative assessment of the quality of the inferred network is required.
6. **Notation:** The notation is occasionally inconsistent. For instance, the use of Θ for the quadratic parameter matrix in the QPGM is fine, but its definition could be clearer.

**Clarity & Presentation:**

1. **Introduction Length:** The introduction is excessively long and contains a substantial amount of background information that could be streamlined.
2. **Equation Formatting:**  Several equations are not properly aligned or formatted.  For example, equation (2) is particularly difficult to read.
3. **Figure Captions:** The captions for Figures 1 and 2 are too brief and do not provide sufficient context for interpreting the results.

**Originality & Significance:**

The novelty lies in the construction of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM. While the idea of modifying the base measure is insightful, the potential impact of these models is somewhat unclear.  The empirical results, while promising, are not sufficiently convincing to demonstrate a significant advance over existing approaches. The claimed ability to model both positive and negative dependencies is valuable, but the practical benefits in real-world applications need to be more thoroughly investigated.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a real limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models.
*   Presents novel extensions to the PGM framework.
*   Includes empirical validation, demonstrating applicability.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Insufficient mathematical rigor in proofs and derivations.
*   Lack of thorough empirical evaluation.
*   Computational concerns not addressed.
*   Presentation issues (lengthy introduction, formatting inconsistencies).
*   The SPGM’s construction feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks strong theoretical justification.




**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper identifies an important problem and proposes potentially interesting solutions, the lack of technical rigor, limited empirical evaluation, and presentation issues outweigh the contributions.  I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the points raised above, provide more detailed proofs, conduct a more comprehensive evaluation, and improve the clarity and presentation of the manuscript before resubmission. A clearer explanation of the theoretical justification for the SPGM's construction would be particularly valuable.
