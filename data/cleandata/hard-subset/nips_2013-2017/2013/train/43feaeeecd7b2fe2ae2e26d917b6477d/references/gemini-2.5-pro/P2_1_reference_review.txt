### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM), which, due to normalizability constraints, can only model negative conditional dependencies between count-valued variables. This severely restricts its practical applicability. The authors make several key contributions to overcome this issue. First, they prove that a previously proposed solution based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution is invalid and cannot form a consistent joint distribution. Second, they introduce a new, valid Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) that allows for both positive and negative dependencies, although it comes with its own trade-offs related to the choice of the truncation level. Recognizing these limitations, the authors then propose two more fundamental and novel models: (1) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which modifies the base measure of the Poisson distribution to be quadratic, ensuring normalizability for arbitrary interaction parameters, much like a Gaussian Graphical Model; and (2) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, allowing for the original Poisson base measure and heavier tails while still permitting both positive and negative dependencies. The utility of the new TPGM and SPGM models is demonstrated through structure recovery experiments on simulated and real-world microRNA expression data.

### Review

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to the field of graphical models for count data. It is well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly written. It identifies a critical gap in the existing literature and provides well-reasoned and novel solutions.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The negative result in Theorem 1, which invalidates the previously proposed Winsorized PGM, is a strong and important contribution. Correcting misconceptions in the literature is a valuable service to the research community.
    *   The theoretical motivation for the QPGM and SPGM is compelling. Theorem 3 provides a clear and insightful diagnosis of why the original PGM fails: the linear growth of the sufficient statistics (`X`) outpaces the sub-linear growth of the base measure (`log(X!)`). This insight directly motivates the two proposed solutions: either increase the growth rate of the base measure (QPGM) or decrease the growth rate of the sufficient statistics (SPGM). This is a very elegant piece of reasoning.
    *   The proposed models are presented as a complete piece of work, from theoretical motivation and model specification to experimental validation.
    *   The authors are honest about the weaknesses of their approaches, particularly noting the trade-offs involved with the TPGM's truncation parameter `R` and the Gaussian-esque thin tails of the QPGM.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   While the paper focuses on model specification, the theoretical analysis for structure recovery (e.g., sparsistency guarantees for neighborhood selection) is left for future work. While understandable given space constraints, including at least a sketch or high-level argument for why existing proofs (e.g., from Ravikumar et al., 2010) would extend to these new models would make the work even more complete.
    *   The selection of hyperparameters for the SPGM (`R0` and `R`) is not deeply explored. In the experiments, they seem to be set based on the data range, but a more principled discussion or sensitivity analysis would strengthen the practical guidance for using this model.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly stating the problem with existing PGMs and outlining the paper's contributions.
    *   The paper follows a logical and intuitive progression: it starts with the standard PGM, critiques existing fixes, proposes a simple but flawed fix (TPGM), and then builds on the insights gained to propose more principled and powerful models (QPGM and SPGM).
    *   The mathematical formulations are precise, and the notation is consistent. The connection to the general framework of exponential family graphical models from Yang et al. [18, 19] is clearly established, which helps place the contributions in the proper context.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The problem of modeling positive dependencies in PGMs is long-standing, but there have been few, if any, satisfactory solutions within the MRF framework. This paper provides the first truly viable set of solutions.
    *   The QPGM and SPGM are novel model classes. The idea of modifying the base measure or sufficient statistics to ensure normalizability in this specific context is a creative and powerful approach.
    *   Proving that the Winsorized PGM of Kaiser and Cressie [20] is invalid is a novel and important finding that corrects the record.

**Significance:** The results are very significant and are likely to have a substantial impact.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   Count data is ubiquitous in many NIPS-relevant fields, including computational biology (e.g., NGS data, as shown in the case study), social network analysis (e.g., interaction counts), and text analysis (e.g., term-document counts). The lack of a flexible graphical model for this data type has been a major hindrance. This paper fills that void.
    *   The proposed models are not just theoretical curiosities; they are shown to be effective in practice for structure learning, outperforming standard methods like Gaussian graphical models on count-based data.
    *   The work opens up numerous avenues for future research, including developing more efficient inference and learning algorithms, extending the models to mixed data types, and performing deeper theoretical analysis of their properties. Researchers and practitioners working with network inference from count data will likely use and build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Addresses a critical, well-known limitation of an important class of graphical models.
*   **Pro:** Provides novel, theoretically sound, and well-motivated models (QPGM, SPGM) that solve the core problem.
*   **Pro:** Includes a valuable negative result that corrects an error in prior literature.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Pro:** Demonstrates strong empirical performance on both simulated and real-world data, highlighting its practical relevance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The theoretical guarantees for the learning procedure (neighborhood selection) are not formally established in the paper and are left to future work.
*   **Con:** The discussion on hyperparameter selection for the proposed models could be more detailed.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a well-executed piece of research that provides a definitive solution to a long-standing problem. I strongly recommend acceptance.
