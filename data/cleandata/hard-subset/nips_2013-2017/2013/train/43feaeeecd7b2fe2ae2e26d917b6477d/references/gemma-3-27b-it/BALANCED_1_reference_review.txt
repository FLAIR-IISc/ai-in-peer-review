This paper addresses the problem of modeling multivariate count data, a challenging area given the limitations of existing approaches like log-linear models and Poisson graphical models (PGMs). The authors identify a key flaw in existing PGMs – their inability to model positive dependencies – and thoroughly investigate various solutions. They demonstrate the incorrectness of a proposed fix based on Winsorized Poisson distributions and then introduce three novel approaches: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper includes theoretical analysis establishing normalizability conditions and demonstrates the models' applicability through simulations and a breast cancer miRNA expression dataset.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods for modeling multivariate count data and provides a strong motivation for the proposed work. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem within various domains where count data is prevalent.
* **Thorough Theoretical Analysis:**  The authors provide rigorous mathematical analysis, including proofs of key propositions and theorems, concerning the normalizability of their proposed models. This is a significant strength and establishes the theoretical foundation of their work. The discussion surrounding the base measure scaling requirements is particularly insightful.
* **Novelty of Approaches:** The introduction of TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represents a significant contribution. Each approach tackles the limitations of previous methods in a distinct way, offering a range of options for modeling different types of count data.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of both simulation studies and a real-world application to breast cancer miRNA expression data provides strong empirical support for the proposed models. The comparison against existing methods demonstrates the potential advantages of the new approaches.
* **Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper is well-connected to existing literature in graphical models, spatial statistics, and count data analysis. The authors appropriately cite relevant work and position their contributions within the broader research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Models:** While the theoretical rigor is commendable, the QPGM and SPGM models introduce complexities (quadratic terms, sublinear sufficient statistics) that might hinder their practical adoption and interpretation. The practical implications of choosing between these models aren’t fully explored.
* **Computational Challenges:** The paper mentions using proximal gradient ascent for learning network structure but doesn’t provide detailed computational complexity analysis. The scalability of these methods to very high-dimensional data is a concern and warrants further investigation. The variational upper bound used for the SPGM, while offering a pathway to computation, introduces approximation errors that aren't thoroughly quantified.
* **Limited Discussion of Model Assumptions:**  The paper focuses heavily on normalizability and theoretical properties but lacks a deeper discussion of the underlying assumptions of each model. For example, the choice of the truncation point *R* in the TPGM could significantly impact performance, and the sensitivity to this parameter isn’t addressed.  Similarly, the rationale behind the specific form of the sublinear sufficient statistic in the SPGM could be expanded.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, the paper occasionally suffers from dense notation, particularly in the mathematical sections.  Some equations could benefit from clearer explanations.
* **Limited Comparison to Recent Alternatives:** While comparisons to standard approaches are included, the paper doesn’t engage with very recent advances in count data modeling, such as those based on negative binomial graphical models or other non-parametric approaches.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important and challenging problem in statistical modeling.
* The proposed models are novel and theoretically well-founded.
* The empirical results demonstrate the potential advantages of the new approaches.
* The thorough analysis and clear presentation make a substantial contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the models may limit their practical applicability.
* The computational challenges associated with model fitting and inference require further investigation.
* The lack of a detailed discussion of model assumptions and sensitivities is a weakness.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the area of graphical models for count data. The authors clearly identify a critical limitation of existing approaches and develop innovative solutions with solid theoretical backing. While the complexity of the models and potential computational challenges are legitimate concerns, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the work outweigh these drawbacks. I believe the paper is suitable for NIPS, but minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen its impact. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of computational complexity, model assumptions, and a comparison to very recent alternatives would be valuable.



**Rating:** Accept with minor revisions.
