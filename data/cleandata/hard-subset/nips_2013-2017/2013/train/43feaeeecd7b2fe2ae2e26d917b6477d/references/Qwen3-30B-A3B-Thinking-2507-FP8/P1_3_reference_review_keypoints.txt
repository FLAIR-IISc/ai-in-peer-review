- Paper fills a gap by proposing tractable Poisson graphical models for high-dimensional count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Main contributions: proof that the Winsorized Poisson approach is invalid, proposal of Truncated PGM (TPGM), and introduction of Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  
- Theorems 1–4 rigorously address normalizability and theoretically justify the new models.  
- Demonstrated empirical superiority on simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA network, identifying biologically relevant hubs.  
- Originality: novel modifications to base measure and sufficient statistics that overcome prior limitations of PGMs.  
- Quality: strong, careful theoretical work with correct identification of prior flaws (Winsorized Poisson).  
- Significance: important advance for genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains with multivariate count data.  
- Clarity: well-structured presentation and accessible mathematical derivations for readers familiar with graphical models.  
- Theoretical limitation: QPGM has a Gaussian-like thin tail; SPGM improves this but needs more detailed tail analysis.  
- Practical limitation: TPGM requires choosing truncation level R with no practical selection guidance.  
- Practical limitation: SPGM requires selecting two parameters (R and R₀), complicating model selection.  
- Computational concern: paper lacks a thorough comparison of inference and learning complexity across the proposed models.  
- Literature context: could more explicitly relate to recent non-Gaussian/non-paranormal graphical-model advances.  
- Pro-accept: addresses a key gap, offers novel rigorous theory, shows practical advantages, and includes meaningful genomics application.  
- Con-accept: tail behavior (QPGM), parameter-selection guidance (R, R₀), and limited comparison to very recent related work are minor drawbacks.  
- Final recommendation: Accept — substantial, technically sound contribution with strong theoretical and empirical support.
