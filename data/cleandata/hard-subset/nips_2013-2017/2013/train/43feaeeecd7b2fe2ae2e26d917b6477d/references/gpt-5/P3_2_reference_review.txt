Summary and contributions
The paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson Markov random fields (MRFs): Besag’s auto-Poisson construction forces nonpositive pairwise parameters, thus only modeling competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. The authors (i) show that a previously proposed fix—using Winsorized (hard-truncated at R) Poisson node-conditionals—cannot yield a consistent joint distribution (Theorem 1); (ii) propose an alternative truncation that preserves the exponential-family form at the node level and yields a valid joint MRF on {0,…,R}p, the Truncated PGM (TPGM) (Theorem 2); (iii) prove a necessity result: with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, allowing both positive and negative interactions while ensuring normalizability requires a base measure that scales at least quadratically, C(x)=Ω(x^2) (Theorem 3); (iv) introduce the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure and admits both positive and negative interactions under a negative-definiteness-type condition on quadratic terms (Proposition 2); and (v) propose the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which retains the Poisson base measure log(x!) but replaces B(x) by a piecewise-linear function that saturates beyond R, proving normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They discuss neighborhood-selection estimators and provide simulation studies and a case study on TCGA miRNA-seq data, showing improved edge recovery versus Gaussian-based methods and the standard Poisson MRF.

Relation to prior work
This work builds directly on Besag’s auto-models and the exponential-family MRF framework of Yang et al., and it connects to earlier multivariate count models (copulas, sums-of-Poissons, log-linear contingency-table models, and hierarchical spatial models), all of which have practical or theoretical drawbacks for high-dimensional inference. The paper’s critique of Winsorized conditionals [Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith] is important, as that approach has been cited as enabling positive dependence in Poisson MRFs. The proposed QPGM is a discrete-domain analogue to Gaussian MRFs in its quadratic energy, while SPGM is related in spirit to “sublinear/saturating” sufficient statistics explored in later exponential-family MRF variants for counts. Related work on overdispersed count models (e.g., negative-binomial and Poisson-lognormal graphical models) or copula-based count networks is not compared empirically but is cited at a high level.

Quality (theory and methodology)
- Theorem 1 (incompatibility of Winsorized conditionals) is a useful and nontrivial negative result. The statement is strong (“no joint exists”) and hinges on the indicator term I(Xs=R)Ψ(ηs) not being representable as a consistent set of local conditionals; it would help to include at least a proof sketch referencing the compatibility/Brook’s lemma conditions and clarify the role of the R>3 assumption (are small R edge cases possible?).
- Theorem 2 (TPGM) is sound and gives a finite-support exponential-family MRF. The drawbacks are correctly discussed: dependence on R, sensitivity when counts approach R, and reemergence of negativity constraints as R→∞.
- Theorem 3 (C(x)=Ω(x^2) necessary with B(x)=x) is an insightful general result. Please clarify the exact assumptions (pairwise cliques only? behavior of θ_s terms?) and the mode of divergence (constructing directions where the quadratic interaction dominates subquadratic base measure). A short proof sketch would strengthen confidence.
- QPGM: Proposition 2 provides a sufficient condition via negative-definiteness of the quadratic form. This is clean, but raises identifiability/practical issues: the learned diagonal θ_ss and pairwise θ_st may trade off; how are these constrained/regularized in estimation? Also, while the Gaussian variational bound for nodewise conditionals is convenient, it blurs the connection to the intended discrete model—what is the impact on statistical consistency and edge selection?
- SPGM: The saturation of B(x) beyond R naturally tames growth and permits positive interactions. However, (a) node-conditional partition functions lack closed form; practical estimation requires truncation/approximation—please detail how gradients and likelihoods are computed; (b) Proposition 3 provides a variational upper bound only for θ≥0; what do you use when θ<0?; (c) sensitivity to (R0,R) and how they are chosen data-adaptively should be discussed.
- Estimation: The paper relies on ℓ1-penalized nodewise estimation. The text claims sparsistency should follow from prior work but defers analysis. Given the novelty of the sufficient statistics/base measures and the variational approximations, at least high-level conditions or a theorem statement would be valuable.

Empirical evaluation
- Simulations: Comparing against Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines is reasonable, though adding a count-specific competitor (e.g., Poisson-lognormal, negative-binomial-based pseudo-likelihood, or copula-based discrete models) would strengthen claims. Report precision-recall and sample-complexity curves; detail symmetrization rules, tuning via stability selection, and computational cost.
- Case study: The TCGA miRNA example is compelling; the discovery of known hubs and a chr19 clique is encouraging. Consider adding held-out predictive performance (e.g., conditional log-likelihood) and an ablation contrasting TPGM vs SPGM vs Gaussian on the same stability threshold to quantify differences. Also discuss biological interpretation of interaction saturation in SPGM.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. Notation for base measures (C), log-partitions (A, D, D̄) could be streamlined; include proof sketches for the main theorems; define Wp when first used; and ensure all algorithmic details for nodewise fitting are provided (objective, gradients, bounds/approximations, and complexity).

Originality
High. The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals, the necessity of quadratic-scale base measure for linear stats, and the concrete QPGM/SPGM constructions are novel and address a prominent gap.

Significance
Significant. Providing valid MRFs for counts with positive interactions is important for genomics and other count applications. The theoretical insights are likely to influence future model design.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add proof sketches for Theorems 1 and 3.
- Provide practical guidance for choosing R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM).
- Detail estimation for SPGM/QPGM, including handling θ<0 in bounds, gradient computation, and convergence.
- Add comparisons to at least one alternative count-network model.
- Discuss identifiability and regularization of quadratic terms in QPGM.
- Report computational times and scaling.

Minor
- Fix small typos and ensure all references are complete and consistent.

Recommendation: Weak accept

Pros:
- Strong theoretical contributions (impossibility, necessity, and new constructions).
- Practical models that enable positive dependencies for counts.
- Clear empirical gains on simulations and a real dataset.

Cons:
- Some proofs and estimation details are sparse.
- Limited empirical baselines specific to counts.
- Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity (R, R0) need more discussion.
