

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses a significant limitation in the modeling of multivariate count data using graphical models. The authors identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only capture negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints (Proposition 1), which severely limits their practical applicability. The paper critically examines prior attempts to overcome this limitation, including the Winsorized Poisson approach [20, 21], and demonstrates that this approach cannot yield a consistent joint distribution (Theorem 1). The authors then propose three novel approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which collectively enable modeling both positive and negative dependencies in count data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Identification:** The paper effectively establishes the fundamental limitation of existing Poisson graphical models, a well-known issue in the field [20, 21]. The motivation is compelling, as count data is ubiquitous in modern applications (genomics, spatial statistics, etc.).

2. **Rigorous Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides strong theoretical results (Theorems 1-4 and Propositions 2-3) that rigorously establish the normalizability and properties of the proposed models. The proofs are well-constructed and address the core technical challenge.

3. **Novel Model Proposals:** The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent a meaningful advancement beyond existing approaches. The SPGM in particular offers a novel solution with heavier tails than QPGM while permitting all real-valued edge parameters.

4. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed models over existing methods (Gaussian graphical models, PGM) on count data. The breast cancer microRNA case study provides compelling evidence of practical utility, with SPGM and TPGM identifying biologically relevant hub miRNAs that other methods miss.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Considerations:** The paper lacks discussion of computational challenges in learning these models, particularly for SPGM which involves infinite sums. While the authors mention neighborhood selection, they don't address how the computational complexity scales with dimensionality or the practical implementation of the log-partition function approximation.

2. **Statistical Properties:** The paper doesn't analyze the statistical properties of the estimators (e.g., consistency, convergence rates) for the proposed models. This is particularly important for high-dimensional settings where the authors claim superiority over existing methods.

3. **Comparison with Alternative Count Models:** The paper could better contextualize its contributions against other recent approaches for modeling count data, such as the multivariate Poisson models in [11, 12, 13, 14, 15]. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

4. **Practical Limitations of TPGM:** The paper acknowledges that TPGM has a bounded domain, but doesn't sufficiently discuss the practical implications of this limitation for real-world applications where counts might exceed the truncation level R.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. Expand the discussion of computational complexity and practical implementation of the proposed models, particularly for SPGM. Consider adding a brief analysis of how the choice of R (for TPGM) or R₀, R (for SPGM) affects computational efficiency.

2. Include a brief analysis of the statistical properties of the neighborhood selection approach for the proposed models. This would strengthen the claim that these models can be learned from data.

3. Add a short discussion comparing the proposed models with the multivariate Poisson models in [14, 15], which also model count data but with different assumptions.

4. In the case study, provide more specific details about why the identified miRNAs are biologically significant (beyond just mentioning their names) and how the proposed models' ability to capture positive dependencies led to these discoveries.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The identification of the fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models and the development of three novel approaches to address it are strong points. The empirical results provide convincing evidence of the practical utility of the proposed methods.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of discussion regarding computational and statistical properties of the proposed models, which are crucial for high-dimensional applications. However, these are relatively minor gaps in an otherwise strong theoretical contribution.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a well-motivated problem with a clear theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art. The proposed models represent a meaningful step forward in modeling multivariate count data, and the empirical validation is compelling. While the paper would benefit from addressing the computational and statistical properties more thoroughly, these are not essential to the core theoretical contribution, which is strong and well-supported. The paper's novelty is clear, and it would be valuable to the NeurIPS community.
