This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), a natural class of models for multivariate count data. As the authors correctly point out, standard PGMs, as constructed by Besag (1974) and extended by Yang et al. (2012), are restricted to modeling only negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints over the infinite domain of counts. This severely limits their practical utility. The paper's main goal is to develop graphical models for count data that can capture both positive and negative ("cooperative" and "competitive") relationships.

The authors begin by analyzing a previously proposed solution based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution (Kaiser and Cressie, 1997). In a significant theoretical contribution, they prove that this approach can never lead to a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1), thus invalidating that line of work and motivating the need for new solutions. The paper then introduces three novel models.
1.  **Truncated PGM (TPGM):** This model is based on a properly normalized truncated Poisson distribution over a finite domain {0, ..., R}. By restricting the domain, the model's partition function is always finite, allowing for arbitrary real-valued edge parameters.
2.  **Quadratic PGM (QPGM):** This model returns to the infinite domain but modifies the base measure of the Poisson distribution. The authors show that for the standard linear sufficient statistics, the base measure must scale at least quadratically to ensure normalizability with positive edge weights (Theorem 3). This leads to a model with a quadratic term, similar to a Gaussian Graphical Model (GGM), which is normalizable provided the matrix of quadratic parameters is negative definite.
3.  **Sublinear PGM (SPGM):** To avoid the "Gaussian-esque" thin tails of the QPGM while retaining a heavy-tailed base measure (like the original Poisson's log(X!)), this model modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear. This tames the growth of the interaction terms, ensuring normalizability for any edge parameters.

The paper concludes with experiments on synthetic and real-world miRNA expression data, demonstrating that the proposed TPGM and SPGM outperform GGM-based methods for structure recovery on count data.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known, important, and unsolved problem in the graphical modeling literature. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive dependencies is a major roadblock to their application. The three proposed models are novel, principled, and provide the first viable and theoretically justified solutions to this problem. The result in Theorem 1, which invalidates a previously proposed method, is also a strong and important contribution.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical contributions are non-trivial and provide deep insight into the model design. Theorem 3, which establishes the connection between the scaling of the base measure and the admissibility of positive parameters, is particularly insightful and elegantly justifies the QPGM. The constructions of TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are clear, and their normalizability properties are formally established.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly, and the logical flow from the failure of existing models to the development of the three new proposals is compelling. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the trade-offs associated with each of their proposed models (e.g., bounded domain for TPGM, thin tails for QPGM).
4.  **Thoroughness:** The authors provide a comprehensive exploration of the problem space, developing three distinct solutions that modify different core components of the exponential family formulation (domain, base measure, and sufficient statistics). This provides a valuable "toolkit" for practitioners, as different models might be appropriate for different types of count data.
5.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while brief, effectively demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods. The comparison against GGM and non-paranormal methods on both synthetic data and a challenging real-world genomics dataset shows that models specifically designed for counts can yield superior performance and more biologically plausible network structures.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees for Learning:** While the paper focuses on model specification, it motivates the work with the goal of learning network structure. The experiments use L1-penalized neighborhood selection, but the paper does not provide theoretical guarantees (e.g., sparsistency) for this procedure applied to the new models. The authors cite the analysis of Yang et al. (2012) and suggest it could be extended. While a full proof is likely beyond the scope of a single conference paper, a brief discussion of whether the necessary conditions for such an analysis (e.g., likelihood concavity, restricted strong convexity) are met for the proposed models would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution to the learning literature.
2.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The TPGM and SPGM introduce new hyperparameters (R, and R0, R, respectively). The paper sets these based on heuristics (e.g., R = max count). A more detailed discussion on the sensitivity of the models to these parameters and potential data-driven methods for their selection (e.g., cross-validation) would enhance the practical applicability of the work.
3.  **Absence of QPGM in Experiments:** The QPGM is well-motivated theoretically but is absent from the numerical experiments section. The authors argue that its behavior would be similar to a GGM due to the variational bound. While plausible, including it in the empirical comparison would have completed the evaluation of all three proposed models and either confirmed this intuition or revealed interesting differences. A short sentence explaining its omission from the experiments would be helpful.
4.  **Details of SPGM Optimization:** The paper proposes using a variational upper bound for the SPGM's node-conditional log-partition function based on the standard Poisson partition function (Proposition 3). However, this bound is stated to hold for θ ≥ 0. It is unclear how the optimization proceeds for θ < 0, or if this restriction impacts the ability to learn networks with negative edges. Clarifying this point would be valuable.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and long-standing problem in graphical models for count data.
*   Provides three novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound models.
*   Strong theoretical contributions, including the invalidation of a previous approach.
*   Paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and well-organized.
*   Empirical results on synthetic and real data demonstrate the practical value of the new models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis for the learning algorithm (structure recovery) is deferred to future work.
*   Practical aspects like hyperparameter selection are not discussed in depth.
*   One of the three proposed models (QPGM) is not included in the experimental evaluation.

### Decision

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the literature on graphical models. It solves a major practical and theoretical problem with clarity and rigor. The strengths decisively outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
