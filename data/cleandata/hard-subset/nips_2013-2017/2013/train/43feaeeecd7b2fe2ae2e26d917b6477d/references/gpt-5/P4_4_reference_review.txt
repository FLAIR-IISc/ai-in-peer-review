Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses modeling conditional dependence structure in multivariate count data via undirected graphical models. It revisits Poisson graphical models (PGMs) based on node-conditional Poisson distributions [Besag 1974; Yang et al. 2012/2013], whose key limitation is normalizability requiring non-positive pairwise parameters, hence only accommodating negative conditional dependencies. The authors first analyze truncation strategies. They prove that “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals (censoring all mass above R at R), as proposed in spatial statistics [Kaiser & Cressie 1997; Griffith 2006], cannot be compatible with any joint MRF (Theorem 1). They then propose a different truncation that preserves an exponential-family form by redistributing mass above R proportionally over {0,...,R}. With these node-conditionals, standard exponential-family MRF construction yields a Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) that allows both positive and negative edges without normalizability restrictions (Theorem 2), at the cost of bounded support and sensitivity as R grows.

Moving beyond truncation, the paper modifies the univariate exponential-family primitives. With linear sufficient statistic B(x)=x but general base measure C(x), it proves that to permit any positive pairwise parameters while ensuring normalizability, C(x) must grow at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with log-density containing θss x_s^2 terms and pairwise x_s x_t terms, normalizable under a negative-definiteness-type condition on the quadratic form (Proposition 2). The authors note a Gaussian-like tail and propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure log x! but replaces B(x) with a piecewise-linear function that saturates beyond R, yielding heavier tails and normalizability for all real pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They also give variational upper bounds for node-conditional log-partitions (e.g., Gaussian integral for QPGM; Poisson for SPGM when θ≥0) to enable neighborhood selection estimators.

Empirically, they apply `1-penalized node-conditional likelihood for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM to structure recovery on simulated data (hub/scale-free graphs) and to a TCGA breast cancer microRNA sequencing dataset, comparing against Gaussian graphical models and nonparanormal variants. Count-based methods outperform Gaussian baselines; TPGM/SPGM recover biologically plausible hubs missed by Gaussian methods and the original PGM (which finds mostly negative edges).

Quality (technical soundness)
- The incompatibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1) is important; however, the main text does not provide proof details. Clarifying the exact use of compatibility conditions (e.g., Brook’s lemma/Hammersley–Clifford) and the role of the I(x=R) term in violating the required multilinearity of clique potentials would strengthen the claim.
- The existence of TPGM (Theorem 2) follows cleanly from the exponential-family conditional specification and prior results on EF-MRFs; this part is sound.
- The necessity result (Theorem 3) that C(x)=Ω(x^2) to allow positive edges with linear sufficient statistics is a strong and interesting statement. It would benefit from precise conditions: asymptotics uniform in parameters, whether the result is worst-case (∃ positive θst) or generic, and explicit growth comparisons relative to pairwise quadratic terms and the counting measure. A brief proof sketch would aid assessment.
- QPGM normalizability (Proposition 2) under XTΘX ≤ −c‖X‖^2 is standard; the condition allows mixed-sign edges if the overall quadratic form is negative-definite. Node-conditional inference requires summing exp(ηx+θ_ss x^2) over x∈N0; the Gaussian upper bound is reasonable for optimization but introduces approximation. The paper does not analyze statistical consequences of this bound for estimation consistency.
- SPGM normalizability for all θst (Theorem 4) is plausible since B(x) saturates and log x! controls tails; formal conditions on R0,R and node parameters θs ensuring finiteness across all coordinates should be stated explicitly. The variational bound (Proposition 3) applies for θ≥0; the approach when θ<0 during optimization should be clarified.
- Learning: The paper uses `1-penalized node-conditional likelihood (neighborhood selection), consistent with [Yang et al. 2012/2013]. However, sparsistency and optimization convergence guarantees for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM with the proposed approximations are not provided; this limits the theoretical completeness.

Clarity
The motivation and progression (limitations of PGM → failed Winsorization → TPGM → QPGM/SPGM) are clearly presented. Definitions of sufficient statistics/base measures are precise. Several key theorems lack proof sketches in the main text; pointing to appendices and clarifying assumptions would improve readability. Practical guidance on choosing R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM) is limited; an explicit selection strategy would aid practitioners. Minor typos/notation issues (e.g., “SGPM”) should be corrected.

Originality
- Identifying and proving incompatibility of Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals with any joint MRF is a novel and useful clarification.
- TPGM in exponential-family form is a natural but, to this reviewer’s knowledge, previously undocumented constructive route to a tractable count MRF with positive edges.
- The necessity of quadratic (or heavier) base measures to allow positive interactions under linear sufficient statistics is a conceptually novel insight.
- QPGM and SPGM are new model classes within EF-MRFs for counts.

Relation to broader prior work: The paper situates itself relative to Gaussian/Ising/multinomial MRFs and EF-MRFs. It references spatial auto-Poisson and multivariate Poisson constructions. It would benefit from discussing latent-variable approaches for count dependencies (e.g., Poisson log-normal graphical models and copula-based count models) which allow positive correlations and are used in genomics; contrasting these with direct MRFs would contextualize significance and trade-offs (interpretability of conditional independencies vs. latent confounding).

Significance
The work advances the foundations of graphical modeling for counts by clarifying impossibility results and proposing three tractable model classes that admit mixed-sign dependencies. If supported with stronger estimation theory and broader empirical comparisons (including latent-variable count models), these models could be impactful for high-throughput sequencing, spatial incidence, and other count settings. As presented, the main contribution is model specification with preliminary empirical evidence; practical uptake may hinge on guidance for hyperparameter selection, computational scalability, and learning guarantees.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear identification of a flaw in a widely cited truncation approach; constructive alternative (TPGM).
- Fundamental theoretical insight (Theorem 3) guiding model design.
- Two novel families (QPGM, SPGM) that lift the negative-edge limitation while retaining node-conditional tractability.
- Empirical results indicating benefits over Gaussian-based baselines on count data; relevant real-data case study.

Cons for acceptance
- Key theorems lack detailed proofs/assumptions in the main text; necessity result needs clearer scope.
- Learning guarantees (sparsistency, effect of variational bounds) are deferred; optimization details for SPGM/QPGM are thin.
- Limited baselines: no comparison to latent Gaussian/Poisson log-normal or copula count graphical models commonly used in genomics.
- Hyperparameter selection for R, R0 is ad hoc; sensitivity analysis is limited.
- QPGM may have overly light tails for some count data; this trade-off is not empirically probed.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide proof sketches (or append references to full proofs) for Theorems 1, 3, and 4, with explicit assumptions and scope.
- Discuss computational strategies for exact/approximate node-conditional log-partitions when θ<0 (SPGM) and evaluate accuracy of variational bounds in practice.
- Add comparisons to Poisson log-normal/covariance models or copula-based count graphical models, and report runtime/memory.
- Offer practical guidance for choosing R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM), with sensitivity analyses.
- Include at least a partial sparsistency result or conditions under which neighborhood selection is consistent for TPGM/SPGM.
- Expand real-data evaluation with held-out predictive checks or goodness-of-fit diagnostics, and quantify biological validation beyond anecdotal hubs.

Overall, the paper makes substantive modeling contributions with promising initial evidence, but strengthening theory and empirical comparisons would improve its rigor and impact.
