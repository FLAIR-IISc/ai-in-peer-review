This paper tackles a fundamental problem in statistical modeling: extending graphical models to handle multivariate count data. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches, particularly the non-negativity constraint imposed on edge weights in standard Poisson graphical models (PGMs). Their proposed solutions – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – represent a significant step forward, offering increasingly flexible ways to model both positive and negative dependencies. The mathematical development is rigorous, and the motivation for each approach is clearly articulated. The empirical validation, while limited, is promising.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core contribution – relaxing the non-negativity constraint on edge weights in PGMs – is highly novel. The TPGM, QPGM and SPGM constructions are all interesting and offer unique trade-offs. The theoretical justification for the quadratic base measure in QPGM is particularly insightful.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The proofs and derivations are generally well-presented and convincing. Theorem 1, demonstrating the inconsistency of the Kaiser and Cressie approach, is a strong result. The exploration of the relationship between the base measure and normalizability is also valuable.
* **Problem Importance:** Modeling multivariate count data is crucial in many domains, and a flexible graphical model framework is sorely needed. This paper directly addresses this need.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex mathematical arguments.
* **Demonstrated Practicality:** The experiments, while not exhaustive, showcase the potential of the proposed models, demonstrating better performance than existing methods in some settings. The comparison to existing methods is strong, especially pointing out limitations of existing PGMs.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. The simulation studies would benefit from a more extensive evaluation across a wider range of network structures and parameter settings. The real-world application is compelling but could be strengthened with a more in-depth analysis and comparison to other relevant methods specifically designed for gene expression data.
* **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost of parameter estimation and inference in the QPGM and SPGM, especially with larger datasets, is not fully addressed. The paper mentions using penalized likelihood, but a discussion of scalability and potential optimization challenges would be beneficial.
* **Practical Interpretation of Parameters:** While the models allow for both positive and negative dependencies, interpreting the meaning of the quadratic or sublinear parameters in a practical context might be challenging for users.
* **SPGM Justification:** The motivation for the sublinear approach feels less intuitive than the quadratic one. Further justification and explanation of why a sublinear sufficient statistic is advantageous would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a significant problem.
* The theoretical contributions are substantial and well-supported.
* The empirical results, while not exhaustive, are encouraging.
* The work has the potential to impact a wide range of fields dealing with multivariate count data.
* The paper pushes the boundaries of existing knowledge and opens up new avenues for research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The empirical validation is limited and doesn't fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed models.
* The computational complexity and scalability concerns are not adequately addressed.
* The practical interpretability of the parameters in QPGM and SPGM could be a barrier to adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a significant advance in the field of graphical models for count data. While the empirical validation could be more comprehensive and the computational aspects require further investigation, the novelty and theoretical soundness of the proposed models are compelling. I lean strongly towards acceptance, prioritizing the innovative nature of the work over minor methodological shortcomings. The potential impact of this research is substantial, and I believe it is a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on expanding the empirical evaluation and addressing the computational complexity concerns.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
