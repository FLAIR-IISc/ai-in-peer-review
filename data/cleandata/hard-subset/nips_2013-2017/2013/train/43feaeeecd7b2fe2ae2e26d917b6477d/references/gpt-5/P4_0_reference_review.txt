Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: normalizability of the standard auto-Poisson model [17, 18, 19] forces non-positive pairwise parameters, hence only negative conditional dependencies are allowed. The authors make three main contributions:

1) Truncation analysis. They show that replacing node-conditionals with the Winsorized (right-censored) Poisson advocated in spatial statistics [20, 21] cannot yield a consistent joint distribution via the Besag construction; specifically, Theorem 1 proves no compatible joint exists for R > 3 when the node-conditional has the Winsorized form (their Eq. 2). In contrast, they propose an alternative exponential-family truncation (their Eq. 3) that preserves exponential-family structure. Theorem 2 shows this induces a valid joint MRF with pairwise interactions, called the Truncated PGM (TPGM), which is normalizable without sign constraints and thus can represent both positive and negative conditional dependencies. They also discuss its practical limitations as R grows.

2) Modified base measure with linear sufficient statistic. Motivated by why the classical PGM needs non-positive edge weights (pairwise terms grow quadratically, while the Poisson base measure log Z! is sub-quadratic), they study a one-parameter exponential family with unchanged sufficient statistic B(Z)=Z but a generalized base measure C(Z). Theorem 3 proves that, to allow positive dependencies while ensuring normalizability, C must grow at least quadratically, C(Z)=Ω(Z^2). This leads to a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic node terms (their Eq. 6). Proposition 2 provides a sufficient normalizability condition: the matrix of quadratic coefficients is negative definite (or, more generally, the quadratic form is upper bounded by −c||X||^2). They relate the node-conditional log-partition to a Gaussian variational upper bound for efficient nodewise regressions.

3) Sublinear sufficient statistics with Poisson base measure. To avoid Gaussian-like thin tails of QPGM while permitting both positive and negative dependencies, they propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains the Poisson base measure log Z! but replaces B(Z)=Z with a bounded, piecewise-sublinear B(Z; R0, R) that saturates above R. Theorem 4 shows normalizability for all pairwise parameters. For estimation, they provide simple variational upper bounds on the node-conditional log-partition using the standard Poisson log-partition when θ ≥ 0.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on the exponential-family MRF construction of [18, 19], resolves confusion around the Winsorized Poisson approach in spatial statistics [20, 21], and contrasts with multivariate Poisson constructions via shared latent summands [14, 15] (which induce only positive correlations and can be intractable), and copula-based/nonparanormal approaches [7–10] used as baselines in experiments.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a fundamental obstacle (normalizability with linear sufficient statistics and sub-quadratic base measure) and principled routes around it.
- Theorem 1 provides a useful negative result clarifying why Winsorization fails to yield compatible joint distributions in the Besag framework.
- TPGM offers a simple, tractable, exponential-family alternative to Winsorization that permits both positive and negative dependencies.
- Theorem 3 gives a clean necessary condition on base-measure growth for positive dependencies with linear sufficient stats; this is conceptually important.
- Two constructive families (QPGM, SPGM) that satisfy these conditions, each with distinct tail and modeling behaviors; normalizability conditions are explicit.
- Practical estimation strategy via nodewise penalized conditional likelihoods; variational bounds for intractable node-conditional partitions are provided.
- Empirical results on simulated and real RNA-seq data show improved structure recovery over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines; demonstration of biologically plausible hubs and clusters.

Weaknesses and questions
- Proof details: Theorem 1 (non-existence for Winsorized conditionals) is central and potentially controversial given [20, 21]; a proof sketch or conditions (e.g., role of R>3, assumptions on E(XV\s)) should be more fully elaborated in the main text to aid verification.
- Necessity vs sufficiency in Theorem 3: The Ω(Z^2) condition is stated as necessary under “θ ≰ 0”; the precise regularity assumptions (e.g., graph size, coefficient bounds, growth of positive interactions) and whether milder conditions might suffice in restricted settings could be clarified.
- QPGM modeling trade-offs: Quadratic base measure produces thin tails relative to common count distributions and departs substantially from Poisson marginals; guidance on when QPGM is preferable vs SPGM, and empirical tail fit diagnostics, would be helpful.
- SPGM estimation: Node-conditional log-partition D(θ, R0, R) lacks closed form; the paper mentions variational upper bounds, but it is not fully specified how these are used in optimization (e.g., are bounds tight enough to preserve sparsistency? Do they optimize a surrogate? Any convergence guarantees?). Computational complexity and truncation strategies for infinite sums are not detailed.
- Structure learning theory: While [18, 19] suggests nodewise GLM sparsistency extends, no theoretical guarantees are provided here for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM; assumptions (e.g., incoherence, tail bounds, curvature) likely differ due to new base measures/sufficient statistics.
- Experimental coverage: Simulations are largely favorable to TPGM when data come from TPGM; SPGM is not evaluated on data generated from SPGM; comparisons to alternative count models (e.g., latent-factor Poisson, copula-Poisson) are absent. Quantitative measures beyond ROC (e.g., precision at fixed recall, calibration, held-out likelihood/predictive checks) and sensitivity to R, R0 choices would strengthen claims.
- Real-data validation: The biological case study is qualitative; independent validation (e.g., enrichment analyses, replication across cohorts) is not reported. Stability selection choices and resulting variability could be discussed.

Quality
- The modeling constructions are mathematically sound at a high level, and normalizability conditions are well-motivated. However, key proofs (notably Theorem 1) and estimation details for SPGM/QPGM need more transparency to fully assess rigor and reproducibility.
- Experimental methodology is reasonable but could be expanded and made more systematic.

Clarity
- Overall well-written with clear motivation and structure. Some notational inconsistencies (e.g., W vs domain, X\s vs XV\s) and missing definitions could be cleaned up. Provide brief proof sketches of main theorems or move key steps to the main text.
- Parameter choices (R, R0) and optimization details (step sizes, stopping criteria, handling of log-partition bounds) should be specified.

Originality
- High. The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals, the necessary-growth condition on base measures, and the specific QPGM/SPGM constructions appear novel relative to [17–19, 20–21] and common multivariate Poisson/copula approaches.

Significance
- Significant. Enabling multivariate count graphical models with both positive and negative dependencies addresses a real gap and is pertinent to genomics and other domains with count data. The proposed families could stimulate further methodological and theoretical work.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of PGMs with clear theoretical insights and constructive alternatives (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
- Novelty and potential impact are strong; results are relevant to NeurIPS topics (graphical models, high-dimensional structure learning).
- Empirical evidence suggests practical gains over Gaussian-based methods on count data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient detail on proofs (especially Theorem 1) and on estimation algorithms/approximations for SPGM/QPGM to fully assess soundness and reproducibility.
- Limited comparative baselines for count models; lack of rigorous structure-learning guarantees.
- Real-data validation is qualitative; more comprehensive evaluation would bolster significance claims.

Suggestions
- Include proof sketches or an appendix with full proofs of Theorems 1 and 3; clarify assumptions.
- Provide algorithmic details and, if optimizing surrogates via variational bounds, discuss consistency/bias.
- Expand experiments: include SPGM-generated data, alternative count baselines, sensitivity analyses for R and R0, and quantitative real-data validation.
