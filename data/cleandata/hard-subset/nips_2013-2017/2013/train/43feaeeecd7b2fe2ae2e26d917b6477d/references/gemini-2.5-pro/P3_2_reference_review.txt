**Summary**
This paper addresses a critical and well-known limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) for modeling multivariate count data: their inability to capture positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints that require non-positive edge weights. The authors make several significant contributions. First, they provide a formal proof that a previously proposed solution based on "Winsorized" Poisson conditionals is invalid and cannot lead to a consistent joint distribution. Second, they propose three novel classes of graphical models for count data that permit both positive and negative dependencies. These are: (1) the Truncated PGM (TPGM), a correctly formulated model on a finite domain; (2) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which modifies the base measure to be quadratic, ensuring normalizability under conditions similar to Gaussian Graphical Models (GGMs); and (3) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, allowing for arbitrary real-valued dependencies while retaining the Poisson `log(X!)` base measure. The paper provides theoretical justification for the validity and properties of these models and demonstrates their practical utility for network recovery on both simulated and real-world miRNA expression data, showing superior performance over GGMs and the original PGM.

**Strengths**
This is an excellent paper that makes a substantial contribution to the literature on graphical models for non-Gaussian data.

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The problem of modeling high-dimensional count data is of immense practical importance in fields like genomics, neuroscience, and social sciences. The standard PGM, while elegant, is severely hampered by its restriction to negative correlations. This paper provides the first well-founded and practical framework for rich dependency modeling in Poisson-like graphical models, filling a major gap in the field. The proposed models are likely to be widely adopted and built upon.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper's contributions are highly original and technically sound.
    *   The disproof of the Winsorized PGM approach (Theorem 1) is a valuable contribution in itself, correcting a misconception in prior literature. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying theory of exponential families and MRFs established by Besag and others.
    *   The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are novel and thoughtfully designed. The progression of ideas is logical: from a simple (but flawed) truncation, to a corrected truncation, and then to more fundamental modifications of the base measure and sufficient statistics. This structured exploration of the design space is very compelling.
    *   The theoretical analysis, while concise, establishes the key properties of the models, particularly the conditions for normalizability which are at the heart of the problem.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem, lucidly explain the shortcomings of prior work, and present their own contributions in a logical sequence. They are also commendably transparent about the limitations of their own models (e.g., the trade-offs in TPGM and the Gaussian-like tails of QPGM).

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results effectively demonstrate the value of the proposed methods. The comparison against relevant baselines like GGMs and the nonparanormal skeptic on synthetic data shows a clear performance advantage. The application to miRNA expression data is a strong case study, not only showing improved network recovery over the original PGM and GGM but also identifying biologically relevant hubs and structures that were missed by other methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**
While the paper is strong, there are several areas that could be improved to make it even more impactful.

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees for Learning:** The paper focuses on model specification and relies on neighborhood selection for learning. While it is stated that sparsistency results from prior work [18, 19] are expected to extend, the paper would be significantly strengthened by providing at least a high-level argument or sketch of the proof. As it stands, the claim that the network structure can be consistently recovered is promissory. Establishing the necessary conditions for consistent estimation (e.g., related to the curvature of the conditional log-likelihoods) would make the work more complete.

2.  **Computational Details:** The paper is sparse on the details of the learning procedure. It mentions using proximal gradient ascent but does not specify the form of the gradients or proximal operators, particularly for the more complex SPGM model. For the SPGM, the node-conditional partition function is intractable, and Proposition 3 provides a variational upper bound. It is unclear if this bound is used during optimization (e.g., as a surrogate objective) or simply for theoretical analysis. Providing more detail on the optimization algorithm would be crucial for reproducibility and for assessing the practical scalability of the methods.

3.  **QPGM Missing from Experiments:** The QPGM is presented as one of the three main contributions but is conspicuously absent from the numerical experiments. The authors theorize that its performance might be similar to a GGM due to its thin tails, but this should be empirically verified. Including the QPGM in the comparative analysis would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between the proposed models.

4.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The proposed models introduce new hyperparameters (`R` in TPGM, `R0` and `R` in SPGM). The experiments set these in a heuristic manner (e.g., `R = max(X)`). A more principled discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters and strategies for their selection (e.g., via cross-validation or stability-based methods like StARS) would enhance the practical utility of the proposed framework. For the TPGM, an empirical characterization of the trade-off between a large `R` (closer to Poisson) and the range of allowable positive dependencies would be particularly insightful.

**Recommendation**
Overall, this is a high-quality, well-written, and significant paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It tackles a long-standing open problem with a series of novel and well-justified solutions. The weaknesses identified are primarily omissions of detail or further analysis that do not undermine the core contributions but would be valuable additions for a final version. The paper successfully defines a new and important class of graphical models for count data that will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
