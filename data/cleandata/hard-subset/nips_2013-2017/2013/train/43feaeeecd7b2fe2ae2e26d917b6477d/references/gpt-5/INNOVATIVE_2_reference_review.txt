Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the classical auto-Poisson/PGM construction (Besag; Yang et al.) is only normalizable when all pairwise parameters are non-positive, hence can only model competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. The authors (i) prove an impossibility result showing that the widely suggested Winsorized (hard-capped) Poisson node-conditionals (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot be assembled into a consistent joint MRF; (ii) introduce a different, exponential-family-preserving truncation that yields a valid joint MRF on {0,…,R}^p (TPGM) without sign constraints on edges; (iii) show a necessity result: if one insists on linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x and wants normalizability with positive edges, the univariate base measure must grow at least quadratically, C(x)=Ω(x^2); (iv) propose the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses linear sufficient statistics with a quadratic base measure and is normalizable under a negative-definite quadratic form; and (v) propose the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which keeps the Poisson base measure log x! but replaces the sufficient statistic with a sublinear, saturating function B(x;R0,R), making pairwise interactions saturate at large counts and yielding normalizability for all real-valued pairwise parameters. They outline neighborhood selection for learning, provide variational partition-function bounds (Gaussian-like for QPGM; Poisson upper bound for SPGM when θ≥0), and present simulations and a miRNA case study, comparing favorably to Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The theoretical thread is clear and compelling: (1) impossibility of Winsorized Poisson conditionals forming a joint; (2) existence/consistency of TPGM; (3) necessity of quadratic base measure for linear stats to permit positive edges; (4) sufficient conditions for QPGM normalizability; (5) normalizability for SPGM with all pairwise parameters. These results are nontrivial and address a core barrier in count-MRFs. I did not see full proofs in the main text; assuming they are in the supplement, please include proof sketches for Theorems 1–4 to improve readability.
- Algorithms/estimation: Node-wise ℓ1-penalized conditional likelihood with proximal gradient is reasonable. For QPGM, the Gaussian upper bound is a neat variational device; for SPGM, the Poisson bound applies only for θ≥0—please clarify how optimization behaves when θ<0, and what truncation/approximation is used for the infinite sums in practice. Discuss convexity of the node-conditional problems and convergence criteria.
- Experiments: Simulations (n=50–200, p=50–100) and a sizable TCGA miRNA network (n=544,p=262) demonstrate feasibility and some advantages over Gaussian/copula methods. However, comparisons to alternative count models (e.g., Poisson log-normal, negative binomial-based models, or latent Gaussian copula count models) are missing; at least a discussion is warranted. Also report runtime, sensitivity to R and (R0,R), and ablations on variational approximations.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The motivation for each construction is well tied to the preceding theorem. Suggestions:
- Provide intuition/proof sketches for Theorem 1 (why Winsorization breaks exponential-family compatibility) and Theorem 3 (why base measure must dominate quadratic interactions).
- For QPGM, clearly state the relationship between “pointwise negative” pairwise parameters vs negative definiteness of the full quadratic form, and give examples illustrating allowed positive edges.
- For SPGM, include the exact piecewise definition of B(x;R0,R) in the main text with a small figure; give guidance on choosing R0 and R from data.
- Clarify how TPGM behaves as R grows and how to avoid degeneracy when many counts are near R.

Originality
High. The impossibility result for Winsorized Poisson MRFs, the necessity result on base-measure growth for linear sufficient statistics, and the concrete QPGM/SPGM constructions that allow positive conditional dependencies are new and important conceptual advances. This goes well beyond incremental tweaks and addresses a fundamental modeling gap for multivariate count data.

Significance
High. Having practically usable MRFs for counts with both positive and negative conditional dependencies is valuable across genomics, neuroscience, spatial statistics, and text analysis. The proposed families (especially SPGM) open a new design space: controlling tails and dependence through sufficient statistics and base measure. I expect others to build on these ideas for improved inference, priors, and scalable learning.

Pros
- Resolves a core deficiency of classical PGMs; enables positive conditional dependencies.
- Clear theoretical contributions: impossibility, necessity, and normalizability results.
- Three concrete model families with distinct trade-offs (TPGM/QPGM/SPGM).
- Practical learning path and promising empirical results, including a real genomic network.

Cons
- Limited comparisons to other modern count models (e.g., Poisson log-normal, negative binomial).
- Estimation details and guarantees (sparsistency, finite-sample behavior) left for future work.
- SPGM partition function handling for θ<0 and numerical stability not fully clarified.
- TPGM’s dependence on truncation R and potential bias near the cap need deeper analysis.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide proof sketches for Theorems 1 and 3 in the main paper?
2) How are R and (R0,R) selected in practice? Any data-driven heuristics or cross-validation strategies?
3) For SPGM, how do you handle θ<0 in the partition function bound and optimization?
4) Please compare (or at least discuss) against Poisson log-normal and negative-binomial-based graphical models.
5) What are the computational costs and convergence behavior for node-wise estimation on p~1k?

Minor
- Clarify whether TPGM sets R to the observed max (risk of overfitting) or a pre-specified cap.
- Consider releasing code for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold and novel paper that advances the theory and practice of graphical models for count data by introducing principled constructions that admit both positive and negative conditional dependencies. While empirical and algorithmic aspects could be strengthened, the conceptual contributions are significant and likely to spur follow-up work.

Scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 7/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10. Reviewer confidence: 0.8.
