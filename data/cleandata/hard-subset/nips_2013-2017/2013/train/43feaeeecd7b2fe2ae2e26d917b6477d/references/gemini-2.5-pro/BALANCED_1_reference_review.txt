### Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs), namely their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints that require non-positive edge weights. The authors make several key contributions. First, they provide a formal disproof (Theorem 1) of a previously proposed solution based on Winsorized Poisson conditionals, showing that this approach cannot lead to a consistent joint distribution. Second, they propose three novel graphical models for count data that overcome this limitation. The Truncated PGM (TPGM) uses a correctly formulated truncated Poisson conditional that remains in the exponential family, allowing for arbitrary edge weights but requiring a bounded domain. The Quadratic PGM (QPGM) modifies the base measure of the Poisson distribution to be quadratic, which (as shown in Theorem 3) is a necessary condition for normalizability with positive weights and linear sufficient statistics over an infinite domain. Finally, the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, allowing for the original `log(X!)` base measure (and thus heavier tails than the QPGM) while ensuring normalizability for all real-valued edge weights. The authors demonstrate the utility of TPGM and SPGM for network recovery on both simulated and real-world miRNA sequencing data, showing improved performance over Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known, critical, and long-standing problem in the modeling of multivariate count data. The inability of standard PGMs to capture positive ("cooperative") dependencies has severely limited their practical application. This work provides a comprehensive and theoretically sound resolution to this issue, proposing not one but three distinct and well-motivated models. This is a significant advance that makes graphical modeling for count data far more applicable to real-world problems in fields like genomics, neuroscience, and social sciences.

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The theoretical contributions are of high quality. The disproof of the Winsorized PGM approach (Theorem 1) is a valuable and definitive negative result that clarifies a misconception in the literature. The core theoretical results (Theorems 2, 3, and 4) that establish the validity and properties of the proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are sound. In particular, Theorem 3, which establishes the necessity of a quadratic-or-faster scaling base measure for models with linear statistics, provides a deep and fundamental insight into the problem.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction. The authors then logically progress from identifying the failure of prior work to developing their own solutions. Crucially, they are transparent about the limitations of each proposed model (e.g., the bounded domain of TPGM, the thin tails of QPGM), using these limitations to motivate the next, more general model. This logical flow makes the paper easy to follow and highlights the thoughtfulness of the research process.

4.  **Originality:** The proposed models are highly novel. The TPGM presents a correct way to use truncation, while the QPGM and SPGM introduce fundamental modifications to the structure of the Poisson density (base measure and sufficient statistics, respectively) to achieve the desired properties. This approach of redesigning the underlying univariate distribution to enable a valid multivariate graphical model is creative and powerful.

5.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section, though concise, effectively demonstrates the practical value of the proposed methods. The simulation studies show clear performance gains over established methods like Gaussian graphical models on data that is not well-suited for them. The application to miRNA expression data is particularly compelling, as it shows that the new models can uncover biologically plausible and important network structures (e.g., known cancer-related miRNA hubs and clusters) that are missed by both the original PGM and GGM approaches.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Absence of QPGM in Experiments:** The QPGM is a central part of the paper's theoretical narrative, bridging the gap between the limited TPGM and the more flexible SPGM. However, it is conspicuously absent from the numerical experiments. Including the QPGM in the comparisons would have been valuable to (a) empirically validate its "Gaussian-esque" behavior and compare its performance directly to GGMs, and (b) provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between the three proposed models. The authors should consider adding these results or, if there is a specific reason for its omission (e.g., computational difficulty), briefly discuss it.

2.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The proposed TPGM and SPGM models introduce new hyperparameters (`R`, `R0`). In the experiments, these are set based on heuristics (e.g., `R` = max count). While this is a reasonable starting point, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion on the sensitivity of the models to these choices and guidance on how to set them in practice. A brief discussion of using cross-validation or other data-driven methods for hyperparameter tuning would be beneficial for practitioners seeking to apply these models.

3.  **Details on Estimation Procedure:** The paper mentions using neighborhood selection with proximal gradient ascent to learn the models. While this is a standard approach, a few more details would improve reproducibility. For instance, there is no mention of the computational cost. Do the log-partition functions of the new conditional distributions (especially SPGM, which involves an infinite sum) pose computational challenges during optimization? A brief analysis or comment on the computational complexity relative to baselines would be helpful.

4.  **Theoretical Guarantees for Estimation:** The authors state that they expect sparsistency analysis to follow from the framework of Yang et al. [18, 19] but leave this for future work. While a full theoretical treatment is likely beyond the scope of a single conference paper, the claim could be strengthened. Even a high-level sketch of the argument, perhaps discussing how the proposed variational bounds on the log-partition functions (Propositions 2 and 3) could be used to satisfy the conditions required for existing theoretical guarantees, would add confidence in the proposed estimation procedure.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental and significant contribution to the literature on graphical models. It definitively solves a major limitation of Poisson graphical models, providing a suite of novel, well-motivated, and theoretically-grounded models for multivariate count data. The paper is exceptionally clear, rigorous, and demonstrates strong empirical performance. The identified weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to providing additional experimental details and discussion rather than any core flaw in the methodology or theory. This paper is a clear and strong candidate for acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, long-standing problem in the field.
*   Presents novel and well-justified models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
*   Strong and rigorous theoretical contributions, including an important negative result.
*   Exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   Convincing empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation of one of the three proposed models (QPGM) is missing.
*   Discussion on hyperparameter selection and computational aspects could be more detailed.
