## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to model negative dependencies between count variables. The authors demonstrate the failure of a common proposed fix (Winsorization) and then introduce three novel extensions: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models progressively relax the constraints on parameter space and offer increasingly flexible ways to capture both positive and negative dependencies in multivariate count data. The paper also demonstrates the applicability of these models for network learning through neighborhood selection, and showcases them on both simulated data and a real-world breast cancer miRNA expression dataset.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the foundational work of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19] defining PGMs. It acknowledges and critiques attempts to extend PGMs via truncation (Kaiser & Cressie [20], Griffith [21]), correctly identifying the inherent inconsistency in their approach. The work connects to broader research on graphical models for count data, referencing log-linear models [11], copula approaches [12], and hierarchical models [16]. The comparison to Gaussian Graphical Models [1, 2, 3, 4] and recent developments like non-paranormal estimation [7, 8, 9, 10] is valuable for contextualizing the contributions.  The case study leverages established tools for analyzing RNA-seq data [22, 23, 24].

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a core limitation:**  The paper tackles a significant issue with the standard PGM, namely the inability to model negative correlations. This is a crucial limitation for many real-world applications.
* **Rigorous theoretical development:** The mathematical derivations, particularly regarding the normalizability conditions and the development of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM, are well-presented and convincing. The theorems and proofs add substantial value.
* **Novelty and Creativity:**  The introduction of quadratic and sublinear base measures is a clever and original approach to overcoming the limitations of the standard PGM. The SPGM particularly offers an intriguing path towards capturing complex dependencies.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation through simulations and a case study. Demonstrating performance on a real dataset using relevant biological data adds credibility.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts. The organization flows logically from problem statement to proposed solutions and evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:**  While the paper mentions neighborhood selection, it doesn’t delve into the computational challenges associated with estimating the parameters of the QPGM and SPGM, particularly for high-dimensional data.  The log-partition function for the SPGM remains intractable, relying on a Gaussian approximation. A more in-depth discussion of these computational aspects would be valuable.
* **Limited Exploration of SPGM:**  The SPGM, arguably the most innovative contribution, receives comparatively less detailed analysis and empirical exploration than the TPGM and QPGM. A deeper investigation of its properties and performance characteristics is warranted.
* **Practical Considerations of Truncation:** The TPGM is a good initial step but the choice of R and its impact on the model’s behavior (as discussed in the paper) is a critical practical consideration. A sensitivity analysis of results to different R values would strengthen the presentation.
* **Connection to Other Count Data Models:** While the paper references other approaches to modeling count data, it could benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the proposed models compare to alternatives like hurdle models or zero-inflated models, particularly regarding their strengths and weaknesses.
* **Limited Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper mentions that the sparsistency of the proposed methods will be discussed in future work. Given the significance of these models, a discussion of theoretical guarantees would greatly increase the impact of the paper.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  The paper addresses a fundamental problem in modeling multivariate count data.
*  The proposed solutions are novel, theoretically sound, and demonstrate promising empirical performance.
*  The work has the potential to significantly impact research areas dealing with count data, such as genomics, epidemiology, and spatial statistics.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational challenges of estimating the parameters of the QPGM and SPGM could limit their practical applicability.
*  The relatively limited exploration of the SPGM leaves some questions unanswered.
* The lack of theoretical guarantees regarding model consistency or sparsity is a concern.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The authors identify a crucial limitation of existing PGMs and propose innovative solutions with strong theoretical foundations and promising empirical results. While some computational and theoretical aspects could be further strengthened, the novelty and potential impact of the work justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions)

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   Expand the discussion of computational complexity and potential optimization strategies for the QPGM and SPGM.
*   Provide a more detailed empirical evaluation of the SPGM, including sensitivity analysis of its parameters.
*   Include a brief discussion of the trade-offs between the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM in terms of computational cost, model flexibility, and interpretability.
*   Briefly address the connection between the proposed methods and other relevant count data models (e.g., hurdle, zero-inflated).
*   Add a discussion of theoretical guarantees in the conclusions.
