Summary
This paper tackles the well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for counts—namely, the need for nonpositive pairwise parameters to ensure normalizability—by (i) debunking Winsorized Poisson conditionals as a route to a proper joint, (ii) proposing a truncated exponential-family version (TPGM) that admits both positive and negative dependencies on a bounded support, (iii) arguing that with linear sufficient statistics one needs at least quadratic base measure growth to allow positive dependencies (leading to a Quadratic PGM, QPGM), and (iv) proposing a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that modifies the sufficient statistic to saturate beyond a threshold, ensuring normalizability with the original log-factorial base measure. They sketch variational upper bounds for nodewise learning and present simulations and a microRNA network example, comparing against Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Besag’s auto-models and the generalized exponential-family conditional-to-joint construction (Yang et al.). It engages with Winsorized Poisson proposals (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) and compares to Gaussian/copula-based graph learning. However, some relevant count-network baselines (e.g., Poisson log-normal networks, negative binomial/overdispersed count MRFs, Conway–Maxwell–Poisson variants, and more recent copula count models) are not discussed or compared.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: clearly identifies why normalizability forces negative pairwise parameters in the classic PGM and explores three orthogonal fixes (finite support, stronger base measure, sublinear sufficient statistics).
- Theoretical contributions: Theorem 1 (incompatibility of Winsorized Poisson conditionals), Theorem 2 (existence/uniqueness of TPGM joint), Theorem 3 (necessity of Ω(z^2) base measure with linear sufficient statistics to allow positive edges), Theorem 4 (SPGM normalizability for all pairwise parameters). These are interesting and potentially impactful.
- Practical framing: nodewise L1-penalized conditional likelihood is a standard and scalable route; the variational bounds to connect QPGM to Gaussian nodewise regressions are intuitive.
- Empirical indication: TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods on count data in the reported ROC curves; the breast cancer microRNA case study highlights plausible biological hubs missed by Gaussian methods.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Theorem 1 (Winsorized conditionals incompatible): This is a strong and important negative result. However, no proof sketch is provided, and the statement allows E(XV\s) to be “an arbitrary function.” Please specify regularity/positivity assumptions and whether your result depends on R > 3 in an essential way. Given prior claims in [20,21], a clear counterexample/compatibility argument is crucial.
- Sign/notation inconsistencies in exponential-family forms: In Sec. 3 you write P(Z)=exp(θB(Z)−C(Z)−D(θ)) but in (4) use +C(Xs). Earlier (1) uses −log(Xs!). You later state the Poisson base measure is C(Z)=log(Z!), which would correspond to a minus sign. Please fix signs and keep a consistent convention throughout; as written it’s confusing and risks errors.
- QPGM: Proposition 2 states normalizability if there exists cθ>0 such that XTΘX ≤ −cθ||X||^2 for all X. This requires Θ ≺ 0; the statement “pairwise parameters are pointwise negative: Θ<0” (entrywise negativity) is neither necessary nor sufficient for negative definiteness; please clarify precisely. Also, the role of linear terms θs is ignored in the condition—state assumptions clearly.
- Theorem 3 (necessity of Ω(z^2) base measure): This is an important necessity claim; please formalize the assumptions (dimensionality fixed? what does “θ not ≤ 0” mean precisely? does the necessity allow negative diagonal quadratic terms in addition to base measure?) and provide a proof sketch. Given that log(Z!) ~ Z log Z is subquadratic, the result is plausible but needs rigor.
- SPGM: B(x;R0,R) saturates for x≥R; please state whether θ≥0 is required anywhere beyond the variational bound (Prop. 3). Theorem 4 claims normalizability “for all pairwise parameters θst ∈ R”; make explicit assumptions on θs and R0,R and provide a proof outline (the argument hinges on bounded sufficient statistics and the log-factorial tail).
- Learning for SPGM and QPGM: For SPGM, the nodewise log-partition D(θ,R0,R) is an infinite sum; the paper provides only an upper bound (DPois) and no detail on how gradients/normalizers are computed in practice (truncation? importance sampling? dynamic programming?). For QPGM, you provide a Gaussian variational upper bound but no concrete algorithm, objective, or convergence guarantees. As presented, reproducibility is limited.
- Identifiability/consistency: The paper explicitly defers sparsistency and does not provide estimation error or model selection guarantees; this significantly limits the contribution as a method paper.

Experimental issues
- Baselines: Comparisons omit relevant count-graphical alternatives (e.g., Poisson log-normal networks, negative binomial MRFs). Comparing only to Gaussian/nonparanormal risks overstating gains.
- Data generation: For the “sums of independent Poissons” baseline, that model does not induce an MRF with interpretable pairwise edge signs; interpreting this as “only edges with positive weights” is not apples-to-apples.
- TPGM parameter R: In simulations “R set to the maximum count value” biases the model to the observed sample maxima; sensitivity to R is not explored, though you note nontrivial tradeoffs. For the case study, R=11 as “the maximum count” seems implausibly small for RNA-seq; please clarify preprocessing (filtering, normalization) and justify R and R0 choices (no sensitivity analysis is shown).
- Sequencing offsets/normalization: For RNA-seq, library size/technical factors are critical; the paper mentions preprocessing per [24] but does not detail whether offsets/normalizations are incorporated into the nodewise GLM-like conditionals. Without this, inferred edges may reflect depth effects.
- Quantitative reporting: ROC curves are referenced but not shown; no AUCs or variances are reported; the original PGM baseline is claimed inferior but not shown. No empirical results are presented for QPGM.

Clarity and presentation
- Many typos/notation issues: e.g., XV\s vs X\V s; “SGPM” vs SPGM; undefined W and Wp; “form count-valued”; mismatched parentheses and summation limits (e.g., Ψ(ηs) definition); Eq. in 2.1.2 missing an explicit division; inconsistent use of bold vs plain symbols. Please proofread thoroughly.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), making it hard to verify claims. Several citations (e.g., [1–5], [8–10]) need full bibliographic details.

Originality and significance
- The idea to ensure normalizability by saturating sufficient statistics (SPGM) and by strengthening base measures (QPGM) is novel in this context and potentially impactful. TPGM is a natural finite-support analogue. However, without solid inference methodology and theory (and with limited, somewhat unbalanced experiments), the practical significance is not yet established.

Pros
- Addresses an important gap: modeling positive dependencies in count MRFs.
- Interesting theoretical insights (Theorems 1–4).
- Simple TPGM conditional enabling tractable learning on finite support.

Cons
- Inconsistent/unclear derivations and notational errors.
- Missing proofs or proof sketches for key claims.
- Incomplete inference details (especially for SPGM/QPGM).
- Limited and arguably unbalanced empirical evaluation; missing key baselines and ablations.
- Bibliographic and formatting issues.

Questions for authors
1. Provide a clear proof sketch or counterexample underpinning Theorem 1 and specify assumptions.
2. Formalize Theorem 3 with precise conditions; is negative definiteness of a learned quadratic term θss an alternative to a quadratic base measure?
3. How do you compute/approximate D(θ,R0,R) and its gradients for SPGM in practice?
4. How are R and R0 chosen? Please include sensitivity analyses.
5. How did you handle RNA-seq normalization/offsets? Why is R=11 appropriate?
6. Provide empirical results for QPGM or clarify it as a conceptual model only.

Recommendation: Reject
The paper presents promising ideas but suffers from numerous technical, methodological, and presentation issues that, taken together, undermine confidence and reproducibility. With rigorous proofs, clarified notation, complete inference algorithms (with guarantees), stronger experiments (including relevant baselines and sensitivity analyses), and polished writing/citations, this could become a strong submission.
