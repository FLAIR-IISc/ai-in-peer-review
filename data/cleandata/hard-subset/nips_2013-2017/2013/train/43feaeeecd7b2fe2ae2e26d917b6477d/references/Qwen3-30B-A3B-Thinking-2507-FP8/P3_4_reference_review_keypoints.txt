- Addresses the lack of graphical models for multivariate count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Demonstrates the Winsorized Poisson approach is invalid.  
- Proposes Truncated PGM (TPGM) to allow positive and negative dependencies on a bounded domain.  
- Introduces Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that maintain normalizability while overcoming TPGM limits.  

- Theorems provide rigorous proofs of invalidity of prior work and normalizability of proposed models.  
- Strong connection to exponential family theory with careful base measure and sufficient statistic analysis.  
- Clearly articulates why existing approaches (Gaussian, Ising, multinomial, sum-of-Poissons) are inadequate for count data.  
- Case study on breast cancer miRNA networks shows practical advantages, identifying biologically relevant hubs missed by Gaussian models.  

- TPGM requires choosing a truncation level R, with no practical guidance for selection on finite datasets.  
- QPGM has a thin (Gaussian-like) tail that may poorly model heavy-tailed count data; practical impact not evaluated.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited: comparisons only to Gaussian methods and sparse biological validation of findings.  
- Paper lacks details on computational complexity and scalability (e.g., for p≈1000) of neighborhood selection and proposed models.  
- Does not address zero-inflation common in count data (important in genomics).  

- Makes a substantial and original theoretical contribution by enabling valid PGMs with both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Theorem 3’s insight on base measure scaling and normalizability advances understanding beyond Gaussian cases.  

- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, significant advance with clear practical implications.  

- Minor suggestions: discuss methods to select R for TPGM, explore connections between SPGM and zero-inflated models, and provide computational complexity analysis.
