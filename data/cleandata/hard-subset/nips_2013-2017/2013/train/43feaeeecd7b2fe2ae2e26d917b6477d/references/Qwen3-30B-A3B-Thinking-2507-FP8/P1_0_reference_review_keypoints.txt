- Paper identifies and corrects a key limitation in Poisson graphical models and fixes an incorrect prior claim by Kaiser and Cressie.  
- Proposes a Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) that allows positive and negative dependencies by bounding the domain.  
- Introduces Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) with quadratic base measure enabling mixed-sign edges.  
- Introduces Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) with sub-linear sufficient statistics that permits all real edge weights and has heavier tails than QPGM.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical proofs (Theorems 1–4) establishing normalizability and conditions for positive edge weights.  
- Thorough analysis of mathematical constraints enabling positive dependencies, addressing a longstanding limitation.  
- Strong experimental validation via simulations showing advantages over existing methods.  
- Real-world breast cancer microRNA case study demonstrating practical utility and identifying biologically meaningful hubs.  
- Balanced discussion of limitations for each model (e.g., TPGM domain bounds, QPGM tail behavior).  
- Lacks detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability for high-dimensional problems.  
- Limited empirical comparison to more recent count-specific methods (e.g., negative binomial graphical models).  
- SPGM lacks practical implementation details and discussion of computational impact.  
- No theoretical guarantees provided for learning procedures (consistency or sparsistency of neighborhood selection).  
- Quality: technically sound with rigorous theory and appropriate experiments.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized, though some complex math could use more intuition.  
- Originality: significant and novel contributions, especially the SPGM and correction of prior work.  
- Significance: addresses an important problem for multivariate count data with immediate practical relevance.  
- Recommendation: overall recommendation to accept, citing theoretical contributions, empirical validation, and real-world utility.  
- Main concerns against acceptance: computational/scalability discussion, comparisons to recent count-methods, and missing learning-theory guarantees.
