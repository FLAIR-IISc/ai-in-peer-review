This paper tackles the challenging problem of developing graphical models for multivariate count data, a common scenario in many modern applications. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches, including the non-negativity constraint in traditional Poisson graphical models and the intractability of other multivariate Poisson distributions. They propose a series of novel extensions – the Truncated PGM, Quadratic PGM, and Sublinear PGM – aiming to overcome these limitations. The mathematical development appears generally sound, and the paper is well-structured, progressing logically from identifying the problem to proposing solutions and illustrating their use.

However, despite the thoroughness of the mathematical treatment, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling empirical validation. While simulation studies are presented, they feel somewhat limited in scope and lack detailed comparisons to established count data modeling techniques *beyond* Gaussian graphical models, which are not ideally suited for this data type. The case study, while using a real dataset, relies heavily on comparing against the original PGM estimator, which the authors themselves establish as flawed. The claim of superiority over Gaussian graphical models is predictable given the data type, and doesn't strongly justify the increased complexity of the proposed models. Furthermore, the discussion of computational tractability is glossed over; the authors mention using a proximal gradient ascent, but offer little detail on scalability or computational cost, which are critical for high-dimensional applications.

A major concern is the somewhat convoluted justification for the more complex models (QPGM and SPGM). The core argument hinges on the need to address the normalizability issue and allow for both positive and negative dependencies. However, the truncation approach (TPGM) is dismissed somewhat quickly despite offering a more straightforward path toward addressing the normalizability issue. The leap to quadratic and sublinear base measures feels driven by mathematical convenience rather than a clear signal from the data or a fundamental limitation of the truncated approach. The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, feels disconnected from practical considerations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Modeling multivariate count data is a significant challenge.
*   **Thorough mathematical development:** The derivations and proofs are generally clear and well-presented.
*   **Identifies limitations of existing approaches:** The authors correctly highlight the drawbacks of existing methods.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent a novel contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited empirical validation:** The simulation studies are not comprehensive, and the case study lacks a robust comparison to relevant baselines beyond the flawed original PGM.
*   **Convoluted justification for QPGM/SPGM:** The motivation for moving beyond the truncation approach is not entirely convincing.
*   **Lack of discussion on computational complexity:** Scalability and computational cost are not adequately addressed.
*   **Overreliance on theoretical results:** The paper is heavily focused on mathematical derivations with less emphasis on practical considerations.
*   **The variational upper bound used for SPGM may be too loose for practical applications.**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces novel graphical models for count data, filling a gap in the existing literature.
*   The mathematical analysis is rigorous and well-executed.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims of superiority.
*   The justification for the more complex models is not entirely convincing.
*   The lack of discussion on computational complexity raises concerns about the practicality of the proposed methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **reject** with a request for major revisions. The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but it falls short of demonstrating practical significance. The authors need to significantly strengthen the empirical validation, provide a more compelling justification for the QPGM and SPGM, and address the computational complexity issues. A more thorough comparison to existing count data modeling techniques (e.g., negative binomial graphical models, latent variable models) is essential. Simply showing improvement over a known-flawed baseline isn’t enough. The paper’s focus feels too theoretical; a greater emphasis on practical applicability is needed before it could be considered a strong NIPS submission. I find the work interesting, but ultimately not substantial enough in its current form to warrant acceptance.
