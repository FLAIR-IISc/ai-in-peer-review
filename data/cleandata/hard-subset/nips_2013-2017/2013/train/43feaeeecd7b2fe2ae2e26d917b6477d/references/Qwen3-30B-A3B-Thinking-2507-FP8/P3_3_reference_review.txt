

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical models for count data. The authors identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only represent negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints, which severely limits their applicability. They critically examine the Winsorized Poisson approach of Kaiser and Cressie [20], proving it cannot yield a consistent joint distribution (Theorem 1). The paper then proposes three novel approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each addressing the core limitation while enabling both positive and negative dependencies.

**Strengths:**
1. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper makes important theoretical contributions, particularly Theorem 3 establishing that a quadratic base measure is necessary for normalizability with linear sufficient statistics. This insight fundamentally clarifies the constraints of Poisson-based graphical models.
2. **Clear problem statement:** The authors effectively motivate the need for count-data graphical models with concrete examples from genomics, spatial statistics, and climate studies.
3. **Novel contributions:** TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuinely new approaches that solve the core problem identified. The SPGM in particular offers the most flexible solution with heavier tails than QPGM.
4. **Empirical validation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate advantages over Gaussian-based methods on count data, with the breast cancer microRNA analysis showing biologically meaningful results (e.g., identifying known cancer-related miRNAs like mir-375 and mir-10b).
5. **Critical assessment:** The authors carefully acknowledge limitations of each approach (e.g., TPGM's bounded domain, QPGM's Gaussian-like tail) rather than presenting an overly optimistic picture.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Computational considerations:** The paper doesn't address computational complexity, particularly for SPGM where the log-partition function involves infinite summation. This is a significant practical concern for high-dimensional applications.
2. **Model selection:** The paper mentions choosing R for TPGM and R0/R for SPGM but doesn't provide guidance on how to select these parameters in practice, which is crucial for real-world application.
3. **Learning guarantees:** While the authors mention sparsistency analysis is "left for future work," this is a critical gap since the paper's main contribution is a model for learning networks from count data. Without theoretical guarantees on network recovery, the practical utility is limited.
4. **Comparison to related work:** The paper could better contextualize its contributions against recent non-parametric extensions [7-10] and hierarchical models [16]. The relationship between SPGM and copula-based approaches deserves more discussion.

**Quality Assessment:**
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical proofs and well-designed experiments. The claims are well-supported by both theory and empirical results. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, including the fact that the sparsistency analysis is left for future work. The paper represents a complete contribution to the field, not merely a position paper.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, theoretical development, and empirical results flow logically. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly explained. The comparison to previous work is particularly clear, especially the critique of Winsorized Poisson.

**Originality:**
The core insight about the quadratic base measure requirement (Theorem 3) is novel and directly addresses a fundamental constraint in the literature. The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are original contributions that collectively solve the problem of modeling both positive and negative dependencies in count data. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from [17-19] and [20-21].

**Significance:**
This work addresses an important problem with broad applicability in genomics, spatial statistics, and other fields where count data is prevalent. The models enable new analyses of count-valued networks that were previously intractable. The breast cancer microRNA example demonstrates clear practical value, identifying biologically relevant connections missed by existing methods.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models. While the computational aspects and model selection guidance could be strengthened, the core theoretical advances are substantial and well-justified. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in modeling multivariate count data.

The authors should address the computational complexity of SPGM in future work, provide more guidance on parameter selection, and discuss how their approach relates to recent non-parametric graphical models. These are minor issues given the paper's substantial theoretical contribution.

**Final Recommendation:** Accept (Strong Accept)

The paper makes a clear, important contribution to the field by resolving a fundamental limitation in Poisson graphical models. The theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, and the empirical results demonstrate practical value. This work will be of significant interest to researchers working with count data in high-dimensional settings.
