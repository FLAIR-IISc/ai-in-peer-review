- Shows Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals are not jointly consistent with a Besag/Yang MRF (Theorem 1).
- Proposes Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) on finite support {0,…,R} that permits positive and negative dependencies (Theorem 2).
- Proves necessity of quadratic-order base measure for linear sufficient statistics to allow both-sign dependencies, motivating Quadratic PGM (QPGM) (Theorem 3).
- Introduces QPGM with negative-definite quadratic-form conditions for normalizability (Proposition 2).
- Proposes Sublinear PGM (SPGM) replacing X with saturating B(x;R0,R) to bound interactions and allow all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4).
- Provides variational upper bounds for node-conditional partition functions: Gaussian integral for QPGM and Poisson bound for SPGM when θ ≥ 0.
- Learns graphs via ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihood (neighborhood selection) with proximal gradients.
- Empirical results: TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines on simulated graphs and TCGA miRNA counts, recovering plausible biological structure.
- Notes original PGM recovers sparse, mostly negative edges as expected.
- Theory judged technically sound: compatibility result, TPGM construction, necessity argument, and normalizability conditions are well-motivated.
- Missing practical details: computational complexity, convergence, sensitivity and selection of R and R0, and handling counts exceeding R.
- No sparsistency or other learning-theory guarantees provided (deferred to future work).
- Synthetic validation lacks data generated from QPGM/SPGM to test in-model recovery.
- Clarity generally good but has notational slips, typos, incomplete references, and needs more guidance on truncation parameter choice.
- Originality high: novel incompatibility result and new model classes enabling positive dependencies in count MRFs.
- Significance: addresses a long-standing limitation in count PGMs with applications in genomics and spatial stats; could replace ad hoc alternatives.
- Strengths: clear problem identification, rigorous negative result, three constructive model families, variational tools, and supportive empirical evidence.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: add learning theory sketches, analyze computational/truncation tradeoffs and error bounds, include more comparators (e.g., Poisson log-normal/copula), and test recovery on QPGM/SPGM-generated data.
- Pros for acceptance: strong theory, principled models enabling positive edges, and relevant empirical gains.
- Cons for acceptance: missing learning guarantees, thin algorithmic/practical details, and sensitivity to truncation parameters.
- Recommendation: weak accept to accept, contingent on clarifying practical issues and expanding experimental/parameter-selection guidance.
