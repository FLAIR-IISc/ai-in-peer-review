Summary
This paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the classical auto-Poisson MRF (Besag; Yang et al.) is only normalizable when all pairwise parameters are non-positive, preventing positive conditional dependencies. The authors make three main contributions. First, they show that “Winsorized” (hard-truncated-at-R) Poisson node-conditionals do not admit a consistent joint MRF of the Besag/Yang form (Theorem 1), correcting claims in prior spatial statistics work (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith). Second, they propose a valid truncated Poisson graphical model (TPGM) that retains an exponential-family node-conditional with a finite state-space; this yields a proper joint MRF (Theorem 2) and permits both positive and negative conditional dependencies, at the cost of a fixed finite support. Third, they move beyond truncation and study what changes to the univariate exponential family (sufficient statistics and base measure) are required to support mixed-sign dependencies while ensuring normalizability. They prove that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, the base measure must grow at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), adding quadratic terms with a negative-definite quadratic form (Proposition 2), and, to avoid Gaussian-like thin tails, the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that uses a piecewise-linear, saturating sufficient statistic B(x;R0,R), recovering heavy-tail behavior and normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They outline nodewise neighborhood selection with ℓ1-penalized conditional likelihoods and provide simulations and a case study on TCGA breast cancer miRNA sequencing data, showing improved structure recovery over Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on the conditional specification framework of Besag and its modern generalization to exponential families on graphs (Yang et al., NIPS and follow-ups). It contextualizes earlier multivariate Poisson constructions (Holgate; Karlis; sums of independent Poissons) and spatial statistics practice. The correction of the Winsorization claim is useful to the community. A potentially relevant omission is the Conway–Maxwell–Poisson (COM-Poisson) family and Poisson log-normal/correlated latent Gaussian approaches, which provide alternative ways to model over/under-dispersion and positive dependence but have different normalization and tractability properties; discussing why those do or do not yield consistent MRFs would strengthen positioning.

Quality
– Theory: The impossibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1) is important; a clear proof sketch would help the reader assess the key obstruction (loss of exponential-family structure via the “mass pile-up” at R). The TPGM consistency (Theorem 2) follows naturally from the exponential-family node-conditional construction on a finite state-space. The necessity of quadratic growth in the base measure (Theorem 3) is a central and insightful result; the intuition (pairwise terms scale quadratically while log z! grows subquadratically) is compelling, but the formal conditions/assumptions (e.g., mixed-sign edge parameters and unbounded support) should be stated precisely. The QPGM normalizability condition (Prop. 2) is standard via a negative-definite quadratic form. The SPGM normalizability for all θst leverages boundedness of B(·); this is convincing. The variational bounds for nodewise learning (Gaussian bound for QPGM; Poisson bound for SPGM when θ≥0) are helpful but leave open how optimization is performed when θ<0 in SPGM.
– Algorithms: The paper sketches ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection with proximal gradient, but computational details are sparse (step sizes, convergence, handling of intractable log-partition for SPGM, screening, runtime).
– Experiments: Simulations compare against GGM and nonparanormal variants; TPGM/SPGM dominate on count data, as expected. However, baselines specific to count data (e.g., Poisson log-normal graphical models, negative binomial copula-based methods) are missing. For TPGM, R is set to the maximum observed count, which risks overfitting and degeneracy; sensitivity analyses for R (and for R0 in SPGM) are needed. ROC curves are averaged over 50 replicates, but details on graph generation, edge weights, and hyperparameters are thin; runtime and calibration analyses are absent. The TCGA case study is compelling qualitatively (biologically plausible hubs and clusters), but lacks quantitative validation (e.g., held-out predictive performance, stability metrics, concordance across methods, edge sign inspection).

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a coherent narrative from limitations, to truncation, to structural modifications. A few suggestions:
– Provide intuition and proof sketches for all main theorems in the main text; move technical proofs to the appendix.
– Clarify notation (e.g., W vs N0; base measure C vs log Z! when it changes; define all constants and domains).
– Explain how R and R0 are chosen in practice; give heuristics, diagnostics, or cross-validation strategies.
– Improve bibliography completeness and formatting; several entries lack venues/pages.
– Expand discussion on when QPGM behaves “Gaussian-esque” and its implications for modeling heavy counts.

Originality
The paper offers several novel and substantive contributions: the impossibility of Winsorized node-conditionals, a principled truncated exponential-family construction (TPGM), a necessary condition on base-measure growth for mixed-sign dependence (Theorem 3), and two new MRF classes (QPGM, SPGM) that broaden the feasible parameter space beyond classic auto-Poisson. While TPGM is conceptually straightforward, Theorem 3 and SPGM are particularly original and insightful.

Significance
Modeling multivariate count data with both positive and negative conditional dependencies is important in genomics, neuroscience, and spatial statistics. Theoretical results here clarify what is and isn’t possible and introduce practically usable alternatives. If accompanied by stronger empirical validation and implementation details, SPGM and QPGM could become standard tools. The impact would be higher with comparisons to state-of-the-art count graph models and with guidance on hyperparameter selection.

Pros
– Clear identification and correction of a widely cited but incorrect construction (Winsorization).
– Strong, insightful theory (especially Theorem 3) guiding model design.
– Introduction of two practically appealing models (QPGM, SPGM) allowing mixed-sign dependencies.
– Demonstrated advantages on synthetic and real count data over Gaussian/copula baselines.

Cons
– Limited algorithmic and computational details; incomplete treatment of SPGM learning when θ<0.
– Sensitivity of TPGM to truncation R; unclear choice/tuning of R and R0.
– Missing comparisons to modern count graph baselines (e.g., Poisson log-normal).
– Experimental reporting lacks quantitative validation beyond ROC curves; no runtime or ablation studies.
– Bibliography and some notation need polishing.

Recommendation
Borderline accept to accept. The conceptual advances and theoretical results are substantial and relevant to NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance contingent on improved experimental comparisons, clearer optimization details (especially for SPGM), and guidance on hyperparameter selection. Confidence: high.
