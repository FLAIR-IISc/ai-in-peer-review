This paper introduces novel graphical models for multivariate count data, addressing limitations of existing approaches like the Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) which can only model negative dependencies. The authors demonstrate that truncation approaches to address this limitation, such as those proposed by Kaiser & Cressie (20), are flawed. They then propose three new models: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each with increasing complexity and flexibility in modeling dependencies. The paper provides theoretical justification for each model and demonstrates their applicability through simulations and a case study on breast cancer microRNA expression data.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a logical progression of ideas. The mathematical derivations are generally clear, and the proofs appear rigorous. The authors identify a genuine flaw in existing truncation-based approaches (Kaiser & Cressie, 20) and build upon this to introduce novel solutions. The numerical experiments, while not exhaustive, provide initial evidence of the performance of the proposed models, particularly the SPGM, compared to existing methods. The choice of datasets for the case study is relevant, and the comparison to other techniques (Gaussian graphical models) is informative. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational challenges associated with estimating parameters for the QPGM and SPGM, especially in high dimensions. A more thorough theoretical analysis of the statistical properties (e.g., consistency, rates of convergence) of the estimators would also strengthen the paper. The claim of superiority of TPGM in certain scenarios should be better substantiated with more extensive simulations.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The mathematical notation is mostly consistent and understandable. However, some sections, particularly those introducing the QPGM and SPGM, are dense and require careful reading. Providing more intuitive explanations of the underlying ideas (e.g., why a quadratic base measure is needed) could improve clarity. The connection between the variational upper bound used for the QPGM and its practical implications could also be made more explicit. The figures are generally informative, but some (e.g., Figure 3) could benefit from clearer labeling and explanations.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The identification of the flaw in the Kaiser & Cressie (20) approach is a significant finding. The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM models represent genuinely new approaches to modeling multivariate count data with flexible dependency structures. The extension beyond simple truncation and modification of the underlying statistical properties of the Poisson distribution demonstrates a strong level of originality. The paper adequately references related work, including Besag (17), Yang et al. (18, 19), and recent advances in graphical model estimation (e.g., Liu et al. 7, 10).

**Significance:** The problem addressed in this paper is significant, as count data is increasingly common in many scientific disciplines. The limitations of existing models for count data motivate the need for new approaches. The proposed models have the potential to advance the state of the art in modeling multivariate count data and could have broad applications in fields such as genomics, epidemiology, and ecology. The demonstration of the models' performance on a real-world dataset (breast cancer miRNA expression) is a positive sign. The SPGM, in particular, seems promising due to its flexibility and ability to model both positive and negative dependencies. The models could be valuable tools for researchers and practitioners working with count data. Further investigation into the theoretical and computational properties of the proposed models, and their application to a wider range of datasets, would further enhance their impact.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant problem in statistical modeling.
*   Presents novel and technically sound solutions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
*   Demonstrates the flaw in existing truncation-based approaches.
*   Provides initial evidence of performance through simulations and a case study.
*   Clear and logical presentation of ideas.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity of parameter estimation is not thoroughly discussed.
*   Theoretical analysis of statistical properties is limited.
*   Simulation results could be more extensive and robust.
*   Some sections are dense and could benefit from clearer explanations.
*   Practical implications of variational upper bounds could be discussed.
