Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the autopoisson construction yields only non-positive pairwise parameters to ensure normalizability, thus modeling only competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. The authors make four main contributions.

1) They analyze truncation-based fixes. They show that the Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals (truncating all mass above R to the value R), suggested in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith), cannot be combined into a consistent joint distribution under the standard construction from exponential-family conditionals (Theorem 1). This clarifies and corrects a misconception in prior applied work.

2) They propose an alternative exponential-family truncation that preserves the EF structure by redistributing the “remnant” mass proportionally across {0,…,R}. They prove that these node-conditionals yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 2), which they term the Truncated PGM (TPGM). TPGM removes the sign constraint on edges but inherits practical issues as R grows (degeneracy near the truncation boundary and a return to negativity constraints as R→∞).

3) They consider more fundamental modifications via the EF template P(Z) ∝ exp(θB(Z) − C(Z)). They prove that if one retains linear sufficient statistics B(Z)=Z and wants normalizability with nonnegative edges, the base measure must grow at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with an added quadratic node term, equivalent to a negative-definite quadratic form over the counts, yielding normalizability with potentially mixed-sign interactions (Proposition 2). They further provide a Gaussian variational upper bound for node-wise regressions.

4) To obtain heavier tails and eliminate the need for quadratic base measures, they introduce the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that uses sublinear, saturating sufficient statistics B(Z;R0,R) (linear up to R0, then concave to a flat plateau), keeping the Poisson base measure. They prove normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4) and give a variational upper bound using the Poisson log-partition (Proposition 3).

They demonstrate these models using l1-penalized node-conditional likelihood (neighborhood selection) and compare against Gaussian graphical model (GGM) baselines and nonparanormal variants on synthetic data (hub and scale-free graphs) and a breast cancer miRNA count dataset. TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods and recover plausible biological hubs.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The central results are plausible and align with known behavior of autopoisson models: Theorem 1’s incompatibility of Winsorized conditionals is consistent with the need for EF structure in the node-conditional-to-joint construction. Theorem 3’s necessity of Ω(Z^2) base measure for linear sufficient statistics and mixed signs is well motivated by asymptotic growth arguments of pairwise terms versus base measure. QPGM normalizability under negative-definite quadratic forms is standard. SPGM normalizability for all edge signs is credible due to bounded interactions via saturating B(·). The variational bounds for QPGM and SPGM are reasonable, though for SPGM the stated bound requires θ≥0.
- Completeness: Several aspects would benefit from additional detail:
  - Proof sketches or key lemmas for Theorems 1 and 3 (especially conditions like R>3) to clarify boundary cases and assumptions.
  - Computational details: complexity of node-conditional normalization (finite sum for TPGM, infinite for SPGM), convergence behavior of proximal gradient, and practical truncation for SPGM’s infinite sums.
  - Statistical guarantees are deferred; while prior EF-MRF sparsistency results suggest extendability, a brief statement of conditions (incoherence, tail conditions for QPGM/SPGM) would strengthen the contribution.
- Empirics: Simulations show clear gains over Gaussian-based models on count data; however, comparisons omit other count-network baselines (e.g., Poisson log-normal or other latent-variable approaches) and do not include QPGM. Sensitivity to R and (R0,R), and robustness to overdispersion are not systematically evaluated.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear progression from diagnosis (autopoisson limitation) to fixes (TPGM) to principled redesigns (QPGM, SPGM). Notation is mostly consistent, though a few symbols (e.g., Wp, D̄, D) could be defined more explicitly at first use. It would help to:
- Provide intuition and a small example for Theorem 1 (why Winsorization breaks EF compatibility).
- Offer guidance for choosing R in TPGM and (R0,R) in SPGM, and discuss interpretability of parameters when B saturates.
- Clarify learning for QPGM (strength/limitations of the Gaussian variational bound in practice).

Originality
- The no-go result for Winsorized Poisson conditionals and the necessity result on quadratic base measures for linear sufficient statistics are novel and valuable clarifications.
- TPGM is a natural but useful EF-consistent truncation; while straightforward, formalizing and analyzing it in the MRF context is worthwhile.
- QPGM is a natural quadratic EF over counts; as a construction it resembles quadratic MRFs in continuous settings but its application to count domains and the normalizability conditions are appropriate.
- SPGM’s use of saturating sufficient statistics to allow arbitrary edge signs while retaining Poisson base measure is an interesting and creative design. It is closely related to other proposals that use sublinear or bounded sufficient statistics to achieve normalizability with mixed signs; comparison to closely related models (e.g., square-root or other sublinear EF graphical models) should be added.
- Related work: Beyond the cited copula and hierarchical approaches, the paper should discuss Poisson log-normal (latent Gaussian) models for counts and recent EF graphical models with sublinear transforms; a brief empirical or conceptual comparison would help position the contribution.

Significance
Addressing the autopoisson sign constraint is important for multivariate count modeling. The paper offers three concrete families with proofs of normalizability and a path to scalable structure learning via neighborhood selection. If coupled with stronger empirical validation and theoretical guarantees, these models could be broadly useful in applications with count data (genomics, text, spatial incidence). The present experiments are promising but limited in scope; adding stronger baselines and practical guidance would enhance impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear identification and correction of a misconception in prior applied work (Winsorized node-conditionals).
- Principled theoretical contributions (normalizability conditions; new model classes).
- Practical algorithms for structure learning and encouraging empirical results on synthetic and real data.
- Broad relevance to multivariate count modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons to key alternative approaches for count networks (e.g., Poisson log-normal/latent Gaussian, other EF models with sublinear statistics).
- Limited evaluation of QPGM and of sensitivity to hyperparameters (R, R0).
- Lack of formal sparsistency/sample complexity results tailored to the proposed models.
- Some clarity gaps in proofs and computational details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include at least conceptual and, if possible, empirical comparisons to latent Gaussian Poisson models and closely related sublinear EF graphical models.
- Provide guidance on selecting R and (R0,R) and analyze sensitivity.
- Add proof sketches for Theorems 1 and 3 in the main text and clarify edge cases.
- Report QPGM results and discuss the practical efficacy of the Gaussian variational bound.
- Outline conditions under which sparsistency extends to TPGM/QPGM/SPGM.

Overall, the paper makes meaningful theoretical and modeling contributions toward flexible count graphical models and is a constructive step beyond autopoisson limitations. Strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying technical details would significantly increase its impact.
