This paper addresses the challenging problem of developing graphical models for multivariate count data, a topic of increasing relevance with the rise of high-throughput sequencing and other count-based datasets. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches, particularly the non-positive dependency constraint of the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM). The paper proposes three novel models – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – to overcome this limitation. 

The introduction is well-written and provides sufficient background. The motivation for the work is clear, and the authors effectively highlight the shortcomings of existing methods. However, the level of detail in the introduction regarding existing approaches feels uneven. While some methods receive brief mentions, others (e.g., hierarchical models) are glossed over, leaving the reader with an incomplete picture.

The mathematical development is generally sound, but suffers from a lack of conciseness and a tendency towards repetitive explanations. The derivation of the TPGM is clearly presented, and the proof that the Winsorized approach of Kaiser and Cressie is flawed is a valuable contribution. The introduction of the QPGM and SPGM is also well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the trade-offs involved. However, the transition between different models feels somewhat abrupt. A more gradual and unified presentation could improve readability. The reliance on theorems without extensive discussion or intuition makes some sections challenging to follow. 

The experimental section is a strong point of the paper. The simulation studies demonstrate the performance of the proposed models and their superiority over existing methods, particularly Gaussian graphical models. The application to breast cancer miRNA expression data is also compelling and provides a practical demonstration of the models’ utility. However, the experiments lack a critical discussion of computational complexity. The authors mention using proximal gradient ascent, but provide no details on scalability or computational cost. This is a significant omission, given the high-dimensional nature of the data. Also, the choice of parameters (R0 and R) for the SPGM seems arbitrary and lacks justification.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem.
*   Identifies a fundamental flaw in existing PGM approaches.
*   Proposes three novel and mathematically sound models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
*   Demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed models through simulation and a real-world application.
*   Provides a clear proof that the Winsorized Poisson approach is invalid.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of conciseness and repetitive explanations in the mathematical development.
*   Abrupt transitions between different model introductions.
*   Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Arbitrary parameter choices in the experimental section.
*   Uneven coverage of related work in the introduction.
*   Minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent spacing around equations). The citation style is also a bit inconsistent.
*   Proposition 2 in Section 3.1 has a condition which seems overly strict and could be weakened.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a significant problem and proposes novel solutions.
*   The mathematical analysis is largely correct and provides valuable insights.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from a lack of conciseness and clarity in its presentation.
*   The computational aspects are not adequately addressed.
*   Minor formatting and stylistic issues detract from the overall quality of the manuscript.
*   The paper, while technically sound, could benefit from greater focus and polish. The sheer number of minor issues suggests a lack of careful review before submission. 

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data, its lack of conciseness, insufficient discussion of computational aspects, and minor stylistic issues raise concerns. The meticulous detail, while commendable, borders on excessive and detracts from the broader impact of the work. I believe the paper is currently *borderline*. I would recommend rejection, but would be open to reconsideration if the authors address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version. Specifically, the authors should focus on streamlining the mathematical presentation, providing a detailed discussion of computational complexity, and addressing the minor formatting and stylistic issues. A more careful and concise presentation would significantly enhance the paper's clarity and impact.
