- The paper proves Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals cannot come from any joint distribution (Theorem 1).
- Authors propose Truncated PGM (TPGM) on {0,…,R} via exponential-family truncation that allows unrestricted sign pairwise parameters (Theorem 2).
- They show that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, positive edges require at least quadratic base-measure growth (Theorem 3).
- This leads to a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with a quadratic penalty admitting mixed-sign dependencies under a negative-definiteness-like condition (Proposition 2).
- They introduce Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure but uses saturating sublinear sufficient statistics to ensure normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4).
- Nodewise structure learning is done with ℓ1-penalized conditional likelihoods using variational bounds to make QPGM/SPGM conditionals tractable.
- Simulations show TPGM/SPGM recover network structure better (improved ROC) than Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines on count data.
- A breast cancer miRNA case study finds biologically plausible hubs missed by Gaussian models.
- The work corrects a misconception in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie) and builds on Besag and exponential-family MRF constructions.
- Strength: clear impossibility result resolving a long-standing misconception about Winsorized conditionals.
- Strength: three theoretically-motivated model families that relax sign constraints in complementary ways.
- Strength: explicit, interpretable normalizability conditions and tail-behavior comparisons for the proposed models.
- Strength: empirical evidence and a real-data demonstration supporting practical utility.
- Weakness: Inference/optimization details are underdeveloped—unclear integration of variational surrogates, bias/tightness, and enforcement of θ≥0 for SPGM.
- Weakness: No convergence or consistency guarantees for the approximate estimators using variational bounds.
- Weakness: Theorem 3’s necessity claim lacks visible assumptions and proof intuition in the main text.
- Weakness: Limited empirical evaluation—no held-out likelihood, goodness-of-fit, calibration, sensitivity analysis for truncation/parameters, or computational scaling/runtime.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to important count baselines (e.g., Poisson log-normal, negative binomial graphical models).
- Weakness: Practical enforcement of global Θ ≺ 0 when using separate neighborhood regressions is not addressed.
- Quality assessment: strong theoretical contributions but empirical and optimization analyses need strengthening.
- Clarity assessment: generally well-written but relies on appendices for key proofs and omits experimental/optimization details.
- Originality assessment: high—novel impossibility result, necessity theorem, and SPGM construction.
- Significance assessment: high potential impact if inference is made robust and scalable and comparisons to count baselines are added.
- Suggestions: add assumptions and a proof sketch for Theorem 3 and discuss edge cases.
- Suggestions: detail the actual optimization objectives, constraints enforced, and provide convergence/consistency analysis for approximate losses.
- Suggestions: include predictive likelihood, calibration metrics, Poisson log-normal/negative binomial baselines, and sensitivity studies for R/R0.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept with medium confidence; strengthen inference and experimental sections to make the contribution unequivocal.
