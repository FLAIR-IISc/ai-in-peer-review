

**Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"**

This paper addresses a significant limitation in graphical modeling for count data: existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) only allow negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors build upon the foundational work of Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2012) who established Poisson graphical models via node-conditional distributions. The paper identifies and corrects a key error in the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie (2000), then proposes three novel models: Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM).

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness through rigorous theoretical proofs (Theorems 1-4). The authors correctly identify and prove the invalidity of the Winsorized Poisson approach (Theorem 1), a significant correction to the literature. The theoretical development of the QPGM and SPGM models is well-supported, with Theorem 3 providing a fundamental characterization of the base measure scaling requirements for normalizability with both positive and negative dependencies. The experimental validation on simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA dataset demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed models, showing superior performance compared to Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM. The work is complete in its theoretical development, though the empirical evaluation could be expanded to include more comprehensive comparisons with count-data-specific models.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical development to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, and the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the theoretical results. The distinction between the proposed models and previous approaches is clearly articulated throughout.

**Originality:** This work makes a substantial original contribution to the field. The authors correctly identify a critical gap in existing Poisson graphical models (the inability to model positive dependencies) and provide the first solution to this problem. The three proposed models represent novel approaches to graphical modeling for count data, with the theoretical characterization in Theorem 3 being particularly original. The paper thoroughly differentiates its contributions from previous work, including the correction of the Kaiser and Cressie approach, and properly cites relevant NIPS work (Yang et al., 2012).

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in graphical models for count data, which is a common data type across many domains (genomics, spatial statistics, etc.). The proposed models enable the modeling of both positive and negative dependencies in count data, which is a substantial advance over existing approaches. The empirical results on the breast cancer microRNA dataset demonstrate clear practical advantages, with the authors identifying biologically meaningful hub miRNAs (mir-375 and mir-10b) that Gaussian graphical models miss. This work significantly advances the state of the art in graphical models for count data.

**Strengths:** The theoretical rigor is exceptional, with the proofs providing new insights into the conditions for normalizability of graphical models for count data. The paper correctly identifies and corrects a significant error in prior work. The proposed models are well-motivated and provide a comprehensive solution to the problem. The empirical validation demonstrates clear practical utility.

**Weaknesses:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on comparisons with Gaussian graphical models rather than other count-data-specific approaches. The paper does not address computational complexity or inference challenges for the proposed models, which would be important for practical adoption. The limitations of the proposed models (e.g., QPGM's Gaussian-esque tail) are acknowledged but not thoroughly explored in terms of practical impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:** This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to graphical models for count data, providing the first solution that allows both positive and negative dependencies without severe restrictions. The theoretical results are rigorous and novel, and the empirical validation demonstrates clear practical advantages. The work directly addresses a significant gap in the literature with important implications for applications in genomics, spatial statistics, and other fields dealing with count data.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, and the paper does not address computational considerations for the proposed models. However, these are relatively minor concerns compared to the substantial theoretical contribution.

Overall, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to graphical modeling with significant practical implications. The theoretical results are rigorous, the empirical validation is relevant, and the work clearly advances the state of the art in modeling count data. The paper meets all four criteria for high-quality NIPS contributions and is recommended for acceptance.
