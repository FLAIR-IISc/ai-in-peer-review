- Paper identifies a fundamental limitation: traditional Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only model negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints.
- The authors prove the Winsorized Poisson approach is invalid (Theorem 1).
- They propose three novel extensions—Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sub-linear PGM (SPGM)—that permit positive dependencies while keeping tractable graphical structures.
- Theoretical contributions are strong: four key theorems establishing model validity and linking base-measure growth to normalizability (Theorems 1–4).
- TPGM offers a simple truncation solution with a bounded domain (0 to R), trading flexibility for normalizability.
- QPGM introduces quadratic base measures to enable positive dependencies but may alter computational properties.
- SPGM uses sub-linear sufficient statistics to achieve heavier tails than QPGM while allowing positive dependencies.
- Empirical validation is thorough: simulations and breast cancer microRNA data show TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods in network recovery.
- The application to breast cancer miRNA networks demonstrates practical relevance and biological insight (e.g., mir-375, mir-10b).
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and clearly explains mathematical derivations and model design.
- Originality is high: the work meaningfully extends PGM literature and addresses an overlooked limitation.
- Significance is strong for domains with count data (genomics, spatial statistics) where richer dependency modeling is needed.
- Weakness: limited discussion of computational complexity and inference/learning costs for the new models, especially QPGM and SPGM.
- Weakness: TPGM’s bounded domain may be problematic when counts exceed the truncation threshold R.
- Weakness: theoretical focus is restricted to exponential-family node-conditionals and does not explore broader conditional distributions.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — novel, well-proven models with solid empirical support outweigh minor limitations.
