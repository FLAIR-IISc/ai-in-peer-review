Summary
This paper tackles a long-standing gap in graphical modeling for multivariate count data. It revisits the Poisson graphical model (PGM), which only admits non-positive pairwise parameters (hence only negative conditional dependencies), and proposes three alternatives: (i) a truncated exponential-family version (TPGM) that permits both positive/negative dependencies on a bounded count space; (ii) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that retains linear sufficient statistics but replaces the Poisson base measure with a quadratic term to ensure normalizability with mixed-sign edges; and (iii) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure but caps the sufficient statistics via a piecewise-linear/concave taper B(·; R0, R), yielding heavier tails and normalizability with unrestricted pairwise parameters. The paper also claims to refute the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditional construction of Kaiser & Cressie by showing no compatible joint distribution can exist in that form. Empirical evaluation via neighborhood selection compares TPGM and SPGM to Gaussian-based methods on simulated and miRNA data.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to Besag’s auto-models and later general node-conditional exponential family MRFs (Yang et al.). It contrasts with multivariate Poisson constructions via summation of independent Poissons and copula approaches, and with Gaussian/nonparanormal graph learning. However, several relevant modern baselines for count graphical models (e.g., Poisson log-normal graphical models, negative binomial counterparts, copula Poisson variants) are missing in the experiments and discussion.

Strengths
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of PGM (sign constraints) and proposes three concrete constructions with clear motivation from normalizability.
- Novel theoretical direction: necessity of quadratic-or-faster base measure for linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3), and a sublinear sufficient statistic solution (SPGM).
- TPGM provides a clean exponential-family truncation that restores tractable node-conditionals and joint consistency without sign restrictions.
- Empirical results suggest gains over Gaussian-based methods on count data; real-data case study is timely and relevant.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Theoretical claims are central but proofs are not provided; the paper states new negative result (Theorem 1), a necessity result (Theorem 3), and normalizability of SPGM (Theorem 4). Without proofs or at least substantial proof sketches, these claims are difficult to verify. Please include proofs in an appendix/supplement.
- Theorem 1: The claim that no joint is compatible with the Winsorized node-conditionals of (2) (for R>3) is strong; the argument likely hinges on incompatibility of the indicator-dependent term and Hammersley–Clifford/Brook’s lemma constraints. Provide a precise statement of assumptions (measurability, support, positivity) and a constructive contradiction or reference. As written, E(XV\s) is “an arbitrary function,” which is too general; one could trivially induce compatibility by restricting E(·).
- QPGM: Proposition 2 imposes XTΘX ≤ −cθ||X||^2 for all X in Wp (Wp undefined). If Θ≺0, θss<0 holds; but in learning, how do you enforce this constraint (and avoid degenerate regions where conditional log-partitions diverge)? The node-conditional optimization for QPGM does not appear convex in general; the paper proposes a Gaussian variational upper bound but does not clarify whether the bound is optimized or used as a surrogate objective (and whether this preserves consistency of neighborhood selection).
- SPGM: Theorem 4 claims normalizability for all θst∈R because B saturates. Provide tail bounds formally (Stirling for log z!), and clarify whether any constraints on θs are needed (they should not be, mirroring the univariate Poisson, but please state).
- Estimation: The conditional log-partitions for QPGM and SPGM involve infinite sums; how are these evaluated in practice (truncation schemes, error control)? For SPGM, Proposition 3 only yields an upper bound for θ≥0; what do you do when θ<0 during optimization?
- Experiments: No comparisons with Poisson log-normal/correlated count models (which permit positive correlations) or recent copula-based count graphical models. Using only Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines is insufficient to establish significance.
- Simulations: Generation via “sums of independent Poissons” is a baseline but not included as a learning competitor. Include held-out likelihood or predictive checks, not just ROC. Hyperparameter selection lacks detail.
- Real data: Setting TPGM R to the maximum count (and R=11 in the case study) is concerning; what preprocessing produces maximum count 11 for miRNA-Seq? Such aggressive truncation can distort dependence. For SPGM, R0=5, R=11 are ad hoc; discuss sensitivity/selection (e.g., via cross-validation or stability objectives) and the interpretability loss from B saturating above R.

Clarity
- Numerous notational issues: Wp is not defined; ∑ Xp is nonstandard (sum over X∈X^p); inconsistent use of X, W, and script X for domains.
- Several typos and grammar errors: “withR,” “form count-valued,” “SGPM,” “may by,” “collate the quadratic term parameters,” etc.
- Reference list is severely incomplete (missing years/venues/pages) and contains placeholders (,, , ,). This is unacceptable for NeurIPS standards.
- Important implementation details omitted: optimization algorithms (step sizes, stopping), constraints enforcement (Θ≺0), convexity, and computational complexity.

Originality
- TPGM (exponential-family truncation) is a natural but valuable twist; the observation that naive Winsorization breaks exponential-family structure is useful.
- The “quadratic base measure necessity” for linear sufficient statistics is interesting if rigorously proved. SPGM’s saturating sufficient statistics seem novel and practically motivated.

Significance
- The problem is important, and providing practically usable multivariate count MRFs with positive and negative dependencies is impactful. However, the empirical evidence is not yet compelling due to missing baselines and methodological gaps in estimation and hyperparameter selection. The lack of proofs also undermines confidence.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a well-known limitation with several principled constructions.
- Potentially influential theoretical insight (Theorem 3).
- Promising empirical performance on simulated and real count data.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing proofs for core theorems; unclear correctness/conditions.
- Incomplete and noncompetitive empirical evaluation.
- Insufficient description of learning algorithms, convexity, constraints, and numerical stability.
- Numerous notational/formatting/reference issues; paper needs substantial polishing.

Questions for authors
1) Provide proofs (or thorough sketches) of Theorems 1, 2, 3, 4 and Proposition 2. Clarify all assumptions (support, positivity, regularity).
2) How are Θ≺0 and θss<0 enforced during learning for QPGM? Is the nodewise problem convex?
3) How are infinite sums for QPGM/SPGM node-conditionals computed or bounded in practice? What error controls?
4) How are R0 and R selected for SPGM? Sensitivity analysis?
5) Please compare against Poisson log-normal and other correlated count graphical models, and report held-out likelihoods.

Minor issues
- Define all symbols (Wp, X, domains). Fix summation notation. Ensure consistency of R across sections (truncation vs SPGM parameter).
- Fix typos/grammar and complete references.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The paper has promising ideas, but the accumulation of theoretical gaps (missing proofs), methodological ambiguities (optimization/constraints), incomplete baselines, and significant clarity/formatting issues outweighs the contributions. I would be enthusiastic to reconsider a revised version with rigorous proofs, stronger experiments, and polished presentation.
