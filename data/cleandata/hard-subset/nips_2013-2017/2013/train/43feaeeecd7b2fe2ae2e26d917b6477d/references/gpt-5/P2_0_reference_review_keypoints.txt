- Paper revisits Poisson graphical models and proposes TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM to allow both positive and negative conditional dependencies for count data.  
- Shows Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals are incompatible with any joint MRF of the Besag/Yang et al. type (Theorem 1).  
- Introduces Truncated PGM (TPGM) that preserves exponential-family structure on a finite domain, guaranteeing existence/uniqueness (Theorem 2).  
- Proves necessity that linear sufficient statistics require a base measure growing at least quadratically to permit positive pairwise interactions (Theorem 3).  
- Proposes Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure to allow mixed-sign dependencies under normalizability (Proposition 2).  
- Proposes Sublinear PGM (SPGM) using saturating sufficient statistics to retain heavier tails and allow arbitrary edge signs while remaining normalizable (Theorem 4).  
- Provides Gaussian variational upper bounds for QPGM node-conditionals and Poisson upper bounds for SPGM only when θ≥0; inference for SPGM when θ<0 is underspecified.  
- Neighborhood selection framework is used for learning, but computation/approximation of node-partition functions D(θ), choice of R/R0, and optimization stability for SPGM are under-detailed.  
- No sparsistency or formal sample-complexity guarantees are provided, though extensions are noted as possible.  
- Simulations show ROC improvements over Gaussian/copula baselines; TPGM performs best when data match its assumptions.  
- miRNA case study yields qualitatively plausible biological insights but lacks quantitative predictive or calibration metrics.  
- Paper is generally well organized and readable but has notational inconsistencies (e.g., R used in multiple ways) and a typo (“SGPM”); key theorems would benefit from intuitive sketches.  
- Original contributions include the negative result on Winsorized conditionals, TPGM construction, the necessity theorem linking base-measure growth to dependencies, and two new model classes (QPGM, SPGM).  
- Significance: advances the ability to model both positive and negative conditional dependencies for counts, with potential impact in genomics and spatial statistics.  
- Pros: clear problem identification, strong theoretical results, novel model classes, and supportive empirical evidence.  
- Cons: incomplete inference details for SPGM, ad hoc hyperparameter choices (R, R0), lack of sparsistency theory, and limited quantitative evaluation.  
- Suggestions: add proof sketches for key theorems, clarify SPGM learning and computation of D(θ) for negative θ with pseudocode/complexity, provide principled selection for R/R0, include predictive metrics and hyperparameter sensitivity, and compare to negative-binomial or other overdispersed count models.  
- Recommendation: recommend acceptance contingent on strengthening learning details and clarifying proofs.
