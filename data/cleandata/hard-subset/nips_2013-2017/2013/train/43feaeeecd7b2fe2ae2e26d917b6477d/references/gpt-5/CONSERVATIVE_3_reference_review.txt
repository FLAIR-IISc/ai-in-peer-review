Summary
The paper addresses limitations of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data. It recalls that the standard PGM requires non-positive pairwise parameters for normalizability, thus only modeling negative conditional dependencies. The authors (i) show that “Winsorized” (capped) Poisson node-conditionals cannot correspond to any consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1); (ii) propose a truncated exponential-family conditional that does yield a valid joint model (TPGM) on a bounded count domain; (iii) prove that to allow both positive and negative dependencies with linear sufficient statistics, the base measure must be at least quadratic (Theorem 3), leading to a Quadratic PGM (QPGM); and (iv) introduce a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) by modifying sufficient statistics to saturate, allowing milder base measures and positive/negative edges. They suggest neighborhood selection with ℓ1-regularized nodewise likelihoods and provide simulations and a miRNA network case study, showing TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-based baselines in ROC for edge recovery and identifying plausible hubs on real data.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the PGM negativity constraint and an instructive analysis of why naive truncation (Winsorization) fails (if the theorem holds generally).
- TPGM gives a valid MRF with positive and negative dependencies on bounded count support; the construction is clean within the exponential family framework.
- Theorem 3 is conceptually interesting, linking normalizability with the growth rate of the base measure when pairwise interactions can be positive.
- SPGM is a pragmatic design to retain count-like base measure while enabling richer dependencies; the proposal is novel.
- Neighborhood selection framework is standard and scalable; simulations suggest benefits over Gaussian/nonparanormal methods on count data.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited and omits strong baselines for count networks (e.g., Poisson log-normal/correlated random effects, negative binomial or copula-based count models). Comparisons to only Gaussian/nonparanormal are insufficient to establish practical superiority.
- Learning/inference details for SPGM (and QPGM) are under-specified: computing node-conditional log-partitions and gradients, handling θ < 0 in the variational bounds, numerical stability, and complexity are not addressed. No guarantees are provided for the proposed variational surrogates (e.g., that nodewise regression with Gaussian upper bounds yields correct estimation).
- Theorem 3’s statement appears only as a necessary condition under broad phrasing; precise assumptions (e.g., growth of B(·), clique order, parameter regimes) and proof sketches are missing in the main text. Similarly, Theorem 1 is strong; the exact conditions for non-existence (e.g., requirements on E(·)) should be clarified and exemplified.
- TPGM’s practical utility is constrained by the bounded domain; sensitivity to R is acknowledged but not studied. SPGM introduces hyperparameters (R0, R) with ad hoc choices; no sensitivity analysis or guidance is provided.
- Real-data evaluation is anecdotal (network visualizations, hub mentions) without predictive metrics, calibration, or robustness analyses. Preprocessing choices (e.g., why miRNA max count R = 11) are unclear.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent; relevant modern baselines for count graphical models are not discussed or evaluated.

Quality
Mixed. The modeling ideas are intriguing and some theoretical statements, if correct, are valuable. However, proofs are not visible in the main paper; algorithmic aspects are vague; and empirical validation against appropriate baselines is lacking. Claims about sparsistency are deferred to future work; variational approximations are proposed without guarantees. The experimental section does not establish state-of-the-art performance in realistic settings.

Clarity
Generally readable with a coherent progression from limitations to constructions. However, many details are deferred or missing: exact proofs, optimization details, partition function computation, and hyperparameter selection. Typos and incomplete references detract. Figures are referenced but not fully explained in text (e.g., Figure 3 for SPGM sufficient statistics). The real-data preprocessing needs clarity.

Originality
The non-existence result for Winsorized conditionals, the necessity of a quadratic (or heavier) base measure under linear sufficient statistics, and the SPGM construction appear novel. TPGM is a natural but useful variant. The theoretical insights are more original than the empirical methodology.

Significance
Potentially significant theoretically for understanding when positive dependencies are possible in count MRFs, and for motivating alternative constructions. Practical significance is unclear without stronger empirical validation, comparisons to established count-network methods, and guidance on learning/selection. As it stands, the paper is more a set of modeling proposals than a demonstrated solution.

Questions for the authors
- Provide precise statements and proof sketches (or clear appendix references) for Theorems 1 and 3; give illustrative counterexamples for Winsorized conditionals.
- How are D(·) and gradients computed for SPGM in practice when θ can be negative? Is exact summation used, truncation, or bounds? Any convergence guarantees?
- Sensitivity of SPGM to R0 and R; recommended defaults or data-driven selection?
- For QPGM, can you provide any statistical guarantees (consistency/sparsistency) for the Gaussian upper-bound surrogate?
- Why not compare against Poisson log-normal graphical models, negative-binomial models, or copula-based count graphical models?
- Clarify preprocessing for the miRNA data and justify R=11.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and potentially impactful theoretical insights on count MRF normalizability.
- Novel model constructions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) expanding the design space for count graphical models.
- Empirical indications that modeling counts improves over Gaussian surrogates.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient empirical validation and weak baselines; no predictive or calibration metrics.
- Incomplete methodological details and lack of guarantees for proposed learning procedures.
- Sensitivity and hyperparameter selection not addressed; TPGM practicality limited by bounded support.
- Presentation issues (references, proofs deferred).

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The paper has promising theoretical contributions, but the empirical and methodological substantiation is not yet at the level required. A strengthened version with complete proofs, robust learning algorithms with guarantees, thorough sensitivity analyses, and comparisons to appropriate count-model baselines would be much more compelling.
