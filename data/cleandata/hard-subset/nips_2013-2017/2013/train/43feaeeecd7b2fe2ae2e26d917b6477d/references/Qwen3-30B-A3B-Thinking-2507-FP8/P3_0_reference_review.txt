

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical modeling: the lack of flexible models for multivariate count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies. The authors correctly identify that the existing Poisson graphical model (PGM) [17, 18, 19] is fundamentally limited by requiring non-positive edge weights for normalizability, making it unsuitable for many real-world applications. They critique the Winsorized Poisson approach [20, 21] (Theorem 1), showing it cannot yield a consistent joint distribution. The authors then propose three novel solutions: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each addressing different aspects of the problem. The work builds upon and extends the foundational graphical model framework established in [18, 19] while tackling a critical limitation in the literature.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical contributions. The authors provide clear proofs for their key theorems (1-4), particularly the important Theorem 3 establishing that a quadratic base measure is necessary for normalizability with positive edge weights. The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM models are all theoretically sound and address different trade-offs. The numerical experiments are appropriate for validating the theoretical claims, showing TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods on count data. However, the paper could strengthen its evaluation by:
- Providing more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity (e.g., how R affects performance in TPGM)
- Including computational complexity comparisons with existing methods
- Demonstrating convergence rates for the learning algorithms

The authors honestly acknowledge limitations of each model (e.g., TPGM's bounded domain, QPGM's Gaussian-like tail), which demonstrates scientific integrity.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivation, and the mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail. The distinction between the three proposed models is clearly articulated, and the connection to previous work is appropriately established. The case study on breast cancer microRNA networks effectively demonstrates practical utility. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation challenges for SPGM (e.g., how to choose R0 and R in practice), but overall the presentation is clear and accessible to the target audience.

## Originality
This work makes several significant original contributions. The critique of the Winsorized Poisson approach (Theorem 1) is novel and important. The TPGM is a natural extension but with theoretical limitations that the authors properly acknowledge. The QPGM and SPGM represent the most significant innovations, as they fundamentally address the core problem of allowing both positive and negative dependencies while maintaining normalizability. The key insight that "the base measure cannot be sub-quadratic" (Theorem 3) is particularly novel and provides important theoretical guidance for future work in this area. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, including the recent [18, 19] foundational papers on PGMs.

## Significance
The significance of this work is high. Multivariate count data is increasingly common in genomics, epidemiology, and other fields, and the inability to model both positive and negative dependencies has been a major limitation. The paper's case study on breast cancer microRNA networks demonstrates tangible practical benefits - the authors identify biologically relevant miRNAs (mir-375 and mir-10b) that Gaussian models miss. The results show clear advantages over existing approaches (Gaussian, non-paranormal) for count data, with SPGM and TPGM finding more biologically meaningful network structures. The theoretical insights about base measures and sufficient statistics will likely inform future work on graphical models for discrete data.

## Strengths
- Clear identification of a fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models
- Rigorous theoretical contributions with well-proven theorems
- Three distinct, well-motivated model proposals addressing different trade-offs
- Appropriate experimental validation on both simulated and real data
- Honest discussion of model limitations

## Weaknesses
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the proposed models
- The SPGM's implementation details and parameter selection (R0, R) could be more thoroughly addressed
- The case study, while promising, could include more biological interpretation of the found networks
- The paper doesn't fully explore how the models perform when data contains both very small and very large counts

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field of graphical models for count data. The work addresses a real and important limitation in the literature, provides clear theoretical justification for the proposed models, and demonstrates practical utility through experiments. The theoretical results (particularly Theorem 3) provide valuable insights that will inform future work in this area. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance. The work advances the state of the art in graphical models for count data in a demonstrable way, with clear theoretical justification and practical validation. While the computational aspects could be more thoroughly explored, the theoretical contributions are substantial enough to warrant publication.

## Specific Suggestions
1. Consider adding a brief discussion of computational complexity for learning each model.
2. In the case study, include more biological context about why the identified miRNAs (mir-375, mir-10b) are significant.
3. Discuss how to practically select R for TPGM and R0, R for SPGM in real-world applications.
4. Consider comparing to the recent work on multivariate Poisson distributions [11-16] to further contextualize the contributions.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that will be valuable to researchers working with count data in various domains. The paper's theoretical insights and practical models represent a meaningful advance in the field.
