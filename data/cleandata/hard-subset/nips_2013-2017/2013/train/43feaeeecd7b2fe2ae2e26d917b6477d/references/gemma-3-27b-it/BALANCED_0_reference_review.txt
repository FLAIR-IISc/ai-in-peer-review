This paper tackles the challenging problem of defining graphical models for multivariate count data, a crucial need in modern applications like genomics and spatial statistics. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches—namely, the inability to model both positive and negative dependencies with standard Poisson graphical models. They meticulously explore several avenues to overcome these limitations, proposing Truncated PGMs (TPGMs), Quadratic PGMs (QPGMs), and Sublinear PGMs (SPGMs). The mathematical derivations are rigorous, and the paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying theory. The numerical experiments, including a case study on breast cancer microRNA data, provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the SPGM, in capturing complex relationships in count data.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Importance:** The paper addresses a significant gap in the statistical modeling toolkit, as existing methods struggle with multivariate count data.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical derivations are thorough and well-justified. The proofs are clear, and the authors convincingly demonstrate the properties of each proposed model.  The theorem proving the inadequacy of the Kaiser & Cressie approach is particularly valuable.
* **Novelty:** The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM models represent genuinely novel contributions, offering new ways to model dependencies in count data. The exploration of sub-linear sufficient statistics is a particularly insightful direction.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments, using both simulated and real data, support the effectiveness of the proposed models. The comparison to existing methods highlights the advantages of the new approaches. The use of a relevant biological dataset (TCGA breast cancer data) adds practical significance.
* **Clarity (mostly):** The core ideas are presented clearly, with a logical flow from motivation to model specification to empirical validation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (Specific Sections):**  While the overall flow is good, sections 3.1 and 3.2 involving the QPGM and SPGM become dense and require careful reading.  The motivation for the specific sub-linear statistic (B(x;R0, R)) is not entirely intuitive and could benefit from further explanation. A figure illustrating B(x) more clearly would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions the use of penalized likelihood estimation for network learning but doesn’t fully address the computational challenges associated with the QPGM and SPGM, particularly the intractable log-partition functions. While variational bounds are mentioned, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and scalability of these methods would be valuable.  A comparison of runtime performance with other methods would strengthen the empirical results.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with existing methods in the simulation studies is somewhat limited. While Gaussian graphical models and non-paranormal methods are included, it would be beneficial to compare against other relevant count data modeling approaches, such as those based on log-linear models or other copula-based methods, even if these are known to have limitations in high dimensions.
* **Assumptions of Simulation Study:** The simulation study relies heavily on TPGM generated data, which provides a natural advantage to the TPGM approach. Exploring data generated from other distributions, like multinomial or negative binomial, would offer a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The details of parameter tuning (e.g., regularization parameters) are somewhat brief. A more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and open problem in statistical modeling.
* The proposed models are theoretically sound and offer novel solutions.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the practical value of the methods.
* The paper is well-written and demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the QPGM and SPGM is not fully addressed.
* The comparison with existing methods could be more comprehensive.
* Some sections of the paper (specifically those detailing the QPGM and SPGM) are dense and require careful reading.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical modeling for count data. The theoretical foundations are solid, and the empirical results are encouraging.  While there are some minor weaknesses regarding computational aspects and the depth of comparison, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically focusing on clarifying the motivations behind the sub-linear statistic, discussing the computational challenges in more detail, and potentially expanding the comparative analysis.  



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
