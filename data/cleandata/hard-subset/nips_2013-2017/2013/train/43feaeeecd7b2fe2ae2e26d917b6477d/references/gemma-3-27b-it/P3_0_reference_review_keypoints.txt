- Summary: Addresses multivariate count-data graphical models, identifies PGMs' inability to represent negative dependencies, and proposes TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM with empirical validation on simulations and a miRNA dataset.
- Relation to prior work: Positions itself relative to Besag/Yang et al., contrasts with log-linear/copula/Poisson-summation methods, and critiques Kaiser & Cressie’s Winsorization approach.
- Strength — Clear problem definition: Clearly frames limitations of existing Poisson graphical models for count data.
- Strength — Rigorous analysis: Provides solid theoretical results (e.g., failure of Winsorization, normalizability conditions in Theorems 1, 3, 4).
- Strength — Novel models: Introduces TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM as new systematic approaches via different base measures.
- Strength — Empirical validation: Demonstrates methods on simulations and a real miRNA dataset, comparing to Gaussian GGM and Nonparanormal approaches.
- Strength — Thorough discussion: Thoughtful analysis of trade-offs, limitations, and potential improvements for each proposed model.
- Weakness — Model complexity: QPGM and SPGM increase estimation and interpretation complexity, with SPGM relying on variational upper bounds for inference.
- Weakness — Limited empirical evaluation: Simulations and real-data analysis are promising but narrow in scope (single real dataset, limited network/parameter diversity).
- Weakness — Computational considerations: Lacks detailed discussion/analysis of computational cost and scalability in high-dimensional settings.
- Weakness — Connection to other count models: Does not deeply explore relationships or extensions to common count models (e.g., negative binomial regression).
- Detailed evaluation — Quality/clarity/originality/significance: Technically sound and original, generally well-written though some derivations need clarification, and results are potentially impactful.
- Arguments for acceptance: Fills a significant gap, presents theoretically grounded novel models, and supports claims with empirical evidence.
- Arguments against acceptance: Added complexity, limited experiments, and missing computational analysis.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, requesting expanded empirical evaluation and more computational detail.
