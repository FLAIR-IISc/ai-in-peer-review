Summary
This paper tackles a longstanding limitation of Poisson Markov random fields (MRFs): the standard Poisson graphical model (PGM) only permits non-positive pairwise parameters, and hence can capture only competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. The authors make three main contributions.

1) They analyze truncation strategies. They prove that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals proposed in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot be assembled into a consistent MRF (Theorem 1), despite earlier claims. They then propose a different exponential-family truncation that redistributes mass over the support {0,…,R}, yielding a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) with both positive and negative dependencies and no normalizability constraints (Theorem 2).

2) They derive fundamental constraints for exponential-family Poisson variants with linear sufficient statistics. They show that to allow both positive and negative conditional dependencies while ensuring normalizability, the base measure must grow at least quadratically (Theorem 3).

3) Guided by this, they propose two new models: (i) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which keeps linear sufficient statistics but uses a quadratic self-potential (or equivalently, a quadratic base measure) and admits positive and negative edges under a negative-definite condition on the quadratic parameter matrix (Proposition 2); and (ii) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which retains the Poisson base measure log(x!) but replaces the linear sufficient statistic with a piecewise sublinear function that saturates beyond R, ensuring normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They provide variational upper bounds to simplify node-conditional estimation.

Experiments on simulated data (hub and scale-free graphs) and a breast cancer miRNA sequencing dataset compare TPGM and SPGM to Gaussian, nonparanormal, and SKEPTIC baselines, showing improved edge recovery on count data and identifying biologically plausible hubs on real data.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on Besag’s auto-models and recent work (Yang et al.) on constructing MRFs from univariate exponential families. It clarifies a misunderstanding in the spatial statistics literature about Winsorized Poisson conditionals, and relates to multivariate Poisson constructions via sums of independent Poissons, which are restrictive (positive correlations only). The proposed models are distinct from Gaussian copula approaches and prior log-linear contingency-table models that do not scale with p.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of the PGM’s negativity constraint and why naive truncation fails (Theorem 1).
- Constructive alternatives: TPGM (clean exponential-family truncation), QPGM (quadratic base measure), SPGM (sublinear sufficient statistics).
- Theoretical results that articulate necessary growth of the base measure (Theorem 3) and normalizability conditions (Theorem 4, Prop. 2).
- Practical estimation via neighborhood selection with l1 penalties; variational bounds for nodewise regressions.
- Empirical evidence on simulated and real count data that Gaussian-based methods underperform, and that TPGM/SPGM recover more structure.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Proof availability and scope: Theorems 1 and 3 are central and nontrivial; the main text sketches intuition but full proofs and assumptions (e.g., graph size, boundary cases) must be carefully vetted in the supplement.
- Estimation details: For QPGM and SPGM, node-conditional log-partition terms are nonstandard. The proposed variational upper bounds (Gaussian for QPGM; Poisson for SPGM when θ≥0) are heuristics; there is no analysis of approximation error, bias in parameter estimates, or impact on sparsistency.
- Hyperparameter selection: SPGM introduces (R0, R), and TPGM requires R. Guidance on scaling with data, robustness, and sensitivity analyses are missing. Setting R to the maximum observed count risks degeneracy and data leakage across folds; principled choices (e.g., via quantiles or cross-validation) should be discussed.
- Experimental validation: Simulations include data from TPGM and “sum of independent Poissons” but not from SPGM or QPGM (the proposed models), which limits assessment of identifiability and recovery under model correctness. No runtime or convergence diagnostics are reported.
- Real data analysis: Qualitative networks are shown without quantitative model fit (e.g., held-out likelihood, calibration), and the very small R=11 on miRNA counts requires justification (normalization, filtering).
- Comparators: Omits negative binomial–based MRF baselines or count copula models; discussion is brief on how NB node-conditionals would fare under the same normalizability constraints.

Quality
Technically ambitious and largely sound, with insightful necessary-condition theory and constructive models. However, estimation methodology is partly heuristic (variational bounds) without theoretical guarantees or empirical ablations quantifying their effect. Experiments support the main claim but could be stronger in breadth and rigor.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Several key definitions (e.g., the exact meaning of C(Z)=Ω(Z^2) and conditions under which Theorem 3 holds) should be stated more precisely in the main text. More intuition around the SPGM sufficient statistic and its effect on tails and dependence would help. Provide algorithmic details (proximal steps, convergence criteria).

Originality
High. Theorem 1’s impossibility for Winsorized Poisson MRFs, the necessity of quadratic base measure for linear sufficient statistics, and the SPGM/QPGM constructions appear novel and address a real gap in multivariate count modeling.

Significance
Significant. Multivariate count data are ubiquitous. Providing valid MRFs that admit both positive and negative dependencies is impactful. If estimation can be made robust and guidance on hyperparameters is clarified, these models could see broad use.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide full proofs and a brief proof sketch in the main text for Theorems 1 and 3, highlighting key steps and assumptions.
- Analyze convexity and Lipschitz properties of the node-conditional objectives; state conditions under which proximal gradient converges.
- Quantify approximation error from the variational bounds and its impact on edge selection; consider tighter bounds or numerical evaluation of D when feasible.
- Add simulations from SPGM and QPGM, with sensitivity to (R0,R) and to the negative-definite condition in QPGM.
- Compare with negative binomial and count copula alternatives; at least discuss expected behavior under your normalizability framework.
- Clarify how R (and R0) are chosen in practice; include robustness plots and timing.
- For the miRNA data, justify the preprocessing and the small R, and provide quantitative model fit metrics.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson MRFs with strong theoretical insights.
- Introduces practically useful models (TPGM, SPGM, QPGM).
- Empirical improvements on realistic count data.

Cons for acceptance
- Estimation procedures rely on heuristics without guarantees or ablations.
- Limited experimental validation for SPGM/QPGM under model correctness and limited comparator set.
- Hyperparameter guidance and robustness are underdeveloped.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. Strong conceptual and theoretical contributions with promising empirical results; would benefit from stronger estimation analysis and experimental validation.
