### **Summary**

This paper addresses a critical limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), namely their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The standard PGM requires all edge-weight parameters to be non-positive, severely restricting its practical applicability. The authors make several key contributions to overcome this issue. First, they rigorously prove that a previously proposed solution based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution [20] is invalid and cannot lead to a consistent joint distribution. Second, they propose a novel and valid truncation scheme, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), which allows for both positive and negative dependencies but introduces a truncation hyperparameter and potential boundary effects. Third, moving beyond truncation, they analyze the fundamental relationship between the sufficient statistics, base measure, and normalizability. This leads to two new model families: the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure to ensure normalizability at the cost of Gaussian-like thin tails, and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which cleverly modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, allowing the model to retain the original Poisson base measure and heavier tails while being normalizable for any real-valued edge parameters. The paper's focus is on model specification, but it demonstrates the utility of the TPGM and SPGM through neighborhood selection-based structure learning on simulated and real-world miRNA sequencing data, showing superior performance over Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a well-defined and significant problem. Count data is ubiquitous in many machine learning applications (e.g., genomics, social sciences), and the lack of a flexible graphical model that can capture both competitive and cooperative relationships has been a major gap in the literature.

2.  **Rigorous and Corrective Contribution:** Theorem 1, which proves that the Winsorized Poisson approach of Kaiser and Cressie [20] does not yield a valid joint distribution, is a strong and valuable contribution. Correcting the record in the literature is an important scientific service.

3.  **Thoughtful Model Development:** The authors do not propose a single ad-hoc fix. Instead, they present a systematic exploration of the design space, leading to three distinct models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM). Each model represents a different trade-off between domain constraints, tail behavior, and parameter restrictions. The derivation of the QPGM and SPGM from fundamental principles regarding the interplay of the base measure and sufficient statistics is particularly insightful.

4.  **Novelty and Significance:** The proposed QPGM and SPGM are, to my knowledge, the first practical and general-purpose MRFs for count data that can model arbitrary dependence structures without domain truncation. The SPGM, in particular, is a clever construction that achieves this flexibility while retaining a Poisson-like base measure. These models are likely to be widely adopted and built upon by researchers working with count data.

5.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed models. The comparison against relevant baselines (GGM, non-paranormal) on both simulated data and a compelling real-world miRNA expression dataset clearly shows the new models' superior ability to recover network structures. The finding of biologically relevant hubs missed by GGM is a powerful argument for the models' utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Learning:** The paper's primary focus is on model specification. While it is reasonable to apply existing neighborhood selection frameworks, the theoretical guarantees (e.g., sparsistency, sample complexity) for the proposed models are stated as an expectation and left for future work. For a NIPS paper, a more formal treatment of the learning properties, even if it's an extension of existing proofs [18], would significantly strengthen the contribution.

2.  **Practical Issues with Hyperparameters:** Both the TPGM and SPGM introduce new hyperparameters (R, and R0, R, respectively). The experimental section sets these in a data-dependent but somewhat ad-hoc manner (e.g., R = max count value). A more detailed discussion on principled hyperparameter selection (e.g., via cross-validation or stability-based methods) would be crucial for practitioners wishing to use these models.

3.  **Omission of QPGM in Experiments:** The QPGM is well-motivated and theoretically analyzed but is conspicuously absent from the numerical experiments. The authors themselves suggest it would behave similarly to a GGM, but this claim should be empirically verified. Its omission leaves a gap in the evaluation and raises questions about its practical performance.

4.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper mentions using proximal gradient ascent but does not discuss the computational cost of solving the node-wise regression problems, especially for the SPGM where the node-conditional partition function is intractable and requires an approximation (Proposition 3). A brief analysis of the computational trade-offs would be beneficial.

### **Detailed Comments**

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The progression from identifying the core problem in PGMs, to debunking a prior solution, and then systematically constructing new solutions is elegant and easy to follow.

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims appear sound and are well-supported. The experimental methodology is standard and the results convincingly support the paper's central claims. The primary weakness in quality is the lack of theoretical analysis for the learning algorithm.

*   **Clarity:** The clarity is excellent. The notation is consistent, and the motivations behind each new model are explained well. Figure 2, comparing the adjacency matrices, is particularly effective at visualizing the differences in the recovered networks.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on the framework of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18], the specific solutions proposed (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are novel. The fundamental insight that normalizability requires either a super-linear base measure or sub-linear sufficient statistics is a key conceptual contribution. The refutation in Theorem 1 also adds to the paper's originality.

*   **Significance:** The significance is high. This work provides a much-needed set of tools for modeling high-dimensional count data. Given the increasing prevalence of such data from sources like next-generation sequencing and user activity logs, these models are poised to have a substantial impact on both research and practice in multiple fields.

**Minor Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   Please include a justification for omitting the QPGM from the experiments or add results for it.
*   A brief paragraph discussing principled methods for selecting the hyperparameters R and R0 would greatly improve the paper's practical value.
*   It would be helpful to explicitly state the form of the node-conditional likelihoods being optimized in the neighborhood selection procedure for TPGM and SPGM.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a long-standing, important limitation in a popular class of graphical models.
*   Provides a rigorous disproof of a previously published (but incorrect) solution.
*   Introduces a family of three novel, well-motivated, and flexible models for count data.
*   The proposed models are shown to outperform relevant baselines on both simulated and important real-world data.
*   The work is likely to be highly influential and broadly useful.

**Cons:**
*   Lacks theoretical guarantees (e.g., sparsistency) for learning the model structures.
*   Practical guidance on hyperparameter selection is minimal.
*   One of the three proposed models (QPGM) is not evaluated experimentally.

**Conclusion:**
The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a comprehensive, rigorous, and highly significant contribution to the field of graphical models. The new models it introduces are a major step forward for analyzing high-dimensional count data. I strongly recommend acceptance. The authors should be encouraged to address the minor weaknesses, particularly the omission of the QPGM in the experiments and the discussion on hyperparameter selection, in the final version.
