### Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental and long-standing limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs): their inability to model positive conditional dependencies, which restricts their applicability to purely "competitive" systems. The authors systematically tackle this problem by proposing three novel classes of graphical models for count data. First, they demonstrate that a previously proposed solution based on "Winsorized" Poisson conditionals is mathematically invalid, as it does not lead to a consistent joint distribution (Theorem 1). Second, they introduce a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) by correctly renormalizing the distribution over a finite domain, which allows for arbitrary dependencies but introduces its own trade-offs related to the truncation level. Third, and most importantly, the paper provides a deep theoretical insight: for models with linear sufficient statistics (like the standard PGM), normalizability with positive interactions requires a base measure that scales at least quadratically with the count values (Theorem 3). This insight motivates their final two models: the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure analogous to a Gaussian MRF, and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which cleverly reverts to the original Poisson base measure but modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, thereby ensuring normalizability for any real-valued interaction parameters. The paper validates these new models on simulated and real-world genomic data, demonstrating their superior ability to recover network structures with mixed dependencies compared to existing methods.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a critical, well-known problem in the field of graphical models. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive correlations has severely limited their practical use. This work provides the first theoretically sound and practically viable solutions to this problem. The progression of ideas, from debunking prior work to providing a fundamental diagnosis (Theorem 3) and then offering multiple, well-motivated solutions, is a significant contribution.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The theoretical contributions are rigorous and insightful. Theorem 1, which proves the inconsistency of the Winsorized PGM approach, is a valuable negative result that clarifies a misconception in the literature. The core insight presented in Theorem 3, linking the scaling of the base measure to the sign constraints on parameters, is elegant and provides a foundational understanding of the problem. The construction of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM follows directly and logically from this analysis.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors build a compelling narrative, starting with the core problem, systematically exploring and refuting existing/natural approaches (truncation), and then using a deeper analysis to propose more fundamental and powerful solutions. The distinction and trade-offs between the three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are clearly articulated.

4.  **Comprehensive Solution Space:** Instead of offering a single "fix," the authors propose a family of models. This is a major strength, as it acknowledges that there is no single best solution for all scenarios. The TPGM is a simple and intuitive model for bounded counts; the QPGM draws a nice parallel to Gaussian models but has thin tails; and the SPGM is a more flexible model with heavier tails. This provides practitioners with a toolkit of options depending on their domain knowledge and data characteristics.

5.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the utility of the proposed models. The superior performance on simulated data with mixed-sign edges is convincing. The application to TCGA microRNA data is particularly compelling, as it shows that the new models can uncover biologically relevant structures (e.g., hub miRNAs, co-regulated clusters) that are missed by both the overly restrictive standard PGM and by Gaussian models which are ill-suited for count data.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors

1.  **Practical Guidance on Model Selection and Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper introduces three new models (and their associated hyperparameters like R and R0 for TPGM/SPGM). However, it offers little practical guidance on how to choose among them for a given dataset. Furthermore, the selection of hyperparameters (e.g., setting R to the maximum observed count) is heuristic. A discussion on cross-validation strategies or other principled methods for selecting both the model type and its hyperparameters would significantly increase the practical value of this work.

2.  **Interpretation of SPGM Parameters:** The sub-linear sufficient statistic in the SPGM is a clever mathematical construction to ensure normalizability. However, it complicates the interpretation of the edge parameters `θ_st`. Unlike a standard GGM or PGM where the parameter has a consistent interpretation across the range of variable values, in the SPGM the effective strength of the interaction `θ_st * B(Xs) * B(Xt)` is non-linear and saturates for large counts. A brief discussion on how to interpret the learned parameters in this model would be helpful.

3.  **Computational Considerations:** The paper briefly mentions using proximal gradient ascent for learning, but details on computational complexity are sparse. The node-conditional partition function for the SPGM is intractable, and the paper proposes using a variational upper bound from the standard Poisson. How tight is this bound, and how does using an upper bound in the pseudo-likelihood objective affect the accuracy and convergence of the learning algorithm? A more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs would be beneficial.

4.  **Formal Analysis of Learning:** The authors state that a theoretical analysis of structure recovery (sparsistency) is left for future work but is expected to follow from prior analyses. While this is reasonable given space constraints, it remains a limitation. A formal statement of the conditions under which sparsistency holds for at least one of the proposed models would strengthen the paper's theoretical contributions beyond model specification.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental and significant contribution to the literature on graphical models for count data. It elegantly solves a long-standing and important problem with a combination of rigorous theory, clear motivation, and strong empirical results. The work is technically sound, highly original, and clearly presented. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to practical implementation details and future theoretical extensions rather than any flaws in the core contribution. This work is highly likely to be influential and to be built upon by future research.

**Recommendation: Accept**
