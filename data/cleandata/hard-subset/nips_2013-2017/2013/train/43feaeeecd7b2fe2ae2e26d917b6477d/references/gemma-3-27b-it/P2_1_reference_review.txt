## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of modeling high-dimensional multivariate count data, a common scenario in fields like genomics and spatial statistics. Existing approaches, like log-linear models and multivariate Poisson distributions, suffer from scalability or restrictive assumptions. The authors identify a key limitation of the original Poisson Graphical Model (PGM): its restriction to negative dependencies. They critically analyze attempts to overcome this using truncated Poisson distributions, demonstrating the inconsistency of a previously proposed approach.  The core contribution lies in proposing three novel extensions – the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – that allow for modeling both positive and negative dependencies in count data.  The paper concludes with illustrative experiments on simulated and real-world data (breast cancer miRNA expression).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Modeling multivariate count data with flexible dependencies is crucial for many applications.
* **Thorough critique of existing work:** The paper provides a clear and well-reasoned analysis of limitations in existing approaches, particularly highlighting the flaw in the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser & Cressie.
* **Novel model proposals:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuine attempts to address the limitations of the original PGM, offering varying trade-offs in terms of flexibility and tractability. The derivation and justification for each model are presented clearly.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The authors provide theoretical justification for the need for quadratic or sublinear base measures to allow for positive dependencies, grounding their model choices in sound statistical principles.
* **Empirical validation:** Demonstrations on both simulated and real data provide preliminary evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed models. The comparison to existing methods like Gaussian graphical models is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited theoretical analysis of SPGM:** While the QPGM is accompanied by a variational bound, the theoretical properties of the SPGM seem less explored.  More investigation into its behavior and potential limitations would be beneficial.
* **Computational complexity:** The paper acknowledges the intractability of the partition function for many of the proposed models. While they mention using `l1` regularization for learning, a more detailed discussion of computational challenges and potential scaling issues would be helpful.
* **Parameter tuning:**  The choice of parameters like `R` (truncation level) and `R0` (SPGM) significantly impacts model behavior. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss a principled way to select these parameters, or perform sensitivity analysis.
* **Limited novelty in the experiments:** While the experiments demonstrate the utility of the models, they rely heavily on comparison to methods already established in the literature. A more in-depth exploration of the unique capabilities of the proposed models, or application to a more challenging dataset, would strengthen the empirical validation.



**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides a well-reasoned argument for its contributions. The mathematical derivations are generally clear, and the theoretical justifications are convincing. However, further analysis of the SPGM and a discussion of computational complexity would enhance the quality. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the motivation, the limitations of existing approaches, and the details of their proposed models.  The notation is consistent and the presentation is easy to follow. **(Excellent)**
* **Originality:** The paper presents genuinely novel extensions to the PGM, addressing a known limitation and offering a path towards modeling more flexible dependencies in count data. The theoretical insights regarding base measures are also original.  **(Strong)**
* **Significance:** The ability to model multivariate count data with both positive and negative dependencies has the potential to be significant in various applications. The results on the breast cancer miRNA data are promising, but more extensive empirical evaluation would be needed to fully assess the impact of the work. **(Good)**

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses an important and open problem in statistical modeling.
* Provides a thorough critique of existing work and identifies a significant limitation.
* Proposes novel and theoretically grounded extensions to the PGM.
* Demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data.

**Con:**

* Limited theoretical analysis of the SPGM.
* Computational complexity not fully addressed.
* Parameter tuning is not discussed in detail.
* Empirical validation could be more extensive.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models, offering novel approaches to modeling multivariate count data. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – specifically, providing more detailed analysis of the SPGM, discussing computational considerations, and exploring parameter tuning strategies – would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I believe the potential significance of this work justifies its inclusion in NIPS.
