Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the auto-Poisson MRF of Besag/Yang et al. is only normalizable when pairwise parameters are non-positive, hence it cannot encode positive conditional dependencies. The authors make three main contributions:

1) They show that the “Winsorized” Poisson node-conditionals proposed in Kaiser & Cressie cannot be assembled into a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1). This clarifies a point of confusion in the spatial statistics literature.

2) They propose a valid exponential-family truncation, yielding a Truncated PGM (TPGM), whose node-conditionals are truncated-Poisson exponential family distributions and whose joint has a finite partition function with no sign restrictions on edges (Theorem 2). They discuss limitations as R→∞ recovers the original negativity constraint.

3) They depart from truncation and modify the univariate exponential family to obtain two new classes: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that retains linear sufficient statistics but uses a quadratic base measure, and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure but uses sublinear sufficient statistics that saturate beyond a threshold. A key theoretical claim is a necessity result (Theorem 3): with linear sufficient statistics, allowing positive dependencies while keeping normalizability requires a base measure growing at least quadratically, motivating QPGM. SPGM achieves heavier tails by modifying the sufficient statistics to be sublinear while keeping a factorial base measure; it is normalizable for all real pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They present variational upper bounds to facilitate nodewise neighborhood selection, and empirically evaluate TPGM and SPGM on simulated graphs and a breast cancer miRNA sequencing dataset, comparing against Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines.

This builds directly on Besag’s auto-models and the exponential-family graphical model framework (Yang et al.), clarifies incorrect claims in spatial literature, and is complementary to alternative multivariate count approaches (e.g., sums-of-Poissons, log-linear contingency models) that either cannot express negative/positive mix or are intractable in high dimensions.

Quality
- Theory: The construction of TPGM (Theorem 2) is sound and follows standard Hammersley–Clifford arguments for exponential-family conditionals on finite supports. The impossibility of Winsorized conditionals (Theorem 1) is an important and nontrivial clarification; the statement is strong (for R>3 and arbitrary E(X\ s)), and I would like to see a self-contained proof or at least a precise reference to compatibility criteria (e.g., Besag’s form of admissible conditionals). Theorem 3 (base measure must be Ω(z^2) for linear stats with positive edges) is insightful; however, the exact scope (e.g., “there exists some θ with θst>0 and A(θ)<∞ implies…”) and the role of dimension should be stated precisely. For QPGM, the normalizability condition via a negative-definite quadratic form is clear; the node-conditional variational Gaussian bound is reasonable. For SPGM, normalizability for all θst leverages bounded interactions via saturating B(·) and factorial decay; this is plausible and useful.
- Algorithms: The paper proposes ℓ1-penalized nodewise likelihoods and cites variational bounds, but practical optimization details are sparse. For QPGM, the node-conditional partition function involves an infinite sum in general; for SPGM likewise. Are gradients computed via truncation, via variational surrogates, or via exact summation with adaptive cutoffs? Are they optimizing true likelihoods or surrogate upper bounds? Convergence, bias, and scalability are not discussed.
- Experiments: The simulations suggest clear gains over Gaussian/nonparanormal methods for count data; ROC curves over hubs and scale-free graphs are standard. However, comparisons omit strong count baselines such as Poisson lognormal/copolymer models or negative binomial/cmp-based models and copula models with discrete margins; given the stated goal (positive dependencies), the Poisson lognormal network methods would be a natural comparator. The data generation includes TPGM (matched to one proposed model) and sums-of-Poissons (positive-only), which is reasonable, but an SPGM-generated setting (even small) would help. The miRNA case study is interesting and suggests biological plausibility (hubs mir-375, mir-10b; chr19 cluster), but key preprocessing details are missing: how do counts end up with max R=11? Are counts binned or normalized? How were R0,R chosen (tuned, fixed, per-node vs global)? Without this, reproducibility and interpretation suffer.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear motivation and a helpful decomposition: truncation vs base-measure/sufficient-statistic modifications. Some definitions (the exact piecewise form of B(x;R0,R), conditions in Proposition 2, and Theorem 3’s quantifiers) would benefit from more precise statements in the main text. A brief algorithm box for nodewise optimization, including how the partition functions/gradients are computed, would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Originality
- Theorem 1’s incompatibility result and the necessity of a quadratic (or stronger) base measure for linear sufficient statistics enabling positive dependencies are novel and insightful.
- QPGM and SPGM are new classes of count MRFs that expand the feasible dependency structure beyond auto-Poisson. The idea of saturating sufficient statistics is simple and powerful.
- The truncation-as-exponential-family TPGM is a clean fix to previous Winsorization attempts, though conceptually close to standard finite-support exponential families.

Significance
Modeling positive and negative conditional dependencies in count-valued graphs is important in many domains. The paper provides principled constructions with theoretical justification that can stimulate further methodological and applied work. That said, the adoption impact would be greater with clearer training procedures, sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters (R, R0), and comparisons to additional baselines used by practitioners (e.g., Poisson lognormal networks, copula-based approaches).

Pros
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson MRFs with clear theory.
- Provides three concrete, principled model classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
- Useful theoretical insights (impossibility of Winsorized conditionals; necessity of quadratic base measure).
- Empirical evidence on synthetic and real data showing advantages over Gaussian-based methods.

Cons
- Missing algorithmic details: how nodewise likelihoods are computed/optimized in practice.
- No sparsistency or finite-sample theory (even sketched) beyond appeals to prior work; extensions are nontrivial due to new partition functions.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to Poisson lognormal/negative binomial/copula-based count models.
- Hyperparameter selection for TPGM/SPGM (R, R0) is ad hoc; no sensitivity analysis.
- Case study preprocessing unclear (R=11 for NGS counts is surprising), limiting reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide details for computing/approximating node-conditional partition functions and gradients; clarify whether a surrogate is optimized and its implications.
- Add comparisons to Poisson lognormal networks and copula-based discrete graphical models.
- Include sensitivity analyses for R, R0 and guidance on their selection (e.g., via cross-validation, stability selection).
- Clarify and, if possible, sketch proofs of Theorems 1 and 3 in the main text; tighten statements.
- Elaborate on data preprocessing in the miRNA study and justify chosen R, R0.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are substantial and timely; the modeling ideas are elegant and likely to influence follow-up work. However, the paper would benefit from stronger empirical baselines, clearer training details, and better guidance on hyperparameters.

Confidence: High.
