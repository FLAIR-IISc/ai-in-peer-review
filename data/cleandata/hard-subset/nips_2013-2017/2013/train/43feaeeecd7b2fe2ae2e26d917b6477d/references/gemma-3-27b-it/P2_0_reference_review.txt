## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a significant gap in the landscape of graphical models – the lack of a robust and flexible framework for modeling multivariate count data. The authors identify limitations in existing approaches like log-linear models, copula transforms, and previous attempts to extend Poisson graphical models (PGMs), particularly the constraint of requiring negative edge weights in the original PGM formulation. They propose three novel extensions: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each building on different modifications to address the normalizability issue and allow for both positive and negative dependencies.  The paper concludes with empirical validation on simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA expression dataset.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a genuinely important and under-served problem in statistical modeling. Count data is increasingly common, and existing tools are often inadequate.
* **Thorough Analysis of Existing Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive review of existing literature, clearly outlining the shortcomings of previous approaches, especially the restrictive nature of the original PGM.
* **Novel Contributions:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent significant and creative extensions of the PGM framework.  The mathematical justification for each approach is well-presented, and the exploration of different base measure modifications is insightful.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes propositions and theorems that formally establish the properties and limitations of each proposed model. The proofs (or clear references to proofs) contribute to the credibility of the work.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of simulation studies and a real-world application to microRNA expression data provides a preliminary assessment of the models' performance.  The comparison to existing methods, including Gaussian graphical models, is valuable.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the subsequent sections build logically upon each other.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the empirical results are promising, they are somewhat limited in scope. The simulation studies could benefit from a more thorough investigation of different network structures and parameter settings.  The real-world application focuses on a single dataset, and further validation on other datasets would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational challenges associated with fitting and performing inference in the QPGM and SPGM, particularly with high-dimensional data.  A discussion of scalability and potential approximation techniques would be valuable.
* **SPGM’s Sub-linear Statistic:** The proposed sub-linear sufficient statistic in the SPGM, while theoretically interesting, appears complex and may be difficult to interpret. The justification for its specific form isn't as strong as for the QPGM.  More intuitive explanation and analysis is needed.
* **Connection to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, the connection to related areas like generalized linear mixed models (GLMMs) and other flexible exponential family models could be more explicit.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality:**  Strong. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed models are mathematically sound. The empirical results, while limited, support the feasibility of the approach.  The thoroughness of the literature review adds to the quality.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, although some sections (e.g., the detailed explanation of the SPGM) could benefit from further simplification and intuition.
* **Originality:** High.  The proposed models represent a novel contribution to the field of graphical models and specifically address a long-standing limitation of existing approaches.
* **Significance:**  High.  A robust framework for modeling multivariate count data has the potential to impact a wide range of applications in fields like genomics, epidemiology, and social science.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical gap in the statistical modeling toolkit.
* Presents novel and theoretically sound extensions of the PGM framework.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results.
* Is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of empirical evaluation.
* Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity.
* The SPGM, while interesting, is less clearly motivated and potentially more complex than the QPGM.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models. The authors have identified a critical problem and proposed innovative solutions. While the empirical validation is somewhat limited, and the computational complexity isn't fully addressed, the theoretical rigor and novelty of the work are compelling.  I believe this paper is **worthy of acceptance** to NIPS, and I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in future work.



**Confidence:** 8/10
