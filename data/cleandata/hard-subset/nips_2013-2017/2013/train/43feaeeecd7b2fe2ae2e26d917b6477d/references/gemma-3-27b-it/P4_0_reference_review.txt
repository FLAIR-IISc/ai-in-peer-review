This paper addresses the problem of modeling multivariate count data, a common scenario in modern applications like genomics and spatial statistics. The authors identify limitations of existing approaches, including the original Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) which can only model negative dependencies, and the unsuccessful attempts to extend it via truncation (Kaiser & Cressie, 2006). They propose three novel approaches: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), aiming to overcome these limitations and capture richer dependence structures. The paper provides theoretical justification for each model, along with empirical evaluation on simulated data and a breast cancer miRNA dataset.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical derivations are rigorous and the proofs, though not fully detailed, appear logically sound. The authors convincingly demonstrate the flaws in previous truncation approaches (Theorem 1) and establish the conditions for normalizability of their proposed models. The numerical experiments, while not exhaustive, provide evidence supporting the effectiveness of the SPGM and TPGM, particularly compared to Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM. The application to a real-world dataset adds further credibility. The work is complete in the sense that it presents a clear problem statement, well-defined solutions, and preliminary validation. However, a more thorough exploration of the computational complexity of learning the model parameters (especially for the SPGM) would be beneficial. The paper is careful in its evaluation, acknowledging limitations of the models and highlighting areas for future work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the research is clearly stated, and the progression of ideas is logical. Mathematical notation is generally clear, though some equations could benefit from more descriptive labeling. The introduction provides a good overview of related work, and the discussion of the limitations of existing models is helpful. The descriptions of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are detailed and well-explained. However, the connection between the sub-linear sufficient statistic and its motivation could be more clearly articulated. Figures and tables are helpful, though the figures in the experimental section are somewhat small and difficult to interpret. 

**Originality:** The paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The exploration of alternative base measures and sufficient statistics for Poisson graphical models is novel. The theoretical results demonstrating the limitations of simple truncation and the conditions for normalizability are original and insightful. While graphical models for count data exist, the proposed QPGM and SPGM offer a unique approach to modeling both positive and negative dependencies. The application of these models to a real-world genomic dataset further demonstrates the originality and potential impact of the work. The paper adequately references related work, acknowledging contributions from Besag (1974), Yang et al. (2013, 2014), Kaiser & Cressie (2006), and others.

**Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant. The ability to model multivariate count data with both positive and negative dependencies is valuable in various domains, including genomics, epidemiology, and social network analysis. The proposed models offer a more flexible and accurate approach compared to existing methods. The successful application to a breast cancer miRNA dataset demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.  The paper advances the state-of-the-art by providing new tools for analyzing count data and potentially uncovering novel relationships between variables.  The extension to neighborhood selection is also a valuable contribution.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling multivariate count data.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing Poisson graphical models.
*   Demonstrated improvement over existing methods in both simulated and real data.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses a practically important problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity of the SPGM is not thoroughly discussed.
*   Empirical evaluation could be more extensive, exploring a wider range of datasets and model parameters.
*   Some mathematical derivations lack sufficient detail.
*   Figures in the experimental section could be larger and more readable.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models. The technical soundness, originality, and potential significance of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
