Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a long-standing gap in undirected graphical models for multivariate count data. Starting from the classical auto-Poisson/Poisson MRF construction (Besag; Yang et al.), the authors recall the well-known normalizability constraint that forces pairwise parameters to be non-positive, limiting the model to competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. They then (i) refute prior proposals to use Winsorized (truncated-at-R) Poisson node-conditionals (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) by proving these conditionals are incompatible with any joint MRF, (ii) propose a valid exponential-family truncation that yields a Truncated PGM (TPGM) with finite support and no sign restriction on edges, (iii) prove that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, normalizability with both positive and negative pairwise interactions necessitates a base measure that grows at least quadratically, and (iv) propose two new models: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which replaces the Poisson base measure with a quadratic energy (Gaussian-esque tail), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which keeps the Poisson base measure but modifies the sufficient statistic to a piecewise-linear function that saturates, enabling positive edges while retaining heavier-than-Gaussian tails. They show how nodewise neighborhood selection can be performed for structure learning and present simulations and a breast cancer miRNA network case study.

Quality and technical soundness
- The incompatibility theorem for Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1) is an important and non-obvious correction to the literature. However, because it underpins a strong “never exist” claim, the precise assumptions and proof details are critical. Please clarify: (a) whether strict positivity is required (Hammersley–Clifford), (b) the exact class of allowable E(XV\s), and (c) whether the impossibility arises from the indicator term I(Xs=R)Ψ(ηs) violating the log-linear compatibility conditions across cliques. A short proof sketch would help reviewers assess scope and tightness.
- The TPGM node-conditionals are exponential-family with finite support and tractable normalization (sum over k=0..R), yielding a valid joint by the Yang–Ravikumar construction. This is technically clean.
- The necessity result (Theorem 3) that subquadratic base measures cannot support positive pairwise couplings with linear sufficient statistics is plausible and insightful. A rigorous argument will likely rely on constructing sequences x with quadratic interactions dominating subquadratic penalties. Please make explicit whether the growth condition is per-node and uniform in p and whether the result is tight (e.g., whether borderline quadratic with small constants suffices).
- QPGM: The model drops the Poisson factorial base measure and uses quadratic and bilinear terms over Z_+; normalizability under Θ ≺ 0 (or bounded negative curvature) matches discrete Gaussian-type models. The analysis via a Gaussian variational upper bound for nodewise conditionals is sensible. However, identifiability between θss and θst, and the implied mean-variance relationship (likely under-dispersed relative to Poisson) should be discussed.
- SPGM: By saturating B(x), pairwise energies are bounded while retaining the Poisson base measure. The normalizability for all real θst is a strong and appealing result. However, nodewise log-partition D(θ,R0,R) is intractable; the proposed variational bound by the Poisson log-partition holds for θ≥0—how are negative θ handled in practice? What is the algorithmic strategy (tight bounds, truncation, or numerical summation), and does it preserve convexity needed for proximal methods?

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The exposition around Section 3 could be more precise about “base measure vs sufficient statistics”: in QPGM the quadratic term is parameterized (θss), which is no longer a base measure in the exponential-family sense but part of the energy. Please tighten terminology. Also, explicitly state computational complexity of each nodewise fit: TPGM O(R) per gradient eval; QPGM closed-form or numerical?; SPGM strategy when θ<0.

Originality
- The correction to the Winsorization approach and the necessity of quadratic growth for linear sufficient statistics are novel and valuable theoretical contributions.
- QPGM and SPGM are new, to my knowledge, in this MRF context; QPGM is close in spirit to discrete Gaussian energy-based models but applied to count support. SPGM’s sublinear sufficient statistic is an elegant device to reconcile positive edges with heavy tails.
- Related work is broadly covered (Besag, Yang et al., copulas, multivariate Poisson constructions, spatial hierarchies). Please discuss Poisson log-normal graphical models/latent Gaussian count models, which are widely used to capture positive correlations in counts, and how your models compare in tail behavior, interpretability, and tractability.

Significance
Providing families of tractable MRFs that allow both positive and negative dependencies for counts is important. The theoretical insights (Theorems 1 and 3) should influence future model design. The models are compatible with standard neighborhood selection workflows.

Empirical evaluation
- Simulations: Comparing TPGM/SPGM to Gaussian/nonparanormal methods is appropriate. It would be helpful to include comparisons to latent Gaussian Poisson (Poisson log-normal) approaches and to the original auto-Poisson (negative-only) baseline (even if poor) for completeness. Please also report calibration via held-out likelihood or pseudo-likelihood, and sensitivity to R and (R0,R).
- Case study: The qualitative findings are interesting (miRNA hubs, chr19 clique), but more quantitative validation (stability frequencies, cross-method overlap, enrichment analyses) would strengthen claims. The choice R=11 and R0=5 seems ad hoc; justify how these were selected and assess robustness.

Pros
- Clear problem statement; corrects a misconception in the literature.
- Three constructive model families with complementary trade-offs.
- Solid theoretical insights into normalizability and tail behavior.
- Practical nodewise estimation approach; promising empirical results.

Cons
- Key proofs (esp. Theorem 1 and 3) need clearer statements and sketches to assess generality.
- SPGM estimation relies on bounds not fully specified for θ<0; computational details and convexity guarantees are missing.
- Hyperparameter selection (R; R0,R) and sensitivity are not addressed systematically.
- Limited baselines (missing Poisson log-normal) and limited quantitative evaluation on real data.
- QPGM may be a poor count model in practice (thin tails, loss of factorial base measure); discuss when it is preferable.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a concise proof sketch for Theorem 1 highlighting the specific incompatibility with finite-order cliques?
- How do you choose R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM) in practice? Can they be learned or tuned via held-out likelihood?
- For SPGM nodewise optimization, how do you handle θ<0 and ensure a tight, tractable surrogate objective?
- Can you report held-out (pseudo-)likelihood and add Poisson log-normal baselines?
- In QPGM, what are the implied mean-variance relationships and dispersion characteristics?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes conceptually and theoretically significant contributions to graphical modeling of counts and proposes practically usable models. Addressing the proof clarity and adding missing computational and empirical details would make this a strong accept. Confidence: high.
