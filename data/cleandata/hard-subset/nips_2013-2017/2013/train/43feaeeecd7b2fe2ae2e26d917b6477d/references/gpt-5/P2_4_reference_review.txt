Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the standard auto-Poisson MRF admits only non-positive pairwise parameters, hence only competitive (negative) conditional dependencies (Besag). The authors (i) prove that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals advocated by Kaiser and Cressie cannot arise from any consistent joint distribution (Theorem 1), (ii) propose an alternative exponential-family truncation that yields a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) on {0,…,R} with no sign constraints (Theorem 2), (iii) analyze normalizability for general Poisson-like families and show that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, allowing positive edges necessitates at least quadratic base-measure growth (Theorem 3), leading to a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with a quadratic penalty that permits positive and negative dependencies under a negative-definiteness-like condition (Proposition 2), and (iv) propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains the Poisson base measure log x! but replaces B(x) by a saturating, piecewise-sublinear function; this ensures normalizability for all real pairwise parameters (Theorem 4) and heavier tails than QPGM. They outline nodewise structure learning using ℓ1-penalized conditional likelihoods, with variational bounds to make QPGM/SPGM conditionals tractable. Simulations show improved ROC for TPGM/SPGM over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines; a breast cancer miRNA case study suggests biologically plausible hubs missed by Gaussian models.

This work builds directly on Besag and the exponential-family MRF construction (Yang et al.), and corrects/clarifies prior spatial statistics suggestions (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith). It is complementary to copula/nonparanormal GGMs and multivariate Poisson sum constructions, and addresses their limitations for mixed-sign dependencies and tractability.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a fundamental barrier in auto-Poisson models and a rigorous impossibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1) that resolves a misconception in the spatial literature.
- Three concrete model families that each relax the sign constraint in different ways: TPGM (finite support, exact EF); QPGM (quadratic base, Gaussian-like behavior); SPGM (saturating sufficient stats, Poisson base). The design choices are theoretically motivated by normalizability analysis (Theorem 3).
- Normalizability conditions are explicit and interpretable (e.g., Θ ≺ 0 for QPGM), with intuitive tail behavior comparisons (Gaussian variational upper bound for QPGM; Poisson bound for SPGM).
- Empirical evidence that the proposed models recover network structure better than Gaussian/nonparanormal on count data, plus a real RNA-seq case where domain-relevant hubs emerge.

Weaknesses
- Inference/optimization details are underdeveloped. For QPGM, the node-conditional log-partition is replaced by a Gaussian variational upper bound; for SPGM by a Poisson bound when θ≥0. It is unclear how these surrogates are integrated into optimization (tightness, bias), whether the objective remains a valid conditional likelihood, and how θ≥0 is enforced for SPGM during fitting. No convergence or consistency guarantees for the approximate estimators are provided.
- The necessity claim in Theorem 3 (C(z)=Ω(z^2) to allow θst>0 with B(x)=x) is pivotal; however, assumptions and proof intuition are not visible in the main text. It would help to state precise regularity conditions (e.g., pairwise-only model, growth comparisons, role of factorial base) and to discuss whether alternative heavier-than-log x! but subquadratic base measures categorically fail.
- Limited empirical evaluation: no predictive metrics (held-out likelihood), goodness-of-fit checks, or calibration. No sensitivity analysis for TPGM truncation R and SPGM parameters (R0,R) beyond one setting; their choice appears ad hoc. Computational scaling (p,n), runtime, and mixing (for data generation) are not reported.
- Comparisons omit important count baselines (e.g., Poisson log-normal or negative binomial graphical models/approximations). Given RNA-seq overdispersion, discussing negative binomial or Poisson-Gamma alternatives would strengthen the case.
- Practical constraints for QPGM: ensuring Θ ≺ 0 under nodewise regression is nontrivial; the paper does not explain how global negative definiteness is enforced across separate neighborhood fits, nor the implications if it is violated.

Assessment by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The theoretical contributions are strong and well-motivated: Theorems 1, 2, and 4 address existence/normalizability head-on. Proposition 2 provides a usable sufficient condition for QPGM. The central necessity result (Theorem 3) is important, but more detail on its assumptions would bolster confidence. The empirical section supports utility but is not comprehensive; the optimization approximations need clearer justification and analysis.
- Clarity: Overall well-written and organized. The modeling choices and their implications are explained clearly. However, the paper relies on appendices for critical proofs; brief proof sketches or assumptions summaries would help. Experimental details (hyperparameter selection, optimization specifics, computational cost) should be expanded. Several references are incomplete.
- Originality: High. The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals in the MRF construction, the general necessity of quadratic-scale base measures with linear sufficient stats, and the specific SPGM construction are, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial.
- Significance: High potential impact. Providing principled, tractable graphical models for count data with mixed-sign dependencies fills a gap, with clear applications in genomics and beyond. Adoption will depend on making inference robust and scalable and demonstrating advantages over modern count baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves a known limitation of Poisson MRFs with principled, theoretically justified alternatives.
- Corrects a misconception in prior literature (Winsorized node-conditionals).
- Introduces SPGM, a practically appealing model with heavier tails and full sign flexibility.
- Promising empirical results and a relevant real-data demonstration.

Cons for acceptance
- Inference relies on variational upper bounds without theoretical analysis of estimation error or guarantees; unclear practical procedure for maintaining constraints (e.g., θ≥0 for SPGM bounds, Θ ≺ 0 for QPGM).
- Limited and somewhat ad hoc empirical evaluation; missing comparisons to key count baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- Theorem 3’s necessity conditions need clearer statements; otherwise, the design space might be perceived as more constrained than shown.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide assumptions and a proof sketch for Theorem 3; discuss edge cases.
- Detail the optimization objectives actually solved for QPGM/SPGM, any constraints enforced, and convergence properties; consider providing consistency/sparsistency under the approximate loss.
- Add predictive likelihood and calibration metrics; include Poisson log-normal/negative binomial copula baselines; add sensitivity studies for R, R0, and robustness to large counts.
- Discuss how to enforce Θ ≺ 0 in practice or characterize when violations still yield well-behaved models.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers substantial and novel theoretical advances and compelling model constructions for an important problem class. Strengthening the inference and experimental sections would make the contribution unequivocal. Confidence: medium.
