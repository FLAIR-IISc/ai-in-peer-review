This paper introduces novel graphical models for multivariate count data, addressing limitations of existing approaches like the Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) which can only model negative dependencies. The authors identify flaws in a proposed truncation approach (Winsorization) and then propose three new models: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). They provide theoretical justification for each model, demonstrating normalizability and the ability to capture both positive and negative dependencies. The paper also presents preliminary experimental results on simulated and real datasets (breast cancer miRNA expression data).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations and proofs appear rigorous, particularly regarding the normalizability conditions. The authors thoroughly address the shortcomings of existing methods and provide a clear rationale for their novel approaches. The experimental section, while limited, demonstrates the practical applicability of the models. The use of simulation studies and a real-world dataset adds credibility. However, the discussion of the computational complexity of parameter estimation and inference is missing. The claim of sparsistency, while alluded to, is not substantiated with theoretical analysis or extensive experimental evaluation. The paper feels incomplete without a more detailed discussion of the computational challenges.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the progression of ideas from the limitations of the PGM to the proposed models is logical. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical expressions are presented clearly. However, the section on the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) is somewhat dense and requires careful reading to understand the sub-linear sufficient statistic. The discussion could be improved with a visual illustration of the sublinear statistic B(x). More explanation regarding the parameter choices in the experiments would also be beneficial.

**Originality:** The work presents a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The identification of the limitations of the Winsorization approach is novel and important. The introduction of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM, along with the theoretical analysis of their properties, represents a substantial advance over existing methods. The exploration of quadratic and sub-linear base measures is a creative approach to overcoming the limitations of the standard Poisson graphical model. The authors properly cite relevant literature and position their work within the broader context of graphical models and count data analysis.

**Significance:** The proposed models address a challenging problem in statistical modeling: representing complex dependencies in multivariate count data. The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies is crucial for many real-world applications. The demonstration of the models on a biological dataset (breast cancer miRNA expression) suggests their potential impact in genomics and other fields. The work advances the state-of-the-art in graphical models and provides a valuable toolkit for researchers working with count-valued data. The potential for extending the sparsistency results from existing work on PGMs to these new models is promising and could further enhance their practical utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling multivariate count data.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing methods (negative dependency only).
*   Clear presentation and logical organization.
*   Demonstrated applicability on both simulated and real datasets.
*   Potential for broad impact in various fields.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Lack of thorough experimental validation of model performance, particularly regarding parameter estimation.
*   The claim of sparsistency requires further justification.
*   The SPGM section could benefit from more intuitive explanations and visualizations.



**Overall Assessment:**  Strong Accept.  This paper presents a significant and original contribution to the field. While some aspects could be further developed (e.g., computational analysis, more extensive experimentation), the technical soundness, clarity, and potential impact of the work are compelling.  The authors have identified a real problem, proposed well-motivated and theoretically justified solutions, and demonstrated their feasibility. This is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.
