

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses an important gap in graphical models for count data, identifying a fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that restricts edge weights to be non-positive. The authors propose three novel extensions: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which allow modeling both positive and negative dependencies. The work builds on the foundational work of Yang et al. (2013) and Besag (1974), extending their framework to count data with richer dependence structures.

**Strengths:**
- The paper clearly identifies a significant limitation in existing PGMs (only negative edge weights) and demonstrates its practical implications.
- The theoretical development of TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM is largely sound, with careful attention to normalizability conditions.
- The numerical experiments provide reasonable validation of the proposed models on both synthetic and real count data.
- The case study on breast cancer microRNA networks demonstrates practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Errors in Key Equations:**
   - In Equation (2), the definition of Ψ(ηs) is mathematically incorrect: it should be log{∑_{k=R}^∞ exp(kηs)/k!} rather than log{R! exp(Rηs) ∑_{k=R}^∞ exp(kηs)/k!}. This error propagates to the analysis of the Winsorized Poisson approach.
   - The piecewise definition of B(x;R0,R) in Equation (7) contains a typo: "RR-R0" should be "R(R-R0)" in the second term.
   - Theorem 1 states "R > 3" without justification. The proof doesn't require R > 3 for the contradiction, suggesting this condition is unnecessary.

2. **Inconsistent Terminology and Definitions:**
   - The paper uses "Winsorized" and "truncated" interchangeably when they are distinct statistical concepts (Winsorizing replaces extreme values with a constant, while truncation removes them).
   - The paper claims SPGM has "heavier tails" than QPGM but provides no formal tail comparison or asymptotic analysis to support this claim.
   - The paper refers to "Poisson MRF" throughout, but the standard term is "Poisson graphical model" or "Poisson Markov random field" (not "MRF" alone).

3. **Theoretical and Methodological Gaps:**
   - The paper states TPGM "can model both positive and negative dependencies" without providing theoretical justification for this claim beyond the absence of negativity constraints.
   - The QPGM's "Gaussian-esque thin tail" is noted but not thoroughly analyzed. The connection to Gaussian graphical models is superficial, as the paper doesn't explore how this affects inference or learning.
   - The numerical experiments lack comparison with other count-data graphical models (e.g., negative binomial graphical models or copula-based approaches).

4. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
   - The paper references "Figure 3 in the appendix" but no appendix is provided in the submission.
   - References have inconsistent formatting (e.g., some include "Arxiv preprint arXiv:1202.2169" while others don't).
   - The case study on microRNA networks lacks statistical justification for the observed performance differences.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper addresses an important gap in graphical models for count data, the technical errors in key equations and inconsistent terminology undermine the theoretical foundation. The proposed models are novel in the context of count data, but the paper fails to properly justify several claims and contains mathematical errors that would require significant correction before publication. The numerical experiments are reasonable but lack sufficient comparison with alternative count-data models.

The paper's most significant weakness is the error in Equation (2), which invalidates the entire analysis of the Winsorized Poisson approach. This is a critical flaw that cannot be overlooked. The authors' claim that Kaiser and Cressie's approach is "incorrect" depends on this equation, and the error makes this claim unsubstantiated.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem, the technical errors in the core theoretical development are substantial. The paper would require significant revision to correct these errors before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. The authors should carefully rederive the Winsorized Poisson node-conditional distribution and properly justify all claims about normalizability and dependency structures. Without these corrections, the paper's main contribution cannot be validated.
