

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical limitation in existing graphical models for count data: the Poisson graphical model (PGM) proposed by Besag [17] and extended by Yang et al. [18, 19] can only model negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints (Proposition 1). The authors identify that the Winsorized Poisson approach suggested by Kaiser and Cressie [20] is invalid (Theorem 1), and then propose three novel extensions: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sub-linear PGM (SPGM). These models overcome the negative-dependency limitation while maintaining tractable graphical model structures. The work builds on the foundational work of [17, 18, 19] but makes significant theoretical contributions to extend Poisson graphical models for practical applications in count data analysis.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contributions:** The paper makes strong theoretical contributions with four key theorems (Theorems 1-4) that rigorously establish the validity of the proposed models. The authors correctly identify and refute a previously proposed approach (Winsorized Poisson), and then provide three new constructions with clear normalizability conditions. The connection between the base measure's growth rate and normalizability (Theorem 3) is particularly insightful.

**Novel Model Design:** The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are genuinely novel extensions to the PGM framework. Each addresses different limitations of the previous approaches: TPGM provides a simple truncation solution with bounded domains, QPGM introduces quadratic base measures to allow positive dependencies, and SPGM uses sub-linear sufficient statistics to achieve heavier tails than QPGM. The authors clearly articulate the trade-offs between these approaches.

**Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments are well-designed, with appropriate comparisons to existing methods (Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal, etc.) on both simulated and real-world breast cancer microRNA data. The results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed models for count data, particularly showing that TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods in recovering network structures.

**Practical Relevance:** The application to breast cancer microRNA networks is highly relevant, demonstrating that the proposed models can identify biologically meaningful relationships (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b as key players in breast cancer) that the original PGM approach misses entirely.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:** While the paper mentions using neighborhood selection with proximal gradient ascent, it doesn't thoroughly address the computational complexity of inference and learning for the proposed models. The QPGM's quadratic terms and SPGM's sub-linear statistics may have different computational profiles than the original PGM or Gaussian graphical models.

**Boundary Effects in TPGM:** The TPGM approach has a bounded domain (0 to R), which may be limiting for applications where counts can exceed R. The paper acknowledges this limitation but could better discuss scenarios where this constraint would be problematic.

**Theoretical Scope:** The paper focuses on node-conditional distributions that are exponential family, but it doesn't explore whether these models could be extended to more complex conditional distributions that might capture additional dependencies.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theorems and proofs. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations of their approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, and the connection between the univariate Poisson distribution and the graphical model extensions is well-explained.

**Originality:** This work makes significant original contributions to the field. While the PGM framework was established in [17, 18, 19], the authors identify and solve a critical limitation that has been overlooked. The three proposed models represent novel extensions that are clearly differentiated from previous work.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a practical need in many application domains (genomics, spatial statistics, etc.) for modeling count data with rich dependence structures. The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed models can capture dependencies missed by existing approaches, with clear biological relevance in the breast cancer case study.

## Recommendation

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant gap in the literature (PGMs can't model positive dependencies)
- Provides three novel, theoretically sound model extensions
- Demonstrates practical utility through rigorous experiments
- Clear theoretical contributions with well-proven theorems
- Strong empirical validation on real-world data

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity
- TPGM's bounded domain may limit some applications
- Could better explore connections to other graphical model frameworks

## Final Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The authors correctly identify a fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models and provide three novel, theoretically sound extensions that overcome this limitation. The empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods, particularly for count-valued data. While the paper has minor limitations in discussing computational complexity and the bounded domain of TPGM, these do not detract from the substantial theoretical and practical contributions.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of clear significance to the machine learning and statistics communities. It represents a meaningful advancement in the area of graphical models for count data, with applications in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains where count data is prevalent.

I strongly recommend acceptance for NeurIPS. The work is original, well-executed, and makes a clear contribution to the field that will likely be of interest to many researchers working with count data.
