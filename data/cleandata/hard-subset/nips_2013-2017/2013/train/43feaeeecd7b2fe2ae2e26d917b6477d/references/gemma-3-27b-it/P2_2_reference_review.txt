## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of modeling multivariate count data, a scenario where standard graphical models like Gaussian graphical models are inappropriate. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) – their inability to model negative dependencies – and thoroughly investigate potential solutions. They demonstrate the flaws in a proposed truncation approach (Winsorization) and then introduce three novel extensions: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models progressively relax the restrictions of the original PGM, allowing for both positive and negative dependencies while maintaining a tractable graphical model structure. The paper also presents preliminary empirical validation on simulated and real-world (breast cancer miRNA expression) datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Modeling multivariate count data is a significant challenge with increasing relevance across numerous domains.
* **Clear Motivation & Thorough Literature Review:** The paper effectively motivates the problem and provides a comprehensive overview of existing approaches and their limitations.
* **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:**  The authors provide strong theoretical justifications for their proposed models, including proofs demonstrating the limitations of the Winsorization approach and the conditions for normalizability of the QPGM and SPGM.  Theorem 1 and 3 are particularly compelling.
* **Novel Contributions:** The introduction of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represents a substantial contribution to the field, offering a progression of solutions to the limitations of existing PGMs.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on simulated and real data provide initial evidence supporting the efficacy of the proposed models, demonstrating competitive performance compared to existing methods.  The case study with breast cancer data is particularly insightful.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors carefully position their work within the broader context of graphical models, exponential families, and existing methods for count data analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The QPGM and SPGM introduce increased complexity compared to the original PGM, both conceptually and computationally. The practical implications of this complexity (e.g., scaling to very high-dimensional data) are not fully explored.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation, while promising, is relatively limited.  More extensive testing across a wider range of datasets and network structures would strengthen the claims. The comparisons primarily focus on ROC curves – exploring other metrics (precision-recall, structural Hamming distance) would be beneficial.
* **Variational Bound as Proxy:** Relying on a variational bound for the QPGM (as in the numerical experiments) is a reasonable starting point, but the tightness of this bound and its impact on the results aren’t thoroughly investigated.
* **Computational Details:** The implementation details regarding the proximal gradient ascent algorithm and parameter tuning are not fully specified, making it difficult to reproduce the results.
* **Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of R and R0 in TPGM and SPGM respectively can greatly affect results. Sensitivity to these choices should be discussed.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported.  The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the empirical validation, while limited, is convincing. The paper represents a complete and well-considered contribution.
* **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although the mathematical notation can be dense at times. A more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the SPGM's sublinear sufficient statistics would be helpful.
* **Originality (9/10):** The proposed models are novel and address a significant limitation of existing PGMs. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the related literature and effectively position their work within the field.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are important and have the potential to impact a wide range of applications dealing with multivariate count data. The models offer a significant advancement over existing approaches and provide a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical limitation of existing methods for modeling count data.
* Presents a rigorous theoretical framework for developing novel graphical models.
* Offers three distinct and innovative solutions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
* Demonstrates promising empirical performance on both simulated and real-world data.
* Provides a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models and statistical inference.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Empirical validation is limited in scope.
* The computational complexity of the QPGM and SPGM could hinder their practical application to very large datasets.
* Implementation details are not fully specified.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models. While some areas could be strengthened with further empirical validation and detailed implementation information, the novelty, rigor, and potential impact of the work are substantial. I believe this paper is a strong fit for NIPS.  I encourage the authors to address the minor weaknesses mentioned above in a future revision.
