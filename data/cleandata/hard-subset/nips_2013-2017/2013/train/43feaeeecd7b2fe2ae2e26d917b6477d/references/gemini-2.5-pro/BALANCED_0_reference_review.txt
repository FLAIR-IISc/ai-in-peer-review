This paper addresses a well-known and significant limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs): their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. Standard PGMs, as formulated by Besag (1974) and extended by Yang et al. (2012, 2013), require non-positive edge parameters, which severely restricts their applicability to real-world multivariate count data where variables often exhibit positive correlations. The authors tackle this problem by proposing three novel classes of graphical models for count data that permit rich dependence structures.

First, the paper re-examines truncation-based approaches. It formally proves that a previously proposed "Winsorized" PGM (Kaiser and Cressie, 1997) does not yield a consistent joint distribution, a valuable and clarifying negative result. The authors then introduce a new Truncated PGM (TPGM) based on a different truncation scheme that preserves the exponential family form of the conditional distributions, thereby guaranteeing the existence of a valid joint MRF. Second, moving beyond truncation, the paper investigates more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. It shows that for a model with linear sufficient statistics to be normalizable with positive edge weights, the base measure must scale at least quadratically. This insight leads to the proposed Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which is analogous to a Gaussian graphical model but on a count domain. Finally, to allow for heavier tails than the QPGM, the authors propose the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear while retaining the original log-factorial base measure. The paper concludes with experiments on synthetic and real-world biological data, demonstrating the superior performance of the proposed models over existing methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem in probabilistic modeling. The inability of standard PGMs to capture positive dependencies is a major barrier to their use. By providing multiple, well-motivated solutions, this work significantly expands the toolkit for modeling high-dimensional multivariate count data, which is increasingly common in fields like genomics, neuroscience, and social sciences.
2.  **Originality and Thoroughness:** The paper's contribution is not a single incremental improvement but a comprehensive exploration of the problem. The authors propose three distinct and novel approaches (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM), each attacking the problem from a different angle (domain truncation, base measure modification, sufficient statistic modification). This provides a rich understanding of the design space for such models.
3.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and rigorous. A key contribution is Theorem 1, which formally refutes a previously proposed model and corrects a misconception in the literature. The constructions of the new models are well-justified, and their normalizability and properties are established through formal theorems and propositions.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, systematically dismantles prior attempts, and then introduces its own contributions in a logical progression. The trade-offs associated with each proposed model (e.g., bounded domain for TPGM, thin tails for QPGM) are discussed honestly and clearly.
5.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the practical value of the proposed methods. On synthetic data, TPGM and SPGM outperform standard Gaussian-based approaches, as expected. The application to miRNA expression data is compelling, showing that the new models can uncover biologically relevant network structures (e.g., hub miRNAs, functional clusters) that are missed by both the standard PGM (due to its negativity constraint) and Gaussian graphical models.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The proposed TPGM and SPGM models introduce crucial hyperparameters (R for TPGM; R0 and R for SPGM). In the experiments, these are set based on the data (e.g., R = max count). For the models to be broadly useful, a more principled discussion on how to set these parameters is needed. Guidance on using techniques like cross-validation or stability-based selection for these hyperparameters would significantly strengthen the paper's practical contribution.
2.  **Absence of QPGM in Experiments:** The QPGM is elegantly derived from theoretical principles (Theorem 3) and presents an interesting bridge to Gaussian graphical models. However, it is conspicuously absent from the experimental section. Its inclusion would have completed the empirical story. The authors should either include results for QPGM or explain its omission. For instance, does it suffer from optimization difficulties, or does its performance closely mirror that of a standard GGM on transformed data?
3.  **Details on Estimation Procedure:** The paper states that estimation is performed via l1-penalized neighborhood selection but provides few details. While the general approach is standard, the specific objective functions for the novel models are not. For example, the SPGM's node-conditional log-partition function is intractable, and a variational upper bound is proposed. It is unclear if this bound was used during estimation and what impact it has on learning accuracy. A more detailed description in the main text or an appendix would improve reproducibility.
4.  **Theoretical Guarantees for Learning:** The paper focuses on model specification and mentions that sparsistency analysis (i.e., theoretical guarantees for correctly recovering the graph structure) is left for future work. While understandable given space constraints, this is a notable omission for a paper introducing new high-dimensional estimators. Even a brief discussion of the technical conditions required for consistency (e.g., relating to the curvature of the conditional log-likelihoods) would add significant value and bolster confidence in the proposed learning framework.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a significant and long-standing limitation of PGMs.
*   Proposes three novel, well-motivated, and technically sound graphical models for count data.
*   Includes a valuable negative result that clarifies and corrects prior literature.
*   The paper is very clearly written and logically structured.
*   Empirical results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.

**Cons:**
*   Lack of a principled discussion on selecting model hyperparameters (R, R0).
*   The theoretically motivated QPGM is not evaluated experimentally.
*   Insufficient detail on the optimization procedures used for model fitting.
*   Theoretical guarantees for structure recovery are deferred to future work.

**Decision:**

Despite the weaknesses, which primarily relate to completeness rather than correctness, the strengths of this paper are substantial. It makes a significant and original contribution to an important problem in machine learning. The proposed models are elegant, well-founded, and shown to be practically useful. The paper is of high quality and would be of great interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance. The authors are strongly encouraged to address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version to further improve the paper's impact and utility.
