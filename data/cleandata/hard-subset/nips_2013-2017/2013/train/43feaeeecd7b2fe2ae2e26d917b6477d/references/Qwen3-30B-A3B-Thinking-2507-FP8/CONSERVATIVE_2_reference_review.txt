

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses an important problem in graphical modeling: extending the Poisson distribution to multivariate count data while allowing for both positive and negative dependencies. The authors identify a limitation in existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that can only model negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints, and propose three novel approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by identifying a genuine limitation in existing PGMs (Proposition 1) and providing rigorous proofs (Theorems 1-4) that establish the validity of their proposed approaches. The theoretical development is well-structured and builds logically upon previous work by Yang et al. (2012, 2013) on graphical models via exponential families. The paper clearly articulates the motivation for their work and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the existing literature on graphical models for count data.

## Weaknesses

Despite the theoretical soundness, the paper falls short in several critical areas that would be expected for a NIPS submission:

1. **Lack of Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is severely limited. The simulation studies only compare the proposed methods to Gaussian graphical models (GGMs) and non-paranormal approaches, but omit comparisons to other count-data specific methods like the multivariate Poisson models discussed in the introduction (e.g., [14, 15]). The case study on breast cancer microRNA networks is interesting but lacks sufficient depth to demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed models. The authors claim their methods "find many more edges and biologically important hub miRNAs" than GGMs, but they don't provide statistical significance tests or demonstrate that these additional edges correspond to biologically meaningful relationships.

2. **Overstated Claims of Superiority:** The paper claims their methods "for the first time specify classes of joint graphical models for count data that permit rich dependence structures," but this appears to be an overstatement. The authors themselves acknowledge that their TPGM has a bounded domain, which is a significant limitation. The SPGM's "heavier tails" are not rigorously justified as being practically advantageous, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that these models outperform simpler alternatives in real-world applications.

3. **Insufficient Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper compares their methods only to Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM, but omits comparison to other established approaches for count data. For example, the authors cite [14, 15] on multivariate Poisson models based on sums of independent Poisson variables, but don't compare their methods to this approach, which is commonly used in practice.

4. **Limited Practical Demonstration:** The paper doesn't address key practical challenges like computational scalability for high-dimensional data (p=1000+), the impact of the truncation parameter R on model performance, or how to select appropriate values for R and R0 in real-world applications. The claim that "the SPGM has two distinct advantages" (heavier tails and broader feasible parameters) is not substantiated with sufficient empirical evidence.

## Overall Assessment

While the theoretical contributions are sound, the paper falls short of the empirical validation expected for a NIPS submission. The authors present a novel extension to existing graphical models for count data, but fail to provide sufficient evidence that their approach offers meaningful advantages over existing methods in practical applications. The experimental section is too limited to support the claims of superiority made in the paper.

The paper's main contribution appears to be theoretical rather than practical, and it doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why these new models would be adopted over existing approaches in real-world applications. The authors acknowledge that their TPGM has a bounded domain, and the QPGM has "Gaussian-esque thin tail" behavior, which are significant limitations that aren't adequately addressed in terms of practical utility.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution, it lacks the comprehensive empirical validation and practical demonstration required for a NIPS submission. The authors have identified a genuine limitation in existing Poisson graphical models, but their proposed solutions don't provide sufficient evidence of practical advantages over existing methods. A stronger version of this paper would include more extensive comparisons to alternative count-data models, demonstrate the scalability of their methods to larger datasets, and provide a clearer justification for why these models would be preferred in practice.

The paper would benefit from a more balanced discussion of limitations, including the computational costs of the proposed methods and the practical challenges of parameter selection. Without these elements, the paper doesn't meet the high standards expected for a NIPS submission.
