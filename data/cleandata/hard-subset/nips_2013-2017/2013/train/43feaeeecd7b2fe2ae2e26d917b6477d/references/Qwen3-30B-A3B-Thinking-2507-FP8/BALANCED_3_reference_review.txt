

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses a significant gap in statistical modeling: the lack of suitable graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors identify that existing approaches like Gaussian graphical models, Ising models, and the Poisson graphical model (PGM) [17-19] either don't fit count data or have severe limitations. Specifically, the PGM only allows negative edge weights due to normalizability constraints, which restricts its applicability. The authors correctly critique the Winsorized Poisson approach [20, 21] as theoretically unsound, then propose three novel approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which overcome the limitations of previous methods.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by rigorously proving the limitations of existing approaches and establishing the necessary conditions for normalizability with both positive and negative dependencies.
2. The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are genuinely novel and address the core problem in a principled way.
3. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4, Propositions 1-3) are well-motivated and carefully proven.
4. The experimental evaluation demonstrates practical utility, particularly in the breast cancer microRNA case study where the proposed models identify biologically meaningful networks that the PGM misses.
5. The paper clearly differentiates between the three models and their trade-offs, providing valuable guidance for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
1. While the paper presents strong theoretical foundations, the empirical validation is limited. The simulation studies only compare with Gaussian-based methods, but it would be valuable to compare with other count-specific approaches (e.g., negative binomial graphical models) or to explore the impact of different truncation levels in TPGM.
2. The practical implementation details for the SPGM are somewhat vague. The paper mentions that the log-partition function doesn't have a closed-form, but doesn't provide practical computational approaches for large-scale problems.
3. The paper states that "the focus of this paper is model specification," but the case study on breast cancer data is somewhat superficial. It would be strengthened by more detailed analysis of the biological significance of the identified networks beyond just mentioning two miRNAs.
4. The theoretical results on normalizability (Theorem 3) are strong, but the paper could better explain why quadratic base measures are necessary beyond the "scaling" argument, perhaps with a simple illustrative example.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. The proofs are well-constructed and address the core problem of normalizability with both positive and negative dependencies. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, though the empirical validation could be expanded. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations of their approaches.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are clear, though some sections (particularly the motivation for the quadratic base measure) could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The distinction between the three proposed models is clearly articulated.

**Originality:**
This paper makes a significant original contribution by identifying and solving a previously unaddressed problem in graphical models for count data. The three proposed models represent novel extensions to the existing literature on graphical models. The critique of the Winsorized Poisson approach is particularly valuable as it corrects a previously accepted method.

**Significance:**
The problem addressed is important as count data is increasingly common in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains. The proposed models could enable more accurate modeling of complex count data structures. The case study demonstrates practical utility, though more extensive empirical validation would strengthen the significance claim.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models, addressing a significant gap in the literature. While the empirical validation could be expanded, the theoretical advances are substantial and well-supported. The paper is clearly written and addresses an important problem with novel solutions.

I recommend **acceptance** with the following suggestions for improvement:
1. Expand the empirical evaluation to include comparisons with alternative count-specific graphical models (e.g., negative binomial-based approaches) and explore sensitivity to truncation parameters in TPGM.
2. Provide more detailed discussion of computational considerations for SPGM, particularly for large-scale applications.
3. Deepen the case study analysis to better connect the identified networks to known biological mechanisms beyond just naming two miRNAs.
4. Add a brief discussion of potential limitations in real-world applications (e.g., computational cost for large p).

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models, and with the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS. The theoretical results are rigorous, the proposed models are novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate practical utility.
