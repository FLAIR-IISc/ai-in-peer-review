- Addresses key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models: they require non-positive edge weights and thus only model negative dependencies.  
- Proves the Winsorized Poisson approach of Kaiser and Cressie is invalid for graphical modeling (Theorem 1).  
- Proposes Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) on {0,…,R} that allows positive and negative edges but is limited by bounded domain size.  
- Proposes Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with a quadratic base measure that permits both positive and negative edge weights (but has Gaussian-like tails).  
- Proposes Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with sublinear sufficient statistics offering heavier tails and broader parameter feasibility (log-partition has no closed form).  
- Provides rigorous theoretical development (Theorems 1–4) establishing normalizability conditions and parameter constraints.  
- Empirical validation via simulations and a breast cancer microRNA case study shows improved performance and discovery of biologically relevant hubs (e.g., mir-375, mir-10b).  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized; theorems, proofs, methodology, and figures (ROC curves) are clear and reproducible.  
- Original contributions include identifying the negative-edge limitation, invalidating prior Winsorized approach, and introducing three new model classes.  
- Theoretical insight that a quadratic base measure is necessary for normalizability with both positive and negative edges (Theorem 3).  
- Advances the state of the art by providing the first count-data graphical models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies, relevant to genomics, spatial statistics, and networks.  
- Strengths: rigorous theory, clear problem identification, multiple well-motivated models, strong empirical support, excellent exposition.  
- Weaknesses: TPGM’s bounded domain, QPGM’s Gaussian-like tails, SPGM’s intractable log-partition complicating inference, and limited discussion of computational complexity.  
- Recommendation: strong accept candidate for NIPS—significant, technically sound, practically useful—though computational aspects and some model limitations warrant further study.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a meaningful problem, novel rigorous theory, clear empirical gains, and high-quality presentation; arguments against center on computational discussion gaps and remaining model trade-offs.
