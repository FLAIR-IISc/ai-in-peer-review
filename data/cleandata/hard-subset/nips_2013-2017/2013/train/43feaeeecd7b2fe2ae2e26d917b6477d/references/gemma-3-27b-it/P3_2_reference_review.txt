## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of modeling high-dimensional multivariate count data, a common scenario in modern applications. Existing approaches suffer from limitations: log-linear models scale exponentially with dimensionality, copula transforms are complex, and previous attempts to extend the Poisson graphical model (PGM) restrict dependencies to be negative. The authors identify a flaw in a recent truncation-based approach aiming to address this negativity constraint and then propose three novel graphical models built upon the Poisson distribution: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The TPGM utilizes a novel truncation scheme, while the QPGM and SPGM modify the base measure of the Poisson distribution to allow for both positive and negative dependencies.  The authors demonstrate the potential of these models through simulations and a case study on breast cancer miRNA expression data.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a significant and increasingly prevalent problem in many scientific disciplines – modeling multivariate count data.
* **Clear Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods and provide strong motivation for developing new approaches.
* **Rigorous Theoretical Development:**  The derivation of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM is presented with a good level of mathematical detail and justification. The proof demonstrating the invalidity of the Winsorized Poisson approach is a strong contribution.  The theorems establishing normalizability conditions are particularly valuable.
* **Novelty:** The proposed models represent a genuine effort to extend the PGM framework beyond its inherent limitations, offering new possibilities for modeling complex dependencies in count data. The quadratic and sublinear base measures are innovative.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The simulations and case study provide initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed models.  The comparison to existing methods, including the original PGM and Gaussian graphical models, is helpful.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately positions its contributions within the context of existing graphical model literature and related approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't deeply address the computational challenges associated with parameter estimation and network learning for the QPGM and SPGM, particularly for large datasets. While they mention using penalized likelihood with proximal gradient ascent, a more detailed discussion of scalability and potential approximations would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation study, while useful, is relatively limited. Exploring more complex network structures and varying data generating processes would strengthen the conclusions. The case study, although promising, could be expanded with more robust statistical comparisons and biological validation.
* **Practical Considerations of Truncation (TPGM):** The discussion of the TPGM acknowledges limitations related to truncation level *R*.  The impact of *R* on model performance and the sensitivity to its selection could be more thoroughly investigated.  A more systematic approach to choosing *R* is needed.
* **SPGM's Sublinear Statistic:** The choice of the specific sublinear function in the SPGM feels somewhat arbitrary. A more principled derivation or exploration of alternative sublinear functions might be valuable.  Further justification for the chosen form is warranted.
* **Clarity of Notation:**  Some of the notation (especially in the theoretical sections) can be dense and challenging to follow. A slightly more accessible presentation could improve readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of the traditional PGM and propose innovative solutions that address this limitation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the initial empirical results are promising. While some computational and practical challenges remain, the proposed models offer exciting new possibilities for modeling complex dependencies in a wide range of applications. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  Addresses an important and open problem in statistical modeling.
*  Presents novel and theoretically well-grounded solutions.
*  Demonstrates promising empirical performance.
*  Significantly extends the existing literature on Poisson graphical models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity of the QPGM and SPGM is not fully addressed.
* Empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* Practical considerations of the TPGM are not fully explored.




**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  I believe this paper is of high quality and makes a significant contribution to the field. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above in a revised version would further strengthen the paper.



**Confidence:** 9/10
