

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses a gap in the literature regarding graphical models for count data, specifically proposing three new variants of Poisson graphical models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that purportedly overcome the limitation of the existing Poisson graphical model (PGM) which requires non-positive edge weights and thus can only model negative dependencies.

The paper begins by correctly identifying the limitation of the standard PGM (Proposition 1), showing that edge weights must be non-positive for normalizability. It then correctly identifies and refutes the Winsorized Poisson approach from [20,21] (Theorem 1), which is a valuable contribution. The authors then propose three alternative approaches: TPGM (a truncated Poisson model), QPGM (with quadratic base measure), and SPGM (with sub-linear sufficient statistics). The theoretical results (Theorems 2-4, Propositions 2-3) appear technically sound, establishing normalizability conditions for their proposed models.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper correctly identifies and refutes a previously proposed approach (Winsorized Poisson), which is an important contribution.
2. The theoretical development is rigorous and well-structured, building logically on the foundation of [17,18,19].
3. The paper correctly identifies a genuine limitation in existing Poisson graphical models.
4. The mathematical derivations are clear and well-presented.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of rigorous empirical validation:** The paper's main claim is that their models can capture both positive and negative dependencies, but the experimental validation is insufficient. The simulation studies only use two network structures (hub and scale-free) and compare against Gaussian methods (GGM, non-paranormal) that are inappropriate for count data. The comparison against existing count-data appropriate methods (like the approach in [15]) is missing. The single case study on miRNA networks is interesting but doesn't convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods.

2. **Overstated novelty:** The paper presents TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM as novel approaches, but these are variations of standard exponential family graphical models. The SPGM, for instance, is essentially a truncated version of the standard graphical model framework. The paper doesn't adequately differentiate its contributions from established methods.

3. **Limited practical justification:** The paper claims their models are "the first to specify classes of joint graphical models for count data that permit rich dependence structures," but this is an overstatement. The approach of modeling count data through transformation (e.g., log transformation followed by Gaussian graphical models) is well-established [7,10], and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that their methods outperform these standard approaches.

4. **Theoretical claims not fully supported:** The paper claims that QPGM "has a Gaussian-esque thin tail" and that SPGM "has a heavier tail," but provides no empirical evidence to support these claims. The theoretical advantages of SPGM over TPGM (as claimed in Section 3.2) aren't validated with experiments.

5. **Inadequate comparison with relevant work:** The paper doesn't properly compare with [15], which proposes a different approach to modeling multivariate count data (sums of independent Poissons), despite using it in their simulations. The comparison with the original PGM is limited to a single sentence.

**Overall assessment:**
While the paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution by correctly identifying the flaw in the Winsorized Poisson approach and proposing alternatives, it falls short in demonstrating that these alternatives provide meaningful practical advantages over established methods. The experimental validation is insufficient to support the paper's main claims, and the novelty is overstated. The paper would benefit from more thorough empirical validation against a broader range of baselines, including the sum-of-Poissons approach [15] and other count-data appropriate methods.

The paper addresses a genuine problem (modeling count data with graphical models that capture both positive and negative dependencies), but the proposed solutions aren't convincingly demonstrated to be superior to existing approaches. The theoretical results are sound, but without strong empirical evidence of practical utility, the contribution is limited.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

The paper would be acceptable for a more specialized statistics or machine learning journal with a focus on theoretical contributions, but for NIPS, where empirical validation and practical significance are paramount, the lack of rigorous comparative experiments against relevant baselines makes it insufficient. The authors should provide more substantial evidence that their proposed models offer tangible benefits over existing methods before reconsidering for NIPS.
