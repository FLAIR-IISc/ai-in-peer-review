## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of modeling multivariate count data, a common scenario in modern datasets, using graphical models. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to represent negative dependencies – and propose several novel extensions. They begin by demonstrating the failure of a natural truncation approach (Winsorization) to overcome this limitation. Subsequently, they introduce the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each building upon the basic PGM structure with modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics.  The paper concludes with empirical validation on simulated data and a breast cancer miRNA expression dataset, demonstrating the utility of their proposed models for network inference.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** Modeling multivariate count data with flexible dependencies is a crucial challenge. This paper directly tackles this limitation of existing methods.
* **Clear identification of the core issue:** The authors accurately pinpoint the non-negativity constraint on edge weights in standard PGMs as a major obstacle.
* **Novel contributions:** The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuinely novel approaches to constructing graphical models for count data. The theoretical justification for each model is well-presented.  The QPGM and SPGM are particularly interesting for their attempts to bypass the negativity constraint.
* **Theoretical soundness:** The paper includes rigorous theoretical analysis, particularly regarding the normalizability conditions for the proposed models. Theorems 1-4 are well-stated and contribute to a deeper understanding of the challenges in this area.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both simulated and real datasets provide initial evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed models, particularly the SPGM, and demonstrate their competitive performance against alternatives.
* **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally well-written and follows a logical structure, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited novelty of TPGM:** While the TPGM correctly identifies the flaw in Kaiser & Cressie's approach, the truncation approach itself isn't dramatically novel. Its limitations, as the authors acknowledge, restrict its applicability.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't fully address the computational challenges of inference and parameter estimation in the QPGM and SPGM, especially the intractability of the normalization constant.  The mention of proximal gradient ascent is brief and lacks detail.  A more thorough discussion of the computational implications would be valuable.
* **Empirical Evaluation could be more comprehensive:** The empirical evaluation, while a good starting point, could be more extensive.  Comparing to a broader range of count data models (beyond Gaussian graphical models and related approaches) would strengthen the results.  The choice of the regularization parameter and its impact on the results need more discussion.
* **Connection to existing literature on graphical models with non-Gaussian distributions:** The paper could benefit from a more extensive discussion of how the proposed methods relate to broader efforts in developing graphical models for non-Gaussian data, such as those based on copulas or other non-parametric techniques.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical justifications and a logical progression of ideas. The proofs are well-presented and the models are mathematically well-defined. However, the computational complexity and scalability of the methods remain a concern.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the motivation, methodology, and results.  The use of notation is consistent and the explanations are easy to follow.
* **Originality (8/10):** The core idea of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics to allow for both positive and negative dependencies in PGMs is original and insightful. The QPGM and SPGM represent novel model formulations.  However, the TPGM is less novel.
* **Significance (8/10):** The paper addresses a significant problem in statistical modeling and offers potentially valuable tools for analyzing multivariate count data. The empirical results suggest that the proposed models have the potential to improve network inference in various applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical modeling.  The authors have identified a fundamental limitation of existing PGMs and proposed innovative solutions.  While some aspects of the work could be further developed (e.g., computational aspects, more comprehensive empirical evaluation), the theoretical soundness and originality of the ideas are impressive.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem.
* Proposes novel and theoretically well-justified models.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity of proposed models is a concern.
* Empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The TPGM model has limited novelty.



**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** I recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS. The strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community. I encourage the authors to address the computational concerns and expand the empirical evaluation in future work.
