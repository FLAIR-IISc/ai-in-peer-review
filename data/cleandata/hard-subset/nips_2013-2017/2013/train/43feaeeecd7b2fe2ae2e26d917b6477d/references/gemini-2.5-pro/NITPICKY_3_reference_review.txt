**Summary**

This paper addresses a critical limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), namely their inability to model positive dependencies due to normalizability constraints that require non-positive edge weights. The authors begin by providing a valuable negative result, formally proving that a previously proposed "Winsorized Poisson" approach by Kaiser and Cressie [20] does not yield a valid joint distribution. The main contribution is the proposal of three novel classes of graphical models for count data that permit both positive and negative dependencies: (1) the Truncated PGM (TPGM), based on a valid exponential family truncation of the Poisson domain; (2) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which modifies the base measure to be quadratic, akin to a Gaussian model on integers; and (3) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear while retaining the original Poisson base measure. The paper provides theoretical justification for the normalizability of these models and demonstrates their empirical performance on simulated and real-world miRNA expression data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a well-defined and significant limitation of a natural graphical model for count data. Enabling PGMs to capture mixed-sign dependencies is a valuable goal.
2.  **Novelty and Contributions:** The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent creative and distinct strategies for solving the core problem. The formal disproval of the Winsorized Poisson approach in Theorem 1 is a clean, definitive contribution that clarifies a misconception in the literature.
3.  **Sound Theoretical Basis:** The core theoretical claims regarding the normalizability of the proposed models appear to be sound. The connection drawn between the base measure's growth rate and the feasibility of positive edge weights (Theorem 3) provides a clear motivation for the QPGM and SPGM.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel ideas, the paper is undermined by a substantial number of methodological gaps, a lack of rigorous practical guidance, and several clarity issues.

1.  **Unprincipled Hyperparameter Selection:** The practical utility of the TPGM and SPGM is severely compromised by the lack of a principled method for selecting their key hyperparameters.
    *   For the TPGM, the truncation level `R` is a critical parameter. In the experiments, it is set post-hoc to the maximum observed count value. This data-dependent choice is methodologically unsound, raises concerns about overfitting, and offers no guidance on how to handle new data that may exceed this maximum. The authors acknowledge that as `R → ∞`, the negativity constraint returns, but they fail to provide any framework for navigating this crucial trade-off.
    *   The SPGM introduces two hyperparameters, `R0` and `R`. The paper offers no guidance on their selection, stating only the values used in the case study (`R=11`, `R0=5`) without justification. The model's performance is likely sensitive to these choices, and their arbitrary selection makes the method difficult to reliably apply in practice.

2.  **Incomplete Theoretical Treatment:** The paper's focus is on model specification, but the treatment of learning is cursory. The authors state they use neighborhood selection and that they "expect extensions of their sparsistency analysis to confirm" that their methods work. For a NIPS paper, asserting that the crucial theoretical validation for the learning algorithm is "left for future work" is a significant weakness. This makes the presented work feel incomplete.

3.  **Questionable Practical Advantage of QPGM:** The paper notes that the QPGM is "Gaussian-esque" and that its node-wise regressions would behave similarly to a Gaussian graphical model. This raises a critical question that is never addressed: what is the advantage of this complex model over simply applying a standard transformation to the count data (e.g., log(x+1), variance-stabilizing transform) and then using a well-understood Gaussian graphical model? Without a clear demonstration of its superiority over such simpler, established baselines, the motivation for the QPGM is weak.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from a lack of precision and polish.
    *   The title, "On Poisson Graphical Models," is overly generic and does not reflect the specific contributions.
    *   Phrases like "It can be seen that..." are used to justify key steps (e.g., in Section 2.1.2) without explicit proof, which is poor form.
    *   The description of the Winsorized conditional in Section 2.1.1 as "reminiscent of the exponential family" is imprecise.
    *   The jump to the generalized QPGM form in Eq. (6) is abrupt and lacks motivation for the `θss` terms.
    *   The provided list of references contains numerous formatting errors (e.g., extraneous commas, missing conference/year details), suggesting a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.

5.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** While the experiments show an advantage over Gaussian-based methods, the scope is narrow. The data is generated from models (TPGM, sums-of-Poissons) that are favorable to the proposed methods. A more robust evaluation would include data generating processes that do not align perfectly with any of the tested models. The biological case study is presented as anecdotal evidence; claims about finding "key players" are not rigorously validated against a ground truth and could be spurious discoveries.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces several novel and interesting model classes for count data graphical models.
*   The refutation of the Winsorized PGM approach is a valuable contribution.
*   The core problem is important, and the work provides a solid foundation for future research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The lack of a principled method for hyperparameter selection for TPGM and SPGM is a major practical flaw.
*   The theoretical analysis of the learning procedure is incomplete and deferred to future work.
*   The practical advantage of the QPGM over simpler, transformation-based approaches is not established.
*   The paper suffers from numerous minor issues in clarity, precision, and presentation that collectively detract from its quality.

**Decision:**

While the paper presents a set of creative and promising ideas for an important problem, the execution falls short of the standard expected for NIPS. The combination of significant methodological gaps (especially in hyperparameter selection), incomplete theoretical support for the learning algorithms, and a general lack of polish leads me to recommend **rejection**. The work feels premature; with a rigorous treatment of hyperparameter selection and a complete theoretical analysis of the associated learning methods, a revised version of this paper would be a much stronger candidate for acceptance.
