This paper attempts to address the longstanding problem of developing a graphical model for multivariate count data that doesn't suffer from the limitations of existing approaches – primarily the inability to model both positive and negative dependencies. The authors correctly identify the issues with the original Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) and previous attempts at using truncated Poisson distributions. They propose a series of novel models – the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – each building on the previous, aiming to overcome these limitations. The paper includes theoretical justification for the models and preliminary experimental validation on simulated and real datasets.

The primary strength of this work lies in its thorough exploration of the mathematical constraints imposed by the need for normalizability in Poisson-based graphical models. The derivation of the quadratic and sublinear base measures is a notable contribution and clarifies the fundamental requirements for modeling both positive and negative dependencies. The attempt to provide concrete models, rather than just theoretical observations, is commendable. The simulation studies and breast cancer miRNA network analysis offer initial evidence of the potential applicability of these models.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. First, the motivation for the TPGM feels underdeveloped. While the authors correctly identify the issue with Kaiser and Cressie’s approach, the explanation for *why* their proposed truncation is superior feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more in-depth comparison with the Winsorized Poisson approach and a detailed analysis of the properties of the proposed truncation would be beneficial.

Second, the QPGM and SPGM, while mathematically interesting, feel somewhat detached from the practical realities of count data. The imposition of a quadratic base measure seems likely to induce strong assumptions about the data distribution, potentially leading to poor performance in many real-world scenarios. The authors acknowledge this with the mention of a Gaussian-esque thin tail, but don't fully address the implications. The SPGM, with its ad-hoc sublinear statistic, feels even more like a mathematical workaround than a natural extension of the Poisson distribution. Justification for the chosen form of the sublinear statistic is minimal.

Third, the experimental validation is insufficient. The simulation studies lack detail on the network generation process and parameter settings. The comparison to existing methods (Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal, etc.) is limited, and it's unclear whether the observed improvements are substantial or simply due to the specific parameter tuning used. The breast cancer analysis, while promising, relies on a single dataset and lacks a rigorous comparison to other state-of-the-art methods for miRNA network inference. The claim that the TPGM finds more "hub" miRNAs is somewhat vague and requires further justification. The provided figure on the learned networks is of low resolution and hard to assess.

Fourth, the paper is overly dense and mathematically challenging. While mathematical rigor is appreciated, the presentation could be more accessible. More intuitive explanations of the models and their properties would broaden the paper's appeal and facilitate understanding.

Finally, the discussion of related work is surprisingly limited. While the authors acknowledge some relevant papers, they fail to adequately contextualize their contributions within the broader literature on count data modeling and graphical models. For instance, discussion of recent advances in zero-inflated models and hurdle models would strengthen the paper’s positioning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in statistical modeling.
*   Presents novel mathematical insights into the constraints of Poisson-based graphical models.
*   Proposes concrete model extensions that, in principle, overcome the limitations of existing approaches.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficient experimental validation to support the claims of improved performance.
*   The QPGM and SPGM models feel somewhat artificial and lack strong practical justification.
*   Limited discussion of related work.
*   The paper is overly dense and mathematically challenging.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. However, its lack of robust experimental validation and the somewhat contrived nature of the proposed models raise serious concerns about its practical significance. The paper falls short of the NIPS standard for originality and significance. I would recommend **rejection** at this time, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version with more comprehensive experimental results and a clearer articulation of the practical benefits of their proposed models. Specifically, a deeper engagement with the existing literature and a more rigorous evaluation of the models' performance on a wider range of datasets are crucial.
