This paper addresses the challenging problem of defining graphical models for multivariate count data, a task complicated by the limitations of existing approaches like the Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) which only allows for negative dependencies. The authors meticulously investigate truncation strategies and ultimately propose novel Quadratic (QPGM) and Sublinear (SPGM) PGMs that overcome the restrictions of the original PGM, enabling the modeling of both positive and negative dependencies. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and technically sound, making a significant contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed QPGM and SPGM are genuinely novel approaches to modeling multivariate count data. They address a significant gap in the existing literature by allowing for both positive and negative dependencies, a critical requirement for real-world applications.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed models. The proofs of Theorems 1-4 are crucial in establishing the validity and limitations of different approaches. The careful analysis of normalizability conditions is particularly commendable.
* **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Complex concepts are explained clearly and intuitively. The mathematical notation is consistent and easy to follow.
* **Comprehensive Exploration:** The authors thoroughly explore various truncation strategies and modifications to the Poisson distribution, providing a detailed justification for their final proposals.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and the application to breast cancer miRNA data demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed models. The comparison with existing methods highlights the advantages of the QPGM and SPGM. The choice of the TCGA dataset and comparison to existing work on that dataset is strong.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, appropriately citing relevant papers and clearly positioning their contributions within the broader context of graphical models and count data analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper briefly mentions the use of proximal gradient ascent, a more detailed discussion of the computational challenges associated with estimating the parameters of the QPGM and SPGM would be valuable. Especially for the SPGM, the lack of a closed-form log-partition function is a potential concern.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The simulation studies are performed on relatively small datasets. The paper could benefit from a discussion of the scalability of the proposed methods to even larger datasets, which are common in many count data applications.
* **Choice of R0 in SPGM:** The selection of R0 in the SPGM is somewhat arbitrary. A more principled method for choosing R0, potentially based on data characteristics, could be explored. Justification for R0=5 isn't provided beyond it being a reasonable choice.
* **Variational Upper Bound Limitations:** The use of a Gaussian variational upper bound for the SPGM could introduce inaccuracies. While acknowledged, a more in-depth discussion of the potential impact of this approximation on the results would be helpful.
* **Lack of Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:**  A more detailed sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter used in the neighborhood selection procedure, across both simulated and real datasets, would strengthen the robustness of the findings.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in statistical modeling with a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* The proposed models overcome the limitations of existing approaches and offer a more flexible framework for modeling multivariate count data.
* The empirical results demonstrate the practical utility of the models in both simulated and real-world scenarios.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the proposed methods is a potential concern, although this is acknowledged by the authors.
* The scalability of the methods to large datasets is not thoroughly discussed.
* While the empirical validation is strong, the sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models and count data analysis. The proposed QPGM and SPGM are innovative and address a long-standing limitation of existing approaches. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall strength of the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding computational complexity and scalability. Specifically, I'd like to see a brief discussion about approximation techniques that could be used to improve computational performance and/or address the log partition function challenges. A more in-depth hyperparameter sensitivity analysis would also strengthen the work.
