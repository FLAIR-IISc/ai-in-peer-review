## Peer Review: On Poisson Graphical Models

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of developing graphical models for multivariate count data, a common data type lacking adequate modeling tools. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to represent negative dependencies – and systematically explore solutions. They demonstrate the inadequacy of a truncation approach proposed by Kaiser & Cressie (20), and then present three novel models: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  The TPGM offers a basic fix, while the QPGM and SPGM introduce modifications to the underlying Poisson distribution to allow for both positive and negative dependencies.  The paper concludes with preliminary experimental validation on simulated data and a breast cancer miRNA expression dataset.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on the foundational work of Besag (17) and Yang et al. (18, 19) on PGMs. It directly addresses the limitations highlighted by Kaiser & Cressie (20) and Griffith (21) regarding negative dependency constraints. The work also draws connections to related areas like log-linear models (11), copula approaches (12), and hierarchical models (16) for count data, clearly articulating why these alternatives are insufficient for high-dimensional settings.  The comparison to Gaussian Graphical Models (1, 2, 3, 4) in the experiments provides a benchmark for performance. The authors appropriately cite relevant literature throughout.

**Strengths:**

* **Clearly Defined Problem:** The paper articulates a significant gap in the statistical modeling toolkit – the lack of flexible graphical models for multivariate count data.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The authors provide theoretical proofs (Theorems 1-4) supporting their claims about the non-normalizability of the Winsorized PGM and the validity of their proposed models. The proofs appear sound and are a major strength.
* **Novel Model Development:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuinely novel contributions, offering increasingly sophisticated approaches to overcome the limitations of traditional PGMs.  The exploration of modified base measures and sufficient statistics is particularly insightful.
* **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of preliminary experiments, including application to a real-world dataset, demonstrates a commitment to practical utility.  The discussion of neighborhood selection and potential sparsity guarantees is valuable.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experimental section feels somewhat cursory. While the results are encouraging, more extensive evaluation is needed.  Specifically:
    * **Simulation Studies:**  The simulation setup lacks detail (e.g., how edge weights were chosen, specifics of the Gibbs sampling). The presented ROC curves are somewhat difficult to interpret without more context.
    * **Real Data Analysis:** The breast cancer dataset analysis is descriptive. A more quantitative comparison against baseline methods, including statistical significance testing, would strengthen the conclusions.
    * **Computational Cost:** The paper does not address the computational cost of learning the parameters of the QPGM and SPGM, particularly the log-partition function challenge. This is a critical consideration for real-world applicability.
* **SPGM Motivation:** The motivation for the sublinear statistic in the SPGM feels less intuitive than the quadratic approach in the QPGM.  A clearer explanation of the benefits of this approach would be helpful.
* **Variational Upper Bound:** The use of a Gaussian upper bound for the SPGM is a simplification.  The authors acknowledge this but should discuss the potential impact of this approximation on the accuracy of the model.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, it could more explicitly discuss how these new models compare to other count data modeling techniques such as negative binomial graphical models.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and previously unresolved problem in statistical modeling.
* The theoretical contributions are strong and well-supported.
* The proposed models represent genuinely novel approaches with the potential to advance the field.
* The preliminary experiments are promising.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental validation is limited and needs to be more comprehensive.
* The computational complexity of the more advanced models is not discussed.
* The motivation for the SPGM could be clarified.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models for count data.  The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed models are novel and well-motivated. While the experimental validation is somewhat limited, the preliminary results are encouraging. I believe this paper is a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings, with a recommendation for **acceptance**, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly expanding the experimental section and discussing computational considerations.

**Confidence:** 7/10 (Strongly leaning towards acceptance, but improvements would solidify the contribution)
