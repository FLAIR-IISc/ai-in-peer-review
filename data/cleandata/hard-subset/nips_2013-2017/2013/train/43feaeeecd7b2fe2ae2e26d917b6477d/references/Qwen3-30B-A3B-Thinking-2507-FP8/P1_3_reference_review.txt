

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical modeling: the lack of tractable models for high-dimensional multivariate count data. The authors identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) suffer from a fundamental limitation—they can only model negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors make three key contributions: (1) they prove the invalidity of the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie; (2) they propose a Truncated PGM (TPGM) with bounded domain; and (3) they introduce two novel approaches—Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that allow both positive and negative dependencies while maintaining normalizability. The paper demonstrates these models on simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA network, showing superior performance compared to existing methods.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The paper identifies a clear gap in the literature—existing graphical models for count data cannot capture both positive and negative dependencies. The authors' solution through novel modifications to the Poisson distribution's base measure and sufficient statistics is highly original. The distinction between TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represents a significant conceptual advance beyond prior work on multivariate count models.

**Quality (High):** The theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified. Theorems 1-4 are rigorously proven, addressing the critical normalizability issue that has plagued previous approaches. The authors demonstrate careful attention to detail, including correct identification of the flaw in the Winsorized Poisson approach (Theorem 1). The experimental validation on both simulated and real data provides strong empirical support for their claims.

**Significance (High):** The problem of modeling multivariate count data is increasingly important in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains. The authors' models fill a critical gap, enabling the use of graphical models for data types that were previously poorly handled by existing methods. The breast cancer microRNA network application demonstrates clear practical value, with the authors identifying biologically relevant hubs that other methods miss.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-structured and logically presented. The motivation for each model is clearly articulated, and the mathematical derivations are accessible to readers familiar with graphical models. The distinction between the three proposed models is well-explained.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Limitations (Moderate):** While the QPGM has a Gaussian-like thin tail, the authors acknowledge this as a drawback. The SPGM addresses this better, but the theoretical analysis of SPGM's tail behavior could be more detailed. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison of the computational complexity of each model's inference and learning procedures.

**Practical Considerations (Moderate):** The TPGM requires choosing a truncation level R, which may be challenging in practice. While the paper demonstrates that increasing R improves approximation to the untruncated case, it doesn't provide guidance on selecting R for real-world applications. The SPGM requires choosing two parameters (R and R₀), which could complicate model selection.

**Comparison with Recent Work (Moderate):** The paper references important prior work but could better situate itself within the growing literature on non-Gaussian graphical models. The connection to recent advances in non-paranormal models [7, 10] could be more explicitly articulated.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models. The authors successfully address a fundamental limitation in Poisson graphical models and provide three novel approaches that enable modeling of both positive and negative dependencies in count data. The theoretical contributions are rigorous, and the empirical validation demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines novel theoretical insights with meaningful empirical validation on a real-world application. The problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS community, particularly given the increasing prevalence of count data in high-dimensional settings.

## Recommendations

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Addresses a significant gap in the literature
- Theoretical contributions are novel and rigorously proven
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing methods
- Provides meaningful applications to genomics
- Well-organized and clearly written

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- The QPGM's Gaussian-like tail behavior is a limitation
- Practical considerations for parameter selection (R, R₀) need more discussion
- Limited comparison with very recent non-Gaussian graphical models

**Final Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of graphical models, providing the first tractable approaches for modeling multivariate count data with both positive and negative dependencies. The theoretical and empirical results are strong, and the problem addressed is highly relevant to the NIPS community. The minor weaknesses noted are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's significant contributions.

The authors have made a clear and important advance that will be valuable to researchers working with count data across multiple domains. The paper should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
