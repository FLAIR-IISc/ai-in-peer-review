This paper attempts to address a long-standing problem: extending Poisson graphical models to allow for both positive and negative dependencies. The motivation is clear – count data is ubiquitous, and existing models are either too restrictive (original PGM), intractable (various multivariate Poisson approaches), or rely on approximations. The authors correctly identify the normalizability issue with the original PGM and systematically explore several approaches – truncation, modified base measures – to overcome this limitation, culminating in the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).

The paper's strengths lie in its thorough exploration of the mathematical constraints and its logical progression of ideas. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of graphical models and the challenges involved in extending them to non-Gaussian data. The theoretical results – particularly regarding the necessity of a quadratic base measure for unrestricted dependencies – are valuable. The introduction of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represents a genuine attempt to address a difficult problem, and the analysis surrounding their normalizability is rigorous. The empirical validation, while limited, demonstrates promising results, particularly the performance of SPGM compared to Gaussian models and the original PGM.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The primary concern is the lack of extensive empirical evaluation. While simulations are presented, they are relatively basic and don't fully explore the parameter space or compare against a wide range of relevant baselines (e.g., other count data models beyond those cited). The case study, while interesting, provides limited insights and relies heavily on a pre-existing dataset previously used in related work. The claim of “superior performance” based on this analysis is overstated. The SPGM, in particular, feels underdeveloped. The connection to variational inference via the Poisson upper bound is mentioned but not deeply explored, and the practical implications of this approximation are unclear. The manuscript is also dense and could benefit from clearer explanations of the mathematical concepts, especially for readers less familiar with graphical models. The notation is sometimes cumbersome. Finally, the paper focuses heavily on theoretical contributions, at the expense of detailing implementation aspects and practical considerations. It is unclear how difficult these models are to fit in practice, and what computational trade-offs are involved.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant open problem in statistical modeling.
*   Provides novel theoretical results regarding the normalizability of Poisson graphical models.
*   Introduces three new graphical model variants (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with potentially useful properties.
*   Demonstrates promising (though limited) empirical results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficient empirical validation and comparison to relevant baselines.
*   Limited exploration of practical considerations (computational complexity, implementation details).
*   The SPGM, while theoretically interesting, feels less developed than the QPGM.
*   Dense writing style and complex notation may hinder accessibility.
*   The jump from theoretical derivations to practical use feels large. A more thorough demonstration of the benefit of these models over simpler or existing alternatives is needed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am skeptical of this paper in its current form. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the lack of robust empirical evidence and the underdeveloped nature of the SPGM raise concerns. The paper feels incomplete; it outlines interesting ideas but doesn’t fully demonstrate their practical utility. The claims of significance are not adequately supported by the presented results. I would recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly through a more comprehensive empirical evaluation and a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of their work. Specifically, a comprehensive comparison against other count data modeling approaches (e.g., negative binomial graphical models) is vital. This is an important direction for research, but this work is not yet ready for NIPS.
