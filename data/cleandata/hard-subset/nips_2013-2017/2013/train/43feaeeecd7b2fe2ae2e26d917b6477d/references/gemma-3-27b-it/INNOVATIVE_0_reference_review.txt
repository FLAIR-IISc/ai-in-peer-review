This paper tackles a fundamental problem: modeling multivariate count data. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches, particularly the restrictive negative dependency constraint of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs). Their contribution is significant – proposing three novel graphical model extensions (TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM) aiming to overcome this limitation. The core idea of modifying the base measure or sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution is insightful and demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying mathematical constraints. The authors also thoughtfully consider practical aspects, illustrating network learning with simulated and real-world data.

The paper’s strength lies in its boldness and conceptual novelty. The derivation of the QPGM and SPGM, driven by the need for normalizability and rich dependency structures, is a genuine contribution. Moving beyond simple truncation (TPGM) to fundamentally altering the Poisson’s building blocks is a significant step. The inclusion of empirical validation, although relatively limited, strengthens the arguments. The connection to existing work on Gaussian graphical models via variational bounds for the QPGM is a clever way to frame the new approach and suggest practical algorithms.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the theoretical derivations are present, they sometimes lack sufficient detail, requiring the reader to fill in gaps. For example, the justification for the sub-linear sufficient statistics in the SPGM feels somewhat less rigorously developed compared to the quadratic case. The empirical evaluation, while present, feels somewhat cursory. The simulation results are valuable but could be expanded to more comprehensively assess the performance of each model across different network structures and parameter settings. More detailed comparisons to alternative count data models (beyond those mentioned) would also be beneficial. A critical omission is a discussion of computational complexity. Estimating parameters in these models, particularly the QPGM and SPGM, seems non-trivial, and a discussion of scalability would be important. Finally, the connection between the choice of R0 in SPGM and its performance isn't thoroughly investigated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The QPGM and SPGM represent genuinely novel approaches to modeling multivariate count data, addressing a long-standing limitation of existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The core ideas are grounded in solid mathematical reasoning and address the normalizability issues inherent in extending the Poisson distribution.
*   **Potential Impact:** If scalable, these models could have a significant impact on fields dealing with high-dimensional count data, such as genomics and epidemiology.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and motivates the need for new methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope and depth.
*   **Lack of Computational Analysis:** The computational complexity of the proposed models is not addressed.
*   **Derivation Gaps:** Some of the theoretical derivations could benefit from more detailed explanations.
*   **SPGM justification:** The justification for the sub-linear statistic seems weaker than the quadratic case.



**Overall:**

Despite the minor flaws, this paper presents a significant contribution. The authors tackle a difficult problem with a bold and innovative approach. The QPGM and SPGM are conceptually sound and offer a promising avenue for modeling complex dependencies in multivariate count data. I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh the limitations. I recommend **acceptance**, with a request for the authors to address the points raised regarding computational complexity and to expand the empirical validation in a future version. The potential for this work to open up new directions in graphical modeling is high, and I am excited to see how it develops.
