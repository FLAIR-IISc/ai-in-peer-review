Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a long-standing gap in undirected graphical models for count data. It starts from the auto-Poisson/Poisson MRF construction of Besag and its generalization via node-conditional exponential families (Yang et al.), noting the well-known limitation that normalizability forces non-positive pairwise parameters, hence only negative conditional dependencies. The paper first examines truncation. It shows that the “Winsorized” (hard-capped) Poisson node-conditionals proposed by Kaiser and Cressie/Griffith are incompatible with any joint distribution under the standard compatibility results for conditional exponential families, thereby correcting a misconception in the literature. It then proposes a proper exponential-family truncation (TPGM) that redistributes tail mass over {0,…,R}, yielding a valid joint model that permits both positive and negative edges, at the cost of a bounded domain and R-dependent behavior.

The second contribution is more principled: it analyzes when a Poisson-like exponential family with linear sufficient statistic B(X)=X can permit positive interactions while remaining normalizable. Theorem 3 shows that if pairwise parameters are allowed to be positive, the base measure must grow at least quadratically. Motivated by this, the paper proposes (i) QPGM, which uses a quadratic base measure (or equivalently quadratic node potentials) and allows both positive and negative edges under a negative-definiteness-type condition on quadratic parameters; and (ii) SPGM, which uses a sublinear, saturating sufficient statistic B(x;R0,R) with the standard Poisson base measure, yielding heavier tails than QPGM and allowing unrestricted pairwise parameters while maintaining normalizability. For learning, the paper adopts neighborhood selection with ℓ1 penalized node-conditional likelihoods and proposes variational upper bounds on the intractable (or infinite) node-conditional log-partition functions for QPGM and SPGM. Experiments on simulated graphs and a breast cancer miRNA count dataset compare TPGM/SPGM to Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines, showing advantages for the proposed methods; the original Poisson MRF underperforms due to its negative-edge constraint.

Quality
- Theory: Theorem 1 (incompatibility of Winsorized node-conditionals) is important; please ensure the proof covers arbitrary E(XV\s) and clarifies which assumptions of the Hammersley–Clifford/conditional EF construction are violated. Theorem 3 (necessity of quadratic growth) is insightful; it would help to make explicit the growth regime (pointwise vs average-case) and whether the result is tight w.r.t. constants. For QPGM, the normalizability condition via Θ ≺ 0 (or negative definiteness of the quadratic form) is clear; a discussion of identifiability between diagonal quadratic node terms and pairwise terms would be helpful. SPGM’s normalizability for all pairwise parameters is convincing given bounded sufficient statistics and the Poisson base measure; however, the handling of negative θ in the variational bound (Proposition 3 is stated only for θ ≥ 0) should be clarified. Overall, the constructions are sound, but key proofs should be made accessible (appendix).
- Algorithms: Using variational upper bounds to surrogate the conditional partition functions is practical, but the statistical consequences (bias/consistency) are not analyzed. Convergence/stability details for proximal gradient with these surrogates are missing.
- Empirics: Simulations show ROC improvements versus Gaussian/nonparanormal. However, comparisons to other count models (e.g., Poisson log-normal latent Gaussian models, multivariate Poisson sum constructions with approximate inference, or negative-binomial-based approaches common in RNA-seq) are absent. Sensitivity to hyperparameters (R for TPGM; R0,R for SPGM) and to the variational approximations is not studied. Real-data analysis is interesting, but lacks quantitative goodness-of-fit or predictive evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with good motivation and clear positioning relative to Besag and GLM-based graphical models. The truncation discussion is helpful. The SPGM sufficient statistic should be plotted and its intuition expanded in the main text (not only appendix). Precisely define W (domain of counts) where it appears. Please streamline notation around partition functions (A, D, D̄) and clarify when sums are finite/infinite. Provide guidance on choosing R, R0 in practice and discuss their interpretability.

Originality
The work is novel in three respects: (i) the impossibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals in the graphical model construction; (ii) the necessity of quadratic base measure for allowing positive dependencies with linear sufficient statistics; and (iii) the concrete QPGM/SPGM constructions with tractable learning surrogates. These go beyond prior auto-Poisson and GLM-graphical model results.

Significance
If supported by stronger empirical evidence and clearer proofs, these models could materially expand the toolbox for multivariate count data with mixed-sign dependencies. The theoretical insights are likely to be influential. The practical impact hinges on guidance for parameter choices, robustness, and comparisons to established alternatives in applied domains (e.g., Poisson log-normal networks in ecology/genomics).

Pros
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of auto-Poisson MRFs; provides theoretically grounded alternatives.
- Clear correction to prior Winsorization claims; useful to the community.
- Insightful necessity result (C(Z)=Ω(Z^2)) shaping model design.
- Two new families (QPGM, SPGM) with complementary tail behaviors and parameter flexibility.
- Promising empirical signals on simulated and real count data.

Cons
- Proof accessibility and some technical gaps (bounds for θ < 0 in SPGM; identifiability).
- Learning relies on variational upper bounds without statistical guarantees.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to Poisson log-normal/latent Gaussian count network methods.
- Sensitivity to truncation/hyperparameters and robustness not explored.
- Real-data evaluation is qualitative; no predictive or held-out likelihood metrics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include complete proofs or detailed sketches for Theorems 1 and 3; clarify tightness and assumptions.
- Analyze the effect of variational surrogates on estimation (e.g., show that nodewise optimization still yields consistent graph recovery under conditions, or provide empirical calibration).
- Provide guidelines and sensitivity analyses for choosing R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM); investigate robustness.
- Compare against Poisson log-normal or negative-binomial-based network models and recent copula-based approaches for counts.
- Add quantitative evaluation on real data (held-out log-likelihood, posterior predictive checks, edge stability).
- Clarify computational cost and convergence behavior; release code if possible.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is theoretically interesting and potentially impactful, but the current empirical evaluation and some technical clarifications are below the bar for NeurIPS. With stronger proofs, broader baselines, and robustness/predictive assessments, this could become a strong submission.
