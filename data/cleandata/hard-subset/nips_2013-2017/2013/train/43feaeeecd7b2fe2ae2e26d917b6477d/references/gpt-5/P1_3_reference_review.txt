Summary
This paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the standard PGM is only normalizable when pairwise parameters are non-positive, which precludes modeling positive conditional dependencies. The authors (i) show that a previously proposed “Winsorized Poisson” approach—using node-conditionals that truncate a Poisson at R and move remaining mass to R—cannot yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1); (ii) propose a different truncation that preserves the exponential-family form, leading to a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that is normalizable on {0,…,R}^p and allows positive and negative edges (Theorem 2); (iii) analyze normalizability for Poisson-like models with linear sufficient statistics B(X)=X and show that to permit positive edges one needs a base measure scaling at least quadratically (Theorem 3); this motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with a quadratic base measure and negative-definite quadratic form to ensure normalizability (Proposition 2); and (iv) propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which keeps factorial base measure but uses sub-linear sufficient statistics that saturate beyond R, yielding normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They outline neighborhood selection for structure learning with proximal gradient, provide variational upper bounds to node-conditional partition functions (Gaussian for QPGM; Poisson for SPGM when θ≥0), and present simulations and a microRNA RNA-seq case study, showing improved network recovery relative to Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on Besag’s auto-models and the generalized GLM-to-MRF construction of Yang et al., and situates itself among multivariate Poisson constructions (sums of independent Poissons), copula/semiparametric GGMs, and spatial auto-Poisson literature (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith). The critique and correction of the Winsorization approach is a useful contribution.

Quality
- Theory: The results are well-motivated and appear technically sound. Theorem 1 (incompatibility of Winsorized node-conditionals with any joint for R>3) is strong; a clear proof sketch or intuition (e.g., via compatibility conditions for exponential-family conditionals) in the main text would help. Theorem 3’s “C(Z)=Ω(Z^2)” requirement is central; please make the asymptotic notion precise (constants, domain), and clarify whether this is a necessary condition for existence with θst of mixed signs or only for existence with some θst>0. The normalizability condition for QPGM via Θ≺0 is clear; the contrast between Θ<0 elementwise and Θ≺0 is insightful. The SPGM normalizability for all θst relies on bounded sufficient statistics; this is plausible and important.
- Optimization/estimation: Node-conditional likelihoods are used with ℓ1 penalties. Please clarify concavity and Lipschitz properties of the node-conditional objectives for QPGM and SPGM in the parameters; for QPGM, you rely on a Gaussian variational upper bound—do you optimize the bound (yielding a surrogate objective) or the exact conditional? If the former, does this bias selection? For SPGM, compute/approximation of D(θ,R0,R): how do you truncate the infinite sum in practice, and what error control is used?
- Experiments: Simulations show ROC improvements for TPGM/SPGM over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines, and the miRNA network case study is compelling. However, quantitative details are sparse: provide AUC numbers with variability, runtime, and sensitivity to R and (R0,R) choices. Since RNA-seq often exhibits overdispersion, a comparison to a negative-binomial-based approach or a copula-based method would strengthen claims. Showing recovery on data generated from SPGM or QPGM would also help separate estimation accuracy from model misspecification.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized despite covering three model families. A notational cleanup would help: C(Z) is used both as generic base measure and as log Z!; D(θ) vs A(θ) vs D̄(·) could be unified. Explicitly state assumptions under which each theorem holds (e.g., R>3 in Theorem 1, domain, boundedness of B(·)). Provide brief proof sketches (or cite supplemental proofs) for Theorems 1–4 so readers can assess key steps.
- Practical guidance for choosing R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM) is needed. Simple rules (e.g., quantiles of observed counts, stability selection over these hyperparameters) would increase usability.

Originality
- The work is original and nontrivial. It corrects a misconception in the spatial statistics literature (Winsorization), provides a necessary growth-rate condition for base measures in Poisson-like MRFs with linear sufficient statistics, and introduces two new model classes (QPGM, SPGM) that allow positive and negative conditional dependencies on count domains. The sublinear sufficient statistic construction is particularly elegant.

Significance
- There is clear need for graphical models for counts that can capture both positive and negative dependencies. The proposed models expand the toolkit and, in principle, enable structure learning in a wide range of applications (sequencing, text, incidence). If the computational aspects are scalable and the learning procedures can be shown sparsistent (as suggested), this could be influential.

Pros
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson MRFs; permits positive edges.
- Clear theoretical contributions (impossibility result; necessary condition; normalizability conditions).
- Three complementary model families with distinct trade-offs (finite support vs. heavier tails vs. Gaussian-like).
- Practical learning strategy and promising empirical results on simulated and real RNA-seq networks.
- Correction of prior literature (Winsorized node-conditionals).

Cons
- Lack of formal consistency/sparsistency guarantees for the proposed estimators.
- Limited guidance on hyperparameter selection (R, R0,R) and sensitivity analyses.
- Computational details (approximation accuracy, runtime) are light; QPGM optimization via variational bounds needs clarification.
- Experimental comparisons omit alternatives tailored to overdispersed counts (e.g., negative binomial, copula-Poisson).
- Some theorems’ statements/assumptions could be more precise in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide proof sketches or pointer to appendix for Theorems 1–4; clarify Theorem 3’s “Ω(·)” rigor.
- Add practical procedures for choosing truncation/sublinearity parameters and report sensitivity.
- Report AUCs with confidence intervals, and runtime scaling with p and n.
- Compare to at least one overdispersed-count baseline; include experiments where data are generated from SPGM/QPGM.
- Clarify whether the neighborhood selection optimizes exact or surrogate objectives; discuss convexity.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers substantial theoretical and modeling advances on an important problem, with promising empirical evidence. Addressing clarity and evaluation gaps would strengthen it further.
