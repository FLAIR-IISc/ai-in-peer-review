- Paper identifies a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson graphical models: they can only model negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints.
- Shows the Winsorized Poisson approach is fundamentally flawed and builds on Besag and Yang et al.'s theoretical foundations.
- Proposes three novel models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that permit both positive and negative dependencies while satisfying normalizability.
- Theoretical contributions are strong, with rigorous proofs (Theorems 1–4, Propositions 1–3) establishing normalizability conditions.
- SPGM is highlighted as especially promising, offering heavier tails than QPGM.
- Empirical results and ROC curves demonstrate the proposed models’ superiority over existing methods for count data.
- Real-data breast cancer microRNA case study shows the original PGM fails to capture biologically meaningful relationships, while the new models do.
- Authors compare appropriately to relevant baselines (Gaussian, non-paranormal) and note original PGM’s negative-weight limitation.
- Paper lacks sufficient discussion of computational complexity and scalability in high-dimensional settings.
- SPGM’s claimed heavier-tail behavior needs more thorough theoretical and empirical analysis.
- Comparison with recent nonparametric graphical-model work for count data is insufficient.
- Experimental section would benefit from parameter sensitivity analysis (e.g., R in TPGM, R0/R in SPGM).
- Implementation details (e.g., proximal gradient ascent) are not fully specified, hindering reproducibility.
- Overall assessment: significant theoretical advance with compelling empirical validation and clear practical relevance.
- Recommendation: strong accept — contributions outweigh the noted minor weaknesses.
