This paper tackles a significant problem: modeling multivariate count data, a common scenario lacking adequate graphical model solutions. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches – exponential parameter growth in log-linear models, intractable multivariate Poisson distributions, and the negative dependency restriction of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs). Their proposed solutions – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – represent a genuinely novel and bold attempt to overcome these hurdles. The core idea of modifying the base measure and/or sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution to allow for both positive and negative dependencies is innovative and conceptually sound.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The exploration of truncated and modified Poisson distributions for graphical modeling is highly original. Existing literature largely accepts the limitations of the standard PGM.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates a strong theoretical foundation, rigorously proving the non-existence of a consistent joint distribution for the Kaiser & Cressie truncation approach (Theorem 1) and establishing the normalizability conditions for the QPGM and SPGM.
* **Potential Significance:** Successful development of graphical models for count data could have a substantial impact on numerous fields, particularly genomics, epidemiology, and spatial statistics where count data is prevalent.
* **Exploration of Alternatives:** The progression from TPGM (truncation) to QPGM and SPGM demonstrates a thoughtful exploration of the design space, addressing the limitations of each preceding approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of simulation studies and a case study with real miRNA data provides initial evidence supporting the practical applicability of the proposed models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Challenges of SPGM:** While theoretically appealing, the computational complexity of the SPGM’s log-partition function (requiring approximation with a Poisson distribution) is a potential practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this but don't fully explore its impact.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The simulation studies, while present, are relatively basic. Investigating more complex network structures and varying the degree of dependence would strengthen the empirical validation.  The performance compared to the non-paranormal SKEPTIC would be particularly informative.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The impact of the truncation parameters (R and R0) in the TPGM and SPGM respectively on model performance is not thoroughly investigated. A sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Connection to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, a deeper connection to the broader literature on generalized linear models and exponential families within the context of graphical modeling could be made. For example, discussing how the proposed models relate to other methods for handling non-Gaussian data in GGMs could add context.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, some notation (e.g., in the definition of B(x;R0,R) ) could be clarified for improved readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
* The theoretical contributions (especially Theorems 1, 2, 3 & 4) are substantial.
* The initial empirical results are promising.
* The paper pushes the boundaries of existing knowledge in graphical modeling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the SPGM remains a concern.
* The empirical validation could be more comprehensive.
* Further investigation into parameter sensitivity is needed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. Despite some minor weaknesses, the novelty and theoretical depth of the proposed solutions outweigh the limitations. The authors tackle a long-standing problem in a creative and rigorous manner. The SPGM, in particular, offers a compelling framework for modeling complex dependencies in count data. I believe this work represents a valuable contribution to the field and warrants acceptance. I would encourage the authors to address the reviewer’s concerns regarding computational complexity and empirical validation in future work.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
