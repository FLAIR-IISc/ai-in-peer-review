This paper introduces novel graphical models for multivariate count data, addressing limitations of existing approaches like the Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) which can only model negative dependencies. The authors first demonstrate the incorrectness of a truncation approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie [20] and then propose three new models: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper provides theoretical justification for each model and illustrates their performance on simulated and real-world breast cancer microRNA expression data.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound. The mathematical derivations, particularly the proofs regarding the normalizability of the proposed models, appear rigorous. The motivation for each model is clearly presented, building logically from the shortcomings of the original PGM and the failed truncation attempts. The experimental validation, while not exhaustive, provides initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed models, especially the SPGM. However, the connection between the theoretical sub-linearity and its impact on the resulting dependencies could be elaborated further. A potential weakness lies in the reliance on variational approximations for the SPGM, which may introduce bias, though this is acknowledged. The discussion of limitations is reasonable, particularly regarding the computational complexity of inference in these models, which is not addressed in detail.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The mathematical notation is consistent and accessible. Each model is introduced with a clear explanation of its underlying principles and assumptions. The simulation setup and results are described adequately. While the paper is dense with mathematical details, the authors do a good job of explaining the key concepts in a digestible manner. Section 2.1.1 could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the proposed truncation approach fails, rather than simply stating it's incorrect.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The identification of the quadratic base measure requirement for enabling both positive and negative dependencies is a significant insight. The introduction of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represents genuinely new modeling approaches. The extensive comparison with existing methods (PGM, Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal) and the application to a real-world dataset further highlight the novelty of the work.  While the concept of truncation is not new, the authors’ theoretical analysis and the development of the TPGM and SPGM based on modified base measures are original contributions.

**Significance:** The proposed models address a significant gap in the literature by providing flexible and theoretically grounded graphical models capable of capturing both positive and negative dependencies in multivariate count data. This is particularly relevant in fields such as genomics, epidemiology, and spatial statistics where count data is prevalent. The application to microRNA expression data demonstrates the potential practical impact of these models.  The development of the SPGM, with its heavier tails, is particularly promising.  The potential for applying these models to other domains and the possibility of extending the inference algorithms are significant avenues for future research.  



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound models for multivariate count data.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing methods (PGM).
*   Provides clear mathematical justification and experimental validation.
*   Potential for broad applicability in various fields.
*   Well-written and clearly organized presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity of inference is not thoroughly discussed.
*   Reliance on variational approximations for SPGM may introduce bias.
*   Limited scope of experimental validation – more extensive simulations and applications would strengthen the findings.
*   The connection between sub-linearity and the types of dependencies modelled could be made more explicit.



**Overall Assessment:**  Strong Accept. This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The authors have addressed a challenging problem with novel and theoretically sound solutions. While there are some minor limitations, the potential impact of this work is substantial, and the paper is well-deserving of acceptance to NIPS.
