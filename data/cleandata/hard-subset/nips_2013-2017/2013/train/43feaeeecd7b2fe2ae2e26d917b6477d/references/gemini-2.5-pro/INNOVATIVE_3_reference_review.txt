This paper addresses a fundamental and long-standing limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs): their inability to model positive dependencies between variables due to normalizability constraints. The authors tackle this problem by proposing three novel classes of graphical models for count data, each allowing for rich dependence structures.

First, the paper provides a crucial negative result, demonstrating that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson truncation approach by Kaiser and Cressie is flawed and cannot lead to a consistent joint distribution. Building on this, the authors introduce their first contribution: a new Truncated PGM (TPGM) based on a different truncation scheme that properly belongs to the exponential family, thus guaranteeing a valid joint MRF. This model successfully allows for both positive and negative dependencies.

Recognizing the limitations of truncation (namely, the dependence on the truncation level R), the paper then introduces two more fundamental and conceptually novel models. The authors insightfully identify that the normalizability issue in the original PGM stems from the linear sufficient statistic (`X`) growing faster than the base measure (`log(X!)`). Their second contribution, the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), addresses this by introducing a quadratic base measure (`X^2`), ensuring normalizability under conditions similar to a Gaussian Graphical Model (GGM). The final and perhaps most creative contribution, the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), takes a different route. Instead of modifying the base measure, it modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear, effectively taming the problematic quadratic interaction terms while retaining the original Poisson base measure. This allows for normalizability with arbitrary edge weights. The paper concludes with simulations and a real-world application on microRNA expression data, demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. It does not offer a mere incremental improvement but instead presents a suite of bold solutions to a core problem in an important model class. The identification of the root cause of the PGM limitation (the scaling of the sufficient statistic vs. the base measure) is insightful. The resulting QPGM and SPGM are not simple extensions but rather fundamentally new models for count data, which is a significant conceptual contribution. The SPGM, in particular, with its modified sufficient statistic, is a very creative and elegant solution.

2.  **Significance and Impact:** The problem of modeling multivariate count data with mixed dependencies is highly relevant and has broad applicability in fields like genomics, neuroscience, and social sciences. By providing the first tractable graphical models for this setting, this work opens up many new avenues for both research and practice. The demonstration on the TCGA breast cancer data, showing the discovery of biologically relevant hubs missed by GGMs and the original PGM, effectively underscores the potential impact of this work.

3.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. It clearly articulates the problem, critiques prior work (including a formal disproof in Theorem 1), and motivates each of its proposed solutions. The theoretical contributions are presented as formal theorems and propositions, providing a solid foundation for the new models.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments are illustrative, they feel somewhat preliminary. The QPGM is introduced theoretically but is absent from the numerical experiments, leaving its practical performance unevaluated. Furthermore, the simulations for TPGM and SPGM are conducted on data generated from other models, which is a fair test of robustness, but it would have been reassuring to also see a confirmation of sparsistency on data generated from the SPGM itself.

2.  **Practical Considerations:** The proposed models introduce new hyperparameters (R for TPGM; R0 and R for SPGM). The paper sets these based on the data's maximum value, but a more principled or data-driven method for their selection (e.g., via cross-validation) is not discussed. This is an important practical detail for future users of these models.

3.  **Deferred Theoretical Analysis:** The authors correctly note that since their models fit within the framework of Yang et al. (2012), theoretical guarantees for structure recovery (sparsistency) are expected to hold. However, this analysis is left for future work. While acceptable for a paper focused on model specification, its inclusion would have made the contribution more complete.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper tackles an important, well-known problem with a series of creative, novel, and theoretically-grounded solutions. The conceptual leap from simple truncation to fundamentally altering the base measure or sufficient statistics is precisely the kind of bold thinking that should be encouraged. The weaknesses identified are minor in comparison to the significance of the contributions. The limited experimental section and deferred theoretical analysis are acceptable trade-offs for a paper that introduces several new and promising directions for modeling a challenging data type. This work is likely to be highly influential and to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.
