### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a critical limitation of the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM), as defined in the lineage of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19]. The standard PGM is restricted to modeling only negative conditional dependencies (i.e., non-positive edge weights) due to normalizability constraints on its infinite domain. This severely limits its applicability to real-world multivariate count data, which often exhibit both positive and negative correlations.

The authors make several key contributions to overcome this limitation. First, they analyze a previously proposed solution by Kaiser and Cressie [20] that uses a "Winsorized" Poisson for the node-conditional distributions. The authors prove that this approach does not lead to a consistent joint distribution, thereby correcting an error in the existing literature.

Second, the paper introduces a new, correctly formulated **Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM)**. By using a different truncation scheme that preserves the exponential family form, they derive a valid graphical model on a finite domain that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.

Third, to address the bounded domain limitation of the TPGM, the paper proposes two novel classes of graphical models for count data with infinite support. It first provides a theoretical result (Theorem 3) showing that for a graphical model with linear sufficient statistics (like the Poisson) to be normalizable with positive edge weights, its base measure must scale at least quadratically. This insight motivates the **Quadratic PGM (QPGM)**, which uses a quadratic base measure, allowing for mixed dependencies at the cost of having Gaussian-like thin tails. To allow for heavier tails, the paper then proposes the **Sublinear PGM (SPGM)**, which retains the original Poisson base measure but modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear. This construction ensures normalizability for any real-valued edge parameters.

Finally, the authors demonstrate the practical utility of the TPGM and SPGM through numerical experiments on simulated data and a real-world application to breast cancer microRNA sequencing data, showing improved performance over Gaussian-based methods and the standard PGM.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical contributions are rigorous. Theorem 1, which refutes the validity of the Winsorized PGM approach from [20], is a significant correction to the literature. The motivation for the QPGM is well-grounded in the theoretical analysis of Theorem 3, which clearly identifies the interplay between the sufficient statistics, base measure, and normalizability. The construction of all three novel models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) is clear and well-justified within the framework of graphical models based on exponential families [18]. The experimental section provides compelling empirical evidence for the utility of the proposed TPGM and SPGM, demonstrating superior network recovery on count data compared to standard methods. A minor weakness is the omission of theoretical guarantees (e.g., sparsistency) for the proposed neighborhood selection estimators. While the authors state that an extension of the analysis from [18, 19] is expected, its absence makes the estimation part of the work less complete. Additionally, the QPGM model is introduced theoretically but is absent from the experimental evaluation, leaving its practical performance unevaluated.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by explaining the limitations of existing models for multivariate count data. The progression of ideas is logical, starting from an analysis of truncation methods (TPGM) and then moving to more fundamental modifications of the Poisson distribution itself (QPGM and SPGM). The mathematical derivations are presented clearly. The distinction between the incorrect Winsorized approach and the authors' valid TPGM is explained well. The figures in the experimental section are informative and support the paper's conclusions.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original. It addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem in an important model class. The contributions are not incremental.
1.  The disproof of the Winsorized PGM approach is a novel and important contribution.
2.  The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are all novel classes of graphical models for count data. The underlying ideas—modifying the base measure or the sufficient statistics to ensure normalizability while allowing for rich dependency structures—are innovative.
3.  The theoretical link established between the growth rate of the base measure and the feasibility of positive dependencies (Theorem 3) is a new and valuable insight.
The work is well-situated within the context of previous literature, and its departures from prior art are made explicit.

**Significance:**
The results presented are highly significant. Multivariate count data is ubiquitous in many scientific fields, including genomics, neuroscience, and social sciences. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive correlations has been a major barrier to their use. This paper provides practitioners and researchers with three new, flexible, and theoretically-grounded tools for analyzing such data. The models are likely to be widely adopted and to inspire further research into graphical models for non-Gaussian data. The successful application to miRNA expression data underscores the practical importance of this work, demonstrating the ability to uncover biologically relevant network structures missed by other methods.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Solves a fundamental problem:** The paper provides the first valid and practical graphical model frameworks for count data that can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies, overcoming a major limitation of the standard PGM.
*   **Strong theoretical contributions:** The paper includes a significant negative result correcting the literature (Theorem 1), a novel theoretical insight motivating model design (Theorem 3), and proofs for the validity of three new model classes.
*   **High novelty and significance:** It introduces three distinct and innovative classes of graphical models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that are of high practical importance for a wide range of applications involving count data.
*   **Clarity and completeness:** The work is well-written, logically structured, and presents a thorough investigation of the problem from multiple angles.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Lack of estimation theory:** The paper focuses primarily on model specification and does not provide theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency or sample complexity) for the learning algorithms. This is cited as future work.
*   **Practicality of hyperparameters:** The SPGM and TPGM introduce new hyperparameters (R, R0) whose selection is critical. The paper provides limited guidance on how to set these in a principled way beyond heuristics used in the experiments.
*   **Incomplete experimental validation:** The QPGM is theoretically developed but not empirically evaluated, making it difficult to assess its practical performance against the other proposed models and baselines like the GGM.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, technical soundness, and significance in solving an important open problem—far outweigh its weaknesses. The theoretical contributions alone are substantial, and the proposed models represent a major step forward for the analysis of multivariate count data.
