This paper addresses a significant and long-standing limitation in graphical models for count data: the inability of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) to model positive dependencies. The authors make several important contributions, proposing three novel classes of graphical models that overcome this issue.

The paper begins by reviewing the standard PGM, which builds a multivariate distribution from Poisson node-conditional distributions. It highlights the model's critical flaw, established by Besag [17], that the joint distribution is only normalizable if the edge parameters (representing conditional dependencies) are non-positive. This severely restricts its applicability. The paper then critiques a previously proposed solution from Kaiser and Cressie [20] based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution. In a key negative result (Theorem 1), the authors prove that this approach is fundamentally flawed and can never lead to a consistent joint distribution.

The authors then introduce their own contributions. The first is the Truncated PGM (TPGM), a model based on a different truncation scheme that correctly preserves the exponential family structure required for a valid joint distribution. This model successfully allows for both positive and negative dependencies. However, the authors astutely note its limitations: the truncation level `R` imposes an a priori bound on the data and can create modeling artifacts, especially for variables with values close to `R`.

Motivated by the limitations of truncation, the paper delves into the fundamental structure of the PGM. In Theorem 3, it provides a crucial insight: for a graphical model with linear sufficient statistics (`B(X)=X`), the base measure must grow at least quadratically (`C(X)=Ω(X^2)`) to ensure normalizability with positive edge weights. This leads to their second model, the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure. While this solves the normalizability problem, it results in a model with Gaussian-like thin tails.

To address the tail behavior of the QPGM while retaining modeling flexibility, the authors propose their third and most innovative model, the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). Instead of modifying the base measure, this model modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear, effectively capping its growth. This clever construction allows the model to retain the original Poisson `log(X!)` base measure (and its associated tail properties) while ensuring normalizability for any real-valued edge parameters (Theorem 4).

Finally, the paper provides empirical validation on both simulated data and a real-world application of learning a breast cancer microRNA expression network. The experiments show that the proposed TPGM and SPGM models outperform existing methods like Gaussian Graphical Models and non-paranormal estimators for count data, and are far more expressive than the original PGM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known, important problem and provides a set of compelling and novel solutions. The inability of PGMs to model positive correlations has been a major roadblock for their practical use. The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM models represent a significant advancement in the statistical toolkit for analyzing multivariate count data. The SPGM, in particular, is a very creative and elegant solution.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. It includes a valuable negative result (Theorem 1) that corrects a misconception in the literature. The theoretical motivations for the QPGM (Theorem 3) and the normalizability proof for the SPGM (Theorem 4) are sound and provide a solid foundation for the proposed models. The logical progression from critiquing existing work to proposing increasingly sophisticated solutions is excellent.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the development of each new model logically follows from the limitations of the previous one. The core ideas are communicated effectively, making a complex topic accessible.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not the primary focus, effectively demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed models. The application to miRNA sequencing data is compelling, showing that the new models can uncover biologically relevant network structures (e.g., hub miRNAs, cliques) missed by both standard PGMs and Gaussian-based methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Learning Theory:** The paper focuses on model specification and appropriately defers a full theoretical analysis of structure learning (e.g., sparsistency) to future work. While understandable given space constraints, including a brief discussion or high-level sketch of how the analysis from Yang et al. [18, 19] might extend to these new models would strengthen the paper's claims about learnability.
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The TPGM and SPGM models introduce new hyperparameters (`R` and `R0`). The experimental section sets these based on the data's maximum value, but a more thorough discussion of their impact and potential strategies for selection (e.g., cross-validation) would be beneficial for practitioners looking to apply these models. A small sensitivity analysis would have been a valuable addition.
3.  **QPGM vs. Transformed GGM:** The paper notes the QPGM's similarity to a Gaussian graphical model. It would be helpful to include a more explicit discussion on the trade-offs between using the QPGM directly on counts versus applying a variance-stabilizing or non-paranormal transformation to the counts and then using a standard GGM. The paper compares against the non-paranormal method empirically, but a deeper discussion of the modeling assumptions would be insightful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides novel and well-motivated solutions to a fundamental, long-standing problem in graphical modeling.
*   The contributions are technically strong, including an important negative result and the development of three distinct and useful models.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and advances the state of the art for modeling a ubiquitous data type (counts).
*   The empirical results, especially the case study, demonstrate clear practical significance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical guarantees for the learning procedure are mentioned but not provided, which leaves a key aspect of the work as an assumption.
*   The practical guidance on setting the new hyperparameters in the TPGM and SPGM is limited.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of probabilistic graphical models that is very well-suited for NIPS. The proposed models are likely to be widely adopted and built upon by researchers working with high-dimensional count data. I strongly recommend acceptance.
