Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the pairwise interaction parameters must be non-positive to ensure normalizability, which has restricted PGMs to model only negative conditional dependencies (Besag, 1974; Yang et al., 2012/2013). The authors make three main contributions.

1) They carefully revisit truncation/Winsorization. They show that the Winsorized Poisson node-conditional advocated in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot be coupled into a consistent joint MRF via the standard Hammersley–Clifford/exponential family construction. They then propose a different truncated exponential-family Poisson that yields a valid joint distribution (TPGM) with finite partition function for any parameters, allowing both positive and negative edges. They also discuss its limitations as R grows.

2) They introduce a broader family of count MRFs by modifying the univariate exponential-family building block. With linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x but a different base measure C(x), they prove that to admit both positive and negative pairwise interactions while maintaining normalizability, C(x) must grow at least quadratically (C(x)=Ω(x^2)). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which augments the energy with quadratic unaries X_s^2, and is normalizable under a negative-definiteness-type condition on the quadratic form, thereby enabling both positive and negative edges (analogous to Gaussian MRFs). They also note a variational upper bound via a Gaussian conditional.

3) To obtain heavier tails and milder penalties on large counts, they propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that modifies the sufficient statistic B(x) to be linear up to a threshold R0 and then gradually saturating until R, while retaining the Poisson base measure log x!. They prove normalizability with no sign constraints on pairwise parameters, and provide a simple variational upper bound on the node-conditional partition function when the canonical parameter is nonnegative.

They demonstrate neighborhood selection with ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihood for TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM, compare against Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines on synthetic data (hub/scale-free graphs) and present a case study on TCGA breast cancer miRNA count data, highlighting hubs and a chromosome 19 clique.

Quality
– Theory: The normalizability arguments and impossibility result for the Winsorized construction are important and appear technically sound at a high level. The necessity result C(x)=Ω(x^2) under linear sufficient statistics is both insightful and useful. However, the main text omits proof details; these need to be fully provided in the supplement, with precise conditions (e.g., growth rates, notions of Ω for integer-valued arguments, boundary cases). Clarify the exact assumptions under which Theorem 1 (no joint for Winsorized conditionals) holds and whether counterexamples exist at small R or special graphs.

– Algorithms: The paper states that estimation uses proximal gradient on node-conditional likelihoods. For QPGM and SPGM, node-conditional log-partition functions are not closed form (except for bounds). It is unclear whether training uses exact sums, truncation, variational bounds (and if so, which), or other approximations, and how this affects consistency and bias. Convergence guarantees and computational complexity are not discussed.

– Experiments: The simulations use ROC curves and show TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-family baselines when data are count-valued. However, the baselines omit key competitors for counts (e.g., Poisson log-normal/latent Gaussian copulas, negative binomial or COM-Poisson based approaches, or spatial count MRFs). The data generation includes the sum-of-Poissons model that only induces positive correlations, but comparisons could be made more comprehensive and quantitative (e.g., AUPRC, calibration, held-out likelihood). The real-data analysis is qualitative; sensitivity to hyperparameters (R, R0) is not assessed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well motivated. The construction via exponential-family node-conditional distributions is clearly articulated. However:
– Several references are incomplete; please provide full bibliographic details and ensure related work is adequately covered (e.g., Conway–Maxwell–Poisson for over/underdispersion; latent Gaussian copula or Poisson log-normal graphical models; spatial count MRFs beyond Winsorization).
– Notation sometimes shifts (e.g., W vs ℕ); define all symbols once and keep consistent.
– Explicitly state assumptions for all propositions/theorems, including domains, boundary cases, and precise definitions for asymptotic notations with integer arguments.
– Provide algorithmic details: gradients, use of bounds vs exact sums, truncation schemes, and complexity.

Originality
High. The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals in the MRF construction is new and clarifying. The necessity of quadratic-or-higher base measure for linear sufficient statistics to permit both positive and negative edges is an insightful structural result. The QPGM and SPGM classes are novel graphical models for counts, bridging Poisson and Gaussian-like behaviors and enabling richer dependencies.

Significance
Promising. Modeling conditional dependence in high-dimensional count data is important in genomics and other domains. Providing families that admit both positive and negative interactions with clear normalizability conditions fills a notable gap. If accompanied by solid, reproducible estimation procedures and stronger empirical validation, these models could see broad use. As it stands, the experimental section is suggestive but not yet definitive.

Pros
– Clear identification of a core limitation of PGMs and rigorous treatment of truncation/Winsorization.
– Elegant theoretical insight (C(x)=Ω(x^2) necessity) guiding model design.
– Introduction of two practical families (QPGM, SPGM) that allow rich dependencies.
– Empirical results show advantages over Gaussian-based baselines for counts.
– Real-data case study yields biologically plausible findings.

Cons
– Missing or incomplete proofs and conditions in the main text; reproducibility hindered.
– Estimation methodology for QPGM/SPGM lacks detail on partition-function handling, approximations, and computational costs.
– Limited baselines; no comparisons to latent-Gaussian or overdispersed count models.
– Sensitivity to hyperparameters (R, R0) and choice rationale are not analyzed.
– Real-data validation remains qualitative; no held-out likelihood or stability analysis beyond STARS for sparsity.

Suggestions for improvement
– Include full proofs and precise statements for all theorems/propositions in the supplement.
– Specify the exact training objectives for each model, how node-conditional normalizers are computed or approximated, convergence diagnostics, and complexity; provide pseudocode.
– Add baselines tailored to counts (e.g., Poisson log-normal, negative binomial, COM-Poisson-based models; copula models for counts).
– Report additional metrics (AUPRC, calibration, held-out predictive likelihood) and ablations on R and R0 choices; analyze robustness and computational scalability.
– Clarify identifiability and interpretability of parameters under SPGM’s saturating sufficient statistics.
– Expand and update related work; complete references.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The conceptual and theoretical contributions are strong and original, but the paper does not yet meet NeurIPS standards in terms of reproducibility and empirical validation. With complete proofs, clearer estimation methodology, stronger and broader experiments, and analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, this could become a compelling contribution.
