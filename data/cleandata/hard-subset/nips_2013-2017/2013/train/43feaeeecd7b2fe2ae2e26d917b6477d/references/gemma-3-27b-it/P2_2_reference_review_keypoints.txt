- Summary: Addresses modeling multivariate count data, identifies PGMs' inability to model negative dependencies, proposes TPGM, QPGM, SPGM, and provides preliminary empirical validation.
- Important problem: Tackles a significant and broadly relevant challenge in modeling multivariate count data.
- Clear motivation & literature review: Strong motivation and comprehensive overview of prior approaches and limitations.
- Rigorous theoretical analysis: Provides solid theoretical results (notably Theorems 1 and 3) demonstrating limitations of Winsorization and normalizability conditions.
- Novel contributions: Introduces three new models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that relax PGM restrictions to allow positive and negative dependencies.
- Empirical validation: Simulation and breast cancer miRNA experiments show promising, competitive performance.
- Connections to existing work: Effectively situates contributions within graphical models, exponential families, and count-data literature.
- Complexity: QPGM and SPGM increase conceptual and computational complexity, raising scalability concerns for high-dimensional data.
- Limited empirical scope: Experiments are relatively narrow; more datasets, network structures, and metrics (e.g., precision-recall, SHD) are needed.
- Variational bound concern: Reliance on a variational bound for QPGM lacks analysis of bound tightness and its effect on results.
- Computational details missing: Implementation specifics (proximal gradient ascent, parameter tuning) are insufficient for reproducibility.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Sensitivity to choices of R and R0 in TPGM/SPGM is not discussed.
- Review scores: Quality 9/10 (technically sound), Clarity 8/10 (well-written but dense math), Originality 9/10, Significance 8/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a key limitation, presents rigorous theory, offers three innovative models, and shows promising empirical results.
- Arguments for rejection: Limited empirical validation, potential computational impracticality for large datasets, and missing implementation details.
- Overall recommendation: Strong Accept, with encouragement to address empirical breadth, computational scaling, and implementation/hyperparameter details.
