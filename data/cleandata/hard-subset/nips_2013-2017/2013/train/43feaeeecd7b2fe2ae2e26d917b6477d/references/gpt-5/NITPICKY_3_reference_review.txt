Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles an important gap: undirected graphical models for count-valued data that allow both positive and negative conditional dependencies. It revisits Besag’s auto-model construction and later generalizations to exponential family node-conditionals (Yang et al.), clarifies the normalizability issue that forces non-positive pairwise parameters in the classical Poisson graphical model (PGM), and proposes three alternatives:

- A negative result: shows that “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) are incompatible and cannot yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1).
- TPGM: replaces Winsorization by an exponential-family truncation that keeps the canonical form on a finite support {0,…,R} and, by Brook’s/Hammersley–Clifford style arguments, admits a unique joint with pairwise potentials (Theorem 2). Finite support makes A(θ) finite for all θ, permitting positive and negative edges, though with practical drawbacks as R increases.
- QPGM: keeps linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x but modifies the base measure C(x), arguing that allowing positive edges requires C(x)=Ω(x^2) (Theorem 3). This motivates quadratic self-potentials (θss x^2) and a normalizability condition equivalent to Θ≺0 (or XTΘX ≤ −c||X||^2), which allows both signs for θst if θss are sufficiently negative (Proposition 2). A Gaussian variational bound is proposed for nodewise learning.
- SPGM: to avoid Gaussian-thin tails, makes the sufficient statistic sublinear and eventually constant beyond R (with R0<R), while retaining the Poisson base measure log x!. This caps the pairwise contribution and, together with log x! growth, guarantees normalizability for any θst (Theorem 4). A Poisson variational bound (for θ≥0) is provided for nodewise learning.

Experiments include ROC-based simulations (data generated by TPGM and by sums of independent Poissons) comparing TPGM/SPGM to Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines, and a case study inferring a breast cancer miRNA network from TCGA RNA-seq.

Strengths:
- Addresses a real and longstanding limitation of Poisson MRFs (negative-only edges).
- Clear articulation of why truncation matters and why Winsorization breaks exponential-family structure.
- Conceptual contributions: (i) compatibility and uniqueness for truncated exponential-family node-conditionals (TPGM), (ii) necessity insight that with linear B(x) one needs ≥quadratic base measure to allow positive pairwise terms (Theorem 3), (iii) a pragmatic alternative (SPGM) with bounded sufficient statistics enabling all-sign edges and heavier tails than QPGM.
- Practical angle: variational bounds for nodewise learning and empirical demonstrations.
- Potential impact for genomics and other count data domains.

Weaknesses and concerns (technical and methodological):
- Theorem 1 (Winsorized incompatibility) is a strong and surprising negative result; no proof sketch is provided here. The statement allows E(XV\s) to be “an arbitrary function,” which weakens the claim’s interpretability. Please provide a rigorous proof or at least a principled sketch clarifying the role of support, clique order, and the indicator term I(Xs=R); otherwise this undermines a key contribution.
- Notation/sign inconsistencies:
  - Section 3 defines the univariate exponential family with −C(Z), but equation (4) uses +C(Xs). This is inconsistent with earlier Poisson parameterization and will confuse readers.
  - W, Wp, and X are used inconsistently to denote domains; Wp is never defined.
  - “XV\s” and “X\s” both appear; unify notation.
- Theorem 3 (necessity of C(z)=Ω(z^2)) needs clearer assumptions and a precise statement. The current “θ≰0” phrasing is vague. The argument presumably exploits growth along directions where a positive θst yields quadratic growth (x^2) in the exponent; please specify mild conditions (e.g., existence of a positive eigenvalue direction), clarify asymptotics (big-Ω over integers), and the role of degree or topology.
- QPGM normalizability (Proposition 2) relies on global negative-definiteness of Θ. This is a restrictive global constraint. The paper proposes nodewise regressions with a Gaussian bound but does not address how to enforce global normalizability constraints across nodes, nor whether the estimated θ from decoupled regressions produce a valid joint. This is a major gap for a graphical model paper.
- Variational bounds:
  - The inequality replacing a discrete log-partition by a continuous Gaussian integral needs justification. The inequality “log ∑ exp(…) ≤ log ∫ exp(…)” is not generally valid without monotonicity or bounding arguments; a precise bound (e.g., via Euler–Maclaurin or Riemann sum bounds) should be provided.
  - Proposition 3 bound for SPGM is plausible (B(x)≤x for θ≥0), but the conditions should be stated clearly and the proof supplied.
- Hyperparameters R and R0: there is no principled selection strategy, sensitivity analysis, or guidance. In practice these heavily control behavior (TPGM boundary effects, SPGM saturation). Setting R to the maximum observed count is ad hoc and data-leaking. This limits practical utility.
- Experiments:
  - Baselines are incomplete. Important comparators for count data (e.g., Poisson log-normal graphical models, latent Gaussian copula for counts, negative binomial variants) are missing.
  - No evaluation of QPGM at all.
  - Simulation details are sparse (Gibbs sampling burn-in/length, parameter ranges, degree, overdispersion). Only ROC curves; no PR curves, no variability bars.
  - The case study is troubling: “R=11, the maximum count” is implausible for raw RNA-seq miRNA counts unless heavily preprocessed/discretized; preprocessing and offsets (library size normalization) are not described. Without offsets, Poisson-based nodewise regressions on RNA-seq are questionable. Biological validation is anecdotal (two hubs) without enrichment analyses or external validation.
- Reproducibility: proofs, implementation details, and figures are missing here; references are incomplete/inconsistent (numerous entries lack years/venues; typos).
- Minor but numerous issues: grammar/typos; equation (2) typesetting; “Sublinear PGM” parameters need clearer constraints (e.g., R0<R and continuity at knots); figure references to an appendix not provided.

Pros:
- Important problem; clear statement of PGM limitations.
- Interesting constructions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with theoretical normalizability insights.
- Practical nodewise learning ideas with variational surrogates.

Cons:
- Key theoretical claim (Theorem 1) lacks proof/clarity.
- Estimation/inference story is incomplete (global validity, constraint handling).
- Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity absent.
- Experimental evaluation limited and missing critical baselines; case study preprocessing unclear and potentially flawed.
- Numerous notation/formatting errors and missing details.

Recommendation: Reject (leaning toward weak reject if proofs and experimental details exist in a full appendix). The ideas are promising, but the accumulation of theoretical, methodological, and presentation issues prevents confident acceptance at this stage.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide rigorous proofs or detailed sketches for Theorems 1–4 and bounds; fix sign/notation inconsistencies.
- Clarify global normalizability constraints in learning and propose a coherent joint estimator (or certify nodewise solutions).
- Add principled procedures for choosing R,R0 (e.g., cross-validation, EB), plus sensitivity analyses.
- Substantially expand experiments: include Poisson log-normal and other count baselines; evaluate QPGM; report setup details and uncertainty.
- For the case study, document preprocessing, use offsets for sequencing depth, and include external biological validation.
- Clean up references and formatting.
