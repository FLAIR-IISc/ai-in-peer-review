## Peer Review: On Poisson Graphical Models

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of modeling multivariate count data, a problem increasingly relevant in modern data science. Existing approaches, such as log-linear models and multivariate Poisson distributions, suffer from scalability issues or restrictive assumptions. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of the classic Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) – its inability to model positive dependencies – and demonstrate the incorrectness of a proposed fix using Winsorized Poisson distributions.  They then introduce three novel extensions: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), designed to overcome these limitations. The paper also briefly explores learning network structure from data using these models and demonstrates their application on both simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA expression dataset.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the foundational work of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19] on PGMs. It critically examines the extensions proposed by Kaiser & Cressie [20] and Griffith [21]. It positions itself within the broader literature on graphical models, acknowledging contributions from Gaussian graphical models [1, 2, 3, 4], Ising models [3, 5], and more recent non-parametric extensions [7, 8, 9, 10]. The comparison to other approaches for count data, like copula transforms [12] and hierarchical models [16], provides context.  The application to microRNA expression data connects to the growing area of applying graphical models to genomics [24].

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the need for improved graphical models for count data and highlights the deficiencies of existing methods.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The theoretical proof demonstrating the failure of the Winsorized Poisson approach (Theorem 1) is a significant contribution.  The theorems establishing the normalizability of the TPGM, QPGM and SPGM provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed models.
* **Novelty:** The introduction of the TPGM, QPGM and SPGM represents a significant advance in the field. The exploration of different base measures and sufficient statistics is a clever approach to address the limitations of the classic PGM.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The paper demonstrates the potential of these models in practice through simulations and a real-world application, including discussion of learning algorithms.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the simulations and case study are valuable, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited. The simulations could benefit from a more thorough investigation of different network structures and parameter settings. The breast cancer analysis is compelling, but lacks a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for microRNA networks.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not fully address the computational challenges associated with inference and parameter estimation in these models, particularly for large-scale datasets.  While neighborhood selection is mentioned, a more detailed discussion of the computational costs and scalability of the proposed algorithms would be beneficial.
* **SPGM Practicality:** The SPGM, while theoretically interesting, seems less directly applicable due to the need for careful tuning of the R0 and R parameters. The authors briefly touch on this, but a more in-depth discussion on practical parameter selection strategies would be useful.
* **Variational Bound Justification:** The use of a Gaussian log-partition function as a variational upper bound for the QPGM feels a bit hand-wavy. A more rigorous justification or exploration of alternative bounds would strengthen this section.
* **Missing Details in Experiments:** Details on the specific regularization parameter selection used in the simulation and case study are missing. It's unclear if cross-validation was used or some other method, limiting reproducibility.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and increasingly relevant problem in statistical modeling.
* It provides a rigorous theoretical analysis and proposes novel solutions with strong theoretical foundations.
* The proposed models offer the potential to model complex dependencies in count data that are beyond the capabilities of existing methods.
* The empirical results, while limited, are promising and demonstrate the practical potential of the proposed models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and rigorous.
* The computational complexity and scalability of the models are not fully addressed.
* Some aspects of the models, like parameter selection in the SPGM, require further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical modeling. The authors have identified a key limitation of existing methods and proposed novel solutions with strong theoretical foundations.  While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and the computational aspects need further exploration, the theoretical contributions and potential practical impact of this work are significant. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, pending minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should consider expanding the empirical analysis, providing a more detailed discussion of computational challenges, and clarifying the parameter selection strategies for the SPGM.

**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
