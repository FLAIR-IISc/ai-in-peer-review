Summary
This paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the requirement of non-positive edge parameters to ensure normalizability, which prevents modeling positive conditional dependencies. The authors (i) prove that “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals (as proposed by Kaiser & Cressie) cannot be globally consistent (Theorem 1); (ii) propose a Truncated PGM (TPGM) based on an exponential-family truncation that yields a proper MRF with both positive and negative edges (Theorem 2), but with bounded count support; (iii) show that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, any normalizable joint that allows positive edges must have a base measure growing at least quadratically (Theorem 3); (iv) introduce a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure and normalizability under negative-definite quadratic form (Proposition 2); and (v) propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure but uses a sublinear sufficient statistic B(·;R0,R), yielding heavier tails than QPGM and allowing arbitrary pairwise parameters while preserving normalizability (Theorem 4). They outline neighborhood selection with l1-penalized node-conditional likelihoods and simple variational bounds, and present simulations and a breast cancer miRNA network case study showing advantages over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on Besag’s auto-models and the generalized exponential-family graphical model framework of Yang et al., and provides a clear critique of “Winsorized” conditionals. It situates itself relative to copula approaches and multivariate Poisson constructions via summation of latent Poissons, and correctly notes the positive-dependence limitations of those. One notable omission is comparison to Poisson log-normal and related latent Gaussian count graphical models, which have become a standard alternative for positive dependence and overdispersion.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual impact: The paper advances the foundations of count MRFs beyond the classic negative-edge barrier. Theorem 3 (Ω(z^2) base measure necessity with linear sufficient statistics) is particularly insightful and likely to influence future model design. The impossibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals is also clarifying.
- Breadth of modeling ideas: Three complementary solutions—TPGM (clean truncation), QPGM (quadratic base measure), SPGM (sublinear sufficient stats)—cover different trade-offs between support, tail behavior, and parameter feasibility.
- Theoretical support: Existence/normalizability results are given for all proposed models; variational bounding connects QPGM nodewise estimation to Gaussian regression, which is pragmatic.
- Empirical indications: Simulations show clear gains over Gaussian/nonparanormal on count data; the miRNA case study highlights biologically plausible hubs missed by GGM and the original PGM.

Weaknesses
- Empirical scope and baselines: No comparisons to Poisson log-normal, negative binomial graphical models, or copula Poisson constructions. These would be important to establish practical significance beyond GGM/nonparanormal.
- Estimation and scalability: For SPGM, node-conditional log-partition is infinite-sum; the paper relies on an upper bound (DPois). More algorithmic detail (e.g., truncation strategies, convergence diagnostics, complexity) is needed to judge scalability to large p and high counts. For QPGM, ensuring global negative-definiteness from separate nodewise fits is nontrivial; the paper does not discuss symmetrization or feasibility projection.
- Theory gaps: Structure recovery guarantees (sparsistency) are deferred. While acceptable given the novelty, even a proof sketch or conditions paralleling [Yang et al.] would strengthen the contribution.
- Hyperparameters and identifiability: SPGM requires (R0,R). Guidance on selection, sensitivity analyses, and interpretability would help. For TPGM, setting R to the observed max may couple model to sample noise; behavior when future counts exceed R is unclear.
- Clarity: Some results (e.g., Theorem 1) would benefit from clearer statement of assumptions (pairwise vs. higher-order; measurability conditions), and a proof sketch. The “for the first time” claim may be toned down given latent-variable alternatives.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Add comparisons to Poisson log-normal (PLN) networks and negative binomial graphical models (or at least discuss why your direct-MRF approach is preferable and when).
- For SPGM, provide practical algorithms for evaluating/approximating D(θ,R0,R) and gradients, including truncation error bounds or adaptive summation schemes. Report runtime.
- For QPGM, describe how you enforce or check global negative-definiteness of the quadratic form when learning edges nodewise; consider a global refinement step or spectral projection.
- Expand on hyperparameter selection for SPGM (R0,R): propose data-driven rules (e.g., percentiles of counts) and include sensitivity plots.
- Include predictive checks (held-out likelihood, calibration of conditional means/variances) in addition to ROC structure recovery.
- Clarify the statement and proof intuition of Theorem 1; if the impossibility hinges on the I(X=R) term breaking the exponential family structure, make that explicit.
- Minor: fix some typos and reference formatting; clarify whether QPGM was evaluated empirically (currently, results shown for TPGM/SPGM only).

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, original theoretical insights that reshape how we build count MRFs.
- Multiple concrete model classes with clear trade-offs and practical estimation routes.
- Demonstrated benefits on synthetic and real data; addresses an important, widely occurring data type.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited empirical comparison set; missing strong latent-variable baselines.
- Some algorithmic and theoretical details deferred, leaving questions on scalability and guarantees.
- Hyperparameterization of SPGM and normalizability enforcement for QPGM need clearer guidance.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces bold, novel directions with solid foundational results that overcome a core limitation of Poisson MRFs. While the experiments and some practical details could be strengthened, the conceptual advance and breadth of new models justify acceptance.
