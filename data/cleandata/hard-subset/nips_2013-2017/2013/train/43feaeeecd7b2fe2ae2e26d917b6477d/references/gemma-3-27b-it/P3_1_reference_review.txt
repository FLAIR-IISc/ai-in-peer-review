## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and valuable contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to model negative dependencies – and propose a series of novel extensions, culminating in the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical validation, while brief, is encouraging. I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance to NIPS.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper begins by highlighting the need for graphical models capable of handling multivariate count data, which is increasingly prevalent in diverse fields. It points out the inadequacy of existing methods, including log-linear models, copula-based approaches, and previous attempts to extend the PGM. The core issue identified is the normalizability constraint that forces PGMs to exhibit only negative dependencies.  The authors then systematically explore solutions, first attempting truncation (and correctly disproving a previous claim by Kaiser and Cressie), then moving towards modifying the underlying Poisson distribution. This leads to the QPGM and SPGM, which, through careful manipulation of the base measure and sufficient statistics, allow for modeling both positive and negative dependencies. The paper concludes with preliminary experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed models.

**Strengths:**

* **Identification of a Fundamental Problem:** The authors clearly articulate a major limitation of existing PGMs that has been overlooked in the literature.  This is a significant contribution in itself.
* **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides solid mathematical foundations for the proposed models. The proofs of Theorem 1 and Theorem 3 are particularly insightful.  The derivation of the QPGM and SPGM is well-motivated and logically sound.
* **Novelty:** The QPGM and SPGM represent genuinely novel approaches to modeling multivariate count data.  The exploration of modified base measures and sufficient statistics is a clever and effective strategy.
* **Completeness:** The paper is self-contained and covers the necessary background information. The derivations are clearly presented, and the notation is consistent.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work within the broader context of graphical models and statistical modeling of count data. They properly cite relevant literature and discuss how their approach differs from previous efforts.
* **Practical Implications:** The proposed models have clear potential applications in areas such as genomics, epidemiology, and spatial statistics.  The preliminary experiments provide a glimpse of this potential.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is relatively brief. While the results are promising, more extensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets and with more thorough comparisons to baseline methods would strengthen the paper considerably.  Specifically, the performance on simulated data beyond the hub and scale-free networks would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not fully address the computational challenges associated with inference and parameter estimation in the QPGM and SPGM. While the mention of proximal gradient ascent is helpful, a more detailed discussion of scalability and potential optimization strategies would be valuable.  The challenges of calculating the partition functions, even with the variational bounds, warrant further elaboration.
* **SPGM’s Intuition:** The intuition behind the sublinear sufficient statistic in SPGM is less clear compared to the QPGM’s quadratic modification. Expanding on the motivations and potential benefits of the sublinear form would be useful.
* **Variational Upper Bound:** The variational upper bound is stated, but a discussion of how tight it is would be helpful. Is it sufficiently tight for practical purposes, or does it significantly affect the performance of the method?




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding graphical models for count data.
* The proposed models are theoretically sound and novel.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and complete.
* The preliminary experimental results are encouraging.
* The work has the potential to impact several research areas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited.
* The computational complexity of the proposed models is not fully addressed.
* The motivation for SPGM could be more thoroughly explained.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The technical aspects of the paper are sound, and the claims are well-supported.
* **Clarity:** Very Good. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. A bit more detail on the computational aspects would improve clarity.
* **Originality:** Excellent. The QPGM and SPGM represent a novel contribution to the field.
* **Significance:** High. The proposed models have the potential to significantly advance the state-of-the-art in modeling multivariate count data.

**Final Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors expanding the empirical evaluation and providing a more detailed discussion of the computational challenges and potential solutions. Specifically, a more extensive set of simulation experiments and a deeper analysis of the computational complexity would significantly strengthen the paper.
