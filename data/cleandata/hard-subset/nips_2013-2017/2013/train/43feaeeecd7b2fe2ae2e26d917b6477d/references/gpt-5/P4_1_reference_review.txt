Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the limitations of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data. Classical PGMs (Besag; Yang et al.) imply non-positivity constraints on pairwise parameters to ensure normalizability, which restricts them to modeling negative conditional dependencies. The authors first analyze truncation strategies. They show that the “Winsorized Poisson” approach (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith), which uses node-conditional Poisson distributions truncated by collapsing all mass above R to R, cannot yield a consistent joint MRF via the Besag/Yang construction (Theorem 1). They then propose an exponential-family truncation that redistributes the excess mass across all states ≤ R, yielding node-conditionals that remain in the exponential family and a valid joint model (TPGM; Theorem 2). TPGM permits both positive and negative dependencies but inherits drawbacks: bounded support and boundary effects as counts approach R, with limiting behavior recovering the original PGM constraints as R→∞.

Motivated to avoid truncation, the authors systematically modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the univariate exponential family used in the node-conditionals. They show a necessary condition: with linear sufficient statistics B(X)=X, allowing positive dependencies while maintaining normalizability forces the base measure C(Z) to grow at least quadratically, C(Z)=Ω(Z^2) (Theorem 3). This motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with a quadratic base measure (and corresponding quadratic node-wise terms), which is normalizable if the quadratic coefficient matrix is negative definite (or, more generally, uniformly negative along all directions), thereby allowing both positive and negative edges (Proposition 2). QPGM has Gaussian-esque thin tails; the authors provide a variational upper bound on the node-conditional log-partition by a Gaussian integral.

To obtain heavier tails with fewer constraints on pairwise parameters, they introduce a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains the Poisson base measure C(Z)=log Z! but replaces B(X)=X with a piecewise sublinear sufficient statistic that saturates beyond a threshold R (with a transition region governed by R0). SPGM is normalizable for all real edge parameters (Theorem 4) and admits an upper bound on the node-conditional log-partition by the Poisson log-partition when θ≥0 (Proposition 3).

For learning, they use neighborhood selection via ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihoods, solved by proximal gradient ascent, and compare against Gaussian graphical models and nonparanormal methods. On simulations (hub and scale-free graphs, data from TPGM or sums of Poissons), TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods in ROC analyses. A case study on TCGA breast cancer microRNA sequencing data shows that TPGM/SPGM recover richer structure than the original Poisson GM and GGM, including known hubs and a chromosome 19 cluster.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation: identifies and addresses the central deficiency of standard PGMs for count data.
- Theoretical contributions:
  - Theorem 1 gives a precise negative result invalidating a prior Winsorized construction.
  - Theorem 2 establishes a valid truncated exponential-family construction (TPGM).
  - Theorem 3 gives a necessary growth condition on the base measure to permit positive dependencies with linear sufficient statistics—an insightful structural result.
  - QPGM and SPGM are principled new families; SPGM notably allows unrestricted edge signs with heavier tails.
- Practical implications: neighborhood selection is compatible with the exponential-family node-conditionals; variational bounds link QPGM/SPGM to tractable surrogates.
- Empirical evidence: competitive or superior structure recovery on simulated count data; real-data illustration aligns with known biology.

Weaknesses:
- Proof accessibility: key proofs are not shown in the main text; rigorous conditions (e.g., for Theorem 1’s impossibility and Theorem 3’s necessity) should be clearly stated and proved in the appendix.
- Computational details:
  - SPGM node-conditional log-partition lacks closed form; the paper relies on an upper bound only for θ≥0. Clarify handling when θ<0 and how exact/approximate gradients are computed.
  - Complexity and convergence details for proximal gradient across models are not fully specified; no runtime/scalability results.
- Hyperparameterization: TPGM depends on R; SPGM depends on R and R0. Guidance for choosing/tuning and sensitivity analyses are limited.
- Comparisons: No evaluation against alternative count-data graphical modeling strategies (e.g., copula-based count models, negative binomial-based approaches, or multivariate Poisson latent-sum constructions adapted for structure learning), beyond the original PGM and Gaussian/nonparanormal.
- Statistical guarantees: Learning theory (e.g., sparsistency) is deferred; conditions under which neighborhood selection consistently recovers edges for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM are not established here.
- Evaluation breadth: Experiments emphasize ROC; additional metrics (precision at fixed FPR, calibration, likelihood-based goodness-of-fit) and ablations (effect of R, R0) would strengthen conclusions.

Quality:
- The modeling constructions are well-motivated and, contingent on complete proofs, technically sound. Normalizability conditions are carefully discussed. Some implementation aspects (SPGM inference under θ<0, optimization details) need clarification.

Clarity:
- Overall well-organized with intuitive discussion. Suggestions:
  - State all assumptions explicitly in theorem statements (domains, positivity, boundedness).
  - Provide algorithmic details for nodewise optimization and any approximations.
  - Define notation consistently (e.g., Wp, domains for Xs).
  - Ensure complete citations.

Originality:
- High. The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals, the necessary growth condition on the base measure, and the SPGM construction are novel contributions.

Significance:
- Potentially substantial impact on learning networks from count data across biology and other domains. The availability of models allowing positive and negative dependencies fills a known gap. Wider adoption will benefit from provided learning guarantees and scalable implementations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Introduces principled, novel families of count graphical models that overcome core PGM limitations.
- Provides nontrivial theoretical insights (necessity of quadratic growth; normalizability conditions).
- Demonstrates empirical benefits on simulated and real count data.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing theoretical guarantees for structure learning and incomplete computational details for SPGM.
- Limited comparative baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- TPGM’s bounded support and QPGM’s thin tails may limit applicability without clear guidance; SPGM hyperparameters require justification.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide full proofs of Theorems 1–4 and precise conditions.
- Detail computation for SPGM node-conditional likelihood and gradients when θ<0; describe approximations and their impact.
- Add sensitivity analyses for R and R0; propose data-driven selection.
- Include runtime and scalability results; clarify convergence criteria.
- Compare against additional count-model baselines (e.g., copula count models, negative-binomial variants) and report goodness-of-fit metrics.
- Outline (even briefly) conditions under which sparsistency can be established for the proposed models.
