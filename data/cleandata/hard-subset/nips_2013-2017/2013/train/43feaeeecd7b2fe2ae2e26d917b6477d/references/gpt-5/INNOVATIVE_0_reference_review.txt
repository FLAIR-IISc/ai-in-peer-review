Summary and contributions
The paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson Markov random fields (MRFs): the standard Poisson graphical model (PGM) is only normalizable when all pairwise parameters are non-positive, so it cannot capture positive conditional dependencies. The authors (i) show that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals proposed in the spatial statistics literature cannot be made consistent with any joint MRF (Theorem 1), (ii) introduce a new exponential-family truncation that does admit a consistent joint (TPGM), (iii) prove a fundamental requirement that, with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability allowing both signs requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3), leading to (iv) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure and (v) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that uses a saturating sufficient statistic while keeping the Poisson base measure. They sketch neighborhood selection for structure learning and provide simulations and a miRNA case study.

Relation to prior work
Builds directly on Besag’s auto-models and the exponential-family graphical model framework of Yang et al. (NIPS/ArXiv), and contrasts with multivariate Poisson constructions via shared latent Poisson components (only positive dependence) and copula methods. The negative-parameters-only constraint for PGM is known; this paper both clarifies its roots (via growth rate mismatch between pairwise terms and base measure) and offers multiple, theoretically motivated fixes. The rejection of Winsorized node-conditionals as inconsistent with any joint is a notable correction to prior claims in spatial statistics.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual depth: Theorem 3 is an insightful, general statement about the necessary growth of the base measure for linear-sufficient-statistic count MRFs to allow positive and negative edges. This reframes the problem and motivates principled alternatives.
- New model families: TPGM (finite support but consistent exponential family), QPGM (quadratic base measure enabling both signs via a negative-definite quadratic form), and SPGM (saturating sufficient statistic with Poisson base measure and heavier tails) collectively expand the toolkit for multivariate counts.
- Correction of literature: Theorem 1 clearly argues the inconsistency of the Winsorized conditional approach.
- Computational angle: The variational Gaussian bound for QPGM nodewise likelihoods is pragmatic and connects to well-understood Gaussian GGM solvers.
- Empirical indications: On synthetic and miRNA data, TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines and uncover plausible biology (e.g., mir-375, mir-10b hubs; chr19 clique).

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical gaps for learning: While the construction follows [Yang et al.], sparsistency and statistical rates are not provided here. Given the novelty, that’s acceptable, but at least sketching conditions and differences from the Poisson/Ising analyses would strengthen the paper.
- Practical guidance on hyperparameters: SPGM relies on R0 and R; TPGM on R. How should practitioners set or cross-validate these? How sensitive are results to misspecification, especially when counts are heavy-tailed or unbounded?
- QPGM feasibility constraints: Learning under the global negative-definiteness constraint on the quadratic parameter matrix is challenging. The paper proposes nodewise regressions with a Gaussian bound, but does not address how to reconcile nodewise fits into a globally negative-definite Θ or enforce symmetry/compatibility. Some discussion of projection or joint estimation would help.
- Partition functions and computation: For SPGM, the node-conditional log-partition lacks a closed form; the Poisson upper bound holds only for θ ≥ 0. What about θ < 0, and what is the resulting approximation quality in practice? Any variance reduction or truncation strategies?
- Experiments: 
  - Comparisons omit several count-model baselines (e.g., latent-factor-based multivariate Poisson or copula count models) and the original PGM results are “not shown.”
  - Some data are generated from TPGM, favoring TPGM; it’s good that the authors also use “sum of Poissons” data, but more diverse generative mechanisms (including both positive and negative dependencies without truncation) would be informative.
  - No predictive evaluations (held-out likelihood or calibration), runtimes, or scalability analysis.
- Clarity: Overall clear, but some proofs are only summarized. The piecewise definition of SPGM’s B(x; R0, R) would benefit from intuition and a short derivation of Theorem 4 within the main text. A brief algorithmic recipe (pseudocode) for each model’s neighborhood selection would aid reproducibility.

Quality
Technical claims are largely plausible and well-motivated. The key theorems align with intuition about growth rates and saturation, and the constructions are consistent with exponential-family MRF theory. Some computational aspects (global consistency, constraints, approximations) are underdeveloped but do not undermine the conceptual contribution.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and readable. A few sections are dense (SPGM definition and bounds), and references need formatting cleanup. Adding guidance on choosing R, R0, and discussing computational complexity would improve clarity for practitioners.

Originality
High. The necessity result (Theorem 3) and the SPGM/QPGM constructions represent a new direction for count MRFs. Demonstrating that Winsorized conditionals cannot yield a joint corrects the record.

Significance
High. There is real demand for tractable count-valued graphical models that admit both positive and negative dependencies in genomics, neuroscience, epidemiology, and text. This work opens several promising paths and is likely to spur further theoretical and algorithmic development.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a short appendix sketch of Theorems 1 and 3 and more intuition for SPGM’s normalizability.
- Discuss how to enforce Θ ≺ 0 in QPGM during learning, or propose a practical symmetrization/projection.
- Give concrete guidance for choosing R, R0; include a sensitivity analysis.
- Add at least one predictive metric and runtime/scale results; include the original PGM as a curve rather than “not shown.”
- Clarify bounds/approximations for SPGM when θ < 0.

Recommendation
Accept (clear accept on novelty and conceptual impact). Despite some incomplete validation and algorithmic details, the paper introduces important, bold ideas that substantially advance graphical modeling for count data.

Reviewer confidence
High on the novelty/significance assessment; medium on the fine-grained technical details of proofs and computational aspects.
