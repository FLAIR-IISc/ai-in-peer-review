## Peer Review: On Poisson Graphical Models

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of modeling high-dimensional multivariate count data, a common occurrence in modern applications like genomics and spatial statistics. Existing approaches either scale poorly with dimensionality (log-linear models), rely on limiting results or intractable distributions (multivariate Poisson distributions, copula transforms), or suffer from restrictive assumptions (original Poisson Graphical Models, which only allow negative dependencies). The authors identify a flaw in a proposed solution using Winsorized Poisson distributions and then introduce three novel approaches: the Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM). These models aim to overcome the limitations of existing methods by modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution, allowing for the modeling of both positive and negative dependencies.  The paper also touches upon learning network structure from data and demonstrates the models on simulated data and a real-world breast cancer miRNA expression dataset.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the foundational work of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19] on Poisson Graphical Models. It correctly identifies the limitation of these models (non-positive edge weights) and positions itself as offering solutions to this well-known issue. The authors acknowledge and critique attempts to address this limitation with Winsorized Poisson distributions [20, 21]. The work also connects to broader literature on graphical models, including Gaussian Graphical Models [1, 2, 3, 4] and other extensions for different data types. The comparison to non-paranormal models [7, 10] in the experiments situates this work within the context of high-dimensional graphical model estimation.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods for modeling multivariate count data and provides a compelling motivation for their work.
* **Novel Contributions:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuine contributions to the field. The authors provide theoretical justifications for the models' properties, including normalizability and the ability to model both positive and negative dependencies.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes theorems and propositions that formally demonstrate the properties of the proposed models, specifically regarding normalizability and sufficient conditions for parameter estimation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and case study demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed models, showing improved performance compared to baseline methods, particularly for count data. The application to breast cancer miRNA data is a compelling example.
* **Connection to Existing Frameworks:**  Framing the work within the existing graphical model framework of [17, 18, 19] allows for potential extensions of existing estimation and inference techniques.
* **Detailed Explanation:** The paper provides a thorough explanation of the motivation behind each model and the mathematical details of their construction.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The models, especially QPGM and SPGM, are mathematically complex. While the authors provide detailed explanations, the barrier to entry for readers unfamiliar with graphical model theory may be high.
* **Practical Challenges:** Implementing and scaling the SPGM, in particular, could be challenging due to the complex form of the sufficient statistic. The paper doesn't fully address the computational cost associated with these models.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the simulation studies showcase performance, a more detailed discussion of the scalability of these methods to extremely high-dimensional datasets (p >> n) would be valuable.
* **Choice of R and R0:** The performance of the TPGM and SPGM relies on the appropriate choice of the truncation parameter R and the sub-linearity parameter R0. The paper lacks guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice.  A cross-validation strategy or other methods for parameter selection are not explored.
* **Theoretical Gap:** The theoretical connections to existing consistency results from [18, 19] are stated, but a more detailed exposition of how these results might be extended to the proposed models would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant problem in a well-motivated manner.
* Proposes novel and theoretically sound models that overcome limitations of existing approaches.
* Provides compelling empirical evidence of the models' performance.
* Represents a valuable contribution to the field of graphical modeling and count data analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the models might limit their practical adoption.
*  Lack of detailed discussion regarding computational scalability.
*  Parameter selection for TPGM and SPGM requires further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the field of graphical modeling for count data. While the models are complex, the authors provide rigorous theoretical justifications and demonstrate their practical utility through simulations and a real-world application. The weaknesses are primarily related to practical considerations and areas for future work, rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of computational scalability and potential optimization strategies for SPGM.
2.  Provide guidance on the selection of the truncation parameters R and R0 (e.g., through cross-validation or other techniques).
3.  Elaborate on the connection to existing consistency results from [18, 19] and how these results might be extended to the proposed models.
4.  Consider adding a section discussing the limitations of the proposed models and potential future research directions.
