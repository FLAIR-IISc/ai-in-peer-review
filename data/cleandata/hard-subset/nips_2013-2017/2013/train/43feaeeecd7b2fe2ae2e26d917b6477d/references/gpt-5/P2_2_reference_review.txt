Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a central limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: Besag’s (and subsequent) results imply that the standard pairwise PGM is normalizable only with non-positive edge weights, thereby precluding positive conditional dependencies. The authors (i) show that Winsorized (hard-truncated-at-R) Poisson node-conditionals, proposed in spatial statistics to address this, cannot be consistent with any joint MRF (Theorem 1), correcting a misconception in prior work (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith); (ii) propose a tractable exponential-family truncation that yields a valid joint MRF over {0,…,R}p (TPGM), allowing both positive and negative edges; (iii) analyze how to modify the univariate exponential family to enable positive dependencies on the infinite count domain, proving that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x one needs a base measure that grows at least quadratically for normalizability (Theorem 3); this motivates (iv) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with a quadratic base measure and joint density with quadratic unaries and pairwise terms, normalizable under negative definiteness conditions (Proposition 2), and (v) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure but makes B(·) sublinear/saturating beyond a range [R0,R], yielding heavier tails than QPGM and normalizability for arbitrary pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They discuss neighborhood selection estimators using node-conditional likelihoods, variational bounds for intractable partition functions, and demonstrate on simulations and a breast cancer miRNA sequencing dataset. The work builds directly on Yang et al.’s generalized exponential-family graphical models and Besag’s auto-models, and contrasts with sum-of-Poissons constructions, copula approaches, and hierarchical spatial models.

Quality
- Theory: The core claims are well-motivated and largely plausible. Theorem 1 (incompatibility of Winsorized conditionals) is an important corrective; the argument hinges on the non-exponential-family nature induced by collecting tail mass at R, which breaks the Hammersley–Clifford-compatible conditional structure. Theorem 2 (existence/uniqueness of TPGM joint) follows cleanly from the exponential-family node-conditionals; the finite support ensures normalizability and permits positive edges. Theorem 3 (necessity of Ω(z^2) base measure growth for linear sufficient statistics if one allows positive interactions) captures the key scaling tension—pairwise interactions grow quadratically in counts and must be dominated by the base measure for summability. Proposition 2’s sufficient condition (negative definiteness of the quadratic form) mirrors Gaussian GMRFs and makes the QPGM interpretable. Theorem 4 (SPGM normalizability for all pairwise parameters) is intuitive because the bounded, saturating B(·) caps effective pairwise growth; pairing with the Poisson base measure appears to guarantee summability. Variational bounds (Gaussian for QPGM, Poisson for SPGM) are reasonable for node-wise estimation.
- That said, several technical details are relegated or missing: (a) precise assumptions and proof sketches for Theorem 3’s necessity (e.g., dependence on graph size, uniformity in p, and constants in the Ω(·)); (b) regularity conditions for uniqueness/identifiability across QPGM/SPGM; (c) computational guarantees for the proposed proximal schemes when node-wise partition functions are approximated by variational bounds (bias/consistency impacts).
- Experiments: Simulations demonstrate better ROC/AUC than Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines on count data and show the expected behavior (TPGM best when well-specified; SPGM competitive). The case study identifies plausible hubs (miR-375, miR-10b) and a chr19 cluster, aligning with biology. However, quantitative evaluation in real data is necessarily limited by lack of ground truth; ablations (e.g., sensitivity to R,R0, misspecification) would strengthen the empirical case. Comparisons to Poisson log-normal or copula-Poisson network methods are absent.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized: motivation, failure of Winsorization, three proposed models, estimation, and experiments. Definitions of TPGM/QPGM/SPGM are clear; the role of base measure vs sufficient statistics is explained. A few points could be clarified:
- Spell out the conditions and proof ideas for Theorem 3; give a simple 2-node example illustrating divergence when C(z)=o(z^2) and θst>0.
- For SPGM, give intuition for choosing (R0,R), how to set or learn them, and robustness to mis-specification.
- Explicitly state how node-wise likelihoods are optimized for SPGM when D(θ,R0,R) lacks closed form; when and how the Poisson variational bound is used (and its effect on gradients).
- Discuss computational complexity as functions of R for TPGM and of degree p and sparsity.

Originality
The contributions are novel and significant:
- The negative result on Winsorized Poisson conditionals for MRFs corrects prior literature.
- The TPGM exponential-family truncation is a neat fix that retains compatibility with auto-model constructions.
- The growth-condition theorem (necessity of quadratic base measure for linear statistics with positive edges) is insightful and motivates new classes (QPGM/SPGM).
- SPGM’s sublinear sufficient statistic is an elegant design yielding heavier tails and unrestricted edges.
The work clearly differentiates itself from prior exponential-family MRFs and from sum-of-Poissons/copula approaches.

Significance
This paper advances multivariate modeling of counts—a need in genomics, spatial epidemiology, and beyond—by providing the first tractable MRFs for counts that permit both positive and negative conditional dependencies without resorting to latent Gaussian copulas. The modeling ideas (altering base measure vs sufficient statistic) and the normalizability analysis are likely to influence future work. Practical impact depends on providing clearer estimation recipes and broader comparisons; nonetheless, the conceptual contribution is strong.

Pros
- Identifies and fixes a longstanding limitation of Poisson MRFs; clear theoretical motivation.
- Important negative result on Winsorized conditionals (Theorem 1).
- Three concrete models covering finite support (TPGM), Gaussian-esque tails with positive edges (QPGM), and heavier tails with unrestricted edges (SPGM).
- Simple node-wise learning strategies; promising empirical results and a relevant case study.

Cons
- Some theoretical results (e.g., Theorem 3) would benefit from clearer assumptions/proofs.
- Estimation details for SPGM/QPGM rely on variational bounds; no consistency/sparsistency guarantees are provided (though likely extendable).
- Limited empirical comparisons (no Poisson log-normal or copula-Poisson baselines); sensitivity to hyperparameters (R,R0) not explored.
- Choice of R=11 in case study and its implications on TPGM behavior deserve justification; potential bias toward hub formation acknowledged but not controlled.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you provide a constructive example illustrating Theorem 1’s incompatibility for R>3?
- For Theorem 3, can you formalize the dependence on p and graph structure? Is Ω(z^2) necessary uniformly or only along certain directions?
- How do you recommend selecting R and (R0,R) in practice? Any cross-validation or marginal goodness-of-fit diagnostics?
- For SPGM, can you characterize the approximation error when replacing D by the Poisson bound in node-wise estimation, and its effect on edge recovery?
- Consider adding comparisons to Poisson log-normal network inference or other recent count graphical models.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes substantial, original theoretical and modeling contributions to graphical models for counts, addresses a well-known limitation of Poisson MRFs, and provides practical instantiations with empirical support. Tightening the theoretical exposition and clarifying estimation details would further strengthen an already compelling submission.
