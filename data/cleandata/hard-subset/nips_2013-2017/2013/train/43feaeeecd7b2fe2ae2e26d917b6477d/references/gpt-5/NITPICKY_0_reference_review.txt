Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs, Besag ’74; Yang et al. ’12/’13) that normalizability forces non-positive pairwise parameters, thereby capturing only competitive (negative) dependencies. The authors:
- Show that “Winsorized” Poisson node-conditionals (Kaiser & Cressie ’97; Griffith ’04) are incompatible with any joint MRF in the Besag/Yang framework (Theorem 1).
- Propose a truncated-exponential-family node-conditional that yields a proper finite-support MRF (TPGM) without sign constraints on edges (Theorem 2).
- Argue that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, a base measure that scales at least quadratically is necessary to admit positive dependencies while preserving normalizability (Theorem 3).
- Propose a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that augments the model with quadratic terms X_s^2 (equation (6)), and give a sufficient normalizability condition via negativity of the quadratic form (Proposition 2).
- Propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure log X! but replaces B(x) by a piecewise sublinear, saturating function B(x; R0,R), proving normalizability for any edge signs (Theorem 4) and giving a variational bound on the nodewise log-partition by the Poisson log-partition when θ ≥ 0 (Proposition 3).
They demonstrate neighborhood selection with ℓ1-penalized node-conditionals, and empirical comparisons on simulations (hub/scale-free) and a miRNA network.

Strengths
- Important problem: enabling both positive and negative dependencies for multivariate count MRFs.
- The negative result for Winsorization (Theorem 1) is useful, given repeated informal claims in the literature.
- TPGM is a clean finite-support construction with tractable nodewise normalization.
- SPGM provides an intuitively appealing way to control tail behavior while allowing arbitrary edge signs.
- Theoretical attention to normalizability is appropriate and largely on point.
- Empirical comparisons suggest improvements over Gaussian/copula baselines on count data; the miRNA case study is of practical interest.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Technical correctness and completeness
- Theorem 1: The R > 3 threshold is surprising and unexplained. Please provide intuition, sharpness, and either a counterexample for R ≤ 3 or a clear statement of the maximal range. Also clarify whether E(XV\s) is constrained to be linear vs arbitrary and how that impacts the incompatibility result.
- Theorem 3 vs QPGM: The text states that with linear sufficient statistics one “needs” a quadratic base measure C(Z)=Ω(Z^2). However, the proposed QPGM achieves normalizability by adding quadratic sufficient statistics with parameters θ_ss (equation (6)), effectively moving curvature into the parameterized terms, not the base measure. This is conceptually different from requiring the base measure to be quadratic and seems inconsistent with the theorem’s claim of necessity. Please reconcile: under what parameter ranges could log Z! suffice if diagonal quadratic terms are strongly negative? Precisely state the assumptions under which “Ω(Z^2)” is necessary (uniform over θ in an open set?) and avoid conflating base measure with parameterized sufficient statistics. The current presentation is confusing.
- Proposition 2 uses X^T Θ X ≤ −cθ ||X||^2 for all X ∈ W^p, but W is not defined earlier (sometimes the domain is denoted X or {0,1,…}). Define W and ensure consistent notation. Also, if Θ ≺ 0 is sufficient, is it also necessary (up to lower-order terms) when the base measure omits log X!? Please discuss tightness.
- SPGM log-partition bound (Proposition 3) holds only for θ ≥ 0. What happens in practice when θ < 0 during optimization? Are you using a surrogate or projecting θ? Without addressing this, optimization stability and guarantees are unclear.
- Optimization: The paper does not establish convexity of the nodewise problems for QPGM/SPGM (with the exact D or with your surrogates) nor does it specify step sizes, Lipschitz constants, or stopping criteria for proximal gradient. As sparsistency is deferred, at least provide well-posedness and convergence of the estimators you optimize.
- Identifiability/regularization: With quadratic terms in QPGM and saturating B in SPGM, discuss identifiability and scale: when are θ_s and θ_st identifiable from node-conditionals? Are there constraints needed (e.g., centering, diagonal dominance)?
- Asymptotics: The claim that “we expect extensions of [18,19] to confirm sparsistency” is too weak. Either include a sketched argument identifying the key conditions (restricted eigenvalue, incoherence, tail bounds under SPGM/QPGM) or temper claims.

Clarity and organization
- Notation is inconsistent: X vs W for domains; R is both truncation cap and later set to “the maximum count value” (which seems data-dependent). Define all symbols once and reuse consistently.
- The definition of B(x;R0,R) is hard to parse in text; please put a clear, typeset piecewise function and explain continuity and differentiability at R0 and R.
- Several typos/grammar: “form”→from; “the log-partition function D̄(·) … allows consistent graph structure recovery [18]” should cite the exact consistency results; define N(s) once. Many sentences are overly long and could be tightened.

Empirical evaluation
- Hyperparameters R and R0: Setting R to the maximum observed count is data-dependent and risks brittle behavior; justify and/or cross-validate, and discuss generalization when test counts exceed train max (SPGM saturates; TPGM caps). For SPGM, how is R0 chosen?
- Baselines: Important missing comparisons include Poisson log-normal graphical models and negative binomial-based graphical models (e.g., hierarchical/latent-Gaussian count models commonly used circa 2010s); you cite copula works but not PLN. Given your stated goal (rich dependence with positive/negative edges), please include at least a PLN baseline or justify its omission.
- Quantitative metrics: Only ROC curves are mentioned (figures not provided). Include AUCs with variability, precision-recall (for sparse graphs), and calibration or held-out likelihood/perplexity to support tail modeling claims.
- Case study: More details needed on pre-processing (normalization, filtering, handling of library sizes), stability selection settings (subsample fraction, threshold), and biological validation. It is surprising that the “maximum count” is 11; explain if counts were binned/thresholded.

Related work and references
- References are severely incomplete (missing years, venues, page ranges), some entries are corrupted (“d’Aspremont.”), and several arXiv-only citations lack version info. This does not meet NIPS formatting standards and hinders verification.
- Please discuss prior constructions that add quadratic terms to discrete MRFs (e.g., auto-Poisson variants with saturation/soft truncation) and latent-variable models for counts.

Pros
- Addresses an important deficiency of classical PGM.
- Clear negative result for Winsorization.
- TPGM/SPGM constructions are simple and practically useful.
- Normalizability conditions are discussed explicitly.

Cons
- Conceptual confusion between base measure vs parameterized quadratic terms (Theorem 3 vs QPGM).
- Optimization details and guarantees are insufficient.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and misses key baselines; hyperparameter choices are ad hoc.
- Numerous notation, clarity, and reference-format issues.

Recommendation: Reject
While the ideas are promising and potentially impactful, the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation issues for NIPS: unresolved conceptual inconsistencies, lack of optimization rigor, insufficient empirical breadth, and poor reference/formatting quality. I encourage a revision that (i) reconciles and tightens the theory (especially Theorem 3 vs QPGM), (ii) strengthens optimization and asymptotic arguments, (iii) broadens and systematizes experiments, and (iv) fixes clarity/formatting.
