Summary
The paper tackles the long-standing difficulty of defining tractable undirected graphical models for count data that can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies. It revisits the classical Poisson auto-model (Besag) and its generalization via node-conditionals from exponential families (Yang et al.), recalling the well-known normalizability restriction that forces nonpositive pairwise parameters in the standard Poisson graphical model (PGM). The authors then:

- Argue that using the “Winsorized Poisson” as node-conditionals (as suggested by Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot produce a consistent joint distribution (Theorem 1).
- Propose a Truncated Poisson GGM (TPGM) where node-conditionals are exponential-family Poissons truncated to {0,…,R}, leading to a finite-domain MRF that allows positive and negative edge weights (Theorem 2).
- Show that with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability with positive pairwise parameters requires a base measure growing at least quadratically (Theorem 3), motivating a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic unary terms and a negative definite quadratic form sufficient for normalizability (Proposition 2).
- Propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM), replacing the linear sufficient statistic with a piecewise sublinear function that saturates, while keeping the Poisson base measure; this yields normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4) and heavier tails than QPGM. Variational upper bounds are suggested for nodewise estimation.
- Empirically, they compare TPGM and SPGM to Gaussian-based methods (GGM, nonparanormal, SKEPTIC) on synthetic data and apply them to a breast cancer microRNA sequencing dataset.

Relation to prior work
The setup builds on Besag’s auto-models and later general formulations for EF-based MRFs. The restriction to nonpositive Poisson pairwise parameters is classical. The paper’s critique of Winsorization connects to conditional compatibility results (Hammersley–Clifford/Brook’s). The proposals differ from copula-based or multivariate Poisson constructions and from hierarchical Poisson lognormal models. However, related baselines such as Poisson-lognormal graphical models and negative binomial alternatives commonly used for RNA-seq are not considered.

Strengths
- Targets an important gap: multivariate models for counts with both positive and negative conditional dependencies in a tractable MRF framework.
- Clear identification of why the standard PGM fails (normalizability) and a principled route to fix it via either truncation (TPGM) or modifying base measure/sufficient statistics (QPGM/SPGM).
- The necessity claim in Theorem 3 is conceptually insightful: with linear sufficient statistics, quadratic growth in the base measure is needed to counter quadratic pairwise interactions.
- The construction of TPGM is straightforward and yields a proper finite-domain MRF free of sign constraints.
- The SPGM idea (bounded sufficient statistics) is intuitive and explains why all pairwise parameters can be allowed.
- The paper provides some empirical evidence that the proposed models can outperform Gaussian-based methods on count data.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical claims need stronger support and clearer assumptions:
  • Theorem 1 (invalidity of Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals): This is a strong negative claim against prior work but the paper does not present a proof or explicit counterexample in the main text. Given the sensitivity of conditional-compatibility arguments, a complete, rigorous proof (even for p=2) is essential.
  • Theorem 3 (C(Z)=Ω(Z^2) necessity): The statement is broad but conditions are not fully specified (e.g., graph structure, sign structure of Θ, dependence on p or max degree). A precise statement/proof sketch would help assess generality.
  • Proposition 2: The negative-definite condition is sufficient, but not necessary; practical identifiability and interpretability of diagonal vs off-diagonal quadratic terms are not discussed.

- Estimation/inference is underdeveloped:
  • For QPGM, node-conditional normalizers lack closed form; the proposed Gaussian variational upper bound is heuristic. There is no analysis of how this surrogate affects bias, sparsistency, or model selection compared with true likelihood-based neighborhood selection.
  • For SPGM, the variational bound is only given for θ ≥ 0; what is used when θ < 0? No general algorithmic details, convergence guarantees, or error bounds are provided.
  • No sparsistency or sample complexity results are established; the paper defers to prior EF-MRF results but those do not directly cover the approximate objectives introduced here.

- Experimental validation is limited:
  • Baselines miss key competitors tailored to counts, e.g., Poisson lognormal graphical models, variants with negative binomial marginals (common for RNA-seq), or copula discrete GMs.
  • The synthetic data include cases generated by TPGM; as expected, TPGM performs best there. More neutral generators with both pos/neg dependencies and heavy tails would better test robustness.
  • Sensitivity to hyperparameters R and R0 is not explored. Setting R to the sample maximum is ad hoc and data-dependent; for real data, the choice R=11 and R0=5 is unexplained and potentially inconsistent with typical RNA-seq count scales.
  • No calibration checks (held-out likelihood, PIT histograms), goodness-of-fit diagnostics, or ablation of the variational approximations.
  • Scalability and runtime are not reported.

- Reproducibility and clarity:
  • Optimization details (step sizes, proximal updates, handling of the partition bounds) are too brief to reproduce.
  • Some notation is overloaded (Θ vs θss), and typos occur in references.

Questions for the authors
1) Provide explicit proofs (or at least detailed sketches) for Theorems 1 and 3 in the main paper or appendix, including the precise assumptions. Can you give a minimal counterexample for Theorem 1?
2) For QPGM/SPGM neighborhood selection, what exactly is the optimized objective? Are there guarantees that the surrogate recovers the same sparsity pattern as the true conditionals under some conditions?
3) How are R and R0 selected in practice? Please report sensitivity analyses and principled selection methods (e.g., cross-validation, marginal fit diagnostics).
4) Can you compare against Poisson-lognormal or negative binomial graphical models? Even if approximate, these are relevant baselines for RNA-seq.
5) For the real data, why is R=11 reasonable given typical count magnitudes? Was the data normalized or filtered?
6) Provide runtime and scalability results for p in the hundreds to low thousands.
7) Discuss identifiability of θss vs θst in QPGM and how their magnitudes influence tail behavior vs dependence.

Pros
- Addresses a real modeling deficiency in PGMs for counts.
- Provides multiple constructions with clear intuitions (finite truncation; quadratic base; bounded sufficient statistic).
- Some theoretical insights on normalizability.
- Promising empirical trends on count data.

Cons
- Key theoretical claims not fully substantiated in the text.
- Estimation relies on unvalidated variational surrogates; no guarantees.
- Experiments lack strong count-specific baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- Hyperparameter choices appear ad hoc; practical guidance is limited.
- Reproducibility is weak.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the ideas are interesting, but the paper in its current form does not meet the level of methodological validation I would expect. Strengthening the theoretical proofs, providing clearer and principled estimation procedures (with at least partial guarantees), and expanding the empirical evaluation with appropriate baselines and sensitivity analyses would substantially improve the paper.
