Summary and relation to prior work
The paper revisits Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data. It starts from Besag’s auto-Poisson model and its generalization to exponential family graphical models (EFGMs), where node-conditionals are Poisson and the joint factors over a graph. A known limitation of the standard PGM is that normalizability forces all pairwise parameters to be non-positive, restricting the model to negative conditional dependencies.

The paper first examines truncation-based fixes. It shows that using Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals (as suggested by Kaiser and Cressie; also discussed by Griffith) does not yield a consistent joint distribution in the EF construction (Theorem 1). It then proposes a different exponential-family truncation that preserves EF structure on a finite support {0,…,R}, and proves it yields a valid joint model (TPGM; Theorem 2) without sign constraints on edges.

Recognizing TPGM’s drawbacks (bounded support; behavior near R), the paper next modifies the underlying univariate EF beyond simple truncation. It shows that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, allowing both positive and negative conditional dependencies while maintaining normalizability necessitates at least quadratic growth in the base measure C(x) (Theorem 3). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which augments the energy with per-node and pairwise quadratic terms and is normalizable under an overall negative-definiteness-type condition on the quadratic form (Proposition 2), thereby permitting both positive and negative dependencies.

To mitigate QPGM’s Gaussian-like thin tails, the paper proposes the Sublinear PGM (SPGM): it keeps the classical Poisson base measure log(x!) but replaces B(x)=x with a piecewise sublinear sufficient statistic that flattens after a threshold R, resulting in heavier tails and normalizability for all real-valued pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). A variational upper bound relates the node-conditional log-partition of QPGM to a Gaussian, and for SPGM a Poisson log-partition bound applies when the canonical parameter is nonnegative (Proposition 3).

For learning, the paper uses neighborhood selection with ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihoods and proximal gradient optimization, following prior work on EFGMs (e.g., Yang et al., NeurIPS 2012). Experiments compare TPGM and SPGM against Gaussian graphical models (GGM), nonparanormal, and SKEPTIC on simulated networks (hub and scale-free) and on a TCGA miRNA sequencing dataset. The proposed methods generally outperform Gaussian-based baselines on count data; a case study highlights plausible biological hubs.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theory: The incompatibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1) addresses a specific, previously claimed construction; the requirement R>3 and the mechanism (non-EF term I(Xs=R)Ψ(ηs)) align with known conditional-to-joint consistency constraints in EFGMs. The existence of a valid joint for the exponential-family truncation (TPGM) follows directly from the EF conditional/joint construction with finite support. The necessity of quadratic base measure growth for linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) is well-motivated by scaling arguments: positive pairwise terms scale quadratically in counts across dimensions, while log(x!) is subquadratic (x log x), so a subquadratic base measure cannot dominate along “all-equal” directions. The QPGM normalizability condition via a negative-definiteness-type bound on the quadratic form is standard. The SPGM construction appears to ensure normalizability by saturating B(x), bounding pairwise contributions, with −log(x!) providing tail decay.
- Algorithms/inference: The paper proposes proximal gradient ascent for ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihoods. For QPGM, it uses a Gaussian variational upper bound for the conditional log-partition; for SPGM, it cites a Poisson upper bound when θ≥0. However, the paper does not provide full details on how these bounds are used during optimization for all parameter signs, nor on the tightness and impact on estimation bias. There is no theoretical analysis of statistical consistency or computational complexity; sparsistency is deferred as “expected by extension,” but not proved.
- Experiments: The simulated study design is sensible (hub, scale-free; both positive/negative and positive-only generative mechanisms). ROC curves are a reasonable summary. However, the paper omits quantitative baselines for the original PGM (stating “results not shown”) and does not compare to other count-network approaches beyond Gaussian/copula (e.g., Poisson GLM neighborhood selection of [18] as a baseline with its known limitations). For the TCGA case study, pre-processing details and the choice R=11 as “maximum count” are unclear for sequencing data, where raw counts are usually much larger; clarification is needed on normalization/binning. No runtime/scalability metrics are reported.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well-organized, with clear motivation, model statements, and theorem placements. Some notational issues and typos appear (e.g., “SGPM” vs “SPGM”; undefined symbols like W; occasional missing punctuation). The construction of SPGM’s piecewise B(x;R0,R) would benefit from an explicit formula and intuition in the main text (the appendix is referenced). The learning section should provide more detail on objective functions, bounds used per model, convergence criteria, and hyperparameter choices (R0, R selection).

Originality
- Negative-result on Winsorization-based node-conditionals yielding no joint (Theorem 1) addresses a specific claim in the spatial statistics literature and is a useful clarification.
- TPGM is a straightforward EF truncation; while natural, it is incremental.
- Theorem 3’s necessity of quadratic base measure for linear sufficient statistics to allow positive/negative dependencies appears novel and conceptually important.
- QPGM and SPGM represent new model classes within the EF graphical modeling framework. QPGM is conceptually close to Gaussian-like energy with count support, whereas SPGM’s sublinear sufficient statistic is a novel construction enabling heavy tails and unconstrained pairwise parameters.

Significance
The paper addresses an important gap: multivariate count models that can accommodate both positive and negative conditional dependencies with tractable conditionals for neighborhood selection. Theoretical insights (especially Theorem 3) are likely to inform future designs. Practically, the learning methods are only partially specified (reliance on variational bounds) and lack guarantees, and empirical comparisons omit some relevant baselines. The case study is promising but primarily illustrative. Overall, significance is moderate to good, with stronger impact contingent on clarified inference and stronger empirical evaluation.

Pros
- Clear identification and rigorous treatment of the normalizability barrier for standard PGMs.
- Negative result correcting prior Winsorization-based claims (Theorem 1).
- Conceptually insightful necessity result on base measure scaling (Theorem 3).
- Two constructive alternatives (QPGM, SPGM) enabling positive and negative dependencies; SPGM allows heavy tails.
- Empirical evidence of improved edge recovery over Gaussian-based baselines on count data.

Cons
- Inference details are incomplete; reliance on variational bounds is not fully justified, and applicability across parameter regimes is unclear.
- No statistical consistency or finite-sample guarantees; sparsistency is not established.
- TPGM’s bounded support and sensitivity to R are acknowledged but not systematically studied; choice of R (and R0) is ad hoc.
- Experimental baselines are limited; the original PGM results are omitted; no runtime/scalability reporting.
- Some clarity and notation issues; pre-processing for the case study (R=11) requires explanation.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide proof sketches for Theorems 1 and 3 in the main text to clarify the key arguments; specify why R>3 is required in Theorem 1.
- Detail the optimization for QPGM and SPGM: exact objectives, how variational bounds are integrated, handling of negative canonical parameters for SPGM, convergence diagnostics, and computational complexity.
- Analyze sensitivity to R and R0 in TPGM/SPGM, and propose data-driven selection procedures beyond setting R to the empirical maximum.
- Include full results for the original PGM estimator and Poisson-GLM-based neighborhood selection to contextualize gains, and report runtimes.
- Clarify data processing for the TCGA case study (normalization, filtering, why maximum count is 11).
- Improve notation consistency (define W, ensure symbols are introduced) and fix typos.

Overall, the paper provides solid theoretical contributions and promising model designs; strengthening the inference methodology and empirical evaluation would substantially improve its impact.
