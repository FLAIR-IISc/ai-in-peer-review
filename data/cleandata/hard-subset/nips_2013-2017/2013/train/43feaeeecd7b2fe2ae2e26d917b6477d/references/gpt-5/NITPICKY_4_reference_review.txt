Summary
The paper tackles the long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) which, in their classical auto-model form, only admit non-positive pairwise parameters and thus model only negative conditional dependencies. The authors (i) prove that a previously proposed “Winsorized Poisson” conditional construction cannot yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1); (ii) propose a valid exponential-family truncation that leads to a Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) on a bounded integer domain that allows both positive and negative dependencies (Theorem 2); (iii) show that for linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, achieving normalizability with nonnegative edges on the full (unbounded) count domain requires a base measure that scales at least quadratically (Theorem 3); this motivates (iv) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base/self-potentials that can permit positive and negative edges via a negative-definite quadratic form (Proposition 2); and (v) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure but replaces the linear sufficient statistic with a piecewise sublinear/saturating one, ensuring normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). Experiments compare TPGM and SPGM to Gaussian-based competitors on synthetic and a microRNA network dataset.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: multivariate count MRFs that can accommodate positive and negative dependencies.
- Negative result (Theorem 1) clarifies a persistent misconception about Winsorized conditionals and auto-Poisson consistency; if correct, this is valuable to the community.
- TPGM is a clean, exponential-family truncation that trivially ensures normalizability and permits positive edges; rigorous and intuitive.
- The necessity argument (Theorem 3) that a quadratic (or heavier) base measure is needed for linear sufficient statistics is conceptually insightful.
- QPGM and SPGM provide two complementary routes: QPGM for convex/gaussianesque tails with a global quadratic penalty; SPGM for heavier tails via saturating sufficient statistics; both allow positive and negative dependencies.
- Empirical indications that TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods on count data; a biologically plausible case study.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical soundness and proofs
- Theorem 1 (Winsorized conditionals inconsistency) is central. However, no proof is provided in the submission, and the statement is very strong (“never exists” for R>3, with arbitrary canonical parameter E(XV\s)). The result appears to hinge on regularity conditions (e.g., compatibility/Markov property structure from Besag’s auto-model framework). Please include a rigorous proof (or clear sketch) and explicitly state assumptions (e.g., positivity, support, regularity of the conditional family). This is crucial.
- Theorem 3 (necessity of Ω(Z^2) base measure with B(x)=x for normalizability when θst not all ≤0) is also pivotal; again, full proof details and precise conditions are needed (are you quantifying over all parameter vectors with some strictly positive edge? or existence of any positive edge? are diagonal/self-potentials allowed as part of the base measure only, or as parameters?). Since log Z! ~ Z log Z − Z is subquadratic, the result is plausible, but the exact quantifiers and conditions matter.
- QPGM variational upper bound: D(θ;XV\s) = log∑x exp{ηx + θss x^2} ≤ log ∫ exp{ηx + θss x^2} dx. The inequality sum ≤ integral is not justified; it does not hold unconditionally. For log-concave integrands one can bound ∑ by an integral up to constants, but please provide a correct inequality (e.g., Euler–Maclaurin or log-concavity-based bounds) and clarify when it is valid. Moreover, the reported closed form has a likely missing factor of 1/4 in the quadratic term in η (the standard Gaussian integral yields −η^2/(4θss), θss<0, but the manuscript shows −(1/θss)η^2). Please check and correct.
- Proposition 2: Define precisely Θ (does it contain θss on the diagonal and θst/2 on off-diagonals, or θst as-is?) and the vectorization of X so that XTΘX matches the joint energy. A clear mapping is required to interpret “Θ ≺ 0.” Also clarify whether “Θ<0” means entrywise negativity or negative-definite; both phrases are used.
- SPGM Theorem 4: The normalizability claim for all θst is believable because the saturating B(x) truncates pairwise growth and −log x! ensures tails; please add a brief proof or tail bound. Also ensure B(x;R0,R) is continuous at the breakpoints and specify R0<R and all constants cleanly.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation is inconsistent: W and Wp are used without definition. XV\s appears multiple times with different spacing. “SGPM” appears once (typo). “form count-valued” and many other typos. Please carefully proofread.
- The piecewise definition of B(x;R0,R) is difficult to parse and lacks explicit constants; the current inline form is prone to misinterpretation. Provide a clear, unambiguous formula and a figure with axes labeled.
- Several references are incomplete or missing bibliographic details (years, venues, pages) and some relevant related work is not discussed (see below).

Algorithmic/empirical concerns
- Learning details are underspecified for QPGM/SPGM. The conditional log-partitions appear in the node-conditional likelihood; how are these computed or approximated in practice (especially for SPGM with infinite sums)? The variational bounds are presented but not integrated into an algorithm with guarantees. Complexity and convergence details of the proximal gradient method are missing (step size selection, stopping, numerics).
- Baselines are limited. Given the focus on count data, comparisons should include Poisson log-normal graphical models, negative binomial MRFs/hierarchical models, or discrete copula graphical models tailored to counts, not only Gaussian/nonparanormal. The original PGM baseline results are “not shown” and should be included for completeness.
- Simulation design: For TPGM/SPGM, “R set to the maximum count value” is data-dependent and can be optimistic. Also, the claim that TPGM excels in high-dimensional settings due to mass near R needs quantitative support (e.g., calibration or sensitivity to R). Evaluate held-out likelihood or calibration in addition to ROC.
- Real data: It states “R=11, the maximum count,” which is implausible for RNA-seq counts unless heavy preprocessing or winsorization was performed. Please describe preprocessing, normalization, and how R0,R were chosen. Without this, the case study is not reproducible, and results are hard to interpret.
- No statistical guarantees for structure recovery are provided; deferring sparsistency to future work weakens the contribution given the emphasis on graph learning.

Related work
- Beyond [7–10], please discuss and compare against Poisson log-normal models and other latent variable approaches for count graphical dependence (e.g., PLN network models), negative binomial variants, and mixed graphical models with count nodes. This will better position your contributions.

Pros
- Important problem with clear motivation.
- Clean negative result on Winsorization (if proven).
- Three concrete model families (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with intuitive properties and normalizability arguments.
- Potentially useful in practice; preliminary empirical benefits.

Cons
- Missing or unclear proofs for key results; an error in the Gaussian bound; unclear conditions.
- Algorithmic details insufficient; unclear computation of node-conditional normalizers and gradients.
- Limited and partly irreproducible experiments; missing baselines; unclear preprocessing and hyperparameter selection.
- Presentation issues (notation, typos, incomplete references).

Recommendation
At present I lean to reject. The conceptual contributions are promising and could be impactful, but the paper needs (i) rigorous and complete proofs (especially Theorems 1 and 3, corrected variational bounds), (ii) clearer algorithms and computational details, (iii) stronger, reproducible empirical evaluation with appropriate count-model baselines, and (iv) substantial clarity/formatting fixes. With these addressed, this work could be a strong contribution.
