This paper addresses the challenging problem of modeling multivariate count data using graphical models. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to represent positive correlations – and propose several novel approaches to overcome this, including the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The contributions are significant, as they offer new tools for analyzing data ubiquitous in fields like genomics and spatial statistics.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates a significant limitation of existing PGMs and motivates the need for new approaches. The identification of the non-positivity constraint is a crucial observation.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical development of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM is thorough and well-presented. The proofs regarding normalizability and the discussion of sufficient statistics are commendable. The theorem establishing the quadratic base measure requirement is particularly insightful.
* **Novelty:** The proposed models – especially the QPGM and SPGM – represent a genuinely novel contribution. They extend the scope of PGMs to encompass a wider range of dependency structures.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of both simulation studies and a real-world case study (breast cancer miRNA networks) adds significant value. The comparison with existing methods (Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal models) demonstrates the potential benefits of the proposed approaches. The use of TCGA data and comparison with prior work using the same dataset is a strong point.
* **Clarity (mostly):** The core ideas are presented clearly, and the notation is generally consistent. The paper is well-organized, progressing logically from the limitations of existing methods to the proposed solutions and their evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (some sections):** While generally clear, sections 3.1 and 3.2, specifically the derivation and justification of the SPGM, are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The motivation for the sublinear sufficient statistic isn’t immediately obvious and feels less organically connected to the problem statement than the quadratic approach.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper acknowledges the computational challenges of estimating the models but doesn't provide detailed computational analysis. The scalability of the proposed methods to very high-dimensional datasets remains an open question. Specifically, the reliance on proximal gradient ascent, while common, doesn't guarantee efficient performance for very large problems.
* **Limited Scope of Simulations:** The simulation studies focus on specific network structures (hub and scale-free). It would be beneficial to explore the performance of the models under a wider range of network topologies and dependence strengths.
* **Parameter Tuning:**  The choice of parameters (R, R0) in the TPGM and SPGM is somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic approach to parameter selection (e.g., cross-validation) could enhance the robustness of the methods. The paper mentions stability selection but doesn't detail the cross-validation strategy used for parameter tuning.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:** The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the assumptions underlying the proposed models. For example, what are the implications of assuming a specific functional form for the base measure or sufficient statistic?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in existing methods for modeling multivariate count data.
* The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are novel and theoretically well-grounded.
* The experimental results demonstrate the potential benefits of the proposed approaches.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Some sections (specifically related to SPGM) lack sufficient clarity and intuitive explanation.
* The computational complexity of the methods is not fully addressed.
* The simulation studies could be more comprehensive.
* The parameter tuning process is not thoroughly explained.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of graphical models for count data. The authors have identified a crucial limitation of existing approaches and proposed innovative solutions. While some aspects of the paper could be further refined (particularly in terms of clarity and computational analysis), the overall quality and potential impact of the work are substantial. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance, and with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, it could make a valuable contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Improve the clarity of the SPGM derivation and motivation.
2.  Provide more discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the methods.
3.  Expand the simulation studies to include a wider range of network topologies.
4.  Elaborate on the parameter tuning strategy used in the experiments.
5.  Explicitly state the assumptions underlying the models and discuss their potential implications.
