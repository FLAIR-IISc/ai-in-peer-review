## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of modeling multivariate count data, a common scenario in modern applications like genomics and spatial statistics, where traditional graphical models struggle. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to represent negative dependencies – and thoroughly investigate solutions. They debunk a proposed fix using Winsorized Poisson distributions, demonstrating its inconsistency. The core contribution lies in proposing three novel PGMs: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models progressively relax the constraints of the original PGM, allowing for both positive and negative dependencies. The paper also briefly explores learning the network structure and provides illustrative results on simulated and real-world data.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on the foundation laid by Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19] regarding PGMs. It directly critiques the work of Kaiser and Cressie [20] and Griffith [21] on Winsorized Poisson distributions. The work is relevant to the broader area of graphical model research, particularly the development of models for non-Gaussian data, connecting to approaches like copula-based methods [12] and non-parametric extensions [7-10].  The application to microRNA expression data places the work within the context of bioinformatics and high-throughput sequencing analysis, which has seen increasing use of graphical models [24].

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper articulates a significant problem in statistical modeling – the lack of suitable graphical models for multivariate count data.
* **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation for their proposed models, including proofs demonstrating the inconsistency of the Winsorized Poisson approach and the normalizability conditions for the QPGM and SPGM. The analysis of the base measure requirements is insightful.
* **Novelty:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent original contributions to the field, offering distinct solutions to the problem of modeling dependencies in count data. The SPGM, in particular, is a clever approach to achieving both normalizability and flexibility.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to breast cancer microRNA expression data demonstrates the potential of the models in a real-world setting.  The empirical results, though brief, suggest the models outperform existing alternatives.
* **Comprehensive Coverage:** The paper thoroughly explores the limitations of existing approaches and provides a detailed justification for the proposed solutions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the application to the microRNA dataset is promising, the experimental section feels somewhat limited. The simulation studies could benefit from more detailed parameter exploration and a comparison to a wider range of baseline models. The parameter settings in simulations are not fully justified.
* **Complexity of Models:**  The QPGM and SPGM, while theoretically appealing, introduce added complexity compared to the original PGM.  The practical implications of this complexity, particularly concerning computational cost and parameter estimation, are not fully addressed. The benefits of SPGM versus QPGM are not clearly delineated.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper mentions using proximal gradient ascent for learning, but lacks details about the optimization process, such as the specific regularization parameters used and convergence criteria. This makes it difficult to assess the reproducibility of the results.
* **Variational Bound Justification:** The justification of using a Gaussian bound for SPGM feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more detailed analysis of the accuracy of this approximation would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a challenging problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of graphical models and Poisson distributions, and their proposed models offer a significant advancement in the field.  While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and the models are complex, the theoretical contributions and potential for practical applications are compelling.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding graphical models for count data.
* Presents novel and theoretically well-supported models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
* Demonstrates the inconsistency of a previously proposed solution (Winsorized Poisson).
* Shows promising results on a real-world dataset.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Empirical evaluation is limited and could be strengthened.
* Models are complex and their computational implications are not fully explored.
* Lack of detailed implementation information hinders reproducibility.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors expanding the empirical evaluation with more extensive simulations and a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and parameter tuning for the QPGM and SPGM. Providing more details about the implementation (optimization algorithm, regularization parameters) would also enhance the reproducibility of the results. The justification for the variational bound used in the SPGM should also be expanded upon.  Addressing these points will further solidify the paper's contributions and increase its impact on the field.
