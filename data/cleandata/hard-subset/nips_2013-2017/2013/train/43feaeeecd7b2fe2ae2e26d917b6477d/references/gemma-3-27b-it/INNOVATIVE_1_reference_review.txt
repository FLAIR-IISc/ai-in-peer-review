This paper tackles a challenging problem: extending graphical models to handle multivariate count data, a common scenario in modern datasets. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches – exponential growth of parameters in log-linear models, intractability of multivariate Poisson distributions, and the restrictive negative-dependency constraint of the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM). Their proposed solutions – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – represent a significant step towards addressing these challenges. The theoretical work demonstrating the necessity of quadratic base measures for normalizability and positive dependencies is particularly compelling. I appreciate the authors' frank assessment of the limitations of the TPGM and their willingness to explore more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. The experimental validation, though relatively limited, provides initial evidence supporting the efficacy of their methods, especially the SPGM, and demonstrates practical applicability to real-world datasets like miRNA expression.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core contributions – the SPGM and QPGM – are genuinely novel and offer a promising direction for modeling multivariate count data. The theoretical justification for the quadratic base measure is a key insight.
*   **Problem Importance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to a growing number of applications. Current tools are inadequate, and the authors clearly articulate this need.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents solid theoretical foundations for the proposed models, including proofs of normalizability and analysis of dependencies.
*   **Practical Demonstration:** The application to miRNA expression data is a valuable demonstration of the practical relevance of the models. The comparison against established methods (Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal) is well-executed.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The authors effectively build a case for their work by meticulously outlining the shortcomings of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Scope:** The experimental section feels somewhat limited. While the results are encouraging, a more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and with more rigorous statistical comparisons would strengthen the claims. Specifically, the simulations are limited to two network structures, and more complex scenarios (e.g., varying sparsity levels, different dependency structures) should be considered.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't fully address the computational challenges associated with inference and parameter estimation in the QPGM and SPGM.  While they mention using `l1` regularization, a discussion of scalability and potential optimization strategies would be beneficial.
*   **SPGM Parameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters R and R0 for the SPGM seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters is warranted.
*   **Limited Theoretical Exploration of SPGM:** The discussion of SPGM feels less developed than the QPGM. A deeper dive into the theoretical properties of the sublinear sufficient statistic and its impact on model behavior would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces fundamentally new approaches to modeling multivariate count data with graphical models.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant and address a critical limitation of existing methods.
*   The experimental results, while limited, are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed models.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, proposed solutions, and their implications.
*   The potential impact of this work is high, as it addresses a growing need in various fields.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is somewhat limited in scope.
*   The computational complexity of the proposed models is not fully addressed.
*   The SPGM, while promising, lacks the same level of theoretical development as the QPGM.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper presents a highly novel and significant contribution to the field of graphical models. The authors have identified a critical gap in the existing literature and proposed innovative solutions that address this gap effectively. While further work is needed to fully explore the computational challenges and validate the models on a wider range of datasets, the theoretical foundations and initial experimental results are compelling. I am willing to overlook the relatively limited experimental scope given the substantial novelty and potential impact of this work. The paper clearly advances the state of the art and is likely to stimulate further research in this area.



**Rating:** Accept.
