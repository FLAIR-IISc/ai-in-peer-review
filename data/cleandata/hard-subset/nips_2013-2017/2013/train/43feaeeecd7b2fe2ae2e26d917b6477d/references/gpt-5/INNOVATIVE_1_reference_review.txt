Summary
This paper tackles a longstanding limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the standard auto-Poisson MRF only admits non-positive pairwise parameters, thus modeling only competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. The authors (i) prove that “Winsorized” Poisson node-conditionals advocated in spatial statistics cannot yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1), (ii) introduce an exponential-family-consistent truncated Poisson that does yield a valid joint model (TPGM) with finite support and unrestricted edge signs (Theorem 2), (iii) establish a necessary condition for having both positive and negative dependencies with linear sufficient statistics: the base measure must scale at least quadratically (Theorem 3), leading to (iv) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with linear sufficient statistics and quadratic base measure, normalizable when the quadratic potential is negative-definite or pointwise negative (Proposition 2), and (v) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure but replaces the sufficient statistic with a sublinear, saturating function to ensure normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They outline neighborhood selection estimators, give simple variational upper bounds for node-conditionals, and present experiments on simulated networks and a TCGA breast cancer miRNA count dataset, showing advantages over Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM.

Relation to prior work
The work is grounded in the exponential-family MRF framework (Besag; Yang et al.), clarifies and corrects prior claims on Winsorized Poisson auto-models (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith), and contrasts with multivariate Poisson constructions via independent summands that only model positive correlations. It complements copula-based and latent Gaussian approaches by giving direct MRFs on counts. One notable related line not cited is the Conway–Maxwell–Poisson (COM-Poisson) family and Poisson log-normal/negative-binomial latent-variable graphical models; discussing differences would strengthen positioning.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Clean, principled pathways to overcome the negative-edge restriction in count MRFs. The necessity result on base-measure growth (Theorem 3) is particularly insightful and guides model design.
- Multiple concrete model classes: TPGM (finite-support EF-consistent), QPGM (quadratic base measure permitting mixed signs), and SPGM (saturating sufficient statistic with factorial base measure and heavy tails).
- Corrects a misconception: Theorem 1 convincingly shows that Winsorized node-conditionals cannot be made consistent with a joint MRF of the Besag/Yang type.
- Practical learning route: Neighborhood selection with node-conditional likelihoods; variational bounds for conditional partition functions.
- Evidence of utility: Simulations and a real RNA-seq case study indicate improved edge recovery over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines and uncover biologically plausible hubs.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Important baselines are missing: (i) Poisson log-normal or latent Gaussian copula models for counts, (ii) COM-Poisson-based methods, and (iii) the original PGM results are mentioned as “not shown.” Including these and reporting calibration of positive/negative edge recovery would make the case more compelling.
- Practical guidance is thin. For SPGM, how should R0 and R be chosen or learned? Sensitivity analyses and heuristics (e.g., via quantiles or cross-validation) would help; currently these hyperparameters seem set ad hoc.
- QPGM connections. QPGM’s quadratic potential on integer lattices resembles discrete Gaussian MRFs; a discussion of identifiability, relation to discrete Gaussian distributions, and interpretability of θss versus θst would orient readers. Also, clarify the scope of Proposition 2 (global negative-definiteness vs. local sufficient conditions).
- Theorem 3 assumptions. The necessity claim C(Z)=Ω(Z^2) is central; please state precise conditions (graph degree constraints, coefficient bounds) and whether the result is uniform over p, or depends on maximum degree.
- Computation and scalability. Node-conditional SPGM involves infinite sums; while bounded by Poisson’s partition function for θ≥0, how are tails handled in practice when θ<0, and what truncation guarantees accuracy? Report runtimes and convergence diagnostics for proximal gradient ascent.
- Clarity and reproducibility. Some notation is dense and a few references look incomplete. Code availability would enhance impact.

Quality
Overall technically solid with several nontrivial results; some proofs are deferred and key conditions could be stated more crisply. The learning methodology is reasonable, but practical details (hyperparameter selection, computational costs) are sparse.

Clarity
Well-organized and mostly clear, though the exposition could better highlight intuitions (e.g., why SPGM’s saturation balances factorial growth) and provide a concise table contrasting PGM/TPGM/QPGM/SPGM properties (support, tails, allowed signs, tractability). Tighten/reference list.

Originality
High. The impossibility result for Winsorization, the necessity of quadratic base measure with linear sufficient statistics, and the SPGM construction are novel and insightful. TPGM is a natural but useful EF-consistent truncation; QPGM and SPGM offer bold design directions.

Significance
High potential impact. Direct MRFs for counts that allow mixed-sign dependencies address a real gap across genomics, neuroscience, and spatial statistics. Even if some models see limited immediate adoption (e.g., QPGM’s thin tails), the design principles and SPGM/TPGM could catalyze follow-on work.

Suggestions for the authors
- Add comparisons to Poisson log-normal and COM-Poisson-based approaches; include original PGM results in figures.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for R, R0 and guidelines for their selection/learning.
- Clarify assumptions and scope of Theorem 3 and Proposition 2; discuss relation to discrete Gaussian MRFs.
- Detail computational choices (truncation of sums, step sizes), report runtimes, and provide code.

Verdict
I value the bold conceptual advances here. Despite empirical and practical gaps, the paper opens a promising direction with solid theory and credible demonstrations. I recommend acceptance.
