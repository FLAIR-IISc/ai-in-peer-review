Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the standard auto-Poisson/PGM construction only admits non-positive pairwise parameters to ensure normalizability, hence cannot model positive conditional dependencies. The authors make four main contributions:

- They show that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals proposed in the spatial stats literature (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) do not yield a valid joint MRF under the Besag/Yang construction. Theorem 1 formalizes this incompatibility by showing that such node-conditionals cannot be jointly consistent.
- They propose a tractable Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) that retains an exponential-family form on a finite support {0,…,R}, yielding a valid MRF that allows both positive and negative dependencies (Theorem 2).
- They analyze necessary conditions for allowing both signs of dependencies while preserving normalizability when sufficient statistics are linear. Theorem 3 argues that the base measure must scale at least quadratically, motivating a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with node-wise quadratic terms and sufficient conditions for normalizability via a negative-definite quadratic form (Proposition 2).
- They propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps log(X!) as base measure but replaces the linear sufficient statistic X with a sublinear, saturating function B(x;R0,R), making pairwise interactions bounded and enabling normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They provide variational upper bounds for node-conditional partition functions for QPGM (via a Gaussian integral) and for SPGM when θ ≥ 0 (via Poisson).

Empirically, they use l1-penalized node-conditional likelihood (neighborhood selection) to recover graph structure and report ROC curves on simulated data (TPGM and sums-of-Poissons generators, hub and scale-free graphs), and a case study on TCGA miRNA sequencing counts. TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines and reveal biologically plausible hubs and a chromosome 19 clique; the original PGM recovers very sparse, mostly negative edges as expected.

Quality
- Theory: The compatibility result (Theorem 1) addresses a widely cited workaround and appears technically sound: the Winsorized conditional includes an indicator term that breaks the exponential-family compatibility needed for a consistent MRF. The TPGM construction is standard for finite-support exponential families, and Theorem 2 follows from Besag/Yang conditions. The necessity of a quadratic-order base measure for linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) is well-motivated via growth-rate comparisons: pairwise terms grow quadratically (XsXt), whereas log(X!) is subquadratic, so C(Z) must counterbalance. QPGM’s sufficient condition for normalizability via a negative-definite quadratic form is standard. SPGM’s bounded interactions via saturating B(x) make Theorem 4 plausible. Variational bounds are reasonable, though the SPGM bound is stated only for θ ≥ 0.
- Algorithms/experiments: The learning approach is straightforward (node-conditional l1-penalized likelihood with proximal gradients). Empirical results are encouraging, but some practical details are missing (complexity, convergence, choice and sensitivity of R and R0, treatment when counts exceed R). No sparsistency guarantees are proved (claimed as future work), and the synthetic validation doesn’t include data generated from QPGM/SPGM themselves to test model match.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well-organized, with a coherent narrative from limitations to constructive solutions. A few notational slips (e.g., domain W, “SGPM” typo), and some references are incomplete. The role and selection of truncation parameters (R, R0) merit more guidance and a sensitivity analysis. The statement of Theorem 3 (necessity) could be complemented by a discussion of sufficiency conditions beyond QPGM’s negative-definite case.

Originality
- High. The incompatibility result for Winsorized conditionals is novel and useful. TPGM is a principled finite-support exponential-family PGM allowing positive edges. The necessity argument for quadratic scaling of the base measure given linear sufficient statistics is insightful. QPGM and SPGM are new classes that fill an important modeling gap by enabling positive dependencies in count-valued MRFs, with clear constructions and normalizability conditions.

Significance
- Modeling conditional dependence in multivariate count data matters in genomics, neuroimaging, spatial statistics, and beyond. The paper addresses a long-standing limitation of PGMs and proposes practical alternatives with theoretical backing. If adopted, these models could replace ad hoc Gaussian copulas or Poisson-only-negative approaches in many applications. The main limitations are computational (partition functions for SPGM, selection of R/R0) and the lack of theoretical guarantees for structure recovery in this submission; nonetheless, the contributions are substantive and likely to influence subsequent work.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a core limitation of existing PGMs for counts.
- Rigorous negative result (Theorem 1) clarifying a misconception in prior literature.
- Three constructive model families (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with explicit normalizability conditions.
- Variational tools enabling nodewise estimation.
- Empirical evidence on simulated and real data demonstrating practical advantage.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Learning theory (sparsistency, sample complexity) is deferred; even a sketch or conditions paralleling Yang et al. would strengthen the paper.
- Limited discussion of computational cost and convergence, especially for SPGM’s infinite sums; consider truncation strategies, error bounds, or efficient approximations.
- Choice and sensitivity of R, R0 require systematic analysis; clarify how to handle out-of-range counts at test time.
- Comparators: consider Poisson log-normal/correlated latent Gaussian models or copula count models where possible, and include data generated from QPGM/SPGM to test in-model recovery.
- Clarify Theorem 3’s scope and any sufficient conditions beyond negative definiteness; tighten notation and fix typos.

Pro/Con for acceptance
Pros:
- Strong theoretical contributions with clear practical motivation.
- Novel, principled models enabling positive dependencies in count MRFs.
- Demonstrated empirical benefits on tasks of current interest (RNA-seq networks).

Cons:
- Missing learning guarantees; some algorithmic/practical details are thin.
- Sensitivity to truncation parameters and tail behavior not fully explored.
- Experimental comparisons could be broadened.

Recommendation
Weak accept to accept. The paper makes a meaningful theoretical and methodological advance on an important problem, and the empirical evidence is supportive. Addressing clarity/experimental scope and adding guidance on parameter selection would further strengthen it.
