Summary
The paper tackles a well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the classical auto-Poisson/MRF construction is only normalizable with non-positive pairwise parameters, hence cannot model positive conditional dependencies. The authors:

- Disprove a popular “Winsorized Poisson” fix (Kaiser & Cressie) by showing no joint distribution is compatible with those node-conditionals (Theorem 1).
- Propose a truncated exponential-family variant (TPGM) on {0,…,R} whose node-conditionals remain EF and yield a valid MRF with both positive/negative edges (Theorem 2). They discuss limitations as R grows.
- Argue that with linear sufficient statistics B(X)=X, allowing mixed-sign edges requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3), motivating a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base/terms and a normalizability condition via a negative-definite quadratic form.
- Propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains the Poisson base measure but uses a piecewise sublinear, eventually saturating sufficient statistic B(x;R0,R), claiming normalizability for any pairwise parameters (Theorem 4) and offering heavier tails than QPGM.
- Outline neighborhood selection with ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihoods, with variational bounds for intractable node-wise partition functions, and present simulations and a miRNA network case study.

Relation to prior work is broadly covered (Besag auto-models, GLM-based neighborhood selection, copula/nonparanormal methods), and the paper positions itself against both classical Poisson MRFs and Gaussian(-copula) approaches.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: modeling multivariate count data with both positive and negative dependencies in an MRF.
- The TPGM construction is clean and leverages the EF compatibility framework of Yang et al., with a clear explanation of trade-offs as R→∞.
- The necessity claim (Theorem 3) clarifies why the original Poisson base measure cannot coexist with mixed-sign interactions under linear stats, which is conceptually insightful.
- QPGM provides a simple sufficient condition (negative-definite quadratic form) for normalizability, connecting to familiar Gaussian intuition.
- SPGM’s bounded sufficient statistic is a plausible way to tame tail behavior and remove sign constraints.
- Empirically, TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines on some simulated count scenarios and yield plausible hubs in the miRNA case study.

Weaknesses
- Theory: Several central claims (Theorem 1’s impossibility result; Theorem 3’s necessity C(Z)=Ω(Z^2); Theorem 4’s unconditional normalizability) are only stated, with no proofs or sufficient detail to assess technical correctness. Given their importance (especially Theorem 1’s negation of prior work), complete proofs or at least proof sketches are essential.
- Model soundness and scope: The “Poisson” label becomes tenuous for QPGM (quadratic base) and SPGM (ad hoc piecewise sufficient statistic). Beyond normalizability, there is no discussion of the induced mean-variance relationship, overdispersion/zero-inflation, or interpretability. Many count datasets are overdispersed relative to Poisson; how do QPGM/SPGM address this?
- Learning/inference details are underspecified. For SPGM, the node-wise log-partition is intractable; the provided variational bound holds only for θ≥0, yet the method is used for mixed-sign parameters. There is no analysis of convexity, Lipschitz constants, or convergence guarantees for the proposed proximal-gradient optimization. Without these, it is unclear the optimization is well-posed or scalable.
- Empirical validation is limited and somewhat circular. TPGM is best when data are generated from TPGM. Competing count models are missing (e.g., Poisson log-normal graphical models, negative binomial/overdispersed models, copulas tailored to counts). The original auto-Poisson estimator is dismissed “results not shown.” ROC curves alone, without calibration or precision-recall, are limited. Sensitivity to hyperparameters R and R0 is not analyzed.
- Real data: The miRNA analysis uses R=11 as maximum count, which is surprisingly low for RNA-seq unless heavy filtering/normalization was applied (details unclear). There is no ground truth; biological anecdotes (known hubs) are suggestive but insufficient as evaluation. No robustness or stability beyond generic stability selection.
- Clarity/presentation: Some notation is undefined (e.g., W, Wp), several references are incomplete, and figures are referenced but not present here. Important implementation choices (how the variational bounds are used in optimization; how R,R0 are selected) are not described in a reproducible way.

Quality
Moderate. The constructions appear plausible, but the most consequential theorems lack proof, and the learning methodology has significant gaps (tractability/convexity, approximation accuracy). The experiments do not rigorously validate against the most relevant alternatives.

Clarity
Fair to good in exposition of ideas; weaker in technical completeness and experimental details.

Originality
Conceptually novel directions (truncation within EF for MRF compatibility; necessity of quadratic base; sublinear sufficient stats). However, several choices feel ad hoc without a stronger generative or variational derivation.

Significance
Potentially significant if the theory is airtight and the learning is made robust. As it stands, the practical impact is unclear due to incomplete validation and missing comparisons to established count graphical models.

Questions for the authors
- Provide proofs or at least detailed sketches for Theorems 1, 3, and 4. In particular, what assumptions are needed for Theorem 3’s Ω(Z^2) claim?
- For SPGM, is the node-wise likelihood concave in θ? How is the variational bound used when θ<0? Any guarantees on estimation consistency or sparsistency under your EF construction?
- How are R and R0 chosen in practice? Sensitivity analyses?
- Why exclude Poisson log-normal/NB/copula-for-counts baselines? How does SPGM compare?
- For the RNA-seq data, what preprocessing yields max count R=11? Could you report normalization steps and distribution of counts?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a recognized limitation of Poisson MRFs with creative constructions.
- Useful conceptual insights about base measure vs interaction growth.
- Positive empirical indications on simulated data.

Cons for acceptance
- Key theoretical claims are unproven in the manuscript.
- Learning/inference methodology lacks rigor and clarity; approximations not justified.
- Limited and potentially biased empirical evaluation; missing strong baselines.
- Some modeling choices appear ad hoc and lack interpretability guarantees for counts.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (lean reject). The paper targets an important problem and proposes interesting ideas, but given the absence of proofs for central claims, the incomplete treatment of estimation and optimization, and limited empirical validation against established count models, I am not comfortable recommending acceptance at this time. Strengthening the theory, clarifying and justifying the learning algorithms, adding robust baselines, and providing sensitivity analyses would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence
High.
