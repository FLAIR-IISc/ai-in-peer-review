

# Review: On Poisson Graphical Models

This paper proposes three novel extensions to Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for modeling multivariate count data: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The authors correctly identify a fundamental limitation of existing PGMs: they can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The paper's main contribution is to propose models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies, which is a significant limitation in current approaches.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong theoretical foundation. The authors provide rigorous mathematical proofs (Theorems 1-4) demonstrating the normalizability of their proposed models under various conditions. The theoretical development is sound, and the paper properly situates its work within the broader context of graphical models, referencing key prior work such as the seminal papers by Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19].

**Strengths:**
- The paper clearly identifies a genuine limitation in existing Poisson graphical models (only negative dependencies).
- The theoretical contributions are mathematically rigorous and well-justified.
- The paper provides a clear roadmap for extending exponential family graphical models.
- The proposed models have a logical theoretical progression from truncation to quadratic base measures to sublinear sufficient statistics.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is insufficient empirical validation. While the authors present simulation studies and a single case study on breast cancer microRNA networks, these experiments are not comprehensive enough to support their claims of superior performance. The paper lacks:
1. Extensive comparison with relevant baselines (e.g., non-paranormal [7, 10], copula-based methods [12], or recent count-data-specific approaches).
2. Analysis of computational complexity and scalability of the proposed models.
3. Discussion of parameter sensitivity and practical implementation challenges.
4. Demonstration of how the models handle different data characteristics (e.g., varying sparsity, different levels of positive/negative dependencies).

The paper claims that "all methods compared outperform the original Poisson graphical model estimator," but fails to provide a meaningful comparison with other established methods for count data. For example, the non-paranormal approach [7] is widely used for count data and would be a more appropriate baseline than Gaussian graphical models (which are designed for continuous data). The authors also don't address why their approach is better than simply applying transformations to existing methods rather than proposing entirely new models.

The case study on breast cancer microRNA networks is superficial. The authors claim their methods find "biologically important hub miRNAs," but don't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim beyond naming two miRNAs that have been previously associated with breast cancer. The paper doesn't demonstrate that these findings are statistically significant or biologically meaningful beyond existing knowledge.

The paper also fails to adequately address a critical practical concern: the need to choose parameters like R (for TPGM) or R0/R (for SPGM). These parameters are not automatically determined and could significantly affect model performance, yet the paper provides no guidance on how to select them in practice.

**Originality and Significance:**
While the theoretical developments are novel, the significance of the work is limited by the lack of comprehensive empirical validation. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed models are necessary or superior to existing approaches across a wide range of count data applications. The authors make strong claims about "rich dependence structures" and "broad applicability," but these claims aren't sufficiently supported by evidence.

The paper's approach of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics is conceptually interesting, but it's not clear that this is the most effective path forward. Many existing methods for count data (e.g., non-paranormal, copula-based approaches) already handle positive and negative dependencies without requiring such fundamental modifications to the model structure.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the field of graphical models, but it falls short of demonstrating the practical significance of the proposed models. The theoretical results are sound, but the empirical evidence is insufficient to support the paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed models. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive experiments comparing the proposed models with relevant baselines across multiple datasets and more thorough discussion of practical implementation considerations.

As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and empirical validation, I find the paper's theoretical contributions interesting but insufficiently supported by evidence. The authors have identified a real limitation in existing Poisson graphical models, but their proposed solutions lack the thorough validation required to convince the community of their practical utility.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper presents interesting theoretical ideas but fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed models. The limited empirical validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed approaches. The authors should conduct more comprehensive experiments comparing their models with relevant baselines before resubmitting.
