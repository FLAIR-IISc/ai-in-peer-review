Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: Besag’s auto-Poisson model and its generalizations admit only non-positive pairwise parameters to ensure normalizability, thus capturing only “competitive” (negative) conditional dependencies. The authors first revisit truncation-based remedies. They show that “Winsorized” Poisson node-conditionals, advocated in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith), cannot correspond to any joint MRF consistent with the Besag/Yang construction (Theorem 1). They then propose a proper exponential-family truncation that yields a valid joint MRF (TPGM) with finite partition function and both positive/negative edges (Theorem 2), but with drawbacks as R→∞ and sensitivity when counts approach the truncation level.

Motivated by these limitations, they analyze the role of base measure and sufficient statistics. They prove that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, normalizability with positive dependencies necessitates a base measure growing at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that modifies the base measure to be quadratic, enabling both positive and negative edges under a negative-definite quadratic form condition (Proposition 2) and allowing Gaussian variational bounds for nodewise estimation. To obtain heavier tails while permitting arbitrary pairwise parameters, they introduce a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains the Poisson base measure log x! but uses a piecewise sublinear sufficient statistic B(x;R0,R) that saturates for large x (Theorem 4). They give variational upper bounds for nodewise log-partitions (Gaussian for QPGM, Poisson for SPGM) to support neighborhood selection.

Empirically, they compare TPGM and SPGM to Gaussian graphical models (GGM), the nonparanormal, and nonparanormal SKEPTIC on simulated hub/scale-free graphs and on RNA-seq miRNA counts from TCGA. TPGM/SPGM recover more edges on count data and identify biologically plausible hubs missed by GGM; the original PGM misses much structure due to its sign restriction.

Quality
- Theory: The structural results are well-motivated and, if correct, important. Theorem 1 (incompatibility of Winsorized node-conditionals with any consistent joint) corrects a misconception in prior applied work. Theorem 3 provides a clear necessity condition tying base-measure growth to the possibility of positive dependencies. Normalizability conditions for QPGM (negative-definite quadratic form) and SPGM (bounded sufficient statistic with factorial base measure) are plausible. Proof details are not included in the main text; their validity is central.
- Methods: Neighborhood selection with variational upper bounds is reasonable, but the paper lacks algorithmic detail (gradients, convergence, constraint handling for QPGM) and theoretical guarantees (e.g., sparsistency) beyond “left for future work.”
- Experiments: Simulations and a case study support the claims qualitatively. However, baselines focused on Gaussian/copula methods; additional comparisons to alternative count-network models (e.g., Poisson log-normal, other latent-variable models) would strengthen the case. Hyperparameter sensitivity (R, R0) and scalability are not explored.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful motivation and discussion of trade-offs (e.g., TPGM degeneracy as R grows; QPGM’s thin tails). Some notation is introduced abruptly (e.g., W, Wp). Key assumptions (e.g., conditions of Theorem 1; exact domains of parameters in Proposition 2) could be stated more explicitly. Algorithmic and implementation details are sparse.

Originality
High. The negative-result on Winsorization for consistent PGMs, the necessity of quadratic-or-higher base measures for positive dependencies, and the design of QPGM/SPGM as viable classes appear to be new contributions in the graphical models literature for counts.

Significance
Modeling multivariate count data with rich dependency structures is important across several application domains (e.g., genomics, spatial data). Providing principled classes of count-MRFs that can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies, along with practical estimation surrogates, is valuable. The work advances understanding of what is and is not possible for Poisson-based MRFs and offers concrete alternatives.

Pros
- Corrects a misconception in prior literature (Theorem 1), clarifying limits of Winsorized node-conditionals.
- Develops three complementary model families (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with clear trade-offs and solid intuitions.
- Theorem 3 provides a fundamental and broadly informative constraint linking base measure growth to normalizability with positive edges.
- Practical estimation strategy via variational node-conditional bounds; empirical results consistent with theory and application relevance.
- Application to RNA-seq shows qualitative advantages and identifies known hubs.

Cons
- Limited algorithmic detail and no formal statistical guarantees for structure recovery; sparsistency deferred.
- Choice and tuning of SPGM parameters (R0, R) and the impact on estimation/interpretation are not systematically analyzed.
- QPGM learning requires handling negative-definiteness of the quadratic form; how this is enforced during nodewise estimation is unclear.
- Comparisons omit strong count-based baselines (e.g., Poisson log-normal graphical models, latent Gaussian copulas tailored to counts).
- Reproducibility: code, details on proximal updates, and computational costs are not provided.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide proofs (or detailed sketches) of Theorems 1 and 3 in the appendix, emphasizing key conditions.
- Elaborate the optimization procedures: exact objective surrogates, gradients, step sizes, constraints (especially for QPGM), and computational complexity; release code.
- Study sensitivity to R and R0 in SPGM and to truncation R in TPGM; offer guidance or data-driven tuning strategies.
- Compare against Poisson log-normal and other latent-variable count graphical models; evaluate edge sign accuracy and calibration.
- Include additional diagnostics (held-out likelihood, posterior predictive checks) and ablations to illustrate the practical regimes where each model is preferable.
- Discuss identifiability and interpretability of parameters when sufficient statistics saturate (SPGM) or when Gaussian-esque behavior arises (QPGM).

Recommendation: Weak Accept
This paper makes conceptually important and novel contributions to the theory and practice of graphical models for counts, addressing a well-known limitation of Poisson MRFs and proposing viable alternatives with clear theoretical underpinnings. While the empirical and algorithmic components could be strengthened, the work’s originality and potential impact justify acceptance. Confidence: medium.
