

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical models: the lack of a principled approach for modeling multivariate count data that permits both positive and negative conditional dependencies. The authors identify that existing Poisson-based graphical models either have restrictive parameter spaces (only allowing negative correlations) or lack proper theoretical foundations. They correctly identify a flaw in Kaiser and Cressie's "Winsorized Poisson" approach (Theorem 1) and propose three novel solutions: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models represent a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for count data.

The paper builds on Yang et al.'s foundational work on Poisson graphical models [18,19] while addressing their major limitation of non-positive edge weights. The authors make a compelling theoretical argument that normalizability with positive edge weights requires specific scaling properties of the base measure (Theorem 3), which leads to their novel model constructions.

## Strengths

The paper's strongest contribution is its rigorous theoretical foundation. The authors provide multiple theorems establishing the normalizability of their proposed models while permitting both positive and negative edge weights—something the existing literature has failed to achieve. The proof of Theorem 1 (that Winsorized Poisson cannot yield a consistent joint distribution) is particularly important and corrects a previously accepted approach.

The three model proposals represent meaningful, distinct approaches to the problem:
1. TPGM offers a simple truncation that maintains exponential family structure
2. QPGM demonstrates how modifying the base measure (to quadratic) enables positive edge weights
3. SPGM provides a more flexible approach with heavier tails by modifying the sufficient statistics

The experimental validation is appropriate for a theoretical paper. The simulation studies show TPGM and SPGM significantly outperform Gaussian-based approaches on count data, and the breast cancer microRNA network case study provides compelling real-world evidence of their utility. The finding that TPGM and SPGM identify biologically relevant hub miRNAs (mir-375 and mir-10b) that Gaussian methods miss is particularly convincing.

## Weaknesses

The paper has several important weaknesses that need addressing before acceptance:

1. **Limited Baseline Comparison**: The experiments compare only to Gaussian graphical models (GGM) and non-paranormal approaches. The authors should compare with other recent count-data models such as the generalized Poisson graphical models [28] or the multivariate Poisson regression models [29] that have been proposed in the statistics literature.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions "neighborhood selection" for structure learning but provides no details about computational complexity or scalability. For high-dimensional applications (p=100+), the computational cost of the proposed methods needs explicit discussion.

3. **SPGM Parameter Selection**: The SPGM requires choosing R₀ and R, which are not discussed in terms of how to select them in practice. The paper shows results with R=11 and R₀=5 for the real data, but doesn't explain how these values were chosen or how sensitive the results are to these parameters.

4. **Theoretical Gaps**: While Theorem 3 establishes the quadratic base measure requirement, the paper could better explain why this leads to Gaussian-like tail behavior (Section 3.1) and how this affects real-world applications where count data often exhibits heavy tails.

5. **Comparison to Related Work**: The paper references [18] and [19] extensively but doesn't sufficiently contrast with the more recent work by Allen and Liu [24] on RNA-seq data, which also addresses count data graphical modeling.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to graphical models for count data, with clear, well-proven theorems that address a genuine limitation in the literature. The three novel model proposals represent distinct and meaningful approaches to the problem. The experimental results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical utility.

However, the paper's weaknesses regarding baseline comparisons, implementation details, and parameter selection for SPGM are significant enough that the authors should address them before publication. The theoretical insights are strong, but the practical utility claims need stronger empirical backing.

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature on graphical models. The paper's main weakness is the limited empirical comparison with other count-data models and insufficient discussion of practical implementation details. The authors should address these points in their revision. The paper is well-written, clearly argues its case, and makes a clear contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in modeling multivariate count data.

The significance of this work is high, as count data is increasingly common in applications from genomics to social sciences. The models proposed here provide the first principled graphical model approach that can capture both positive and negative dependencies in count data—something that has been missing from the literature. The breast cancer microRNA case study demonstrates clear practical value beyond theoretical interest.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (with rigorous theorems), clearly written, original (addressing a gap in the literature), and significant (with clear practical applications). With the suggested revisions, it would be a strong contribution to the NeurIPS proceedings.
