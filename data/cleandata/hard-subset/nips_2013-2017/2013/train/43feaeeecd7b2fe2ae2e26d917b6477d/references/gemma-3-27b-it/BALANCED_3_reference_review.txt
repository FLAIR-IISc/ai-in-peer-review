This paper introduces novel graphical models for multivariate count data, addressing a significant limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) which can only model negative dependencies. The authors convincingly demonstrate the inadequacy of simply truncating the Poisson distribution to achieve both positive and negative correlations, and propose three new approaches: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The work is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and accompanied by promising experimental results on both simulated and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** The inability of standard PGMs to model positive dependencies is a major drawback, limiting their applicability to many real-world scenarios. This paper directly tackles this issue with a rigorous and insightful approach.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides clear proofs and justifications for its proposed models, including a theorem demonstrating the failure of the truncation approach suggested by Kaiser and Cressie [20]. The derivation of the QPGM and SPGM is logically sound and well-explained.
* **Novel Contributions:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuine innovations in the field of graphical models for count data.  The exploration of quadratic and sublinear base measures is particularly noteworthy.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and application to breast cancer miRNA data provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed models.  The comparison with existing methods, including Gaussian graphical models and non-paranormal models, demonstrates the advantages of the new approaches, particularly for count-valued data.  The use of a relevant biological dataset strengthens the impact of the work.
* **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the complex theoretical developments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper mentions using proximal gradient ascent for learning, a detailed discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed methods is lacking.  Especially for the QPGM and SPGM, the complexity of the log-partition function could be a significant bottleneck in practice.
* **Limited Exploration of SPGM:**  The SPGM is introduced as a potentially powerful extension, offering heavier tails, but receives less detailed analysis and empirical evaluation than the QPGM. A more thorough investigation of its properties and performance would be beneficial.  The connection to the variational upper bound using the Poisson distribution feels somewhat ad-hoc and could be expanded.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters (e.g., R and R0 in the TPGM and SPGM) could significantly impact performance.  The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to select these parameters effectively, beyond the use of cross-validation implicitly mentioned in the experimental setup.
* **Connections to Existing Literature:** While the paper cites relevant work, there’s an opportunity to connect more strongly with the literature on generalized linear models (GLMs) and their graphical extensions beyond just PGMs. Discussing how the proposed models relate to other GLM-based graphical model approaches could provide valuable context.
* **Assumptions of the Data Generating Process:** The simulations rely on generating data from the proposed models.  It would be insightful to include simulations using more realistic data generating processes common in the applications where these models are intended to be used.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing graphical models capable of capturing both positive and negative dependencies in multivariate count data.
* The theoretical contributions are substantial and rigorously justified.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed models to outperform existing approaches.
* The work is well-written and presented in a clear and organized manner.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational complexity of the proposed models may limit their practical applicability, especially for large-scale datasets.
* The lack of detailed guidance on parameter tuning could make it challenging for practitioners to use the models effectively.
* The SPGM, despite its potential, receives less thorough treatment than the QPGM.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of graphical models. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths clearly outweigh them. The novelty of the approach, combined with the theoretical rigor and promising empirical results, makes this paper a worthy candidate for acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

*  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed methods and potential strategies for improving scalability.
*  Expand the empirical evaluation of the SPGM, including a more thorough analysis of its performance and sensitivity to parameter settings.
*  Offer more concrete guidance on parameter selection (e.g., R, R0) beyond cross-validation.
*  Expand the discussion of related work, particularly in the context of GLM-based graphical models.
* Include simulations using more realistic data generating processes.
