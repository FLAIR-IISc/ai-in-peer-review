

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses an important gap in graphical modeling for count data, specifically the limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that can only model negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors propose three novel approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each designed to overcome this limitation. The paper builds on Yang et al.'s work [18,19] and addresses a clear deficiency in the literature.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by identifying the key constraint (base measure scaling) that prevents modeling both positive and negative dependencies in count data. The proofs of normalizability for the proposed models are generally well-constructed, and the experimental results demonstrate the practical utility of these models on both simulated and real-world count data. The case study on breast cancer microRNA networks is particularly compelling, showing clear advantages over existing methods.

**Weaknesses:** Despite these strengths, the paper contains several critical technical and presentation flaws that undermine its contribution:

1. **Theoretical Inconsistency:** The paper claims "the original Poisson graphical model (1) discussed in Section 2 can be derived from this construction with sufficient statistics B(X) = X, and base measure C(X) = log(X!)." However, the standard PGM formulation (1) uses θsXs - log(Xs!) + θstXsXt, while the general construction they reference would have θsB(Xs) - C(Xs) + θstB(Xs)B(Xt). This requires B(X) = X and C(X) = log(X!), which is correct, but the phrasing suggests a derivation rather than a direct specification.

2. **Misstatement in Theorem 3:** The paper states "the base measure cannot be sub-quadratic" when Theorem 3 actually proves "C(Z) = Ω(Z^2)" (meaning it must grow at least quadratically), not that it cannot be sub-quadratic. This is a critical misstatement that could confuse readers.

3. **Contradictory Simulation Design:** The paper claims TPGM can model both positive and negative correlations, yet states "Data of dimension n = 200 samples and p = 50 variables... is generated via the TPGM distribution using Gibbs sampling or via the sums of independent Poissons method of [15]." The sums of independent Poissons method [15] only generates positive correlations, contradicting the claim that TPGM can model both types of dependencies.

4. **Incomplete Explanation of Sublinear Statistic:** The SPGM's sufficient statistic B(x;R0,R) is defined with a quadratic piece for R0 < x ≤ R, but the transition point at R0-1 is unexplained. The paper states "Up to R0, B(x) increases linearly, however, after R0 its slope decreases linearly and becomes zero at R" without clarifying why the transition occurs at R0-1 rather than R0.

5. **Missing Appendix Reference:** The paper references "Figure 3 in the appendix" without providing the appendix, which is a formatting error that would prevent reviewers from verifying the claims.

6. **Ambiguous Terminology:** The claim that QPGM has a "Gaussian-esque thin tail" is misleading since QPGM is discrete while Gaussian is continuous, potentially confusing readers about the nature of the tail behavior.

7. **Lack of Reproducibility Details:** In the case study, the paper refers to "R = 11, the maximum count" but fails to specify what the actual maximum count was in the TCGA data, making replication difficult.

While the paper's core idea is valuable, these consistent technical and presentation flaws significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The misstatement in Theorem 3 alone is a serious error that affects the entire theoretical foundation. The contradictory simulation design further weakens the experimental validation. These are not minor issues but fundamental problems in the paper's execution.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. The paper identifies an important problem and proposes theoretically interesting solutions, but the technical inconsistencies and presentation flaws prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for rigor and clarity. The authors should address these issues before considering resubmission to a venue with more lenient standards.
