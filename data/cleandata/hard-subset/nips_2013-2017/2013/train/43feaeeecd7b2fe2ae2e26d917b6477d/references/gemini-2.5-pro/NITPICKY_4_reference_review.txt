### Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary**

This paper addresses a significant and long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): their inability to model positive dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors make several contributions. First, they provide a formal disproof of a previously proposed solution based on Winsorized Poisson conditionals (Kaiser and Cressie [20]), demonstrating that this approach cannot yield a consistent joint distribution. Second, they propose three novel graphical models for count data that permit both positive and negative dependencies. These are: (1) the Truncated PGM (TPGM), based on a valid re-normalization of the Poisson distribution on a finite domain; (2) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which modifies the base measure to be quadratic, ensuring normalizability in a manner analogous to Gaussian graphical models; and (3) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, allowing for the original Poisson base measure and heavier tails. The authors demonstrate how these models can be learned via neighborhood selection and provide empirical results on simulated and real-world microRNA expression data, showing superior performance over Gaussian-based methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important problem in probabilistic modeling. The inability of standard PGMs to capture positive correlations is a major practical drawback, and this work provides the first comprehensive and theoretically-grounded set of solutions.
2.  **Theoretical Contributions:** The contributions are theoretically sound and interesting. The disproof of the Winsorized PGM approach (Theorem 1) is a valuable negative result that clarifies confusion in the existing literature. The motivation for the QPGM via the analysis of the base measure's required scaling (Theorem 3) is elegant. The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are novel and derived from first principles.
3.  **Clarity of Motivation:** The paper is structured logically, with each proposed model motivated by the shortcomings of the previous one. This progression from a simple truncation (TPGM) to more fundamental modifications of the exponential family form (QPGM, SPGM) makes the narrative compelling and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and points of unclarity that, in aggregate, detract significantly from its quality.

1.  **Lack of Polish and Attention to Detail:**
    *   **Referencing:** The bibliography is inconsistent and appears hastily assembled. For instance, journal names are sometimes present ([3], [7]) and sometimes omitted ([1], [2], [4]). Some entries are for arXiv preprints ([9], [10], [19], [25]) when published versions may exist. Reference [19] appears to be the preprint version of [18], making it redundant. The character 'ā' in reference [13] is improperly rendered. Such inconsistencies suggest a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
    *   **Missing Figures/Appendices:** The text explicitly refers to "Figure 3 in the appendix" to depict the crucial sublinear sufficient statistic for the SPGM. However, no appendix is provided, making it impossible to visualize this core component of the model.
    *   **Unsupported Claims:** The paper claims that "all methods compared outperform the original Poisson graphical model estimator... (results not shown)". Important comparative results, even if for a flawed baseline, should not be omitted or relegated to an off-hand comment. This weakens the empirical evaluation.

2.  **Technical and Methodological Clarity:**
    *   **Arbitrary Conditions:** Theorem 1 is stated to hold for a truncation level R > 3. The origin or necessity of this specific constraint is not explained, leaving the reader to wonder about its significance.
    *   **Imprecise Terminology:** In Proposition 2, the condition "pairwise parameters are pointwise negative: Θ < 0" is ambiguous. It is unclear if this means element-wise negativity or is a typo for negative semi-definiteness. This is presented as an alternative to Θ being negative-definite, but the lack of precise definition is confusing.
    *   **Incomplete Analysis:** The variational upper bound for the SPGM's log-partition function (Proposition 3) is provided only for the case of non-negative canonical parameters (θ ≥ 0). The analysis is incomplete without addressing the case of θ < 0, which is certainly possible in practice. Furthermore, no analysis is provided on the tightness of the proposed variational bounds for QPGM or SPGM, which is critical for understanding the performance of the associated learning algorithms.
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The proposed TPGM and SPGM models introduce crucial hyperparameters (R for TPGM; R0 and R for SPGM). The paper offers no principled method for their selection. In the experiments, they are set to ad-hoc values ("R set to the maximum count value", "R0 = 5"). The performance of these models is likely sensitive to these choices, and the lack of a cross-validation strategy, sensitivity analysis, or theoretical guidance is a major practical omission.
    *   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper states that "we expect extensions of their sparsistency analysis to confirm" that the model structure can be learned. This is a hand-wavy claim that substitutes expectation for proof. While detailed proofs may be out of scope, the claim should be framed more carefully as future work rather than an expected property.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The simulation study uses a data generating process ("sums of independent Poissons") that can only produce positive correlations. This seems like a missed opportunity, as a key advantage of the proposed models is their ability to capture mixed (positive and negative) dependencies, which is not evaluated in this setting.
    *   The experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. Key parameters for the Gibbs sampler used for data generation are not specified. For the case study, the stability selection parameters (e.g., number of subsamples, error control rate) are not provided.

**Decision**

While the paper presents novel ideas of high potential significance, the execution in its current form does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS. The numerous issues—ranging from cosmetic formatting problems to more serious omissions in the theoretical analysis and experimental design—collectively undermine confidence in the work. The core contributions are strong, but they are buried under a layer of imprecision and incompleteness. I therefore recommend rejection. I would be open to reconsidering a substantially revised version that meticulously addresses the weaknesses outlined above.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, unsolved problem in graphical models for count data.
*   Proposes three novel, theoretically-motivated models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
*   Includes a valuable negative result that corrects a misconception in prior literature.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Numerous formatting and presentational errors suggest a lack of thoroughness.
*   Key methodological details are missing (e.g., Figure 3, hyperparameter selection).
*   Theoretical analysis is incomplete in places (e.g., bounds for SPGM, justification for constants).
*   Experimental design and reporting lack rigor and reproducibility.
*   Several claims are unsubstantiated or hand-waved (e.g., performance of PGM, sparsistency).
