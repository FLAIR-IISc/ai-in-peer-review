## Peer Review: On Poisson Graphical Models

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of modeling multivariate count data, a problem increasingly relevant with the rise of high-throughput data in fields like genomics and spatial statistics. Existing approaches suffer from limitations: log-linear models scale poorly, copula approaches are complex, and the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) restricts dependencies to be negative. The authors identify a flaw in a proposed extension using Winsorized Poisson distributions and then propose three novel approaches: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models aim to overcome the limitations of previous methods by allowing for both positive and negative dependencies. The paper also demonstrates the practical application of these models through neighborhood selection and validates them using both simulated data and a real-world breast cancer miRNA expression dataset.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the foundational work of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19] regarding PGMs. It critically analyzes extensions proposed by Kaiser and Cressie [20] and Griffith [21].  The work is also situated within the broader context of graphical model research (e.g., Gaussian Graphical Models [1, 2, 3, 4], Ising models [3, 5]) and recent developments in non-parametric extensions [7, 8, 9, 10].  The comparison with non-paranormal methods [7, 10] in the experiments places the contribution within the current landscape of high-dimensional graphical model estimation.

**Strengths:**

* **Identifies a Fundamental Limitation:** The paper correctly identifies and rigorously proves the flaw in the Winsorized Poisson approach for creating a valid PGM, a significant contribution.
* **Novel Model Development:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuine attempts to address a long-standing problem in statistical modeling. The derivation of these models is clearly presented and theoretically justified.
* **Theoretical Insight:** The paper provides valuable theoretical insight into the relationship between normalizability, base measures, and the ability to model positive dependencies in graphical models for count data.  The theorem regarding the quadratic scaling of the base measure is particularly insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed models, showing their superiority over existing methods in both simulated and real-world scenarios. The use of a relevant biological dataset strengthens the applicability of the results.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The derivations, especially for QPGM and SPGM, are mathematically dense and might be challenging for readers without a strong background in statistical modeling.  More intuition building around these models would be helpful.
* **Computational Considerations:**  While the paper mentions neighborhood selection, a more detailed discussion of the computational challenges associated with estimating the parameters of these models, particularly in high dimensions, would be beneficial.  The authors mention variational upper bounds, but a detailed description of the proximal gradient ascent method would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental section, while solid, could be expanded. Exploring different network structures and datasets would strengthen the claims. A more comprehensive comparison against existing count data models (e.g., those based on hierarchical models [16]) would be helpful.
* **Parameter Tuning:**  The choice of R and R0 in TPGM and SPGM is presented without a clear justification or sensitivity analysis. How do these parameters affect the results, and how should they be selected in practice?
* **SPGM Intuition:** The intuition behind the sub-linear sufficient statistic in the SPGM is not as clear as the quadratic approach. A visual illustration comparing the sufficient statistics of each model could aid understanding.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important and open problem in statistical modeling.
* The proposed models are novel and theoretically sound.
* The empirical results provide strong evidence of the practical utility of the proposed approaches.
* The analysis is rigorous and the writing is clear (despite some complexity).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The mathematical complexity of the models may limit their accessibility to a broader audience.
* The computational challenges are not fully addressed.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical modeling. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of existing approaches and propose novel solutions that overcome this limitation. While the mathematical complexity and limited discussion of computational aspects are weaknesses, the theoretical rigor, empirical validation, and clarity of writing outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and recommend **acceptance**.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
