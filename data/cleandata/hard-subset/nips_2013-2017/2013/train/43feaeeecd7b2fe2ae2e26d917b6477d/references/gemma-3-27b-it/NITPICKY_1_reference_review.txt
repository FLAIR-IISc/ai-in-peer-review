This paper attempts to address a significant problem: developing graphical models for multivariate count data that overcome the limitations of existing approaches like the Poisson Graphical Model (PGM), which suffers from only modeling negative dependencies. The authors explore truncation strategies and, ultimately, propose Quadratic and Sublinear PGMs to address this issue. While the motivation is strong and the proposed models are mathematically interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous justification for certain claims, ultimately leading to a recommendation for rejection.

The introduction is well-written and clearly outlines the problem. However, it’s overly verbose and could benefit from significant condensation. The background on PGMs is adequate, but the discussion of alternatives (log-linear models, copulas, etc.) lacks depth, appearing more as a list than a critical comparison.

The central claim regarding Kaiser and Cressie's truncation approach (Theorem 1) is crucial. While the theorem is stated, the proof is not included, and the authors simply state it exists. This omission is a major weakness. Similarly, the “new approach to truncation” leading to the Truncated PGM (TPGM) feels somewhat underdeveloped. The justification for choosing this specific truncation strategy over others isn't compelling. The statement that “the TPGM is normalizable while permitting both positive and negative dependencies” is true, but the explanation feels rushed.

The QPGM and SPGM proposals are more mathematically sophisticated. However, the derivation and justification for the quadratic base measure in the QPGM are not entirely convincing. The leap from needing a non-subquadratic base measure to *specifically* a quadratic one feels arbitrary. The connection to Gaussian graphical models via a variational upper bound is a good idea but feels tacked on rather than integrally linked to the model's design. The SPGM, with its sublinear sufficient statistic, is the most novel contribution, but its justification is surprisingly light. The claim that it offers heavier tails is not quantitatively demonstrated. The discussion of its advantages over the QPGM feels speculative.

Numerous minor issues detract from the paper's overall quality. The notation is inconsistent. For example, the neighborhood of node 's' is denoted as N(s) in one instance and then used without definition elsewhere. The writing often lacks precision. Phrases like “problems arise” are vague and unhelpful. The figures (mentioned in the experimental section) are not included in the submitted manuscript, hindering evaluation.  The experimental section is also weak.  It’s unclear *how* the regularization parameter was varied in the ROC curves, and the results presented (Figure 1 – not present in the submission) are simply stated without detailed analysis. The comparison to existing methods lacks sufficient detail.  The claim that the TPGM “finds more hub miRNAs” (Figure 2 – also missing) is not substantiated with quantitative metrics. The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., [17] vs. [18, 19]).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in statistical modeling.
*   Proposes novel graphical models (QPGM, SPGM) that attempt to overcome limitations of existing approaches.
*   Demonstrates potential applications in biological data analysis (miRNA networks).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of proofs for key theorems (Theorem 1).
*   Insufficient justification for key modeling choices (quadratic base measure, truncation strategy).
*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, writing clarity, and experimental presentation.
*   Missing figures severely hinder evaluation.
*   Experimental results are presented without sufficient detail and analysis.
*   The presentation feels incomplete and rushed in several sections.



**Overall:** While the paper presents interesting ideas, the numerous flaws and omissions, combined with the lack of rigorous justification, outweigh the potential contributions. The paper feels like a preliminary exploration of ideas rather than a polished, complete piece of work suitable for NIPS. A major revision addressing the points outlined above, particularly providing proofs, clarifying justifications, and including the missing figures, would be necessary for reconsideration.



**Recommendation:** Reject.
