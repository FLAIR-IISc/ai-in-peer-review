Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a long-standing gap in multivariate modeling of count data: undirected graphical models (MRFs) that (i) live on the nonnegative integers, (ii) have tractable node-conditionals, and (iii) can represent both positive and negative conditional dependencies. It starts from the classical Poisson graphical model (PGM) of Besag and its generalization via node-conditional exponential families (Yang et al.), recalling that normalizability forces all pairwise parameters to be non-positive, thus precluding positive dependencies.

The paper’s contributions are fourfold:
- It revisits truncation. It first shows that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals proposed in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot be stitched into a consistent joint MRF via the standard construction (Theorem 1).
- It then proposes an exponential-family-consistent truncation for node-conditionals, yielding a Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) whose joint has the same parametric form as PGM but a finite domain; hence normalizability without sign constraints (Theorem 2).
- It studies deeper modifications of the univariate base measure and sufficient statistics. With linear sufficient statistics, it proves that to permit positive dependencies while ensuring normalizability, the base measure must scale at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure and permits both signs of edges under a negative-definite quadratic form condition (Proposition 2).
- To avoid Gaussian-like thin tails induced by quadratic base measures, it proposes the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure log(Z!) but replaces the linear statistic by a sublinear, saturating statistic B(·; R0, R). This yields normalizability for any edge weights because pairwise interactions are bounded by saturation (Theorem 4). Variational upper bounds connect QPGM to Gaussian node-conditionals and SPGM to Poisson when θ ≥ 0.

Empirically, the authors perform neighborhood selection with ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihoods for TPGM and SPGM and compare against Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines on simulated hubs/scale-free graphs and on a TCGA miRNA count dataset. TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods on counts; the PGM baseline recovers only negative edges. The miRNA case study reports hubs known to be biologically relevant.

Quality
- Theory: The impossibility result for Winsorization (Theorem 1) addresses a long-standing misconception and is valuable. The existence/uniqueness for TPGM (Theorem 2) and the necessity of a quadratic-order base measure under linear stats (Theorem 3) are insightful; the latter clarifies why PGM-like models struggle with positive dependencies. Normalizability conditions for QPGM (negative-definite Θ) and for SPGM (bounded B) are sensible. The variational bounds are useful to motivate learning procedures.
- Empirics: Simulations are reasonable and illustrate the key points. The real-data case study is illustrative, though primarily qualitative. The learning procedure needs more algorithmic detail (convexity, gradients, step sizes, convergence criteria) and a clearer treatment of hyperparameter selection (R, R0, R in SPGM/TPGM) and sensitivity.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-structured. The paper explains the constructions and their intuition. Some notational slips (e.g., W, domain symbols) and reference formatting should be fixed. Explicit statements of assumptions for Theorems 1 and 3 (e.g., clique size k=2, tail conditions, precise interpretation of θ 6≤ 0) would improve rigor. More detail on optimization and complexity would aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. The impossibility result for Winsorization in the node-conditional exponential-family framework, the necessity result (base measure must be Ω(z^2) for linear stats), and the SPGM construction with saturating sufficient statistics are, to my knowledge, new. The QPGM connection to Gaussian models is well-motivated.

Significance
Significant. Many modern applications (RNA-seq, ecology, spatial counts) need multivariate count models with both positive and negative dependencies. The paper offers principled classes of MRFs addressing the PGM limitation and clarifies the structural reasons behind that limitation. The models and proofs will likely spur follow-up on estimation, inference, and extensions (e.g., higher-order cliques, dynamic settings).

Main strengths
- Clear identification and resolution of a core limitation of Poisson MRFs.
- Elegant theory: impossibility of Winsorized conditionals; necessity of quadratic base measure; normalizability conditions for QPGM/SPGM.
- Practical constructions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with reasonable learning approach, and empirical evidence on both simulations and a real dataset.
- Bridges to Gaussian methods via variational bounds, aiding practitioner uptake.

Main weaknesses and suggestions
- Hyperparameters R and R0: The paper sets R to the maximum observed count in TPGM and (R0, R) ad hoc in SPGM. This risks overfitting and can bias conditionals when many counts are near saturation. Please provide guidance or data-driven selection (e.g., cross-validation, stability curves), sensitivity analyses, and discussion of interpretability vs. bias when B saturates.
- Learning details: Provide explicit node-conditional objectives, gradients/Hessians, proof or discussion of convexity for the node-wise problems (under the proposed bounds where relevant), and optimization settings. For SPGM, computing D(θ, R0, R) involves infinite sums—how are these truncated and with what error control?
- Comparisons: Include a stronger set of count-model baselines (e.g., Poisson log-normal/latent Gaussian graphical models, multivariate Poisson constructions where feasible) and report precision/recall or AUPRC in addition to ROC. If these models are hard to scale, discuss tradeoffs.
- Real data: Provide more quantitative evaluation (e.g., stability selection parameters, edge stability, held-out predictive/perplexity comparisons), and more thorough biological validation beyond naming two hubs.
- QPGM expressivity: While Θ ≺ 0 allows mixed signs, discuss interpretability of negative-definite constraints and whether they unduly restrict networks in practice; provide examples of learned Θ patterns.
- Theorem 3: Make assumptions explicit and discuss tightness (e.g., can one do better than quadratic growth under additional structural constraints?).

Minor
- Fix reference formatting and missing details.
- Clarify notation (domain symbols W, X) and the constraint R > 3 in Theorem 1.
- Provide code or pseudo-code for Gibbs sampling (TPGM) and for node-wise estimation.

Decision
Accept (weak accept). The paper makes substantial theoretical and modeling contributions to an important problem, with clear advances over prior work and promising empirical results. Addressing the practical/algorithmic clarifications and strengthening empirical comparisons would further increase impact.

Pros
- Novel, principled models enabling positive and negative dependencies for counts.
- Strong theoretical results clarifying feasibility and normalizability.
- Practical neighborhood selection framework; promising results.

Cons
- Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity underexplored.
- Limited comparisons to alternative multivariate count models.
- Learning/optimization details need more depth.

Reviewer confidence: High.
