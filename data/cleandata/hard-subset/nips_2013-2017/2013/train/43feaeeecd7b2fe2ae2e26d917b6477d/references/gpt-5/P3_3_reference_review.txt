Summary
The paper tackles an important gap in undirected graphical models for count data. It first revisits Poisson graphical models (PGMs), noting the classical normalizability constraint that forces pairwise parameters to be non-positive, thereby permitting only competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. It then critically examines a line of work that proposed “Winsorizing” Poisson node-conditionals (truncation at R) and shows that such conditionals cannot be globally consistent with a joint MRF (Theorem 1). Next, the paper proposes: (i) a valid truncated exponential-family construction (TPGM) yielding a proper joint MRF over {0,…,R}^p with no sign restrictions; (ii) a deeper analysis showing that with linear sufficient statistics B(X)=X, any model permitting both positive and negative conditional dependencies and remaining normalizable must have at least quadratic base measure (Theorem 3); (iii) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which introduces quadratic self-potentials to ensure normalizability and allows mixed-sign dependencies under a global negative-definiteness condition; and (iv) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which retains the Poisson base measure log(X!) but replaces the linear sufficient statistic by a sublinear/saturating B(·), leading to normalizability for arbitrary pairwise parameters and heavier tails than QPGM. Empirically, the paper uses neighborhood selection with proximal methods (sometimes with variational bounds on node-conditional partition functions) to recover networks on synthetic data and RNA-seq miRNA counts, comparing against Gaussian and nonparanormal methods.

Quality
- Theory: The negative result on Winsorized conditionals (Thm 1) is important and, if correct, closes a popular but flawed path. The constructive TPGM result (Thm 2) follows from standard exponential-family MRF arguments and is sound. The necessity result (Thm 3) that a base measure must scale as Ω(Z^2) under linear sufficient statistics to admit positive edges and preserve normalizability is a strong and insightful contribution; a careful statement of assumptions (e.g., growth conditions, form of pairwise potentials, presence/absence of self-quadratic terms) is crucial. The QPGM normalizability condition via a uniform quadratic coercivity (X^TΘX ≤ −c||X||^2) is clear; however, this is a global constraint that is nontrivial to enforce in estimation. The SPGM normalizability (Thm 4) is plausible since the pairwise terms saturate while the base log factorial dominates tails; the variational upper bound for the node-conditional log-partition (Prop 3) when θ≥0 is helpful but incomplete for θ<0.
- Estimation: The learning procedures are sketched (penalized node-conditional likelihoods, variational upper bounds), but constraints required for normalizability (e.g., Θ ≺ 0 for QPGM) are not enforced in the proposed optimization. Absent such constraints, fitted parameters may define non-normalizable models. No theoretical guarantees (consistency/sparsistency) are provided beyond a statement that extensions of prior work should apply.
- Experiments: Simulations show ROC improvements for TPGM/SPGM over Gaussian/nonparanormal when data are count-valued; TPGM unsurprisingly performs best on TPGM-generated data. The comparison to sum-of-Poissons data (positive dependence only) is useful. The real-data case study is interesting and biologically plausible. However: (i) QPGM is not evaluated empirically; (ii) no held-out likelihood or predictive evaluation; (iii) sensitivity to hyperparameters R, R0 in SPGM/TPGM is not explored; (iv) comparisons omit Poisson log-normal or negative-binomial latent Gaussian copula models that are common baselines for multivariate counts.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear narrative from negative results to constructive models. Some notation could be tightened:
- Define W and Wp precisely and consistently.
- Clarify what entries are in Θ (self vs. cross-terms) and symmetry assumptions.
- Theorem statements should explicitly list assumptions (bounded/unbounded domain, presence of self-quadratic terms) and quantifiers (e.g., “θ ≰ 0”).
- The SPGM sufficient statistic B(·) is piecewise; include the exact expression and continuity properties in the main text (currently deferred to the appendix).
- Some references are incomplete; fix bibliographic details.

Originality
The work is original and timely. The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals, the necessity of quadratic-scale base measures for linear statistics (Thm 3), and the introduction of QPGM and SPGM as broadly applicable count MRFs that admit both signs of dependence are novel and valuable. The TPGM is a natural but still useful construction, and the overall framework extends the exponential-family MRF program for count data.

Significance
Addressing the long-standing limitation that Poisson MRFs only accommodate negative edges is significant for applications in genomics, neuroimaging, and spatial modeling with counts. If adopted, QPGM/SPGM provide flexible modeling families with clear statistical mechanics. That said, the practical impact depends on (i) principled and tractable estimation procedures that respect normalizability constraints (especially for QPGM), (ii) guidance for setting SPGM hyperparameters R0,R, and (iii) stronger empirical comparisons and diagnostics. With these strengthened, the work could become a reference in the area.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Provide proof sketches or intuition for Theorem 1 and Theorem 3 in the main text; these are central.
- QPGM learning: propose and evaluate a constrained estimator that enforces Θ ≺ 0 (e.g., projected gradient, spectral constraints, or parametrization via −LL^T + S to allow some positive off-diagonals while ensuring negative-definiteness).
- SPGM hyperparameters: discuss selection strategies (e.g., profiling, cross-validation, empirical Bayes), and analyze sensitivity; in the case study, R=11 as “maximum count” seems surprisingly small for RNA-seq—clarify preprocessing and the rationale.
- Report computational costs and convergence diagnostics for variational approximations; assess the tightness of the Gaussian bound for QPGM and Poisson bound for SPGM across parameter ranges (especially θ<0 for SPGM).
- Add baselines: Poisson log-normal graphical models, negative binomial alternatives, and copula-based count models would contextualize gains.
- Include predictive metrics (held-out log-likelihood, pseudo-likelihood) in addition to ROC.
- Discuss identifiability and interpretability: in QPGM, signs of θst versus signs of conditional associations; in SPGM, the effect of saturation on large-count interactions.

Pros
- Addresses an important deficiency of Poisson MRFs with clear theoretical insights.
- Novel and well-motivated model classes (QPGM, SPGM) that admit both positive and negative dependencies.
- Useful negative result debunking Winsorized node-conditionals.
- Solid, interpretable constructions grounded in exponential-family MRF theory.
- Promising empirical results on simulated and real data.

Cons
- Estimation procedures are underdeveloped; constraints for normalizability not enforced.
- Limited empirical evaluation (no QPGM results, missing strong count-model baselines, no predictive metrics).
- Sensitivity to and selection of SPGM/TPGM truncation parameters not addressed.
- Some theoretical results (e.g., Thm 3, Thm 4) would benefit from clearer assumptions and proof outlines.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers substantive theoretical and modeling advances for count graphical models with the potential for significant impact. I recommend acceptance if the authors can strengthen the empirical section (especially include QPGM and stronger baselines), clarify and better justify key theorems, and discuss/enforce normalizability constraints during estimation.

Reviewer confidence: High.
