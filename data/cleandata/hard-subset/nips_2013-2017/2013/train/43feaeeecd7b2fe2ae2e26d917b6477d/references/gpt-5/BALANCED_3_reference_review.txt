Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): when node-conditionals are Poisson with linear predictors, normalizability forces non-positive edge parameters, hence only competitive (negative) conditional dependencies are permitted (Besag; Yang et al.). The authors make three main contributions.

1) They analyze Winsorized (truncated-at-R) Poisson node-conditionals suggested in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) and prove that such conditionals cannot be the full conditionals of any joint MRF when R>3. This closes a tempting but incorrect path.

2) They propose a Truncated Poisson GM (TPGM) by using a truncated exponential-family Poisson over {0,…,R}, which restores a finite partition function and permits both positive and negative edges. They note its inherent tradeoffs as R grows.

3) They pursue fundamental modifications of the univariate exponential family: with linear sufficient statistics but a different base measure, they show normalizability with positive edges requires at least quadratic growth in the base measure. This motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with linear sufficient stats and quadratic base measure, allowing both positive and negative dependencies under a negative-definite quadratic form. To mitigate Gaussian-like thin tails, they further introduce a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure log(x!) but replaces the sufficient statistic by a piecewise sublinear function that saturates, yielding normalizability for any pairwise parameters and heavier tails.

For learning, they use neighborhood selection with ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihoods and proximal gradient methods, and report simulations (hub and scale-free graphs; data from TPGM and sum-of-Poissons generators) and a breast cancer miRNA RNA-seq case study. SPGM/TPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines and uncover plausible biological hubs missed by the original PGM.

Quality
- Theoretical: The incompatibility result for Winsorized conditionals (Theorem 1) and the necessity of quadratic-or-heavier base measure to allow positive edges with linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) are conceptually strong and relevant. The existence/normalizability results for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM are plausible and useful. However, several key proofs and conditions are only sketched; in particular, the exact assumptions underlying Theorem 3, and complete proofs for Theorem 4, would benefit from more detail and careful handling of edge cases.
- Methodology: Estimation procedures for QPGM and SPGM rely on variational upper bounds or infinite sums; the paper does not fully specify how gradients are computed, how truncation/approximation errors are controlled, nor how constraints (e.g., negative-definiteness in QPGM) are enforced during optimization. No sparsistency/consistency guarantees are provided; while extensions of Yang et al. are plausible, this is non-trivial and central to the promise of the framework.
- Empirics: Simulations demonstrate ROC gains over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines when data are from count models; this is encouraging. However, QPGM is not evaluated; SPGM/TPGM hyperparameter choices (R, R0) are ad hoc with no sensitivity analysis; and comparisons omit strong latent-variable competitors for multivariate counts (e.g., Poisson lognormal/negative-binomial graphical models or Gaussian copula count models). The real-data case study is illustrative but not conclusive.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, with appropriate context relative to Besag and the GLM-based conditional framework. Some equations have typographical issues (e.g., the piecewise definition of B(x;R0,R) and constants), and several important algorithmic choices are under-specified (computation of D(θ) for SPGM; constraint handling for QPGM). A clearer distinction between generative modeling goals and structure-learning use-cases would help interpret TPGM’s bounded domain.

Originality
- Strong on the theoretical side: the impossibility of Winsorized conditionals forming a valid MRF, and the growth-rate necessity result for the base measure, are original and insightful.
- The TPGM is a straightforward but useful finite-domain fix. The QPGM/SPGM designs—especially the sublinear, saturating sufficient statistic combined with Poisson base measure—are novel and interesting.

Significance
The work addresses a recognized gap: tractable graphical models for counts that admit both positive and negative conditional dependencies. The theoretical results advance understanding of what is and isn’t possible with Poisson-like exponential families. If supported by stronger learning theory and more thorough empirical validation, the proposed models could be impactful for genomics, text, and spatial counts. As it stands, the empirical and algorithmic evidence is promising but not yet sufficient for a NeurIPS standard.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear identification of a core limitation of PGMs and a rigorous negative result (Theorem 1).
- Conceptually significant necessity result on base-measure growth (Theorem 3).
- Two novel model classes (QPGM, SPGM) that relax negativity constraints while preserving tractability at the conditional level.
- Demonstrated advantages over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines on simulated counts and a relevant genomics dataset.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing empirical evaluation of QPGM; limited discussion of optimization, constraints, and computational complexity.
- SPGM/TPGM hyperparameters (R, R0) lack principled selection and sensitivity analysis.
- No sparsistency or statistical guarantees for structure recovery; not trivial to inherit from prior work.
- Incomplete comparison set: no Poisson lognormal, negative-binomial, or modern copula/count graphical baselines.
- Clarity issues in equations/notation and some proof sketches.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full proofs or a detailed appendix for Theorems 1, 3, and 4; clarify precise conditions.
- For QPGM, specify and test an estimation procedure that enforces negative-definiteness (e.g., projected proximal methods) and report runtime/accuracy; include QPGM in experiments.
- For SPGM, detail computation of node-conditional log-partition and gradients: truncation strategy, tail bounds, and error control; analyze sensitivity to R0 and R; propose data-driven selection (e.g., via cross-validation or marginal fit diagnostics).
- Expand baselines to include Poisson lognormal/negative-binomial graphical models and recent copula count methods; add scenarios with large counts and overdispersion.
- Provide at least partial sparsistency results or conditions under which the neighborhood selection with your node-conditionals recovers the true graph.
- Improve clarity of the B(x;R0,R) definition and fix typographical issues.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper offers strong and original theoretical insights and promising model constructions, but it falls short on algorithmic specification, learning guarantees, and breadth/depth of empirical validation expected at NeurIPS. With strengthened proofs, clearer optimization details, inclusion of QPGM experiments, principled hyperparameter handling, and broader comparisons, this work could become a solid contribution.
