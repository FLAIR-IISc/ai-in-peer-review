

# Review: On Poisson Graphical Models

This paper addresses a significant limitation in existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which can only model negative dependencies between variables due to normalizability constraints (edge weights must be non-positive). The authors propose three novel models—Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that allow modeling both positive and negative dependencies in multivariate count data. They provide rigorous theoretical guarantees for normalizability and demonstrate empirical advantages over existing approaches on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field of graphical models. The identification of the key limitation in existing PGMs (only negative dependencies) is well-motivated and well-documented. The theoretical contributions are strong, with careful proofs of normalizability conditions for all proposed models. The authors correctly identify and refute the Winsorized Poisson approach (Theorem 1), which is a valuable correction to prior work. The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM models represent a meaningful extension of existing work, with clear theoretical distinctions from prior approaches. The empirical validation is compelling, particularly the breast cancer microRNA case study where their models identify biologically relevant structures missed by existing methods (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b). The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and carefully situates its contributions relative to prior work.

**Weaknesses:**

The TPGM approach has a bounded domain (truncation at R), which limits its applicability to count data with potentially unbounded counts. While acknowledged, this limitation could be more thoroughly discussed in terms of specific scenarios where it would be problematic (e.g., data with counts exceeding R). The QPGM's Gaussian-esque thin tail may not be desirable for count data with heavy tails, and the paper could better discuss when this would be a concern. The computational aspects of the proposed models receive limited attention—particularly for SPGM, where the infinite sum in the log-partition function could be computationally challenging. While the paper mentions using the Poisson log-partition as a variational upper bound, it doesn't discuss the computational trade-offs or accuracy implications. The empirical validation, while strong, is limited to a single case study; additional datasets would further strengthen the significance claim.

**Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses a clear and important gap in the literature with theoretically sound solutions
- Demonstrates practical utility through compelling empirical validation
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for normalizability
- Well-written and carefully situates work relative to prior literature
- Makes a demonstrable advance in the state of the art for count data graphical models

*Con:*
- TPGM's bounded domain limits applicability to certain count data scenarios
- Computational aspects of SPGM receive limited discussion
- Empirical validation limited to a single case study

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The problem addressed is important, the theoretical solutions are sound, and the empirical validation demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing the first classes of joint graphical models for count data that permit rich dependence structures. While the bounded domain of TPGM and computational considerations for SPGM are limitations, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the significance of the contribution.

The paper aligns well with NeurIPS' focus on high-quality theoretical contributions with practical implications. The work is clearly situated within the field, builds upon prior work appropriately, and makes a meaningful advancement. The authors have carefully addressed the limitations of existing approaches and provided novel solutions with rigorous theoretical foundations.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Expand the discussion of the TPGM's bounded domain limitation, including concrete examples where this would be problematic and when the other models (QPGM, SPGM) would be preferable.

2. Add a brief discussion of computational considerations for SPGM, particularly how the variational upper bound affects computational efficiency and accuracy in practice.

3. Consider adding a sentence or two on how the models might handle zero-inflated count data, which is common in many applications (e.g., genomics).

4. While the breast cancer case study is compelling, including one additional dataset would further strengthen the empirical validation.

The paper is of high quality and makes a valuable contribution to the field. With these minor improvements, it would be an excellent addition to NeurIPS.
