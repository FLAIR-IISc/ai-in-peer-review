## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of developing graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors identify a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to represent negative dependencies – and thoroughly investigate several approaches to overcome this. They first demonstrate the failure of a natural truncation approach (Winsorization) proposed in previous work.  Then, they introduce the Truncated PGM (TPGM), followed by more fundamental modifications based on quadratic and sub-linear base measures, leading to the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper concludes with empirical validation on simulated data and a real-world microRNA expression dataset.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a solid foundation of work in graphical models, starting with Besag’s original formulation of PGMs and extending to the generalizations by Yang et al.  It correctly positions itself within the existing literature on handling count data, contrasting its approach with log-linear models, copula-based methods, and indirect Poisson summation techniques. The critique of Kaiser & Cressie’s Winsorization approach is particularly valuable, clarifying a potentially misleading direction in the field.  The authors also draw connections to related work in Gaussian graphical models and the challenges of applying those methods to count data.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches for modeling multivariate count data and frames the problem effectively.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, particularly the proof that Winsorization doesn’t lead to a consistent joint distribution (Theorem 1) and the conditions for normalizability (Theorem 3 & 4), are substantial contributions. 
* **Novel Model Development:** The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent genuinely novel approaches to constructing graphical models for count data. The exploration of different base measures provides a systematic investigation of possible solutions.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of both simulated experiments and a real-world case study strengthens the paper’s claims and demonstrates practical applicability. The comparison with existing methods (Gaussian GGM, Nonparanormal) and the link to biological insights from the miRNA network are particularly compelling.
* **Thorough Discussion:** The authors provide insightful discussions on the trade-offs of each model (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM), recognizing their limitations and potential areas for improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Models:** The QPGM and SPGM, while theoretically elegant, introduce increased complexity in terms of parameter estimation and interpretation. The reliance on variational upper bounds for inference in the SPGM is a potential limitation.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** While the empirical results are encouraging, the simulation studies could benefit from more extensive evaluation across a wider range of network structures and parameter settings. The real-world analysis, while interesting, is limited to a single dataset.
* **Computational Considerations:** The paper lacks detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with learning the parameters of these models, especially in high-dimensional settings. This is a critical aspect for potential users.
* **Connection to other count data models:** While the paper does a good job of relating to graphical models, it doesn't deeply explore how these models relate to other common count data models (e.g. negative binomial regression) or their potential for extension within a graphical modeling framework.




**Detailed Evaluation (Based on NIPS Criteria):**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs are rigorous, and the theoretical derivations are well-motivated. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and provide a careful evaluation of its strengths and weaknesses. The empirical results support the theoretical claims.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, although some sections (especially those involving the mathematical derivations) could benefit from further clarification. The organization is logical, and the notation is consistent. 
* **Originality:**  The work is highly original. The proposed models represent a significant departure from existing approaches to modeling multivariate count data and offer a novel perspective on the relationship between graphical models and Poisson distributions.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant.  Developing flexible and interpretable graphical models for count data has important implications for a wide range of applications, including genomics, epidemiology, and social science. The potential for uncovering both positive and negative dependencies in these datasets is a valuable contribution.




**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical models for count data.
* Presents novel and theoretically well-grounded models.
* Provides empirical evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approaches.
* Rigorous analysis and clear articulation of limitations.

**Con:**

* Increased model complexity compared to existing methods.
* Limited scope of empirical evaluation.
* Lack of detailed computational analysis.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a substantial contribution to the field of graphical modeling and offers a promising new direction for analyzing multivariate count data. Addressing the weaknesses related to computational considerations and expanding the empirical evaluation would further strengthen the paper.  I believe this work is of high quality and has the potential to have a significant impact on the field.
