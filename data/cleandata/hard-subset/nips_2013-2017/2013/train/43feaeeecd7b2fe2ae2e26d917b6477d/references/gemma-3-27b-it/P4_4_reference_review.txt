This paper introduces novel graphical models for multivariate count data, addressing limitations of existing approaches like the Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) which can only model negative dependencies. The authors first demonstrate the incorrectness of a proposed truncation approach [20], then present the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models aim to allow both positive and negative dependencies while maintaining normalizability. The paper includes theoretical analysis and empirical validation on simulated and real datasets.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical derivations, particularly the proof of Theorem 1 demonstrating the failure of the Winsorized Poisson approach, are rigorous and clearly presented. The introduction of the QPGM and SPGM builds logically upon the limitations of the TPGM and the underlying mathematical constraints. The discussion of normalizability conditions is thorough. The experimental section is well-designed, with comparisons to relevant baseline methods and evaluation on both simulated and real data. However, the discussion of the computational complexity of estimation and inference for the proposed models is lacking. While the authors mention using a `1-penalized likelihood approach, details are scarce, hindering assessment of scalability.  The claim of sparsistency is stated but left for future work, which is a weakness. The analysis is complete in the sense of presenting theoretical justification and empirical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the progression of ideas from the limitations of the PGM to the proposed solutions is logical. Mathematical notation is consistently used and clearly defined. The descriptions of the models are precise. However, some sections, particularly those detailing the more complex derivations, could benefit from further explanation and illustrative examples. The use of Figure 3 for the sublinear statistic is helpful. The discussion of the variational upper bound for the SPGM could be more detailed. The connection between the model parameters (e.g., Θ in the QPGM) and the resulting network structure is not always explicitly stated, making it harder to understand the practical implications.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. While extensions of the PGM have been explored, the introduction of quadratic and sublinear base measures to address the normalizability and dependency constraints is a significant departure from existing work. The demonstration of the incorrectness of the Winsorized Poisson approach [20] is also a valuable contribution. The authors appropriately cite relevant literature and position their work within the context of existing research. The application to breast cancer miRNA expression data is a practical demonstration of the models’ potential. The novelty is high, as the presented models offer a substantial advancement over existing methods.

**Significance:** The proposed models have the potential to be impactful in fields dealing with multivariate count data, such as genomics, epidemiology, and social sciences. The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies is crucial for capturing the complexity of real-world phenomena. The empirical results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed models compared to existing approaches in both simulated and real datasets. The application to miRNA expression networks highlights the potential for biological discovery. While the computational challenges mentioned above may limit the immediate applicability of the models to very high-dimensional datasets, the theoretical foundation and promising empirical results suggest that further research in this area could lead to significant advances. The work addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research, and the results have the potential to be widely used by researchers in related fields.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound models for multivariate count data.
*   Clear demonstration of the limitations of existing approaches.
*   Promising empirical results on both simulated and real datasets.
*   Potential for significant impact in various fields.
*   Well-written and logically organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Sparsistency analysis left for future work.
*   Some sections could benefit from further explanation and illustrative examples.
*   Limited discussion of the practical implications of model parameters.
