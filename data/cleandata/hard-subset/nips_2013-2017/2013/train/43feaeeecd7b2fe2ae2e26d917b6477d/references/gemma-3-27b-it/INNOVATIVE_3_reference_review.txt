This paper tackles a fundamental problem: extending graphical models to effectively represent multivariate count data, a situation increasingly common in modern datasets. The motivation is clear and well-articulated, and the authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches – namely, the non-positive dependency restriction in standard Poisson graphical models. The core contribution lies in proposing three novel graphical model variants – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – each attempting to overcome the limitations of the original model with increasing sophistication. The theoretical work demonstrating the normalizability conditions and the exploration of sufficient statistics are particularly strong. I found the exploration of base measure scaling requirements to be a key insight.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper introduces genuinely novel approaches to modeling multivariate count data with graphical models. The truncation strategy for the TPGM, and especially the exploration of quadratic and sublinear base measures, represent significant departures from existing work. This is a strong point in favor of acceptance.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis surrounding normalizability conditions and the connection to exponential families is well-developed and convincing. The theorems are clearly stated and their implications are carefully discussed.
*   **Problem Importance:** Modeling count data effectively is a crucial problem, and the limitations of current approaches are well-motivated.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of both simulation studies and a case study on breast cancer miRNA data strengthens the paper. Demonstrating performance gains over existing methods, even if preliminary, is valuable.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, and the mathematical derivations are relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Depth:** While experiments are present, they feel somewhat preliminary. The simulation studies could benefit from more thorough exploration of different network structures and parameter settings. The case study, while promising, could be expanded with more detailed biological validation.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The SPGM, in particular, introduces potentially significant computational challenges due to the complexities of its sufficient statistic and log-partition function. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't offer concrete solutions or approximations.
*   **Practicality of Truncation:** The TPGM’s reliance on truncation introduces the sensitivity to the choice of ‘R’. The paper hints at limitations of this approach as R increases, but further exploration of the impact of R on model performance is needed.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** While the paper cites relevant work, some potentially related literature on generalized linear models with non-canonical link functions could be explored to further contextualize the contributions. (e.g., quasi-Poisson models).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a significant problem in statistical modeling.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the properties of these novel models.
*   The initial empirical results are encouraging and suggest that these models have the potential to outperform existing methods.
*   The paper’s focus on model specification, rather than optimization, is appropriate given the novelty of the approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The empirical validation is somewhat limited and doesn't fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed models.
*   The computational challenges associated with the SPGM could limit its applicability in practice.
*   The TPGM introduces a potentially problematic truncation parameter.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a compelling new direction for modeling multivariate count data. While the empirical validation could be more extensive and the computational challenges of the SPGM need further consideration, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the work are significant. I'm willing to overlook the limitations in the current version, given the potential impact of this research. I lean towards acceptance. The authors have identified a critical gap in the literature and proposed a creative and well-justified set of solutions. Further development and validation of these models are warranted, and this paper provides a solid foundation for future work.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. I suggest the authors expand the empirical analysis, particularly focusing on the sensitivity of the TPGM to the truncation parameter and providing more details on the computational feasibility of the SPGM. Also, a more thorough discussion of potential connections to generalized linear models could strengthen the paper.
