

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical models for count data. The authors identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only model negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints, which severely limits their applicability. The paper builds upon the foundational work of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19], who established the theoretical framework for PGMs, but demonstrates that the proposed Winsorized Poisson approach [20, 21] is fundamentally flawed. The authors then propose three novel approaches to overcome this limitation: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). Each model addresses the normalizability constraint while allowing both positive and negative dependencies, representing a significant theoretical advancement in graphical models for count data.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with well-constructed proofs (Theorems 1-4, Propositions 1-3). The authors clearly demonstrate why existing approaches fail and rigorously establish the normalizability conditions for their proposed models.

2. **Clear Problem Identification**: The paper effectively identifies a critical limitation of existing PGMs (non-positive edge weights) and demonstrates its practical significance through the breast cancer microRNA case study, where the original PGM fails to capture biologically meaningful relationships.

3. **Novel Contributions**: The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent genuinely novel approaches to the problem, with distinct theoretical properties that address different aspects of the normalizability constraint. The SPGM, in particular, offers a promising solution with heavier tails than QPGM.

4. **Practical Validation**: The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed models over existing approaches for count data, with clear biological insights from the breast cancer microRNA network analysis. The ROC curves in Figure 1 show clear advantages for count data.

5. **Careful Comparison**: The authors appropriately compare their models with relevant baselines (Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal, etc.) and correctly note that the original PGM can only recover edges with negative weights.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Computational Discussion**: The paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed models, particularly for high-dimensional settings. While the authors mention neighborhood selection, they don't discuss how the computational burden compares to existing methods.

2. **SPGM Tail Behavior Analysis**: The paper states that SPGM has "heavier tails" than QPGM but doesn't provide a thorough theoretical or empirical analysis of this property. The connection to tail behavior could be strengthened with more detailed analysis.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it could better contextualize its contributions against recent advances in non-parametric graphical models for count data [7, 8, 9, 10]. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis**: The experimental section would benefit from a more detailed analysis of how model performance varies with key parameters (e.g., R in TPGM, R0 and R in SPGM), particularly in high-dimensional settings.

5. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions using "proximal gradient ascent" for learning but doesn't provide sufficient implementation details for reproducibility, which is important for a methodological contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The identification of the fundamental limitation in existing PGMs and the development of three novel approaches to overcome it represent a substantial advance. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is compelling, and the application to a real-world biological dataset demonstrates clear practical value.

The paper addresses an important problem with clear relevance to many domains where count data is prevalent (genomics, spatial statistics, etc.). The proposed models fill a critical gap in the literature and provide a theoretically sound foundation for modeling count data with rich dependence structures.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in terms of computational discussion and parameter sensitivity analysis, these do not detract from the core theoretical contribution. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for its significance.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a significant theoretical advance in graphical models for count data that addresses a fundamental limitation in existing work. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates both theoretical and practical value. The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) provide the first classes of joint graphical models for count data that can capture rich dependence structures with both positive and negative dependencies.

The paper's contribution is sufficiently novel and significant compared to previous work at NIPS, as evidenced by the clear distinction from existing approaches and the identification of a previously unaddressed limitation in PGMs. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed models.

This work will be valuable to researchers working with count data across multiple domains, and the theoretical insights will likely influence future work on graphical models for non-Gaussian data. The paper makes a clear and substantial contribution to the field of graphical models and meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Identifies and solves a fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models
- Provides rigorous theoretical proofs for three novel graphical model extensions
- Demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing methods through both simulation and real data
- Addresses an important problem with broad applicability in data analysis

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and parameter sensitivity
- Could provide more detailed comparison with recent non-parametric approaches
- Implementation details for learning algorithms could be more complete

Overall, the significant strengths of the paper outweigh these minor weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
