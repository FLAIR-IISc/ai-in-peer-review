Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): when the node-conditionals are Poisson with canonical linear predictors, normalizability forces non-positive pairwise parameters, thereby restricting conditional dependencies to be non-positive (Besag; Yang et al.). The authors (i) argue that a popular “Winsorized Poisson” truncation used in spatial statistics cannot yield a consistent joint MRF via the Besag/Yang construction, proving an impossibility result (Theorem 1); (ii) propose a different exponential-family truncation that leads to a proper truncated PGM (TPGM) over {0,…,R}, with no sign constraints on edges and a finite log-partition (Theorem 2); (iii) show that with linear sufficient statistics B(X)=X, allowing positive dependencies requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3); (iv) propose a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure and a negative-definite quadratic form ensuring normalizability (allowing positive and negative edges); and (v) propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that uses a piecewise sublinear sufficient statistic with the standard Poisson base measure, yielding normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). For learning, they suggest ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihoods; for QPGM they use a Gaussian variational upper bound on the nodewise log-partition. Experiments compare TPGM and SPGM to Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines on simulated data and a breast cancer miRNA count dataset.

Quality
- Theory: The direction is interesting, but several key claims are insufficiently substantiated in the submission. Theorems 1–4 are central; however, proofs and precise conditions are largely absent here. In particular, Theorem 1 asserts non-existence of a joint for Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals for any functional form of the canonical parameter E(·) when R>3; this is a strong negative result that requires careful assumptions (Markov property, positivity, Hammersley-Clifford conditions). Without a proof or conditions, it’s hard to verify. Theorem 3 (necessity of quadratic growth of the base measure to admit positive dependencies) is a major claim; again, the exact regularity conditions, notion of “θ not ≤ 0,” and whether the result is tight need clarification and proof. For QPGM, the normalizability condition (Θ negative-definite) is plausible, but the interpretation of signs of pairwise parameters vis-à-vis conditional dependence is not discussed. For SPGM, normalizability “for all θst” is attractive, but practical identifiability and tail behavior depend critically on R0 and R.
- Algorithms: The proposed learning procedures are only sketched. For QPGM the estimator relies on a variational upper bound on the nodewise log-partition; no consistency or even monotonicity guarantees are given. For SPGM, the nodewise log-partition involves an infinite sum; the paper does not specify truncation schemes, numerical stability, or complexity. Hyperparameters (R, R0) are set ad hoc; there is no model-selection protocol or sensitivity analysis.
- Empirics: The simulations largely favor the proposed models, but the study is limited. (i) Data generated from TPGM unsurprisingly favors TPGM; (ii) the “sum-of-Poissons” generator only yields positive correlations, which arguably advantages SPGM/TPGM over the original PGM but does not probe robustness to model mismatch; (iii) important baselines for counts are missing (e.g., Poisson log-normal graphical models, copula-based count models, negative binomial variants, transform+GGM baselines beyond nonparanormal). There is no assessment of goodness-of-fit, predictive performance, or calibration. The case study reports plausible hubs, but lacks quantitative validation (held-out likelihood, posterior predictive checks), and the choice of R and R0 is not justified.

Clarity
The paper is generally well structured and readable. However, many claims are stated without the assumptions/proofs needed for verification, and key implementation details are missing (optimization routines, convergence criteria, computation of partition sums, selection of R and R0, runtime/scalability). The discussion of how positive/negative θst translate to conditional associations in QPGM/SPGM would help. A clearer comparison of tail behaviors (Poisson vs QPGM vs SPGM) with plots would be useful.

Originality
The critique of Winsorization via an impossibility theorem appears new and valuable if correct. The TPGM (finite-support exponential-family truncation) is natural; the main novelty is to show that it admits a joint MRF via the Besag/Yang construction. The insight that quadratic (or stronger) base measures are needed to allow positive dependencies with linear sufficient statistics is interesting. The SPGM’s use of sublinear sufficient statistics is also original in this context. Overall, the modeling ideas are novel, but they depart from standard count modeling and require careful validation.

Significance
If fully validated, these constructions would enrich the toolkit for multivariate count GMs beyond the classical Poisson auto-model. However, practical significance is currently limited by: (i) lack of theoretical proofs and guarantees in the submission; (ii) unclear hyperparameter selection and sensitivity; (iii) limited and arguably biased empirical evaluation with missing strong baselines; and (iv) computational questions for SPGM/QPGM in high dimensions. As presented, it is premature to conclude state-of-the-art advances.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: modeling positive and negative dependencies in count GMs.
- Potentially impactful theoretical insights (impossibility of Winsorization; necessity of quadratic base measure).
- Three concrete model classes with intuitive trade-offs.
- Promising empirical signals on simulated and real data.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical claims lack proofs/conditions; some results are very strong and need verification.
- Estimation procedures are underspecified; no statistical guarantees.
- Empirical evaluation lacks rigorous baselines, goodness-of-fit, and sensitivity analyses.
- Hyperparameter choices (R, R0) are ad hoc; practical guidance is missing.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide complete proofs (or at least detailed sketches with assumptions) for Theorems 1–4.
- Clarify the precise conditions under which Theorem 3 holds; is Ω(Z^2) tight?
- For QPGM, discuss the relationship between Θ’s entries and conditional dependencies; provide diagnostics for ensuring Θ ≺ 0 in practice.
- Detail the optimization algorithms, complexity, and convergence; include runtime benchmarks.
- Propose principled selection/tuning of R and R0 (e.g., via cross-validation or marginal fit), and provide sensitivity analyses.
- Include stronger baselines: Poisson log-normal/correlated latent Gaussian models, copula-based count GMs, negative binomial variants, and simple transforms (Anscombe/log1p)+GGM.
- Report predictive metrics (held-out log-likelihood), posterior predictive checks, and calibration.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The paper raises an important issue and proposes interesting ideas, but the current submission lacks the rigor and empirical validation needed to endorse new model classes over established methods. With full proofs, stronger baselines, and more thorough experiments, it could be a strong contribution.

Confidence: medium.
