Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the auto-Poisson construction (Besag; Yang et al.) is only normalizable when pairwise parameters are non-positive, hence it cannot model positive conditional dependence. The authors (i) show that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals proposed in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot be extended to a consistent joint MRF via the Besag/Yang construction; (ii) propose a valid truncated Poisson graphical model (TPGM) by keeping Poisson-like node-conditionals over a bounded support, yielding a proper joint with no sign constraints on edges; (iii) prove that, with linear sufficient statistics, any joint that allows both positive and negative edge parameters must have a base measure that scales at least quadratically; (iv) introduce a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with linear sufficient statistics and a quadratic base measure, which permits mixed-sign dependencies under a negative-definite quadratic form; and (v) propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains the Poisson base measure but replaces the linear sufficient statistic with a sublinear function that saturates, ensuring normalizability for all pairwise parameters and heavier tails than QPGM. They illustrate structure learning via ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection and present simulations and a case study on miRNA sequencing data. The work builds directly on graphical models via univariate exponential families (Yang et al.) and clarifies/rectifies claims in the spatial auto-Poisson literature.

Quality
- Theory: The negative result on Winsorization (Theorem 1) is important and, if correct as stated, closes a tempting but invalid path. The TPGM construction (Theorem 2) is a clean application of the exponential family compatibility result and yields a tractable joint over finite supports. The necessity result (Theorem 3) that C(z)=Ω(z^2) when using linear sufficient statistics and allowing θst>0 is insightful and explains why the classic PGM fails; the QPGM normalizability condition (Proposition 2) is natural. The SPGM normalizability (Theorem 4) follows from bounded pairwise contributions past R and the Poisson base measure’s factorial tail; the variational upper bound (Proposition 3) is reasonable.
- Algorithms: The paper uses neighborhood selection with proximal gradients. For QPGM, the Gaussian variational bound on the node-conditional log-partition is used; for SPGM, the Poisson bound is suggested. However, the optimization objective is not fully specified when replacing true log-partitions by bounds: are these used only for analysis, as surrogates, or within a majorization-minimization scheme that guarantees descent and consistency? This needs clarification.
- Empirics: Simulations show improved ROC curves vs Gaussian-based methods on count data; TPGM excels when the data match its generative assumptions, and SPGM performs well more broadly. The case study is suggestive. That said, baselines are limited: methods commonly used for multivariate count data such as Poisson log-normal/latent Gaussian models, negative-binomial-based models, COM-Poisson variants, and copula-based count models are not compared. There is no assessment of goodness-of-fit or predictive likelihood.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The high-level motivation and the normalizability arguments are clearly conveyed. Some notational issues (e.g., use of W vs X for supports, occasionally undefined constants) and the lack of detail on optimization with variational bounds impede reproducibility. Choice and tuning of SPGM hyperparameters (R0,R) and their effect on inference are not discussed.

Originality
The combination of (a) a no-go theorem for Winsorization-based node-conditionals, (b) the necessity of a quadratic-scale base measure for mixed-sign dependencies with linear sufficient statistics, and (c) constructive alternatives (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) is original and conceptually valuable. The SPGM idea—saturating sufficient statistics to guarantee normalizability while keeping factorial base measure—is particularly neat.

Significance
Count-valued networks arise across genomics, neuroscience, and spatial statistics; having principled joint models that permit both positive and negative conditional dependence is important. The theoretical insights on when such models are possible should influence future model design beyond the specific proposals here. Practical impact will depend on stronger empirical validation, scalable inference, and guidance for hyperparameter selection.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of why classic PGMs fail and why Winsorization does not yield a valid joint.
- Three constructive model families with different trade-offs; SPGM offers heavy tails and unrestricted edges.
- Insightful normalizability result linking sufficient statistics and base measure growth.
- Nodewise learning framework fits within familiar exponential family machinery.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparisons to strong baselines for count data (latent Gaussian Poisson log-normal, negative binomial, COM-Poisson, copula-based count GMs).
- Learning theory (sparsistency, sample complexity) deferred; optimization with variational bounds not rigorously justified.
- Practical guidance for choosing R in TPGM and (R0,R) in SPGM is missing; no sensitivity analysis.
- QPGM’s “Gaussian-esque” tails and interpretability of quadratic base measure are under-discussed.
- Case study analysis is qualitative; no held-out likelihood, calibration, or stability across hyperparameters.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify how variational bounds are used in optimization: if using surrogate objectives, provide convergence guarantees (e.g., MM) and discuss bias; otherwise, describe how exact node-conditional normalizers/gradients are computed or approximated.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM), with data-driven selection strategies (e.g., cross-validation, stability-based criteria).
- Broaden baselines to include Poisson log-normal/latent Gaussian, negative-binomial, and COM-Poisson-based approaches; report precision/recall as well as held-out predictive likelihood or pseudo-likelihood.
- Add ablations showing the effect of mixed-sign edges and tail behavior on recovery under model misspecification.
- Improve clarity on notation and assumptions (support sets; conditions for uniqueness/positivity; computational complexity).
- Include (even brief) proof sketches in the appendix for Theorems 1 and 3 to aid verification.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a fundamental modeling gap with clean theory and constructive alternatives.
- Provides new insights (necessity of quadratic growth) likely to influence future work.
- Methods empirically outperform Gaussian-based competitors on counts.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is not yet comprehensive relative to the broader count-modeling literature.
- Inference details are underspecified; learning guarantees are missing.
- Practical guidance for hyperparameters (especially SPGM) and robustness is limited.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The conceptual and theoretical contributions are compelling and relevant to NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical comparisons and inference details would further solidify the paper.
