This paper addresses a critical and long-standing limitation in the field of graphical models for count data: the inability of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) to model positive conditional dependencies. The authors systematically tackle this problem by first analyzing the failure of previous attempts and then proposing three novel classes of models that permit rich dependence structures.

The paper begins by clearly outlining the problem: the standard PGM formulation requires non-positive edge weights for the distribution to be normalizable over an infinite domain. This severely restricts its applicability. The authors then critically examine a prior proposal by Kaiser and Cressie, which used a "Winsorized" Poisson for the node-conditional distributions. A key contribution of this paper is Theorem 1, which rigorously proves that this Winsorized approach can never lead to a consistent joint distribution, correcting a misconception in the literature.

Building on this, the authors propose their first novel model, the Truncated PGM (TPGM). This is based on a different, proper truncation of the Poisson distribution that remains in the exponential family, thereby allowing a valid joint distribution to be constructed. The paper honestly discusses the trade-offs of the TPGM: while it allows for both positive and negative dependencies, it requires a pre-specified upper bound `R` on the counts and can exhibit undesirable behavior as parameters or data values approach this bound.

The paper's most significant contributions are the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The authors provide a crucial theoretical insight (Theorem 3), showing that for a graphical model with linear sufficient statistics (like the standard PGM), the base measure must scale at least quadratically with the count values to ensure normalizability with positive edge weights. This motivates the QPGM, which uses a quadratic base measure, effectively creating a "Gaussian-like" model for count data. The QPGM is normalizable under a negative-definiteness condition on the parameter matrix, similar to Gaussian graphical models, thus allowing for rich dependency structures. The authors again fairly note its potential drawback: a thin, Gaussian-esque tail.

To address the tail behavior of the QPGM, the paper introduces its final and perhaps most elegant model, the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). Instead of modifying the base measure, the SPGM modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear for large count values. This clever construction bounds the interaction terms in the log-probability, ensuring normalizability for any real-valued edge parameters (Theorem 4) while retaining the original `log(X!)` base measure, which allows for heavier tails than the QPGM. The paper concludes with simulation studies and a real-world application to miRNA expression data, demonstrating that the proposed models outperform standard Gaussian-based methods for count data and reveal more biologically plausible network structures than the original restrictive PGM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly significant problem in statistical modeling. The inability to model positive correlations has been a major barrier to the widespread use of PGMs. The proposed QPGM and SPGM are novel, principled, and powerful solutions that represent a major advance for modeling high-dimensional count data. The disproof of the Winsorized PGM approach is also a valuable and original contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The theoretical development is rigorous, logical, and insightful. The progression from identifying the core problem, to showing why prior solutions fail, to deriving the conditions for a valid model (Theorem 3), and finally to proposing constructions that meet these conditions (QPGM, SPGM) is exceptionally clear and compelling. The claims are well-supported by theorems and propositions.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the narrative is easy to follow, and the mathematical formulations are precise. The authors are transparent about the strengths and weaknesses of each proposed model, which gives the reader great confidence in the work.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The application to breast cancer miRNA-seq data effectively demonstrates the real-world utility of the proposed models. By uncovering biologically relevant hubs and interactions missed by both Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM, the paper makes a strong case for the practical importance of these new methods.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Analysis of Learning Algorithm:** While the paper's focus is on model specification, the discussion on learning is brief. The authors state that sparsistency analysis is left for future work. While this is understandable due to space constraints, a brief theoretical justification or sketch of why existing proofs for neighborhood selection would extend to these models would strengthen the paper's claims about practical learnability.

2.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The TPGM and SPGM introduce new hyperparameters (`R` and `R0`). The experiments set these based on the maximum observed count value. A more detailed discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters and guidance on how to set them in practice (e.g., via cross-validation) would enhance the paper's practical utility.

3.  **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational complexity of fitting these models would be beneficial. In particular, evaluating the node-conditional log-partition function for the SPGM involves an infinite sum, which is bounded but may pose computational challenges. A comparison of the practical runtime of the proposed methods would be a useful addition.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of graphical models. It elegantly solves a well-known and important problem with a combination of sharp theoretical insights and novel model constructions. The work is of high quality, clarity, originality, and significance. It provides the community with a new, powerful, and much-needed set of tools for analyzing multivariate count data and is highly likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the scope of the paper than to any fundamental flaws in the work presented. I strongly recommend acceptance.
