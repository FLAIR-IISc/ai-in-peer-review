This paper addresses a significant and well-known limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) for modeling multivariate count data. Standard PGMs, as formulated by Besag [17] and generalized by Yang et al. [18, 19], are restricted to non-positive pairwise parameters to ensure the joint distribution is normalizable. This severely limits their applicability, as they can only capture negative conditional dependencies. The authors propose to resolve this issue by introducing three novel classes of graphical models for count data that permit both positive and negative dependencies.

The paper's contributions are threefold. First, it investigates truncation-based approaches. It proves that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson construction by Kaiser and Cressie [20] does not lead to a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1), a valuable negative result that corrects the literature. The authors then propose a novel and valid truncation scheme that preserves the exponential family form of the node-conditionals, leading to the Truncated PGM (TPGM). Second, recognizing the limitations of truncation, the paper explores more fundamental modifications to the univariate Poisson distribution. It provides a key theoretical insight (Theorem 3) that for a model with linear sufficient statistics (`B(X)=X`) to be normalizable with positive interactions, the base measure must scale at least quadratically (`C(X)=Ω(X^2)`). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure and allows for mixed dependencies under conditions similar to Gaussian Graphical Models. Third, to overcome the thin-tailed nature of the QPGM, the paper proposes the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). This model retains the original Poisson base measure (`log(X!)`) but employs a sub-linear sufficient statistic, which ensures normalizability for any real-valued pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). The paper concludes with experiments on simulated and real-world miRNA sequencing data, demonstrating the superior performance of the proposed TPGM and SPGM for network recovery compared to Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and practical problem in the modeling of multivariate count data. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive correlations is a major bottleneck, and this work provides multiple, well-motivated solutions. The introduction of the QPGM and SPGM, derived from first principles by modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics, is highly original. The SPGM, which allows for arbitrary real-valued interaction parameters while retaining a heavy-tailed distribution, is a particularly novel and significant contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and its claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs. Theorem 1, which invalidates a previous approach, is an important contribution in its own right. Theorem 3 provides a clear and insightful theoretical justification for why standard PGMs fail and what is required to fix them, guiding the design of the QPGM. The theoretical guarantees of existence, uniqueness, and normalizability for the TPGM (Theorem 2) and SPGM (Theorem 4) are thoroughly established.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation for the problem, systematically reviews prior work, and logically develops each of its proposed models. The authors are transparent about the trade-offs and limitations of each model (e.g., bounded domain for TPGM, thin tails for QPGM), which strengthens the manuscript. The progression from simple truncation to more fundamental modifications is logical and easy to follow.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section effectively demonstrates the utility of the proposed models. The comparison on simulated data with varying structures and dimensions shows a clear advantage for TPGM and SPGM over widely used Gaussian-based methods. The application to real-world miRNA expression data is compelling, showing that the new models can uncover biologically relevant network structures (e.g., hub miRNAs, functional clusters) missed by both the original PGM and standard Gaussian Graphical Models.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Theoretical Analysis of Learning:** The paper's primary focus is on model specification. While a plausible learning framework based on L1-penalized neighborhood selection is proposed and implemented, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of this procedure for the new models. The authors state that an extension of the sparsistency analysis from Yang et al. [18, 19] is expected to hold, but providing at least a sketch of this analysis would significantly strengthen the paper and confirm that the model structures are learnable in a high-dimensional setting.

2.  **Model and Hyperparameter Selection:** The proposed models introduce new hyperparameters (`R` for TPGM; `R0` and `R` for SPGM). In the experiments, these are set in a data-driven but somewhat ad-hoc manner (e.g., `R` set to the maximum observed count). A more detailed discussion on principled methods for selecting these parameters, such as cross-validation or information criteria, would enhance the practical applicability of the work.

3.  **Evaluation of QPGM:** The QPGM is theoretically well-motivated but is absent from the numerical experiments section. While the authors note its similarity to Gaussian models, an empirical evaluation on count data would have been valuable to understand its performance and limitations in practice, especially compared to the TPGM and SPGM. This omission leaves its practical utility unevaluated.

4.  **Details on SPGM Optimization:** The node-conditional log-partition function for the SPGM is intractable. The authors provide a variational upper bound in Proposition 3 but do not explicitly state whether this bound was used for optimization in the experiments. Clarifying the exact objective function optimized and discussing the potential impact of the bound's tightness would improve the reproducibility and completeness of the experimental methodology.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper successfully resolves a major, long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models, enabling them to model both positive and negative dependencies.
*   **Pro:** It introduces three novel and well-motivated classes of graphical models for count data, supported by strong theoretical analysis.
*   **Pro:** The negative result regarding the Winsorized Poisson model is an important correction to the existing literature.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results on both simulated and real-world high-dimensional data are compelling and demonstrate clear practical benefits.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The paper lacks a theoretical analysis (e.g., sparsistency guarantees) for the proposed structure learning algorithm.
*   **Con:** The discussion on hyperparameter selection for the new models is limited.
*   **Con:** One of the three proposed models (QPGM) is not included in the experimental evaluation.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its significant and novel contributions to an important modeling problem, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It is a well-executed piece of work that combines strong theory, clear exposition, and convincing empirical results.
