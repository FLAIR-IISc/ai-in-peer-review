

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper proposes three novel graphical model extensions for count data: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The authors identify a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGM) that restricts edge weights to non-positive values, preventing modeling of positive dependencies. They correctly identify flaws in the Winsorized Poisson approach [20] and propose alternative constructions. While the problem is well-motivated and the proposed models represent a potentially useful extension to the literature, the paper contains numerous technical and presentation flaws that undermine its scientific contribution.

**Major Technical Flaws**

1. The paper contains a fundamental error in its exponential family representation. In Equation (1), the authors write P(X) = exp{...}, but the standard exponential family form requires the normalization constant to be in the denominator (not as part of the exponent). This is a basic error that invalidates their theoretical foundation.

2. The authors mischaracterize the sum-of-independent-Poisson approach as "intractable to characterize with even a few variables" (Section 1), but this approach is well-known in the literature [14, 15] and the joint distribution is tractable as a sum of independent Poissons.

3. The Winsorized Poisson definition (2.1.1) is incorrect. The authors define Z = I(Z' < R)Z' + I(Z' ≥ R)R, which is not standard Winsorization (which should be Z = min(R, Z')). This mischaracterization undermines their critique of [20].

4. Proposition 2 contains a critical omission: the condition "XTΘX ≤ −cθ‖X‖22" uses Wp without definition. This undefined notation renders the proposition mathematically meaningless.

5. The paper reverses the standard exponential family form: "the original Poisson graphical model (1) discussed in Section 2 can be derived from this construction with sufficient statistics B(X) = X, and base measure C(X) = log(X!)." This is incorrect; for Poisson, the sufficient statistic is X, and the base measure is log(X!).

**Minor but Significant Issues**

1. Inconsistent notation: The paper uses both "Xs" and "X_s" to denote random variables, creating confusion. Mathematical spacing around symbols is also inconsistent (e.g., "log(Xs!)" vs "log(Xs!)").

2. Theorem 1 claims to show "no joint distribution" exists, but the proof is not provided (referenced to an appendix not included in the submission). This is a major omission for a theoretical paper.

3. Theorem 3 uses "Ω(Z²)" without defining this asymptotic notation, which is unacceptable for a theoretical contribution.

4. In Section 3.2, the piecewise definition of B(x;R0,R) contains a typo: "− 12(R−R0) x 2" should be "− (1/2)(R−R0)x²" or similar.

5. The citation [15] is incorrectly associated with "sums of independent Poissons" when the reference describes an EM algorithm for multivariate Poisson, not the sum approach.

**Weaknesses in Experiments**

The simulation studies claim "data is generated via the TPGM distribution using Gibbs sampling or via the sums of independent Poissons method of [15]" but [15] does not describe this method, creating confusion. The case study on microRNA networks cites [24] as using the same data set, but the authors don't clarify how their methods differ from those in [18, 24].

**Strengths**

The paper correctly identifies a limitation of existing Poisson graphical models. The TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent novel approaches to modeling count data with both positive and negative dependencies. The theoretical motivation for modifying the base measure is sound, and the experimental results suggest their methods outperform Gaussian-based approaches on count data.

**Conclusion**

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and presents potentially useful extensions to graphical models for count data, the numerous technical errors and presentation flaws significantly undermine its contribution. The fundamental errors in the exponential family representation and the mischaracterization of existing work are particularly concerning for a theoretical contribution. The paper would benefit from substantial revisions to address these issues before being considered for publication.

The authors have identified an interesting problem, but their solution is marred by basic technical errors that suggest insufficient attention to detail. Given the stringent standards for NIPS, these flaws warrant rejection despite the potentially valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation: Reject**
