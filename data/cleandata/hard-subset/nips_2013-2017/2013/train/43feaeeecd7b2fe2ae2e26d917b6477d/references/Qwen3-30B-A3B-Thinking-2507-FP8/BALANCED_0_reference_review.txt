

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical modeling for count data. The authors identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only capture negative conditional dependencies due to normalization constraints, limiting their practical applicability. They critique the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie [20], proving it cannot yield a consistent joint distribution. The authors then propose three novel approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each designed to enable modeling of both positive and negative dependencies in count data. These models build on the framework established by Yang et al. [18, 19] but modify either the domain, base measure, or sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous mathematical proofs (Theorems 1-4) demonstrating why existing approaches fail and how the proposed models overcome these limitations. The proof that Winsorized Poisson cannot yield a consistent joint distribution is particularly valuable.

2. **Clear Problem Identification**: The paper effectively articulates the limitation of current Poisson graphical models (only modeling negative dependencies) and explains why this is a significant constraint for real-world applications.

3. **Novel Contributions**: The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent meaningful advances in the field. The SPGM, in particular, offers a promising approach with heavier tails than Gaussian-like models while permitting both positive and negative dependencies.

4. **Practical Demonstration**: The case study on breast cancer microRNA networks demonstrates the practical relevance of the proposed models, showing they identify biologically meaningful network structures that Gaussian models miss.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Experimental Comparison**: The paper only compares to Gaussian graphical models and the original Poisson graphical model. Crucially, it doesn't compare to negative binomial graphical models (a natural alternative for count data with overdispersion), which would provide stronger evidence of the proposed models' superiority.

2. **Incomplete Practical Guidance**: The paper doesn't adequately address how to choose key parameters (R for TPGM, R0 and R for SPGM) in practice. For instance, it's unclear how to select R for real-world applications beyond the breast cancer case study.

3. **Theoretical Gap in Computational Complexity**: While the paper demonstrates theoretical normalizability, it doesn't discuss the computational complexity of learning these models compared to existing approaches, which is crucial for high-dimensional applications.

4. **Motivation for SPGM's Sufficient Statistics**: The choice of the specific sublinear sufficient statistics in SPGM (Equation 7) lacks strong motivation. The authors should better explain why this particular form was chosen over alternatives.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- The paper identifies and rigorously addresses a significant limitation in current graphical models for count data.
- The proposed models are theoretically sound and represent novel contributions to the field.
- The practical case study demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches.
- The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework for extending graphical models to count data.

**Against Acceptance:**
- The experimental section lacks comparison to relevant alternative count data models (e.g., negative binomial graphical models).
- The paper doesn't adequately address practical parameter selection for the proposed models.
- The computational complexity of learning these models isn't discussed, which is a critical consideration for NIPS.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Add a comparison to negative binomial graphical models in the experiments, as this is the most natural alternative for count data with overdispersion.

2. Include a section on practical parameter selection for the truncation level R (and R0 for SPGM), perhaps through a sensitivity analysis on the breast cancer data.

3. Discuss the computational complexity of learning the proposed models versus existing approaches, including details about how the neighborhood selection procedure would scale with dimension.

4. Provide stronger motivation for the specific form of the sublinear sufficient statistics in SPGM, perhaps by showing how it relates to other count data models or by deriving it from first principles.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how these models might be extended to handle overdispersion (a common characteristic of count data), which would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The identification of the normalization constraint limiting existing Poisson graphical models is important, and the proposed models represent meaningful advances. While the paper has strong theoretical foundations and a compelling case study, the limited experimental comparison and lack of practical guidance for parameter selection represent significant weaknesses. With the suggested improvements, particularly adding a comparison to negative binomial graphical models and providing more practical guidance, this paper could be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the paper is well-written and theoretically sound, but the limited experimental scope prevents it from fully demonstrating the significance of the proposed models.
