- Paper tackles multivariate count data modeling, highlights limitations of existing approaches, and proposes three new Poisson graphical models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
- Identifies and rigorously proves a flaw in the Winsorized Poisson extension to PGMs.
- TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are novel model formulations that allow both positive and negative dependencies.
- Provides theoretical insights on normalizability, base measures, and the role of quadratic scaling of the base measure.
- Demonstrates practical application via neighborhood selection and validates methods on simulations and a breast cancer miRNA dataset.
- Builds on Besag and Yang et al., critiques Kaiser & Cressie and Griffith, and situates work among GGM, Ising, and nonparametric graphical model literature.
- Strength: clear identification and proof of a fundamental limitation in prior work.
- Strength: genuine and theoretically justified development of three new models.
- Strength: meaningful theoretical contribution (e.g., theorem on quadratic base measure).
- Strength: empirical validation showing superiority over existing methods on simulated and real data.
- Strength: generally clear and well-organized writing.
- Weakness: mathematical derivations (especially QPGM and SPGM) are dense and may be hard for non-experts.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of computational challenges and parameter estimation in high dimensions; proximal gradient details lacking.
- Weakness: experimental scope limited—more network structures, datasets, and comparisons (e.g., hierarchical models) needed.
- Weakness: choice and sensitivity of tuning parameters R and R0 in TPGM/SPGM not justified.
- Weakness: SPGM lacks clear intuition; visual comparisons of sufficient statistics would help.
- For acceptance: addresses an important open problem with novel, theoretically sound models and strong empirical support.
- Against acceptance: limited accessibility due to mathematical complexity, incomplete computational treatment, and a narrower experimental evaluation.
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution; reviewer recommends acceptance.
