

# Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in graphical modeling for count data: existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only capture negative conditional dependencies, severely limiting their applicability. The authors identify this limitation as a fundamental flaw in the current literature and propose three novel approaches to overcome it: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper makes significant theoretical contributions by rigorously proving the limitations of previous approaches (including a refutation of the Winsorized Poisson method) and establishing the conditions for normalizability with both positive and negative edge weights. The authors also demonstrate the practical utility of their models through simulations and a breast cancer microRNA network case study.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide well-constructed proofs (Theorems 1-4) that clearly establish the limitations of previous approaches and the conditions for normalizability of their proposed models. The theoretical development is thorough, with careful attention to the relationship between base measures, sufficient statistics, and normalizability constraints. The experimental validation is compelling, showing that their models outperform Gaussian-based approaches on count data, and the breast cancer microRNA case study demonstrates real-world utility.

However, the paper could strengthen its quality by providing more detailed learning algorithms. While the authors mention using neighborhood selection with L1 regularization, they don't elaborate on the optimization procedures or computational complexity. The theoretical guarantees for structure learning (sparsistency) are mentioned but not developed, which is a significant omission for a NIPS paper.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to empirical validation. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem within existing graphical models literature, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The figures referenced in the text (e.g., the sublinear sufficient statistics) would likely be helpful for readers.

The main clarity issue is the explanation of the SPGM's sublinear sufficient statistics. While the mathematical definition is provided, a more intuitive explanation of why this form works would help readers better understand the approach. Similarly, the discussion of QPGM's Gaussian-esque thin tail could be expanded to discuss practical implications for real-world applications.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field. While the general idea of extending exponential families to graphical models isn't new, the specific approaches to address the normalization issue for count data are novel. The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous approaches (notably refuting the Winsorized Poisson approach) and provide multiple complementary solutions with different trade-offs.

The most original contribution is the SPGM, which allows for both heavier tails than QPGM and all real-valued edge parameters. This represents a substantial advance over previous work, as it solves the core problem of modeling both positive and negative dependencies without restrictive assumptions. The paper properly cites related work and clearly articulates how their approach differs from previous contributions.

## Significance
This paper addresses a highly significant problem with broad applicability across domains where count data is prevalent (genomics, spatial statistics, climate studies, etc.). The models proposed have the potential to substantially improve the analysis of count data in these fields. The breast cancer microRNA case study is particularly compelling, demonstrating that their models identify biologically meaningful network structures that are missed by existing approaches (including the original PGM, which only recovers negative dependencies).

The experimental results show that their models consistently outperform Gaussian-based approaches on count data, with the SPGM showing particular promise. The fact that their methods find miRNAs known to be key players in breast cancer (mir-375 and mir-10b) that are missed by Gaussian graphical models provides strong evidence of practical significance.

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed models are novel, and the empirical validation is compelling. The paper clearly identifies a significant gap in the literature and provides multiple practical solutions to address it.

The main weaknesses are the limited discussion of computational aspects and the lack of detailed theoretical guarantees for structure learning. However, these are minor compared to the paper's substantial strengths.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications, addressing a problem that has been recognized as important but previously unsolved. The quality of the theoretical analysis is excellent, and the empirical validation is convincing. This work would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

## Specific Comments
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational aspects, including the complexity of learning the models and scalability to large graphs.

2. The theoretical guarantees for structure learning (sparsistency) should be addressed more thoroughly, as this is crucial for practical applications.

3. The explanation of SPGM's sublinear sufficient statistics could be made more intuitive to help readers understand why this approach works.

4. The comparison with other count data models could be expanded to include more recent approaches beyond the original PGM.

5. The paper mentions that the SPGM's log-partition function usually doesn't have a closed-form, but doesn't discuss practical approximation methods. A brief mention would be helpful for implementation.

This is a strong paper that advances the state of the art in graphical models for count data, with both theoretical novelty and practical significance. I believe it would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
