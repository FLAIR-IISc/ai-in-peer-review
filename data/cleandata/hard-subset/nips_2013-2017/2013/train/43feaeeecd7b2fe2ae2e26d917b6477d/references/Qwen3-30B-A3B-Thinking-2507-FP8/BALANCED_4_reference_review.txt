

# Review: On Poisson Graphical Models

This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical models for count data. The authors identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) only allow negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints, limiting their practical applicability. They systematically analyze previous approaches (Winsorized Poisson, truncated Poisson) and propose three novel models: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each designed to overcome the limitations of previous approaches while maintaining theoretical soundness.

The paper builds on foundational work by Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19] who introduced the original PGM framework. It properly situates its contributions relative to related work in graphical models for count data, including the recent work by [20, 21] on Winsorized Poisson distributions. The authors make a clear theoretical contribution by proving that the Winsorized Poisson approach cannot yield a consistent joint distribution (Theorem 1), which corrects a previously accepted approach.

**Strengths:**
1. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides rigorous proofs of normalizability conditions for the proposed models (Theorems 1-4), which is the paper's most significant contribution. The authors clearly demonstrate that both positive and negative dependencies can be modeled with appropriate modifications to the Poisson base measure.
2. **Clear distinction from previous work:** The paper effectively differentiates its contributions from existing approaches, particularly by correcting the misconception about Winsorized Poisson graphical models.
3. **Practical validation:** The simulation studies and breast cancer microRNA case study demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed models, showing superior performance compared to Gaussian-based methods on count data.
4. **Well-organized structure:** The paper flows logically from problem statement to theoretical development to experimental validation.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited discussion of computational complexity:** While the paper demonstrates the utility of the models on moderate-sized datasets, it doesn't adequately address the computational challenges of scaling these methods to very large datasets. The log-partition functions for QPGM and SPGM involve summations over infinite terms, which could be computationally intensive for large-scale applications.
2. **Insufficient comparison of model trade-offs:** The paper introduces three distinct models but doesn't thoroughly compare their relative strengths and weaknesses (e.g., TPGM's bounded domain vs. SPGM's heavier tails vs. QPGM's Gaussian-like behavior). A more detailed discussion of when to use each model would strengthen the paper.
3. **Limited exploration of parameter estimation challenges:** While the paper mentions using neighborhood selection, it doesn't address potential challenges in estimating parameters for the new models, particularly for the SPGM where the log-partition function lacks a closed form.
4. **Biological interpretation in case study:** The breast cancer case study identifies biologically relevant miRNAs but could provide more specific biological insights about why these miRNAs form significant connections.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. The proofs are well-constructed and address the core problem identified in the introduction. The experimental validation is appropriate for the proposed models, though the computational aspects could be more thoroughly addressed.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical derivations are presented systematically, though some of the notation could be slightly clarified (e.g., the definition of Ψ(ηs)). The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution by identifying and solving a fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models. The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent novel approaches that have not been previously explored in the literature. The authors have effectively distinguished their work from previous contributions.

**Significance:** The problem addressed (modeling count data with rich dependency structures) is important in many application domains, particularly in genomics, spatial statistics, and network analysis. The proposed models fill a critical gap in the literature, and the experimental results demonstrate their practical utility. The paper's significance is strengthened by its application to a real-world breast cancer microRNA dataset.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contributions are substantial and address a meaningful limitation in the field of graphical models for count data. The paper is well-written and provides both theoretical and empirical validation of the proposed models.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Include a more detailed discussion of computational considerations for each model, particularly for large-scale applications.
2. Provide a comparative analysis of the three proposed models, highlighting when each would be most appropriate.
3. Expand the biological interpretation in the case study to better connect the network structure to known biological mechanisms.
4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how these models might be extended to handle zero-inflated count data, which is common in many real-world applications.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of graphical models for count data that advances the state of the art in a meaningful way. The paper addresses a clear limitation in existing methods and provides a theoretically sound and practically useful solution.
