

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature: the lack of a graphical model framework for multivariate count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies. The authors correctly identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) only allow negative edge weights due to normalizability constraints, severely limiting their practical utility. They make three key contributions: (1) demonstrating the invalidity of the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie [20], (2) proposing a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that allows both positive and negative dependencies with a bounded domain, and (3) introducing two novel models—Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that overcome the limitations of TPGM while maintaining normalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's strongest aspect is its mathematical precision. Theorems 1-4 provide rigorous proofs that the Winsorized approach is invalid and that the authors' proposed models satisfy normalizability constraints while allowing both positive and negative dependencies. The connection to exponential family theory and the careful analysis of base measures and sufficient statistics demonstrate deep theoretical understanding.

2. **Clear Problem Identification:** The authors effectively contextualize their work within the broader literature on graphical models (Gaussian, Ising, multinomial), clearly articulating why existing approaches are inadequate for count data. The critique of previous methods (e.g., Winsorized Poisson, sum-of-Poissons) is well-founded and properly cited.

3. **Practical Relevance:** The case study on breast cancer microRNA networks provides compelling evidence of the method's utility. The finding that TPGM/SPGM identify biologically significant hubs (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b) that Gaussian models miss demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Domain Limitation of TPGM:** While TPGM is theoretically sound, its requirement of a fixed truncation level R is a significant practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address how to choose R in practice, which is crucial for real-world applications where counts can be arbitrarily large. The paper suggests R→∞ recovers the original PGM, but doesn't explore how to select R for finite datasets.

2. **QPGM's Thin Tail:** The authors correctly note that QPGM has a Gaussian-esque thin tail, which may be undesirable for count data that often exhibits heavier tails. While they provide a variational upper bound to Gaussian, they don't discuss how this affects practical performance in applications with heavy-tailed count distributions.

3. **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The simulation studies are promising but somewhat superficial. The paper compares only with Gaussian-based methods, not with other count-specific approaches (e.g., negative binomial graphical models). The case study lacks biological validation of the identified hubs beyond citing two known breast cancer miRNAs.

4. **Computational Considerations:** The paper mentions "neighborhood selection" but doesn't provide details on computational complexity or scalability for high-dimensional data. For large p (e.g., p=1000), the computational burden of the proposed models isn't addressed.

5. **Zero-Inflation:** Count data often exhibits zero-inflation (many zeros), which isn't addressed in any of the proposed models. This is a significant limitation for applications like genomics where many genes have zero expression.

**Significance and Originality:**

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by resolving a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models. The authors successfully demonstrate how to construct valid graphical models for count data that permit both positive and negative dependencies—a problem that has remained unsolved since Besag's initial work. The three proposed approaches (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent a novel and comprehensive solution to this long-standing problem.

The paper is highly original, with no clear prior work that achieves the same theoretical guarantees. The connection between base measure scaling and normalizability (Theorem 3) is particularly insightful and advances our understanding of graphical models beyond the Gaussian case.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The authors have identified a real gap in the literature and provided rigorous solutions that advance both theory and practice. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a compelling case for the importance of their work.

The main limitations (domain restriction of TPGM, QPGM's tail behavior, and limited experimental evaluation) are important but do not undermine the core theoretical contribution. These could be addressed in future work or in a longer version of the paper.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is technically sound, makes a clear advance over prior work, and has clear practical implications for fields dealing with count data. The proposed models represent a fundamental step forward in graphical modeling for count-valued observations.

**Minor Suggestions:**
1. Discuss how to select R in TPGM for real data (e.g., using domain knowledge or data-driven methods).
2. Explore the relationship between SPGM and zero-inflated count models.
3. Provide more detailed computational complexity analysis for the proposed models.

Overall, this is an excellent contribution to the field of graphical models that should be of interest to the NIPS community.
