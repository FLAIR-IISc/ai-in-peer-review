Summary
The paper addresses an important limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the classical auto-Poisson/EF construction only normalizes when pairwise parameters are non-positive, precluding positive conditional dependencies. The authors make three contributions. (i) They argue Winsorizing/truncating the univariate Poisson at R (as in Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) and using those node-conditionals cannot yield a consistent joint distribution in the Besag/Yang EF framework (Theorem 1). (ii) They propose an exponential-family truncation that redistributes tail mass over {0,…,R}, yielding a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that is normalizable for any edge signs (Theorem 2), albeit with drawbacks as R increases. (iii) They propose two non-truncated EF variants: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that keeps sufficient statistic B(x)=x but uses a quadratic base measure, and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure log x! but uses a piecewise sublinear sufficient statistic B(x;R0,R) that saturates after R. They prove a necessary condition that, with linear sufficient statistic, the base measure must be at least quadratic to allow positive dependencies under normalizability (Theorem 3), derive a normalizability condition for QPGM (Proposition 2), and show SPGM is normalizable for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). For learning, they use ℓ1-penalized node-conditional likelihood (neighborhood selection), with variational bounds for intractable node-wise log-partitions. Simulations compare TPGM/SPGM to Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines; a miRNA sequencing case study is presented.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-known and practically important deficiency of PGMs for counts (negative dependence restriction).
- The identification and correction of the “Winsorized Poisson” misconception is useful; Theorem 1, if correct, clarifies the limits of conditional EF constructions.
- The EF-based truncation (TPGM) is clean, immediately yields a valid joint with finite partition function, and allows positive edges.
- The necessity result C(z)=Ω(z^2) for linear sufficient statistics is insightful and guides model design.
- Two concrete alternatives (QPGM and SPGM) are proposed with normalizability arguments and intuitive interpretations (thin-tailed quadratic vs heavier-tailed sublinear).
- Empirical comparisons show TPGM/SPGM can outperform Gaussian/nonparanormal on synthetic count data; a real RNA-seq network yields plausible hubs.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/rigor
- Several key theoretical claims are only stated, with no proof sketches or precise conditions (e.g., Theorem 1’s R>3 assumption; the scope of “arbitrary” canonical functions; formal assumptions behind Theorem 3). The paper needs clear statements of assumptions and proof outlines (appendix is implied but not provided here).
- For SPGM, node-conditional log-partition D(θ,R0,R) is intractable; the proposed variational upper bound DPois(θ)=exp(θ) applies only for θ≥0. How are gradients and likelihoods computed when θ<0 during optimization? What is the algorithmic guarantee when optimizing a bound rather than the true node-conditional likelihood?
- For QPGM, normalizability via Θ≺0 is standard, but then the model loses Poisson-like dispersion and becomes Gaussian-esque, as the authors note. There is no consistency or sparsistency analysis for any of the proposed estimators; reliance on “expected extensions” of [18,19] is not sufficient for NeurIPS.
- TPGM and SPGM introduce data-dependent truncation/saturation hyperparameters (R, R0). The choices (R = max count; R0 fixed) are ad hoc. There is no sensitivity analysis, no guidance for selection (e.g., via likelihood, CV, information criteria), and no discussion of bias as sample size changes.
- Parameter identifiability and interpretability: with saturated B(x) in SPGM, interactions between large counts become flat; what does an edge mean when many observations lie in the plateau? This affects network interpretability.

Empirical validation
- Comparisons omit strong count-specific baselines: Poisson log-normal (latent Gaussian) graphical models, negative-binomial-based models, copula models for counts, or recent pseudo-likelihood approaches tailored to counts. Comparing only to Gaussian/nonparanormal is too weak to substantiate significance.
- QPGM is not empirically evaluated, yet it is one of the core proposed models.
- Simulations are modest in scale (p up to 100 in high-d setting). No runtime/scalability results; no study of calibration of edge signs/weights; no robustness to misspecification (e.g., overdispersion).
- Using R equal to the sample max is problematic: it is sample-size dependent and effectively bakes in the dataset into the model support. This should be contrasted with a held-out setup or principled selection.
- Real-data case study lacks quantitative evaluation (no held-out likelihood, no stability across resamples beyond STARS sparsity selection; no biological validation beyond anecdotal hubs). Claims that PGM “misses much of the structure” are unsurprising due to its negativity constraint and do not validate the new approaches.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but several derivations are sketched. The construction linking node-conditionals to joint for TPGM (Theorem 2) should explicitly state conditions and factorization.
- Notation: using X for both domain and random vector can be confusing; define W for nonnegative integers consistently.
- Provide algorithmic details: how are gradients of node-wise log-partitions computed/approximated; step sizes for proximal gradient; convergence criteria.

Originality and relation to prior work
- The EF truncation (TPGM) is a natural adaptation within the Besag/Yang framework. The novelty lies mainly in pointing out the flaw in the Winsorized approach and in proposing concrete alternative EF choices (quadratic base; sublinear sufficient statistics) with normalizability analysis.
- The “first time” claim of permitting rich positive/negative dependencies for count-valued joint GMs seems overstated given existing latent-variable approaches (e.g., Poisson log-normal), copula-based models, and hierarchical CAR-type models that allow positive dependence; the distinction is the strict pairwise MRF with count support, which should be clarified.

Significance
- The problem is important; however, the current empirical and theoretical validation is not sufficient to recommend adoption. Without comparisons to stronger baselines, guidance on hyperparameter selection, and consistency guarantees, it is hard to assess practical impact.

Questions for the authors
- Provide proof outlines (or an appendix) for Theorems 1 and 3. For Theorem 1, how tight is the R>3 condition? Does impossibility hold for smaller R?
- SPGM inference: how do you compute/approximate D(θ,R0,R) and its gradient when θ takes negative values during optimization? Are you optimizing a bound or the exact node-conditional likelihood?
- Hyperparameters R and R0: how should they be selected in practice? Please include sensitivity analyses and principled selection strategies.
- Why is QPGM omitted from experiments? Can you include results and discuss its practical behavior vs SPGM/TPGM?
- Add comparisons to Poisson log-normal graphical models and other count-specific baselines; report runtime and scalability.
- For the real data, can you provide stability (edge selection frequency), held-out likelihood, and overlap with prior pathway/miRNA interaction knowledge?

Pros
- Addresses a real deficiency of PGMs for counts.
- Provides clear EF-based constructions with normalizability arguments.
- Useful correction of prior Winsorized approach.
- Promising initial empirical gains on synthetic count data.

Cons
- Incomplete theory (proofs/assumptions) and no learning guarantees.
- Inference for SPGM/QPGM underexplained; reliance on loose bounds.
- Ad hoc hyperparameter choices; no sensitivity or selection guidance.
- Weak empirical validation and missing strong baselines; QPGM not evaluated.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The ideas are interesting, but given the methodological novelty, stronger theoretical support and more comprehensive empirical validation against appropriate baselines are necessary for NeurIPS.
