Summary
The paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): normalizability forces nonpositive pairwise parameters, preventing modeling of positive conditional dependencies. The authors (i) refute prior “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals (Kaiser–Cressie/Griffith) by proving no consistent joint MRF can exist under that construction; (ii) introduce a truncated-exponential-family version (TPGM) that yields a valid joint MRF over {0,…,R} with no sign restriction on edges; (iii) prove that, with linear sufficient statistics, any normalizable EF-MRF that allows both positive and negative dependencies must have a base measure growing at least quadratically; this motivates (iv) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with linear sufficient statistics and quadratic base measure, and (v) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains Poisson’s base measure but uses a piecewise sublinear sufficient statistic that saturates, permitting positive/negative edges while avoiding Gaussian-thin tails. They propose neighborhood selection via conditional likelihood and show simulated ROC curves and a breast cancer microRNA case study; both TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based competitors and recover plausible biological hubs.

Quality
- Theory: The paper’s theoretical contributions are the main strength. Theorem 1 (impossibility of a joint for Winsorized conditionals) addresses a known, widely cited idea and clarifies a misconception; the result is important if rigorous. The construction of TPGM as an exponential-family truncation that is compatible with the EF-MRF recipe is sound and addresses positive edges within bounded counts. Theorem 3 (necessity of quadratic growth of the base measure for linear sufficient statistics) is insightful and explains the negativity restriction at its root. The QPGM normalizability condition via a negative-definite quadratic form is standard and appropriate. The SPGM normalizability result, together with a Poisson variational upper bound for nodewise inference, is well motivated.
- Empirics: Simulation studies show consistent gains over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines when data are counts; the real-data analysis is compelling and consistent with domain knowledge. However, the empirical section under-supports some claims:
  - No quantitative comparison to the original PGM (only “results not shown”), which weakens the story about the PGM’s practical deficiency.
  - Missing comparisons to other multivariate/count models often used in practice (e.g., Poisson log-normal, negative binomial or COM-Poisson regressions, copula-based count models) limits scope of evidence.
  - For SPGM, optimization uses a variational bound for the conditional log-partition; it would help to quantify approximation error or show ablations versus exact (truncated) computations where feasible.
  - No run-time or scalability analysis; neighborhood selection for SPGM may be computationally heavier due to nonstandard sufficient statistics.
- Completeness: Proof-level details are sometimes deferred; given the centrality of Theorem 1 and Theorem 3, clear statements of assumptions (e.g., exact regularity conditions, growth regimes) and proof sketches would increase confidence. For SPGM, guidance for choosing (R0, R) and sensitivity analyses are needed.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well organized: the EF-MRF derivation is recapped concisely; the shortcomings of truncation are explained; and the progression TPGM→QPGM→SPGM is logical.
- Some notation and conditions could be tightened: in Proposition 2, explicitly relate Θ to both pairwise and diagonal quadratic terms, and state whether Θ includes θss; clarify domains (Wp vs Z+). For SPGM, define B(·) cleanly in-line and discuss interpretability.
- Figures and numerical details (e.g., how R was set to max count in TPGM, stability selection parameters, regularization path) should be described in text for reproducibility.

Originality
- The paper provides genuinely new modeling insights: the impossibility theorem for Winsorized conditionals in EF-MRFs; the necessity of quadratic base measure for linear sufficient statistics; and concrete EF constructions (QPGM, SPGM) that admit both positive and negative edges. While related ideas exist (e.g., copulas, Poisson log-normal, sums-of-Poissons), the EF-MRF path with explicit normalizability guarantees and conditional likelihood-based structure learning is novel and well positioned within the Yang et al. EF-MRF framework.
- Related work could be expanded to situate against multivariate count models used in practice (Poisson log-normal; generalized Poisson; COM-Poisson) and prior spatial/auto-Poisson modifications beyond Winsorization.

Significance
- Addressing positive dependencies in count graphical models is important for genomics, neuroimaging, and spatial data. The results provide new tools with theoretical backing and practical procedures.
- Adoption will hinge on practical guidance (hyperparameter choices, computational performance) and guarantees (sparsistency, approximation quality for SPGM). The paper signals these but does not fully deliver them; nonetheless, the conceptual advance is significant.

Pros
- Clear identification and rigorous resolution of a core limitation in Poisson MRFs.
- Three concrete model classes with normalizability: TPGM, QPGM, SPGM.
- Useful theoretical insights (necessity of quadratic base measure).
- Promising empirical performance on simulated and real count data.

Cons
- Missing or limited comparisons to some relevant baselines; original PGM results omitted.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability; SPGM inference relies on variational bounds without error analysis.
- Sensitivity to and selection of SPGM’s (R0, R) not addressed; potential identifiability and interpretability issues with saturated sufficient statistics.
- Several technical details deferred (e.g., proofs, sparsistency), reducing confidence for a conference-length paper.

Recommendations and suggestions
- Include PGM baseline results and add comparisons to Poisson log-normal/negative-binomial (if feasible) to strengthen empirical claims.
- Provide proof sketches or detailed appendices for Theorems 1 and 3; tighten assumptions and notation in Proposition 2.
- Analyze SPGM hyperparameter selection (data-driven choice of R0, R) and sensitivity; discuss identifiability and interpretability.
- Report computational costs and convergence behavior; quantify the impact of the variational bound in SPGM nodewise regressions.
- Consider simple goodness-of-fit diagnostics and moment existence statements for QPGM/SPGM.

Overall assessment
- Quality: good theory, moderate empirics (needs stronger baselines, more detail).
- Clarity: generally clear; some notational/assumption gaps.
- Originality: high.
- Significance: high potential impact.

I lean toward acceptance if the authors strengthen the empirical section and clarify proofs/assumptions; otherwise, borderline accept.
