

# Peer Review: "On Poisson Graphical Models"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which can only model negative conditional dependencies between count-valued variables due to the requirement that edge weights must be non-positive for normalizability. The authors identify this as a significant drawback for modeling real-world count data that often exhibits both positive and negative dependencies. 

The paper makes three key contributions: First, it corrects a previous claim by Kaiser and Cressie [20] that Winsorized Poisson distributions could form a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1). Second, it proposes a Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) with a bounded domain that allows both positive and negative dependencies but has limitations in domain restriction. Third, it introduces two novel models: the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) with a quadratic base measure, and the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) with sub-linear sufficient statistics, both of which allow positive and negative edge weights while maintaining normalizability. The authors demonstrate these models through simulations and a breast cancer microRNA case study.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous proofs (Theorems 1-4) establishing the normalizability of the proposed models under appropriate conditions. The authors carefully analyze the mathematical constraints that enable positive edge weights, which is the paper's most significant contribution.

2. **Clear Problem Identification**: The paper effectively identifies and articulates a critical limitation in existing work that has been overlooked in the literature on Poisson graphical models. The correction of the Kaiser and Cressie approach (Theorem 1) is particularly valuable.

3. **Novel Contributions**: The three proposed models represent genuine advancements. The SPGM is especially elegant, providing a solution that maintains normalizability for all real edge weights while having heavier tails than QPGM. This addresses a fundamental limitation in the field.

4. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The numerical experiments demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed models over existing methods for count data. The breast cancer microRNA case study provides compelling evidence of practical utility, identifying biologically meaningful hubs that other methods miss.

5. **Balanced Assessment**: The authors carefully discuss the limitations of each model (e.g., TPGM's bounded domain, QPGM's Gaussian-like tails), showing intellectual honesty and thoroughness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Complexity Discussion**: While the paper mentions using neighborhood selection with L1 regularization, it lacks detailed discussion of computational complexity compared to existing methods. This is particularly important for high-dimensional applications where scalability is crucial.

2. **Limited Comparison to Recent Count Data Methods**: The paper compares primarily to Gaussian-based methods but could strengthen its position by comparing to more recent methods specifically designed for count data (e.g., negative binomial graphical models).

3. **SPGM Implementation Details**: The SPGM's sub-linear sufficient statistics are theoretically interesting but lack discussion of practical implementation challenges or computational impact.

4. **Theoretical Guarantees for Learning**: While the paper mentions neighborhood selection, it doesn't provide theoretical guarantees for the consistency or sparsistency of the learned models, which would further strengthen the contribution.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations and well-supported empirical results. The proofs are thorough, and the experimental design is appropriate for validating the claims. The work is complete as a scientific contribution.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically organized. The theoretical contributions are clearly presented, though some of the more complex mathematical discussions could be made more accessible with additional intuition. The figures and case study provide valuable context.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant original contribution by identifying a fundamental limitation in existing PGMs and providing three novel solutions. The correction of the Kaiser and Cressie approach alone represents an important contribution to the literature. The SPGM in particular represents a genuinely novel approach to the problem.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is highly significant given the prevalence of count data in modern applications (genomics, spatial statistics, etc.). The proposed models provide a practical solution that can be immediately applied to real-world problems. The breast cancer microRNA case study demonstrates tangible value, identifying biologically relevant hubs that other methods miss.

## Recommendation

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Addresses a critical limitation in existing Poisson graphical models
- Provides three theoretically sound models that allow both positive and negative dependencies
- Rigorous theoretical proofs and strong empirical validation
- Demonstrated practical utility in a real-world biological application
- Careful discussion of limitations and trade-offs

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Comparison to more recent count data-specific methods could be strengthened
- Theoretical guarantees for model learning could be more detailed

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of graphical models by solving a fundamental problem in Poisson graphical models that has limited their practical applicability. The theoretical contributions are sound, the experimental validation is compelling, and the real-world application demonstrates clear value. While the paper could be strengthened by addressing computational considerations and expanding comparisons to recent count data methods, these are minor concerns relative to the paper's overall quality and significance.

The work represents a significant advance in the modeling of multivariate count data, which is increasingly important in modern data analysis. The authors have provided a rigorous foundation for a new class of models that will likely be adopted in applications across multiple domains.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NeurIPS.
