- Paper addresses modeling multivariate count data and the inability of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) to represent negative dependencies.
- Authors show truncation approach (Kaiser & Cressie) is inadequate and propose three new models: TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM.
- TPGM provides a basic fix; QPGM and SPGM modify the Poisson base to allow both positive and negative dependencies.
- Theoretical contributions include Theorems 1–4 proving non-normalizability of the Winsorized PGM and validity of the proposed models.
- Exploration of modified base measures and sufficient statistics is insightful and novel.
- Preliminary experiments include simulations and a breast cancer miRNA dataset to demonstrate practical utility.
- Paper is generally well-written, clearly organized, and cites relevant prior work (Besag, Yang, Kaiser & Cressie, etc.).
- Comparison to Gaussian graphical models provides useful performance benchmarks.
- Experimental validation is limited: simulation details (edge weights, Gibbs sampling) are insufficient and ROC interpretation is unclear.
- Real-data analysis is descriptive and lacks quantitative comparisons and statistical significance testing against baselines.
- Computational cost and the challenge of the log-partition function for QPGM/SPGM are not addressed.
- Motivation for the SPGM’s sublinear statistic is less intuitive and needs clearer justification.
- The Gaussian variational upper bound for SPGM is a simplification and its impact on accuracy should be discussed.
- The paper could more explicitly compare the new models to other count-modeling techniques (e.g., negative binomial graphical models).
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, strong theoretical results, novel models with promising preliminary experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental validation, unaddressed computational complexity, and unclear SPGM motivation.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper worthy of acceptance if authors expand experiments and discuss computational considerations.
- Reviewer confidence: 7/10 (strong leaning toward acceptance).
