### Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

**Summary**

This paper addresses a well-known and significant limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), namely their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints on the infinite domain of count data. The authors propose three novel graphical models for multivariate count data that overcome this restriction. First, they demonstrate that a prior approach based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution [20] is flawed and cannot yield a consistent joint distribution. They then propose a correct Truncated PGM (TPGM) that allows for arbitrary dependencies but is limited to a bounded domain. To address this, they introduce two models for unbounded count data by modifying the standard Poisson formulation: (1) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure, enabling positive dependencies under conditions similar to Gaussian Graphical Models, but at the cost of thin, Gaussian-like tails; and (2) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, permitting arbitrary dependencies while retaining the original Poisson base measure and heavier tails. The paper provides brief experimental validation on simulated data and a real-world miRNA expression dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of modeling mixed-sign dependencies for multivariate count data is of high practical and theoretical importance. Existing PGMs are severely limited by their restriction to negative dependencies. The paper's contributions—refuting a prior approach and proposing three distinct and principled solutions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM)—represent a substantial and novel advancement in this area. These models provide a much-needed toolkit for researchers working with high-dimensional count data.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper's core theoretical claims appear sound. The critique of the Winsorized PGM is presented as a formal theorem (Theorem 1). The development of the new models is well-grounded in the theory of exponential families and their graphical model extensions [17, 18]. The propositions and theorems regarding the normalizability of the proposed models (Theorems 2, 4, Proposition 2) form the technical backbone of the work.

3.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper follows a logical progression, starting with the core problem, critiquing existing attempts at a solution, and then systematically developing its own novel approaches. The motivation for moving from truncation (TPGM) to more fundamental modifications (QPGM, SPGM) is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the clear significance of the contributions, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its technical details, presentation, and experimental validation. These issues, in aggregate, detract significantly from the quality of the manuscript and suggest a lack of careful preparation.

1.  **Lack of Technical Precision and Unsubstantiated Claims:**
    *   **Theorem 1:** The theorem states a condition `R > 3` without any justification. The proof is omitted, leaving this critical condition arbitrary and unsupported. The expression for the conditional distribution in the theorem statement also appears to be a re-parameterization of Eq. (2) that is not explained.
    *   **TPGM Limitations:** The discussion of the TPGM's drawbacks is purely qualitative. The claim that parameters must become "increasingly negative or close to zero" as R increases needs to be formalized with a precise mathematical relationship.
    *   **QPGM Normalizability:** Proposition 2 requires `XTΘX ≤ −cθ‖X‖22` for all `X` on the integer lattice `Wp`. The paper claims this is satisfied if the matrix `Θ` is negative-definite (`Θ ≺ 0`), a condition for real vectors. The sufficiency of this condition on an integer lattice is a non-trivial point that is asserted without proof or citation.
    *   **SPGM Upper Bound:** Proposition 3 provides a variational upper bound for the SPGM's node-conditional log-partition function, but only for `θ ≥ 0`. This is a major limitation, as the model is designed to handle arbitrary parameters. The utility of this bound for learning is questionable without addressing the `θ < 0` case.
    *   **Arbitrary Model Choices:** The specific piecewise form of the sub-linear sufficient statistic in the SPGM feels ad-hoc. A more thorough justification for this particular functional form, beyond its desirable properties, would strengthen the contribution.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation and Analysis:**
    *   **Missing Theory for Learning:** The paper punts on providing theoretical guarantees (e.g., sparsistency) for learning the model structures, dismissing it as "future work due to space limitations." For a paper introducing new models, demonstrating that their structure can be reliably learned from data is a critical component, not an afterthought.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the truncation level `R` is set to the maximum count value in the data. This is an ad-hoc heuristic. A proper evaluation would include a sensitivity analysis to show how the model performance varies with `R` (and `R0` for SPGM).
    *   **Superficial Results Analysis:** The analysis of the case study is cursory. Statements like "TPGM seems to find more hub miRNAs" are vague. The comparison in Figure 2 is interesting, but the discussion lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., edge overlap, hub score comparisons) to substantiate the qualitative claims.

3.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   **References:** The reference list is in a state of disarray. Many entries are incomplete, containing extraneous commas and missing publication details (e.g., [1], [3], [7]). The formatting "Arxiv preprint arXiv:..." is redundant. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Inconsistent Notation:** The domain of the count variables is inconsistently denoted. The introduction states variables take values in the "set of all positive integers," which should be non-negative integers. The set is later referred to as `X` (Sec 2.1.2) and `W` (Prop 2).
    *   **Typos and Errors:** There are numerous minor errors. For instance, `λks` in the Winsorized conditional formula should be `λ_s^k`. The definition of `Ψ(ηs)` appears convoluted and potentially incorrect. Figure 3 is referenced in the text but not included.

**Recommendation**

**Reject.**

While the paper presents a set of novel and potentially important models for a significant problem, its execution is deeply flawed. The combination of un-justified technical claims, a weak and incomplete experimental section, and an egregious lack of attention to detail in presentation and formatting makes the paper fall well short of the standards for publication at NIPS. The core ideas are strong, but the manuscript requires a major revision to address the technical gaps, bolster the empirical results, and correct the numerous presentation errors before it can be considered a complete and convincing piece of work.
