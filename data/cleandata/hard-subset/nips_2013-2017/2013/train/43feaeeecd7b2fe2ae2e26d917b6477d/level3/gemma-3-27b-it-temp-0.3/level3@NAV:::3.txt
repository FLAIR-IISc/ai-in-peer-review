## Review of “On Poisson Graphical Models”

This paper addresses the challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure of an undirected graph. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – namely, their inability to model positive dependencies due to normalizability constraints. They propose several novel approaches, including the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), aiming to overcome this limitation. 

The motivation is well-articulated, and the paper builds logically from the shortcomings of the original PGM to the proposed extensions. The theoretical contribution is valid and interesting, particularly the analysis of base measure requirements for normalizability. The introduction of the SPGM, with its sub-linear sufficient statistics, is a particularly creative attempt to address the issues of the QPGM’s potentially thin tails. The numerical experiments, while limited, demonstrate the potential of the proposed methods on both simulated data and a real-world microRNA expression dataset, showing improvements over Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The QPGM, while theoretically interesting, yields marginally thinner tails than desired, a point the authors acknowledge. More critically, the SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining the conditional-specification motivation and raising questions about whether it is truly “Poisson” in spirit. The practical motivation feels somewhat underdeveloped; while the authors mention sequencing data replacing microarrays, an updated reference is needed. More importantly, they should demonstrate the SPGM PMF visually (e.g., plot it) alongside empirical data (like the breast cancer example) to justify its use. 

Furthermore, the method details are unclear. The role and selection of constants R and R0 are not sufficiently explained, and practitioners would benefit from guidance on choosing appropriate values. The literature review is incomplete; the authors seem to misunderstand copulas and misrepresent reference [8]. They also neglect relevant work in spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count methods, failing to adequately position their work within the broader field. While theoretically sound, the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate why a practitioner would prefer these models over simpler alternatives like a Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel theoretical contribution, addresses a challenging problem, demonstrates potential through simulations and a case study.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of clarity in method details, incomplete literature review, questionable practical motivation, SPGM’s lack of closed-form conditionals, and insufficient justification for preference over simpler models.

**Overall:** I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly clarifying the method details, strengthening the practical motivation, and providing a more thorough literature review. A more compelling argument for the advantages of these models over existing approaches is crucial.
