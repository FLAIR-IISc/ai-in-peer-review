This paper addresses the challenging problem of constructing multivariate count distributions, specifically focusing on Poisson graphical models (PGMs). The authors correctly identify limitations of existing approaches, namely the inability of standard PGMs to model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. They rigorously demonstrate the invalidity of a previously proposed truncation approach (Winsorization) and propose three alternatives: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). While the theoretical contributions are appealing, the experimental validation falls short of establishing practical merit.

The paper's strength lies in its theoretical development. The proof that the Winsorization approach fails to yield a consistent joint distribution is a significant contribution, clarifying a misconception in the literature. The TPGM, built on a novel truncation strategy, is a natural and well-justified extension. Furthermore, the QPGM and SPGM, which modify the exponential family base measure and sufficient statistics, are innovative attempts to overcome the limitations of traditional PGMs. The connection to existing work by Yang et al. provides a solid foundation, although this also limits the novelty of some aspects of the theoretical development.

However, the experimental evaluation is disappointing and appears biased. The synthetic experiments are tailored to the proposed methods, and the real-data comparison omits relevant alternatives like copula-based models. The reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal; a more objective evaluation metric, such as test log-probability, is needed to assess performance rigorously. The use of copula competitors in an overly black-box fashion, with inappropriate marginal choices, undermines the fairness of the comparison. Better marginal choices, such as kernel density estimation (KDE), would provide a more robust assessment. 

The observation that the proposed models achieve identical performance to Glasso is highly suspicious and suggests potential issues with the baseline implementation or the experimental setup. This warrants further investigation. The paper is generally well-written and clear, but all proofs should be included in the supplementary material for complete transparency. Section 3.1 is overly detailed, and the bound presented appears unnecessary. The key figure illustrating the sublinear statistic in Section 3.2 should be presented in the main text and explained more thoroughly.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical contributions addressing a significant problem in modeling count data.
*   Rigorous proof of the invalidity of a previously proposed method.
*   Well-defined and mathematically sound model specifications (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient experimental validation to support the practical merit of the proposed models.
*   Biased experimental setup and lack of comparison with relevant alternatives.
*   Qualitative results and absence of objective evaluation metrics.
*   Suspicious performance compared to a baseline method (Glasso).

**Overall:**

The paper presents theoretically appealing approaches to modeling multivariate count data. However, the experimental evidence is insufficient to establish their practical merit. While the theoretical work is valuable, a more robust and comprehensive experimental evaluation is needed to justify acceptance. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the experimental validation. Specifically, a more comprehensive comparison against established methods using objective evaluation metrics and a thorough investigation of the unexpected results with the Glasso baseline are crucial.
