Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a longstanding limitation of undirected graphical models for counts: the auto-Poisson/Poisson MRF construction (Besag; Yang et al.) only admits non-positive pairwise parameters for normalizability, thereby precluding positive conditional dependencies. The authors (i) prove that the commonly proposed “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals (truncating at R by pooling all mass at R) cannot arise from any compatible joint MRF (Theorem 1); (ii) propose a different truncation that remains in the exponential family and yields a valid joint MRF on {0,…,R} (TPGM), removing sign constraints on edges; (iii) show that with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, normalizability with positive edges requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3); this motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure/terms and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with sublinear sufficient statistics, both allowing positive and negative dependencies. Estimation is via penalized neighborhood selection using node-conditional likelihoods; simulations and a microRNA-seq case study illustrate performance vs Gaussian and semiparametric Gaussian copula baselines.

This situates within extensive work on multivariate counts: auto-models for Poisson (Besag; Yang et al.), Poisson-lognormal and related hierarchical/spatial models, sums-of-Poissons constructions, and copula/discretized Gaussian models. The paper’s novelty is to produce tractable Poisson-like MRFs that directly allow positive conditional dependencies while preserving simple node-conditional forms for estimation (except SPGM’s partition term).

Strengths
- Clear identification and proof of the incompatibility of Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals with an MRF joint; this corrects a claim in the spatial literature.
- Three constructive model families with distinct trade-offs: TPGM (finite support, simple), QPGM (unbounded support, positive/negative edges via quadratic terms), SPGM (heavier tails, broad feasibility).
- Insightful characterization (Theorem 3) linking tail behavior/base measure growth to the feasibility of positive dependencies under linear sufficient statistics.
- Practical learning strategy via penalized nodewise likelihood; empirical results show improved edge recovery over Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines on synthetic and RNA-seq data.
- Empirical case study recovers biologically plausible hubs missed by GGM and by the original auto-Poisson.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- QPGM tail behavior: the quadratic base measure induces Gaussian-esque thin tails. This may be ill-suited to heavy-tailed count data (overdispersion, zero inflation). Please quantify tail behavior and compare fitted marginal tails to Poisson-lognormal (or NB-based) alternatives.
- SPGM tractability: node-conditional log-partition D(θ,R0,R) has no closed form and requires infinite sums/approximations; the paper leans on variational upper bounds. Provide details on how D and its derivatives are computed in practice, error control, and computational complexity. Otherwise the conditional-specification motivation (simple nodewise GLM-style estimation) is weakened for SPGM.
- Hyperparameters R and R0: the modeling and estimation impact of these constants is insufficiently developed. Offer principled guidance (e.g., based on quantiles, tail mass control, or cross-validation), sensitivity analyses, and diagnostics for misspecification.

Clarity
- Literature review is thin and occasionally misleading. The discussion of copulas and semiparametric Gaussian copula models should clarify that such methods model conditional independence in the latent Gaussian space, not the observed counts; nonetheless they provide strong practical baselines. Likewise, hierarchical Poisson-lognormal (and spatial/log-Gaussian Cox) models deserve fuller discussion as they explicitly address overdispersion and heavy tails.
- Please revisit references around contingency-table log-linear models and ensure accurate attribution and numbering (current numbering appears inconsistent; e.g., [11] cited as contingency-table log-linear models vs Lauritzen’s book).
- Practical motivation: Poisson is often a poor marginal fit for RNA-seq; many datasets are overdispersed. The paper should show marginal goodness-of-fit on the breast cancer data and illustrate the SPGM/QPGM implied PMFs (with fitted parameters) to justify appropriateness.

Originality
- The core theoretical contribution (Theorem 1; Theorem 3; QPGM/SPGM constructions) is original and interesting. The TPGM is a natural but useful exponential-family truncation with a valid joint; the base-measure growth condition is a valuable conceptual insight. Please better contrast with prior Poisson-lognormal MRF attempts or latent-Gaussian count graphs (if any).

Significance
- The proposed families broaden the toolbox for multivariate counts with positive dependencies in an MRF framework. However, practical impact will depend on (i) tractable, stable estimation for SPGM; (ii) guidance on R,R0; (iii) comparative benefits over simpler latent-Gaussian + Poisson measurement models. Consider adding comparisons to Poisson-lognormal graphical approaches or Gaussian copula models tailored to counts, and posterior predictive checks for real data.

Specific questions and requests for the authors
- SPGM computation: how do you evaluate D and its gradient for large counts? Do you truncate the sum adaptively? Any guarantees on approximation error and its effect on statistical consistency?
- Hyperparameters: provide a data-driven recipe for R and R0 and a sensitivity study. How do choices affect identifiability and sparsistency?
- Tail behavior: show fitted marginal distributions for QPGM and SPGM vs empirical histograms on the RNA-seq data; report overdispersion metrics.
- Compare against Poisson-lognormal (or NB) graphical models and Gaussian copula models designed for discrete data. If exact graphical counterparts are unavailable, at least include latent-Gaussian pairwise models with Poisson measurement as baselines.
- Clarify the statement that sequencing is “replacing microarrays” and provide an updated reference; alternatively, soften the claim.
- Provide details on optimization (step sizes, convergence criteria, runtime) and scalability.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled constructions that overcome a key limitation of auto-Poisson MRFs.
- Corrects an influential but incorrect claim about Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals.
- Clear path to estimation and encouraging empirical results on simulated and real data.

Cons against acceptance
- SPGM’s lack of closed-form conditional normalization undermines the main practical advantage; computational details are insufficient.
- QPGM’s thin tails and potential misfit for overdispersed counts are not empirically addressed.
- Incomplete/misleading related-work discussion; missing comparisons to strong latent-Gaussian or Poisson-lognormal baselines.
- Limited practitioner guidance for critical constants R and R0.

Overall evaluation
A theoretically solid and creative paper with the potential to influence how we build MRFs for count data. Strengthen the empirical and computational sections (SPGM tractability, guidance on R/R0, tail-fit diagnostics), expand the literature review/comparisons, and clarify claims about data modalities to make the work more compelling to both researchers and practitioners.
