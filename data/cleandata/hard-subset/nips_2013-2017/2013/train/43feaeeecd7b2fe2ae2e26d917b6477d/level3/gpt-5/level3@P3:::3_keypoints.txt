- Paper addresses constructing multivariate count MRFs respecting an undirected graph, starting from Besag’s auto-Poisson and conditional-exponential-family framework.  
- Authors show standard Poisson MRF only admits nonpositive interactions and propose three remedies: TPGM (truncated exponential-family), QPGM (quadratic base measure), and SPGM (sublinear sufficient statistic).  
- Estimation uses nodewise penalized conditional likelihood (neighborhood selection) following Yang et al.  
- Theoretical contributions include impossibility for Winsorized truncation, normalizability proofs for TPGM/SPGM, and a key trade-off theorem (Theorem 3) linking sufficient-statistic growth to base-measure growth.  
- Empirical results include simulations and a TCGA miRNA network case study showing gains over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines.  
- Strength: Rigorous identification and treatment of the negativity constraint in auto-Poisson models, including a useful impossibility result.  
- Strength: Three concrete model classes enable mixed-sign dependencies with relatively simple convex nodewise estimation.  
- Strength: Theorem 3 insight that linear sufficient statistics require at least quadratic base measures to allow mixed-sign edges.  
- Strength: Empirical gains and an interpretable biological case study support practical relevance.  
- Weakness: Practical marginal fit concerns—Poisson is often a poor fit for RNA-seq (overdispersion, zero inflation); authors should directly assess marginal fits (Poisson, NB, QPGM, SPGM) on the breast cancer data.  
- Weakness: QPGM likely has thin, Gaussian-like tails; need diagnostics/sensitivity analysis (extremal counts, predictive log-likelihood) comparing QPGM vs SPGM.  
- Weakness: SPGM node-conditional log-partition has no closed form and uses an upper bound—authors must clarify what is computed, quantify approximation error, and assess impact on edge recovery and Poisson-like behavior.  
- Weakness: Hyperparameters R and R0 lack practitioner guidance—request interpretable selection strategies, sensitivity analysis, and scaling recommendations with n and p; also guidance for QPGM diagonal terms and regularization.  
- Weakness: Literature coverage needs expansion and correction—copula graphical models, sparse log-linear models, and latent Gaussian/spatial count models (e.g., Poisson–lognormal, CAR, spatial GLMMs) should be properly discussed and compared.  
- Weakness: Experimental details missing—provide optimization specifics (step sizes, convergence), computational cost vs p/degree, and robustness to misspecification (non-Poisson data).  
- Weakness: Update biological sequencing claims and references (post-2008 surveys/benchmarks circa 2009–2013).  
- Quality: Theory is careful and interesting, but SPGM estimation relying on bounds and missing marginal/tail analyses weakens practical claims.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well written but some derivations are dense; request intuition for Theorem 3, a figure for B(x; R0,R), and clear statement of what is optimized for SPGM conditionals.  
- Originality: Impossibility result for Winsorization and the sufficient-statistic/base-measure trade-off are novel; the model trio is a useful exploration of count MRF design space.  
- Significance: Advances theory of count-valued MRFs and could influence model design, but needs stronger justification over latent-Gaussian alternatives for practitioner impact.  
- Pros for acceptance: Strong theoretical insights, valid constructions enabling mixed-sign dependencies, and a simple, scalable estimation framework.  
- Cons for acceptance: Insufficient empirical validation (marginal fit/tails), SPGM lacks closed-form conditionals with unquantified approximation effects, incomplete literature positioning, and limited guidance on hyperparameters.  
- Recommendation: Revise to improve empirical justification (fit and tails), clarify SPGM methodology and hyperparameter selection, and expand/accurately position literature and baseline comparisons.
