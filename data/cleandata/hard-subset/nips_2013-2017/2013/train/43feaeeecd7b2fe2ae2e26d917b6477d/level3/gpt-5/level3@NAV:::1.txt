This paper tackles a core limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the Yang et al. construction only normalizes when edge parameters are nonpositive, precluding positive conditional dependencies. The authors first show that a commonly suggested fix—Winsorized node-conditionals à la Kaiser–Cressie/Griffith—cannot yield a consistent joint model. They then introduce three alternatives: (i) a valid exponential-family truncation leading to a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that supports both signs; (ii) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that retains linear sufficient statistics but replaces the base measure with a quadratic term, with a necessity result showing quadratic growth is required for normalizability under positive edges; and (iii) a Sub-Linear PGM (SPGM) that restores the Poisson base measure but uses a sub-linear sufficient statistic to achieve heavier tails and unconstrained edges. They use neighborhood selection for learning and provide experiments on synthetic data and a TCGA miRNA network. This work builds directly on Besag’s auto-models and the GLM-based NIPS line on exponential-family graphical models, and is thoughtfully contrasted with Gaussian, nonparanormal, and multivariate Poisson sum constructions.

Quality: The theory is careful and novel. The impossibility for Winsorization, the normalizability conditions for QPGM, and the SPGM construction are well-supported. Empirical results are sensible and relevant, though scalability and model-selection details (R, R0) could be discussed more, and sparsistency is deferred.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. Minor edits: on p.3 change “graphical mode distributions” to “model.” On p.5 revise “Note that the log...” to “Note that although the log...”. Please define “sparsistent” on first use and briefly cite canonical sources.

Originality: High. The necessity of a quadratic base measure under linear statistics is an insightful structural result, and both SPGM and the corrected truncation are new.

Significance: Strong. Many modern datasets are counts; enabling both positive and negative dependencies with tractable learning is impactful. The empirical case study is compelling.

Concerns and suggestions:
- I am unconvinced by the strong “big negatives” claimed for TPGM; in domains with intrinsically low counts, a principled truncation seems well-motivated and empirically effective.
- The critique of QPGM’s Gaussian-like thin tails feels overstated; such tails may be appropriate (e.g., stabilized or deconvolved counts). Discuss settings where QPGM is preferable.
- Clarify how R and R0 are selected in practice; provide sensitivity analyses and computational costs.
- Elaborate on which parameters are learned for QPGM (diagonal vs. full Θ) and the conditions ensuring negative definiteness in high dimensions.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves a long-standing limitation of Poisson auto-models with rigorous theory.
- Introduces practically learnable models that handle both signs and varying tail behaviors.
- Demonstrates clear empirical advantages on synthetic and real data.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of hyperparameter selection, scalability, and sparsistency guarantees.
- Some critiques (TPGM, QPGM tails) could be more nuanced and empirically grounded.

Overall: Well-written, original, and significant; I lean toward acceptance.
