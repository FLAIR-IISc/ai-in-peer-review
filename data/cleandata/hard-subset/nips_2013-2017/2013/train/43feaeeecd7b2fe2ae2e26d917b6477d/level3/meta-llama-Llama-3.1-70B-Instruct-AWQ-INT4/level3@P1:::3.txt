This paper proposes novel multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect an undirected graph's Markov structure, addressing the long-standing challenge of building multivariate count distributions. The authors introduce three new classes of Poisson-like graphical models, including the Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM). These models aim to capture rich dependence structures between count-valued variables, overcoming the limitations of existing Poisson graphical models that can only model negative conditional dependencies.

The paper's main contributions include the derivation of these new models, their theoretical properties, and empirical evaluations on simulated and real-world data. The authors demonstrate that their models can be used to learn network structures from iid samples of high-dimensional multivariate count data via neighborhood selection. The results show that the proposed models outperform existing approaches, including Gaussian graphical models and the original Poisson graphical model.

The strengths of the paper include its clear and well-organized presentation, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and derivations. The paper also provides a thorough literature review, although some omissions and inaccuracies are noted. The empirical evaluations are convincing, and the use of real-world data from the Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) adds credibility to the results.

However, there are also some weaknesses and limitations to the paper. One major concern is that the QPGM yields marginally thinner tails than desired, which may not be suitable for modeling count data with heavy tails. Additionally, the SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which undermines the conditional-specification motivation and raises questions about whether it is truly "Poisson." The paper could benefit from more discussion on the practical motivation for using Poisson distributions, as well as more guidance on the selection of constants R and R0.

Furthermore, the literature review is not exhaustive, and some references are missing or misstated. The authors seem to misunderstand copulas and misrepresent some existing work. The paper also neglects spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count methods, which may be relevant to the topic.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a valid and interesting theoretical contribution, but practitioners may need stronger justification to prefer these models over simpler alternatives, such as Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement models. The authors could provide more discussion on the advantages and limitations of their models in practice.

Overall, the paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. However, it could benefit from more attention to the limitations and potential improvements of the proposed models.

1. The paper proposes novel multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect an undirected graph's Markov structure, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field.

3. The empirical evaluations are convincing, and the use of real-world data adds credibility to the results.
4. The paper makes a valid and interesting theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models.

1. The QPGM yields marginally thinner tails than desired, which may not be suitable for modeling count data with heavy tails.
2. The SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which undermines the conditional-specification motivation and raises questions about whether it is truly "Poisson."
3. The literature review is not exhaustive, and some references are missing or misstated.
4. The paper neglects spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count methods, which may be relevant to the topic.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Provide more discussion on the practical motivation for using Poisson distributions and the advantages and limitations of the proposed models in practice.
2. Address the limitations of the QPGM and SPGM, such as the thin tails and lack of closed-form conditional distributions.
3. Improve the literature review to include more relevant references and correct any inaccuracies.
4. Consider including more guidance on the selection of constants R and R0 for practitioners.
