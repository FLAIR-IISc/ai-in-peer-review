Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the classical node-conditional Poisson construction (Besag; Yang et al.) yields a joint distribution that is normalizable only when all pairwise parameters are non-positive, effectively allowing only negative conditional dependencies. The authors develop three alternative constructions that admit both positive and negative dependencies:

1) A principled truncation of the Poisson node-conditionals (TPGM) that preserves exponential-family form and yields a valid finite-domain MRF. The paper also proves that the previously proposed “Winsorized” truncation (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot lead to a consistent joint distribution, contrary to prior assertions.

2) A quadratic-base-measure model (QPGM) derived from a necessity result: with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, any normalizable joint model that allows positive and negative edge weights requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (C(x)=Ω(x^2)). This motivates a model with Gaussian-like quadratic terms, with normalizability guaranteed under a negative-definite quadratic form.

3) A sub-linear sufficient statistic model (SPGM) that retains the Poisson base measure log x! but replaces B(x) with a piecewise sub-linear function that saturates beyond a threshold. This yields heavier tails than QPGM and admits all real pairwise parameters while ensuring normalizability. The paper provides variational upper bounds for nodewise normalization to enable neighborhood selection.

The authors relate these constructions to the generalized exponential-family graphical model framework of Yang et al., showing compatibility of node-conditionals and joint distributions, and connect to classical multivariate Poisson and copula-based alternatives that either restrict to positive dependence or lose tractability.

Empirical evaluation includes simulations (hub and scale-free graphs; data from TPGM and sums-of-Poissons generators) and a TCGA breast cancer microRNA sequencing case study. Across settings, TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based baselines and the original PGM, which misses positive edges by design.

Quality
- Theory: The impossibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1), the constructive validity of TPGM (Theorem 2), the necessity of a quadratic-or-higher base measure for linear sufficiency (Theorem 3), and SPGM normalizability (Theorem 4) are well-motivated and technically careful. The normalizability condition for QPGM via a negative-definite quadratic form (Proposition 2) is clear and interpretable.
- Methods: Nodewise penalized conditional likelihood with proximal gradients is appropriate and consistent with prior work on exponential-family graphical models.
- Empirics: Simulations are standard and informative; the real-data analysis is compelling and uses stability selection. That said, additional evaluation via held-out likelihood or predictive calibration would strengthen the case, as would runtime/memory scaling reports and sensitivity analyses for R and (R0,R).

Clarity
The paper is generally very well written and organized. Two points of confusion:
- The term “sparsistent” is used without definition; although established in the literature, please define it explicitly (e.g., graph selection consistency in high dimensions) and cite canonical sources.
- The critique of models could be more nuanced. See comments below on truncation and quadratic tails.

Originality
High. The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals, the necessity of quadratic base measure under linear sufficient statistics, and the SPGM construction are original and important. The paper advances a long-standing bottleneck in modeling multivariate count data.

Significance
High. Many modern applications involve counts (sequencing, incidence data, web events), and the inability of standard PGMs to model positive dependence is a severe limitation. The proposed models should catalyze further methodological and applied work.

Specific comments and suggestions
- On truncation (TPGM): The paper emphasizes “big negatives” of truncation. While these caveats are real (finite support; potential mass piling near R; reversion to negativity constraints as R→∞), they may be acceptable—and even advantageous—in domains with inherently low counts or hard caps (e.g., sequencing with depth limits, rating scales). Please temper the language and provide guidance for selecting R in practice (e.g., via quantiles, cross-validation, or domain constraints).
- On QPGM tails: The “Gaussian-esque” thin tails are presented as a drawback. In some settings (e.g., under-dispersed counts, engineering reliability), thin tails may be appropriate and numerically stable. It would help to frame QPGM and SPGM as complementary options and discuss diagnostics for tail adequacy (e.g., comparing empirical overdispersion to model-implied).
- On SPGM hyperparameters: Offer practical defaults or a data-driven scheme for R0 and R (e.g., set R0 near an upper quantile; ensure R beyond the max observed count; tune via stability selection).
- Baselines: Consider including a negative binomial-based graphical alternative (if available) or a copula-based method, to position the proposed models relative to heavy-tailed marginals.
- Learning guarantees: Since sparsistency is claimed as an anticipated extension, clarifying assumptions (irrepresentability, sample-size scaling) and providing a brief sketch or appendix would boost completeness.

Minor editorial notes
- p.3: “graphical mode distributions” → “graphical model distributions” (or simply “model”).
- p.5: “Note that the log …” → “Note that although the log …” to emphasize the contrast between intractable joint log-partition A(θ) and tractable node-conditional normalization.
- Clarify notation for the count domain (W vs X) and ensure consistency.
- Where using “sparsistent,” define at first use and cite standard references.

Pros for acceptance
- Solves a central limitation of Poisson graphical models.
- Strong theoretical contributions (impossibility, necessity, and normalizability results).
- Three complementary, practically learnable model classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
- Solid empirical demonstrations on synthetic and real sequencing data.
- Clear writing and good connection to prior NIPS work on exponential-family GMs.

Cons against acceptance
- No formal sparsistency proof yet; learning guarantees are deferred.
- Limited discussion on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity (R, R0).
- Evaluation lacks predictive metrics and broader baselines (e.g., negative-binomial, copulas).
- The critique of truncation and quadratic tails could be more balanced given some application regimes.

Overall assessment
A well-written, original, and significant contribution that substantially advances graphical modeling for counts. With minor clarifications, a more balanced discussion of model trade-offs, and added guidance on hyperparameter choices, this will be a strong NIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
