This paper addresses the challenge of modeling multivariate count data, a task for which traditional graphical models like Gaussian and Ising models are ill-suited. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs): their inability to represent positive correlations due to normalizability constraints. To overcome this, they propose three novel variants: a Truncated PGM (TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each aiming to expand the range of dependencies that can be modeled. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and demonstrates a solid understanding of the existing literature in graphical models and count data analysis.

The paper effectively builds upon the work of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19] by recognizing the limitations of their PGM extension. The introduction provides a comprehensive overview of related work, including log-linear models, copula transforms, and multivariate Poisson distributions, correctly positioning the paper’s contribution within the broader landscape of count data modeling. The mathematical derivations, particularly concerning the normalizability conditions and the justification for the quadratic base measure, are rigorous and well-explained. The authors convincingly argue that a quadratic base measure is necessary to allow both positive and negative dependencies while maintaining normalizability under certain conditions. 

A particular strength of the paper is the thorough exploration of different approaches. The authors don’t settle for a single solution but investigate various modifications to the Poisson distribution, carefully analyzing the trade-offs of each. The TPGM, while having limitations, offers a starting point for addressing the issue and is a useful contrast to the more sophisticated QPGM and SPGM. The SPGM, with its sub-linear sufficient statistics, appears particularly promising, offering a potential balance between flexibility and interpretability. The demonstration of the models’ applicability through both simulated data and a real-world microRNA expression dataset is commendable. 

However, the paper's strong criticism of the Winsorized Poisson truncation approach as presented by Kaiser and Cressie [20] feels somewhat overstated. While the authors correctly prove its inconsistency, the TPGM's reliance on truncation may still be relevant for datasets with limited count ranges. Dismissing it outright seems unnecessarily harsh, as such truncations are common practice in statistical modeling. Similarly, the authors’ concern about the Gaussian-like thin tails of the QPGM may not be a universal drawback. In certain biological applications, for example, such tail behavior might be biologically plausible or even desirable.

The term "sparsistent," used in the discussion of numerical experiments, is not fully defined for the reader unfamiliar with the field, despite its established usage. Providing a brief explanation or citation would enhance clarity. Finally, minor editorial improvements could enhance readability. Replacing "graphical mode distributions" with "graphical models" on page 3 and rephrasing the sentence beginning "Note that the log…" on page 5 would improve the flow of the text.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Originality:** The proposed models represent a novel contribution to the field of count data modeling and extend the existing framework of graphical models.
* **Significance:** The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies in count data has significant implications for various applications, including genomics, epidemiology, and social sciences.
* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous mathematical derivations and clear explanations.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible to a broad audience of researchers.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Overly Critical of Truncation:** The paper’s strong dismissal of truncation-based approaches may be too restrictive.
* **Potential Limitations of QPGM:** Concerns about the Gaussian-like tails might be application-specific and shouldn't be presented as a universal drawback.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models. The proposed models offer a promising solution to the challenges of modeling multivariate count data, and the experimental results demonstrate their effectiveness. Despite minor criticisms, the paper’s strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
