

This paper proposes three novel extensions to Poisson graphical models (PGMs) to address the limitation that standard PGMs can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors first demonstrate that the Winsorized Poisson approach by Kaiser and Cressie [20] is invalid for graphical models, then introduce Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models allow both positive and negative conditional dependencies with theoretical guarantees of normalizability.

The paper's strengths include a clear theoretical contribution showing how to modify Poisson distributions to enable richer dependence structures, and relatively simple estimation algorithms via neighborhood selection. The TPGM and SPGM are evaluated on simulated networks and a breast cancer miRNA sequencing dataset, showing improvements over existing methods.

However, several weaknesses significantly limit the paper's impact. The QPGM yields marginally thinner tails than desired, contradicting the authors' motivation for a Poisson-like model. The SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions despite the authors' emphasis on conditional specification, undermining their core motivation. The practical justification is questionable: while the authors claim Poisson is often a poor empirical fit, they fail to provide data plots showing the SPGM PMF on real count data (e.g., breast cancer), which is essential for convincing practitioners. The role and selection of constants R and R0 in TPGM and SPGM are unclear, lacking practitioner guidance. The literature review is incomplete, with misrepresentations of copulas (mischaracterizing [8]) and possible misstatements of [11]. The paper also neglects to compare with spatial statistics methods and other high-dimensional count approaches that do not impose MRF independence in observable space. Most critically, the authors fail to convincingly justify why practitioners should prefer these models over simpler alternatives like Gaussian random fields with Poisson measurement models.

While the theoretical contribution is valid and interesting, the paper would benefit from stronger empirical justification, clearer methodological details, and a more comprehensive literature review. The claim about sequencing reads replacing microarrays requires an updated reference. The paper falls short of demonstrating sufficient significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
