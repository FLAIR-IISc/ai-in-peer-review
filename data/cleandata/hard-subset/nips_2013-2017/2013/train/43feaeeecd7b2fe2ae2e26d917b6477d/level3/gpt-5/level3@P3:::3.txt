Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a classic and difficult problem: constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect an undirected graph’s Markov structure. Starting from Besag’s auto-Poisson and the generalized exponential-family conditional-specification framework, the authors show why the standard Poisson MRF only admits nonpositive interactions, then propose three remedies: (i) TPGM, a proper exponential-family truncation that yields a valid joint MRF on bounded counts; (ii) QPGM, which keeps linear sufficient statistics but replaces the factorial base measure with a quadratic one to restore normalizability under mixed-sign interactions; and (iii) SPGM, which preserves the Poisson base measure but uses a sublinear sufficient statistic B(·; R0, R) to allow arbitrary-sign dependencies while keeping support on the nonnegative integers. Learning proceeds via nodewise penalized conditional likelihood (“neighborhood selection”), following Yang et al. Theoretical results establish non-existence of a joint model for the “Winsorized” truncation, normalizability of TPGM and SPGM, and necessary conditions linking sufficient statistic growth and base measure growth (Theorem 3). Simulations and a TCGA microRNA network case study illustrate use.

Strengths
- Clear identification and rigorous treatment of a long-standing obstacle (the negativity constraint in auto-Poisson models), including a useful impossibility result for the Winsorized construction.
- Three concrete model classes that yield valid MRFs over counts and allow positive and negative conditional dependencies; estimation is relatively simple via convex nodewise problems.
- Theoretical contribution (Theorem 3) is insightful: linear sufficient statistics necessitate ≥ quadratic base measures to permit mixed-sign edges.
- Empirical results show gains over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines on synthetic count data and an interpretable miRNA case study.

Weaknesses and requests for revision
- Practical motivation and marginal fit: Poisson is often a poor empirical model for RNA-seq and many other counts (overdispersion, zero inflation). Please assess marginal fit directly on your breast cancer data (e.g., compare Poisson, negative binomial, QPGM, SPGM marginals) and show SPGM/QPGM implied PMFs (or at least node-conditional PMFs) vs empirical histograms. A plot of the SPGM PMF across a range of neighbor settings would clarify tail behavior and the “sublinear saturation” effect.
- QPGM tail behavior: The quadratic base measure yields Gaussian-esque thin tails. In applications with heavy-tailed counts, how problematic is this in practice? Provide diagnostics or a sensitivity analysis (extremal counts, predictive log-likelihood) contrasting QPGM vs SPGM.
- SPGM conditional-specification concern: The SPGM node-conditional log-partition lacks a closed form and is handled via an upper bound. This undermines one of the selling points of exponential-family conditionals (closed-form normalizers) and raises whether SPGM is meaningfully “Poisson.” Please (a) clarify exactly what is computed during estimation, (b) quantify approximation error and its impact on edge recovery, and (c) explain what aspects of “Poisson-like” behavior SPGM retains beyond the factorial base measure.
- Hyperparameters R and R0: The roles of R (saturation) and R0 (hinge) are central yet practitioner guidance is missing. Please provide: (i) an interpretable selection strategy (e.g., data-driven via quantiles, prior on expected saturation), (ii) sensitivity analyses, and (iii) scaling suggestions with n and p. Similarly, for QPGM, guidance on diagonal quadratic terms and their regularization would help.
- Literature coverage and positioning:
  - Copulas: The paper’s discussion of “indirect copula transforms” is too cursory. Gaussian copula graphical models (e.g., Dobra & Lenkoski 2011; Liu et al. 2012/2013) allow arbitrary univariate margins and infer conditional independence in a latent Gaussian space; they need not—and typically do not—impose MRF conditional independence in the observed counts. Please correct any mischaracterizations and clarify the distinction between observable-space vs latent-space Markov structure.
  - Log-linear/contingency-table models: The statement that parameters “grow exponentially” is imprecise; modern sparse log-linear approaches mitigate this via regularization. Please revise the discussion and citations accordingly.
  - Spatial statistics and hierarchical count models: There is a large body of work (e.g., spatial GLMMs/Poisson-lognormal, CAR models) where dependence is modeled via latent Gaussian fields. These are strong practical baselines even though they encode CI in latent space. The paper would benefit from acknowledging these models explicitly and clarifying when an observable-space MRF is preferable. An empirical comparison against a Poisson–lognormal (latent Gaussian) model would strengthen the case for your approach.
- Experimental details: Provide more information on optimization (step sizes, convergence criteria), computational cost vs p and degree, and robustness to model misspecification (data generated from non-Poisson marginals).
- Claim that sequencing reads are replacing microarrays: Marioni et al. (2008) is early. Please update with more recent references circa 2009–2013 (e.g., surveys and large benchmarking consortia) to substantiate this claim.

Quality
The theory is careful and interesting (Theorems 1–4), and the constructions are valid. However, estimation for SPGM depends on bounds rather than closed forms, and the empirical section does not yet address marginal fit and tail behavior—key to practical utility.

Clarity
Generally well written, but some derivations are dense. Please add intuition for Theorem 3, a figure for B(x; R0, R), and concrete guidance on R/R0 selection. Clarify exactly what is optimized for SPGM conditionals.

Originality
The impossibility result for Winsorization and the sufficient-statistic/base-measure trade-off are new and valuable. The trio of constructions is a novel exploration of the design space for count MRFs.

Significance
The work advances the theory of count-valued MRFs and could influence future model design. For practitioner impact, the paper should more convincingly justify these models over simpler latent-Gaussian count models and demonstrate superior fit/prediction on real data.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical insights; valid constructions enabling mixed-sign dependencies.
- Simple, scalable estimation framework.
- Addresses a longstanding limitation of Poisson MRFs.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical motivation and empirical validation are not yet compelling (marginal fit, tails).
- SPGM lacks closed-form conditionals; approximation impact is unquantified.
- Incomplete literature positioning and missing comparisons to key latent-Gaussian baselines.
- Limited guidance on crucial hyperparameters R and R0.

Overall, I recommend a revision focusing on (i) clearer empirical justification (fit, tails, baselines), (ii) methodological clarity for SPGM and hyperparameter selection, and (iii) an expanded, more accurate literature discussion.
