## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses a significant limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to model negative correlations due to normalizability constraints. The authors convincingly argue that the Yang et al. default model is too restrictive and propose several novel approaches to overcome this, including a new truncation method, a quadratic base-measure statistic, and a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a substantial contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. 

The paper builds upon the established framework of graphical models (Lauritzen, 2009) and extends it to the challenging domain of count data, where traditional Gaussian graphical models fall short. The authors correctly identify the core issue with existing PGMs, rooted in the properties of the Poisson distribution and its impact on the log-partition function. The proposed solutions are theoretically sound, with proofs supporting the validity of the new models. The experimental evaluation, using both synthetic and real (microRNA sequencing) data, provides empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the SPGM. 

A key strength of the paper is its thoroughness. The authors not only propose solutions but also rigorously analyze their properties and limitations. The discussion of the truncation method, demonstrating why the Kaiser & Cressie approach is flawed, is particularly insightful. The introduction of the quadratic and sub-linear PGMs represents a significant step forward in modeling complex dependencies in count data.

However, I have some reservations. While the authors claim “big negatives” for the truncation model, I believe its utility might be limited to domains with inherently low counts. The critique of the quadratic model, suggesting its Gaussian-like thin tails are inappropriate, feels somewhat dismissive. In certain scenarios, such a characteristic might be desirable or even expected. Furthermore, the term "sparsistent" is used without sufficient explanation, despite being an established concept. A brief definition or reference would be helpful.

**Minor Editorial Suggestions:**

*   Page 3: Change "graphical mode distributions" to "model".
*   Page 5: Clarify "Note that the log..." to something like "Note that although the log...".

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Originality:** The proposed models and theoretical analysis are novel and address a significant gap in the literature.
*   **Significance:** The work has the potential to impact various fields dealing with count data, such as genomics, epidemiology, and marketing.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and a well-designed experimental evaluation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the topic.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limitations of the truncation model are not fully acknowledged.
*   The critique of the quadratic model feels somewhat subjective.
*   The term "sparsistent" is used without sufficient explanation.



Overall, I strongly recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further strengthen the manuscript.
