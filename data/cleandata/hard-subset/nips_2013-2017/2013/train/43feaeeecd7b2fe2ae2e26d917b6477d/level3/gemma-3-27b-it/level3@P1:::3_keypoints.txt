- Problem: Constructs multivariate distributions for unbounded count data respecting an undirected graphical Markov structure, addressing limitations of standard Poisson graphical models.
- Proposed methods: Introduces three approaches—Truncated PGM, Quadratic PGM, and Sublinear PGM—to allow both positive and negative dependencies.
- Strength (theory): Provides novel and theoretically sound modifications to Poisson models to overcome normalizability constraints.
- Strength (estimation): Results in relatively simple estimation algorithms.
- Strength (modeling): Enables modeling of both positive and negative dependencies among counts, a notable advance.
- Strength (experiments): Includes numerical experiments and a real-data case study (microRNA) demonstrating applicability.
- Weakness (Quadratic): Quadratic PGM produces marginally thinner tails than desirable.
- Weakness (Sublinear): Sublinear PGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining its interpretation as a graphical model.
- Weakness (practical motivation): Insufficient practical justification for these complex models over simpler alternatives (e.g., Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement).
- Weakness (empirical evidence): Fails to provide data or visualizations (e.g., sample vs fitted PMF) to show Poisson is a poor empirical fit.
- Weakness (parameters): Roles and selection guidance for constants R and R0 (Truncated/Sublinear PGMs) are inadequately explained.
- Weakness (literature review): Literature review is incomplete/misleading—misrepresents copula capabilities, is dismissive of Liu et al., and underestimates high-dimensional/spatial count models.
- Weakness (references): Application-related reference about microarrays vs sequencing is dated and should be updated.
- Overall assessment: Theoretical contributions are valuable but practical concerns, missing guidance, and literature issues weaken impact.
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends a weak reject.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel theory overcoming Poisson limitations; demonstrated applicability via experiments and case study; potential to model complex count dependencies.
- Arguments against acceptance: Weak practical motivation and limited empirical evidence; lack of parameter-selection guidance; incomplete/misleading literature review; SPGM’s lack of closed-form conditionals questions its graphical-model interpretation.
