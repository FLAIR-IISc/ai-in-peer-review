This paper addresses the challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure encoded by an undirected graph. The authors build upon a long history of work in this area, beginning with Besag [17] and continuing with contributions from Arnold et al. [11] and others. They identify a key limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to model negative conditional dependencies – and propose three novel approaches: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The core contribution lies in modifying the Poisson distribution’s base measure and sufficient statistics to allow for both positive and negative dependencies. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of learning network structure from simulated and real microRNA sequencing data using penalized neighborhood selection.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the need for graphical models capable of handling count data with complex dependencies. The proposed constructions are mathematically sound, permitting marginal count distributions and leading to relatively simple estimation algorithms. The TPGM, while having limitations, offers a concrete step toward addressing the shortcomings of the original PGM. The QPGM and SPGM represent more fundamental modifications and are theoretically interesting, expanding the possibilities for modeling count data. The empirical validation on both simulated and real data provides initial evidence of the utility of the proposed methods.

However, several weaknesses warrant consideration. The QPGM, as acknowledged by the authors, yields marginally thinner tails than desired, potentially limiting its applicability in scenarios where heavier tails are expected. More significantly, the SPGM’s departure from a traditional exponential family structure raises questions about whether it can truly be considered a “Poisson” graphical model, given that its conditional distributions lack a closed form, undermining the initial conditional-specification motivation. 

The practical motivation for this work could be strengthened. While the authors highlight the ubiquity of count data, the Poisson distribution is often a poor empirical fit. Demonstrating the SPGM’s performance on real data (e.g., the breast cancer data) with visualizations of the SPGM probability mass function (PMF) alongside the observed data distribution would provide a compelling justification for its use.

Methodological details require further clarification. The role and selection of the constants R and R0 are not sufficiently explained. Practitioners would benefit from guidance on how to choose these parameters based on the characteristics of the data. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of varying R and R0 on the inferred network structure would be valuable.

The literature review is somewhat incomplete and contains potential inaccuracies. The authors’ characterization of copula approaches seems to misunderstand their flexibility, and the description of Liu et al. [8] is light. The claim that sequencing reads are “replacing” microarrays requires an updated reference – while sequencing is increasingly prevalent, microarrays remain a valuable tool in many contexts. Additionally, the paper neglects a considerable body of work in spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count methods. Many existing approaches, such as Gaussian copula models, do not explicitly impose MRF independence in observable space and offer alternative solutions for modeling correlated count data.

The theoretical contribution is valid and intriguing. However, practitioners require stronger justification for preferring these models over simpler alternatives, such as a Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model. A detailed comparison of computational complexity and scalability would further inform this decision.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and mathematically sound approach to constructing multivariate count distributions with flexible dependence structures.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing PGMs by allowing for both positive and negative dependencies.
*   Demonstrated feasibility of learning network structure from simulated and real data.
*   Potential for broader applicability in areas with abundant count data (e.g., genomics, epidemiology).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Practical motivation could be strengthened with more compelling empirical evidence.
*   SPGM's departure from a traditional exponential family raises concerns about its interpretation as a “Poisson” model.
*   Literature review is incomplete and contains potential inaccuracies.
*   Methodological details (R and R0 selection) require further clarification.
*   Lack of a comprehensive comparison to simpler alternatives.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. While some weaknesses remain, the novelty and theoretical soundness of the proposed methods warrant serious consideration. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon addressing the concerns regarding the literature review, methodological details, and practical motivation. Specifically, the authors should provide a more thorough literature review, clarify the role of parameters R and R0, and include visualizations demonstrating the SPGM PMF on real data to bolster the practical justification for the proposed model.
