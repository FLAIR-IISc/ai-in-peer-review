- Addresses constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect undirected graph Markov structure.
- Builds on prior Poisson graphical model (PGM) work and identifies PGMs' inability to model negative conditional dependencies.
- Proposes three new models: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).
- Core contribution: modifying the Poisson base measure and sufficient statistics to allow both positive and negative dependencies.
- Demonstrates feasibility of learning network structure on simulated and microRNA sequencing data via penalized neighborhood selection.
- Well-motivated and clearly articulated need for graphical models handling complex count dependencies.
- Proposed constructions are mathematically sound and permit marginal count distributions with relatively simple estimation algorithms.
- TPGM offers a concrete, though limited, improvement over original PGM.
- QPGM and SPGM are theoretically interesting and expand modeling possibilities for count data.
- Empirical validation provides initial evidence of utility on simulated and real data.
- QPGM produces marginally thinner tails than desirable, limiting applicability for heavy-tailed data.
- SPGM departs from exponential family structure and lacks closed-form conditional distributions, raising questions about its “Poisson” interpretation.
- Practical motivation is weak: Poisson often poorly fits real data and SPGM performance should be shown with PMF visualizations on real datasets.
- Methodological details are unclear: selection and role of constants R and R0 need explanation and sensitivity analysis.
- Literature review is incomplete and contains inaccuracies (mischaracterization of copula methods, light coverage of Liu et al., overstatement about sequencing replacing microarrays).
- Neglects relevant bodies of work (spatial statistics, other high-dimensional count methods) and alternative approaches like Gaussian copula or Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement.
- Theoretical contribution is valid but needs stronger justification versus simpler alternatives and explicit comparisons of computational complexity and scalability.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, mathematically sound, allows negative dependencies, demonstrated feasibility, broad applicability.
- Arguments against acceptance: weak practical motivation, SPGM interpretability concerns, incomplete literature review, unclear R/R0 choices, lack of comparison to simpler methods.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on improving literature review, clarifying R and R0, and adding SPGM PMF visualizations on real data.
