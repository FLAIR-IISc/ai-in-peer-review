## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses the important problem of modeling multivariate count data, a common scenario in many modern applications. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) stemming from their inability to represent negative correlations due to normalizability constraints. The core contribution lies in proposing three alternative approaches – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – to overcome this limitation. The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, demonstrating the invalidity of a previously suggested truncation method and justifying the novel modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics.

The TPGM truncation strategy is well-motivated and offers a natural extension of the original PGM. The introduction of QPGM and SPGM, representing more fundamental departures from the standard Poisson framework, is a significant step towards capturing richer dependency structures. However, a substantial portion of the theoretical development closely follows prior work by Yang et al. (2013, 2014), diminishing the overall novelty of the theoretical contributions. The practical impact hinges heavily on demonstrating superior performance in real-world applications. 

Unfortunately, the experimental evaluation is a key weakness. The simulation studies appear tailored to favor the proposed models, and the real-data analysis lacks comparison with established copula-based approaches. The use of overly simplistic marginal distributions for the copula competitors is a concern and doesn't represent a fair comparison. Furthermore, the reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal; a more rigorous, quantitative evaluation using metrics like test log-probability is necessary. The identical performance achieved against a Glasso baseline is highly suspicious and suggests potential issues with the baseline implementation or the experimental setup. 

While the paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the theoretical framework, moving all proofs to the supplementary material is advisable. Section 3.1 contains excessive detail, while the key findings of Section 3.2, particularly the illustrative figure, should be presented and more thoroughly explained in the main text.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem with clear theoretical justification.
* Introduces novel PGM variants with potentially useful properties.
* Well-written and presents a cohesive framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical novelty due to reliance on existing work.
* Weak and potentially biased experimental evaluation.
* Lack of comparison to relevant competing methods.
* Insufficient quantitative analysis of results.



**Overall:** The paper presents theoretically appealing approaches to modeling multivariate count data. However, the lack of robust experimental evidence significantly undermines the claim of practical merit. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after a substantial overhaul of the experimental evaluation. The authors need to provide a more compelling demonstration that these models outperform existing alternatives in realistic scenarios.
