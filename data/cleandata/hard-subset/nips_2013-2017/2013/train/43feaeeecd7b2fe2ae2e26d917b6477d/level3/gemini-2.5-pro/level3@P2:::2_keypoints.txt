- Problem: standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) cannot model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints.  
- Main contribution: proposes three Poisson-like graphical models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Negative result: proves the previously proposed Winsorized Poisson conditional (Kaiser & Cressie, 1997) cannot yield a valid joint distribution.  
- TPGM: introduces a valid truncation-based PGM preserving the exponential family form.  
- QPGM: modifies the base measure with a quadratic term to allow mixed-sign parameters but produces Gaussian-like thin tails.  
- SPGM: uses a sub-linear sufficient statistic to permit heavier tails while maintaining normalizability for arbitrary real parameters.  
- Theory quality: derivations are technically sound and the constructions follow logically from identified problems.  
- Empirical weakness: experimental evaluation is inadequate and does not convincingly support superiority claims.  
- Missing objective metrics: lacks held-out test log-likelihood or other density-fit measures essential for probabilistic model assessment.  
- Biased simulations: synthetic experiments seem tailored to the proposed models (e.g., data generated from TPGM), reducing generality of conclusions.  
- Missing baselines: fails to compare against strong alternatives like copula-based/nonparanormal methods.  
- Suspicious implementation/results: nonparanormal SKEPTIC reported identical performance to Glasso, suggesting implementation or application errors.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written; proofs should be moved to supplement and some sections (QPGM derivation) condensed.  
- Presentation suggestion: move the SPGM explanatory figure from the appendix into the main text for clarity.  
- Originality: refutation of Winsorized PGM and the TPGM/QPGM/SPGM ideas are novel, but the joint-construction framework relies heavily on prior work (Yang et al.).  
- Significance: the problem is important with broad applications, but practical impact is undermined by weak empirical validation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, provides a useful negative result, and proposes creative, theoretically sound models.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient and potentially biased experiments, missing baselines and objective metrics, and limited practical demonstration.  
- Overall: theoretically promising and novel, but currently an under-validated contribution lacking convincing empirical evidence.
