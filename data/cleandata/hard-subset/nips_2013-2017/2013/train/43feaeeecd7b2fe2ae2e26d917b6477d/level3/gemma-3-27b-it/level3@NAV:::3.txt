## Review of “On Poisson Graphical Models”

This paper addresses the challenging problem of modeling multivariate count data using graphical models, specifically tackling the limitations of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) which can only represent negative dependencies. The authors propose several novel extensions to the PGM, including the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), aiming to capture both positive and negative correlations. The core idea of modifying the Poisson distribution's base measure and/or sufficient statistics is a valid and interesting theoretical contribution.

The paper builds upon a long history of work in graphical models (Besag, Lauritzen) and multivariate count distributions (Arnold et al.).  It correctly identifies the key restriction of standard PGMs and attempts to circumvent it. The introduction of the TPGM offers a practical starting point, while the QPGM and SPGM represent more fundamental departures to allow positive dependencies.  The connection to Gaussian Graphical Models via variational bounds for the QPGM is a useful intuition. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The QPGM, while theoretically sound, yields marginally thinner tails than desirable, a significant drawback for real-world count data.  More critically, the SPGM, despite aiming for flexibility, lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining the initial motivation of conditional specification and calling into question its “Poisson” nature. The practical motivation also feels weak; the authors should demonstrate the utility of these models with real data examples beyond the microRNA-sequencing data. Plotting the SPGM PMF alongside a real dataset (e.g., the breast cancer data) would clearly illustrate its suitability. 

Furthermore, the description of the parameters R and R0, crucial for the TPGM and SPGM respectively, is unclear.  More guidance on their selection and impact is needed for practitioners. The literature review is surprisingly incomplete, with misunderstandings of copulas and misrepresentations of existing work ([8], [11]). There's also a noticeable neglect of spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count methods that, while not necessarily enforcing MRF independence in the observable space, provide valuable alternatives. Finally, the claim that sequencing reads are replacing microarrays requires an updated reference. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** 
* Valid and interesting theoretical contribution in extending PGMs.
* Clear identification of a limitation in existing models.
* Exploration of multiple approaches to address the problem.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Weak practical justification; lack of compelling empirical validation.
* SPGM’s lack of closed-form conditionals is a major drawback.
* Unclear parameter selection guidance. 
* Incomplete/misleading literature review.



Overall, this paper presents a worthwhile theoretical investigation. However, the lack of robust empirical support and several technical shortcomings prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with a request for substantial revisions addressing the points outlined above.
