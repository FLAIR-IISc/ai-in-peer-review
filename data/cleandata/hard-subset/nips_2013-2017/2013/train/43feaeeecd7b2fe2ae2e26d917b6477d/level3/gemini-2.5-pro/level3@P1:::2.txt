This paper addresses the important problem of learning undirected graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors correctly identify a major limitation of the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM), which, due to normalizability constraints over an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. The main contribution of the paper is the proposal of three new classes of "Poisson-like" graphical models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.

The paper begins by analyzing existing approaches. It provides a valuable contribution by proving that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson truncation by Kaiser and Cressie (1997) does not, in fact, lead to a valid joint distribution under the standard construction framework. The authors then propose their first model, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), based on a different truncation strategy that ensures the node-conditional distributions remain in the exponential family, thus guaranteeing the existence of a valid joint MRF. Recognizing the limitations of truncation (e.g., the choice of truncation level R), the authors then introduce two more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. The Quadratic PGM (QPGM) alters the base measure to be quadratic, which allows for positive dependencies but results in a thin-tailed, "Gaussian-esque" distribution. The Sublinear PGM (SPGM) modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, which permits a heavier-tailed model that is normalizable for any real-valued interaction parameters. For all models, structure learning is performed via penalized neighborhood selection.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem. As count data becomes more prevalent in fields like genomics and social sciences, the need for flexible graphical models that can capture complex dependency structures is critical. The authors clearly articulate the severe limitations of the standard PGM.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The proposed models are novel and conceptually interesting. The disproof of the validity of the Winsorized PGM construction is a clean and useful theoretical result. The TPGM is a natural and well-justified alternative. The QPGM and SPGM are particularly creative, as they move beyond simple truncation and explore more fundamental modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics of the underlying exponential family distribution. This opens up a promising new direction for designing graphical models for other data types.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The motivation for each model is clearly explained, and the progression from TPGM to QPGM to SPGM is logical.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Novelty:** While the specific models are new, the theoretical machinery used to establish the existence and form of the joint distribution is a direct application of the framework developed by Yang et al. (2012, 2013). This places a greater burden on the empirical evaluation to demonstrate the practical merit and superiority of the proposed models.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is the most significant weakness of the paper and does not provide convincing evidence for the practical utility of the proposed models.
    *   **Biased Synthetic Data:** The synthetic data is generated from the TPGM distribution or a model that only has positive dependencies (sums of independent Poissons). This setup is overly favorable to the proposed methods and does not assess their robustness or performance under more realistic conditions or model mismatch.
    *   **Weak and Missing Baselines:** The paper omits a crucial class of competitors: copula-based graphical models. Methods like the nonparanormal (Liu et al., 2009) are designed precisely for this setting (learning graphs for non-Gaussian data) and represent a very strong baseline. While the nonparanormal is included in the experiments, its reported performance is identical to the standard Gaussian graphical model (Glasso), which is highly suspicious. This suggests either an implementation issue or an inappropriate choice of marginal transformation—for count data, a simple empirical CDF transformation can be problematic and alternatives (e.g., transformations based on kernel density estimates or parametric fits) should have been considered. A proper comparison against a well-implemented copula-based method is essential.
    *   **Inadequate Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation relies solely on ROC curves for structure recovery. While useful, this metric does not assess the quality of the learned *distribution*. A more objective and comprehensive evaluation would include metrics like the average log-probability on a held-out test set, which would measure how well the models actually fit the data.
    *   **Anecdotal Real-Data Analysis:** The analysis of the microRNA data is qualitative. The authors claim their methods find "many more edges and biologically important hub miRNAs," but this is not substantiated with rigorous evidence. Without external validation or a quantitative measure of model fit, it is impossible to know if these additional edges represent true biological signal or simply false positives.

**Clarity Suggestions:**

*   To improve readability, all proofs should be deferred to the supplementary material.
*   Section 3.1 on the QPGM is overly detailed, and the derivation of the variational upper bound feels tangential. This section could be condensed.
*   The sub-linear sufficient statistic is the core idea of the SPGM. The corresponding figure (mentioned as Figure 3 in the appendix) is critical for understanding the model and should be included in the main text.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper addresses an important problem, corrects a mistake in the existing literature, and proposes three novel and theoretically elegant models for count data graphical models. The ideas of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics are particularly interesting.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The claims of the models' utility are not supported by a rigorous or convincing experimental evaluation. The experiments appear biased, lack strong and properly implemented baselines (especially copula models), and rely on limited evaluation metrics. The theoretical contribution builds heavily on an existing framework.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces several theoretically appealing ideas for an important problem domain. However, the empirical evidence presented is insufficient to establish their practical merit over existing, more general-purpose methods. The weaknesses in the experimental design and evaluation prevent a clear assessment of the models' performance. While the ideas are promising, the work in its current form feels incomplete and does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS.
