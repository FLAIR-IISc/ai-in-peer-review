### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the important problem of learning undirected graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors begin by highlighting a major limitation of the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM), which, due to normalizability constraints over an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. The paper's main contribution is the proposal of three novel classes of "Poisson-like" graphical models that overcome this limitation and can capture both positive and negative dependencies.

First, the paper investigates truncating the support of the Poisson distribution. It provides a valuable negative result by proving that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional distribution (Kaiser and Cressie, 1997) cannot lead to a valid joint distribution. The authors then propose their first model, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), based on a different, valid truncation scheme that preserves the exponential family form. Recognizing the limitations of truncation, the paper then proposes two more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution itself, built upon the framework of Yang et al. (2012). The Quadratic PGM (QPGM) introduces a quadratic base measure, which ensures normalizability for mixed-sign parameters but results in Gaussian-like thin tails. The Sub-linear PGM (SPGM) modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear, which allows for a heavier-tailed model while still ensuring normalizability for arbitrary real-valued parameters. The authors demonstrate the models' utility for network recovery on simulated data and a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its theoretical derivations. The proof that the Winsorized PGM is invalid is a solid and useful contribution that corrects a misconception in the literature. The construction of the three new models is clear and follows logically from the identified problems. However, the quality of the experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The claims of superiority are not well-supported by the provided experiments. The evaluation lacks objective metrics like test set log-likelihood, which is crucial for assessing the fit of a probabilistic model. Instead, the paper relies on ROC curves for structure recovery and anecdotal evidence from a case study. Furthermore, the synthetic experiments appear tailored to the proposed models (e.g., generating data from a TPGM), which does not provide a convincing case for their general applicability. The work feels somewhat incomplete without a more rigorous and unbiased empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the progression from the limitations of the standard PGM to the series of proposed solutions is easy to follow. The mathematical formulations are presented clearly.
Suggestions for improvement:
*   All proofs should be moved to the supplementary material to improve readability and save space for a more thorough experimental section.
*   Section 3.1 on the QPGM feels overly detailed, particularly the derivation of the variational upper bound, which seems secondary to the main contribution. This section could be condensed.
*   Conversely, the sub-linear sufficient statistic in Section 3.2 is a key novel idea. The corresponding figure (Figure 3, currently in the appendix) is essential for understanding this construction and should be moved into the main text.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions. The refutation of the Winsorized PGM is a novel finding. The TPGM is a natural and well-justified alternative. The core novelty lies in the QPGM and SPGM, which creatively modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the underlying exponential family, respectively. This is a more fundamental approach than simple truncation and is a new way to think about constructing valid graphical models for count data. However, it is important to note that the theoretical machinery for extending these novel univariate distributions to a graphical model framework relies heavily on the work of Yang et al. [18, 19]. While the application is new, the theoretical framework for showing the existence of a valid joint distribution is not.

**Significance:** The problem of modeling multivariate count data is highly significant, with applications in genomics, social sciences, and many other fields. The paper's goal of developing flexible models that capture mixed dependencies is therefore very important. The proposed models are theoretically appealing and could potentially be of great use to practitioners and researchers. However, the significance of the contribution is severely undermined by the weak experimental evidence. Without a convincing demonstration that these models outperform existing, well-established alternatives (like copula-based methods) on standard, objective metrics, their practical impact remains uncertain. The current experiments are insufficient to establish that these new models advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

### Detailed Comments

The experimental section needs substantial revision to be convincing.
1.  **Missing Baselines:** The paper omits comparisons to strong and relevant baselines, particularly copula-based graphical models (e.g., the nonparanormal methods of Liu et al. [7, 9, 10] or Dobra and Lenkoski [8]). These methods are designed to handle non-Gaussian data by transforming marginals and are a standard approach for this type of problem.
2.  **Weak Baselines:** When the nonparanormal SKEPTIC is used, its performance is reported as identical to the standard Gaussian graphical model (Glasso). This is highly suspicious and suggests either an implementation error or a sub-optimal application of the method. For count data, using a simple Gaussian transformation is inappropriate; a more flexible transformation based on the empirical CDF or kernel density estimates should be used for the marginals.
3.  **Lack of Objective Evaluation:** The primary evaluation metric is structure recovery (ROC curves). While useful, this does not assess how well the models actually fit the data distribution. The review committee and readers would need to see a comparison of held-out log-probability/log-likelihood to judge which model is superior from a density estimation perspective.
4.  **Biased Simulation:** Generating data from the TPGM distribution and showing that the TPGM method performs best is not a surprising or compelling result. The evaluation should include data generated from more neutral processes or from competitors' models.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated and important problem in machine learning.
*   Provides a definitive and useful negative result by invalidating a previously proposed model (Winsorized PGM).
*   Proposes three novel, theoretically sound, and interesting models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) for count data that, unlike the standard PGM, can capture both positive and negative dependencies.
*   The ideas of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics are creative and could inspire further work.

**Arguments against:**
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the paper's claims of practical utility and superiority.
*   The experiments lack crucial objective metrics (test log-likelihood), omit strong baselines (copula models), and contain suspicious results that cast doubt on the validity of the comparison.
*   The theoretical novelty is somewhat limited, as the general framework for constructing the graphical models from node-conditionals is borrowed directly from prior work. The paper's practical merit is thus paramount, but it is not convincingly demonstrated.

**Overall:** This paper introduces theoretically appealing and novel approaches to a significant problem. However, the experimental evidence provided is not strong enough to establish their practical merit over existing methods. The contribution is currently more of a promising theoretical proposal than a complete and validated piece of work.
