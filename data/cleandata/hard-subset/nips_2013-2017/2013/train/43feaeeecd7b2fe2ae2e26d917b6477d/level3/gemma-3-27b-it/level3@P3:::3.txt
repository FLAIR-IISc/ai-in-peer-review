This paper addresses the challenging problem of modeling multivariate count data using graphical models, extending beyond the limitations of traditional Poisson graphical models (PGMs) which can only represent negative dependencies. The authors propose three novel approaches – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – to overcome this limitation. The core idea is to modify the Poisson distribution's base measure and sufficient statistics to allow for both positive and negative correlations. The paper includes theoretical analysis demonstrating the normalizability of the proposed models and empirical validation on simulated and real microRNA sequencing data.

The work builds upon a long history of attempting to extend PGMs (Besag [17], Yang et al. [18, 19]). The authors correctly identify the key issue with standard PGMs – the inability to model positive dependencies due to normalizability constraints. They also correctly point out previous attempts via truncation (Kaiser & Cressie [20], Griffith [21]) and demonstrate that a straightforward truncation approach is flawed (Theorem 1). The introduction positions the work well within the existing literature and highlights the increasing importance of count data in various fields. The connection to contemporary methods like Gaussian graphical models [1, 2, 3, 4] and copula-based approaches [12] is relevant, though could be expanded.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical contribution. The analysis of truncation and the derivation of the QPGM and SPGM distributions are mathematically sound and provide valuable insights into the necessary conditions for modeling multivariate count data with graphical models. The introduction of the sublinear sufficient statistic in the SPGM is particularly innovative. The empirical evaluation, though limited, demonstrates the potential of the proposed models, particularly the SPGM, in recovering network structures from both simulated and real data. The comparison to existing methods, including the original PGM and Gaussian graphical models, highlights the advantages of the proposed approach.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the explanation of the constants R and R0 used in the TPGM and SPGM, respectively, is insufficient. The authors need to provide more guidance on how to select these parameters in practice. A sensitivity analysis showing the impact of these choices on model performance would be valuable. Second, the authors claim sequencing reads are replacing microarrays [22] but need an updated reference. This statement was accurate several years ago but more current publications would strengthen this claim.

A significant concern is the lack of a compelling practical motivation for the QPGM. The authors acknowledge that Poisson distributions often provide a poor empirical fit, yet the QPGM exacerbates this issue by introducing thinner tails. While theoretically interesting, the practical benefits of the QPGM over simpler alternatives, like a Gaussian random field plus a Poisson measurement model, are not convincingly demonstrated.  Plotting the SPGM probability mass function alongside the Poisson and Gaussian distributions for illustrative datasets would help to clarify its properties and potential advantages. 

The literature review is incomplete. The authors seem to misunderstand copulas, stating they “do not impose MRF independence in observable space,” which isn’t necessarily true and misses significant nuances in copula-based graphical model construction.  The characterization of [8] is vague, and the claim regarding [11] requires clarification. More importantly, the paper largely neglects spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count methods that *do* directly address the problem of modeling correlated count data.  Many approaches, like those utilizing Gaussian copulas, can implicitly capture dependence without explicitly imposing MRF independence in observable space.

Finally, while the authors mention the possibility of extending sparsistency analysis from [18, 19] to their models, a preliminary investigation or discussion of potential challenges in this regard would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical contributions to the field of Poisson graphical models.
*   Sound mathematical analysis demonstrating the normalizability of the proposed models.
*   Empirical validation showing promising results on simulated and real data.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in statistical modeling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficient explanation and guidance on parameter selection (R, R0).
*   Limited practical motivation for the QPGM due to its thinner tails.
*   Incomplete literature review and potential misrepresentation of related work.
*   Neglect of relevant existing methods in spatial statistics and high-dimensional count data analysis.
*   Lack of in-depth investigation of theoretical properties, such as sparsistency.



Overall, this is a technically sound and theoretically interesting paper. However, the weaknesses, particularly regarding practical motivation and the literature review, prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the reviewer comments and potentially resubmit after significant revisions.
