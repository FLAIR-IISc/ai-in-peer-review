This paper proposes several new models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables, addressing the limitations of existing Poisson graphical models. The authors argue that the Yang et al. default model is unsatisfactory because it only allows negative correlations to normalize. The proposed fixes include a new truncation method, a quadratic base-measure statistic, and a sub-linear sufficient statistic.

The paper is well-written, original, and its results are significant. The authors provide a thorough review of existing work on Poisson graphical models and clearly explain the limitations of these models. The proposed models are well-motivated and theoretically sound. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets is convincing, and the results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed models over existing approaches.

One potential weakness of the paper is the critique of the Yang et al. model. While the authors argue that the model is limited because it only allows negative correlations to normalize, they do not provide a clear explanation of why this is a problem in practice. Additionally, the authors' claim that the truncation model is not suitable for domains with low counts may be overstated, as the model may still be useful in certain situations.

The reviewer is also unclear on the term "sparsistent" despite its established usage. The authors should provide a clear definition of this term and explain its relevance to the proposed models.

Minor editorial suggestions include changing "graphical mode distributions" to "model" (p.3) and clarifying "Note that the log..." (p.5) to something like "Note that although the log...".

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, this paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their ideas. The paper is original and novel, and the results are significant and relevant to the field.
