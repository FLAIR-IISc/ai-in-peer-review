This paper addresses the important problem of modeling multivariate count data, a growing need in fields like genomics and spatial statistics. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches – namely, the inability of standard Poisson graphical models to capture negative correlations – and propose three novel modifications: the Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM). A key theoretical contribution is proving that a previously suggested truncation approach is invalid, and providing justifications for their alternatives.

The work builds upon the foundations laid by Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19], extending the framework of graphical models based on univariate exponential families. While this reliance on existing work limits the degree of *fundamental* theoretical novelty, the authors skillfully navigate the constraints imposed by normalizability to propose viable solutions. The TPGM offers a natural truncation strategy, while the QPGM and SPGM represent innovative modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution.  The theoretical justification for needing a quadratic or sub-linear base measure to allow for both positive and negative dependencies is clearly presented.

However, the strength of the theoretical contributions isn't fully matched by the empirical evaluation. The experimental section feels somewhat tailored to favor the proposed models. Synthetic experiments, while useful, are designed in a way that clearly benefits the methods, and the real-data analysis lacks comprehensive comparison. Specifically, omitting a comparison to copula-based alternatives – a prominent approach for modeling multivariate count data [12] – is a significant oversight. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge employing copula models in a somewhat “black-box” manner with potentially suboptimal marginal choices. Utilizing more sophisticated marginal estimations, such as kernel density estimation (KDE), would provide a fairer assessment.

The reported results lack objective quantification. While visual comparisons are provided, a quantitative evaluation, such as testing log-probability on held-out data, would offer stronger evidence of performance.  The claim of achieving identical performance to Glasso is also concerning, suggesting potential issues with the baseline implementation or the experimental setup. A more rigorous statistical comparison is needed to substantiate the claimed improvements.

Despite these concerns, the paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical underpinnings of the proposed models. However, some sections are overly detailed – for example, the bound in Section 3.1 – and could be streamlined.  The key insight presented in Section 3.2, regarding the sub-linear sufficient statistics, would benefit from a more prominent presentation within the main text, potentially through a dedicated figure.  All proofs should be moved to the supplementary material to improve readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and increasingly relevant problem in statistical modeling.
*   Presents theoretically sound and novel extensions to Poisson graphical models.
*   Correctly identifies and disproves a previously proposed approach.
*   Well-written and clearly explains the underlying mathematical concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation is insufficient and potentially biased.
*   Lacks comprehensive comparison to existing alternatives, particularly copula-based methods.
*   Quantitative results are lacking, relying heavily on qualitative observations.
*   Theoretical novelty is incremental, building heavily on prior work by Yang et al. 

**Overall:**

This paper presents theoretically appealing approaches to modeling multivariate count data. However, the experimental evidence is currently insufficient to convincingly demonstrate their practical merit. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after significantly strengthening the empirical evaluation, incorporating a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods, and providing quantitative results to support the claims of improved performance. Addressing the concerns regarding the experimental setup and providing more rigorous statistical analysis would substantially improve the paper's contribution.
