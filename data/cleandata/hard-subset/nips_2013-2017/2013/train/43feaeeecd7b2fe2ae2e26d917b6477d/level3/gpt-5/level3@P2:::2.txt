Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the long-standing mismatch between commonly used undirected graphical models (Gaussian, Ising, multinomial) and multivariate count data. It revisits Poisson graphical models (PGMs) derived from node-conditional Poisson distributions (Besag, 1974; Yang et al., NIPS 2012, arXiv 2013), which are known to admit only non-positive pairwise parameters for normalizability, hence only negative conditional dependencies. The paper makes three contributions toward enabling rich dependencies (including positive): (i) it proves that the widely suggested Winsorized (hard-truncated-at-R) node-conditional Poisson model (Kaiser & Cressie, 1997; Griffith, 2003) cannot yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1); (ii) it proposes and analyzes a different exponential-family-preserving truncation that leads to a proper joint model, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), with finite partition function for any parameters; (iii) it introduces two new Poisson-like families via modifications to the exponential-family form: the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which keeps linear sufficient statistics but uses a quadratic base measure to ensure normalizability with potentially positive edges, and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which retains the Poisson base measure but employs sublinear sufficient statistics to obtain heavier tails and fewer restrictions on pairwise parameters. Structure learning is done by penalized neighborhood selection following the generalized linear model framework of Yang et al. Comparisons are provided to Gaussian Graphical Models (GGM), the nonparanormal, and the original PGM on simulated data and a TCGA miRNA-seq case study.

Relative to prior work: the critique of positive-dependence limitations in auto-Poisson models is well known; the construction of MRFs from univariate exponential families (Yang et al.) provides the scaffolding the paper uses to show normalizability conditions and to derive node-conditional likelihoods amenable to l1-penalized estimation. Alternative multivariate Poisson formulations (sums-of-Poissons; Holgate; Karlis) allow only positive correlations and become intractable with many variables. Copula-based approaches (e.g., Dobra & Lenkoski; Liu et al. for Gaussian copulas) offer a different path to modeling dependence but are indirect for counts.

Strengths
- Correctly identifies and cleanly resolves a long-standing misconception: Theorem 1 is a useful, clarifying negative result about Winsorized conditionals.
- Proposes three concrete families (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that each permit positive and negative conditional dependencies, with clear constructions within the exponential-family GLM graphical model framework.
- Provides normalizability analyses: the necessity of at-least-quadratic base measure for linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) is conceptually insightful; QPGM conditions are stated succinctly; SPGM allows all real pairwise parameters.
- Clear presentation overall; node-conditional likelihoods enable scalable structure learning; practical motivation via sequencing counts is timely.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical novelty is incremental beyond Yang et al.: much of the machinery for deriving joint distributions, node-conditionals, and neighborhood selection is borrowed; the main theoretical contributions are the Winsorization impossibility and the base-measure growth condition.
- Empirical evaluation is not convincing. Synthetic data are largely tailored to favor the proposed models (TPGM-generated data, sums-of-Poissons for positive dependence), with limited exploration of regimes where competitors should excel. Real-data evaluation is qualitative; there is no objective predictive assessment (e.g., held-out log-likelihood, PSIS-LOO, or test pseudo-likelihood).
- Comparisons to copula-based and latent-variable count models are incomplete or superficial. Nonparanormal and SKEPTIC are included, but copula competitors are treated as black boxes with marginal choices that are ill-suited for discrete counts; more appropriate semiparametric copulas with discrete/KDE marginals or Poisson log-normal graphical models would be stronger baselines.
- Identical performance curves to Glasso in some plots are suspicious and suggest implementation or tuning issues for baselines.
- Practical guidance is thin: sensitivity to R (TPGM) and to (R0, R) (SPGM) is not systematically studied; optimization details, runtime, and scalability are only sketched. Section 3.1’s Gaussian variational bound seems unnecessary for the core message and crowds out space for empirical diagnostics.

Quality
- Theory: Statements are plausible and, where sketched, appear technically sound. Please include full proofs for all theorems and propositions in the supplement, and clarify conditions (e.g., domains ensuring Theorem 3).
- Experiments: The study design and metrics are insufficient to substantiate practical benefits. Add rigorous predictive metrics, ablations on hyperparameters (R, R0), and stress tests (zero-inflation, overdispersion).
- Implementation: Clarify preprocessing for sequencing counts (normalization, library size effects), optimization details (step sizes, stopping), and how tuning parameters were selected (beyond stability selection).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Suggestions:
  - Move the key SPGM figure (sublinear sufficient statistics) to the main text with clearer intuition and parameter-setting guidance.
  - Trim Section 3.1 and relegate the Gaussian variational upper bound to appendix unless it is used in estimation.
  - Ensure all proofs and algorithmic details are in the supplement; improve captions and reproducibility details.

Originality
- Moderate. The Winsorization impossibility and the base-measure growth condition are novel and useful. QPGM/SPGM are natural, well-motivated variants rather than radically new architectures.

Significance
- Potentially significant: if validated empirically, these models could become standard tools for multivariate count networks. As presented, the impact is limited by weak evidence of practical superiority and incomplete comparisons.

Specific suggestions
- Add held-out predictive log-likelihood (or test pseudo-likelihood) across methods; include calibration (e.g., probability integral transform for copulas).
- Include stronger baselines: Poisson log-normal graphical models; Gaussian copulas with appropriate discrete/KDE marginals; negative binomial-based MRFs if available.
- Perform sensitivity analyses for R (TPGM) and (R0, R) (SPGM); discuss how to set them in practice.
- Investigate zero-inflation and overdispersion, common in RNA-seq; discuss extensions (e.g., NB variants).
- Resolve the “identical to Glasso” issue; verify implementations and tuning.
- Report computational cost and scalability (p up to several hundred/thousand).

Pros for acceptance
- Clarifies an error in the literature (Winsorization) and provides principled alternatives.
- Conceptually clean constructions that integrate with existing GLM-MRF estimation.
- Timely application domain and potentially broad utility.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evidence is insufficient and somewhat biased; key baselines and predictive metrics are missing.
- Limited practical guidance and sensitivity analyses for new hyperparameters.
- Some theoretical pieces (e.g., bounds) feel tangential relative to the experimental gaps.

Overall recommendation
Promising and theoretically appealing, but the current empirical evaluation does not establish practical merit. I would lean toward rejection or a very weak accept conditional on substantially strengthened experiments, clearer guidance on hyperparameters, inclusion of stronger baselines, and complete proofs. Confidence: medium.
