This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling multivariate count data using Poisson graphical models (PGMs). The authors address a major limitation of existing PGMs, which can only model negative conditional dependencies due to the requirement of normalizability. The proposed approach modifies the Poisson distribution by introducing a quadratic base measure, leading to the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and a sub-linear sufficient statistic, resulting in the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM).

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the limitations of existing PGMs and the need for a more flexible model. The proposed QPGM and SPGM models are technically sound and well-derived. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the properties of these models, including their normalizability and the behavior of their tails.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies, which is a significant improvement over existing PGMs. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their models in learning network structures from simulated data and a real-world dataset.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The QPGM model has a Gaussian-esque thin tail, which may not be desirable in some applications. The SPGM model, on the other hand, has a heavier tail, but its log-partition function does not have a closed-form, which may make it more challenging to work with.

The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of spatial statistics and high-dimensional count methods. The authors seem to misunderstand copulas and misrepresent some of the existing work in the field.

The practical motivation for the paper is also somewhat questionable. The authors claim that sequencing reads are replacing microarrays, but this is not entirely accurate. While it is true that sequencing reads are becoming more popular, microarrays are still widely used in many applications.

The method details are not entirely clear, particularly with regards to the role and selection of constants R and R0. The authors could provide more guidance on how to choose these constants in practice.

Overall, the paper makes a valid and interesting theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models. However, the authors need to provide stronger justification for why practitioners should prefer these models over simpler alternatives, such as Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement models.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling multivariate count data using Poisson graphical models.
* The proposed QPGM and SPGM models are technically sound and well-derived.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their models in learning network structures from simulated data and a real-world dataset.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The QPGM model has a Gaussian-esque thin tail, which may not be desirable in some applications.
* The SPGM model has a heavier tail, but its log-partition function does not have a closed-form, which may make it more challenging to work with.
* The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review, particularly in the area of spatial statistics and high-dimensional count methods.
* The practical motivation for the paper is somewhat questionable.
* The method details are not entirely clear, particularly with regards to the role and selection of constants R and R0.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations mentioned above, particularly with regards to the literature review and method details. They should also provide stronger justification for why practitioners should prefer these models over simpler alternatives.
