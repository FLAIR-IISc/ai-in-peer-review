

# Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical modeling: the lack of flexible undirected graphical models for count-valued data. The authors propose three novel extensions to the Poisson graphical model (PGM), which previously could only capture negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The contributions include a Truncated PGM (TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each designed to overcome the limitations of the original PGM while maintaining the graphical model structure.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2013) on Poisson graphical models. It correctly identifies the key limitation of the original PGM (only negative edge weights) and makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing the necessary conditions for normalizability with both positive and negative dependencies. The authors show that modifying the base measure (QPGM) or sufficient statistics (SPGM) can overcome this constraint, which is a significant theoretical advance.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical contribution and the demonstration of practical applicability. The authors successfully establish conditions for normalizability of their proposed models, which is a non-trivial theoretical contribution. The TPGM and SPGM are shown to outperform Gaussian-based methods on count data in simulation studies and a breast cancer microRNA case study. The demonstration of finding biologically relevant hub miRNAs (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b) that Gaussian models miss is compelling evidence of the practical value of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper has several notable weaknesses. First, the practical motivation is questionable: the authors claim Poisson is commonly used for count data, but in reality, Poisson is often a poor empirical fit for real-world count data (e.g., overdispersion is common). The paper should demonstrate the empirical fit of their models to real data by showing PMF plots of the SPGM on the breast cancer data. The lack of this evidence undermines the practical relevance of the proposed models.

Second, the SPGM's lack of closed-form conditional distributions contradicts the paper's core motivation of using node-conditional specifications. This makes the SPGM less appealing as a practical modeling tool compared to the TPGM and QPGM. The QPGM's Gaussian-esque thin tail is also a drawback, as it may not adequately model heavy-tailed count data common in applications.

Third, the literature review is incomplete and contains misrepresentations. The paper mischaracterizes the use of copulas in [8] and [9] (which do not impose MRF independence in observable space) and misunderstands [11] (log-linear models for contingency tables). The authors also neglect to discuss spatial statistical approaches for count data, which have a rich history in modeling count data with complex dependencies.

Fourth, methodological details are unclear. The role and selection of the constants R and R0 for the TPGM and SPGM require more explanation and practitioner guidance. The paper claims their methods can learn network structure via neighborhood selection, but the details of this procedure are sparse.

**Quality:** The theoretical contributions are sound and novel. The authors correctly prove the limitations of Winsorized Poisson (Theorem 1) and establish the conditions for normalizability (Theorems 3-4). However, the experimental validation is limited to simulations and one case study, with insufficient demonstration of empirical fit to real data.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though some technical details (particularly regarding the SPGM) could be more thoroughly explained. The theoretical arguments are rigorous but could benefit from more intuitive explanations.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and valuable theoretical contribution by showing how to modify Poisson graphical models to allow both positive and negative dependencies. This is distinct from previous work and addresses a genuine limitation in the field.

**Significance:** The significance is somewhat limited by the practical drawbacks noted above. While the models are theoretically interesting, the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why practitioners should prefer these models over simpler alternatives (e.g., Gaussian random fields with Poisson measurement models) or more established methods like non-paranormal approaches. The case study on microRNA data is promising but lacks sufficient detail on how the SPGM outperforms simpler alternatives.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The paper makes a theoretically important contribution to graphical modeling for count data. The proposed models are the first to allow both positive and negative conditional dependencies while maintaining a graphical model structure. The empirical validation on real data is compelling.

*Con:* The practical justification is weak (Poisson is often a poor fit), the SPGM lacks closed-form conditionals, the literature review is incomplete, and the methodological details are insufficient for practitioners. The paper would be significantly strengthened by empirical evidence showing the SPGM fits real count data better than Poisson.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the practical limitations, incomplete literature review, and lack of sufficient empirical justification for the proposed models outweigh the theoretical novelty. The paper would benefit from addressing the concerns above, particularly demonstrating empirical fit to real count data and clarifying the practical advantages over simpler alternatives. The current version does not provide enough evidence to justify the significant theoretical and methodological investment required to use these models.
