This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of constructing undirected graphical models for multivariate count data. The central motivation is that standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), derived from Poisson conditional distributions, are limited to modeling only negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors propose three novel classes of models to overcome this limitation: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper begins by demonstrating a flaw in a previous "Winsorized" Poisson approach, then introduces its TPGM as a valid truncated model. Recognizing the limitations of truncation, the authors then explore more fundamental modifications, leading to the QPGM, which uses a quadratic base measure, and the SPGM, which employs sub-linear sufficient statistics. The work is situated in the context of graphical model construction via conditional specification, following the framework of Besag and subsequent extensions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The development of flexible and tractable graphical models for high-dimensional count data is a well-motivated and important problem, with applications in genomics, text analysis, and other fields. The paper correctly identifies the primary limitation of existing PGMs—their inability to model positive dependencies—and makes a concerted effort to solve it.

2.  **Novel Model Constructions:** The paper presents three distinct and novel approaches (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) to achieve its goal. This exploration of the model design space is commendable. The theoretical contribution, particularly the analysis of why truncation must be handled carefully and the investigation into the relationship between sufficient statistics, base measures, and normalizability, is interesting and valid.

3.  **Tractable Estimation:** A major advantage of the proposed framework is that it leads to relatively straightforward estimation procedures. By leveraging the conditional specification, the authors can use neighborhood selection based on penalized pseudo-likelihood, which is a computationally efficient method for learning graph structures in high dimensions.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Model-Specific Limitations:**
    *   **QPGM:** The authors rightly note that the quadratic base measure of the QPGM results in a model with "Gaussian-esque thin tails." This is a significant drawback for count data, which is often characterized by overdispersion and heavier-than-Gaussian tails. This similarity to the Gaussian case begs the question of why a practitioner should prefer this more complex model over a standard Gaussian Graphical Model applied to transformed data.
    *   **SPGM:** While the SPGM is designed to address the tail-weight issue, its construction introduces a new problem. The sub-linear sufficient statistic results in a node-conditional log-partition function that lacks a closed form. This undermines the elegance and primary motivation of the conditional specification approach, which typically relies on simple, tractable conditional distributions. It also makes the model feel less "Poisson-like," as the simple exponential form of the Poisson PMF's partition function is lost.

2.  **Incomplete Literature Review and Contextualization:**
    *   The paper's review of alternative methods for modeling multivariate count data is light. The dismissal of copula-based methods as "indirect" is a mischaracterization. For instance, works like the semi-parametric Gaussian copula graphical model [9, 10] provide a powerful, well-studied framework that does not impose conditional independence assumptions on the observed count variables, but rather on latent variables. These methods are direct competitors and should be discussed more thoroughly.
    *   The paper neglects a large body of work in spatial statistics and other fields that use latent variable models (e.g., a latent Gaussian random field where observations are conditionally Poisson). Such models are a very common and simple way to induce arbitrary correlation structures on count data. A stronger case needs to be made for why the proposed direct modeling approach is superior to these widely used latent variable methods.
    *   The reference to log-linear models [11] as unsuitable for high dimensions may be an oversimplification, as modern work has explored sparse versions of these models.

3.  **Practical Motivation and Guidance:**
    *   The paper's motivation rests on the Poisson distribution, but it is well-known that empirical count data often deviates significantly from a Poisson distribution (e.g., exhibiting overdispersion). The authors should provide more evidence that their proposed models are a good empirical fit. For the breast cancer data, for example, it would be highly informative to see plots of the marginal distributions and compare them to the fitted PMFs of the proposed models.
    *   The TPGM and SPGM models introduce crucial hyperparameters (R and R0). The paper provides no guidance on how these should be selected in practice. This is a critical omission that limits the usability of the methods. Should R be set to the maximum observed count, as done in the experiments? What are the implications of this choice? How sensitive are the results to R0?

4.  **Clarity and Minor Issues:**
    *   The claim that "sequencing reads...are replacing microarrays" is supported by a reference [22] from 2008. While true, a more contemporary citation would strengthen this statement, as the landscape has evolved considerably since then.
    *   The experimental comparison is somewhat limited. The data is generated from TPGM or a sum-of-Poissons model, which may favor the proposed methods. Including data generated from a latent Gaussian model would provide a more challenging and informative test case.

**Summary Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The theoretical contributions are sound. The derivation of the models and the proof that the Winsorized approach is invalid are solid. However, the practical justification and experimental validation are not as strong. The work feels more like an exploration of model specification than a complete demonstration of a new, superior tool for data analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the model derivations are easy to follow. The main weakness in clarity concerns the practical implementation details, specifically the lack of guidance on hyperparameter selection for R and R0.
*   **Originality:** The proposed model classes (QPGM, SPGM) are novel constructions for count-based graphical models. The key contribution of enabling both positive and negative dependencies in a PGM-like framework is original. However, the originality is slightly tempered by an incomplete engagement with established alternative frameworks like latent variable and copula models.
*   **Significance:** The problem is highly significant. The proposed solutions offer a new direction for modeling multivariate count data. However, their practical impact is currently uncertain. For practitioners, it is not yet clear whether these more complex models offer a compelling advantage over simpler, more established methods like applying Gaussian graphical models to transformed data or using latent variable models. The paper needs to build a stronger bridge between its interesting theoretical ideas and practical data analysis problems.
