This paper addresses the long-standing and challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions for count data that respect the Markov properties of an undirected graph. The authors correctly identify a major limitation of the classic Poisson graphical model (PGM) derived from Besag's auto-models: its inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints on an infinite domain. The main contribution is the proposal of three new models to overcome this limitation: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). Each model modifies the standard Poisson distribution in a different way—by truncating the domain, altering the base measure, or changing the sufficient statistic—to allow for a rich dependency structure. The authors demonstrate that the graph structure for these models can be learned via neighborhood selection and apply their methods to simulated data and a microRNA sequencing dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's primary strength lies in its direct and novel attack on a well-known, fundamental limitation of PGMs. Moving beyond the restrictive negative-only dependency space is a significant theoretical step. The constructions are principled, stemming from a careful analysis of the normalizability conditions that plague the original PGM.
2.  **Enabling Practical Estimation:** The proposed models are constructed within a framework that allows for tractable node-conditional likelihoods. This is a crucial practical advantage, as it enables the use of well-established and scalable estimation techniques like penalized neighborhood selection for learning the graph structure in high dimensions.
3.  **Correction of Prior Work:** The authors provide a valuable service by proving that the Winsorized Poisson conditional specification proposed by Kaiser and Cressie [20] does not lead to a valid joint distribution, correcting a misconception in the literature.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Conceptual and Practical Issues with Proposed Models:**
    *   **QPGM:** By adopting a quadratic base measure, the QPGM becomes "Gaussian-esque," possessing thin tails. This raises the question of its utility for count data, which often exhibits heavier tails than a Gaussian. The model may lose the very properties that motivate moving beyond Gaussian models in the first place.
    *   **SPGM:** The SPGM's primary weakness is its lack of a closed-form conditional distribution, as the log-partition function is an infinite sum. This undermines one of the key motivations for the conditional specification framework of Besag, which is built on the simplicity and tractability of the full conditionals. If the conditionals are intractable, the model's connection to the "Poisson" family becomes tenuous and its practical appeal diminishes significantly. The authors rely on a variational upper bound, but this is a workaround for a fundamental model deficiency.

2.  **Unclear Methodological Details and Practical Guidance:** The paper lacks crucial details for practitioners. The constants R (in TPGM and SPGM) and R0 (in SPGM) are central to the model definitions, yet there is no discussion of how they should be selected in practice. Are they chosen based on the data (e.g., max value), cross-validation, or some other principle? This omission makes the methods difficult to reproduce and apply responsibly.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Motivation and Justification:** The practical motivation for these specific models is not well-established. It is widely known that the Poisson distribution is often a poor fit for real-world count data (e.g., RNA-seq data is famously overdispersed). The authors should provide stronger evidence for their models' utility. For example:
    *   Show plots of the actual marginal distributions from the breast cancer data to demonstrate the need for a non-Poisson model.
    *   Plot the probability mass function (PMF) of the proposed SPGM for different parameter settings to give readers an intuition for its shape and tail behavior. Without this, it is hard to judge if it is a useful distribution family.

4.  **Inadequate Literature Review and Positioning:** The literature review is surprisingly light and contains inaccuracies.
    *   The discussion of copulas is superficial. The authors seem to misunderstand that copula-based approaches (e.g., the Gaussian copula with Poisson marginals) are a major competing methodology. These models do not impose MRF independence assumptions on the *observed* counts but rather on a latent Gaussian variable, which is a fundamentally different and powerful approach that should be discussed and compared against.
    *   Reference [8] (Dobra & Lenkoski) is mischaracterized; it uses a Gaussian copula, which is a very relevant alternative the authors should engage with. The discussion of [11] (Lauritzen) may also be imprecise.
    *   The paper entirely neglects the vast literature in spatial statistics and econometrics on modeling correlated count data (e.g., spatial autoregressive models for counts, integer-valued GARCH models, generalized linear mixed models), many of which can be adapted to a graphical model setting.

5.  **Need for Stronger Justification over Alternatives:** The authors do not make a compelling case for why a practitioner should prefer their models over simpler, more flexible, and well-understood hierarchical models. For example, a latent Gaussian random field model where observations are conditionally Poisson given the latent variables (a log-Gaussian Cox process on a graph) is a standard and powerful approach. This method naturally models both positive and negative dependence and overdispersion. The paper needs to clearly articulate the advantages of its proposed direct-specification models over such latent variable approaches.

**Minor Point:** The citation for sequencing reads replacing microarrays ([22] from 2008) is dated. A more recent reference would strengthen the claim.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting and valid approach to extending Poisson graphical models to handle both positive and negative dependencies. The core idea is original and addresses an important open problem. However, the manuscript suffers from significant weaknesses that limit its current impact. The proposed models have conceptual drawbacks (thin tails in QPGM, intractable conditionals in SPGM), the paper lacks critical details for practical application, the empirical motivation is weak, and the positioning with respect to existing literature is poor. The work is a good starting point, but a much stronger justification and more thorough comparison with relevant alternatives are needed before these models can be recommended for widespread use.
