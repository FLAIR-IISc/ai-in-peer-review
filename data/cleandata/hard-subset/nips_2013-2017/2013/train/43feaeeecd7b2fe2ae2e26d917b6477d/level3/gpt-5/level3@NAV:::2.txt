This paper addresses the long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs), namely the negative-edge constraint required for normalizability. The authors (i) prove that the Winsorized truncation advocated by Kaiser and Cressie and others cannot yield a consistent joint distribution, (ii) introduce an exponential-family truncation that leads to a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM), and (iii) propose two further families—QPGM and SPGM—by altering the exponential-family base measure and sufficient statistics. The work is grounded in the Besag/Yang et al. framework for constructing graphical models from node-conditionals and is positioned among alternatives such as multivariate Poisson constructions and copula-based approaches.

Quality: Theoretical contributions are careful and, to my reading, correct. The impossibility result for Winsorization is useful, and the TPGM truncation is natural and well justified. QPGM and SPGM are appealing, with clear normalizability conditions; the insight that linear sufficient statistics necessitate at least quadratic base measures to allow both signs of dependence is interesting. However, much of the machinery follows directly from Yang et al., limiting novelty. No sparsistency or computational guarantees beyond variational bounds are provided. The empirical evaluation is the weakest aspect: synthetic data are tailored to the proposed models; real-data comparisons omit competitive copula-based methods for counts (e.g., Gaussian/Poisson-lognormal copulas with appropriate marginals). Reported results are qualitative; objective metrics (held-out/test log-probability, calibration, posterior predictive checks) are needed. The identical performance to Glasso is suspicious and suggests baseline or implementation issues. Copula competitors appear used in a black-box way with inappropriate marginals; please use better marginals (e.g., empirical/KDE or discrete CDF/jittered ranks) and include Poisson log-normal and vine copulas.

Clarity: Generally well written. Please move all proofs to the supplement. Section 3.1 is overly detailed; the Gaussian upper bound seems unnecessary for the main narrative. Bring the key figure for SPGM (currently in the appendix) into the main text and explain it more clearly.

Originality: Moderate. The TPGM fix and the QPGM/SPGM design choices are novel within this line of work, but rely heavily on existing exponential-family GM theory.

Significance: Potentially high for practitioners modeling counts, but current evidence does not yet establish practical merit.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear identification of a real limitation; clean impossibility result
- Well-motivated TPGM; conceptually novel QPGM/SPGM constructions
- Solid use of exponential-family GM principles

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical novelty beyond prior frameworks
- Insufficient, biased, and largely qualitative experiments
- Missing/weak copula and Poisson-lognormal baselines; suspicious Glasso parity

Overall: Interesting and theoretically appealing, but I cannot endorse acceptance without substantially stronger and more objective empirical validation.
