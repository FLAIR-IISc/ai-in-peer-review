This paper addresses the important problem of constructing flexible undirected graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors begin by correctly identifying a major limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), which, due to normalizability constraints over an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. The paper's primary contributions are threefold: first, it proves that a previously proposed "Winsorized" truncation approach is invalid for constructing a valid joint distribution. Second, it proposes a valid alternative, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), based on a more natural truncation. Third, to overcome the limitations of truncation, it introduces two novel model classes, the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the underlying exponential family, respectively, to allow for both positive and negative dependencies. The work builds directly on the theoretical framework of Yang et al. [18, 19] for constructing graphical models from specified node-conditional distributions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** Modeling dependencies in multivariate count data is a crucial problem in many modern applications (e.g., genomics, social sciences), and the limitations of existing methods are well-articulated.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution by formally proving the invalidity of the Winsorized Poisson approach suggested by Kaiser and Cressie [20]. This clarifies a misconception in the literature.
3.  **Novel Model Specification:** The proposed TPGM is a natural and well-justified fix for the truncation issue. More impressively, the QPGM and SPGM models represent a creative and more fundamental approach to the problem by altering the core components of the exponential family distribution. This conceptual novelty is a significant strength.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, with the motivations and derivations of the new models being clear and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its insufficient experimental validation, which fails to establish the practical merit of the proposed models.

1.  **Limited Theoretical Novelty:** While the univariate distributions are new, the theoretical framework for constructing the joint graphical model from these conditionals is taken directly from Yang et al. [18, 19]. The novelty is thus concentrated in model specification rather than in the theory of graphical model estimation or inference.
2.  **Biased and Incomplete Experiments:** The experimental evaluation appears designed to favor the proposed methods.
    *   The synthetic data is generated from either the TPGM distribution itself or from a sums-of-Poissons model (which only has positive dependencies), creating scenarios where the proposed models have a strong built-in advantage. A more convincing evaluation would test robustness on data from a wider variety of generative processes.
    *   The comparison to baseline methods is inadequate. The experiments omit a crucial class of competitors: copula-based models with appropriate count-data marginals. While the non-paranormal approach (a Gaussian copula) is included, it is used in a black-box fashion that is ill-suited for discrete count data. A stronger comparison would involve using copulas with better-fitted marginals, such as those from kernel density estimation on jittered data or from parametric families like the Negative Binomial.
3.  **Lack of Objective Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation relies on ROC curves for structure recovery and qualitative visualizations of learned networks. For probabilistic models, it is essential to report quantitative metrics of model fit, such as the average test log-probability on held-out data. Without this, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed models provide a better description of the data distribution than the baselines. The claims of superiority are therefore anecdotal rather than rigorously demonstrated.
4.  **Suspicious Baseline Performance:** In the real-data example, the comparison is made to a standard Gaussian Graphical Model (GGM) and the original PGM. The GGM is known to be a poor choice for count data, and the PGM is known to be limited to negative dependencies. Finding more "biologically important hubs" than these flawed baselines is not a surprising or particularly strong result. The near-identical performance of TPGM and SPGM in some simulations also raises questions about whether the experiments are nuanced enough to differentiate between the proposed methods.
5.  **Presentation:**
    *   The paper would be more focused if all proofs were moved to the supplementary material.
    *   Section 3.1 is overly detailed regarding the variational bound for the QPGM, which distracts from the main model definition.
    *   The sub-linear sufficient statistic is the core idea of the SPGM, yet its visualization (Figure 3) is relegated to the appendix. This figure is critical for understanding the model and should be presented and explained in the main text of Section 3.2.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper introduces several theoretically appealing and well-motivated models for an important problem. The critique of prior art and the novel model designs (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are commendable contributions. However, the work is let down by a weak and inconclusive experimental section. The evidence provided is insufficient to establish that these new models offer a tangible practical advantage over properly implemented existing methods. While the ideas are promising, the paper in its current form feels incomplete, lacking the rigorous empirical validation necessary to demonstrate its significance to the NIPS community.
