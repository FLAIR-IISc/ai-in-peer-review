- Addresses modeling multivariate count data with graphical models and the negative-dependency limitation of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs).  
- Proposes three extensions—Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—to allow positive and negative dependencies.  
- Core technical idea: modify the Poisson base measure and/or sufficient statistics to expand representational flexibility.  
- Builds on prior graphical-model and multivariate-count literature (Besag, Lauritzen, Arnold et al.).  
- TPGM offered as a practical starting point; QPGM and SPGM are more fundamental departures enabling positive correlations.  
- QPGM connects to Gaussian Graphical Models via variational bounds, providing useful intuition.  
- QPGM produces marginally thinner tails than desirable for many real-world count datasets.  
- SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining its conditional-specification motivation and “Poisson” characterization.  
- Practical motivation and empirical validation are weak—only microRNA-sequencing data shown; more real-data examples needed.  
- Suggestion to plot SPGM PMF against real datasets (e.g., breast cancer) to demonstrate fit.  
- Parameters R (TPGM) and R0 (SPGM) are poorly explained; need guidance on selection and impact.  
- Literature review is incomplete and contains misunderstandings of copulas and misrepresentations of cited works ([8], [11]).  
- Neglects relevant areas such as spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count methods as alternative approaches.  
- Claim that sequencing reads are replacing microarrays is outdated and requires an updated reference.  
- Arguments for acceptance: valid theoretical contribution, clear identification of limitations, and exploration of multiple solutions.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak empirical support, SPGM’s lack of closed-form conditionals, unclear parameter guidance, and incomplete/misleading literature review.  
- Overall recommendation: reject in current form but consider resubmission after substantial revisions addressing empirical validation, technical shortcomings, and literature issues.
