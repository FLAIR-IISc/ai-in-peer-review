Summary
This paper addresses the important problem of constructing multivariate graphical models for count data. The standard Poisson graphical model (PGM) derived from Poisson node-conditionals (Besag; Yang et al.) is known to admit only non-positive pairwise parameters due to normalizability, which precludes positive conditional dependencies. The paper first revisits truncation and proves that the “Winsorized” truncation approach previously suggested in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot yield a valid joint MRF via the Yang et al. construction. It then proposes three alternatives: (i) TPGM, an exponential-family-consistent truncation that distributes the tail mass across the truncated support; (ii) QPGM, which keeps linear sufficient statistics but replaces the base measure with a quadratic function to ensure normalizability with both positive and negative dependencies; and (iii) SPGM, which keeps the Poisson base measure but uses sublinear sufficient statistics to permit rich dependencies while avoiding Gaussian-like thin tails. The paper discusses neighborhood selection for structure learning and provides synthetic experiments and a miRNA case study.

Relation to prior work is appropriate: Besag’s auto-models and Yang et al.’s univariate exponential-family-to-MRF construction are central, and the paper positions its truncation and base-measure/sufficient-statistic modifications against those. It also cites alternative approaches (copulas, sums of Poissons, spatial hierarchical models), though experimental comparisons are limited.

Strengths
- Importance: Modeling multivariate counts is practically relevant across genomics, epidemiology, recommendation, and text.
- Correct negative result: The proof that Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals cannot yield a consistent joint distribution is valuable and clarifies a misconception in the literature.
- TPGM: The proposed exponential-family truncation is natural, principled, and restores the ability to model positive and negative conditional dependencies on a bounded support.
- QPGM/SPGM: It is novel and insightful to shift focus to the exponential-family components—showing that linear sufficient statistics require at least quadratic base measures for normalizability with mixed-sign edges (Theorem 3), and proposing the sublinear sufficient statistic SPGM to achieve heavier tails and an unconstrained parameter space (Theorem 4).
- Writing: Generally clear and well organized; the model constructions and the normalizability logic are easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical novelty: Much of the framework and several arguments follow directly from Yang et al.’s exponential-family graphical model theory; the main new theoretical elements are the invalidity of Winsorization, the normalizability condition (quadratic base measure), and the two modified families. The variational bound in Section 3.1 adds little insight beyond intuition.
- Experiments are weak and appear biased:
  - Synthetic setups are tailored to favor the proposed methods (e.g., data generated from TPGM, edge patterns benefitting truncation), with no scenarios where competitors’ assumptions hold.
  - Real-data evaluation is qualitative/anecdotal (graphs and hubs) without objective metrics such as held-out/test log probability, per-variable predictive log-likelihood, or posterior predictive checks.
  - Copula-based competitors are not properly represented. Using copulas in a black-box way with poor marginal choices can severely handicap them; for fairness, employ nonparametric marginals (e.g., smoothed ECDF/rank likelihood, KDE on jittered counts, or appropriate discrete/overdispersed marginals such as NB) and report sensitivity.
  - The reported “identical performance to Glasso” raises red flags about baselines, tuning, or implementation; e.g., rank-based NPN/SKEPTIC should not collapse to GGM unless configured suboptimally.
- Missing comparisons: No multivariate Poisson-lognormal or other latent-variable count models; no discrete copula models with appropriate margins.
- Presentation details:
  - Section 3.1 is overly detailed; the Gaussian variational upper bound is unnecessary in the main text and could move to the supplement. The key takeaway is the quadratic base-measure requirement.
  - The sublinear sufficient statistic for SPGM (currently Figure 3, appendix) should be in the main text with more explanation of R0, R, and their effect on tails and dependence.
- Reproducibility: Some proofs appear deferred; all proofs should be included in the supplementary. Sensitivity analyses for R (TPGM) and (R0, R) (SPGM) are missing.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the negative result on Winsorization and normalizability arguments are carefully done. However, empirical support is insufficient and sometimes questionable (baseline configuration, lack of quantitative metrics). Claims of practical superiority are not yet substantiated.

Clarity
Generally well written. Suggestions:
- Move the lengthy bound in 3.1 to the supplement and emphasize the minimal condition (C(z) = Ω(z^2)).
- Bring and explain the SPGM sufficient statistic figure into the main text.
- Provide algorithmic details (optimization, convergence checks), and full proof details in the supplement.

Originality
Moderate. The core idea—altering the base measure/sufficient statistics within the Yang et al. framework—is neat, and the invalidation of Winsorization is useful. But the construction paradigm and learning strategy are largely inherited.

Significance
Potentially significant if validated: enabling positive and negative dependencies for counts within an MRF framework is valuable. The current experimental evidence does not yet establish practical merit relative to strong alternatives.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add objective evaluations: held-out test log-probability, predictive log-likelihood per variable, and calibration checks.
- Strengthen baselines:
  - Gaussian copula models with proper nonparametric/discrete-aware marginals (rank likelihood, smoothed ECDF, KDE with jitter, NB).
  - Poisson-lognormal (multivariate) and other latent-variable count models.
- Audit baselines where performance matches Glasso; verify tuning, rank transforms, and stability-selection settings.
- Sensitivity analyses for R (TPGM) and (R0, R) (SPGM).
- Include proofs for all theorems in the supplement and release code.

Pros
- Addresses an important gap (positive dependencies for counts).
- Clear negative result on Winsorization.
- Principled and interpretable constructions (TPGM/QPGM/SPGM).
- Good exposition of normalizability and parameter-space implications.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is weak and potentially biased.
- Limited theoretical novelty beyond the framework of Yang et al.
- Missing strong and fairly-configured baselines (especially copulas with suitable marginals).
- Some presentation choices (Section 3.1) distract from the key insights.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The modeling ideas are appealing and could be impactful, but the empirical evidence—currently qualitative and with questionable baselines—is insufficient to establish practical merit. Strengthening the experimental section with fair, quantitative comparisons could change this assessment.
