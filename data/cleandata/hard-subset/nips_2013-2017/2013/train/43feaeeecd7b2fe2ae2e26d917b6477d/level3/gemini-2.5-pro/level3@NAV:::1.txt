This paper addresses a key limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for count data, building upon the framework of Yang et al. (2012, 2013). The authors correctly identify that the standard PGM is restricted to modeling only negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints over an infinite domain. To overcome this, the paper proposes three novel and distinct models: a Truncated PGM (TPGM) based on a new, valid truncation scheme; a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) which modifies the base measure to be quadratic (proven necessary under certain conditions); and a Sub-linear PGM (SPGM) which uses sub-linear sufficient statistics to allow for heavier tails. The paper provides theoretical justification for each model and demonstrates their utility in recovering network structures from both synthetic data and real-world microRNA sequencing data.

The paper’s strengths are numerous.
- **Significance:** It tackles a well-known, important, and highly practical problem in graphical models for count data, significantly advancing the state of the art by enabling the modeling of both positive and negative dependencies.
- **Originality:** The work is highly original, not only in proposing three distinct and well-motivated solutions but also in proving that a previously suggested "Winsorized" approach is invalid.
- **Quality:** The technical contributions are sound, and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and a solid experimental evaluation against relevant baselines. The paper is a complete and valuable piece of work.
- **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized, clearly motivating the problem and logically progressing through each proposed solution.

Despite the high quality, there are a few points for the authors to consider. The paper’s critique of its own TPGM and QPGM models seems slightly overstated. The authors claim the TPGM has "big negatives" due to its bounded domain, but for many applications involving low counts, this model could be a pragmatic and efficient choice. Similarly, the "Gaussian-esque thin tail" of the QPGM is presented as a drawback, but this could be an appropriate modeling assumption in certain domains. These are characteristics, not necessarily universal flaws. Furthermore, while the term "sparsistent" is established, a brief definition would improve clarity for a broader audience.

Overall, this is a strong, original, and significant contribution to the field that is likely to be widely used and built upon. I strongly recommend acceptance.

- Page 3: "graphical mode distributions" should likely be "graphical model distributions".
- Page 5: The sentence beginning "Note that although the log..." could be clarified, perhaps to "Note that although the log-partition function A(θ) is usually intractable...".
