

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints in their infinite domain. Building on Yang et al.'s foundational work, the authors propose three novel approaches to enable both positive and negative dependencies: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The TPGM uses a novel truncation method that preserves exponential family properties, while QPGM and SPGM modify the base measure and sufficient statistics to achieve normalizability with flexible dependency structures.

The paper excels in theoretical rigor, with clear proofs of normalizability conditions and model properties. The experimental evaluation on synthetic data and breast cancer microRNA sequencing demonstrates practical utility, showing superior performance to Gaussian-based methods for count data. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate connections to prior work in graphical models.

Strengths include strong theoretical contributions that resolve a fundamental limitation in count data modeling, significant empirical validation, and clear exposition. The models address an important gap in the literature for applications like genomic sequencing where count data are prevalent.

However, I have three concerns. First, the claim that TPGM's "big negatives" are problematic seems overstated; this model may be particularly suitable for domains with low counts, which are common in count data applications. Second, the critique of QPGM's Gaussian-like thin tails appears unnecessarily dismissive—such tail behavior could be appropriate in certain settings. Third, the term "sparsistent" remains unclear despite its established usage in the field.

Minor editorial improvements are needed: "graphical mode distributions" should be corrected to "model" on page 3, and the note on page 5 should be clarified to "Note that although the log...".

Overall, this is a high-quality, original contribution with significant potential impact for practitioners working with count data. The theoretical advances are sound, and the empirical results are compelling. While the concerns about model interpretations are valid, they don't undermine the paper's substantial contributions. I recommend acceptance.
