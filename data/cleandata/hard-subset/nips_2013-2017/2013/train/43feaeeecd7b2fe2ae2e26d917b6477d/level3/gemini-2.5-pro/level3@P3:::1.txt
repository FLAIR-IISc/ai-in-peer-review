This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) for modeling joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors correctly identify that the standard PGM, as formulated by Yang et al. building on Besag's work, can only capture negative conditional dependencies due to the normalizability constraint over an infinite domain. To overcome this restriction and allow for richer dependence structures, the paper introduces three novel and well-motivated models. First, the authors analyze truncation-based approaches, demonstrating that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional is inconsistent, and instead propose a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) that supports both positive and negative dependencies on a finite domain. Recognizing the limitations of truncation, they then propose two models for infinite domains: the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which modifies the base measure to be quadratic (a condition they prove is necessary for linear statistics), and the Sub-linear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear. The paper provides theoretical justification for the validity and normalizability of these models and demonstrates their effectiveness in learning network structure from both synthetic and real-world microRNA sequencing data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem addressed is important, as count data is prevalent in many machine learning applications (e.g., genomics, user ratings), and existing graphical models are often ill-suited. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive dependencies is a major practical drawback. The three proposed models are novel, creative, and well-motivated solutions to this long-standing problem. The analysis correcting the prior work of Kaiser and Cressie is a particularly valuable contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical contributions, including the proof of inconsistency for the Winsorized approach (Theorem 1), the necessity of a quadratic base measure for linear statistics (Theorem 3), and the proofs of validity for the proposed TPGM and SPGM (Theorems 2 and 4), provide a strong foundation for the new models. The experimental evaluation is convincing, showing that the proposed models outperform standard baselines like Gaussian graphical models on count data and successfully identify biologically relevant structures in a real-world application.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the limitations of the existing PGM, logically motivate each of their proposed solutions, and present their results in an organized manner.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While this is an excellent paper, there are a few points where the arguments could be nuanced or the presentation improved.

*   The authors' critique of their own proposed models sometimes feels overly strong. For instance, the drawbacks listed for the TPGM may not be "big negatives" in all contexts. In domains where counts are naturally low or have a known upper bound, the TPGM seems like a very practical and effective model. The fixed truncation level `R` is a modeling choice, not necessarily a fundamental flaw.
*   Similarly, the critique of the QPGM's "Gaussian-esque thin tail" as a drawback could be reframed. In many real-world settings, a distribution with thin tails might be a perfectly appropriate, or even desirable, modeling assumption. It would be beneficial for the authors to acknowledge that this property is a characteristic of the model that may be suitable for certain types of data.
*   The term "sparsistent" is used in the experimental section without a brief definition or citation for readers who may not be familiar with it. While it is an established term in the high-dimensional statistics literature, a quick clarification would improve readability for a broader audience.
*   There are a couple of minor editorial points that would improve the manuscript:
    *   On page 3, in the first paragraph of Section 3, "graphical mode distributions" should likely be "graphical model distributions".
    *   On page 5, in the last sentence of the first paragraph of Section 3, the sentence beginning "Note that although the log partition function..." is slightly awkward. Rewording it to something like, "Note that although the log-partition function A(θ) is usually computationally intractable, the log-partition function D̄(·) of its node-conditional distribution..." would improve clarity.

In summary, this is a well-written and technically solid paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the modeling of multivariate count data. The proposed models elegantly solve a major deficiency in existing Poisson graphical models, and the work is likely to be highly influential for both researchers and practitioners. The weaknesses are minor and can be easily addressed.
