This paper addresses the important problem of constructing multivariate count distributions, which is a crucial task in many applications, including genomics, neuroimaging, and spatial statistics. The authors correctly identify the limitations of current approaches, such as the Poisson graphical model, which can only capture negative dependencies between variables. They propose three novel approaches, namely the Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM), which can capture both positive and negative dependencies between count-valued variables.

The TPGM is a natural and well-justified approach, which truncates the Poisson distribution to a finite domain. The QPGM and SPGM are more innovative, as they alter the exponential-family base measure and sufficient statistics. The theory behind these models is well-established, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of the results.

However, the experimental evaluation of the proposed models is somewhat disappointing. The synthetic experiments are tailored to the methods, and the real-data comparisons omit copula-based alternatives. The reported results are qualitative and anecdotal, and an objective evaluation, such as test log-probability, is needed to establish the practical merit of the proposed models. Furthermore, the copula competitors are used in an overly black-box way, with inappropriate marginal choices, and better marginals, such as kernel density estimation (KDE), should be used.

The paper is generally well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of the results. However, some sections, such as Section 3.1, are overly detailed, and a bound seems unnecessary. The key figure for Section 3.2 should be presented and better explained in the main text.

In conclusion, the proposed approaches are theoretically appealing, but the experimental evidence is insufficient to establish their practical merit. Further experiments and evaluations are needed to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed models in real-world applications.
