This paper addresses the important problem of modeling multivariate count data, a task for which existing graphical model approaches are ill-suited. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous work, particularly the restrictive nature of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) which can only capture negative dependencies. They demonstrate that a previously proposed truncation approach to overcome this limitation is, in fact, invalid, and then propose three novel alternatives – the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – which aim to model both positive and negative dependencies.

The paper builds heavily on the theoretical framework established in prior work, notably Yang et al. [18, 19], extending it by modifying the underlying Poisson distribution. The TPGM offers a natural truncation strategy, while the QPGM and SPGM represent more fundamental departures, altering the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family. The theoretical derivations appear sound and the connection to existing literature is clearly established. However, the theoretical novelty is somewhat limited, largely consisting of extensions to the framework presented in Yang et al. Consequently, the practical merit hinges on demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods.

The experimental evaluation, unfortunately, falls short of convincingly establishing such an advantage. The synthetic experiments appear tailored to favor the proposed models, and the comparisons with competing methods are incomplete. Specifically, the omission of copula-based alternatives – a common approach to modeling multivariate count data – is a significant weakness. Moreover, the use of copula methods appears somewhat rudimentary, with marginal choices potentially impacting performance. A more thorough comparison employing, for instance, kernel density estimation for marginals, would be more informative.

The reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. While the authors demonstrate performance gains over the original PGM, a more objective evaluation using metrics such as test log-probability or area under the ROC curve (as presented for the simulation study) is needed across all experiments. The claim of performance comparable to Glasso, a well-established method, is also suspect and warrants further investigation. It raises concerns about potential issues with the baseline implementation or the experimental setup.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. However, some sections, notably 3.1, contain excessive detail, and proofs should be relegated to the supplementary material. The key illustration for Section 3.2, depicting the sublinear statistic, should be presented within the main text for clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in statistical modeling.
*   Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the limitations of existing approaches and proposes novel solutions.
*   The TPGM offers a natural and well-justified truncation strategy.
*   The QPGM and SPGM introduce innovative modifications to the Poisson distribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited theoretical novelty beyond extending the work of Yang et al.
*   Experimental evaluation is insufficient to establish practical merit.
*   Comparisons with competing methods are incomplete and potentially biased.
*   Results are largely qualitative, lacking objective evaluation metrics.
*   The implementation of competing methods appears rudimentary.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents theoretically sound and potentially valuable extensions to existing graphical model frameworks.
*   The proposed models offer a novel approach to modeling multivariate count data with both positive and negative dependencies.
*   The paper identifies a flaw in a previously proposed solution (Winsorized Poisson truncation).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The experimental validation is weak and fails to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The lack of a thorough comparison with relevant alternatives (e.g., copula-based models) limits the impact of the work.
*   The reliance on qualitative results and the absence of objective evaluation metrics raise concerns about the robustness of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents theoretically appealing approaches to modeling multivariate count data. However, the experimental evidence is insufficient to establish their practical merit. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the lack of convincing empirical validation makes it difficult to recommend acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to strengthen the experimental evaluation and address the concerns raised above. Specifically, the authors should include a more comprehensive comparison with relevant competing methods, utilize objective evaluation metrics, and provide a more rigorous assessment of the performance of their proposed models on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
