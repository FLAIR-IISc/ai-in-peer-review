- Addresses the Poisson graphical model limitation requiring negative-edge constraints for normalizability.
- Proves Winsorized truncation cannot produce a consistent joint distribution.
- Introduces an exponential-family truncation that yields a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM).
- Proposes two additional families, QPGM and SPGM, by modifying the exponential-family base measure and sufficient statistics.
- Builds on the Besag/Yang et al. node-conditional framework and situates work among multivariate Poisson and copula-based approaches.
- Theoretical contributions are careful and appear correct.
- The impossibility result for Winsorization is useful and the TPGM truncation is natural and well justified.
- QPGM and SPGM are appealing with clear normalizability conditions.
- Insight: linear sufficient statistics require at least quadratic base measures to permit both positive and negative dependence.
- Much of the technical machinery follows from Yang et al., limiting perceived novelty.
- Lacks sparsistency and computational guarantees beyond variational bounds.
- Empirical evaluation is weak: synthetic data tailored to proposed models and real-data comparisons omit strong copula-based baselines.
- Results are largely qualitative; objective metrics (held-out log-probability, calibration, posterior predictive checks) are needed.
- Identical performance to Glasso is suspicious, suggesting possible baseline or implementation issues.
- Copula competitors appear used with inappropriate marginals; recommend using better marginals and including Poisson log-normal and vine copulas.
- Clarity: generally well written; move proofs to supplement, shorten Section 3.1, and move/explain the SPGM figure into the main text.
- Originality: moderate—novel fixes and constructions but heavily reliant on existing exponential-family GM theory.
- Significance: potentially high for count modeling, but practical merit not yet demonstrated.
- Pros: identifies real limitation with a clean impossibility result; well-motivated TPGM; solid use of exponential-family GM principles.
- Cons: limited theoretical novelty, insufficient and potentially biased experiments, missing/weak copula and Poisson-lognormal baselines.
- Overall: interesting and theoretically appealing, but not ready for acceptance without substantially stronger, objective empirical validation.
