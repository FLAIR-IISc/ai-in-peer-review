- Addresses Poisson graphical model limitation: Yang et al. normalization requires nonpositive edges, disallowing positive conditional dependencies.  
- Shows Winsorized node-conditionals (Kaiser–Cressie/Griffith) cannot yield a consistent joint model.  
- Proposes Truncated PGM (TPGM): a valid exponential-family truncation supporting both positive and negative edges.  
- Proposes Quadratic PGM (QPGM): keeps linear sufficient statistics but replaces the base measure with a quadratic term; proves quadratic growth is necessary for normalizability under positive edges.  
- Proposes Sub-Linear PGM (SPGM): retains the Poisson base measure with sub-linear sufficient statistics to get heavier tails and unconstrained edges.  
- Uses neighborhood selection for learning and evaluates on synthetic data and a TCGA miRNA network.  
- Situates work relative to Besag’s auto-models and GLM-based exponential-family graphical models; contrasts with Gaussian, nonparanormal, and multivariate Poisson sum constructions.  
- Theory is careful and novel; key results (Winsorization impossibility, QPGM normalizability, SPGM construction) are well-supported.  
- Empirical results are sensible and relevant, though scalability, model-selection details (R, R0), and sparsistency are under-discussed.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; minor editorial fixes and definition/citation for “sparsistent” suggested.  
- Originality is high: the necessity of a quadratic base measure and the new SPGM and corrected truncation are novel contributions.  
- Significance is strong: enables positive and negative dependencies for count data with tractable learning; empirical case study is compelling.  
- Concern: critique of TPGM’s “big negatives” may be overstated—truncation can be appropriate for intrinsically low-count domains.  
- Concern: critique of QPGM’s Gaussian-like thin tails may be overstated; such tails can be suitable in stabilized/deconvolved count settings.  
- Request: clarify how R and R0 are selected, provide sensitivity analyses, and report computational costs.  
- Request: specify which QPGM parameters are learned (diagonal vs. full Θ) and conditions ensuring negative definiteness in high dimensions.  
- Pros for acceptance: resolves a long-standing limitation, introduces practically learnable models with flexible tail behavior, and demonstrates empirical advantages.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited discussion of hyperparameter selection, scalability, and sparsistency guarantees; some critiques need more nuance and empirical support.  
- Overall recommendation: well-written, original, and significant; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
