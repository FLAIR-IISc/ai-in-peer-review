

This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical models for count data, proposing three novel extensions to Poisson graphical models that can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies. The authors build on Besag's work [17] and Yang et al.'s generalization [18, 19], which established a Poisson graphical model (PGM) distribution where node-conditional distributions are Poisson, but with a severe limitation: the model can only represent negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints (θst ≤ 0). The authors identify two flawed approaches in prior work (Winsorized Poisson [20] and [21]) and propose three valid alternatives: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).

The paper's main contributions are theoretically sound and well-executed. The authors rigorously prove that the Winsorized Poisson approach cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1), which is an important correction to prior literature. The TPGM construction (Theorem 2) provides a valid graphical model with a truncated domain, while QPGM and SPGM solve the normalizability constraint through fundamental modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics (Theorems 3-4). The authors demonstrate that for linear sufficient statistics, a quadratic base measure is necessary for normalizability with positive edge parameters, which leads to the QPGM. SPGM further improves by using sub-linear sufficient statistics to achieve heavier tails.

The paper's strengths include: (1) clear theoretical development that advances the understanding of exponential family graphical models; (2) practical demonstration on both simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA dataset; (3) well-motivated extensions that address a genuine limitation of existing approaches; and (4) a solid theoretical foundation for structure learning via neighborhood selection.

However, several weaknesses merit consideration. First, the QPGM's Gaussian-esque thin tail (as noted in Section 3.1) is a limitation, as the authors acknowledge, and may be less suitable for count data with heavy tails. Second, the SPGM's lack of closed-form conditional distributions undermines the conditional-specification motivation that underpins the entire graphical model framework, making it less practical than the authors suggest. Third, the practical motivation for using Poisson-based models is weakly supported; the authors claim sequencing reads are replacing microarrays (a claim needing updated citation), but provide no evidence that Poisson is a good empirical fit for their breast cancer data. A plot of the SPGM PMF alongside empirical counts would strengthen this argument.

The literature review is incomplete and contains several inaccuracies. The authors misrepresent copula approaches (e.g., [8] is not a "non-parametric extension" as claimed, but a Gaussian copula method), misstate [11] as focusing on log-linear models for contingency tables (which is accurate but misses the key point that these models are intractable in high dimensions), and neglect significant work in spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count methods. The paper would be significantly improved by engaging with these approaches, particularly Gaussian copula methods [7, 9, 10] that do not impose Markov independence in observable space but can model complex count dependencies.

Methodological details are unclear. The paper provides no guidance on selecting truncation constants R (for TPGM and SPGM) and R0 (for SPGM), which are critical for practitioners. The authors should provide empirical rules or sensitivity analyses for these parameters. Additionally, while the paper claims their models are "Poisson-like," the SPGM's sub-linear sufficient statistics fundamentally alter the distribution's behavior, making the "Poisson" label potentially misleading.

The significance of this work is moderate. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the practical advantages over simpler alternatives (e.g., Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model) are not sufficiently justified. The simulation results (Figure 1) show modest improvements over Gaussian methods, and the breast cancer case study (Figure 2) demonstrates that TPGM and SPGM identify biologically relevant hubs missed by PGM, but the paper does not convincingly show these methods are necessary for the task.

Quality: The theoretical results are sound and well-proven, though the practical implementation details are lacking. The experimental evaluation is sufficient but could be strengthened with more empirical evidence of Poisson fit.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good mathematical exposition. However, the practical guidance for parameter selection is missing.

Originality: The three proposed model extensions represent a significant advance over prior work, solving a genuine limitation of Poisson graphical models. The work builds meaningfully on [17, 18, 19] while correcting prior errors.

Significance: The contribution advances the theoretical understanding of graphical models for count data, but the practical significance is limited by the lack of compelling evidence that these models outperform simpler alternatives in real-world applications.

Pro: Theoretical contribution is solid and corrects a prior error in the literature; the model extensions are well-motivated and solve a genuine problem.

Con: SPGM lacks closed-form conditionals, undermining the framework; QPGM has undesirable thin tails; practical motivation is weak without empirical evidence of Poisson fit; literature review is incomplete and contains inaccuracies; no guidance for parameter selection.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models, but the practical utility is questionable without stronger empirical justification. I recommend a borderline decision: strong acceptance if authors address the literature review gaps, provide parameter selection guidance, and strengthen the practical motivation with empirical evidence of Poisson fit in the breast cancer data.
