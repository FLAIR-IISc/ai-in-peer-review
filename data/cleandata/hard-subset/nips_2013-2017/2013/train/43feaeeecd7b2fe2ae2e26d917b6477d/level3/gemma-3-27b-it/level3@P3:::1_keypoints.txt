- Paper tackles modeling multivariate count data and critiques limitations of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs).  
- Standard PGMs can only represent negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints.  
- Authors propose three modifications: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  
- Empirical validation is provided on synthetic data and real microRNA sequencing datasets.  
- Paper is well-situated in related work (Gaussian, Ising, multinomial models) and cites prior PGM attempts.  
- Authors identify and prove a flaw in the Kaiser & Cressie truncation approach.  
- Analysis of exponential family/base measure scaling and its effect on model properties is thorough.  
- Strength: Theoretical contributions (proofs and base measure analysis) are rigorous and well-motivated.  
- Strength: SPGM and QPGM are novel and address positive and negative dependency modeling limitations.  
- Strength: Potentially significant impact for high-dimensional count data applications (e.g., genomics).  
- Strength: Paper is clear, logically structured, and presents mathematical derivations accessibly.  
- Strength: Experimental results on both synthetic and real data bolster the proposed methods.  
- Weakness: TPGM appears useful mainly for low-count domains; limitations with increasing truncation R are not fully addressed.  
- Weakness: QPGM’s Gaussian-like thin tails may be inappropriate in some settings; trade-offs are underexplored.  
- Weakness: Term “sparsistent” is not clearly defined and should be briefly explained for accessibility.  
- Weakness: Minor editorial issues (e.g., "graphical mode/model", clarify log remark) need correction.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound extensions with promising empirical support and clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical benefits of TPGM unconvincing, QPGM tail behavior insufficiently analyzed, and sparsistency recovery not fully studied.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper; accept with minor revisions to address TPGM/QPGM limitations, define “sparsistent,” and discuss computational complexity.
