This paper addresses the long-standing and challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure of an undirected graph. Standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), as established by Besag and others, are limited to modeling only negative dependencies. The authors propose three novel models—the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—to overcome this limitation and capture both positive and negative dependencies.

The primary strength of this work is its novel theoretical contribution. The proposed constructions are interesting and valid, and they successfully relax the restrictive parameter space of traditional PGMs. A key practical advantage is that these models lead to relatively simple estimation algorithms based on neighborhood selection, which is valuable for high-dimensional applications.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that temper its impact. First, the proposed models have notable limitations. The QPGM, with its quadratic base measure, yields marginally thinner tails than might be desired for count data, making it behave more like a Gaussian model. Conversely, the SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which undermines the conditional-specification motivation of this line of work and raises questions about whether it is truly a "Poisson" model in a meaningful sense.

Second, the practical motivation and empirical validation are weak. The paper should provide stronger evidence that the underlying Poisson assumption is a good fit for real-world data, such as the breast cancer dataset used. Plotting the empirical and model-fitted probability mass functions would be a valuable addition. Furthermore, practitioners need a clearer justification for adopting these models over simpler and well-understood alternatives, such as latent Gaussian random field models with a Poisson measurement layer. Methodological clarity is also lacking; the role and selection of the constants R and R0 need detailed explanation and practical guidance.

Finally, the literature review is insufficient. The authors neglect significant related work in spatial statistics and other high-dimensional count data methods. The paper appears to misunderstand the role of copulas (which do not impose MRF independence in the observable space) and misrepresents the contributions of cited work [8, 11]. This incomplete survey of the landscape weakens the paper’s claims of originality and significance. A minor point is that the claim that sequencing reads are replacing microarrays should be supported by a more current reference.
