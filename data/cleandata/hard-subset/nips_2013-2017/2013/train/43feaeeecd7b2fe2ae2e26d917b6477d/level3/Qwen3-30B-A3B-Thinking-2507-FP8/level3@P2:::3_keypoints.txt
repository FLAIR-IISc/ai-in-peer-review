- Proposes three extensions to Poisson graphical models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) to allow positive and negative conditional dependencies.  
- Establishes necessary conditions for normalizability and shows how modifying base measure or sufficient statistics overcomes PGM limitations.  
- Builds on Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2013) and provides non-trivial theoretical contributions (Theorems 1, 3–4).  
- TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods in simulations and a breast cancer microRNA case study.  
- Case study finds biologically relevant hub miRNAs (e.g., mir-375, mir-10b) missed by Gaussian models, supporting practical value.  
- Practical motivation is weak because Poisson often poorly fits real count data (overdispersion); empirical fit to real data is not shown.  
- SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining the node-conditional modeling motivation and practical appeal.  
- QPGM has Gaussian-like thin tails, which may fail to capture heavy-tailed count data common in applications.  
- Literature review is incomplete and contains misrepresentations (mischaracterizes copula work and log-linear models; omits spatial count methods).  
- Methodological details are unclear: selection/role of constants R and R0 and neighborhood selection procedure need more explanation and guidance.  
- Theoretical proofs are sound and novel, correctly proving limitations of Winsorized Poisson and normalizability conditions.  
- Experimental validation is limited to simulations and one case study; insufficient demonstration of empirical fit and comparison to simpler alternatives.  
- Clarity is generally good, but technical aspects of the SPGM and more intuitive explanations could be improved.  
- Originality is high—first models to allow both positive and negative conditional dependencies while preserving graphical structure.  
- Significance is tempered by practical drawbacks and lack of justification versus simpler methods (e.g., Gaussian random fields with Poisson observations).  
- Recommendation: reject—valuable theoretical work but practical issues, incomplete literature review, and insufficient empirical evidence outweigh novelty.
