- Addresses important problem of modeling multivariate count data relevant to genomics and spatial statistics.
- Correctly identifies limitation of standard Poisson graphical models in capturing negative correlations.
- Proposes three novel modifications: Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM).
- Theoretically disproves a previously suggested truncation approach and justifies the proposed alternatives.
- Builds on Besag and Yang et al.; theoretical novelty is incremental due to reliance on prior frameworks.
- Navigates normalizability constraints to propose viable model modifications.
- TPGM offers a natural truncation strategy; QPGM and SPGM modify the base measure and sufficient statistics innovatively.
- Provides clear theoretical justification for needing quadratic or sub-linear base measures to allow positive and negative dependencies.
- Experimental evaluation is weak and appears tailored to favor the proposed models.
- Synthetic experiments are designed to benefit the methods; real-data analysis lacks comprehensive comparison.
- Omits comparison to copula-based alternatives, a major oversight for multivariate count modeling.
- Copula baselines used in a “black-box” way with potentially suboptimal marginal choices; suggests KDE for better marginal estimation.
- Lacks objective quantitative evaluation (e.g., held-out log-probability); reliance on visual comparisons is insufficient.
- Claim of identical performance to Glasso raises concerns about baseline implementation or experimental setup; needs more rigorous statistical comparison.
- Paper is well-written and explains mathematical underpinnings clearly, but some sections (e.g., Section 3.1 bound) are overly detailed.
- Key insight on sub-linear sufficient statistics (Section 3.2) should be highlighted more prominently, possibly with a figure; proofs should be moved to supplementary material.
- Acceptance arguments: addresses an important problem, presents theoretically sound extensions, disproves previous approach, and is well-written.
- Rejection arguments: insufficient/bias experimental evaluation, missing comparisons (notably copulas), lack of quantitative results, and limited theoretical novelty.
- Overall recommendation: reject now but encourage revision and resubmission after strengthening empirical evaluation, adding comprehensive comparisons, and providing quantitative and statistical validation.
