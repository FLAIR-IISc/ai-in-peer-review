- Addresses the important problem that standard Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only model negative dependencies and proposes models to overcome this limitation.
- Proposes three novel model classes—Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—to enable positive dependencies in count graphical models.
- Identifies and corrects a flaw in a previous "Winsorized" Poisson approach and introduces TPGM as a valid truncated model.
- QPGM uses a quadratic base measure but produces Gaussian-like thin tails, making it ill-suited for overdispersed count data and raising the question of why use it over Gaussian models on transformed data.
- SPGM addresses tail-weight issues but its sub-linear sufficient statistic yields a node-conditional log-partition without closed form, reducing the tractability and "Poisson-like" simplicity of the conditional-specification approach.
- Theoretical contributions are strong: careful analysis of truncation, and the relationship between sufficient statistics, base measures, and normalizability.
- Estimation is tractable via penalized pseudo-likelihood and neighborhood selection, which is computationally efficient for high dimensions.
- Literature review is incomplete: copula-based and semi-parametric Gaussian copula graphical models are mischaracterized or under-discussed despite being direct competitors.
- Omits substantial prior work using latent-variable approaches (e.g., latent Gaussian random fields) that readily induce arbitrary correlations for count data and should be compared against.
- Overstates the incompatibility of log-linear models with high-dimensional settings by not acknowledging modern sparse extensions.
- Practical motivation is weak: many real datasets show overdispersion vs. Poisson assumptions, and the paper lacks empirical fit diagnostics (e.g., marginal distribution plots) to justify model choice.
- Crucial hyperparameters (R and R0) for TPGM/SPGM lack practical selection guidance and sensitivity analysis, limiting usability.
- Some references are dated (example about sequencing replacing microarrays), and more current citations would strengthen claims.
- Experimental evaluation may be biased (data generated from TPGM or sum-of-Poissons); experiments should include data from latent Gaussian models for a more challenging benchmark.
- Overall: solid and original theoretical work that enables positive/negative dependencies, but practical impact and validation are uncertain due to insufficient comparisons, empirical evidence, and implementation guidance.
