This paper addresses the problem of modeling multivariate count data, a common challenge in modern applications like genomics and user-ratings. The authors critique existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) which, due to normalizability constraints, can only represent negative dependencies. They propose several modifications to the PGM to overcome this limitation, including a novel truncation method (Truncated PGM or TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The work is accompanied by experimental validation on both synthetic and real (microRNA sequencing) datasets.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in graphical models. The authors correctly situate their work within the context of Gaussian, Ising, and multinomial graphical models, highlighting the limitations of these models when applied to count data. They acknowledge prior attempts at addressing this issue, such as those by Kaiser & Cressie and Yang et al., and importantly, demonstrate a flaw in the Kaiser & Cressie truncation approach. The connection to exponential families and the influence of base measure scaling on model properties are well-articulated, drawing on concepts from established literature in statistical modeling.  Related work, including non-parametric extensions [7, 8, 9, 10] and copula-based approaches [12], is appropriately referenced.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical contributions—particularly the proof demonstrating the invalidity of the Kaiser & Cressie truncation and the analysis of base measure requirements—appear rigorous and well-motivated. The development of the QPGM and SPGM provides novel extensions to the PGM framework.
*   **Originality:**  The proposed models, especially the SPGM with its sub-linear sufficient statistic, represent a genuinely novel approach to modeling count data dependencies. Addressing the inherent limitations of the standard PGM is a significant contribution.
*   **Significance:** The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies in count data has the potential to impact a range of applications, particularly in fields dealing with high-dimensional count data like genomics and epidemiology.  The demonstration on microRNA sequencing data lends practical relevance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The motivation for each modification is clearly explained, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments strengthens the evaluation of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **“Big Negatives” Claim:** The reviewer's skepticism regarding the TPGM's utility, particularly its suitability only for domains with low counts, is valid. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the truncation approach and the potential for the performance to degrade as the truncation level *R* increases. The argument that it may be “better able to infer edges from highly connected networks” feels speculative without more detailed justification.
*   **Critique of Quadratic Model:** The reviewer's point concerning the appropriateness of Gaussian-like thin tails in certain settings is well-taken. While the authors acknowledge this, they don’t fully explore scenarios where the QPGM might be less ideal. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between tail behavior and dependency modeling would be beneficial.
*   **“Sparsistent” Definition:** The reviewer’s difficulty understanding “sparsistent” is understandable, despite its usage in related work. While not a major issue, briefly defining or contextualizing this term within the paper would improve accessibility.
*   **Minor Editorial Points:** The suggested changes – altering "graphical mode distributions" to "model" (p.3) and clarifying "Note that the log..." (p.5) – are sound and should be implemented.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents novel and theoretically sound extensions to the PGM framework.
*   The proposed models address a significant limitation of existing approaches to modeling multivariate count data.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the potential benefits of the new models, particularly compared to standard Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and technical details of the proposed methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical benefits of the TPGM are not convincingly demonstrated and its limitations are not fully addressed.
*   The potential drawbacks of the QPGM's thin-tailed behavior are not thoroughly explored.
*   The lack of a detailed sparsistency analysis limits the confidence in the model's ability to accurately recover network structure in high-dimensional settings.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models. The proposed models are innovative and address a well-defined problem with significant practical implications. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above – specifically, providing a more thorough discussion of the TPGM’s limitations and the QPGM's tail behavior, and including a brief definition of "sparsistent".  Adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed models compared to existing approaches would also be valuable.
