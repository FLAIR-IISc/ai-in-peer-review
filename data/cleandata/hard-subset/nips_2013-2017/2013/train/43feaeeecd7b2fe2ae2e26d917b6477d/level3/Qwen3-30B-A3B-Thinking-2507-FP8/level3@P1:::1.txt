

This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical models for count data, which are increasingly prevalent in applications like genomics and spatial statistics. The authors build upon Yang et al.'s Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) [18, 19], which suffers from a fundamental limitation: it can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors propose three novel approaches to overcome this limitation, enabling rich dependence structures with both positive and negative conditional dependencies.

The paper makes three key contributions. First, it rigorously demonstrates that Kaiser and Cressie's Winsorized Poisson approach [20] cannot yield a valid joint distribution, correcting an important misconception in prior work. Second, it introduces a Truncated PGM (TPGM) by truncating the Poisson domain to {0, 1, ..., R}, which permits both positive and negative dependencies but has limitations in modeling unbounded count data. Third, it develops two fundamentally new variants: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure (proven necessary for normalizability with linear sufficient statistics) and a Sub-linear PGM (SPGM) with sub-linear sufficient statistics that allows heavier tails and broader parameter ranges.

The theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified. The authors provide clear proofs for normalizability conditions (Theorems 1-4) and demonstrate how their models generalize the standard Poisson graphical model framework. The experimental evaluation on synthetic data shows that both TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods for count data, with SPGM particularly effective for networks with both positive and negative edges. The real-world application to breast cancer miRNA sequencing data demonstrates practical significance, revealing biologically meaningful networks that the original PGM missed entirely (finding only 14 miRNAs with negative relationships versus 20+ with the authors' methods, including known cancer-related miRNAs like mir-375 and mir-10b).

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, with careful mathematical exposition and effective organization. The minor editorial suggestions noted (changing "graphical mode distributions" to "model" on p.3, clarifying "Note that the log..." on p.5) would further enhance readability. The authors adequately contextualize their work against prior NIPS contributions, including Yang et al.'s PGM [18, 19], the nonparanormal [7], and Gaussian graphical models [4].

The main limitations are relatively minor. The critique of the QPGM's Gaussian-like thin tails seems overly negative; while it's true the model has thinner tails than Poisson, this could be advantageous in some applications where such behavior is desired. The paper's assertion that TPGM has "big negatives" for domains with low counts appears overstated—TPGM may actually perform well in such settings. The use of "sparsistent" without definition is slightly confusing, though this term is established in high-dimensional statistics.

This work makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. It solves a fundamental problem in graphical modeling for count data that has been recognized but not properly addressed [20, 21]. The three novel model classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) provide the first complete solution for modeling rich dependence structures in count-valued data. The theoretical rigor is excellent, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the real-world application demonstrates tangible benefits over existing methods. The paper advances the state of the art in graphical modeling by extending the framework to handle count data with both positive and negative dependencies—a capability previously unattainable.

The significance of this work is substantial for fields relying on count data analysis, including genomics, spatial statistics, and social science. The models can be learned via neighborhood selection, making them practical for high-dimensional data. While the QPGM's thin tails may be a limitation in some contexts, the authors' careful characterization of this trade-off demonstrates methodological honesty. The SPGM's ability to handle both positive and negative edges with unbounded count domains represents a particularly valuable advance.

Overall, this paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models. It addresses a clear gap in the literature with rigorous solutions, compelling theoretical justification, and strong empirical validation. The work represents a substantial advance over the current state of the art and will likely be of broad interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
