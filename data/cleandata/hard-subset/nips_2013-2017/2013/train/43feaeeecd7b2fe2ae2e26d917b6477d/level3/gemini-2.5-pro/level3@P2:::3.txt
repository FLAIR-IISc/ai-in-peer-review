This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of constructing undirected graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors begin by correctly identifying a major limitation of the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) derived from the Besag construction: for the joint distribution to be normalizable, it can only model negative conditional dependencies. The paper's main contribution is the proposal of three novel "Poisson-like" graphical models—the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that overcome this limitation and can capture both positive and negative dependencies. The paper demonstrates that a previous attempt at truncation (Winsorized PGM) is invalid, and proposes a valid alternative (TPGM). For unbounded counts, the QPGM and SPGM are developed by modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the underlying univariate distribution, respectively. The models are learned via penalized neighborhood selection, and their performance is evaluated on simulated data and a microRNA sequencing dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** Developing flexible and tractable graphical models for multivariate count data is a crucial problem in machine learning, with applications in genomics, social sciences, and beyond. The paper's focus on moving beyond the negative-dependence-only limitation of classical PGMs is well-motivated and important.

2.  **Novel Model Constructions:** The paper presents a series of theoretically grounded model constructions. The proof that the Winsorized PGM does not lead to a valid joint distribution is a valuable clarification for the field. The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent novel and creative attempts to build a PGM-like framework that allows for a rich dependence structure.

3.  **Tractable Estimation:** The proposed models fit within the framework of neighborhood selection using penalized conditional likelihood, which makes parameter estimation and structure learning computationally feasible in high dimensions. This is a significant practical advantage.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Practical Motivation and Model Fit:** The paper's motivation rests on the Poisson distribution, but it is well-known that real-world count data (especially from genomics, as in the case study) are often overdispersed and poorly fit by a standard Poisson. The paper would be much stronger if it provided empirical evidence that its proposed models are a good fit for the data they analyze. For instance, the authors could show goodness-of-fit plots for the breast cancer data or plot the probability mass function (PMF) of the proposed SPGM to illustrate its shape and tail behavior compared to a standard Poisson or Negative Binomial distribution.

2.  **Model Limitations and Trade-offs:**
    *   The **QPGM** uses a quadratic base measure, which, as the authors note, results in Gaussian-like thin tails. This may limit its applicability for data with heavy tails, which is common for counts. The paper needs to better justify why a practitioner would choose this model over a simpler and more established approach, such as modeling the data with a latent Gaussian graphical model and a Poisson measurement model (i.e., a Gaussian copula model or log-Gaussian Cox process).
    *   The **SPGM** is perhaps the most interesting construction, but it appears to lack a closed-form conditional distribution, relying instead on a variational upper bound for the log-partition function. This undermines the elegance and original motivation of building a joint model from simple conditional specifications (the Besag construction). It also raises the question of whether the model is truly "Poisson-like" or a new family of distributions entirely. The connection becomes somewhat tenuous.

3.  **Clarity and Guidance for Practitioners:** The paper lacks sufficient detail on the role and selection of the hyperparameters R and R0 for the TPGM and SPGM. How should a user choose these values? Are they fixed based on domain knowledge, set to the maximum observed count (as done in the experiments), or chosen via cross-validation? A sensitivity analysis and clearer guidance are needed for the methods to be practically useful.

4.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The review of related work is somewhat light and contains some potentially misleading statements.
    *   The discussion of copulas seems to misinterpret their purpose. For example, methods like the nonparanormal [7] or Gaussian copulas [8] model dependence in a latent space precisely to avoid imposing restrictive MRF assumptions on the observed (non-Gaussian) data. A more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between modeling dependencies in the observable space (as this paper does) versus a latent space would be beneficial.
    *   The characterization of log-linear models from [11] as unsuitable for high dimensions because parameters grow exponentially seems to overlook decades of work on sparse log-linear models for high-dimensional contingency tables.
    *   The paper neglects a large body of work from spatial statistics and econometrics on modeling high-dimensional count data, which often uses hierarchical models or latent variable approaches.

### Questions and Suggestions

*   Could the authors provide a more detailed comparison with latent Gaussian models (e.g., Gaussian copulas)? These models can capture arbitrary dependencies and handle count data via a Poisson link function, representing a strong and flexible baseline. What are the specific advantages of the proposed models over this class?
*   Please provide more details on setting R and R0. The choice of R = max(count) seems ad-hoc and data-dependent. Does this have theoretical implications?
*   The claim in Section 4 that "sequencing reads...are replacing microarrays" cites a 2008 paper [22]. While true, a more recent citation would strengthen the statement's currency.
*   Given that the SPGM conditional lacks a closed form, could the authors comment further on the computational cost of the proximal gradient ascent, especially the line search step which requires evaluating the objective function?

In summary, this paper makes a valid and interesting theoretical contribution to the construction of graphical models for count data. However, its practical significance is weakened by a lack of empirical validation of model fit, insufficient guidance on hyperparameter selection, and an incomplete discussion of strong alternative modeling approaches. The weaknesses in the QPGM (thin tails) and SPGM (no closed-form conditional) also raise questions about their general applicability. With revisions to address these points, particularly by strengthening the empirical motivation and comparison to alternatives, the paper could represent a more significant contribution.
