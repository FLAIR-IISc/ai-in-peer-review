

This paper addresses an important gap in graphical models: the lack of effective approaches for modeling multivariate count data, which is increasingly common in applications like genomics and spatial statistics. The authors correctly identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only capture negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints, severely limiting their practical utility. They build on Yang et al.'s framework for graphical models via exponential family distributions and make three key contributions: (1) proving the invalidity of Kaiser and Cressie's Winsorized Poisson approach for graphical models; (2) proposing a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that uses a natural truncation to allow both positive and negative dependencies; and (3) introducing two novel variants, Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), by modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution.

The paper's primary strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors correctly demonstrate that the Winsorized Poisson approach cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1), which is a significant contribution to the field. The TPGM approach is well-justified and provides a practical solution for count data with bounded domains. The QPGM and SPGM variants are theoretically novel in their modification of the exponential family structure to allow both positive and negative dependencies while maintaining normalizability. The paper is generally well-written and the theoretical development is clear, though the supplementary material should include all proofs as noted.

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that undermine its significance claim. The theoretical novelty is limited, as much of the framework directly follows Yang et al.'s work. The experimental evaluation is particularly concerning: the synthetic experiments are tailored to the proposed methods (e.g., generating data with both positive and negative edges for TPGM/SPGM, while the comparison methods can't model positive edges), creating an artificial advantage. The real-data case study on breast cancer miRNA networks omits critical comparisons with copula-based approaches like the nonparanormal, which could be appropriately applied to count data with proper marginal modeling. The authors use copula competitors in a black-box manner with inappropriate marginal choices, rather than using better alternatives like KDE for the marginals.

Most troubling is the lack of objective evaluation metrics. The paper reports qualitative observations about "more hub miRNAs" and "biologically important hub miRNAs" without providing test log-probabilities or other quantitative measures to demonstrate superior model fit. The claim that "TPGM and SPGM have superior performance" is unsubstantiated by rigorous comparison - the results appear suspiciously similar to Gaussian graphical models, suggesting potential issues with the baseline comparison or the method itself. The synthetic experiments show TPGM performing best on data generated from its own distribution, but fail to demonstrate consistent advantages across different data-generating processes.

The paper's structure also has issues. Section 3.1 is overly detailed with a bound that seems unnecessary for the main contribution, while the key figure illustrating the sublinear sufficient statistics (which is central to SPGM's advantage) should be in the main text rather than the appendix. The claim that SPGM "has a heavier tail with milder base measures" is not adequately supported by empirical evidence.

The significance of the work is questionable given the limited experimental validation. While the proposed models are theoretically interesting and address a real limitation of existing approaches, the paper fails to provide convincing evidence that these models offer practical advantages over existing methods for real-world count data. The field needs more than theoretical proposals; it requires demonstrated improvements in model performance on standard benchmarks.

In summary, this paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the problem of modeling count-valued data with graphical models, but the experimental evidence is insufficient to support the claim of practical significance. The theoretical framework is well-developed, but the evaluation is biased and lacks objective metrics. The authors should strengthen their experimental section by including appropriate baselines (particularly with proper marginal modeling for copula approaches), providing quantitative metrics beyond qualitative observations, and demonstrating consistent advantages across multiple data-generating processes. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution remains largely theoretical with unclear practical value. The paper is technically sound but the significance claim is not adequately supported.
