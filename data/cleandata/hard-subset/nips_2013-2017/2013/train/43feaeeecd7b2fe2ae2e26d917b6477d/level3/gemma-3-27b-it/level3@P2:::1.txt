This paper addresses the important problem of modeling multivariate count data, a task for which standard graphical models like Gaussian graphical models are ill-suited. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) – their inability to represent positive correlations due to normalizability constraints – and propose three novel approaches: a Truncated PGM (TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivations and technical details of each contribution. The experimental evaluation on both simulated and real data demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed methods. Overall, this is a strong and original contribution with significant potential impact.

The authors effectively situate their work within the existing literature on graphical models and count data. They correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches, including log-linear models, copula transforms, and the standard PGM formulation. The discussion of the theoretical underpinnings of PGMs and the challenges associated with truncation is particularly insightful. The connection to exponential families and the careful derivation of the QPGM and SPGM distributions are commendable. The use of variational bounds to connect the QPGM to Gaussian graphical models provides a useful intuition and potential computational advantage. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the problem addressed is important and increasingly relevant with the growing availability of count data in diverse fields. Second, the proposed solutions are novel and theoretically sound. The proofs establishing the normalizability of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are a significant contribution. Third, the experimental results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the SPGM, in recovering network structure from both simulated and real data. The comparison to existing methods, including Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM, highlights the advantages of the proposed approaches. Finally, the inclusion of a real-world case study using microRNA expression data demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.

However, there are also some weaknesses. The authors’ strong claims regarding the limitations of the truncation approach (TPGM) seem somewhat overstated. While the theoretical result demonstrating the impossibility of a consistent joint distribution for the Kaiser & Cressie Winsorized Poisson is correct, the practicality of the TPGM for domains where count values are generally low (e.g., number of events in a small time window) is not fully addressed. The comment that problems arise when variables take large count values is true, but doesn't preclude usefulness in other scenarios. The critique of the QPGM based on its Gaussian-like thin tails also feels a bit strong. In some applications, such a tail behavior may be desirable or at least not detrimental. The authors should acknowledge the potential applicability of QPGM to specific datasets.

Furthermore, the use of the term "sparsistent" is somewhat jarring, despite being a relatively established term in the field. A brief explanation or reference to its formal definition would be beneficial for readers less familiar with high-dimensional statistics. Finally, there are a couple of minor editorial points: On page 3, "graphical mode distributions" should be "graphical models." On page 5, the phrase "Note that although the log..." could be rephrased for clarity, e.g., “Note that the log…”.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded contributions to the modeling of multivariate count data.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the methods and results.
*   Strong experimental validation on both simulated and real datasets.
*   Addresses a significant problem with increasing practical relevance.
*   The proposed SPGM model appears particularly promising.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overly strong criticisms of the TPGM and QPGM may limit their consideration by practitioners.
*   Minor editorial points need addressing.
*   Lack of a formal analysis of the statistical properties (e.g., consistency) of the proposed estimators.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant advance in the field of graphical modeling for count data. The authors have identified a fundamental limitation of existing methods and proposed innovative solutions that are both theoretically sound and empirically validated. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. The paper is clearly above the acceptance threshold for NIPS.
