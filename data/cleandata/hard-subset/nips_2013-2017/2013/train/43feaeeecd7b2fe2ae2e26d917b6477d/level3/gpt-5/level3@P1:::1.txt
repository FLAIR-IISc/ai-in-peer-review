This paper tackles the long-standing mismatch between standard undirected graphical models and multivariate count data. The authors start from the Poisson graphical model (PGM) of Besag and of Yang et al., which yields Poisson node-conditionals but is normalizable only when all pairwise parameters are non-positive—hence it can only model competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. They first revisit truncation: they show that the commonly proposed “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith) cannot correspond to any joint MRF under the Yang et al. construction (Theorem 1). They then propose a different exponential-family truncation that yields a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) with finite support and no sign constraints on edges (Theorem 2). To move beyond truncation, they study modifications to the exponential family: (i) with linear sufficient statistics B(X)=X, normalizability with mixed-sign edges requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3), which motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base terms and a normalizability condition akin to negative-definiteness of a quadratic form (Proposition 2); and (ii) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that preserves a Poisson-like base measure but employs piecewise sublinear sufficient statistics B(·;R0,R) to achieve heavier tails and allow arbitrary edge signs (Theorem 4). For learning, they use ℓ1-penalized nodewise neighborhood selection with tractable variational bounds: a Gaussian bound for QPGM and a Poisson bound for SPGM. Empirically, on simulated hub and scale-free graphs and on a TCGA breast cancer microRNA sequencing dataset, TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines, and they recover biologically plausible hubs (e.g., miR-375, miR-10b). These contributions build directly on, and remedy key limitations of, prior work on exponential-family MRFs and Poisson models at NIPS and beyond.

Quality
- Theory: The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals (Theorem 1), the constructive validity of TPGM (Theorem 2), and the necessity of quadratic growth for mixed-sign dependencies under linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) are valuable and clearly positioned. The normalizability condition for QPGM (Proposition 2) and SPGM (Theorem 4) is well stated. It would help to add more intuition around the necessity result (e.g., simple counterexamples illustrating why sub-quadratic base measures fail) and to summarize proof strategies (now likely in the appendix).
- Methodology: The nodewise estimation framework is sound and leverages variational upper bounds to keep conditionals tractable. Please clarify convexity/log-concavity of the nodewise objectives for QPGM and SPGM under the stated conditions, and outline computational complexity in p, degree, and maximum count.
- Experiments: The structural recovery results are compelling. Consider adding held-out predictive log-likelihood or calibration diagnostics to assess goodness-of-fit, and sensitivity analyses over R and (R0,R). Reporting runtime and convergence behavior (e.g., Gibbs mixing for data generation; proximal gradient step sizes) would aid reproducibility. It would also be informative to compare against additional count models (e.g., Poisson log-normal/correlated latent factor models) where feasible.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and clearly written. Define “sparsistent” on first use (e.g., “sparsistent: consistent recovery of the edge set/sparsity pattern”).
- Notation could be tightened: R is used both as a truncation parameter and as the real line in integrals; consider using ℝ for reals and a different symbol for truncation to avoid confusion.
- Minor edits:
  - p.3: “graphical mode distributions” → “graphical model distributions.”
  - p.5: “Note that the log…” → “Note that although the log…”
- A brief practical guide for choosing among TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM (e.g., based on tail behavior and count ranges) would help practitioners.

Originality
- The paper delivers several nontrivial and novel contributions: (i) a negative result ruling out Winsorized conditionals as a valid joint MRF; (ii) a valid exponential-family truncation (TPGM); (iii) a necessity result linking base-measure growth to the possibility of positive dependencies; (iv) QPGM and SPGM constructions enabling mixed-sign dependencies. This is a substantial advance in the modeling of multivariate count data.

Significance
- The ability to model both positive and negative conditional dependencies in count-valued networks addresses an important gap and has broad relevance (genomics, text, spatial incidence, recommender counts). The empirical results and the sequencing case study indicate practical impact. While the authors list some limitations of TPGM and QPGM, I am not fully convinced these are “big negatives” in all use cases: bounded-support TPGM may be entirely appropriate when counts are intrinsically low (e.g., single-cell counts after QC), and Gaussian-like thin tails in QPGM may be desirable for regularization and stability in moderate-count regimes. Framing these as trade-offs, with guidance on when each model is preferable, would strengthen the narrative.

Questions and suggestions
- Theorem 1: provide a brief intuition for why the indicator term at the truncation boundary breaks joint consistency.
- TPGM: discuss practical selection of R (e.g., via percentiles or cross-validation) and robustness if true counts occasionally exceed R.
- SPGM: give intuition for setting (R0,R) and how B(·) behaves with very large counts; consider an automatic scheme based on empirical quantiles.
- Learning guarantees: since sparsistency is claimed by appeal to prior results, a concise statement of assumptions under which those results extend (e.g., incoherence, bounded moments under each model) would be valuable.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and original theoretical contributions with clear practical modeling implications.
- Three concrete model classes addressing the core limitation of existing Poisson MRFs.
- Solid empirical evidence on synthetic and real data; biologically meaningful findings.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited guidance on model/hyperparameter selection and on when to prefer each variant.
- Experimental evaluation could include additional count baselines and predictive metrics.
- Some notational and expository rough edges (e.g., “sparsistent,” overlapping symbols).

Overall, this is a well-written, original, and significant paper that advances the state of the art in count-valued graphical modeling. With minor clarifications and a bit more guidance on practical usage, it would make a strong contribution.
