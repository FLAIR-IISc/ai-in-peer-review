Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) derived from Poisson node-conditionals (Besag; Yang et al.). In the classical construction, normalizability forces pairwise parameters to be non-positive, restricting models to competitive (negative) conditional dependencies—an issue for multivariate count data with both positive and negative associations. The authors make three contributions:

1) Truncation-based model: They show that the widely cited “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith), which concentrate tail mass at a fixed R, cannot yield a consistent MRF via the Besag/Yang construction (Theorem 1). They then propose a different truncation that preserves exponential-family form by redistributing tail mass across all remaining support points. This induces a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) with finite partition function and no sign restriction on pairwise parameters (Theorem 2). They discuss limitations as R grows.

2) Quadratic base measure: They show that with linear sufficient statistics B(X)=X, any model permitting both positive and negative conditional dependencies must have a base measure that grows at least quadratically, C(Z)=Ω(Z^2) (Theorem 3). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which augments the nodewise base measure with quadratic terms and is normalizable under a negative-definite quadratic form while allowing both positive and negative edges (Proposition 2). They give a Gaussian variational upper bound for nodewise partition functions.

3) Sub-linear sufficient statistics: To avoid Gaussian-like thin tails induced by quadratic base measures, they introduce sub-linear sufficient statistics B(x;R0,R) that saturate beyond R, yielding the Sub-linear PGM (SPGM). SPGM retains the log-factorial base measure, is normalizable without sign constraints on pairwise parameters (Theorem 4), and admits a Poisson variational upper bound for nodewise partition functions when θ≥0 (Proposition 3).

They estimate graphs via `1-penalized neighborhood selection and demonstrate performance on synthetic data (hub and scale-free graphs; data generated either from TPGM or from sums of Poissons) and a TCGA miRNA RNA-seq dataset. SPGM/TPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM (which only recovers negative edges) and recover biologically plausible hubs.

Quality
- Theory: The paper provides clear constructions, necessity results (C(Z)=Ω(Z^2) for linear B), and normalizability conditions for each model. The impossibility of the Winsorized construction (Theorem 1) is important and corrects a misconception in the applied spatial literature. It would help to provide more intuition for why R>3 is required in Theorem 1 and to comment on boundary cases (R≤3).
- Algorithms: Penalized neighborhood selection aligns with generalized linear MRF literature. The variational upper bounds for QPGM/SPGM are sensible, but the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of approximation error and whether it affects edge sign recovery or thresholds.
- Experiments: The synthetic studies are appropriate and show clear advantages on count data; however, two points would improve the empirical section: (i) sensitivity analyses over R and R0 for TPGM/SPGM, since these hyperparameters govern tail behavior and effective signal strength; (ii) comparisons to alternative count models, e.g., Poisson log-normal or latent Gaussian copula approaches for counts, which are plausible baselines beyond Gaussian/nonparanormal. The real-data case study is compelling and qualitatively consistent with biology.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with careful exposition of model assumptions and limitations. The use of the term “sparsistent” might be unclear to some readers—please define it explicitly (model-selection consistency for graph recovery) on first use. Minor editorial suggestions:
- p.3: “graphical mode distributions” -> “graphical model distributions.”
- p.5: “Note that the log…” could be clarified to “Note that although the log-partition function A(θ) of the joint model is intractable, the node-conditional log-partition functions remain tractable, enabling neighborhood selection.”
- Abstract/§4: “form count-valued next generation sequencing data” -> “from count-valued next generation sequencing data.”

Originality
High. The negative-dependence constraint of PGMs is well known, but the paper contributes (i) a rigorous impossibility for Winsorized node-conditionals in MRFs, (ii) a necessity result on the base-measure growth for linear sufficient statistics, and (iii) two practical new model classes (QPGM, SPGM), plus a truncated model that remains exponential-family and valid as a joint distribution. These are conceptually clean and fill an important gap.

Significance
High. Count-valued networks are increasingly important (sequencing, spatial incidence, clicks, ratings). Removing the negative-dependence constraint while retaining tractable node-conditionals is impactful. The modeling choices (quadratic vs sub-linear) provide a toolkit spanning tail behaviors and parameter constraints. Practitioners are likely to use and extend these ideas.

Pros for acceptance
- Corrects a misconception in the literature with a clear impossibility result.
- Introduces three principled, tractable alternatives enabling positive and negative dependencies.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings (normalizability, necessity of base-measure growth).
- Practical estimation via neighborhood selection; promising empirical results on synthetic and real data.
- Relevance to core NIPS topics in graphical models and high-dimensional statistics.

Cons / points to address
- The critique of TPGM’s “big negatives” may be overstated; for domains with inherently low counts or known dynamic range, truncation is realistic and advantageous. Please temper and clarify guidance on when TPGM is recommended.
- The “thin tails” critique of QPGM may also be context-dependent; in many applications Gaussian-like concentration is desirable. Consider reframing this as a modeling trade-off rather than a drawback.
- More discussion of tuning R and (R0,R) and their impact on identifiability and sample complexity.
- Empirical comparisons could include Poisson log-normal or latent count copula models.
- Provide more detail on optimization (step sizes, stopping criteria, runtime) and robustness of the variational bounds in practice.

Questions for the authors
1) Theorem 1: Could you give intuition for why R>3 is required, and what happens for R≤3? Are there restricted graph classes where a Winsorized construction might still be valid?
2) Theorem 3: What assumptions are needed (e.g., pairwise-only, linear B, unbounded support) for C(Z)=Ω(Z^2) to be necessary? Does adding higher-order cliques change the conclusion?
3) QPGM: The normalizability condition requires a negative-definite quadratic form. Can you characterize the range of positive edge weights admissible given diagonal terms? Any practical guidance for regularizing the quadratic parameters to ensure normalizability during estimation?
4) SPGM: How sensitive is performance to (R0,R)? Can these be estimated from data (e.g., via profile likelihood or cross-validation) without biasing sparsistency claims?
5) Experiments: How do results change if R in TPGM is not set to the maximum observed count (e.g., held-out or prior-based choice)? Please report runtime and convergence diagnostics.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a well-executed and timely contribution that advances the modeling of count-valued graphical structures with both theoretical insight and practical methods. With clarifications and modest additional comparisons, it will be a strong addition to the program.

Confidence
High.
