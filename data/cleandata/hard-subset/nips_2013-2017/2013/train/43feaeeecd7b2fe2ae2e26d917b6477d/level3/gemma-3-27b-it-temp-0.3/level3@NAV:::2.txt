## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses the important problem of constructing multivariate count distributions, correctly identifying limitations in existing approaches like the standard Poisson Graphical Model (PGM) which can only model negative dependencies. The authors propose a thorough investigation into modifications of the Poisson distribution to overcome this limitation, presenting the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). A key contribution is the proof that previously suggested truncation approaches, like those of Kaiser & Cressie (2006), are invalid for constructing a consistent joint distribution. 

The TPGM truncation is a natural and well-justified extension, offering a viable solution. The QPGM and SPGM are novel in their attempts to alter the exponential-family base measure and sufficient statistics, demonstrating a creative approach to achieving both positive and negative dependencies. The theoretical development largely builds upon the framework established by Yang et al. (2013, 2014), which somewhat limits the theoretical novelty. The core strength of the paper lies in the systematic exploration of modifications to the Poisson distribution to address the inherent limitations of the original PGM.

However, the experimental evaluation is disappointing and appears biased. The synthetic experiments are tailored to the proposed methods, and the real-data comparison omits relevant alternatives like copula-based approaches. The reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal; a more objective evaluation, such as test log-probability, is needed to rigorously assess performance. The use of copula competitors in an overly “black-box” manner, with potentially inappropriate marginal choices (e.g., Gaussian instead of KDE), weakens the comparison. The identical performance of the SPGM to Glasso is suspicious and suggests potential issues with the baseline implementation or the method itself. 

Section 3.1 is overly detailed, and the presented bound seems unnecessary. The key figure illustrating the sublinear statistic in Section 3.2 should be presented and better explained within the main text for improved clarity. The paper is generally well-written, but all proofs should be relegated to the supplementary material.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Theoretically appealing approaches with a clear identification of limitations in existing methods. Systematic exploration of potential solutions.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Insufficient experimental evidence to establish practical merit. Biased experimental setup and qualitative results. Suspicious performance compared to baseline methods.

**Overall:** While the theoretical contributions are interesting, the lack of a convincing empirical demonstration of the proposed methods’ advantages prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance. Major revisions addressing the experimental shortcomings are required.
