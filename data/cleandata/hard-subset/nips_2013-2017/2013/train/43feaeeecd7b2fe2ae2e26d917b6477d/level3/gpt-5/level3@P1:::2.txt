This paper addresses an important and timely gap: modeling conditional dependence structures for high-dimensional count data. Building on Besag’s auto-model framework and the “exponential-family node-conditionals imply a consistent joint” results of Yang et al., the authors (i) prove that the commonly suggested Winsorized (hard-truncated-at-R) Poisson node-conditionals cannot be glued into a valid joint MRF (Theorem 1), (ii) propose a different truncation that preserves the exponential-family form and yields a proper joint MRF over {0,…,R}p (TPGM; Theorem 2), and (iii) introduce two non-truncated alternatives that modify the exponential-family ingredients: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that retains linear sufficient statistics but uses a quadratic base measure to permit both positive and negative dependencies (Proposition 2), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure but replaces the linear sufficient statistic with a sublinear function that saturates at large counts (Theorem 4). Learning proceeds via penalized neighborhood selection, consistent with prior GLM-based structure learning for exponential-family MRFs. Experiments on simulated graphs and a TCGA miRNA sequencing dataset compare TPGM and SPGM to Gaussian-based baselines.

Relation to prior work: The paper is clearly situated with respect to Gaussian GGMs, Ising/multinomial graphical models, Poisson auto-models (Besag), multivariate Poisson constructions, and the GLM-based MRF framework of Yang et al. It correctly highlights the normalizability-induced negativity constraint of the standard Poisson MRF, and it clarifies the limitations of “sum-of-Poissons” constructions (positive correlations only). Theorem 1 rectifies claims in Kaiser and Cressie/Griffith by showing their Winsorization does not yield a valid joint within the Besag/Yang framework.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a real modeling gap for counts and a careful analysis of why the classic Poisson MRF fails to admit positive edges.
- A principled truncation (TPGM) that stays within the exponential family and yields a valid joint, plus two non-truncated variants (QPGM, SPGM) that enlarge the feasible dependence structures.
- The normalizability argument for linear sufficient statistics implying a quadratic-or-heavier base measure (Theorem 3) is insightful and helps delineate the design space.
- Learning via penalized node-wise fits is natural and leverages prior theory on sparsistency for exponential-family graphical models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited theoretical novelty: much of the machinery relies on Yang et al.’s construction; the core new ingredients are (a) the counterexample to Winsorization, (b) the alternative truncation, and (c) particular choices of base measure/sufficient statistics. Theorem 3 is the most conceptually novel result.
- Experimental evaluation is thin and appears tailored to favor the proposed models. Synthetic data are generated from TPGM or positive-dependence-only processes, and the reported results are primarily ROC curves. Please add objective metrics (e.g., held-out pseudo-likelihood/test conditional log-probability, calibration of conditional means/variances, goodness-of-fit on marginals and pairwise counts).
- Real-data comparison set is incomplete. Gaussian baselines are a weak foil for count data. Please include copula-based competitors that are appropriate for discrete/count margins (e.g., Gaussian copula with empirical CDF or rank-likelihood; Dobra & Lenkoski; nonparanormal-SKEPTIC with KDE or monotone transforms more faithful to counts; Poisson log-normal models as an alternative latent-variable baseline). The current use of copula competitors seems black-box with suboptimal marginals; replacing Gaussian marginals with KDE or count-appropriate marginals would be fairer.
- Identical performance between baselines (e.g., Glasso and some variants) is suspicious; this suggests either implementation, preprocessing, or tuning issues. Please verify regularization paths, standardization choices, and stability selection settings across methods.
- Hyperparameters R, R0 in SPGM are chosen ad hoc (e.g., R equals the sample max). Provide sensitivity analysis and guidance for selection. For TPGM, discuss implications of data-dependent truncation on estimation and generalization.
- Section balance: 3.1 is overly detailed around a variational Gaussian upper bound that is not essential to the main message and risks conflating QPGM with Gaussian behavior. Conversely, the key intuition/figure for SPGM (Section 3.2) should be in the main text with clearer explanation of how sublinearity curbs explosive interactions while preserving heavy tails.
- Proof organization: ensure all proofs (especially Theorem 1 and Theorem 3) appear in the supplement with enough detail to be checked.

Quality
- The theoretical statements are plausible and, where nontrivial (Theorem 1; Theorem 3), of interest. However, empirical support is inadequate to establish robustness and practical advantage, and sparsistency of the proposed node-wise estimators is deferred.

Clarity
- Generally well written. Improve exposition of SPGM’s sufficient statistic and its impact on tails and dependence; streamline the QPGM variational discussion; move key figures into the main paper.

Originality
- Moderate. The main conceptual novelty is the design principle in Theorem 3 and the concrete SPGM construction; TPGM is a careful but natural fix to an incorrect truncation.

Significance
- Potentially significant, as count-valued network models are widely needed. The lack of strong, fair empirical evidence and ablations currently limits impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a recognized deficiency of Poisson MRFs and corrects a misconception in the literature.
- Introduces two promising families (QPGM, SPGM) with broader dependence patterns and sound normalizability.
- Clear path to scalable learning via node-wise penalized likelihood.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient and appears biased; key baselines and metrics are missing.
- Practical guidance (hyperparameters, sensitivity, robustness, computational costs) is limited.
- Theoretical contribution, while solid, is incremental relative to the Yang et al. framework.

Recommendation
- Leaning reject in its current form due to incomplete and potentially biased empirical validation. With stronger, fair comparisons (including copulas and Poisson log-normal), objective test metrics, sensitivity analyses for R/R0, and streamlined exposition (Section 3.1), this work could become a valuable contribution.
