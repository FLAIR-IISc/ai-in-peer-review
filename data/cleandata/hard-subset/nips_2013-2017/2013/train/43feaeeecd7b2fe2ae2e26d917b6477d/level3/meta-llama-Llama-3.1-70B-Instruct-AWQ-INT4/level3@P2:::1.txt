This paper proposes several new models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors argue that existing models, such as the Poisson graphical model (PGM), are unsatisfactory because they only allow negative correlations to normalize. The authors propose three novel approaches to address this limitation: a new truncation method, a quadratic base-measure statistic, and a sub-linear sufficient statistic.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and thorough introduction to the problem and the existing literature. The proposed models are original and significant contributions to the field. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets is also well-done and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed models.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to clearly articulate the limitations of existing models and to propose novel solutions to address these limitations. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the properties of the proposed models, including their normalizability and sparsistency.

However, there are some aspects of the paper that could be improved. For example, the reviewer is unconvinced by the "big negatives" claimed for the truncation model. While the authors argue that the truncation model is limited because it can only model a small range of counts, it is possible that this model could be suitable for domains with low counts. The authors could provide more evidence to support their claim that the truncation model is not suitable for most applications.

Additionally, the reviewer questions the critique of the quadratic model. While the authors argue that the quadratic model has Gaussian-like thin tails, it is possible that this property could be desirable in some settings. The authors could provide more discussion on the potential benefits and drawbacks of the quadratic model.

Finally, the reviewer is unclear on the term "sparsistent" despite its established usage. The authors could provide a clearer explanation of this term and its relevance to the proposed models.

Minor editorial suggestions include changing "graphical mode distributions" to "model" (p.3) and clarifying "Note that the log..." (p.5) to something like "Note that although the log...".

In terms of the criteria described in the Qualitative Evaluation section, the paper scores highly on originality, significance, and clarity. The paper proposes novel solutions to a well-defined problem and provides strong evidence to support the effectiveness of these solutions. The writing is clear and concise, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the properties of the proposed models.

However, the paper could be improved in terms of its critique of existing models and its discussion of the potential benefits and drawbacks of the proposed models. Additionally, the authors could provide more evidence to support their claims and more discussion on the relevance of the proposed models to real-world applications.
