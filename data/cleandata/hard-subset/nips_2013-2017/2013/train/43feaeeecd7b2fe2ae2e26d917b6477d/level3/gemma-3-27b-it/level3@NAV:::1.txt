## Review of “On Poisson Graphical Models”

This paper addresses the important problem of modeling multivariate count data, specifically extending graphical model frameworks to handle Poisson-distributed variables. The authors convincingly argue that existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) are limited by their inability to represent negative correlations due to normalizability constraints. They propose several innovative solutions: a novel truncation method, a quadratic base-measure, and a sub-linear sufficient statistic, each aiming to overcome these limitations and enable the modeling of richer dependency structures. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the mathematical derivations and motivations behind each proposed model. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real (microRNA sequencing) datasets provides valuable insights into the performance of the methods. Overall, this is a significant contribution that expands the toolkit for analyzing count data within a graphical modeling framework.

The strength of this work lies in its originality and theoretical soundness. The critique of the Yang et al. default model is well-reasoned, and the proposed modifications are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor. The introduction of the quadratic and sub-linear models is particularly clever, offering alternative routes to overcome the limitations of the standard PGM. The experimental validation, although limited in scope, supports the claim that the proposed models outperform standard approaches, like Gaussian Graphical Models, on count data. The inclusion of a real-world application to microRNA sequencing data further strengthens the paper’s relevance.

However, I have some reservations. While the authors convincingly demonstrate the shortcomings of the Winsorized Poisson truncation approach of Kaiser and Cressie, I'm not entirely convinced by the claim that the "big negatives" of the truncation model are generally problematic. For domains where counts are inherently low, a truncation at a reasonable level might be sufficient and practical. Additionally, the critique of the quadratic model, based on its Gaussian-like thin tails, feels somewhat dismissive. In certain scenarios, such a behavior could be appropriate and even desirable. Furthermore, the term “sparsistent,” while established in the literature, is not intuitively clear without prior knowledge and could benefit from a brief explanation within the context of the paper.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant limitation in existing PGM frameworks.
*   Proposes novel and theoretically sound solutions.
*   Demonstrates improved performance on both synthetic and real data.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Potential overstatement of limitations of the truncation method.
*   Dismissive of potential benefits of the quadratic model’s thin tails.
*   Lack of intuitive explanation of the term "sparsistent."



**Minor Editorial Suggestions:**

*   Replace “graphical mode distributions” with “model” on page 3.
*   Clarify “Note that the log…” on page 5 to something like “Note that although the log…”.



I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the minor concerns regarding the discussion of the truncation and quadratic models, and adding a brief explanation of the term "sparsistent". This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to influence future research in graphical modeling of count data.
