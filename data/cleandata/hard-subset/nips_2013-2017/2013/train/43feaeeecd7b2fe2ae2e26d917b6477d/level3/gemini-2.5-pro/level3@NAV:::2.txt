This paper addresses the important problem of constructing flexible graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors correctly identify a major limitation of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), which can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The paper’s main contributions are threefold: first, it provides a valuable theoretical clarification by proving that a previously proposed truncation method is invalid. Second, it introduces three novel models—the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that allow for both positive and negative dependencies. These models are developed within the established framework of Yang et al. for constructing graphical models from univariate exponential families.

The paper's strengths are significant. It tackles a well-motivated problem, and the formal proof invalidating the previously suggested Winsorized PGM is a solid contribution that corrects a misconception in the literature. The proposed TPGM is a natural and well-justified alternative, while the QPGM and SPGM are particularly novel and theoretically appealing, creatively modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family to solve the core normalizability issue. The manuscript is also well-written and presents its theoretical derivations clearly.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its experimental evaluation, which is insufficient to establish the practical merit of the proposed models. The evaluation appears biased toward the new methods, with synthetic experiments tailored to the proposed data-generating processes. The comparisons with existing methods are incomplete; for instance, the real-data analysis omits strong and relevant competitors like copula-based graphical models, which are a standard approach for such problems. Furthermore, the reported results are largely qualitative (ROC curves, network visualizations) or anecdotal. A rigorous, objective evaluation using standard metrics like held-out test log-probability is necessary to properly assess the quality of the learned distributions. The theoretical similarity of the QPGM to a Gaussian graphical model also raises questions about its practical distinctiveness, which the experiments do not adequately resolve.

In summary, while the paper introduces theoretically appealing approaches, the experimental evidence is not yet convincing enough to establish their practical utility over existing methods. A major revision of the experimental section would be needed to demonstrate the significance of the proposed models.
