This paper addresses a significant limitation in Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for modeling multivariate count data. Standard PGMs, as developed by Besag and extended by Yang et al., are restricted to modeling only negative conditional dependencies due to the normalizability requirement of the joint distribution over an infinite domain. This severely limits their practical applicability. The authors' objective is to develop classes of Poisson-like graphical models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it analyzes two truncation-based strategies. It provides a formal proof that a previously proposed method based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution (Kaiser and Cressie, 1997) does not lead to a valid joint distribution. The authors then propose a new, valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) that does belong to the exponential family and allows for arbitrary dependencies. Second, to overcome some limitations of the TPGM, the paper investigates more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. It proves that for a model with linear sufficient statistics to be normalizable with positive dependencies, the base measure must scale at least quadratically. This leads to the proposal of a novel Quadratic PGM (QPGM). Finally, to allow for heavier tails than the Gaussian-like QPGM, the authors propose a Sub-linear PGM (SPGM) which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, thereby ensuring normalizability with the original Poisson base measure. The paper concludes with experiments on simulated data and a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed models compared to existing methods.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis, including proofs for the invalidity of a prior approach (Theorem 1), the necessity of a quadratic base measure under certain conditions (Theorem 3), and the validity and normalizability of the newly proposed models. The work is presented as a complete and polished contribution. The experimental evaluation is sound, comparing the proposed models against relevant baselines on both synthetic and real data, which convincingly demonstrates their practical utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the progression of ideas is logical and easy to follow. The authors first identify a core problem, critique existing but flawed solutions, and then systematically develop and present their novel contributions. The mathematical formulations are precise.

A few minor points could further improve clarity:
- The authors' critiques of their own models, while commendably honest, may be slightly overstated. For instance, the stated drawbacks of the TPGM (issues when counts approach the truncation level R) might not be a major concern in domains where counts are naturally low or have a known upper bound. Similarly, the "Gaussian-esque thin tails" of the QPGM are presented as a drawback, but this property could be entirely appropriate or even desirable for certain types of count data. Framing these points as model characteristics rather than inherent weaknesses would be more nuanced.
- The term "sparsistent" is used without definition. While it has an established meaning in the high-dimensional statistics literature, a brief parenthetical definition or footnote would improve accessibility for the broader NIPS audience.
- A minor typo was noted: "graphical mode distributions" should likely be "graphical model distributions".

**Originality:** The work is highly original. It tackles a well-known, fundamental problem in graphical models for count data. The proposed solutions—the valid TPGM, the QPGM derived from first principles, and the SPGM with modified sufficient statistics—are all novel contributions. The disproof of the validity of the Winsorized PGM construction is also a significant and original finding. The paper does an excellent job of situating its contributions within the context of related work and clearly distinguishing itself from prior art.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and impactful. Count data is ubiquitous in machine learning and applied statistics (e.g., genomics, user activity, spatial statistics), and the lack of flexible graphical models that can capture rich dependency structures has been a major gap. This paper provides a well-founded and practical set of solutions to this problem. Researchers and practitioners in various fields are likely to find these new models useful and build upon them. The work represents a clear advance in the state of the art for modeling high-dimensional count data. The successful application to discovering a microRNA network from cancer data underscores the practical importance of the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Solves a Key Problem:** The paper successfully addresses the critical limitation of existing PGMs, enabling them to model both positive and negative dependencies.
2.  **Novel and Sound Contributions:** It introduces three distinct, theoretically-grounded, and well-motivated models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
3.  **Valuable Negative Result:** The formal disproof of a previously proposed method is an important contribution in its own right, clarifying the literature.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The models are shown to outperform relevant baselines on both synthetic and important real-world biological data.
5.  **High-Quality Presentation:** The paper is clear, well-written, and methodologically rigorous.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Minor Presentation Nuances:** The discussion of the limitations of the proposed models could be framed more as a characterization of their properties, acknowledging that these properties may be suitable for certain data-generating processes. This is a very minor point and easily addressed.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to an important area. It is technically strong, clearly written, and demonstrates practical value. I strongly recommend acceptance.
