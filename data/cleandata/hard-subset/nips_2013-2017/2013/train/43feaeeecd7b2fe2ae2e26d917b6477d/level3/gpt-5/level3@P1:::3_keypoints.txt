- Paper addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models: inability to represent positive conditional dependencies on unbounded counts.  
- Main theoretical contributions: Theorem 1 (Winsorized/point-mass truncation cannot yield a consistent joint MRF) and Theorem 3 (necessity of Ω(z^2) base measure for linear sufficient statistics).  
- Introduces three constructive families: TPGM (exponential-family truncation giving a proper MRF), QPGM (quadratic base measure with linear sufficient stats), and SPGM (sublinear sufficient statistic preserving Poisson-like base measure).  
- Estimation via ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection; uses variational upper bounds to handle intractable log-partitions for node-conditionals.  
- Empirical evaluation includes simulations and an RNA-seq miRNA case study showing advantages over standard PGM negativity restriction.  
- Strength: clearly identifies a genuine modeling gap and provides solid theoretical results refining normalizability for count MRFs.  
- Strength: constructive modeling offers complementary trade-offs; SPGM is an elegant middle ground preserving Poisson-like behavior while permitting positive dependencies.  
- Strength: estimation pipeline integrates with standard ℓ1-regularized methods and pragmatic variational approximations.  
- Weakness: QPGM induces thin, Gaussian-like tails that may mismatch overdispersed, heavy-tailed count data (RNA-seq, spatial incidence); needs marginal fit diagnostics versus NB/PLN.  
- Weakness: SPGM node-conditionals lack closed-form log-partitions and rely on bounds, undermining conditional-specification motivation and raising questions about approximation error and numerical stability.  
- Weakness: tuning and practical guidance for R and R0 (SPGM/TPGM) are sparse; need principled selection strategies and sensitivity analyses.  
- Weakness: model evaluation focuses on edge-recovery ROC; lacks goodness-of-fit checks, held-out predictive performance, and calibration diagnostics.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to key baselines like Poisson log-normal, negative-binomial graphical models, and Gaussian-copula count models common in genomics/spatial stats.  
- Clarity issue: literature discussion conflates latent-Gaussian/coupling approaches with observable-space models; needs clearer distinctions and correct citations (e.g., copulas, modern scalable log-linear variants).  
- Clarity issue: spatial statistics literature (Poisson/PLN/CAR hierarchies and high-dimensional variants) is under-discussed; should articulate differences to observable-space MRFs.  
- Clarity issue: provide intuition/sketch for Theorem 3 linking base-measure growth to pairwise term growth and normalizeability.  
- Clarity issue: clean up notation (define B(·), C(·) once), and specify algorithmic details (step sizes, stopping criteria, complexity, gradient computation for SPGM surrogate).  
- Empirical presentation issue: for RNA-seq, include overdispersion diagnostics and plot SPGM-implied PMFs vs empirical marginals for representative miRNAs; update supporting refs.  
- Originality: non-existence result for Winsorized conditionals, Ω(z^2) necessity, and SPGM construction are novel; adapting neighborhood selection is incremental but suitable.  
- Significance: models could become standard for observable-space count dependence if practical guidance and empirical validation are strengthened.  
- Actionable: add comparisons to Poisson-lognormal/negative-binomial and Gaussian-copula count models with sparse latent precision.  
- Actionable: provide selection guidance and sensitivity studies for R and R0 and report computational costs as R varies.  
- Actionable: include marginal/conditional fit diagnostics, tail analyses, and held-out predictive metrics to assess QPGM/SPGM vs baselines.  
- Actionable: quantify approximation error from SPGM’s variational bound and measure its effect on edge recovery in controlled experiments.  
- Actionable: expand and correct related-work discussion (copulas, spatial models, scalable log-linear methods) and clarify modeling trade-offs.  
- Actionable: report code/implementation details to ensure reproducibility.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a real limitation with rigorous theory, introduces learnable families allowing positive/negative dependencies, and provides theorems of independent interest.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical applicability underdeveloped (tuning, diagnostics, thin tails for QPGM), SPGM relies on unquantified approximations, and comparisons/literature coverage are incomplete.
