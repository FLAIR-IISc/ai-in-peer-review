

This paper addresses an important gap in modeling multivariate count data using graphical models. The authors correctly identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only capture negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. They propose three novel approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which aim to enable both positive and negative dependencies. The paper builds on Yang et al.'s framework but extends it through novel modifications to the exponential-family base measure and sufficient statistics.

The paper's strengths include a clear problem statement, rigorous mathematical proofs regarding truncation limitations (Theorem 1), and the well-justified TPGM construction. The theoretical framework is sound, and the paper is generally well-written with appropriate mathematical notation.

However, significant weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. The theoretical novelty is limited as much of the framework follows directly from Yang et al. The experimental evaluation is particularly concerning: synthetic experiments are tailored to the proposed methods, real-data comparisons omit copula-based alternatives (using them in a black-box manner with inappropriate marginal choices), and reported results lack objective metrics like test log-probability. The identical performance to Glasso is suspicious and suggests either baseline issues or insufficient evaluation. The paper also unnecessarily details Section 3.1 while failing to present the key Figure 3.2 in the main text.

While the proposed models are theoretically appealing, the experimental evidence is insufficient to establish their practical merit. The authors should include all proofs in supplementary material and provide more comprehensive validation with appropriate baselines and objective metrics. The paper would benefit from addressing the copula competitors with proper marginal choices (e.g., KDE) and demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods.

Overall, this paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to Poisson graphical models but fails to convincingly demonstrate practical utility through rigorous experimental evaluation. The significance is therefore limited by the insufficient experimental validation, despite the sound theoretical foundation.
