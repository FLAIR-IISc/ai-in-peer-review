- Addresses modeling multivariate count data, a setting where Gaussian graphical models are ill-suited.  
- Identifies a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs): inability to represent positive correlations due to normalizability constraints.  
- Proposes three novel approaches to overcome this: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  
- Paper is well-written with clear motivations and technical exposition of each method.  
- Experimental evaluation on simulated and real data demonstrates practical utility of the proposed methods.  
- Overall contribution is strong, original, and has significant potential impact.  
- Effectively situates work within existing literature and correctly identifies limitations of log-linear models, copula transforms, and standard PGM.  
- Theoretical discussion of PGMs and truncation challenges is insightful.  
- Careful derivation of QPGM and SPGM with connections to exponential family theory.  
- Variational bounds linking QPGM to Gaussian graphical models provide useful intuition and possible computational advantage.  
- Strengths: important problem, novel/theoretically sound solutions, and proofs of normalizability for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM are significant.  
- Experiments strongly support effectiveness—SPGM in particular recovers network structure well; comparisons and a microRNA case study show practical relevance.  
- Weakness: criticism of TPGM’s truncation approach may be overstated; practicality in low-count domains is not fully considered.  
- Weakness: critique of QPGM’s Gaussian-like thin tails may be too strong since thin tails can be acceptable or desirable in some applications.  
- Suggestion: authors should acknowledge scenarios where QPGM is appropriate.  
- Terminology issue: the term “sparsistent” should be briefly defined or referenced for readers unfamiliar with it.  
- Minor editorial fixes: correct “graphical mode distributions” → “graphical models” (p.3) and rephrase unclear sentence on p.5.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/theoretically grounded, clear presentation, strong experiments, addresses important problem, SPGM promising.  
- Arguments against acceptance: overly strong criticisms may limit practitioner adoption, minor editorial issues, and lack of formal analysis of estimator properties (e.g., consistency).  
- Overall assessment: significant advance; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
