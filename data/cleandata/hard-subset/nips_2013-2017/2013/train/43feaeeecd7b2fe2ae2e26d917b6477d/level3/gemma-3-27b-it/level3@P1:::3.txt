This paper addresses the challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure defined by an undirected graph. The authors build upon a long history of work in this area, starting with Besag’s foundational contributions and continuing with more recent approaches like those by Arnold et al. The core issue is that standard Poisson graphical models are limited to modeling negative dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The paper proposes three novel approaches – Truncated PGM, Quadratic PGM, and Sublinear PGM – to overcome this limitation and enable the modeling of both positive and negative dependencies between count variables.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical contribution and the relative simplicity of the resulting estimation algorithms. Allowing for both positive and negative dependencies is a significant step forward for modeling count data with complex relationships. The authors correctly identify the normalizability issues with existing approaches and provide a clear rationale for their proposed modifications to the Poisson distribution. The inclusion of numerical experiments, including a case study using microRNA expression data, strengthens the paper and demonstrates the practical potential of the methods. The demonstration on real data is particularly valuable, as it attempts to connect the theoretical work to a relevant application.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the Quadratic PGM offers theoretical advantages, it yields marginally thinner tails than desirable. The Sublinear PGM, while addressing this issue, lacks closed-form conditional distributions, raising concerns about whether it truly adheres to the conditional specification motivation inherent in graphical models.  This is a crucial point; without closed-form conditionals, the interpretation as a graphical model becomes less direct, and the benefits of the graphical structure are somewhat diminished.

A significant concern is the lack of strong practical justification for adopting these models over simpler alternatives. The authors state that the Poisson distribution is often a poor empirical fit, yet they do not provide any supporting data or visualizations demonstrating this. Presenting a sample of the breast cancer data alongside the fitted SPGM probability mass function (PMF) would significantly strengthen their argument. It would allow readers to see how well the proposed models capture the features of real-world count data.

Several aspects of the method lack sufficient detail for practical application. The roles of the constants R and R0, used in the Truncated and Sublinear PGMs respectively, are not adequately explained. The paper needs to offer clearer guidance on how to select these parameters, perhaps through a discussion of their impact on model behavior and potential sensitivity analysis.  Without this guidance, practitioners may struggle to effectively utilize the proposed methods.

The literature review is surprisingly incomplete. The authors seem to misrepresent the capabilities of copula-based approaches, implying they do not impose MRF independence. While some copula approaches may not directly model the graphical structure in the observable space, careful construction can achieve this. Furthermore, the discussion of work by Liu et al. [8] appears somewhat dismissive. The authors also seem to underestimate the relevance of existing high-dimensional count models from fields like spatial statistics. A more thorough review would demonstrate a deeper understanding of the existing landscape and position this work more effectively. The reference to microarrays being replaced by sequencing reads ([22]) is dated. An updated reference reflecting the current state-of-the-art would improve the credibility of the application section.

In summary, the theoretical contributions of this paper are valid and interesting, but the practical motivation for preferring these complex models over simpler alternatives, like a Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model, remains weak. The lack of detailed guidance on parameter selection and the shortcomings in the literature review further detract from the paper’s overall impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to overcoming limitations of existing Poisson graphical models.
*   Demonstrated applicability through numerical experiments and a case study.
*   Potential for modeling complex dependencies in count-valued data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak practical motivation and limited empirical evidence.
*   Lack of detailed guidance on parameter selection.
*   Incomplete and potentially misleading literature review.
*   SPGM lacks closed-form conditionals, questioning its graphical model interpretation.



I recommend a **weak reject**. The paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, but the practical concerns and deficiencies in the presentation outweigh its strengths. Addressing the issues related to practical justification, parameter selection, and the literature review would significantly improve the paper’s appeal and impact.
