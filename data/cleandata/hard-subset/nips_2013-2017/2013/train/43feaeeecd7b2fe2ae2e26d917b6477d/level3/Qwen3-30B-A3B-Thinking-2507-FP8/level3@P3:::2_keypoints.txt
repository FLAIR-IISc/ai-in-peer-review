- Paper addresses lack of flexible multivariate count models and extends Yang et al.'s node-conditional framework to allow positive conditional dependencies.
- Main contributions: (1) proof that Winsorized Poisson cannot yield a valid joint graphical model (Theorem 1), (2) Truncated PGM (TPGM) enabling both dependency signs, (3) Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) altering base measure/sufficient statistics.
- Theoretical result: normalizability while permitting both dependency signs requires quadratic scaling of the base measure, motivating QPGM (Theorem 3).
- SPGM uses sub-linear sufficient statistics to obtain heavier tails and broader parameter ranges.
- Strength: correctly identifies a fundamental limitation of existing Poisson graphical models and provides rigorous theoretical fixes.
- Strength: Theorem 1 corrects a common misconception about Winsorized Poisson models.
- Strength: TPGM is natural and well-justified; QPGM/SPGM are novel modifications of the exponential-family Poisson.
- Strength: Paper is clearly written with complete proofs in the supplement and includes a real-data microRNA sequencing application.
- Weakness: Limited theoretical novelty since much of the framework follows Yang et al. (2013); contributions not sufficiently differentiated.
- Weakness: Experimental evaluation appears biased—synthetic data is generated from the proposed models, favoring them.
- Weakness: Important baselines (copula-based alternatives) are omitted, and competing methods are used with inappropriate marginals (e.g., Gaussian for count data).
- Weakness: Real-data results are qualitative and lack objective metrics (e.g., test log-probabilities) for rigorous comparison.
- Weakness: Identical performance to Glasso on breast cancer data is suspicious and suggests baseline or evaluation issues.
- Weakness: Section 3.1 contains an unnecessary detailed bound; a key figure from Section 3.2 should be moved from the appendix to the main text.
- Quality assessment: Theoretical development is technically sound, but insufficient experimental validation and missing sparsistency analysis limit the paper's completeness.
- Clarity assessment: Overall well-organized and clear, though some sections should be streamlined; proofs appropriately in supplementary material.
- Originality assessment: Specific models are novel but overall approach closely aligns with prior work, limiting perceived originality.
- Significance assessment: Problem is important, but practical advantages are not convincingly demonstrated due to biased experiments and missing objective evaluations.
- Overall recommendation: Theoretically appealing but recommend rejection pending substantial revisions—add objective evaluation metrics, fair comparisons with appropriate copula-based baselines, and address experimental biases.
