This paper introduces novel approaches to constructing multivariate count distributions, specifically Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which are essential in modeling count data. The authors correctly identify the limitations of current approaches, including the inability to capture positive conditional dependencies due to the requirement of non-positive edge weights for normalizability.

The paper begins by discussing the standard Poisson graphical model distribution and its limitations. The authors then investigate two natural ways to truncate the Poisson distribution and show that only one of these approaches leads to a valid joint distribution. This new approach, called the Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), can accommodate a wider range of conditional dependencies but still has limitations.

To address these limitations, the authors propose two novel variants of the Poisson distribution: the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) and the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM). The QPGM alters the exponential-family base measure to allow for both positive and negative conditional dependencies, while the SPGM modifies the sufficient statistics to control the linear Poisson sufficient statistics. Both models are shown to be normalizable and can capture rich dependence structures between count-valued variables.

The paper also presents numerical experiments to evaluate the performance of the proposed models. The authors compare the TPGM and SPGM methods with Gaussian graphical models and other approaches on simulated networks and a real-world dataset of microRNA expression. The results show that the proposed models outperform the competitors, especially in capturing positive conditional dependencies.

However, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the authors could have provided more objective evaluations, such as test log-probability. Additionally, the comparison with copula-based alternatives is not thorough, and the use of inappropriate marginal choices may have biased the results.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approaches. However, some sections, such as Section 3.1, are overly detailed and could be condensed. The key figure for Section 3.2 is not presented in the main text, which would have helped to better explain the SPGM.

In conclusion, the paper proposes theoretically appealing approaches to constructing multivariate count distributions. However, the experimental evidence is insufficient to establish their practical merit. Further evaluation and comparison with other approaches are needed to fully assess the performance of the proposed models.
