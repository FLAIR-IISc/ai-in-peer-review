- Addresses multivariate graphical models for count data and the PGM limitation to non-positive pairwise parameters.
- Proves Winsorized truncation cannot produce a valid joint MRF under the Yang et al. construction.
- Proposes three alternatives: TPGM (exponential-family-consistent truncation), QPGM (quadratic base measure), and SPGM (sublinear sufficient statistics).
- Discusses neighborhood selection for structure learning and presents synthetic experiments plus a miRNA case study.
- Positions work appropriately relative to Besag, Yang et al., and alternative approaches (copulas, sums of Poissons, spatial models).
- Strength: tackles an important, widely applicable problem in genomics, epidemiology, recommendation, and text.
- Strength: provides a correct and useful negative result clarifying Winsorization misconceptions.
- Strength: TPGM is principled and restores mixed-sign dependencies on bounded support.
- Strength: QPGM/SPGM give novel insights on base measures and sufficient statistics with theorems on normalizability.
- Strength: Writing is generally clear and model constructions are easy to follow.
- Weakness: Limited theoretical novelty beyond Yang et al.’s framework; main new theory is narrow.
- Weakness: Variational bound in Section 3.1 adds little insight and could be moved to the supplement.
- Weakness: Synthetic experiments appear tailored to favor the proposed methods and omit scenarios favoring competitors.
- Weakness: Real-data evaluation is qualitative/anecdotal and lacks objective metrics (held-out log-probability, predictive likelihood).
- Weakness: Copula baselines are unfairly implemented; need nonparametric/discrete-aware marginals and sensitivity checks.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to Poisson-lognormal and other latent-variable count models.
- Weakness: Presentation issues—overly detailed Sec 3.1 and SPGM sufficient-statistic figure should be in the main text.
- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns—some proofs deferred and missing sensitivity analyses for truncation parameters (R, R0).
- Quality: Technically sound overall, but empirical support is insufficient and sometimes questionable.
- Clarity: Generally clear; recommend moving lengthy bounds to supplement and expanding SPGM explanation and algorithmic details.
- Originality: Moderate—useful ideas (base-measure/sufficient-statistic changes and Winsorization invalidation) but built on established framework.
- Significance: Potentially significant if validated with stronger empirical evidence.
- Suggestions: Add objective evaluations, strengthen and correctly configure baselines (proper copulas, Poisson-lognormal), audit baseline implementations, run sensitivity analyses, include all proofs, and release code.
- Pros: Addresses positive-dependency gap for counts; principled, interpretable model families; clear normalizability exposition.
- Cons: Weak and possibly biased empirical evaluation, limited new theory, missing strong baselines, and some distracting presentation choices.
- Recommendation: Weak reject — promising modeling ideas but insufficient empirical validation; stronger quantitative comparisons could change the decision.
