This paper proposes several new models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables, specifically addressing the limitations of the existing Poisson graphical model (PGM) which can only model negative conditional dependencies. The authors argue that the PGM's default model is unsatisfactory and propose three novel approaches to fix this issue.

The first approach involves a new truncation method, which leads to the Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM). While the TPGM can model both positive and negative dependencies, it has some drawbacks, such as a bounded domain and issues with large count values.

The second approach modifies the Poisson distribution more fundamentally by changing its sufficient statistic and base measure. This leads to the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), which has a Gaussian-esque thin tail and allows for both positive and negative edge-parameters.

The third approach, the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM), uses a sublinear sufficient statistic and a milder base measure, resulting in a heavier tail and a broader set of feasible pairwise parameters.

The paper presents experimental evaluations on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating the performance of the proposed models. The results show that the TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM in recovering the true network structure from count-valued data.

Strengths of the paper:

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of the existing PGM and proposes novel solutions to overcome it.
* The proposed models are well-motivated and theoretically sound.
* The experimental evaluations are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed models.

Weaknesses of the paper:

* The reviewer is unconvinced by the "big negatives" claimed for the truncation model, as it may be suitable for domains with low counts.
* The critique of the quadratic model is questionable, as Gaussian-like thin tails could be appropriate in some settings.
* The term "sparsistent" is unclear, despite its established usage.

Minor editorial suggestions:

* Change "graphical mode distributions" to "model" (p.3)
* Clarify "Note that the log..." (p.5) to something like "Note that although the log...".

Overall, the paper is well-written, original, and significant, with results that are important and likely to be used by other researchers. The reviewer recommends acceptance of the paper.
