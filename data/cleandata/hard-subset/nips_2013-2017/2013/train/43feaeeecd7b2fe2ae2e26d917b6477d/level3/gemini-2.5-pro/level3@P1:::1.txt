This paper addresses a significant and well-known limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) for modeling multivariate count data: their inability to capture positive conditional dependencies between variables. Standard PGMs, as formulated by Besag and extended by Yang et al., require non-positive edge weights for the joint distribution to be normalizable over the infinite domain of counts. This severely restricts their applicability. The authors make several key contributions to overcome this problem by proposing three novel classes of Poisson-like graphical models that can accommodate both positive and negative dependencies.

The paper begins by analyzing truncation-based approaches. It first provides a valuable negative result, proving that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional distribution (Kaiser and Cressie, 2000) cannot lead to a valid joint distribution, correcting a misconception in the literature. The authors then propose their first novel model, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), which uses a different truncation strategy that preserves the exponential family form and thus yields a valid, normalizable joint distribution without restrictions on the sign of the edge weights.

Recognizing the limitations of the TPGM (e.g., a pre-specified upper bound on counts), the paper then explores more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution itself. The authors first prove that to allow for positive dependencies while retaining linear sufficient statistics (i.e., B(X)=X), the base measure of the distribution must scale at least quadratically. This insight leads to their second model, the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure. This model is normalizable under conditions analogous to Gaussian Graphical Models, such as having a negative-definite parameter matrix. Finally, to offer a model with heavier tails than the "Gaussian-esque" QPGM, the authors propose the Sub-linear PGM (SPGM). This third model reverts to the original Poisson base measure but modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, which ensures normalizability for any real-valued pairwise parameters. The paper concludes with an empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world microRNA sequencing data, demonstrating that the proposed models, particularly TPGM and SPGM, outperform both Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM in network recovery.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a highly significant and practical problem. Count data is ubiquitous, and the inability of existing PGMs to model cooperative or excitatory relationships is a major roadblock. This work provides a comprehensive and effective set of solutions, which will likely be of great interest and utility to both researchers and practitioners in fields like genomics, neuroscience, and social network analysis.
*   **Originality:** The contributions are highly original. The disproof of the Winsorized PGM approach is a novel and important clarification. The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are new and are motivated by a clear and logical progression of ideas, moving from simple truncation to more fundamental modifications of the underlying distribution. The theoretical result on the necessity of a quadratic base measure (Theorem 3) is particularly insightful.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs, and the derivations are clear. The proposed framework for model learning via neighborhood selection is appropriate and builds on established methods. The experimental validation is solid, using relevant simulation settings (hub, scale-free graphs) and a compelling real-world case study that highlights the ability of the new models to uncover biologically meaningful network structures missed by previous methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, and the narrative flow—identifying a problem with one approach and using that to motivate the next—makes the paper easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The weaknesses of this paper are minor and mainly relate to the framing of the contributions and small points of clarity. They do not detract from the overall strength of the work.

*   The paper presents the limitations of the TPGM (a priori bounded domain) and QPGM ("Gaussian-esque thin tails") as significant drawbacks that strongly motivate the subsequent models. This framing could be more nuanced. For many real-world datasets where counts are naturally low or an upper bound is reasonable, the TPGM might be a perfectly suitable and simpler choice. Similarly, the thin-tailed property of the QPGM could be considered a feature, not a bug, for systems where extreme co-occurrences are rare. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the types of data or assumptions for which each of the three proposed models is most appropriate.
*   The critique of the QPGM as being "Gaussian-esque" feels slightly understated. The model is essentially a Gaussian graphical model on an integer lattice. While this is a valid and useful model, it would be helpful to explicitly state this connection and perhaps discuss more directly when one might prefer this over, for instance, a non-paranormal transformation of the data followed by a standard GGM.
*   The paper uses the term "sparsistent" without providing a definition or citation. While this term is established in the high-dimensional statistics literature, a brief parenthetical definition (e.g., "consistent for sparse model selection") would improve the paper's accessibility to the broader machine learning audience at NIPS.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that provides a thorough, well-motivated, and technically sound solution to a long-standing problem in probabilistic modeling. It is well-written, the contributions are significant and original, and the empirical results are convincing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
