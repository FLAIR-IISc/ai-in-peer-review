This paper tackles a longstanding limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the inability to represent positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints on unbounded count supports. Building on Besag’s auto-models [17] and the exponential family MRF framework of Yang et al. [18,19], the authors (i) prove that the commonly proposed Winsorized/point-mass truncation cannot yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1), (ii) introduce an alternative exponential-family truncation that does yield a proper MRF (TPGM), (iii) establish a necessary condition for allowing both positive and negative dependencies with linear sufficient statistics—namely, a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3)—and (iv) propose two new families: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with linear sufficient statistics and quadratic base measure, and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains a Poisson-like base measure but replaces the sufficient statistic with a piecewise sublinear function to temper growth. Structure learning is done via `1-penalized neighborhood selection, with simulations and an RNA-seq miRNA case study.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself against auto-Poisson models [17] and the exponential family graph selection literature [1,5,18,19], clarifies limitations of multivariate Poisson constructions via sums [14,15] (positive correlations only) and copula-based approaches [7–10], and revisits truncation ideas in spatial statistics [20,21]. The theoretical results extend and refine what is known about normalizability for exponential family MRFs over count supports, which is of broad methodological interest.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a genuine gap: PGMs over unbounded counts cannot encode positive conditional dependencies; naive truncations can fail to define a joint model.
- Solid theory: Theorem 1 (non-existence for Winsorized-node conditionals), Theorem 3 (Ω(Z2) base-measure condition for linear statistics), and normalizability conditions for QPGM/SPGM are interesting and useful contributions.
- Constructive modeling: Three families (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) offering complementary trade-offs; SPGM in particular is an elegant way to preserve a Poisson-like base measure while permitting richer dependencies.
- Estimation pipeline is compatible with standard `1-regularized neighborhood selection; variational bounds give a pragmatic way to optimize nodewise conditionals when log-partitions are intractable.
- Empirics show advantages on simulated count networks and a meaningful application (miRNA RNA-seq), where original PGM’s negativity restriction is indeed limiting.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- QPGM tail behavior: With quadratic base measure, QPGM induces Gaussian-esque thin tails. Many count data sets (RNA-seq, spatial incidence) are overdispersed and heavy-tailed; please quantify the mismatch via marginal fit diagnostics (tail probabilities, dispersion) and compare to Poisson-lognormal or negative binomial benchmarks.
- SPGM conditional likelihoods: Node-conditionals lack closed-form log-partition and rely on upper bounds. This undercuts the conditional-specification motivation and raises whether “Poisson-like” is still appropriate. Please detail numerical stability, approximation error, and its effect on edge recovery (e.g., bounds-to-truth gap in a controlled setting).
- Practical tuning of R and R0: The roles of R and R0 (SPGM) are central but guidance is sparse. Provide principled selection strategies (e.g., via percentile of counts, cross-validation on held-out likelihood, prior elicitation), sensitivity analyses, and discuss computational implications as R increases (TPGM) or as R0, R vary (SPGM).
- Model fit: Beyond ROC for edge recovery, include goodness-of-fit checks (posterior predictive checks or PIT histograms for nodewise counts, calibration of conditional mean-variance curves), and report held-out predictive performance.
- Alternative baselines: Please compare to latent Gaussian count models (Poisson log-normal MRFs or Gaussian copula + Poisson/negative binomial marginals), which are widely used in genomics and spatial statistics, even if their conditional independencies live in latent space rather than observable space.

Clarity
- Literature review gaps/misstatements: The discussion of copulas seems to conflate marginal transform models with joint count copulas; clarify that many Gaussian copula approaches [7–10] impose sparsity in a latent Gaussian domain, not the observed count domain, explaining the modeling trade-off rather than dismissing them. Revisit [11] to accurately describe the high-parameter regime for log-linear contingency models and acknowledge scalable modern variants (e.g., sparsity/low-rank structure).
- Spatial statistics: Expand coverage of spatial Poisson/Poisson-lognormal/CAR-type hierarchical models [16] and their high-dimensional variants; clearly articulate how your observable-space MRF differs in aims and properties.
- Provide intuition for Theorem 3: A brief sketch connecting growth rates of base measure versus quadratic pairwise terms would help readers.
- Notation and algorithms: Define B(·), C(·) once and keep consistent; specify step sizes, stopping criteria, and complexity for proximal-gradient, and clarify how you compute gradients for SPGM’s variational surrogate.
- Empirical presentation: For the RNA-seq data, include overdispersion diagnostics and a plot of the SPGM implied PMF versus empirical marginal for a few representative miRNAs. Please update or supplement [22] with more recent RNA-seq-vs-microarray references (circa 2011–2013) to support the “replacing” claim.

Originality
- The non-existence result for Winsorized conditionals, the Ω(Z2) necessity, and the SPGM construction are novel and insightful. Adapting neighborhood selection is incremental but appropriate.

Significance
- If the practical guidance and empirical validation are strengthened, these models could become a standard tool for observable-space dependence in count networks. As is, the case for preferring them over simpler latent-Gaussian approaches remains suggestive rather than conclusive; more thorough comparisons and fit diagnostics would materially raise impact.

Actionable suggestions
- Add comparisons to Poisson-lognormal/negative-binomial graphical models or Gaussian-copula count models with sparse latent precision.
- Provide selection guidance and sensitivity studies for R and R0; report computational costs.
- Include marginal/conditional fit diagnostics and tail behavior analyses (QPGM vs SPGM vs baselines).
- Quantify approximation error from SPGM’s variational bound and its effect on edge recovery.
- Clarify and expand related work, especially spatial statistics and copulas; correct any misstatements.
- Report code/implementation details to ensure reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a real and fundamental limitation of PGMs for counts with rigorous theory.
- Introduces practically learnable families that can encode both positive and negative dependencies.
- Clear methodological contributions (Theorems 1 and 3) of independent interest.
- Promising empirical results on synthetic and genomic data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical applicability underdeveloped: unclear tuning of R, R0; limited fit diagnostics; thin tails for QPGM.
- SPGM relies on approximations without quantified error; conditional-specification narrative is weakened.
- Comparisons omit key latent-Gaussian/negative-binomial baselines; literature review needs strengthening.
- Limited guidance for practitioners on when to prefer these models over simpler, well-established alternatives.
