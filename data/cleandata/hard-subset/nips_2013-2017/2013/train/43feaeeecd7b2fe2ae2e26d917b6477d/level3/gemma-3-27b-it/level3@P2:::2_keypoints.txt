- Addresses the important problem of modeling multivariate count data where existing graphical models are ill-suited.  
- Correctly identifies the limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) in only capturing negative dependencies.  
- Shows a previously proposed truncation fix is invalid.  
- Proposes three new models: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) to model both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Builds on Yang et al.’s theoretical framework by modifying the underlying Poisson distribution.  
- TPGM is a natural truncation approach; QPGM and SPGM modify the base measure and sufficient statistics more fundamentally.  
- Theoretical derivations are sound and well connected to prior literature but offer limited novelty beyond extensions of Yang et al.  
- Practical impact depends on demonstrating clear empirical advantages, which the paper fails to convincingly do.  
- Synthetic experiments appear tailored to favor the proposed models and comparisons are incomplete.  
- Major omission: no comparison to copula-based multivariate count methods.  
- Copula comparisons that are present are implemented rudimentarily and marginal choices may bias results; better marginal estimation (e.g., kernel density) is needed.  
- Results are largely qualitative and anecdotal; objective metrics (test log-probability, AUC, etc.) are missing across experiments.  
- Claim of performance comparable to Glasso is questionable and suggests possible baseline implementation or experimental setup issues.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, but Section 3.1 is overly detailed and proofs should be moved to supplement; key illustration for Section 3.2 should be in the main text.  
- Strengths: tackles an important problem, provides rigorous analysis, and introduces plausible new model variants (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).  
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical novelty, weak/biased experimental validation, incomplete comparisons, lack of objective metrics, and rudimentary baseline implementations.  
- Acceptance arguments: theoretically sound extensions that fix a known flaw and introduce models capable of positive and negative dependencies.  
- Rejection arguments: insufficient empirical evidence and missing comparisons reduce the paper’s impact; needs stronger, more rigorous experiments.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically appealing but empirically under-validated; recommend rejection with encouragement to strengthen experiments, include copula comparisons, use objective metrics, and evaluate on diverse datasets.
