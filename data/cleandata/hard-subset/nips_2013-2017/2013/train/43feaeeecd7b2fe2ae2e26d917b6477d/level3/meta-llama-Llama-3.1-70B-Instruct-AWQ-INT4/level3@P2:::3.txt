This paper proposes novel multivariate Poisson distributions for modeling count data, a common type of data in many applications, including genomics and spatial statistics. The authors' objective is to specify a joint graphical model distribution over the set of non-negative integers that can capture rich dependence structures between variables. The paper is well-motivated, as existing classes of Poisson graphical models can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability issues.

The authors begin by discussing the limitations of existing Poisson graphical models, which can only capture negative dependencies. They then investigate two strategies for truncating the Poisson distribution and show that only one of these leads to a valid joint distribution. This model, called the Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), can accommodate a wider range of conditional dependencies but still has some limitations. To address these limitations, the authors propose two additional novel variants of the Poisson distribution and their corresponding joint graphical model distributions, called the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) and the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM).

The QPGM has a quadratic base measure, which allows for both positive and negative conditional dependencies. However, this model has a Gaussian-esque thin tail, which may not be desirable in some applications. The SPGM, on the other hand, has a heavier tail and allows for a broader set of feasible pairwise parameters. However, it lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which may undermine its conditional-specification motivation.

The authors demonstrate the performance of their methods by learning simulated networks and a network from microRNA-sequencing data. The results show that the TPGM and SPGM methods outperform Gaussian graphical models and other existing methods in recovering the true network structure.

* The authors propose novel multivariate Poisson distributions that can capture rich dependence structures between variables.

Weaknesses of the paper:

* The practical motivation for using the Poisson distribution is questioned, as it is often a poor empirical fit.
* The role and selection of constants R and R0 need explanation and practitioner guidance.
