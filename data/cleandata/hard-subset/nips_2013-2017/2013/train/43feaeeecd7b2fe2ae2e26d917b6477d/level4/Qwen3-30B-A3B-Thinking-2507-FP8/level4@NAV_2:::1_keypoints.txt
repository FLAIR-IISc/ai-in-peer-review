- Problem: Existing Poisson graphical models (e.g., Yang et al.) force non-positive edge weights, preventing modeling of positive conditional dependencies in count data.

- Contribution 1: Truncated PGM (TPGM) — a novel truncation approach producing a valid joint distribution with more flexible dependencies.

- Contribution 2: Quadratic PGM (QPGM) — uses a quadratic base measure to allow both positive and negative conditional dependencies while maintaining normalizability.

- Contribution 3: Sublinear PGM (SPGM) — employs sub-linear sufficient statistics to yield heavier tails and broader feasible parameter regions.

- Validation: Methods validated on synthetic experiments and a breast cancer microRNA sequencing case study, showing improved recovery of networks with mixed-sign edges and biologically relevant findings.

- Strength — Quality: Strong theoretical contributions with rigorous normalizability proofs (Theorems 1–4) and consistent experimental advantages over existing methods.

- Strength — Clarity: Paper is well-organized and clearly written with detailed derivations, proofs, and experimental setup.

- Strength — Originality: The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are novel extensions not previously considered and are well-differentiated from prior work.

- Strength — Significance: Addresses a critical gap for count-data graphical modeling with clear applications (genomics, spatial statistics); case study demonstrates practical value.

- Weakness — Quality: Discussion of limitations for TPGM (bounded domain) and QPGM (Gaussian-like thin tails) lacks nuance and should acknowledge contexts where these traits are desirable.

- Weakness — Clarity: Minor typographical errors and use of undefined terms (e.g., "sparsistent") could confuse readers.

- Weakness — Significance: Needs stronger articulation of why the approach is superior to simpler alternatives (e.g., log-transforming counts or nonparametric methods) and trade-offs in complexity vs. interpretability.

- Overall: High-quality, well-justified contribution with strong empirical validation; recommend acceptance.
