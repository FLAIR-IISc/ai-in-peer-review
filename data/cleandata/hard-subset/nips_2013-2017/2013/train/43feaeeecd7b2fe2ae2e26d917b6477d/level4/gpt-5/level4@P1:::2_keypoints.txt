- Tackles construction of multivariate count models and limitations of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that enforce only negative conditional dependencies.  
- Shows the Winsorized Poisson conditional construction (Kaiser & Cressie) is inconsistent under Besag/Yang-style node-conditional frameworks.  
- Proposes three alternatives: Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), and Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM).  
- TPGM: truncation that preserves exponential-family form and avoids negativity constraints.  
- QPGM: replaces Poisson base measure with a negative-definite quadratic to allow both positive and negative edges.  
- SPGM: retains Poisson base measure but uses sublinear sufficient statistics to achieve normalizability without restricting pairwise parameters.  
- Learning approach: advocates L1-penalized neighborhood selection for structure learning.  
- Theoretical contributions are clear and mostly sound; the correction of the Winsorized result is important.  
- Much of the theoretical framework follows Yang et al., so novelty is mainly in model design rather than new theory.  
- Recommends including all proofs in the supplement given the paper corrects prior work.  
- Empirical evaluation is weak: synthetic experiments appear tailored to the proposed models.  
- Real-data study omits key copula-based competitors and presents only qualitative results.  
- No objective out-of-sample metric (e.g., test log-likelihood) is reported despite methods defining joint distributions.  
- Clarity issues: Section 3.1’s variational bound is overly detailed and not necessary; Section 3.2 needs slower pacing and better intuition, with the sublinear statistic figure moved to main text.  
- Originality: novel in relaxing negative-dependence constraint via changes to base measure and sufficient statistics, opening a useful design space.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for count data if empirically validated, but current experiments do not convincingly show superiority over strong alternatives.  
- Specific critique of baselines: reliance on the nonparanormal is problematic; copula models should use sensible marginal fits (e.g., kernel or parametric marginals) and mirror TPGM choices like R.  
- Pros: fixes Winsorized issue, proposes three intuitive alternatives, gives clear learning path, and has broad applicability.  
- Cons: biased and limited empirical evaluation, missing/weak baselines, lack of test log-likelihood, and some presentation flaws.  
- Suggested improvements: add copula baselines with reasonable marginals, report test log-likelihood or pseudo-likelihood, include proofs in supplement, streamline Section 3.1, expand/visualize Section 3.2, and discuss TPGM’s R trade-offs for model selection.  
- Conclusion: theoretically appealing models that allow positive/negative dependencies, but practical merit is not yet demonstrated due to insufficient empirical validation.
