The paper provides a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that obey the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building a multivariate distribution over unbounded counts is a challenging problem that has been studied extensively over the past 30 years, including notable works by Besag and others (e.g., Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models", 1999, which extends some of the authors' observations to other distributions such as conditionally-specified exponential distributions).

The authors' proposal has both strengths and weaknesses. On the positive side, their construction allows for marginal distributions over counts and leads to relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, the proposed models have some drawbacks. Specifically, the QPGM has marginally thinner tails, and the SPGM does not have closed-form conditional distributions, which somewhat defeats the purpose of building a conditionally specified model. Furthermore, it is unclear how the SPGM can be considered a Poisson distribution (although, for QPGM, one can argue that only the base measure is being modified). This raises questions about the main motivation for modeling counts, given that the Poisson distribution itself is not well-suited for fitting empirical data. While the Poisson distribution is a useful building block for many models, such as components of stochastic processes and latent variable models, it may not accurately capture the underlying data distribution.

A plot of the probability mass function of the SPGM would be helpful, as the current formulation appears convoluted. Additionally, it is assumed that R and R0 are constants (otherwise, these would not be exponential families). However, it is unclear how these parameters are chosen and what advice the authors would give to practitioners.

Despite these limitations, constructing a multivariate distribution over counts that obeys the independence model of a Markov random field is a difficult task. The authors' effort is appreciated, and the results are of theoretical interest to the NIPS community. However, the literature review feels somewhat incomplete. For instance, the authors' discussion of copulas and their application to discrete data (including count data) is lacking. Specifically, the authors seem to misunderstand [8], which presents a method for building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data, including count data, and provides a range of MCMC and approximate inference methods.

The authors' discussion of [11] is also unclear, as the book's primary contribution is showing how to build discrete models with log-linear parameters without exponential growth in the number of variables. However, the most significant omission is the neglect of the vast spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been conducted for an extended period.

While the authors' theoretical contribution is valid, the practical benefits of their approach are not entirely clear. As a practitioner, it is unclear why one would prefer this model space over a simpler structured Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model, which has been the standard approach for some time.

Finally, a more recent reference than [20] would be appreciated, as the claim that "counts of sequencing reads... are replacing microarrays" may not reflect the current state of gene expression analysis.
