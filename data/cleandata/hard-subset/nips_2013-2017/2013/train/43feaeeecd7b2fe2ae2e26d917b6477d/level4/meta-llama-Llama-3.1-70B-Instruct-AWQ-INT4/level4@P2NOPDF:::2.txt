The construction of multivariate distributions suitable for count data is an important goal, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches. 

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. The authors begin by proving that previously suggested truncation does not lead to a valid model, and they propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation is warranted due to its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, although technically simple, are novel in that they modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation of the Poisson distribution. The changes made are well-motivated and appealing. However, the actual theory involved largely follows from the work of Yang et al., leaving us with several possible model suggestions that need to be evaluated in practice.

The experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly given the paper's goal of addressing the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some potential, the data is geared toward the models suggested. In the real experiments, the comparison to copula-based alternatives is noticeably absent. Furthermore, the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. Since all methods involve the construction of joint distributions, a more objective evaluation using the log-probability of test data is necessary.

A more significant concern is that the copula competitors are used in an overly simplistic manner. When using a Gaussian copula, it would make more sense to use a sensible marginal model rather than the one used in the non-paranormal, which has no density and was chosen for its asymptotic properties rather than practical merits. The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform better. Similarly, if applied to real data, the choice of using R=11 could also be translated to a choice for the marginal of the copula. While I do not expect an in-depth exploration, a reasonable baseline is warranted.

The paper is generally well-written. Although I believe all results are true, particularly since the authors correct an error in another work, I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. I felt that the end of section 3.1 was overly detailed, and the bound did not contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. On the other hand, I would take section 3.2 more slowly, as it is the heart of the suggested method, and not defer the figure to the supplementary material but rather present it and better explain its intuition.

Overall, the authors present an appealing approach with some theoretical novelty, but the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving the merit of the proposed multivariate Poisson-like model unclear.
