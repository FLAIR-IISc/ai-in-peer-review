This paper addresses the important problem of constructing undirected graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors correctly point out a major limitation of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), which, due to normalizability constraints over an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. The main goal of this work is to introduce new classes of Poisson-like graphical models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies, making them more suitable for real-world applications.

The paper makes several contributions. First, it provides a formal proof demonstrating that a previously proposed "Winsorized" truncation of the Poisson distribution does not lead to a valid joint distribution under the Besag construction, correcting a misconception in the literature. Second, the authors propose a valid Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) by re-normalizing the distribution over a finite domain. While this allows for mixed dependencies, it has the drawback of requiring a pre-specified truncation level, R. Third, to overcome this, the paper introduces two more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution's exponential family form. The Quadratic PGM (QPGM) modifies the base measure to be quadratic, which ensures normalizability for mixed-sign parameters, albeit with Gaussian-like thin tails. Finally, the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear, allowing the model to retain the original Poisson base measure while still permitting both positive and negative dependencies. The models are evaluated on simulated data and a microRNA sequencing dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Motivation:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and significant problem. Modeling multivariate count data is crucial in many domains (e.g., genomics, social sciences), and the limitation of existing PGMs to only negative dependencies is a severe practical constraint.
2.  **Originality and Conceptual Contribution:** The paper offers novel and interesting ideas. The proof invalidating the Winsorized PGM approach is a valuable technical contribution. More importantly, the conceptual move to modify the base measure (QPGM) and sufficient statistics (SPGM) of the exponential family representation is a creative and principled way to design new models. These proposals are well-motivated and offer clear trade-offs.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivations for each of the three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are laid out logically, building upon the limitations of the previous one.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental validation. While the goal is to address a practical limitation, the experiments are not comprehensive enough to establish the practical utility of the proposed models over existing alternatives.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The comparison to copula-based alternatives (e.g., the non-paranormal) is handled in a "black-box" manner and is insufficient. For count data, a Gaussian copula with carefully chosen marginals (e.g., empirical CDFs, or parametric models like the Negative Binomial) would be a much stronger and more appropriate baseline than the standard non-paranormal, which was designed for continuous data. The near-identical performance of GLasso and the non-paranormal is suspicious and suggests the baseline was not optimally configured. A more thoughtful comparison is needed.
    *   **Lack of Objective Metrics:** The evaluation relies on ROC curves for simulations and visual inspection of learned networks for the real data. A more objective and standard measure, such as the average log-likelihood on a held-out test set, is necessary for a rigorous comparison of these generative models. The current results are largely anecdotal and do not provide conclusive evidence of superiority.
    *   **Biased Simulations:** The synthetic data is generated from the TPGM or a related sum-of-Poissons model. While useful for a sanity check, this setup is inherently biased towards the proposed methods. Performance on data generated from a different process (e.g., a copula model or a negative binomial graphical model) would be more convincing.

2.  **Limited Theoretical Novelty:** While the model proposals are novel, the underlying theoretical framework for constructing and learning the models (i.e., consistency of node-conditional distributions and neighborhood selection) follows directly from the work of Yang et al. (2012, 2013). The contribution is therefore primarily in model specification rather than new theory.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  To strengthen the paper's claims, the experimental section requires significant enhancement. A comparison against a well-constructed Gaussian copula graphical model with appropriate marginals for count data is essential. Furthermore, evaluating all models using test set log-likelihood would provide the objective evidence currently missing.
2.  The paper begins by rigorously pointing out an error in previous work. To maintain this high standard, I suggest including the full proofs for all theorems in the supplementary material.
3.  The discussion at the end of Section 3.1 regarding the variational upper bound on the QPGM partition function feels a bit detailed and does not add substantially to the main "Gaussian-esque" argument. This could be condensed.
4.  Conversely, Section 3.2, which introduces the SPGM, is arguably the most novel part of the paper. This section could be expanded. In particular, the figure of the sub-linear sufficient statistic should be moved from the appendix to the main paper to help build intuition for this core idea.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a set of appealing and well-motivated alternatives to the standard Poisson graphical model, with the goal of flexibly modeling both positive and negative dependencies in multivariate count data. The conceptual ideas are novel and the correction of prior work is a clear contribution. However, the paper is let down by a limited and somewhat biased experimental evaluation that fails to convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the new models over sensible existing alternatives.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem.
*   Provides a valuable correction to a result in the existing literature.
*   The proposed models, particularly the QPGM and SPGM, are novel, conceptually interesting, and well-motivated.
*   The paper is clearly written and presents a logical progression of ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the paper's claims of practical utility.
*   Key baselines, such as properly configured copula-based models, are not included in the comparison.
*   The evaluation lacks objective quantitative metrics like test log-likelihood, making the results difficult to interpret.
*   The theoretical framework is largely adopted from prior work, limiting the paper's theoretical novelty.
