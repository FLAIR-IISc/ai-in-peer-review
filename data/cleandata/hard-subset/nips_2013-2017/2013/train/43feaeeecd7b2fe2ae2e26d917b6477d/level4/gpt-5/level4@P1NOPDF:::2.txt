The goal of constructing multivariate distributions tailored to count data is important, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches (with one reservation noted below). 

On the theoretical and conceptual side, the paper has several merits. It begins by proving that a previously suggested truncation does not yield a valid model, and then proposes several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while obvious, is justified by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM are technically simple yet novel in explicitly treating the base measure and sufficient statistics in the exponential-family representation as levers for modifying the Poisson distribution. These modifications are well motivated and appealing. That said, much of the theory follows almost directly from Yang et al., leaving us with multiple candidate models whose relative merits need to be established empirically.

Given this, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, especially since the paper’s stated goal is to address the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. The synthetic study shows some promise but appears tailored to the proposed models. On real data, comparisons to copula-based alternatives are conspicuously absent. The reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. Because all methods construct joint distributions, a more objective evaluation using test log-probabilities (held-out log-likelihood) is needed.

More importantly—and central to the question of whether a new model is necessary—the copula competitors are used in an overly black-box manner. Even with a Gaussian copula, it is more sensible to choose appropriate marginal models rather than the nonparanormal transform (which lacks a proper density) that is typically selected for its asymptotic properties rather than practical merits. The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious; I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates for the marginals would perform substantially better. Similarly, in the real-data experiments, the (sensible) choice of R=11 could be mirrored by an analogous choice for the copula’s marginals. I do not expect an in-depth exploration, but a reasonable baseline is warranted.

The paper is generally well written. I believe the results are correct—particularly since the authors begin by pointing out an error in prior work—but I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material. The end of Section 3.1 feels overly detailed, and the bound provided does not substantively contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. Conversely, Section 3.2, which is the heart of the proposed method, would benefit from a slower, clearer exposition; the figure currently deferred to the supplementary material should appear in the main text with a better explanation of its intuition.

In sum, based on a conditional exponential-family construction, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model with the goal of enabling flexible joint modeling that accommodates both positive and negative dependencies. The approach is appealing and has some theoretical novelty. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving us with yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical value remains unclear.
