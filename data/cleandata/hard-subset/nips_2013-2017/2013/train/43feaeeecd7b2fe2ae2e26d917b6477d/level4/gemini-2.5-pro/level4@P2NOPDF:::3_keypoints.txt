- Novel contribution: constructs multivariate count distributions that are Markov w.r.t. an undirected graph — a nontrivial theoretical result of interest.  
- Strength: constructions yield valid count marginals and lead to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms.  
- Weakness: QPGM produces marginals with thinner tails than Poisson, limiting fit to overdispersed data.  
- Weakness: SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining a main motivation for conditionally specified models.  
- Naming concern: calling QPGM/SPGM “Poisson” variants is questionable, especially for SPGM.  
- Motivation concern: unclear why models so tightly tied to Poisson are useful given Poisson’s poor fit to many empirical counts.  
- Empirical gaps: paper needs stronger empirical justification (e.g., plot breast cancer data vs fitted Poisson; plot SPGM PMF).  
- Parameter clarity: unclear treatment and selection of parameters R and R0 — are they fixed constants for exponential-family membership, and how should practitioners choose them?  
- Literature treatment is superficial and at times inaccurate (imprecise copula discussion; mischaracterizes ref [8] and book [11]).  
- Major omission: ignores large spatial statistics literature on high-dimensional count data and alternative approaches.  
- Practical uptake: paper does not convincingly explain why practitioners should prefer these models over standard alternatives (e.g., Gaussian random field + Poisson observation).  
- Minor: citation about sequencing reads replacing microarrays is outdated (2008); more current genomics references needed.
