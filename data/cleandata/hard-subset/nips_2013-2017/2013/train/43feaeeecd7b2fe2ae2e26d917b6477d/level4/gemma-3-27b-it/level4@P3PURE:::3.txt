The paper presents a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building such distributions is a challenging problem, extensively studied over the past 30 years, notably by Besag and others (including Arnold et al.’s “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models”, 1999, which generalizes these observations to other distributions like conditionally-specified exponential distributions). The authors’ proposal ultimately succeeds in several key respects, offering constructions that allow for marginal distributions over counts and leading to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms. However, the approach has drawbacks: the Quasi-Poisson Gaussian Mixture (QPGM) exhibits marginally thinner tails, and the Sparse Poisson Gaussian Mixture (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions – a limitation that somewhat undermines the benefit of a conditionally specified model. Indeed, I question whether SPGM can legitimately be considered a Poisson distribution (though, for QPGM, one could argue that only the base measure is altered). This raises a broader question regarding the motivation for modeling counts, given that the Poisson distribution itself often proves a poor fit for empirical data. (The authors might consider plotting their breast cancer data to illustrate this point; does it resemble a Poisson distribution?) A plot of the SPGM probability mass function would also be valuable, as its formulation appears initially complex. I assume R and R0 are treated as constants, as otherwise they wouldn't belong to exponential families. How are these parameters chosen, and what practical guidance can be offered to users of the method?

Despite these points, constructing a multivariate distribution over counts that respects the independence model of a Markov Random Field (MRF) is demonstrably difficult. I appreciate the effort invested in this work and believe the results are of theoretical interest to the NIPS community. My primary concern lies with the somewhat superficial engagement with existing literature. For example, the authors’ understanding of copulas feels incomplete. They appear to misrepresent the contribution of [8], which aims precisely to construct multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data – including count data – and for which a variety of MCMC and approximate inference techniques have been developed. This omission leads me to question the validity of the discussion regarding [11], whose central argument revolves around building discrete models with log-linear parameters that scale favorably with the number of variables (although I do not have the text readily available to confirm specifics regarding Poisson distributions). Perhaps the most significant oversight is the complete neglect of the extensive spatial statistics literature, which has long addressed high-dimensional count data analysis. It should be noted that several existing approaches (including the sparse precision Gaussian copula model of [8]) do not directly model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space. Nonetheless, the paper's theoretical contribution remains valid. However, as a practitioner, I am not yet convinced that the added complexity of this model space justifies its use over the simpler and widely established approach of a structured Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model. 

Finally, regarding the claim that counts of sequencing reads are “replacing microarrays,” a more recent citation than [20] would strengthen this assertion. In summary, this paper offers a method for constructing multivariate count distributions that are Markov with respect to undirected graphs. As with any nontrivial multivariate construction, it presents both advantages and disadvantages. The literature review, however, feels incomplete.
