Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) derived from node-conditional exponential families: for normalizability on an infinite support, the pairwise parameters must be non-positive (Besag 1974; Yang et al., NIPS 2012/2013), which restricts the model to negative conditional dependencies. The authors propose three alternatives to capture richer dependence structures for count data:

- Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM): they show that a commonly suggested “Winsorized” truncation (Kaiser and Cressie; Griffith) does not yield a valid joint distribution under the Besag/Yang construction, and instead propose a different truncation that preserves exponential-family form and yields a valid joint MRF on {0,…,R}^p with no sign constraints on edges.

- Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM): they prove that with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability with both positive and negative dependencies requires a base measure that scales at least quadratically. This motivates a model with a quadratic base measure and linear sufficient statistics, permitting mixed-sign edges under a negative-definite quadratic form.

- Sub-Linear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM): to avoid Gaussian-like thin tails of QPGM, they introduce sub-linear sufficient statistics while keeping the Poisson base measure, proving normalizability without restrictions on edge signs and giving a variational bound for the node-wise log-partition.

They estimate graphs via penalized neighborhood selection, and present simulations and a breast cancer microRNA (miRNA) network learned from RNA-seq counts. The work is well grounded in prior PGMs for exponential families (Yang et al.), classical limitations of multivariate Poisson constructions, and applied needs in genomics.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Theorems are clearly stated and address key hurdles: impossibility of Winsorized conditionals yielding a valid joint, necessity of quadratic base measure growth for mixed-sign edges under linear stats, and normalizability of SPGM. The normalizability conditions for QPGM (negative-definite quadratic form) are reasonable. Using variational upper bounds for node-wise estimation (Gaussian for QPGM, Poisson for SPGM when θ ≥ 0) is sensible.

- Empirical evaluation: Simulations compare TPGM and SPGM against Gaussian graphical models and nonparanormal baselines, showing improved ROC for count data; the miRNA case study finds hubs consistent with known biology. That said, additional baselines tailored to counts (e.g., Poisson log-normal, negative binomial/correlated count models, or copula-based count models) would strengthen claims.

- Methodological completeness: Neighborhood selection and proximal gradient are appropriate, but important practical details are light: how to set R and (R0,R) in practice; sensitivity analyses; computational complexity and convergence criteria; and formal graph recovery guarantees (acknowledged as future work).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well structured. The exposition of why standard PGMs fail for counts and how each modification addresses a specific barrier is effective. A few points could be clearer:
- Define “sparsistency” (or “sparsistent”) on first use (i.e., consistent recovery of the edge set), with an appropriate citation.
- Provide intuition for Theorem 3 (why sub-quadratic base measures fail under linear sufficient statistics).
- For SPGM, summarize how the infinite-sum partition function is handled numerically and when the Poisson variational bound is tight.

Originality
The combination of (i) a proof invalidating Winsorized node-conditionals as a valid joint, (ii) a principled necessity result for quadratic base measures under linear stats, and (iii) the sub-linear sufficient-statistics construction appears novel and substantive. The work advances the design space for count-valued MRFs beyond existing PGM, copula, or sum-of-Poissons constructions.

Significance
Count data are ubiquitous (RNA-seq, spatial incidence, user activity). Enabling mixed-sign conditional dependencies in a tractable MRF framework is important for both methodology and applications. The proposed models should be of interest to researchers building networks from counts, and the SPGM in particular seems practically attractive.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real and important limitation of Poisson MRFs with clear theoretical contributions.
- Provides three concrete, learnable model families supporting positive and negative dependencies.
- Empirical results show clear benefits over Gaussian/nonparanormal methods for count data and meaningful biological findings.
- Generally well written and connected to prior NIPS work on exponential-family PGMs.

Cons and suggestions
- Practical guidance for choosing truncation R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM) is limited; add heuristics or data-driven selection and sensitivity plots.
- Thin tails in QPGM are portrayed as a major drawback; in some applications Gaussian-like tails may be appropriate. Consider reframing as a trade-off, and demonstrate where QPGM excels.
- Estimation details are sparse: specify optimization settings, complexity, and numerical stability, especially for SPGM’s partition function. Discuss the impact of variational bounds on estimation bias.
- Provide or cite theoretical graph recovery guarantees for at least one of the new models, even if under simplifying conditions.
- Broaden baselines to include Poisson log-normal or other correlated count models if feasible, or discuss why they were omitted.
- Reproducibility: consider releasing code and scripts.

Minor comments and typos
- Define “sparsistent/sparsistency” on first use.
- Page 3, above 2.1.1: “graphical mode distributions” → “graphical model distributions.”
- Page 5, above 3.1: “Note that the log ...” → “Note that although the log ...”
- Abstract: “form count-valued next generation sequencing data” → “from count-valued next-generation sequencing data.”
- Ensure consistent capitalization and hyphenation (e.g., “node-conditional,” “log-partition,” “Winsorized”).
- Some references lack complete bibliographic information; please standardize.

Overall assessment
This is a high-quality and original contribution that advances graphical modeling for counts. With improved practical guidance (hyperparameter selection, estimation details) and slightly broader comparisons, it will be a strong NIPS paper. I recommend acceptance.
