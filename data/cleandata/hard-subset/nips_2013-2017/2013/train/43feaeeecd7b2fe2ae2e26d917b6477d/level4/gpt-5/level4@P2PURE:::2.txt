Constructing multivariate distributions appropriate for count data is an important and timely goal, and the authors correctly identify shortcomings of current approaches (with a reservation noted below). On the theoretical and conceptual side, the paper has several merits. The authors begin by proving that a previously proposed truncation does not yield a valid model and then suggest several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM are technically simple yet novel in that they explicitly use the base measure and sufficient statistics in the exponential-family representation as levers to modify the Poisson distribution. These modifications are well motivated and appealing. That said, much of the underlying theory follows closely from Yang et al., leaving us with several candidate models whose value ultimately needs to be established empirically.

Given this, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly since the stated aim is to address the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. The synthetic experiment shows some promise, but the data are tailored to the proposed models. In the real-data experiments, comparisons to copula-based alternatives are conspicuously absent. Moreover, the reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. Since all methods involve constructing joint distributions, an objective evaluation using the log-likelihood (or log-probability) on held-out data is needed.

More importantly—this goes to the core question of whether a new model is needed—the copula baselines are treated in an overly black-box fashion. Even when restricting to a Gaussian copula, it would be more sensible to use an appropriate marginal model rather than the nonparanormal transformation (which lacks a density) that appears chosen primarily for its asymptotic properties rather than practical performance. The near-identical performance to Glasso is suspicious; I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform better. Similarly, for the real-data analysis, the (reasonable) choice of R = 11 could be translated into a principled marginal choice for the copula. I do not expect an exhaustive exploration here, but a credible baseline is warranted.

The paper is generally well written. Although I believe the results are correct—especially given that the authors start by identifying an error in prior work—I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material. I found the end of Section 3.1 overly detailed; the bound there does not add meaningfully to the Gaussian-like argument. By contrast, Section 3.2, which is central to the proposed approach, would benefit from a slower, clearer presentation; the figure should not be deferred to the supplement but included in the main text with better explanatory intuition.

In summary: Based on a conditional exponential-family construction, the authors propose alternatives to the Poisson graphical model aimed at flexible joint modeling with both positive and negative dependencies. The approach is appealing and has some theoretical novelty, but the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving the contribution as yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical merit remains unclear.
