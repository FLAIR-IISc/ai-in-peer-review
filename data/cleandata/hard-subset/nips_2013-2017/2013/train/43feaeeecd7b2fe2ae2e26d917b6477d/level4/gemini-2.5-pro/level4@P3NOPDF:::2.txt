This paper addresses the important goal of constructing multivariate distributions for count data, correctly identifying the limitations of existing approaches. Based on a conditional exponential family construction, the authors present several alternatives to the standard Poisson graphical model—specifically, the Truncated (TPGM), Quadratic (QPGM), and Submodular (SPGM) Poisson Graphical Models—with the aim of flexibly modeling a mix of positive and negative dependencies. While the proposed models are appealing and have some theoretical novelty, the experimental evaluation is limited and does not sufficiently demonstrate their practical utility, leaving their overall merit unclear.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Motivation and Theoretical Contributions:** The paper tackles a significant and challenging problem. The authors provide value by first proving that a previously suggested truncation method for Poisson graphical models does not result in a valid probability distribution. They then propose several well-motivated alternatives. The TPGM, while a straightforward idea, is a warranted and natural construction. The QPGM and SPGM are novel in their approach of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation to alter the Poisson distribution's properties. These modifications are well-motivated and conceptually appealing.

2.  **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of this paper is the experimental evaluation, which is not thorough enough to support the practical claims made. Given that the paper's stated goal is to fix the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models, and the theoretical contributions are largely direct applications of the framework from Yang et al. (2012), a more convincing empirical study is essential.

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Design:** The synthetic experiment, while showing some potential, uses a data generation process that is closely aligned with the proposed models. This fails to demonstrate how these models would perform on data from a wider range of underlying distributions. In the real-data experiments, the evaluation is anecdotal and qualitative. To properly assess generative models like these, a more objective and quantitative metric, such as the average log-probability on held-out test data, is necessary.

2.  **Missing and Weak Baselines:** A major omission is the lack of comparison to modern copula-based alternatives for modeling multivariate count data. The competitors that are included are handled in an overly simplistic, "black-box" manner. For instance, the non-paranormal method uses a marginal transformation that lacks a proper density and was chosen primarily for its asymptotic properties, not its practical effectiveness. The resulting performance, being nearly identical to Glasso, is suspicious and suggests a weak baseline. I strongly suspect that a more sensible choice for the marginals, such as a simple Gaussian kernel density estimate, would provide a much more competitive baseline.

3.  **Unfair Comparison:** The paper does not leverage the flexibility of competing methods to create a fair comparison. For example, in the real-data experiment, the choice of R=11 (the maximum count) for the proposed models could have been used to inform the marginal distribution choice for a copula-based competitor (e.g., a discrete distribution on {0, 1, ..., 10}). While an exhaustive exploration is not expected, the current evaluation does not provide a reasonable assessment of how the proposed models stand against properly configured state-of-the-art alternatives. This weakness undermines the core motivation for the new models.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Revise Experimental Section:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more rigorous experimental evaluation. This should include:
    *   Comparisons against strong, well-configured baselines, particularly Gaussian copulas with appropriate discrete marginals (e.g., empirical or parametric).
    *   The use of quantitative evaluation metrics such as test set log-probability.
    *   Potentially, experiments on a wider variety of datasets to better demonstrate the models' applicability.

2.  **Improve Presentation of Core Ideas:** Section 3.2 is the heart of the paper, introducing the proposed methods. This section feels rushed. It should be expanded with a more deliberate explanation of the model mechanics and intuition. The key illustrative figure, currently in the supplementary material, should be moved into the main paper to support this explanation. Conversely, the end of Section 3.1 is overly detailed, and the bound presented adds little to the main argument; this part could be condensed.

3.  **Include Proofs:** Since the paper begins by highlighting an error in a previous work, maintaining a high standard of rigor is important. I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material for verification.

In summary, the paper introduces a set of interesting and well-motivated models for multivariate count data. However, the contribution is undermined by an inadequate experimental evaluation that fails to establish the practical value of these new models over existing methods. Without a substantial revision of the empirical results, the paper reads as a proposal of yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical advantages are unproven.
