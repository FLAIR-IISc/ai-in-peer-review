The paper develops several new models for multivariate count data. Building on prior work showing that the standard Poisson graphical model (e.g., Besag; Yang et al.) only admits nonpositive pairwise parameters for normalizability, the authors propose three remedies: (i) a different truncation scheme that retains an exponential-family form for the node-conditionals and yields a valid joint distribution; (ii) a variant with a quadratic base measure while keeping linear sufficient statistics, together with a proof that such base-measure growth is required to allow both positive and negative dependencies under this setup; and (iii) a model with sublinear sufficient statistics that preserves the Poisson base measure. They demonstrate learning via penalized neighborhood selection and report results on simulated data and a microRNA sequencing application.

The paper is clearly written and presents thoughtful, technically grounded solutions for modeling count-valued networks. The contributions appear original, and the empirical and theoretical results suggest meaningful impact.

That said, the arguments emphasizing the downsides of the truncation-based and quadratic-base-measure models did not fully persuade me. For truncation, many practical settings have counts that rarely exceed moderate values, in which case such a model could be quite effective. For the quadratic-base-measure case, Gaussian-like thin tails may be appropriate in some applications.

I am unfamiliar with the term “sparsistent,” though I recognize it has been used in the literature; it would help to define it at first mention.
