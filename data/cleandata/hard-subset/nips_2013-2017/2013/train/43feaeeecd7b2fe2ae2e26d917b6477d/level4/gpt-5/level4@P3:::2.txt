Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): in the standard auto-model construction, normalizability forces nonpositive pairwise parameters, thereby restricting the model to negative conditional dependencies. The authors first show that a previously proposed remedy—using Winsorized (hard-truncated-at-R) Poisson node-conditionals—cannot yield a valid joint distribution in the Besag/Yang construction (Theorem 1). They then propose three alternatives:

- Truncated PGM (TPGM): replace the univariate Poisson with an exponential-family Poisson truncated to {0,…,R} whose “remnant” mass is redistributed proportionally across the truncated support. This yields a valid joint model with no sign restriction on edges, but the domain is bounded and degeneracy can occur near R.

- Quadratic PGM (QPGM): retain linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x but modify the base measure to scale quadratically, yielding node-conditional log-densities with quadratic self-terms and allowing both positive and negative dependencies under a negative-definite quadratic form. The tails become Gaussian-like.

- Sublinear PGM (SPGM): retain the Poisson base measure log x! but replace the sufficient statistic with a piecewise sublinear function B(x;R0,R) that flattens after R0, producing heavier tails than QPGM and admitting unrestricted edge signs. Variational upper bounds are provided for intractable node-conditional log-partition functions.

The models are learned via `1-penalized neighborhood selection, following the generalized linear model framework of Yang et al. The paper includes simulations and a case study on TCGA microRNA sequencing counts. This work is grounded in Besag’s auto-models and their exponential-family extensions, and it complements previous multivariate Poisson constructions based on sums of independent Poissons and copula-based approaches.

Quality
- Theory: The negative result for Winsorized node-conditionals is useful and corrects the literature. The construction of TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM follows the exponential-family auto-model framework; normalizability conditions and variational bounds are clearly stated. However, several claims central to practice (e.g., Theorem 3’s Ω(z^2) necessity, SPGM normalizability for all pairwise parameters) would benefit from full proofs in the supplement and clearer articulation of assumptions.

- Estimation: The paper argues that node-wise penalized likelihood is applicable, but offers no sparsistency or sample-complexity guarantees (even by adaptation of Yang et al.), and provides limited computational details (approximations for SPGM’s infinite sums, enforcement of QPGM’s negative-definiteness in node-wise regression, convergence diagnostics).

- Experiments: Synthetic results are promising but tailored to the proposed models (e.g., TPGM data exhibits truncation effects that TPGM is designed to exploit). Real-data results are largely qualitative (network plots, hubs) without quantitative held-out evaluation.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written. Suggestions:
- Move the variational Gaussian bound in Section 3.1 to the supplement; it does not materially reinforce the “Gaussian-esque” claim.
- Expand and slow down Section 3.2; include the figure of B(x;R0,R) in the main text and clarify its intuition and parameter roles (R0 vs R).
- Unify notation for domains (e.g., use a consistent symbol for the count set).
- Minor copyedits (e.g., “put on the table,” “from” instead of “form”).

Originality
The idea of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics within the auto-model framework to enable positive dependencies for count data is novel and conceptually appealing. The specific constructions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are technically simple but well motivated. The theoretical negative result on Winsorization adds value. Relative to Yang et al., the novelty is moderate but meaningful.

Significance
If supported by robust empirical evidence and practical estimation procedures, these models could expand the toolkit for multivariate count data in genomics and beyond. Currently, the practical significance is limited by the evaluation: key baselines are missing, metrics are largely qualitative, and sensitivity analyses are absent.

Major concerns and requests
- Baselines: Include copula-based competitors with sensible count marginals. A Gaussian copula with Poisson or negative binomial marginals (fitted via maximum likelihood or rank-based estimators) is a more appropriate baseline than the nonparanormal transform, which lacks a proper density and is not tailored to counts. Even simple kernel-smoothed PIT-based marginals would be more informative. The near-identical performance to Glasso suggests the current baselines are not competitive.

- Quantitative evaluation: Report held-out test log-likelihood or pseudo-likelihood for all methods. This is especially important since all approaches define (or approximate) joint distributions. Include calibration of node-conditionals, parameter recovery error, and ablations demonstrating the necessity of the proposed modifications (e.g., compare linear vs sublinear sufficient statistics under matched settings).

- Sensitivity and robustness: For TPGM and SPGM, study sensitivity to R and R0, and propose a data-driven selection strategy. Justify R=11 in the TCGA analysis, and assess stability across subsamples (beyond sparsity selection). For QPGM, clarify how negative-definiteness is enforced during node-wise estimation.

- Computation: Detail how SPGM’s infinite sums are truncated or approximated, error controls, and runtime comparisons. Provide diagnostics for convergence of proximal methods.

- Theory: Include full proofs in the supplement, state all regularity assumptions, and, if feasible, sketch sparsistency guarantees by adapting the generalized linear model analysis to QPGM/SPGM node-conditionals.

Pros
- Clear identification and correction of a mistake in prior work (Winsorization).
- Conceptually elegant use of base-measure and sufficient-statistic modifications.
- Three concrete model classes covering different tail behaviors and constraints.
- Potentially broad applicability to count-valued high-dimensional data.

Cons
- Missing strong baselines (copula with appropriate marginals).
- Limited, partly biased empirical evaluation; lack of test log-likelihood.
- No sparsistency theory or detailed computational treatment.
- Sensitivity to truncation parameters not explored.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical ideas are appealing and the correction to prior work is valuable, but the empirical evaluation does not yet demonstrate clear and robust advantages over well-tuned alternatives. With stronger baselines, quantitative held-out assessments, sensitivity analyses, and fuller proofs, this could become a solid contribution.
