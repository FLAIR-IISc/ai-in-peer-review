The paper describes a number of new models for representing a joint distribution over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the standard model arising from the work of Yang et al. is not satisfactory because it can only model negative correlations to ensure the distribution’s normalizability. They then consider a series of modifications to address this limitation, including a novel truncation method, utilizing a quadratic base measure statistic (for which they provide a theoretical justification), and finally, exploring a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors present experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed methods.

This is a well-written paper describing innovative solutions for modeling count data. I believe the work is original, and the results are significant. The problem of modeling multivariate count data with both positive and negative dependencies is an important one, with applications across numerous fields, and the authors offer compelling approaches.

I found the discussion of the limitations of the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unconvincing. For the truncation approach, I can envision domains where counts are inherently bounded and the model would perform well. Regarding the quadratic model, the observation that “Gaussian-esque thin tails” might be appropriate in certain scenarios is valid. These points don’t detract significantly from the overall contribution, but represent potential areas for further discussion. 

I am unfamiliar with the term “sparsistent,” but acknowledge its established use within the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Originality:** The proposed models offer novel approaches to addressing a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models.
*   **Significance:** The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies in count data is crucial for a wider range of applications.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis and derivations are rigorous and well-presented.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the complex concepts accessible.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limitations of the truncation and quadratic models, while acknowledged, could benefit from a more in-depth discussion.
*   The analysis of the statistical properties of the proposed models (e.g., consistency, rates of convergence) could be further developed.
