The paper proposes several new models for representing a joint distribution over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the default model, as described in Yang et al., is unsatisfactory because it can only capture negative correlations in order to ensure normalization. To address this limitation, they explore a series of fixes, including a novel truncation method, the use of a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove to be necessary, assuming all else is fixed), and a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors also present experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data.

This is a well-written paper that describes some elegant solutions for modeling count data. I believe the work is original, and the results are significant.

However, I found the criticisms of the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unconvincing. For instance, I could imagine many domains where counts typically do not get excessively high, and the truncation model would work well. Similarly, it's possible that "Gaussian-esque thin tails" could be an appropriate choice in certain situations.

I was unfamiliar with the term "sparsistent," but it appears to have been used in the literature for some time.

I did notice a couple of minor errors in the text. On page 3, above section 2.1.1, "graphical mode distributions" should be replaced with "graphical model distributions." On page 5, above section 3.1, the sentence "Note that the log..." would be clearer if rephrased as "Note that although the log..."

Overall, this is a well-written, high-quality paper on modeling a joint distribution over count variables.
