The goal of constructing multivariate distributions that are appropriate for count data is an important one, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches. On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. Starting with a proof that previously suggested truncation does not lead to a valid model, the authors suggest several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, though obvious, is warranted due to its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, while technically simple, are novel in that the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation are put on the table as the means to modify the Poisson distribution. Further, the changes made are well motivated and appealing. That said, the actual theory involved follows almost directly from the work of Yang et al so that we are left with several possible model suggestions whose merit need to be evaluated in practice.

The paper's main ideas are well related to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the limitations of existing Poisson graphical models and their proposed alternatives. The connection to the work of Yang et al is properly acknowledged, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying concepts.

Regarding the strengths of the paper, the authors provide a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem of modeling count data. The proposed alternatives to the Poisson graphical model are novel and have some theoretical novelty. The paper is generally well written, and the authors provide a good overview of the main ideas.

However, there are several weaknesses to the paper. The experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, which makes it difficult to assess the merit of the proposed models. The comparison to copula-based alternatives is missing, and the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. A more objective evaluation of the models using metrics such as log-probability of test data is needed. Additionally, the authors do not provide a reasonable baseline for comparison, and the choice of marginal models for the copula-based alternatives is not well justified.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on quality, as the proposed models are technically sound and well motivated. However, the paper scores lower on clarity, as the experimental evaluation is not well presented, and the results are not clearly summarized. The paper scores well on originality, as the proposed models are novel and have some theoretical novelty. However, the paper scores lower on significance, as the experimental evaluation is limited, and the merit of the proposed models is unclear.

Overall, I would recommend that the authors revise the paper to address the weaknesses mentioned above. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including a comparison to copula-based alternatives and a more objective evaluation of the models, is needed. Additionally, the authors should provide a more detailed explanation of the proposed models and their intuition.
