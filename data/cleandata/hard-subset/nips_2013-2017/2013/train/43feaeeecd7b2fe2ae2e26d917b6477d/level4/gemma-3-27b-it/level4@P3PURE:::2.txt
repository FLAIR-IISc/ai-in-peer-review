The goal of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data is an important one, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches (though a reservation on this point is noted below). 

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. Beginning with a proof demonstrating that previously suggested truncation does not lead to a valid model, the authors propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is warranted by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, though technically simple, are novel in that they explicitly present the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation as means to modify the Poisson distribution. Further, the modifications are well-motivated and appealing. However, the underlying theory largely follows directly from the work of Yang et al., leaving us with several possible model suggestions whose practical merit requires evaluation.

In light of the above, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly given the paper’s stated goal of addressing the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some potential, the data is specifically designed to favor the proposed models. The real-data experiments notably lack comparison to copula-based alternatives. Furthermore, the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. As all methods involve constructing joint distributions, a more objective log-probability evaluation of test data is needed. 

More importantly – and this touches upon the core question of whether these new models are necessary – the copula competitors are used in an overly black-box manner. Specifically, even when employing only a Gaussian copula, it is more sensible to utilize a reasonable marginal model rather than the one used in the non-paranormal approach (which, crucially, lacks a density function!), a choice primarily justified by its asymptotic properties rather than its practical merits. The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspect, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would yield better results. Similarly, the (sensible) choice of R=11 in the real-data analysis could also inform the selection of the marginal distribution for the copula. While I do not expect an in-depth exploration of this, some reasonable baseline is warranted. 

Finally, the paper is generally well written. Although I believe the results are accurate – particularly given that the authors begin by identifying an error in another work – I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. Additionally, I found the end of section 3.1 overly detailed, and the bound did not significantly contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. Conversely, I would recommend a more deliberate presentation of section 3.2, as it represents the core of the proposed method; in particular, the figure should not be deferred to the supplementary material, but rather presented in the main text with a more thorough explanation of its intuition. 

Based on a conditional exponential construction, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model with the goal of allowing for flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies. On the positive side, the approach is appealing and exhibits some theoretical novelty. On the negative side, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving the overall merit of yet another multivariate Poisson-like model unclear.
