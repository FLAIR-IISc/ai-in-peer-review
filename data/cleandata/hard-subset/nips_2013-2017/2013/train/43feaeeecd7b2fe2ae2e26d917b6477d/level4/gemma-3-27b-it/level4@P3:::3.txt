This paper addresses the challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded count data that respect the Markov structure of an undirected graphical model. Building such distributions is inherently difficult, as highlighted by decades of work in spatial statistics and graphical modeling (e.g., Besag, Arnold et al., 1999). The authors propose three novel approaches – Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) – to overcome the limitations of existing Poisson graphical models, which are restricted to modeling negative dependencies. 

The core contribution lies in relaxing these restrictions and enabling the modeling of both positive and negative correlations between count-valued variables. The TPGM offers a valid graphical model by proposing a novel truncation approach, while the QPGM and SPGM explore modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution. The paper also discusses estimation via penalized neighborhood selection and demonstrates performance on simulated networks and a microRNA expression dataset.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** Constructing flexible multivariate models for count data is an important open problem with applications across various domains.
* **Novel approaches:** The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM offer innovative solutions to the limitations of existing Poisson graphical models.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The derivations and theoretical justifications for the models are well-presented. The discussion around normalizability conditions and the limitations of truncation approaches is particularly insightful.
* **Empirical validation:**  Demonstrating the models on both simulated and real-world data provides initial evidence of their applicability and performance.
* **Clear presentation of mathematical details:** The paper is mathematically rigorous and clearly explains the key concepts and derivations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incomplete literature review:** While the paper cites relevant work, it overlooks several important areas. The discussion of copulas (e.g., [8]) feels superficial, and the authors appear unaware of the extensive literature in spatial statistics dealing with high-dimensional count data. The treatment of log-linear models ([11]) also lacks nuance.
* **SPGM’s connection to the Poisson distribution is tenuous:** The authors acknowledge this point, but the description of the SPGM as a “Poisson” distribution feels strained given its modified sufficient statistics.  A clear visual representation of the SPGM probability mass function would improve understanding.
* **Practical considerations:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges and computational costs associated with the proposed models, particularly the SPGM. Guidance on choosing parameters (R, R0) for the TPGM and SPGM would be valuable for practitioners.
* **Justification for model complexity:** The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate why one would choose these more complex models over simpler alternatives, such as a Gaussian graphical model with a Poisson measurement model. A more thorough comparison and discussion of the trade-offs are needed.
* **Outdated citation:** The claim that sequencing reads are replacing microarrays requires a more recent citation than [20].

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graphical modeling for count data. The proposed models offer novel ways to overcome the limitations of existing approaches. However, the incomplete literature review and the lack of detailed discussion regarding practical considerations detract from the overall impact. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a challenging and important problem.
* Introduces novel and theoretically sound models.
* Demonstrates promising results on simulated and real-world data.
* The theoretical contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to NIPS attendees.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The literature review is incomplete and overlooks key related work.
* Practical considerations and implementation details are not adequately addressed.
* The justification for the increased complexity of the proposed models is not fully convincing.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses regarding the literature review, practical considerations, and justification for model complexity.  Adding a discussion of the computational cost and providing guidance on parameter selection would greatly enhance the paper’s value.  A visual representation of the SPGM probability mass function would also be helpful.
