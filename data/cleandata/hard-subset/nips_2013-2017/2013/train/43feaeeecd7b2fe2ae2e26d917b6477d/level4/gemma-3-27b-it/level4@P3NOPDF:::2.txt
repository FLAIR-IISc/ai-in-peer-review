The goal of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data is an important one, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches – although a reservation on this point is noted below.

On the theoretical and conceptual front, this paper presents several merits. Beginning with a proof demonstrating the invalidity of previously suggested truncation methods, the authors propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, though technically simple, are novel in their approach; they explicitly present the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation as mechanisms for modifying the Poisson distribution. Furthermore, these modifications are well-motivated and intuitively appealing. However, the underlying theory largely follows from the work of Yang et al., leaving us with a collection of potential model suggestions whose practical value requires thorough evaluation.

In light of this, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly given the paper’s stated goal of addressing the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment demonstrates some potential, the data is heavily tailored to favor the proposed models. The real-data experiments suffer from a glaring omission: a comparison to copula-based alternatives. Moreover, the reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. A more objective evaluation, such as a log-probability score on held-out test data, is necessary given that all methods involve constructing joint distributions. 

More critically – and this touches on the fundamental question of whether these new models are needed – the copula competitors are employed in an overly “black-box” manner. Even utilizing a simple Gaussian copula, it is more sensible to incorporate a well-chosen marginal model instead of relying on the non-paranormal distribution (which lacks a density function!) simply due to its asymptotic properties. This choice seems driven by theoretical convenience rather than practical performance. The near-identical performance observed compared to Glasso is also suspicious, and I strongly suspect that even straightforward Gaussian kernel density estimation would yield superior results. Similarly, the (reasonable) choice of R=11 when applied to the real data could inform a sensible selection of the copula’s marginal distribution. While I do not expect an exhaustive exploration, a more justifiable baseline comparison is warranted.

The paper is generally well-written. Despite my concerns, I believe the results are likely correct, particularly given the authors’ correction of an error in prior work. I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material. Additionally, the end of section 3.1 feels overly detailed, and the presented bound does not significantly strengthen the Gaussian-esque justification. Conversely, I suggest a more deliberate and detailed presentation of section 3.2, as it represents the core of the proposed methodology. Specifically, the figure currently relegated to the supplementary material should be included in the main text, and its underlying intuition should be explained more thoroughly.

In summary, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model based on a conditional exponential construction, aiming to allow for flexible joint modeling with both positive and negative dependencies. While the proposed approach is appealing and demonstrates some theoretical novelty, the limited and somewhat biased experimental evaluation leaves the overall merit of these models unclear. It remains to be seen whether these new models provide a significant advantage over existing techniques.
