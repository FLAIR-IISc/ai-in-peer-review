

The paper presents a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure of an undirected network. While building such distributions is inherently challenging—well-documented in literature since Besag's work and covered in texts like Arnold et al. (1999)—the authors achieve notable progress. Key strengths include valid marginal count distributions and computationally efficient estimation algorithms. However, limitations persist: QPGM exhibits marginally thinner tails, and SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining its conditional specification premise. The claim that SPGM qualifies as a "Poisson distribution" is questionable (unlike QPGM, which merely modifies the base measure); moreover, the practical motivation for modeling counts is unclear, given the Poisson distribution's known inadequacy for empirical data (e.g., breast cancer read counts rarely resemble Poisson). A plot of the SPGM's probability mass function would clarify its behavior, and the selection of parameters \(R\) and \(R_0\) (assumed constant for exponential family validity) requires explicit practitioner guidance.  

The theoretical contribution is valid for constructing count distributions under MRF independence constraints. Yet, the literature review is deficient: the authors misrepresent copula-based approaches (e.g., [8], which addresses arbitrary discrete data with established inference methods) and overlook spatial statistics, where high-dimensional count analysis has long been standard. While acknowledging the paper's theoretical merit for NIPS, the practical justification remains weak. A structured Gaussian random field coupled with a Poisson observation model remains a simpler, well-established alternative for practitioners. Finally, reference [20] for "counts replacing microarrays" requires updating to a more recent source. The work is theoretically interesting but suffers from an incomplete literature review and unresolved practical concerns.
