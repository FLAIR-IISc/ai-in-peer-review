The paper introduces novel models for representing a joint distribution over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the "default" model, as proposed by Yang et al., is not satisfactory due to its limitation in modeling only negative correlations to ensure normalization. To address this issue, the authors present a series of modifications, including a new truncation method, the use of a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove to be necessary given the other fixed components), and a sub-linear sufficient statistic. Experimental results are provided for both synthetic and real data.

This is a well-written paper that presents innovative solutions for representing count data. The work appears to be original, and the results are significant. 

However, I found the discussion of the "big negatives" of the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unconvincing. For the truncation method, it is possible to imagine various domains where counts typically do not exceed a certain threshold, and the model would perform well in such cases. Similarly, for the quadratic model, "Gaussian-esque thin tails" may be suitable in certain situations.

I am unfamiliar with the term "sparsistent," although it appears to have been used in the literature for some time.

There are a couple of minor errors in the text. On page 3, above section 2.1.1, "graphical mode distributions" should be corrected to "graphical model distributions." On page 5, above section 3.1, the sentence "Note that the log..." would be clearer if rephrased as "Note that although the log..."

Overall, this is a well-written, high-quality paper on modeling a joint distribution over count variables.
