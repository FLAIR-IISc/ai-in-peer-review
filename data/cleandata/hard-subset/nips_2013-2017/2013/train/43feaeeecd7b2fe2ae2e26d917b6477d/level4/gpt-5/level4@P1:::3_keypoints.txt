- Paper proposes new multivariate count MRFs (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that permit positive and negative conditional dependencies and studies their normalizability.  
- Shows the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditional construction is incompatible with a valid joint (Theorem 1).  
- Introduces an exponential-family truncation (TPGM) that yields a valid joint on bounded support.  
- Proposes QPGM (quadratic base measure) and SPGM (sublinear sufficient statistics) to allow positive dependencies while addressing tail behavior and normalizability.  
- Structure learning is done via penalized neighborhood selection with tractable node-wise objectives or variational bounds.  
- Empirical evaluation includes simulations and a breast cancer miRNA sequencing case study showing improved performance over Gaussian baselines and identifying plausible hubs missed by standard PGM.  
- Situates work within a long literature on conditional specification/compatibility (Besag; Arnold et al.), multivariate Poisson constructions, log-linear models, copulas, and spatial hierarchical models.  
- Theorems clarifying incompatibility of Winsorization, providing a valid truncation, necessary base-measure conditions, and SPGM normalizability are strong technical contributions.  
- The new model classes broaden graphical models for counts by enabling both positive and negative conditional dependencies.  
- Neighborhood selection estimation strategy is practical and computationally tractable in principle.  
- Simulation results show advantages over Gaussian-based baselines.  
- Central computational weakness: SPGM node-conditional log-partition D(·) has no closed form and relies on infinite summation with only a variational upper bound proposed, raising concerns about optimization accuracy and runtime.  
- Request authors to detail how D and its derivatives are computed/approximated, quantify approximation error, and report runtimes.  
- Terminology issue: calling SPGM “Poisson-like” is unclear since it alters sufficient statistics; request justification and characterization of SPGM marginal PMFs and tail behavior versus Poisson/QPGM.  
- Practical guidance missing: how to choose user-specified thresholds R and R0 for SPGM (data-driven rules, cross-validation, theoretical guidance) and sensitivity of TPGM to R.  
- Model-fit concerns: Poisson family often misfits real counts (overdispersion/zero-inflation); ask for marginal histograms/dispersion summaries and comparisons to Poisson/negative binomial baselines for the miRNA data.  
- Literature coverage is incomplete/misleading: better acknowledge Gaussian copula methods for discrete data, sparse/hierarchical log-linear approaches, and spatial GLMMs (Poisson/NegBin observation models with Gaussian random fields); clarify differences and include empirical comparisons to spatial GLMMs or sparse-precision Gaussian copulas.  
- Theory vs practice gap: sparsistency of estimators is deferred; provide discussion of conditions for consistency of neighborhood selection for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM.  
- Minor: update an older reference about sequencing replacing microarrays and clarify scope of the claim.  
- Quality assessment: theory is careful and valuable, but SPGM computational treatment needs more detail and empirical validation of approximation quality.  
- Clarity assessment: paper is mostly well organized but lacks key implementation details (SPGM normalizer approximation, parameter selection) and needs expanded related work.  
- Originality: incompatibility of Winsorization, exponential-family truncation, and base-measure necessity results are novel; QPGM/SPGM constructions are original in this framework.  
- Significance: advances understanding of count-valued graphical models and offers useful alternatives, but impact depends on stronger empirical comparisons and practitioner guidance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear theoretical advances on compatibility/normalizability; new model classes allowing positive/negative dependencies; promising empirical performance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient treatment of SPGM computation and approximation error; incomplete literature discussion and missing strong baselines; limited practical guidance on hyperparameter selection and model fit.
