The paper provides a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that obey the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building a multivariate distribution over unbounded counts is in general a hard problem, as studied at length by Besag and others for the past 30 years (including textbooks such as Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models", 1999, which extends some of the observations of the authors to other distributions such as conditionally-specified exponential distributions). In the end, the proposal given by the authors succeeds in some relevant ways. The upsides are constructions that do allow for marginal distributions over counts and which lead to relatively simple estimation algorithms. The downsides are that the QPGM has marginally thinner tails and the SPGM does not have closed-form conditional distributions (which somewhat defeats the point of building a conditionally specified model). Indeed, I question whether the SPGM can even be rightfully called a Poisson distribution (though for QPGM one could at least claim that “only” the base measure is being changed). This led me to consider the primary motivation for modeling counts, since the Poisson distribution itself is often not a good fit for empirical data (though it is a very useful building block in stochastic processes and latent variable models). It would be valuable to see a plot of the probability mass function of the SPGM, which initially seems somewhat convoluted. I assume R and R0 are constants (otherwise, they wouldn't define exponential families). How are they chosen, and what guidance can be offered to practitioners?

That being said: constructing a multivariate distribution over counts obeying the independence model of an MRF is challenging. I appreciate the effort put into this paper and believe the results are of theoretical interest to NIPS. My main concern is the somewhat cursory engagement with the existing literature. For instance, the authors seem unaware of the broader context of copula models. The presentation of [8] feels incomplete; its core contribution is building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data (including count data) with a suite of MCMC and approximate inference methods. This raises questions about the relevance of the discussion of [11], as that work pioneers building discrete models with log-linear parameters that don't scale exponentially with the number of variables (though, admittedly, I don’t have a copy readily available to verify specifics regarding Poisson distributions). Perhaps the most significant omission is a complete disregard for the extensive literature in spatial statistics, which has long addressed high-dimensional count data analysis. It should be noted that many of these approaches (including the sparse precision Gaussian copula model of [8]) don't model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space. Thus, the theoretical contribution of this paper is valid. However, as a practitioner, I am not yet convinced why I should adopt this model space instead of the simpler structured Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model, which has been a standard approach for some time.

Final comment: I would appreciate a more recent reference than [20] claiming that “counts of sequencing reads…are replacing microarrays.” A method for constructing multivariate distributions for counts that is Markov with respect to undirected graphs. Like any nontrivial multivariate construction, it has its advantages and shortcomings. The literature review feels incomplete.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a challenging problem in modeling multivariate count data.
*   Provides novel constructions that allow for marginal Poisson distributions and relatively simple estimation.
*   Theoretically sound and potentially of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Literature review appears incomplete, overlooking related work in copula models and spatial statistics.
*   Practical benefits over existing methods (e.g., Gaussian graphical model + Poisson measurement model) are not fully demonstrated.
*   SPGM's connection to Poisson distributions is tenuous, and the choice of parameters R and R0 requires further justification.
*   Lack of a plot of the SPGM probability mass function hinders understanding.
