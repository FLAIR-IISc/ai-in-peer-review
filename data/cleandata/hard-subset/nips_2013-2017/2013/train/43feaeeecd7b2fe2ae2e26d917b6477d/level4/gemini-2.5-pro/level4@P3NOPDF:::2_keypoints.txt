- Paper addresses an important problem: constructing flexible multivariate count distributions and correctly identifies limits of existing approaches.  
- The authors prove a previously suggested truncation method for Poisson graphical models is invalid.  
- They propose three alternatives (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) based on conditional exponential-family modifications to allow positive and negative dependencies.  
- QPGM and SPGM introduce novel changes to the base measure and sufficient statistics; TPGM is natural and warranted.  
- Writing is generally clear and easy to follow.  
- Major weakness: experimental evaluation is insufficient to support practical claims.  
- Synthetic experiments are biased toward the proposed models’ data-generating assumptions and lack variety.  
- Real-data experiments are anecdotal and qualitative; no objective quantitative metrics (e.g., held-out log-probability) are reported.  
- Important baselines are missing or weak, notably modern copula-based methods for multivariate counts.  
- Competitors are handled simplistically (e.g., non-paranormal marginals without proper densities), yielding suspiciously similar performance to Glasso.  
- Comparisons are unfair: competing methods’ flexibility isn’t leveraged (e.g., using R=11 to inform copula marginals), undermining claims of superiority.  
- Suggested fixes: stronger, well-configured baselines (Gaussian copulas with discrete marginals), quantitative evaluation (test log-probability), and more diverse datasets.  
- Presentation improvements: expand and clarify Section 3.2, move key illustrative figure into main text, and condense less relevant bounds in Section 3.1.  
- Rigor: include all proofs in the supplementary material to support claims and corrections to prior work.  
- Overall: models are interesting and theoretically motivated, but practical advantages are unproven due to inadequate empirical validation.
