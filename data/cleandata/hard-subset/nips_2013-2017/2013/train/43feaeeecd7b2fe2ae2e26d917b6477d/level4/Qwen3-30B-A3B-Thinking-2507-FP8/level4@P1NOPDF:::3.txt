

The paper proposes a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded count data that respect the Markov properties of undirected graphical models. While building such distributions is notoriously challenging—extensively studied by Besag and colleagues over the past three decades (including foundational work like Arnold et al.'s *Conditional Specification of Statistical Models*, 1999, which generalizes these ideas to conditionally specified exponential families)—the authors achieve meaningful progress in specific aspects. Their constructions enable marginal count distributions and yield relatively straightforward estimation algorithms, representing notable advantages.  

However, significant limitations exist. QPGM exhibits marginally thinner tails than standard Poisson models, while SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions—a critical drawback for a conditionally specified model. This raises a fundamental question: how can SPGM reasonably be termed a "Poisson distribution" when QPGM merely modifies the base measure? While this is acceptable theoretically, it prompts deeper reflection on the practical motivation for modeling counts. The Poisson distribution is rarely a good fit for empirical count data (e.g., breast cancer read counts), despite its utility as a building block in hierarchical models or stochastic processes. The authors should provide a plot of SPGM's probability mass function to clarify its behavior and address the apparent complexity of its formulation. Additionally, it remains unclear how parameters like *R* and *R₀* are selected—this is essential for practitioners. Are these fixed, or estimated? What guidance do the authors offer?  

Theoretical contributions are valid: constructing multivariate count distributions respecting Markov constraints for undirected graphs is nontrivial, and the authors’ effort merits recognition. However, the literature review is notably incomplete and undermines the paper’s credibility. The discussion of [8] (a seminal work on copula-based multivariate discrete models) suggests a misunderstanding of copulas, which are explicitly designed for arbitrary discrete data—including counts—with established MCMC and approximate inference methods. The authors misrepresent [8]’s core purpose, which is to build flexible discrete models without exponential growth in parameters. Similarly, the reference to [11] appears misaligned with its actual contribution (a method for log-linear discrete models avoiding exponential parameter growth), though I cannot verify this without the text at hand. Most critically, the paper neglects the vast spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been extensively developed for decades. While approaches like the sparse precision Gaussian copula model in [8] may not directly enforce observable-space Markov constraints, their relevance to count data modeling is substantial and should be acknowledged.  

As a practitioner, I remain unconvinced why one should adopt this framework over the well-established structured Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model, which is simpler and widely validated. The paper does not adequately justify the trade-offs.  

Finally, the claim in [20] that "counts of sequencing reads are replacing microarrays" requires updating. The authors should cite more recent literature supporting this shift (e.g., newer studies in *Nature Methods* or *Bioinformatics*).  

In summary, the paper offers a theoretically interesting contribution to multivariate count modeling under Markov constraints, but its practical relevance is weakened by unresolved technical issues (SPGM’s definition, parameter selection), an incomplete literature review, and insufficient justification against established alternatives. The theoretical value is clear, yet the practical motivation remains unclear.
