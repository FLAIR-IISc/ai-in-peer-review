### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses a significant limitation in existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), which are used for modeling multivariate count data. As the authors correctly point out, standard PGMs derived from the construction of Besag and Yang et al. are restricted to non-positive edge parameters to ensure the distribution is normalizable. This severely limits their applicability, as they can only model negative conditional dependencies ("competitive" relationships).

To overcome this, the paper proposes and analyzes three novel classes of graphical models for count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies:

1.  **Truncated PGM (TPGM):** The authors first investigate truncation of the support. They demonstrate that a prior approach, the Winsorized Poisson, does not lead to a valid joint distribution. They then propose a new, valid truncation method that results in the TPGM, an exponential family model on a finite domain that allows for arbitrary dependencies.
2.  **Quadratic PGM (QPGM):** To address the limitations of a fixed truncation level, the paper explores more fundamental modifications. The authors prove that for a model with linear sufficient statistics (like the standard PGM) to be normalizable with positive edge weights, the base measure must scale at least quadratically. This leads to the QPGM, which uses a quadratic base measure (`c*X^2`) and has a normalizability condition analogous to that of Gaussian Graphical Models.
3.  **Sub-linear PGM (SPGM):** Acknowledging that the QPGM's quadratic term results in "Gaussian-esque" thin tails, the authors propose a final model that modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear. This allows the model to retain the original `log(X!)` base measure of the Poisson distribution while still ensuring normalizability for all real-valued edge parameters.

The authors provide theoretical justification for each model and demonstrate their practical utility through structure recovery experiments on both simulated networks and a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and important problem in the modeling of multivariate count data. The inability of standard PGMs to handle positive dependencies is a major practical bottleneck. This work provides a set of principled, well-motivated, and novel solutions. The proposed models significantly advance the state-of-the-art for this data type and are likely to be built upon by future research.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are sound and rigorously presented. The paper's progression is very logical: it clearly identifies a problem, shows why a seemingly natural solution (Winsorized Poisson) is flawed (Theorem 1), and then systematically builds a series of valid models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM), with each new model addressing a potential shortcoming of the previous one. The proofs regarding the necessity of a quadratic base measure (Theorem 3) and the normalizability of the proposed models are valuable contributions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the development of the different models is easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind each modeling choice.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the advantages of the proposed models. On both simulated and real data, the TPGM and SPGM outperform methods designed for continuous data (like the GGM and nonparanormal) when applied to count data. The case study on microRNA data is particularly compelling, as it shows that the new models can uncover biologically relevant structures missed by both the standard PGM and Gaussian-based methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in how it frames the limitations of the intermediate models (TPGM and QPGM), which could be presented with more nuance.

*   The drawbacks of the TPGM (a priori bounded domain and behavior near the truncation level `R`) are presented as major flaws motivating the subsequent models. While these are valid limitations, the authors could strengthen the paper by also discussing the many practical scenarios where these might be minor issues. In numerous applications, counts are naturally bounded or rarely exceed a certain value, making the TPGM a perfectly suitable and perhaps simpler choice than the SPGM. A more balanced discussion of these trade-offs would be beneficial.
*   Similarly, the "Gaussian-esque thin tails" of the QPGM are presented as a definitive negative. However, for certain types of count data, a thin-tailed distribution might be an appropriate modeling assumption. Acknowledging that this property could be desirable in some contexts, rather than just a weakness, would make the argument more balanced.

While the focus is on model specification, the claim that network structure can be learned via neighborhood selection would be slightly strengthened by a brief theoretical argument for why the sparsistency results of Yang et al. are expected to extend to these new models, even if the full proof is left for future work.

**Minor Comments and Typos**

*   Page 3, paragraph before Section 2.1.1: "graphical mode distributions" should be "graphical model distributions".
*   Page 5, first paragraph of Section 3.1: The sentence "Note that although the log partition function A(θ) is usually computationally intractable, the log-partition function D̄(·) of its node-conditional distribution (4) is still tractable..." is slightly dense. It could be clarified for easier reading, for example: "Although the joint log-partition function A(θ) is usually intractable, the node-conditional log-partition function D̄(·) remains tractable..."

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-written paper that makes a significant and original contribution to an important area. The proposed models are elegant, theoretically sound, and practically useful. I strongly recommend acceptance.
