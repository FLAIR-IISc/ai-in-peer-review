Addressing an important problem – the construction of multivariate distributions suitable for count data – the authors appropriately identify shortcomings in existing methods, with a minor caveat noted below.

The paper demonstrates several theoretical and conceptual strengths. Beginning with a proof demonstrating the invalidity of previously proposed truncation, the authors introduce several alternative approaches. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its clear interpretability. The QPGM and SPGM models are technically simple yet novel in their framing of the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation as tools for modifying the Poisson distribution. These modifications are well-motivated and intuitively appealing. However, the underlying theory largely follows from the work of Yang et al., resulting in a collection of model suggestions whose practical value remains to be determined.

Consequently, the experimental evaluation is underwhelming, especially considering the paper’s stated aim of addressing practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some promise, the data is tailored to favor the proposed models. The real-data experiments lack a crucial comparison to copula-based alternatives, and the reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. A more objective evaluation, such as a log-probability assessment of test data, is necessary given the joint distribution construction inherent to all methods.

More fundamentally, the evaluation’s treatment of competing copula methods raises concerns about the necessity of the proposed models. The copula competitors are employed in an overly “black-box” manner; even a simple Gaussian copula, paired with a sensible marginal model (rather than the non-parametric choice used, which lacks a density and was motivated primarily by asymptotic properties), would likely yield superior results. The performance nearly identical to Glasso is suspect, and it is probable that even basic Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform better. Similarly, the choice of R=11 in the real-data analysis could inform a more appropriate marginal distribution for the copula. While an exhaustive exploration isn’t expected, a more reasonable baseline comparison is warranted.

The paper is generally well-written. Although I believe the results are valid – particularly given the correction of an error in prior work – I recommend moving all proofs to the supplementary material. Section 3.1 contains excessive detail, and the presented bound does not substantially strengthen the Gaussian-esque argument. Conversely, Section 3.2, representing the core of the proposed methodology, should be expanded; the figure currently relegated to the supplement should be included in the main text, and its underlying intuition should be more thoroughly explained.

In summary, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model based on a conditional exponential construction, aiming for flexible joint modeling capable of capturing both positive and negative dependencies. The approach is conceptually appealing and exhibits some theoretical novelty. However, the limited and somewhat biased experimental evaluation leaves the practical merit of this new multivariate Poisson-like model unclear.
