Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles a well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the canonical auto-Poisson/Poisson MRF constructions (Besag, 1974; Yang et al., 2012/2013) are only normalizable when pairwise parameters are non-positive, hence capture only negative conditional dependencies. The authors analyze why this happens and propose three alternatives that preserve conditional specification while permitting both positive and negative dependencies for count-valued variables.

First, they show that the “Winsorized” (hard-truncated) univariate Poisson used as a node-conditional (Kaiser & Cressie, 1997; Griffith, 2004) cannot be embedded in a compatible joint MRF (Theorem 1). They then introduce an exponential-family truncation that redistributes the tail mass over the truncated support, yielding a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) with finite partition function and no sign constraints on edges.

Next, they consider more fundamental modifications of the exponential-family components. They prove that, with linear sufficient statistics B(x)=x, any normalizable joint model allowing positive pairwise interactions requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which replaces the Poisson base measure log x! by a quadratic term and admits both positive and negative interactions under a negative-definite quadratic form. Finally, to obtain heavier tails than QPGM, they propose the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which keeps the Poisson base measure but replaces B(x) with a sublinear function that flattens beyond a threshold (parameters R0, R). They derive variational upper bounds to handle the SPGM node-conditional normalizers and adopt ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection for structure learning. Experiments on simulated networks and a TCGA microRNA-seq dataset indicate improved edge recovery over Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines, and over the original PGM.

The work relates to: auto-models and exponential-family MRFs (Besag; Yang et al.), multivariate Poisson constructions via latent summation (Holgate; Karlis), log-linear models for contingency tables (Lauritzen; classic texts), Gaussian copula graphical models for discrete data (Dobra & Lenkoski; Liu et al.), and spatial count models (CAR/SAR, Poisson log-normal GLMMs; Cressie). Unlike copula or latent-Gaussian models, the proposed models impose conditional independencies directly in the observed count space.

Strengths:
- Clear diagnosis of why the standard PGM forbids positive interactions, and a rigorous impossibility result for the Winsorized conditional approach.
- Three concrete, compatible joint models that expand the feasible dependency structure: a principled exponential-family truncation (TPGM), a quadratic-base model with Gaussian-like behavior (QPGM), and a heavier-tailed alternative with sublinear sufficient stats (SPGM).
- Use of penalized node-conditional likelihood fits naturally within the exponential-family MRF framework, suggesting scalability and applicability of existing algorithms.
- Empirical results show improved recovery over Gaussian-based methods on synthetic and real count data, and highlight the original PGM’s limitation in practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality
- SPGM computation: The node-conditional normalizer lacks a closed form and is handled via an upper bound. Please clarify precisely what is optimized (true conditional likelihood vs bound), how the bound is used in proximal updates, and whether the resulting estimator is consistent for the SPGM parameters. Provide complexity and convergence details.
- Choice and role of R and R0: The performance and tail behavior of SPGM hinge on these thresholds. Are they fixed across nodes? How are they chosen in practice (e.g., via held-out likelihood, stability selection grid, quantiles of the marginal counts)? Report sensitivity analyses.
- TPGM truncation level R: In simulations, R is set to the observed maximum; in general, high counts near R can distort dependence. Please discuss bias–variance trade-offs and provide guidance for choosing R, especially when counts are unbounded.
- Theorem 1: Given its importance (overturning a prior line of work), a proof sketch in the main text would help readers grasp the incompatibility intuition.

Clarity
- Terminology: SPGM is “Poisson-like” but no longer Poisson in either base measure or sufficient statistic; consider clarifying the naming and emphasizing that the univariate marginals are not Poisson.
- Intuition and visualization: Include univariate PMFs for TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM under comparable parameters, highlighting tail behavior. For SPGM, plot B(x; R0, R) and the induced PMF for representative θ to demystify the construction.
- Experimental details: Specify optimization settings (step sizes, stopping criteria), regularization path, and the stability selection parameters. Provide variance/confidence bands on ROC curves.

Originality
- The necessity result on the base measure growth (Ω(x^2) under linear sufficient stats) and the impossibility of the Winsorized approach are novel and insightful. Emphasize how these results delimit the design space of count MRFs.

Significance
- The ability to model both positive and negative conditional dependencies for counts in an MRF is important. That said, many practitioners use latent Gaussian/Poisson log-normal models or copula-based approaches, which trade exact observed-space MRF structure for modeling convenience and heavier tails. A clearer empirical comparison to a Poisson log-normal or copula-based discrete GGM would strengthen the case for your models.
- For the RNA-seq case study, justify the count-modeling assumptions with marginal histograms/overdispersion diagnostics, and provide updated references supporting the claim that RNA-seq counts are supplanting microarrays (beyond Marioni et al., 2008).

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a long-standing limitation of Poisson MRFs with clear theoretical contributions.
- Provides practical model classes with straightforward estimation strategies.
- Demonstrates improved performance on relevant datasets.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete empirical comparisons to prominent alternatives (Poisson log-normal, copula-based discrete GGM).
- Limited guidance on selecting SPGM/TPGM hyperparameters and limited analysis of sensitivity.
- Some computational aspects (SPGM optimization via bounds) need clearer exposition and guarantees.

Overall evaluation:
A thoughtful and technically solid contribution that extends the toolbox for undirected graphical modeling of counts. While some modeling choices (especially SPGM) require clearer exposition and more guidance for practitioners, the theoretical insights and the provision of feasible alternatives to the negative-only PGM make this paper a valuable addition. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity, literature positioning, and practical guidance points above.
