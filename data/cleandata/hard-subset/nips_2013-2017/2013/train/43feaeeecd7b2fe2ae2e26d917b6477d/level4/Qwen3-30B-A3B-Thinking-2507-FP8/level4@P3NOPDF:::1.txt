

This paper proposes novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables, addressing a key limitation in Yang et al.'s approach, which restricts the model to negative correlations for valid normalization. The authors introduce three key contributions: (1) a new truncation method, (2) a quadratic base measure statistic (proven necessary under fixed other components), and (3) a sub-linear sufficient statistic. Experimental validation on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the efficacy of these approaches.  

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the modeling of count data. The technical innovations are original and address a genuine gap in the literature. The experimental results are thorough and convincingly support the authors' claims. The work is a strong fit for NIPS, with clear potential impact for applications involving discrete data.  

That said, two key limitations warrant discussion. First, the critique of the truncation method's "big negatives" appears somewhat overstated. The authors argue truncation is unsuitable for domains where counts typically remain low (e.g., rare events), but the model could perform well in such settings. A more nuanced discussion of *when* truncation is advantageous—perhaps through domain-specific examples or theoretical bounds on truncation thresholds—would strengthen the argument. Second, the quadratic model's restriction to "Gaussian-esque thin tails" is questioned. While the authors dismiss this as undesirable, there are contexts where Gaussian-like tail behavior is appropriate (e.g., certain biological or social phenomena with bounded counts). A brief exploration of such cases—or a justification for why thin tails are *inherently* problematic for the target applications—would better defend this design choice.  

The term "sparsistent" appears in the paper without definition. While this term is used in related literature (e.g., for sparse model consistency), its precise meaning in this context would aid readability. A one-sentence clarification would be beneficial.  

Minor editorial notes:  
- Page 3, above Section 2.1.1: "graphical mode distributions" → "graphical model distributions" (assuming "mode" is a typo for "model").  
- Page 5, above Section 3.1: The phrase "Note that the log..." is incomplete. The intended meaning appears to be "Note that although the log..."—please clarify this sentence.  

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with meaningful technical contributions. The core ideas are sound, the writing is clear, and the results are compelling. The identified limitations are manageable and do not undermine the paper's significance. With minor revisions addressing the points above, this work would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
