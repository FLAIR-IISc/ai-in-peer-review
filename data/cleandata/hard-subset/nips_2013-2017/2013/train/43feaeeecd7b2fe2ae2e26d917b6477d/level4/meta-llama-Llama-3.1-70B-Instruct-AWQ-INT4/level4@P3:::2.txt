This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling multivariate count data using graphical models, which is an important problem in many applications. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches, including the Poisson graphical model, and provide a clear motivation for their work.

The paper has several theoretical and conceptual merits. The authors start by proving that a previously suggested truncation approach does not lead to a valid model, and then propose several alternative approaches. The Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) truncation is a natural and intuitive approach, while the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) and Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) are novel in that they modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation. The changes made are well-motivated and appealing.

However, the actual theory involved follows almost directly from the work of Yang et al, so that we are left with several possible model suggestions whose merit needs to be evaluated in practice. In light of this, the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing, particularly since the declared goal of the paper is to fix the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models.

The synthetic experiment shows some potential, but the data is geared towards the models suggested. In the real experiments, the comparison to copula-based alternatives is missing, and the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. Since all methods involve the construction of joint distributions, a more objective log-probability of test data evaluation is needed.

More importantly, the paper raises the question of whether there is a need for the new model. The copula competitors are used in an overly black-box manner, and it makes more sense to use some sensible marginal model rather than the one used in the non-paranormal. The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious, and it is likely that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would do much better.

The paper is generally well-written, and the results are likely true. However, including all proofs in the supplementary material would be helpful. The end of Section 3.1 is overly detailed, and the bound does not contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. On the other hand, Section 3.2 is the heart of the suggested method, and it would be beneficial to take it more slowly and present the figure in the main text.

Overall, the approach suggested is appealing and has some theoretical novelty. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, so that the bottom line is yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose merit is unclear.

**Strengths:**

* The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling multivariate count data using graphical models.
* The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches and provide a clear motivation for their work.
* The theoretical and conceptual contributions are well-motivated and appealing.

**Weaknesses:**

* The experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing and biased towards the proposed models.
* The comparison to copula-based alternatives is missing, and the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal.
* The paper raises the question of whether there is a need for the new model, and the copula competitors are used in an overly black-box manner.

**Suggestions:**

* Include all proofs in the supplementary material.
* Take Section 3.2 more slowly and present the figure in the main text.
* Provide a more objective log-probability of test data evaluation.
* Compare the proposed models to copula-based alternatives using a more sensible marginal model.
* Consider using simple Gaussian kernel density estimates as a baseline.
