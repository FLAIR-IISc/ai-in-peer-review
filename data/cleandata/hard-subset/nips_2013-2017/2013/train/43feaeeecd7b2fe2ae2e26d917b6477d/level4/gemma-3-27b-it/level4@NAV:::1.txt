The paper introduces novel models for representing joint distributions of integer-count variables. The authors identify limitations in the previously proposed model from Yang et al., specifically its inability to represent positive correlations while maintaining normalization. To address this, they investigate several improvements, including a new truncation approach, the incorporation of a quadratic base measure statistic (demonstrated as a necessary component), and the introduction of a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The effectiveness of these models is evaluated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Overall, this is a clearly written paper presenting effective solutions for modeling count data. The work appears original and the findings are impactful. 

I have some reservations regarding the discussion of the truncation and quadratic models’ limitations. The argument against truncation seems potentially overstated; many practical applications might involve count data with naturally bounded upper limits where this model could perform well. Similarly, the criticism of the quadratic model’s “Gaussian-esque thin tails” doesn’t fully resonate, as such behavior may be appropriate in certain contexts. 

The term "sparsistent" is unfamiliar, though I acknowledge its established usage within the field. 

Minor corrections: on page 3, above section 2.1.1, “graphical mode distributions” should be revised to “model.” On page 5, above section 3.1, “Note that the log…” would benefit from clarification – perhaps changing it to “Note that although the log…” This is a high-quality paper addressing an important problem in modeling joint distributions over count variables.
