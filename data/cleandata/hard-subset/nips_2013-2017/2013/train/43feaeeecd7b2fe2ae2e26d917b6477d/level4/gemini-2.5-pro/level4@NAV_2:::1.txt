This paper addresses a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which, due to normalizability constraints on an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. The authors propose three novel classes of graphical models for count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies. The paper first examines truncation as a solution, demonstrating that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional does not yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1). In its place, the authors propose a new Truncated PGM (TPGM) based on a valid exponential family formulation (Theorem 2). Acknowledging the limitations of truncation, the paper then explores more fundamental modifications. It proves that for linear sufficient statistics, a quadratic base measure is necessary to allow for positive dependencies (Theorem 3), leading to the development of the Quadratic PGM (QPGM). Finally, to allow for heavier tails than the QPGM, the authors introduce the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, thereby ensuring normalizability for any real-valued pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). The performance of these models is demonstrated on simulated data and a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and important open problem in the modeling of multivariate count data. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive ("cooperative") relationships severely limits their applicability. The proposed models provide a theoretically sound and practical solution, significantly advancing the state of the art for this data modality, which is increasingly common in fields like genomics.
*   **Originality:** The contributions are novel and well-motivated. The refutation of the Winsorized PGM approach is an important negative result. The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent new and distinct theoretical contributions, each developed from a careful analysis of the limitations of the preceding model.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs (e.g., Theorems 1-4), and the motivation for each new model is rigorously established. The experimental evaluation, while focused on model specification, effectively demonstrates the practical advantages of the proposed methods over Gaussian-based approaches and the standard PGM on both synthetic and real data.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is stated clearly, and the progression from the standard PGM to the TPGM, QPGM, and finally the SPGM is logical and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The dismissal of the TPGM and QPGM models seems somewhat overstated. The paper presents their limitations as major drawbacks, but these might be less critical in practice. For the TPGM, many real-world applications may involve count data with a natural, effective upper bound where the model would perform well without issue. For the QPGM, the "Gaussian-esque thin tails" could be an appropriate and desirable modeling assumption for certain types of count data, rather than an inherent weakness. The discussion could be strengthened by clarifying the specific scenarios or data characteristics where these limitations would be most problematic.
*   The theoretical analysis for structure recovery is mentioned as future work. While the focus is on model specification, a brief discussion outlining how the sparsistency analysis of Yang et al. (2012, 2013) would extend to these new models would further strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The term "sparsistent" is used on page 5. While established in the literature, a brief definition or citation upon its first use would improve readability for a broader audience.
*   Page 3, last paragraph before section 2.1.1: "graphical mode distributions" should likely be "graphical model distributions".
*   Page 5, first paragraph of section 3.1: "Note that the log partition function..." reads slightly awkwardly. Consider rephrasing to "Note that although the log partition function...".
