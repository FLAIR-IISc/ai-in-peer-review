This paper presents a series of novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The work is motivated by a key limitation in the Poisson graphical model framework of Yang et al. (2012), which, as the authors demonstrate, can only model negative conditional correlations to ensure the distribution is normalizable. This paper systematically addresses this limitation by proposing and analyzing three distinct solutions: a new truncation method for the variable domains, a model employing a quadratic base measure statistic (which is proven to be a necessary condition for modeling positive correlations under certain constraints), and finally a model with a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The effectiveness of these approaches is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths and Significance**

This is a well-written and clear paper that addresses a significant and practical limitation in existing models for multivariate count data. The contributions are original and technically sound. The authors clearly motivate the problem and systematically develop several solutions, each with a thoughtful analysis of its properties and trade-offs. The progression from the truncation method to the quadratic and sub-linear models provides a comprehensive exploration of the design space. The theoretical results, particularly the proof regarding the necessity of a quadratic base measure, are valuable contributions that deepen our understanding of this model class. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real data provides convincing evidence for the proposed methods' utility.

**Points for Discussion**

While the paper's motivation and solutions are strong, the arguments against the intermediate models (truncation and quadratic) could be nuanced further. The authors frame the limitations of these models as significant drawbacks, but their applicability might be broader than implied.

1.  **Regarding the truncation model:** The authors rightly point out the issue with selecting the truncation level K. However, for many practical domains, counts are naturally bounded or rarely exceed a certain value (e.g., word counts in short documents, or counts of items in a small inventory). In such scenarios, a reasonably chosen K might yield a very effective and computationally convenient model. A brief discussion of such use cases would strengthen the paper by providing a more balanced view of the model's utility.

2.  **Regarding the quadratic model:** The critique focuses on its "Gaussian-esque thin tails," which may not be suitable for all count data. While true, this property could be desirable for certain types of data where extreme counts are genuinely rare and can be considered outliers. Could the authors comment on specific applications where this tail behavior might actually be an appropriate modeling choice rather than a strict limitation?

**Minor Comments and Typos**

The paper is of high quality, with only a few minor points for improvement:

*   The term "sparsistent" may not be familiar to all readers. While it appears to be an established term in the literature, a brief parenthetical definition or a citation to its origin could improve accessibility for a broader audience.
*   Page 3, above section 2.1.1: "graphical mode distributions" should likely be "graphical model distributions".
*   Page 5, above section 3.1: The sentence "Note that the log partition function..." might be clearer if rephrased. For example: "Note that although the log partition function is still intractable..."

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting significant and well-executed work on an important topic. The proposed models are elegant solutions to a concrete problem in probabilistic modeling.
