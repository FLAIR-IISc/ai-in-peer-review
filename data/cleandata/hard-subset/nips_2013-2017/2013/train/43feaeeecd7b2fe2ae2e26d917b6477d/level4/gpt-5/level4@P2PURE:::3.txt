The paper proposes a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building multivariate distributions over unbounded counts is generally difficult—an issue explored extensively by Besag and others over the past three decades (see also Arnold et al.’s “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models,” 1999, which extends some of the authors’ observations to other conditionally specified distributions, such as exponential families).

Overall, the authors’ proposal succeeds in several meaningful ways. On the positive side, the constructions yield valid marginal distributions over counts and lead to relatively simple estimation procedures. On the negative side, QPGM appears to have marginally thinner tails, and SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions—which undercuts the rationale for a conditionally specified model. In fact, it is unclear why SPGM should be described as “Poisson”; for QPGM one can at least argue that only the base measure is changed. That is acceptable in principle, but it raises the question of why modeling counts via Poisson-like forms is the main motivation here, since the Poisson distribution is often a poor fit to empirical count data (while still being a valuable building block in stochastic processes and latent variable models). For example, could you plot the breast cancer data and show whether it resembles a Poisson? It would also be very helpful to see a plot of SPGM’s probability mass function, which seems somewhat convoluted at first glance. I assume R and R0 are treated as constants (otherwise these would not be exponential families). How are these selected in practice, and what guidance can you offer practitioners?

That said, constructing a multivariate distribution over counts that obeys an MRF independence structure is genuinely challenging. I appreciate the effort in this paper, and I believe the results are of theoretical interest to NIPS. My main concern is the relatively light engagement with the literature. For example, the discussion suggests some confusion about copulas. The treatment of [8] seems inaccurate (or at least misrepresented): the central idea there is to build multivariate models for arbitrary discrete data (including counts), with an established toolkit of MCMC and approximate inference methods. This also casts doubt on whether the discussion of [11] is on point, since that pioneering book focuses on constructing discrete models with log-linear parameters in a way that does not grow exponentially with the number of variables (though to be fair, I do not have a copy on hand and do not recall its treatment of Poisson models). Perhaps the most significant omission is the extensive spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data have long been analyzed. To be clear, several of these approaches (including the sparse-precision Gaussian copula model in [8]) do not enforce MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space. Thus the theoretical contribution of this paper remains valid. However, from a practitioner’s perspective, I am not yet convinced of the benefits of restricting to this model class compared with the simpler—and long-standard—structured Gaussian random field plus Poisson measurement model.

Final comment: I am not a specialist in gene expression analysis, but a reference newer than [20] supporting the claim that “counts of sequencing reads ... are replacing microarrays” would be appreciated.

A method for constructing multivariate distributions for counts that is Markov with respect to undirected graphs. Like any nontrivial multivariate construction, it has its advantages and shortcomings. Literature review feels incomplete.
