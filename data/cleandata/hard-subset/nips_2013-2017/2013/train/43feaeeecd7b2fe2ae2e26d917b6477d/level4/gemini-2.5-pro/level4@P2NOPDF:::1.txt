This paper introduces and analyzes several new models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors begin by identifying a key limitation of the model proposed by Yang et al. (2012): its inability to model positive correlations while ensuring the distribution is normalizable. To address this deficiency, the paper systematically explores a series of solutions. These include a novel truncation method for the variable support, the use of a quadratic base measure statistic (which is proven to be a necessary condition given other model components), and finally, a model employing a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The proposed models are validated empirically on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

This is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem in modeling discrete data. The motivation is clear, the technical contributions are original, and the results appear significant. The logical progression from identifying a problem with an existing model to developing and analyzing several potential solutions makes the paper easy to follow and compelling.

My primary critique concerns the framing of the drawbacks of the truncation and quadratic models, which the authors present as major limitations. I found this characterization somewhat unconvincing, as the perceived weaknesses may not be problematic in all application contexts.

For instance, the truncation model is set aside due to the need to pre-specify a maximum count. However, many interesting real-world domains exist where counts are naturally bounded or rarely exceed a known threshold. In such cases, this model could be a practical and effective choice.

Similarly, the quadratic model is critiqued for producing "Gaussian-esque thin tails." While heavy-tailed distributions are indeed important, thin-tailed behavior could be an appropriate and desirable modeling assumption for certain types of data. It would strengthen the paper if the authors acknowledged the potential utility of these earlier models in specific scenarios, positioning them as valid alternatives rather than simply as flawed predecessors to their final proposed model.
