This paper introduces novel graphical models for multivariate count data that can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies, addressing a major limitation of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) which are restricted to only negative dependencies. The authors first analyze truncation-based approaches, demonstrating that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson model does not yield a valid joint distribution. They then propose a valid alternative, the Truncated PGM (TPGM). To address the limitations of a bounded domain, the paper develops two additional models by fundamentally modifying the univariate Poisson distribution: the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which employs a quadratic base measure to ensure normalizability, and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which uses sub-linear sufficient statistics to allow for heavier tails. The authors provide a structure learning framework based on penalized neighborhood selection and demonstrate the models' performance on simulated networks and a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known and challenging problem in the graphical models literature: creating a tractable multivariate distribution for count data that allows for a rich dependency structure. The standard PGM's inability to model positive dependencies is a severe practical limitation. This work offers a significant and principled step forward by proposing three distinct and novel constructions to overcome this issue.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents its contributions clearly. A key contribution is the formal proof (Theorem 1) that the Winsorized Poisson conditionals do not lead to a consistent joint distribution, correcting a previous claim in the literature. The subsequent development of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The analysis connecting the normalizability of models with linear sufficient statistics to the need for a quadratic base measure is particularly insightful.
*   **Potential Impact:** Given the increasing prevalence of high-dimensional count data in fields like genomics, the proposed models have high potential for practical impact. The experiments on microRNA expression data suggest that these new models can identify important network structures and hub nodes that are missed by both Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM. The fact that model estimation can be framed within the well-understood neighborhood selection framework is a major advantage for adoption.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** The paper's positioning with respect to the broader literature on multivariate count data could be strengthened.
    *   A significant omission is a comparison with latent variable models. A standard and highly flexible approach for modeling correlated count data involves using a latent Gaussian graphical model to specify the dependency structure, with the observed counts drawn from a Poisson or Negative Binomial distribution conditional on the latent variables. A discussion on the relative merits of the proposed direct modeling approach versus this established hierarchical framework is needed to contextualize the contribution.
    *   The paper also overlooks the extensive literature in spatial statistics, which has a long history of developing models for high-dimensional, correlated count data.
    *   The discussion of existing alternatives like copula-based models (e.g., [8] in the paper) is brief and could be expanded to provide a more complete overview of the landscape.
*   **Practical Considerations and Model Limitations:** While the proposed models are innovative, their practical application comes with trade-offs that warrant further discussion.
    *   The QPGM, by using a quadratic base measure, results in a model with Gaussian-like thin tails. This may not be appropriate for many real-world count datasets, which often exhibit overdispersion (variance greater than the mean).
    *   The SPGM is more flexible but at the cost of tractability. Its node-conditional distributions are not in closed form, which complicates inference and may make estimation computationally challenging. The reliance on a variational upper bound for the log-partition function is a practical solution, but the implications of this approximation should be discussed.
    *   The paper lacks clear guidance on selecting the new hyperparameters introduced (the truncation level R for TPGM, and R and R0 for SPGM). For these models to be useful to practitioners, principled methods for hyperparameter selection (e.g., based on cross-validation) should be discussed.
*   **Experimental Justification:** The empirical validation could be made more compelling. The motivation for applying a Poisson-like model to the microRNA dataset is assumed rather than demonstrated. The paper would be strengthened by including a brief exploratory analysis of the data (e.g., plots of marginal distributions) to justify that a Poisson-like structure is appropriate, as opposed to, for example, a Negative Binomial which explicitly models overdispersion. Additionally, a figure showing the probability mass function of the novel SPGM would greatly aid the reader's intuition.

**Summary of Comments**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to an important and difficult problem. The authors creatively design three novel classes of graphical models for count data that successfully move beyond the restrictive assumptions of the standard PGM. The theoretical analysis is clear and insightful. The main weaknesses are the limited positioning with respect to alternative modeling paradigms (especially latent variable models) and the lack of discussion on practical issues like hyperparameter selection and model choice. Despite these concerns, the novelty and significance of the work are high, and the paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses noted above would substantially increase its impact.
