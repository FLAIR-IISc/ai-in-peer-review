- Tackles an important limitation of Poisson graphical models by enabling modeling of both positive and negative dependencies in count data.  
- Formally shows the previously proposed "Winsorized" truncation (Kaiser & Cressie) is invalid (Theorem 1), clarifying the literature.  
- Proposes three novel models: TPGM (valid truncation on a finite domain), QPGM (quadratic modification of Poisson base measure), and SPGM (sub-linear sufficient statistics).  
- The QPGM and SPGM are conceptually novel strategies that modify exponential-family components to ensure normalizability without sign restrictions.  
- The theoretical development appears sound and well-motivated, following Yang et al.'s framework.  
- Main empirical weakness: experiments are not comprehensive enough to demonstrate practical superiority of the proposed models.  
- Synthetic experiments are biased, using data generated from TPGM or related sum-of-Poissons, showing recovery of own-gen models but not performance under misspecification.  
- Baselines are insufficient, notably lacking robust comparisons to copula-based approaches (e.g., Gaussian copula/nonparanormal with better marginals).  
- Real-data comparisons to nonparanormal appear superficial; more appropriate marginals (e.g., kernel-estimated) could yield stronger baselines.  
- Evaluation lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., held-out log-likelihood); relying on ROC curves and qualitative network inspection is inadequate.  
- Authors should include full proofs of all theorems in the supplement for verification.  
- The Gaussian variational upper bound discussion in Section 3.1 is overly detailed and could be condensed.  
- Section 3.2 (SPGM) feels rushed; needs stronger intuition, expanded explanation, and moving its figure from the appendix to the main text.  
- For acceptance: addresses a well-known problem, provides a valuable negative result, and introduces elegant, novel models (TPGM/QPGM/SPGM).  
- Against acceptance: empirical validation is preliminary and biased, baselines are incomplete, and lack of quantitative test evaluation leaves practical benefits unproven.
