- Tackles constructing multivariate count distributions with Markov properties to overcome PGMs' restriction to negative dependencies.  
- Proposes three models: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  
- Demonstrates the previously proposed Winsorized Poisson conditional [20] cannot yield a valid joint distribution.  
- Introduces TPGM that produces a valid MRF and permits positive and negative dependencies but is sensitive to truncation level R.  
- QPGM changes the base measure to C(Z) ∝ Z², guaranteeing normalizability for arbitrary interactions but yielding thin, Gaussian-like tails.  
- SPGM uses sub-linear sufficient statistics to allow heavier tails while maintaining normalizability for arbitrary interactions.  
- Provides structure-learning algorithms based on neighborhood selection, making the models practically applicable.  
- Theorem 3: establishes that a quadratic base measure is necessary for normalizability with linear sufficient statistics—an important theoretical insight.  
- Literature review is incomplete and sometimes imprecise, overlooking extensive spatial statistics work.  
- Copula-based approaches are mischaracterized and under-discussed as a major alternative for multivariate count modeling.  
- Omits hierarchical/latent Gaussian (Poisson–log-Gaussian) models, a standard and powerful baseline in many applications.  
- Paper lacks empirical justification for retaining the Poisson functional form despite common overdispersion in real counts.  
- SPGM has practical drawbacks: no closed-form conditional distribution, tenuous link to Poisson, and needs PMF plots for intuition.  
- No guidance is provided for selecting critical hyperparameters (R and R0) for TPGM and SPGM.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to comparisons with Gaussian-based methods and should include latent Gaussian and other stronger baselines.  
- Overall: solid theoretical contributions and correction of prior work, but significance is reduced by incomplete related work discussion and insufficient experimental baselines—authors should broaden comparisons and practical justification.
