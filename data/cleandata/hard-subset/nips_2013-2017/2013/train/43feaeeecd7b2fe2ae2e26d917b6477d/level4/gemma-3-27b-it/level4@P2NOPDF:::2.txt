This paper addresses the important problem of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data, and correctly identifies limitations in existing approaches. While the theoretical and conceptual contributions are notable, the empirical validation is less convincing.

The paper begins with a valuable proof demonstrating the invalidity of a previously proposed truncation method. The authors then introduce several alternative truncation schemes – TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM – each with its own strengths. The TPGM truncation is intuitively justified, and the QPGM and SPGM are novel in their explicit manipulation of the base measure and sufficient statistics within an exponential representation to modify the Poisson distribution. These modifications are well-motivated and conceptually appealing. However, much of the underlying theory directly builds upon the work of Yang et al., leaving the practical merit of these model suggestions largely unevaluated.

The experimental evaluation is disappointing, especially given the paper’s stated goal of addressing practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. The synthetic experiment, while showing some promise, appears tailored to favor the proposed models. Critically, the real-data experiments omit comparison to relevant copula-based alternatives. Furthermore, the reported results are largely qualitative and lack objective evaluation; a quantitative metric such as log-probability of test data is needed for a more rigorous assessment.

A key concern is the somewhat arbitrary application of competing methods. The use of the non-paranormal copula, chosen primarily for its asymptotic properties, with a poor marginal model is questionable. A more sensible marginal distribution—even a simple Gaussian—would likely yield more meaningful results. The performance parity with Glasso is also suspect, and simple Gaussian kernel density estimation might prove superior. The choice of R=11 in the real-data analysis could similarly inform the selection of a more appropriate marginal distribution for the copula. While an exhaustive exploration is not expected, a more reasonable baseline comparison is warranted.

The paper is generally well-written, though the inclusion of all proofs in the supplementary material is recommended, given that the initial motivation stems from identifying an error in prior work. Section 3.1 contains overly detailed information, and the associated bound does not substantially strengthen the Gaussian approximation argument. Conversely, Section 3.2, which details the core methodology, deserves more thorough presentation. The illustrative figure should be moved from the supplementary material to the main body of the paper, and the underlying intuition should be more clearly explained.

In summary, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model based on a conditional exponential construction, aiming for flexible joint modeling with both positive and negative dependencies. The approach is theoretically sound and conceptually appealing. However, the limited and somewhat biased experimental evaluation prevents a clear assessment of its practical advantages, leaving it as yet another multivariate Poisson-like model with an uncertain contribution.
