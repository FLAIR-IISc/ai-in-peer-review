This paper introduces a series of novel graphical models for representing joint distributions over multivariate count data. The authors begin by highlighting a major limitation of the standard Poisson graphical model (PGM), which, due to normalizability constraints on its infinite domain, can only capture negative conditional dependencies. To overcome this, the paper proposes three solutions in a logical progression. First, it introduces a new truncation method for the Poisson distribution that leads to a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) capable of modeling both positive and negative dependencies. Second, acknowledging the limitations of a fixed truncation level, it investigates more fundamental changes. The authors prove that for a model with linear sufficient statistics to be normalizable with positive dependencies, it must have a base measure that scales at least quadratically, leading to the proposed Quadratic PGM (QPGM). Finally, to allow for heavier-tailed distributions than the Gaussian-like QPGM, the paper presents a Sub-linear PGM (SPGM) that employs sub-linear sufficient statistics. The performance of these new models is demonstrated on both synthetic data and a real-world application involving microRNA expression networks.

This is a well-written and thoughtfully constructed paper that addresses a significant gap in the literature. The problem of modeling both positive and negative dependencies in multivariate count data is important, and the proposed solutions are original and significant. The theoretical contributions, particularly the proof that the previously proposed Winsorized PGM is invalid and the derivation of the necessity of a quadratic base measure for linear statistics, are strong and provide clear motivation for the proposed models. The experimental results effectively showcase the advantages of the new models over existing methods.

While the paper is strong, the discussion of the drawbacks of the TPGM and QPGM could be more nuanced. The authors argue that the TPGM is limited because the domain is bounded and that as the truncation level R increases, the model re-introduces constraints on the parameters. However, one can imagine many domains where counts are naturally bounded or rarely exceed a certain value, making the TPGM a perfectly suitable and powerful model. Acknowledging these potential use cases would strengthen the paper. Similarly, the "Gaussian-esque thin tails" of the QPGM are presented primarily as a drawback. For certain types of data, this property might be an appropriate modeling assumption rather than an inherent weakness. A more balanced discussion of the respective trade-offs and application contexts for each model would be beneficial.

Minor suggestions for improvement:
*   The term "sparsistent" (page 5) is used in the literature but may not be familiar to the entire NIPS audience. A brief in-text definition or a specific citation would improve clarity.
*   On page 3, just before section 2.1.1: "graphical mode distributions" should be "graphical model distributions".
*   On page 5, just before section 3.1: The sentence beginning "Note that the log..." appears to be missing a conjunction. "Note that although the log..." would make grammatical sense and seems to fit the context.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents novel, well-motivated, and significant solutions for modeling a joint distribution over count variables. It is technically sound, clearly written, and represents a solid contribution to the field.
