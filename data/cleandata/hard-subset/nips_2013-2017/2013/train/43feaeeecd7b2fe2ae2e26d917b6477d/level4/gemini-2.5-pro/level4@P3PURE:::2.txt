This paper addresses the important goal of constructing multivariate distributions for count data, and the authors correctly identify several limitations of current approaches.

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. It begins by proving that a previously suggested truncation method does not lead to a valid model, then proceeds to propose several alternatives. The Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), while a straightforward extension, is warranted due to its natural interpretation. The Quadratic and Submodular Poisson Graphical Models (QPGM and SPGM), though technically simple, are novel in their approach of leveraging the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation to modify the Poisson distribution. Furthermore, these modifications are well-motivated and appealing. That said, the theory involved follows almost directly from the work of Yang et al. (2012), leaving us with several new model proposals whose merits must be evaluated empirically.

In light of this, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly since a stated goal of the paper is to address the practical limitations of existing Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some potential, the data appears specifically designed to favor the suggested models. In the real-world experiments, the absence of a comparison to copula-based alternatives is a significant omission. Moreover, the reported results are presented in a qualitatively anecdotal manner. Since all the compared methods involve constructing joint distributions, a more objective evaluation using the log-probability of test data is necessary.

More importantly, the treatment of the copula-based competitors is overly simplistic, which calls into question the demonstrated need for the proposed models. Specifically, when using a Gaussian copula, it would be more sensible to use an appropriate marginal model for count data rather than the non-paranormal transformation, which lacks a density and was chosen primarily for its asymptotic properties over practical performance. The reported performance being nearly identical to Glasso is suspicious, and I strongly suspect that even a simple baseline using Gaussian kernel density estimates for the marginals would perform much better. Similarly, in the real-data analysis, the authors' choice of R=11 for their model could have been used to inform a more suitable marginal distribution for a stronger copula-based baseline. While an in-depth exploration is not expected, a more reasonable baseline is warranted.

Finally, the paper is generally well-written. However, given that the work starts by identifying an error in a previous paper, I suggest that the authors include all proofs in the supplementary material for verification. I also found the end of Section 3.1 to be overly detailed, and the bound presented there did not seem to contribute meaningfully to the main argument. Conversely, Section 3.2, which is central to the proposed method, would benefit from a slower pace. In particular, the illustrative figure should be moved from the supplementary material into the main paper and used to better explain the model's intuition.

**Summary:**

Based on a conditional exponential construction, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model that allow for flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies. While the suggested approach is appealing and demonstrates some theoretical novelty, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased. The result is yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical merit remains unclear.
