This paper proposes a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building a multivariate distribution over unbounded counts is generally a challenging problem, extensively studied by Besag and others over the past 30 years. Notably, this problem is discussed in textbooks such as Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models" (1999), which extends some of the authors' observations to other distributions, including conditionally-specified exponential distributions.

The authors' proposal has both strengths and weaknesses. On the positive side, their constructions allow for marginal distributions over counts and lead to relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, the proposed models have some drawbacks. Specifically, the Quasi-Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) has marginally thinner tails, and the Sparse Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which somewhat defeats the purpose of building a conditionally specified model. Furthermore, it is unclear how the SPGM can be considered a Poisson distribution, as it does not seem to share the defining characteristics of a Poisson distribution. In contrast, the QPGM can at least be seen as a modification of the base measure.

This raises questions about the primary motivation for modeling counts using a Poisson distribution, given that the Poisson distribution is not typically a good fit for empirical data. While the Poisson distribution is a useful building block for many models, such as components of stochastic processes and latent variable models, it often fails to accurately capture the underlying data. For instance, a plot of breast cancer data would likely not resemble a Poisson distribution. It would be helpful to include a plot of the probability mass function of the SPGM, as its form appears somewhat convoluted at first glance.

It is assumed that R and R0 are considered constants, as otherwise, these models would not be exponential families. However, it is unclear how these constants are chosen and what advice the authors would give to practitioners in this regard.

Despite some limitations, the paper's attempt to construct a multivariate distribution over counts that obeys the independence model of a Markov random field is commendable. The results are of theoretical interest to the NIPS community. However, the literature review feels incomplete, with an overly light appreciation of existing work. For example, the authors seem to misunderstand or misrepresent the concept of a copula and its application in building multivariate distributions for discrete data, including count data. The discussion of reference [8] appears to be inaccurate, and the authors seem to overlook the vast spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been conducted for an extended period.

While the sparse precision Gaussian copula model of reference [8] does not explicitly model Markov random field-style independence constraints in the observable space, it is still a relevant approach that warrants discussion. As a practitioner, it is unclear why one would prefer the proposed model over a simpler structured Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model, which has been the standard approach for some time.

Finally, it would be helpful to include a more recent reference than [20] that supports the claim that "counts of sequencing reads... are replacing microarrays."
