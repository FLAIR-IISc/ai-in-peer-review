This paper introduces a novel construction for multivariate distributions over count data that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected graphical model. Building such distributions is a challenging problem, extensively studied in the past three decades (e.g., Arnold et al., 1999), with extensions to conditionally-specified exponential distributions. The authors successfully propose approaches that allow for count-valued marginal distributions and lead to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms. However, the Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) exhibits marginally thinner tails, and the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions, potentially diminishing the benefits of a conditionally specified model. The authors’ deviation from a standard Poisson distribution for the SPGM raises questions about its classification as a “Poisson” model; however, the change to the base measure is understandable. 

A key consideration is the motivation for modeling counts directly, given that the Poisson distribution itself often doesn’t perfectly fit empirical data. While useful as a building block in more complex models, visualizing the empirical data alongside the proposed distributions (particularly the probability mass function of the SPGM, which initially appears complex) would be beneficial. Clarification on the selection of parameters *R* and *R₀* for the SPGM, along with practical guidance for practitioners, would further enhance the paper.

The theoretical contribution is valuable. However, the literature review feels somewhat incomplete. The authors seem to understate the relevance of copula-based approaches (e.g., [8]), which offer methods for constructing multivariate distributions for discrete data – including count data – alongside established MCMC and approximate inference techniques. The discussion of [11] lacks nuance; this work pioneered log-linear models for discrete data with parameters that don't scale exponentially with the number of variables. A significant omission is the extensive literature in spatial statistics, which has long addressed high-dimensional count data analysis. While methods like the sparse precision Gaussian copula model [8] may not directly model Markov-style independence constraints in the observable space, their existence warrants more explicit acknowledgement.

Despite these points, the paper presents a valid theoretical contribution by extending the framework of graphical models to count data. However, as a practitioner, it’s unclear whether the complexity of these models justifies deviating from simpler, established approaches like a structured Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model. Further justification for choosing these models over existing alternatives would be helpful.

Finally, a more recent citation than [20] supporting the claim that sequencing read counts are replacing microarrays would strengthen the introduction.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel construction of multivariate distributions for count data with Markov properties.
*   Theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models.
*   Demonstrated performance on both simulated and real data.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in statistical modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incomplete literature review, particularly regarding copulas and spatial statistics.
*   SPGM lacks closed-form conditionals and its connection to the Poisson distribution is tenuous.
*   QPGM has thinner tails.
*   Lack of clear guidance for practitioners regarding parameter selection and model choice.
*   Insufficient justification for choosing the proposed models over existing alternatives.

**Overall assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting approach to modeling multivariate count data. Addressing the identified weaknesses in the literature review and providing more practical guidance would significantly strengthen the contribution.
