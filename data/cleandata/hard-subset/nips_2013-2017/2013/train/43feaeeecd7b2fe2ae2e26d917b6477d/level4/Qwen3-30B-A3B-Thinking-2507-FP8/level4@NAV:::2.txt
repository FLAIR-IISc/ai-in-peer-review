

The objective of developing multivariate distributions suitable for count data is significant, and the authors accurately highlight shortcomings in existing methods (a caveat is noted below).  
Theoretically, the paper offers several strengths. By establishing that prior truncation approaches yield invalid models, the authors propose viable alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while conceptually straightforward, is justified by its intuitive interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, though technically uncomplicated, introduce novelty by explicitly specifying the base measure and sufficient statistics within the exponential family framework to modify the Poisson distribution. These modifications are well-motivated and compelling. However, the underlying theory largely derives from Yang et al., leaving several proposed models whose practical utility requires empirical validation.  
Consequently, the experimental evaluation is underwhelming, especially given the paper’s stated aim to resolve practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While synthetic experiments demonstrate potential, the data is specifically tailored to the proposed models. In real-data experiments, comparisons with copula-based alternatives are absent. Moreover, reported results are qualitative and anecdotal; an objective metric such as log-probability on test data is essential for joint distribution methods.  
Critically, the evaluation of copula competitors is overly opaque. Even with a Gaussian copula, employing a sensible marginal model—rather than the non-paranormal’s choice (which lacks a density and was selected for asymptotic properties, not practicality)—would be preferable. The near-identical performance to Glasso is questionable; simple Gaussian kernel density estimates likely outperform the current implementation. Similarly, the real-data choice of R=11 for the copula marginal could be reasonably justified, yet this is unexplored. A minimal, sensible baseline is warranted.  
The paper is well-written. While all results appear valid—particularly given the authors’ correction of an error in prior work—I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material. Section 3.1 concludes with excessive detail that does not advance the Gaussian-esque argument; the bound should be omitted. Conversely, Section 3.2, central to the proposed method, merits slower pacing, with the key figure presented in the main text and its intuition clarified.  
In summary, the conditional exponential construction yields appealing alternatives to Poisson graphical models for flexible joint modeling with mixed dependencies. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and biased, leaving the practical merit of this new model unclear.
