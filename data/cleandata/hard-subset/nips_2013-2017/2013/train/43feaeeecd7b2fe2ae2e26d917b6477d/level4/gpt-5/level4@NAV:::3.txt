The paper introduces a way to construct multivariate distributions over unbounded count data that respect the Markov structure of an undirected graph.

Forming multivariate distributions over unbounded counts is generally challenging, a topic explored extensively by Besag and others over the past three decades (see also Arnold et al.’s “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models,” 1999, which broadens some of the authors’ points to other families, such as conditionally specified exponential distributions).

Overall, the authors’ proposal succeeds in several meaningful respects. On the positive side, the constructions yield valid marginal distributions over counts and enable fairly straightforward estimation procedures. On the negative side, QPGM has somewhat thinner tails, and SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions (which arguably undermines the purpose of a conditionally specified model). In fact, I am not sure in what sense SPGM should be regarded as Poisson (for QPGM one could at least argue that “only” the base measure is modified). That is acceptable, but it raises the question of the central motivation for modeling counts here, given that the Poisson is often a poor fit to empirical data on its own (to be clear, the Poisson is very useful as a component in stochastic processes and latent variable models, etc.). Could you plot the breast cancer data to show whether it resembles a Poisson? It would also help to include a plot of the SPGM probability mass function, which appears somewhat involved at first glance. I assume R and R0 are treated as constants (otherwise the models would not be exponential families). How are these selected in practice? What guidance can you offer practitioners?

That said, constructing a multivariate count distribution that satisfies the MRF independence structure is a hard problem. I genuinely value the effort in this paper and view the results as theoretically interesting for NIPS. My main concern is the rather light engagement with prior work. For example, it reads as if the authors are not fully comfortable with the concept of a copula. The treatment of [8] seems off (or at least misstated): the central point there is to build multivariate models for arbitrary discrete data (including counts), for which a suite of MCMC and approximate inference techniques is available. This also makes me question whether the discussion of [11] is appropriate, since that seminal book is about constructing discrete models with log-linear parameters in a way that avoids exponential growth in complexity with the number of variables (though I do not have a copy on hand and cannot recall its specific treatment of Poisson models). Perhaps most notably, the paper overlooks a substantial spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data have been studied for a long time.

It should be noted that several of these alternatives (including the sparse-precision Gaussian copula of [8]) do not impose MRF-style independence constraints in the observed space. Thus, the paper’s theoretical contribution remains valid. However, from a practitioner’s perspective, I am not yet persuaded to accept the cost of restricting to this model class rather than using the simpler structured Gaussian random field plus Poisson measurement model, which has been a longstanding standard.

Final comment: I am not a specialist in gene expression, but a reference more recent than [20] supporting the claim that “counts of sequencing reads … are replacing microarrays” would be appreciated.

A method for constructing multivariate distributions for counts that is Markov with respect to undirected graphs. Like any nontrivial multivariate construction, it has its advantages and limitations. The literature coverage appears incomplete.
