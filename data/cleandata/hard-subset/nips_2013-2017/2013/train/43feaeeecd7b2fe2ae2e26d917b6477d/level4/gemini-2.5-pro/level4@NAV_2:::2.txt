This paper addresses a significant limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which, due to normalizability constraints on an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. The authors propose to develop graphical models for count data capable of capturing richer dependence structures, including both positive and negative relationships. The work begins by demonstrating that a previously proposed "Winsorized" truncation approach by Kaiser and Cressie [20] does not yield a valid joint distribution. The authors then introduce three novel models based on the construction of Yang et al. [18, 19]: (1) the Truncated PGM (TPGM), which uses a valid truncation scheme on a finite domain; (2) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which modifies the base measure of the Poisson distribution to be quadratic; and (3) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear. These modifications are designed to ensure the distribution remains normalizable without restricting the sign of the edge parameters.

**Strengths**

*   **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles an important and well-recognized problem: extending graphical models to effectively handle multivariate count data with mixed (positive and negative) dependencies. This is a highly relevant area with applications in genomics, social sciences, and beyond.
*   **Novelty and Contributions:** The paper makes several valuable contributions. The formal proof that the Winsorized PGM construction is invalid (Theorem 1) is a useful clarification for the field. The core proposals, particularly the QPGM and SPGM, are conceptually novel. Modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation is a creative and well-motivated strategy to overcome the limitations of the standard PGM.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical development of the proposed models appears sound, though it largely follows the established framework of Yang et al. [18, 19]. The motivation for each model is clearly explained in relation to the normalizability problem.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper is the empirical validation, which is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the practical superiority of the proposed models. The paper's stated goal is to fix a practical limitation, but the experiments do not provide sufficient evidence of this.
*   **Biased Simulations:** The synthetic experiments generate data from either the TPGM distribution or a related sum-of-Poissons model. While this shows the methods can recover their own generating structures, it does not demonstrate how they perform on more general count data distributions where the model may be misspecified.
*   **Insufficient Baselines:** The comparison to competing methods in the real-data analysis is lacking. A glaring omission is a robust comparison with copula-based approaches, such as the nonparanormal [7, 10]. The authors' use of these methods as baselines seems superficial; for example, a Gaussian copula with more appropriate marginals (e.g., estimated via kernel density) would likely provide a much stronger baseline than the default nonparanormal transformation, whose performance is suspiciously similar to the standard Gaussian graphical model.
*   **Lack of Quantitative Assessment:** The evaluation relies on ROC curves for simulations and qualitative inspection of the learned networks for the real data. A more objective metric, such as the log-likelihood on held-out test data, is necessary to quantitatively assess how well these models capture the underlying data distribution. Without such an evaluation, it is difficult to determine whether the proposed models offer a tangible improvement over existing, potentially simpler, methods.

**Clarity and Detailed Comments**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, there are several areas that could be improved:

*   Given that the paper starts by pointing out an error in previous work, it is essential that the authors include the full proofs for all their theorems in the supplementary material for verification.
*   The discussion at the end of Section 3.1 regarding the Gaussian variational upper bound feels overly detailed and does not add much to the core argument that the QPGM is "Gaussian-esque". This part could be condensed.
*   Conversely, Section 3.2, which introduces the SPGM (arguably the most novel contribution), feels rushed. The intuition behind the sub-linear sufficient statistic is critical, and the paper would benefit greatly from moving its corresponding figure from the appendix to the main text and providing a more thorough explanation.

**Summary of Arguments**

**For Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-known and important limitation in a popular class of statistical models.
*   Provides a valuable negative result by formally disproving a previously suggested approach (Winsorized PGM).
*   Introduces three novel and conceptually appealing models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that allow for both positive and negative dependencies in count data. The underlying idea of modifying the exponential family components is elegant.

**Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is not strong enough to support the claims of practical utility. The validation feels preliminary and somewhat biased.
*   The comparison against state-of-the-art baselines (especially copula-based models) is insufficient and not thoroughly performed.
*   The lack of a quantitative evaluation on test data makes it impossible to judge whether the proposed models offer a meaningful improvement in practice. The paper presents several new models without providing clear evidence for when and why they should be preferred.
