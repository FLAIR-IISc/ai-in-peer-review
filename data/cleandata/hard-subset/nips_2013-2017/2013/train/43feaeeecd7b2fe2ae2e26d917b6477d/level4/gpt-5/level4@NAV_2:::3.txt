The paper develops multivariate distributions over unbounded count variables that respect the conditional independence structure of an undirected graphical model. Constructing such models is notoriously difficult, as explored extensively since Besag’s work and in broader treatments like Arnold, Castillo, and Sarabia’s “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models” (1999), which generalizes some of the authors’ observations to other conditionally specified families (e.g., conditionally exponential). Within this challenging landscape, the authors’ proposals succeed in notable ways: they yield joint distributions with count marginals and lead to relatively straightforward estimation procedures. On the other hand, QPGM induces noticeably lighter tails, and SPGM lacks closed-form node-conditionals, which somewhat undermines the rationale for a conditionally specified construction. It is also unclear in what sense SPGM remains “Poisson” (for QPGM one can at least argue the sufficient statistic is linear and the base measure is altered). This prompts a broader question about the motivation for insisting on Poisson-like forms for observed counts, given that Poisson often fails to fit empirical count data directly (though, of course, Poisson components are extremely useful within stochastic-process or latent-variable constructions). It would help to see plots comparing the breast cancer count data to a Poisson fit, and also a plot of the SPGM probability mass function, which is not immediately transparent. I assume R and R0 are treated as fixed constants (otherwise the conditional families would not be exponential); how are these set in practice, and what guidance can you give practitioners?

All that said: building multivariate count distributions that obey MRF conditional independences is hard. I appreciate the effort here and view the results as of theoretical interest for NIPS. The aspect that gives me pause is the relatively light treatment of existing literature. For example, the discussion of copula-based methods suggests a misunderstanding; [8] explicitly targets multivariate modeling for arbitrary discrete data (including counts), with a range of MCMC and approximate inference methods. This also raises doubts about the presentation of [11]: the thrust of that classic work is precisely how to specify discrete graphical models with log-linear parameters without naive exponential blow-up in the number of variables (though I do not have the book at hand to check what it says specifically about Poisson). Perhaps the most significant omission is the extensive spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data have long been modeled and analyzed. To be fair, many of these approaches (including sparse-precision Gaussian copula models like [8]) do not enforce MRF-style independences in the observed space. Thus, the theoretical contribution of this paper—MRF-consistent joint models over counts—stands. But from a practitioner’s standpoint, it remains unclear why one should accept the constraints of this model class instead of using a simpler Gaussian random field with a Poisson observation model, which has been a workhorse for years.

Final remark: I am not a domain expert in gene expression, but it would be helpful to cite a more recent source than [20] to support the claim that “counts of sequencing reads … are replacing microarrays.”

A framework for constructing multivariate count distributions that are Markov with respect to undirected graphs; as with any nontrivial multivariate construction, it offers clear benefits and notable limitations. The literature coverage feels incomplete.
