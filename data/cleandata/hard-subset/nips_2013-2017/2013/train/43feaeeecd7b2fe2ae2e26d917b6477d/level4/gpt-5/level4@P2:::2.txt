This paper addresses the challenge of modeling multivariate count data, where standard undirected graphical models (Gaussian, Ising, multinomial) are ill-suited and existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) permit only negative conditional dependencies. Building on Besag and the node-conditional exponential family framework of Yang et al., the authors (i) prove that “Winsorized” Poisson truncation cannot yield a consistent joint model, (ii) propose a truncated PGM (TPGM) that remains in the exponential family and thus admits a valid joint distribution without sign constraints on edges, and (iii) introduce two Poisson-like variants: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that replaces the base measure with a quadratic term to allow positive and negative dependencies, and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that modifies the sufficient statistics to achieve heavier tails and broader feasibility conditions. They suggest neighborhood selection for structure learning and present simulations and a miRNA sequencing case study.

Relation to prior work: The paper follows the conditional-to-joint construction for exponential families developed by Besag and generalized by Yang et al. Its main theoretical contributions are (a) the impossibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals and (b) the insight that, with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability under mixed-sign edges requires at least a quadratic base measure. The proposed QPGM and SPGM instantiate these principles.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: enabling positive and negative conditional dependencies among counts is practically important.
- The impossibility proof for the Winsorized approach clarifies a misinterpretation in prior work.
- The TPGM is a natural, tractable truncation that preserves the exponential-family form and eliminates normalizability constraints.
- QPGM and SPGM are simple, well-motivated modifications that broaden the feasible dependency structures. The normalizability conditions are transparent, and the variational arguments for nodewise learning are reasonable.
- The framework is compatible with standard neighborhood selection, promising scalability.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Theoretical novelty is incremental: much of the construction follows directly from Yang et al.; the main additions are the Winsorization impossibility result and the base-measure/sufficient-statistic tradeoff. The ultimate value therefore hinges on empirical validation.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and, at times, biased toward the proposed models. The synthetic data appear aligned with TPGM/SPGM assumptions, and the real-data results are largely qualitative.
- Comparisons to copula-based methods are missing. Given their prominence for multivariate count modeling, the paper should include at least a Gaussian copula with sensible marginals (e.g., Poisson-lognormal, negative binomial, or semiparametric kernel marginals), rather than relying on the nonparanormal transform, which lacks a proper density and was chosen for asymptotic properties. The near-identical performance to the graphical lasso is concerning; more appropriate marginals or simple kernel estimates would likely improve these baselines.
- Evaluation metrics should include objective held-out log-likelihood or pseudo-likelihood, beyond ROC curves, to assess the quality of the joint distributions.
- For the miRNA study, choices like R = 11 (and R0 for SPGM) should be justified via sensitivity analysis or data-driven selection; similar choices could be mirrored in copula marginals for a fair comparison.
- Complexity and scalability: provide timing, convergence behavior of the proximal-gradient implementations, and sample-size requirements across TPGM/QPGM/SPGM.
- Presentation: include all proofs in the supplementary material for verification, given the correction of prior work. Section 3.1 could be condensed; the variational bound adds little to the Gaussian-esque intuition. Section 3.2 deserves more space, with the sublinear sufficient statistic figure in the main text and a clearer explanation of its intuition and parameter effects.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a real gap: modeling mixed-sign dependencies in count graphical models.
- Clear, simple constructions with sound normalizability conditions.
- Corrects an error in the literature and articulates a useful design principle (sufficient statistics vs. base measure).

Cons for acceptance:
- Empirical evidence is not yet compelling: missing key baselines, objective likelihood-based evaluation, and sensitivity analyses.
- Novelty over established conditional-exponential constructions is modest.

Overall assessment: The paper presents appealing and conceptually clean models that likely will be of interest to practitioners working with count data. However, the current experimental evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate practical superiority over strong, properly configured alternatives. Strengthening the empirical section with appropriate copula baselines (with sensible marginals), held-out likelihood, sensitivity analyses for R and R0, and computational benchmarks would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
