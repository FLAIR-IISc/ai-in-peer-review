This paper presents a novel method for constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov properties of an undirected graphical model. The problem of defining such distributions is known to be challenging, as explored extensively in the literature on conditionally specified models over the last three decades (e.g., Besag, and Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models", 1999).

The authors' proposed construction is successful in several important aspects. The primary strengths are the development of models that possess valid marginal distributions over counts and that lead to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms. These are non-trivial achievements in this domain.

However, the proposed models also have significant drawbacks. The Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) is characterized by marginally thinner tails than a standard Poisson, which may limit its applicability to overdispersed data common in practice. More problematically, the Submodular Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions. This limitation seems to undermine the primary motivation for adopting a conditional specification framework in the first place, which is typically prized for its computational convenience. Furthermore, the nomenclature for SPGM is questionable; it is not immediately clear why it should be classified as a Poisson model, given its construction (unlike QPGM, where the deviation can be seen as a change in the base measure).

These limitations raise broader questions about the practical motivation. The Poisson distribution, while a fundamental building block in many statistical models (e.g., as a component of stochastic processes or in latent variable models), is often a poor fit for empirical count data on its own due to its fixed mean-variance relationship. It would be highly beneficial for the authors to provide empirical evidence, such as a histogram of their breast cancer dataset, to justify whether the marginals of their model are a plausible fit for the data. Additionally, a plot of the probability mass function for the SPGM would be very helpful for readers to build intuition about this rather complex distribution.

Several practical details also require clarification. The paper should explain how the parameters R and R0 are to be chosen in practice, as they are presumably treated as fixed constants to maintain the exponential family structure. Clear guidance for practitioners on setting these parameters is essential.

While the theoretical effort in constructing a multivariate count distribution that respects the independence structure of a Markov Random Field is commendable and of interest to the NIPS community, the paper's engagement with existing literature is a major weakness. The discussion of related work feels superficial and, in some cases, incorrect. For instance, the treatment of copulas suggests a misunderstanding of their purpose. The description of [8] is particularly misleading; that work's central contribution is a general framework for building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data (including counts), for which a variety of inference methods exist. The discussion of the pioneering work in [11] also seems questionable, as that text details methods for building log-linear models without the number of parameters growing exponentially.

Perhaps the most significant omission is the complete neglect of the vast literature in spatial statistics, where the analysis of high-dimensional count data is a well-established field. While it is true that many of these alternative approaches (including the Gaussian copula model of [8]) do not explicitly enforce MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space, the authors' theoretical contribution would be much stronger if contextualized against them. As it stands, a practitioner is left unconvinced as to why they should adopt the proposed models over a standard and flexible alternative, such as a structured Gaussian random field with a Poisson observation model.

Finally, on a minor note, the claim that "counts of sequencing reads ... are replacing microarrays" would be strengthened by a reference more recent than [20], which is now over a decade old.

In summary, the paper makes a valid theoretical contribution to a difficult problem. However, its practical utility is not yet clearly demonstrated, and it suffers from a seriously incomplete review of highly relevant literature. The authors should be strongly encouraged to situate their work within the broader context of multivariate discrete data modeling and to provide a more compelling argument for why their specific construction is preferable to existing, well-understood alternatives.
