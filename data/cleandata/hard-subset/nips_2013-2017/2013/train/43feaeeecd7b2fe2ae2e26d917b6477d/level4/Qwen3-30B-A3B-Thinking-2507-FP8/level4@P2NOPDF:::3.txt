

The paper proposes a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure of undirected networks. While building such distributions is notoriously challenging—highlighted in foundational work by Besag and extended in Arnold et al.'s 1999 text—the authors present two models: QPGM and SPGM. QPGM offers tractable marginal distributions and efficient estimation, though its marginal tails are thinner than standard Poisson. SPGM, however, lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining the core premise of conditionally specified models; it cannot reasonably be termed a "Poisson distribution" (unlike QPGM, where only the base measure differs). This raises questions about the practical motivation for modeling counts, particularly since Poisson distributions rarely fit empirical count data well (e.g., breast cancer read counts). The authors should provide a probability mass function plot for SPGM and clarify whether parameters like *R* and *R*₀ are constants (required for exponential family structure) and how practitioners should select them.  

The theoretical contribution—constructing multivariate count distributions obeying Markov constraints—is valid and of interest to NIPS. However, the paper’s literature review is notably incomplete. The treatment of copula-based methods (e.g., [8]) is superficial; the cited work explicitly addresses multivariate discrete data (including counts) with established inference techniques, yet the paper misrepresents its purpose. Similarly, the discussion of [11] appears confused: that book pioneered log-linear parameterizations for discrete models without exponential growth in variables, yet the paper fails to engage with its relevance to Poisson-like constructions. Most critically, the paper neglects spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count analysis has been extensively studied for decades. While methods like the sparse precision Gaussian copula ([8]) do not model observable-space Markov constraints, the omission of this field weakens the paper’s justification.  

As a practitioner, I remain unconvinced why this model space merits adoption over the established structured Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model, which is simpler and well-validated for count data. The paper does not demonstrate clear advantages in flexibility, accuracy, or scalability that justify abandoning this standard approach.  

Minor concerns include the outdated reference [20] for claims about sequencing counts replacing microarrays in gene expression analysis; a more recent citation is needed. The concluding summary—“a method for constructing multivariate distributions for counts that is Markov with respect to undirected graphs... literature review feels incomplete”—accurately captures the work’s strengths and weaknesses.  

In summary, the paper makes a plausible theoretical contribution but falters in contextualizing its work within existing literature and justifying its practical utility. The SPGM’s lack of closed-form conditionals and the superficial engagement with copulas and spatial statistics are significant drawbacks. While the core idea is sound, the paper would benefit from a more rigorous literature review, empirical validation of model fit, and a clearer argument for why this framework supersedes simpler, well-established alternatives.
