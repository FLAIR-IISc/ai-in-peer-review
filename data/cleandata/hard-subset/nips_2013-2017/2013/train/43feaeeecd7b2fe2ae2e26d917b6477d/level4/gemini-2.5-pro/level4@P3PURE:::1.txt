This paper describes several new models for representing a joint distribution over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the default model arising from Yang et al. is unsatisfactory, as it can only model negative correlations for the distribution to be normalizable. They then propose and analyze a series of solutions to this limitation, including a new truncation method, the use of a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove is necessary under certain conditions), and finally, a model based on a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors validate their contributions with experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data.

This is a well-written paper presenting novel and significant solutions for modeling count data. The contributions appear to be original and the results are compelling.

My main criticism is that I found the arguments regarding the "big negatives" of the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unconvincing. For the truncation-based model, one can imagine many interesting domains where counts are naturally bounded or do not typically reach high values, making this approach quite suitable. Similarly, for the quadratic model, the authors criticize its "Gaussian-esque thin tails," but such tail behavior could be appropriate for certain types of count data.
