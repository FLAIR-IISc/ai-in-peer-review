This paper addresses the important problem of constructing flexible undirected graphical models for multivariate count data. The authors correctly note that standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) are limited to modeling only negative conditional dependencies, a significant drawback for many real-world applications. The paper's main goal is to develop PGM variants that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.

The authors begin by making a valuable theoretical contribution: they prove that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson truncation method does not, in fact, lead to a valid joint distribution. Building on this, they propose three novel models. The first, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), uses a different truncation scheme that does result in a valid MRF. While a natural approach, the TPGM has its own limitations related to the choice of the truncation level. The other two proposals, the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), represent more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. These models are novel in that they alter the base measure and sufficient statistics of the underlying exponential family representation to ensure normalizability while allowing for a rich dependency structure. The motivation for these modifications is clear and compelling.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The development of graphical models suitable for high-dimensional count data that can capture mixed dependency structures is a well-motivated and important research direction.
2.  **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides a key negative result by invalidating a previously proposed method. Furthermore, the core idea of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics (leading to QPGM and SPGM) is a novel and principled way to overcome the limitations of the standard PGM. The proposed models are technically sound and their derivations follow clearly from the framework of Yang et al.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivations for each proposed model are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which is not thorough enough to convincingly establish the practical advantages of the proposed models over existing alternatives.

1.  **Limited Baselines:** The experiments are missing a crucial comparison against modern copula-based methods, which are a standard approach for modeling non-Gaussian data. The non-paranormal methods that are included are applied in a "black-box" fashion. The non-paranormal's transformation, chosen for its asymptotic properties, often has suboptimal practical performance. It is suspicious that its performance is nearly identical to that of the standard Gaussian graphical model (Glasso). A stronger baseline could be formed using a Gaussian copula with more suitable marginals, such as those derived from Gaussian kernel density estimates.
2.  **Lack of Objective Evaluation:** The evaluation of the real-data experiment is anecdotal and qualitative. Since all competing methods are generative models that define a joint distribution, a more objective and quantitative comparison using the log-likelihood of held-out test data is necessary. This would provide a much clearer picture of how well each model fits the data.
3.  **Biased Synthetic Experiments:** The synthetic data is generated from models (TPGM and sums-of-Poissons) that are closely related to the proposed methods. This setup favors the new models and does not demonstrate their robustness or performance on data generated from different underlying processes.
4.  **Incremental Theory:** While the model proposals are novel, the underlying theory for constructing the joint distribution from conditionals follows directly from the work of Yang et al. Therefore, the paper's main contribution is the introduction of several new model classes, whose practical utility must be rigorously demonstrated. As it stands, the paper introduces more potential models without a clear verdict on their merit.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Strengthen Experiments:** The experimental section requires significant expansion. I strongly recommend including a comparison against a well-chosen Gaussian copula baseline. For the microRNA data, the truncation level R=11 chosen for TPGM could inform the domain for a discrete marginal distribution in the copula model, enabling a fairer comparison. Most importantly, please report test-set log-likelihoods for all models on the real-world dataset.
*   **Improve Presentation:** The paper is mostly clear, but some sections could be improved. The end of Section 3.1 feels overly detailed, and the variational bound does not seem essential for making the "Gaussian-esque" argument. Conversely, Section 3.2 is central to the paper's contribution and feels rushed. The figure of the sub-linear sufficient statistic should be moved from the appendix to the main text and used to provide a more intuitive explanation of the SPGM.
*   **Include Proofs:** Given that the paper begins by identifying an error in a previous work, it is especially important to provide full proofs for all theorems and propositions in the supplementary material to ensure rigor and reproducibility.

**Summary:**

This paper presents a theoretically appealing and novel approach for extending Poisson graphical models to handle both positive and negative dependencies. The identification of an error in prior work and the introduction of the QPGM and SPGM models are definite strengths. However, the experimental evaluation is preliminary and not comprehensive enough to demonstrate the practical value of these new models over existing, powerful alternatives like copula-based approaches. The work shows promise, but its significance is currently limited by the lack of a rigorous empirical comparison.
