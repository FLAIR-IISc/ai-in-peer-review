Summary
This paper proposes new undirected graphical models for multivariate count data that overcome the well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs), namely the requirement of non-positive pairwise parameters for normalizability. The authors first show that a previously proposed “Winsorized” truncation of Poisson node-conditionals cannot yield a valid joint MRF (Theorem 1). They then propose a different truncation that preserves the exponential-family form, leading to the Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), which admits both positive and negative dependencies (Theorem 2). To address drawbacks of truncation, they introduce two Poisson-like families: (i) the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which keeps linear sufficient statistics but replaces the Poisson base measure by a quadratic term, enabling positive and negative edges under negative-definiteness-like conditions (Proposition 2); and (ii) the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which retains the Poisson base measure but uses sublinear sufficient statistics, yielding normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). Graph structures are learned via ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection. Simulations (hub and scale-free graphs) and a microRNA sequencing case study illustrate empirical performance, with TPGM and SPGM outperforming Gaussian-based baselines on count data and recovering plausible biological structure.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Besag’s auto-models and recent exponential-family graphical model constructions that derive joint MRFs from univariate exponential families. It revisits truncation ideas from spatial statistics and contrasts with multivariate Poisson constructions based on sums of independent Poissons, which only allow positive correlations. The work is positioned against Gaussian and copula-based graph learning methods, which are commonly used for continuous or transformed data.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: clear identification of why standard PGMs enforce negative interactions, with constructive alternatives (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that permit both positive and negative conditional dependencies.
- Theoretical contributions: non-existence of a joint model for Winsorized node-conditionals; existence of a valid joint for the proposed exponential-family truncation; necessity of quadratic scaling in the base measure for linear sufficient statistics; normalizability results and variational bounds.
- Practical relevance: neighborhood selection strategy is compatible with standard high-dimensional workflows; empirical results show gains over Gaussian-based baselines on synthetic count networks and a miRNA dataset.
- Clarity of the core ideas: the factorization arguments and the role of base measures and sufficient statistics are well motivated.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- SPGM tractability: The node-conditional SPGM lacks a closed-form partition function, which undercuts a main appeal of conditionally specified models (simple node-wise likelihoods). The paper uses variational upper bounds; please detail the optimization procedure, approximation quality, and its impact on estimation and edge recovery (e.g., calibration of penalties, convergence diagnostics).
- Tail behavior and modeling intent: QPGM’s quadratic base measure yields thin tails; SPGM aims for heavier tails via sublinear statistics. Please (i) plot and compare univariate pmfs for SPGM/QPGM versus Poisson across representative parameter settings, and (ii) show marginal histograms for the breast cancer counts to motivate tail choices empirically.
- Hyperparameters R and R0: Clarify whether these are global or node-specific; how they are chosen in practice (cross-validation, prior knowledge, data-driven rules); and sensitivity analyses. Without guidance, SPGM/TPGM may be hard to apply.
- Terminology: SPGM departs from Poisson in its sufficient statistics; “Poisson-like” may be clearer than “Poisson,” and the paper should articulate precisely what “Poisson” properties are retained (e.g., base measure).
- Literature coverage: The discussion of copula-based methods (e.g., [8]) is cursory and partially misleading. Gaussian copula models admit discrete margins (including counts) via appropriate link functions and have MCMC/approximate inference toolkits; while they do not generally enforce MRF constraints on observables, they are viable multivariate models for counts. Likewise, the treatment of the log-linear literature (e.g., hierarchical log-linear models) suggests parameter explosion, but modern formulations avoid full exponential scaling via sparsity and hierarchical constraints. Finally, the spatial statistics literature contains extensive work on high-dimensional count data via latent Gaussian random fields with Poisson measurements; these serve as strong practical baselines even if their conditional independences live in the latent space. A more balanced comparison—conceptually and empirically—would strengthen the paper.
- Empirical evaluation: Please include comparisons against latent Gaussian models with Poisson observation (e.g., GLMM/INLA/MCMC implementations) and discrete copula-based approaches, alongside Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines. Report computational cost and scalability for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM. Provide guidance on stability selection settings and their effect on sparsity and recovery.
- Claims about RNA-seq vs microarrays: The statement that RNA-seq counts are replacing microarrays is typically supported by older references such as [22]; a more recent citation would be appropriate.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The theoretical results are interesting and appear sound; however, practical estimation for SPGM relies on approximations that need more detail and validation. Sparsistency is deferred; even a sketch or empirical proxy would help.
- Clarity: The main constructions are clearly presented. The literature review needs revision for completeness and accuracy, and practical guidance (choice of R, R0; algorithmic details) should be expanded.
- Originality: The framework that diagnoses and remedies the negativity constraint via base-measure/sufficient-statistic design is novel and insightful.
- Significance: The work advances modeling of multivariate counts under MRF structure and may stimulate further research. Practical impact will hinge on clearer guidance for hyperparameter selection, tractable inference for SPGM, and stronger comparisons to established latent-variable approaches.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a long-standing limitation of Poisson MRFs with constructive, theoretically grounded alternatives.
- Demonstrates improved recovery on synthetic and real count data.
- Offers a unifying perspective on how base measures and sufficient statistics govern normalizability and dependence.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical comparison to leading practical alternatives (latent Gaussian + Poisson measurement; copula-based discrete models).
- Incomplete/inaccurate literature discussion.
- SPGM inference and hyperparameter selection lack clear, reproducible guidance.

Minor comments
- Specify whether parameter constraints in QPGM are enforced during estimation (e.g., negative definiteness) and how.
- Report runtime, iterations, and convergence criteria for node-wise optimization.
- Consider releasing code and scripts to facilitate replication.

Overall assessment
A theoretically interesting and potentially impactful contribution to graphical modeling for counts. With a more accurate literature review, additional practical guidance (especially for SPGM), and stronger empirical comparisons to latent Gaussian and copula-based models, this would be a compelling NIPS paper.
