

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical models for count data. The authors build upon Yang et al.'s Poisson graphical model (PGM), which, while natural for count data, is limited to modeling only negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors propose three novel approaches to overcome this limitation: (1) a Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) that truncates the domain to {0, 1, ..., R}, (2) a Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) with quadratic base measure, and (3) a Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) with sub-linear sufficient statistics. These models collectively enable the representation of both positive and negative conditional dependencies in count-valued variables, which is crucial for applications like genomic sequencing data where both types of dependencies exist.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of graphical models by resolving a fundamental limitation in existing approaches. The theoretical development is rigorous, with the authors proving necessary conditions for normalizability (Theorem 3) and demonstrating the validity of their constructions (Theorems 1-4). Their analysis of the trade-offs between the three approaches—particularly the bounded domain limitation of TPGM, the Gaussian-esque thin tail of QPGM, and the heavier tails of SPGM—provides valuable insights for practitioners. The experimental results on both synthetic data and a breast cancer microRNA-sequencing dataset demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed models over existing methods, including Gaussian graphical models and the original Poisson graphical model.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the connection between the theoretical development and practical applications is well-maintained. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the NIPS literature, citing relevant prior work such as Yang et al. (2013) and the non-paranormal approach. The experimental section provides thorough comparisons with existing methods, including the original Poisson graphical model, Gaussian graphical models, and non-paranormal approaches. The results on the microRNA dataset are particularly compelling, showing that the proposed models identify biologically relevant hub miRNAs (such as mir-375 and mir-10b) that were missed by Gaussian models.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and convincing experimental results. The authors carefully address potential limitations of each model, demonstrating honesty about their trade-offs. The clarity is excellent, with the paper providing sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The originality is strong, as the three proposed models represent a significant departure from previous work on graphical models for count data, addressing a clear gap that has persisted since the initial PGM work.

The significance of this work is substantial. Count data is increasingly prevalent in big data applications, and the inability to model both positive and negative dependencies has been a major limitation in existing approaches. The authors demonstrate practical utility through their analysis of microRNA sequencing data, where their models identify biologically meaningful network structures that other methods miss. The paper's contributions are directly applicable to practitioners working with count data in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains.

Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical development with necessary and sufficient conditions for normalizability
- Three distinct, well-motivated approaches to overcome the negative dependency limitation
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world data
- Clear demonstration of practical utility through breast cancer microRNA network analysis
- Careful discussion of trade-offs between the proposed models

Weaknesses:
- The paper could more thoroughly address the practical implications of the truncation parameter R in TPGM, particularly regarding how to select R in real-world applications
- While the authors mention that QPGM has "Gaussian-esque thin tails," they could provide more specific guidance on when this property might be desirable versus when it might be problematic
- The connection between the theoretical analysis and computational efficiency could be expanded, particularly regarding the complexity of learning these models
- The term "sparsistent" is used without definition, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with this specific terminology

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS, addressing an important gap in modeling count data with graphical models. The contributions are both theoretically sound and practically significant, with clear applications to emerging big data challenges in genomics and other domains. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the field by resolving a fundamental limitation of previous work.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, novel approaches, significant experimental validation, clear writing, and important practical applications.
Con: Some limitations of the models could be more thoroughly discussed, and the practical implementation details could be expanded.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models. It is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The authors have demonstrated the significance of their work through compelling experimental results, particularly on the breast cancer microRNA dataset where their models identify biologically relevant network structures that other methods miss. I recommend acceptance.
