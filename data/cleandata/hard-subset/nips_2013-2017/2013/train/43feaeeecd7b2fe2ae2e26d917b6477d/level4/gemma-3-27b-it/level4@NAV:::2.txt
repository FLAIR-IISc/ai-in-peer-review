Addressing an important problem – the construction of multivariate distributions suitable for count data – the authors appropriately identify shortcomings in existing methods, though a minor reservation exists.

The paper demonstrates several theoretical and conceptual strengths. Beginning with a proof disconfirming previously proposed truncation validity, the authors introduce a number of alternative approaches. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its clear interpretability. The QPGM and SPGM models, though technically simple, are novel in their presentation of the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation as a means to modify the Poisson distribution. These modifications are well-motivated and intuitively appealing. However, the underlying theory largely follows from the work of Yang et al., resulting in a set of proposed models that require practical validation to demonstrate their value.

The experimental evaluation, however, is underwhelming, particularly given the paper's stated aim of addressing practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment suggests some promise, the data appears tailored to favor the proposed models. The real-data experiments notably lack comparison to copula-based alternatives, and the presented results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. A more objective evaluation metric, such as log-probability of test data, is necessary given the focus on joint distribution construction.

Critically, the copula-based competitors are employed in an overly opaque manner, raising questions about the necessity of the new models. Specifically, even a simple Gaussian copula benefits from a more thoughtfully chosen marginal distribution than the one utilized in the non-paranormal model (which lacks a density function), a choice primarily driven by asymptotic properties rather than practical advantages. The near-identical performance compared to Glasso is concerning, and it is likely that even basic Gaussian kernel density estimation would yield superior results. Similarly, the application of R=11 to the real data could reasonably inform the choice of the copula's marginal distribution. While an exhaustive exploration is not expected, a more sensible baseline comparison is warranted.

Overall, the paper is well-written. While the results appear valid – particularly considering the correction of an error in prior work – moving the proofs to the supplementary material is recommended. The level of detail at the end of section 3.1, specifically the bound presented, does not significantly enhance the Gaussian-esque argument. Conversely, section 3.2, which represents the core of the proposed methodology, deserves more deliberate attention; the figure should be included in the main text, and its underlying intuition should be more thoroughly explained.

In summary, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model based on a conditional exponential construction, aiming for flexible joint modeling capable of capturing both positive and negative dependencies. The approach is appealing and exhibits some theoretical innovation. However, the limited and somewhat biased experimental evaluation leaves the practical merit of this latest multivariate Poisson-like model uncertain.
