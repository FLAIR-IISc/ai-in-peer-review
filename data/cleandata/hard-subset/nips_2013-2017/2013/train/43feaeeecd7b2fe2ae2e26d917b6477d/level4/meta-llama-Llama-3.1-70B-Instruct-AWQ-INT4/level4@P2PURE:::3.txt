The paper proposes a method for constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected network. Constructing such distributions is generally a challenging problem, extensively studied by Besag and others over the past 30 years, including in textbooks such as Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models" (1999), which extends some of the authors' observations to other distributions, including conditionally-specified exponential distributions.

The authors' proposal has both advantages and disadvantages. On the positive side, their constructions allow for marginal distributions over counts and lead to relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, the proposed models have limitations. Specifically, the QPGM has marginally thinner tails, and the SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which somewhat defeats the purpose of building a conditionally specified model. Furthermore, it is unclear how the SPGM can be considered a Poisson distribution, as it does not seem to share the characteristic properties of the Poisson distribution (although, for QPGM, one could argue that only the base measure is being modified).

This raises questions about the main motivation for modeling counts, as the Poisson distribution itself is not typically a good fit for empirical data. While the Poisson distribution is a useful building block for many models, such as components of stochastic processes and latent variable models, it is not clear whether it accurately represents the underlying data. For instance, a plot of the breast cancer data would likely not resemble a Poisson distribution. It would be helpful to include a plot of the probability mass function of the SPGM, which appears somewhat convoluted at first glance. Additionally, it is assumed that R and R0 are constants (otherwise, they would not be exponential families); however, it is unclear how these parameters are chosen and what advice the authors would give to practitioners.

Despite these limitations, constructing a multivariate distribution over counts that obeys the independence model of a Markov random field is a challenging task. The authors' effort is appreciated, and the results are of theoretical interest to the NIPS community. However, the literature review appears somewhat incomplete, with an overly light appreciation of existing work. For example, the authors seem to misunderstand the concept of a copula and misrepresent reference [8], which proposes a method for building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data, including count data. The discussion of reference [11] also appears questionable, as the book's main contribution is to show how to build discrete models with log-linear parameters without exponential growth in the number of variables.

A more significant omission is the neglect of the vast spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been conducted for an extended period. While some of these approaches, including the sparse precision Gaussian copula model of [8], do not model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space, the theoretical contribution of this paper is still valid. Nevertheless, as a practitioner, it is unclear why one should prefer this model over the simpler structured Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model, which has been the standard approach for a long time.

Finally, a reference newer than [20] would be appreciated, supporting the claim that "counts of sequencing reads... are replacing microarrays."
