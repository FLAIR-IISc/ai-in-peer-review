The goal of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data is an important one, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches (though a reservation on this point is noted below).

On the theoretical and conceptual front, this paper has several merits. Beginning with a proof demonstrating that previously suggested truncation does not lead to a valid model, the authors propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, though technically simple, are novel in their approach: they explicitly consider the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation as means to modify the Poisson distribution. Moreover, the changes made are well-motivated and appealing. That said, the underlying theory largely follows directly from the work of Yang et al., leaving us with several possible model suggestions whose practical value remains to be evaluated.

In light of the above, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly given the paper’s stated goal of addressing the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some potential, the data is tailored to favor the proposed models. The real-data experiments lack a crucial comparison to copula-based alternatives. Furthermore, the reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. As all methods involve constructing joint distributions, a more objective evaluation – such as the log-probability of test data – is needed. 

More importantly, and central to the question of whether these new models are necessary, is the fact that the copula competitors are used in an overly black-box manner. Specifically, even when using a simple Gaussian copula, it would be more appropriate to employ a sensible marginal model rather than the one used in the non-paranormal model (which lacks a density function!). The latter was chosen primarily for its asymptotic properties, not its practical merits. The near-identical performance to Glasso is also suspect, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform better. Similarly, the (reasonable) choice of R=11 in the real-data analysis could also inform the choice of marginal for the copula. While I do not expect an in-depth exploration of this point, some reasonable baseline is warranted.

Finally, the paper is generally well written. Though I believe the results are correct—particularly given that the authors begin by identifying an error in another work—I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. I also felt that the end of section 3.1 was overly detailed, and that the bound did not significantly contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. On the other hand, I would recommend expanding section 3.2, as it represents the core of the proposed method. In particular, the figure should not be deferred to the supplementary material, but rather presented within the main text, with a clearer explanation of its intuition.

Based on a conditional exponential construction, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model with the goal of enabling flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies. The suggested approach is appealing and possesses some theoretical novelty. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving the overall merit of this yet another multivariate Poisson-like model unclear.
