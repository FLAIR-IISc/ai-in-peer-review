

The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical models for count data by proposing three novel extensions to Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that overcome the fundamental limitation of existing PGMs: their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors begin by establishing the context of existing work, noting that while univariate Poisson distributions are standard for count data, multivariate extensions face challenges in maintaining both graphical structure and normalizability. The paper builds on the foundational work of Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2012), who showed that node-conditional Poisson distributions lead to a valid joint distribution but with strictly negative edge weights. The authors then systematically explore two approaches to overcome this limitation: truncation and modification of the base measure/sufficient statistics.

The paper's main contributions are well-defined and technically sound. The authors first demonstrate that the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie (2006) cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1), correcting an important misconception in the literature. They then propose a Truncated PGM (TPGM) with a bounded domain, followed by two fundamentally different variants: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measures and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with sublinear sufficient statistics. The mathematical derivations are rigorous, with careful attention to normalizability conditions (Theorems 3-4), and the paper provides theoretical guarantees for model validity.

The strengths of this work are significant. The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field by providing the first classes of graphical models for count data that can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies—a limitation that has hindered previous approaches. The mathematical treatment is thorough, with careful attention to the conditions required for normalizability. The empirical evaluation demonstrates practical utility, showing that the proposed models outperform Gaussian-based approaches on both simulated count data and a real microRNA sequencing dataset. The case study on breast cancer miRNA networks is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates how the proposed models identify biologically meaningful hubs (mir-375 and mir-10b) that the original PGM and Gaussian graphical models miss.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses that should be addressed. First, the connection to the broader literature needs strengthening. The paper cites spatial statistics work (e.g., Griffith, 2003) but fails to adequately discuss how their approach relates to the extensive literature on spatial count models (e.g., the work of Cressie and Wikle, 2011, or the spatial Poisson log-normal models in Gelfand et al., 2003). The dismissal of copula-based approaches (citing [8] as "not modeling MRF-style independence constraints") is somewhat misleading—the nonparanormal copula model in Liu et al. (2012) does model conditional independence structure, albeit in a transformed space. The authors would benefit from a more nuanced comparison with these established approaches.

Second, while the paper proposes three variants, the practical utility of SPGM is questionable given its lack of closed-form conditional distributions (which undermines the conditional specification approach central to the paper's methodology). The authors do not provide clear guidance on how practitioners should choose hyperparameters R and R0, which are critical for model performance. A more thorough empirical investigation of parameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the claim that count data "are replacing microarrays" (citing [22]) is unsupported; the authors should cite more recent work that demonstrates this transition.

Third, the paper does not sufficiently address why these specific models are preferable to simpler alternatives, such as the Gaussian copula approach or a latent Gaussian model with Poisson observations (as used in many genomics applications). While the authors note that their models directly model count data without transformation, they do not provide a convincing argument for why this is preferable to more established methods that have been shown to work well in practice.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions, though the empirical validation could be strengthened. The clarity is excellent—both the mathematical derivations and the experimental results are well-presented. The originality is strong, as the three proposed variants represent novel solutions to a well-known problem that has persisted since Besag's work. The significance is high, particularly for applications in genomics and spatial statistics where count data are prevalent, though the practical advantages over simpler methods need better justification.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for count data, filling an important gap in the literature. While the practical implications could be better demonstrated, the mathematical foundation is solid and the proposed models represent a meaningful advance beyond existing approaches. The authors should address the literature connections and provide clearer guidance on model selection and parameter choices.

Strengths:
- Rigorous mathematical treatment of a well-defined problem
- Clear theoretical contributions addressing a fundamental limitation
- Empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data
- Well-organized presentation of novel models

Weaknesses:
- Inadequate connection to spatial statistics and copula literature
- Limited practical guidance for model selection (R, R0)
- Insufficient justification for choosing these models over simpler alternatives
- Overstated claims about count data replacing microarrays

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. It addresses a significant gap in the literature with a mathematically sound approach, and the empirical results demonstrate practical utility. While the literature connections need strengthening and the practical guidance could be improved, the paper represents a meaningful advance that would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance with the expectation that the authors will address the literature connections and practical considerations in the camera-ready version.
