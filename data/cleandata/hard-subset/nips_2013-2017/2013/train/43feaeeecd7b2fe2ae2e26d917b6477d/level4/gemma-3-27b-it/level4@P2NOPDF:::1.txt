This paper introduces novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors identify a limitation in the default model proposed by Yang et al. – its inability to represent positive correlations while maintaining normalization. To address this, they explore a series of improvements, including a new truncation method, the incorporation of a quadratic base measure statistic (demonstrated to be a necessary component), and the introduction of a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The effectiveness of these models is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Overall, this is a well-written and clearly presented paper that offers valuable solutions for modeling count data. The work appears to be original, and the reported results are significant.

I have some reservations regarding the authors’ dismissive characterization of the truncation and quadratic models. Concerning the truncation approach, it seems plausible that many practical applications involve count variables with naturally bounded upper limits, in which case this model could perform quite well. Similarly, the authors’ critique of the quadratic model – suggesting it implies “Gaussian-esque thin tails” – doesn’t fully convince me. There are likely scenarios where such a tail behavior would be appropriate and desirable.

I encountered an unfamiliar term, “sparsistent,” though it appears to be established within the relevant literature. 

I also noted a minor editorial suggestion: on page 3, above section 2.1.1, “graphical mode distributions” should likely be revised to “model.” Additionally, on page 5, above section 3.1, the phrase “Note that the log…” could be clarified, perhaps as “Note that although the log…” 

Despite these minor points, this remains a high-quality paper offering a substantial contribution to the field of modeling joint distributions over count variables.
