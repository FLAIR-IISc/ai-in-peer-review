

This paper addresses an important gap in graphical models for count data by proposing novel extensions to Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that overcome the limitation of only modeling negative conditional dependencies. The authors correctly identify a key flaw in prior work by Kaiser and Cressie [20] regarding Winsorized Poisson distributions, demonstrating that their approach cannot yield a valid joint distribution. This theoretical foundation is well-established, with Proposition 1 and Theorem 1 providing rigorous proofs of the limitations of existing approaches.

The paper makes three significant theoretical contributions: (1) a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that uses a natural truncation of the Poisson domain to obtain a valid joint distribution; (2) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that modifies the base measure to allow both positive and negative dependencies; and (3) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that modifies sufficient statistics to achieve similar goals with potentially heavier tails. The theoretical development is well-motivated and follows from the framework established by Yang et al. [18, 19], though the authors clearly demonstrate how their modifications address the fundamental constraints identified in Proposition 1.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality. The authors carefully establish normalizability conditions for each proposed model (Theorems 3 and 4) and provide clear mathematical justification for their modifications. The connection to exponential family theory is well-executed, and the paper correctly identifies the trade-offs between the three approaches. The theoretical contributions are significant and address a clear limitation in the current literature.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections and appropriate mathematical notation. The exposition of the TPGM model is particularly clear, while the explanation of the SPGM sufficient statistics could benefit from more intuitive motivation (the authors rightly suggest including Figure 3 in the main text). The paper would be strengthened by more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly regarding the choice of truncation parameters.

The originality of the paper is high. While the authors build on the work of Yang et al. [18, 19], they identify a critical limitation in their framework and propose three novel solutions that fundamentally modify the model structure. The paper thoroughly reviews related work, including the limitations of existing approaches, and clearly articulates how their methods differ from previous contributions. This represents a meaningful advancement over the state of the art.

The significance of the paper is somewhat limited by the experimental evaluation. While the authors demonstrate the ability to learn network structures from simulated data, the experimental section lacks rigorous comparison to relevant baselines. The synthetic experiments focus on cases where the data is generated from the proposed models, which does not sufficiently demonstrate the models' advantages in general settings. The real-data analysis of breast cancer microRNA networks is promising but lacks critical comparisons to relevant competitors, particularly the non-paranormal approach which is mentioned as a baseline but not properly evaluated.

The most significant weakness is the experimental evaluation. The authors compare their methods to Gaussian graphical models (GGMs) and non-paranormal approaches but fail to properly evaluate how the copula-based methods would perform with sensible marginal models. The claim that "the essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious" is valid, and the authors should address this by comparing to Gaussian copula models with appropriate marginal distributions rather than the non-paranormal method with its problematic marginal assumptions. The paper would be substantially strengthened by including log-probability comparisons on test data and a more comprehensive analysis of the real-world performance of all methods.

The paper is well-written overall, with clear mathematical exposition. The authors would benefit from moving the key figure for the SPGM sufficient statistics from the supplementary material to the main text and providing more intuition about its behavior. The discussion of the QPGM's Gaussian-like tail properties in Section 3.1 is somewhat tangential and could be shortened.

In conclusion, this paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The authors correctly identify a fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models and propose three novel approaches that address this limitation. The theoretical development is strong, and the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature. However, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods, particularly in real-world settings. The authors should strengthen their empirical evaluation by including more comprehensive comparisons with relevant baselines and providing objective performance metrics.

The paper's main strength is its theoretical innovation, while its main weakness is the limited experimental validation. The authors should address the experimental shortcomings to fully demonstrate the significance of their contribution. With stronger empirical validation, this paper would represent a significant advance in the modeling of count data using graphical models.
