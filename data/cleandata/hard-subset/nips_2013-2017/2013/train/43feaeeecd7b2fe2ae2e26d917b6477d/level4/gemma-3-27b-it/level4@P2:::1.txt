This paper introduces novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables, addressing limitations of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs). The authors demonstrate that standard PGMs, as developed by Yang et al., can only model negative correlations due to normalizability constraints. They propose several solutions: a novel truncation approach, a quadratic Poisson graphical model (QPGM) leveraging a quadratic base measure, and a sub-linear Poisson graphical model (SPGM) with a modified sufficient statistic. The performance of these models is evaluated through simulations and analysis of microRNA sequencing data.

This is a well-written and technically sound paper presenting original and potentially significant contributions to the field of graphical models for count data. The authors clearly articulate the problem with existing PGMs and systematically explore different avenues for addressing it. The theoretical justifications for the proposed models, particularly the necessity of a quadratic base measure for positive correlations, are well-explained. The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets provide evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approaches, especially compared to Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM estimator. 

While the paper is strong overall, some arguments regarding the limitations of the truncation and quadratic models felt less compelling. The potential applicability of the truncated model to domains with naturally bounded counts is a valid point, and the “Gaussian-esque thin tails” of the QPGM might be suitable for certain datasets. These are not necessarily weaknesses, but rather areas where further exploration or justification could strengthen the paper. The use of the term “sparsistent” without definition initially could be confusing to readers unfamiliar with the field, though it is clear it is a well-established concept.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Originality:** The proposed models offer a novel approach to modeling count data, overcoming the limitations of existing methods.
*   **Significance:** The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies in count data has broad implications for various applications, including genomics, epidemiology, and user behavior analysis.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical derivations are rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed models.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limitations of the truncation and quadratic models, while acknowledged, might benefit from more detailed discussion regarding potential use cases where they would be appropriate.
*   The term "sparsistent" is used without prior definition, which may be confusing for some readers.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

*   **Page 3, above 2.1.1:** Change "graphical mode distributions" to "graphical model."
*   **Page 5, above 3.1:** Clarify "Note that the log..." to "Note that although the log..." 

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the minor suggestions for improvement. The paper is well-researched, clearly presented, and offers a significant advancement in the modeling of count data.
