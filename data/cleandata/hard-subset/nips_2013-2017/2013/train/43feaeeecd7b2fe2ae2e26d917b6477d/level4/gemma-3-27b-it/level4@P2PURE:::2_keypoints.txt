- Problem identification: Constructing multivariate count distributions is important and existing approaches have limitations (with a noted caveat).

- Theoretical contribution: Paper proves invalidity of previously proposed truncation methods and introduces alternatives.

- Proposed methods: Introduces TPGM (intuitive truncation), QPGM and SPGM (novel modifications considering base measure and sufficient statistics).

- Conceptual appeal: Modifications are well-motivated and conceptually attractive, extending the exponential-family/Poisson framework.

- Dependence on prior work: Underlying theory largely follows Yang et al., so models’ empirical value remains unestablished.

- Experimental weaknesses: Evaluation is disappointing and synthetic data appears tailored to favor the proposed models.

- Missing baselines: Real-data experiments lack comparisons to copula-based alternatives and more sensible marginal models.

- Evaluation metrics: Reported results are mainly qualitative/anecdotal; objective measures (e.g., test log-probability) are needed since all methods yield joint densities.

- Unprincipled copula usage: Copula competitors were used problematically (non-paranormal marginal lacks a density), likely biasing comparisons and masking better alternatives like kernel density marginals.

- Suspect results: Near-identical performance to Glasso raises suspicion; simple alternatives or different marginal choices (e.g., informed by R=11) might perform better.

- Presentation and proofs: Paper is well-written; recommend including all proofs in the supplement.

- Sections to revise: Section 3.1 is overly detailed and adds little; expand section 3.2 (move figure to main text and better explain intuition).

- Overall assessment: Approach is theoretically appealing and novel but empirical evaluation is limited and biased, leaving practical merit unclear.
