Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes new multivariate distributions over unbounded count data that are Markov with respect to an undirected graph. The starting point is the classical Poisson graphical model (PGM) of Besag and its generalizations, which are attractive but only normalizable when all pairwise parameters are non-positive, thereby capturing only negative conditional dependencies. The authors first clarify and correct a common truncation approach: they show that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditional construction cannot yield a valid joint distribution. They then propose an exponential-family truncation that does produce a valid joint model (TPGM), permitting both positive and negative dependencies on a bounded support. To address boundedness and normalizability constraints more fundamentally, they modify the univariate exponential family: (i) they prove that with linear sufficient statistics, the base measure must scale at least quadratically to allow positive dependencies; this motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and (ii) they propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that uses sublinear sufficient statistics, thereby allowing heavier tails than QPGM while retaining normalizability. Structure learning proceeds via penalized neighborhood selection. Simulations and a breast cancer microRNA sequencing case study illustrate empirical performance.

These questions—conditional specification, compatibility of node conditionals, and normalizability—have a long history in spatial statistics and Markov random fields (e.g., Besag; Arnold et al., Conditional Specification of Statistical Models, 1999). The paper also relates to multivariate Poisson constructions via sums of independent Poissons, log-linear models for contingency tables, and to copula-based methods for multivariate discrete data and spatial hierarchical models.

Strengths
- Technical contributions: Theorems establishing the incompatibility of Winsorized node conditionals (Theorem 1), a valid truncated exponential-family construction (TPGM), necessary conditions on base measures for positive dependencies (Theorem 3), and normalizability of SPGM (Theorem 4). These clarify longstanding issues around conditional specification for counts.
- Modeling scope: TPGM/QPGM/SPGM broaden the space of graphical models for counts to allow positive and negative conditional dependencies, overcoming a key limitation of the standard PGM.
- Estimation: Neighborhood selection using tractable node-wise objectives (or variational bounds) provides a practical learning strategy.
- Empirics: On simulated networks, the proposed methods outperform Gaussian-based baselines; the miRNA case study identifies plausible hubs missed by the standard PGM.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- SPGM node conditionals: The node-conditional log-partition D(·) lacks a closed form and requires summation over an infinite domain, with only a variational upper bound proposed. This undermines a central appeal of conditionally specified models—closed-form node conditionals—and raises practical concerns about optimization accuracy and computational burden. Please detail how you compute or approximate D and its derivatives, assess approximation error, and report runtimes.
- “Poisson-like” interpretation: QPGM changes only the base measure, but SPGM also alters the sufficient statistics. It would help to justify calling SPGM “Poisson-like,” and to characterize its marginal PMFs. Please include plots of the SPGM PMF for representative parameter settings and compare tail behavior to Poisson/QPGM.
- Choosing R and R0: SPGM’s sufficient statistics hinge on user-specified thresholds. How should practitioners select R and R0? Are there data-driven rules (e.g., quantiles of observed counts), cross-validation strategies, or theoretical guidance? Similarly, for TPGM, how sensitive are results to R?
- Model fit to count data: The Poisson family often fits empirical counts poorly due to overdispersion/zero-inflation. For the miRNA data, please show marginal histograms or dispersion summaries, and compare fits of PGM/TPGM/QPGM/SPGM to standard Poisson/negative binomial baselines.
- Literature coverage and positioning: The discussion of copula-based approaches appears to understate their scope. Gaussian copula models for discrete data (e.g., [8] and related work) provide multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete margins with MCMC and approximate inference, though they do not encode MRF sparsity in the observable space. Likewise, the log-linear literature does not necessarily entail exponential growth in parameters once hierarchical structure or sparsity is imposed. Most importantly, the spatial statistics literature contains extensive work on high-dimensional count data via Poisson (or negative binomial) observation models with Gaussian random field priors (structured precision matrices). Please better acknowledge these lines, clarify what is and is not captured by them, and empirically compare against a standard spatial GLMM (Gaussian random field + Poisson link) or a sparse-precision Gaussian copula model for discrete data.
- Theory vs practice: The sparsistency of the proposed estimators is deferred. A brief discussion of conditions under which your neighborhood selection is consistent for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM (even if proofs are deferred) would strengthen the practical message.
- Minor: Provide a more recent reference than [20] for the claim that sequencing counts are replacing microarrays; clarify whether your claim pertains broadly or to specific domains.

Quality
The theory is interesting and generally careful; clarifying compatibility/normalizability is valuable. However, the computational treatment of SPGM node conditionals needs more detail and empirical validation of approximation quality.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well organized, but key implementation details (SPGM normalizer approximation, parameter selection) are missing. Expanding the related work to accurately reflect copula and spatial GLMM literatures would improve positioning.

Originality
The incompatibility result for Winsorization, the exponential-family truncation, and the base-measure necessity for positive dependencies are novel and insightful. The QPGM/SPGM constructions are original within this conditional-specification framework.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of count-valued graphical models and offers potentially useful alternatives to existing approaches. Its impact will be higher if the empirical section demonstrates robust advantages over strong hierarchical/coupled baselines and provides clear guidance for practitioners.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear theoretical advances on compatibility and normalizability for count MRFs.
- New model classes enabling positive and negative dependencies with practical estimators.
- Promising empirical performance on simulated and real data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient treatment of SPGM computation and approximation error.
- Incomplete and partly inaccurate discussion of relevant literatures; missing strong comparative baselines (e.g., spatial GLMMs, discrete copulas).
- Limited practical guidance on selecting SPGM/TPGM hyperparameters and assessing model fit.

Overall, this is a theoretically interesting and potentially impactful paper; strengthening the empirical comparisons, implementation details, and literature positioning would make it a stronger contribution for NIPS.
