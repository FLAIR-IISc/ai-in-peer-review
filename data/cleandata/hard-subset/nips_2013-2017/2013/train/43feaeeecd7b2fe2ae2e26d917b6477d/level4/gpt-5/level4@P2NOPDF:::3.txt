This paper proposes a construction of multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure of an undirected graph. Building such distributions is notoriously difficult, as emphasized in the literature by Besag and others, and in texts like Arnold et al.’s “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models” (1999), which also treats related conditionally specified exponential families. Against that backdrop, the paper delivers several interesting ideas.

Strengths
- The proposed constructions yield valid multivariate models with count-valued marginals while retaining a graph-structured dependence. 
- The estimation procedures appear relatively simple and computationally tractable.
- The work addresses a genuinely hard problem, and the theoretical contribution is of interest to the NIPS community.

Concerns and questions
- QPGM’s tails appear thinner than those of a Poisson, which may be undesirable for many empirical count datasets that are overdispersed or heavy-tailed.
- SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which undercuts a key advantage of conditionally specified models (efficient local computation and inference).
- It is unclear in what sense SPGM should be called “Poisson.” For QPGM one might argue only the base measure is altered, but for SPGM the connection seems more tenuous; please clarify the nomenclature and what properties justify the label.
- Please provide empirical motivation for modeling counts with these Poisson-type families in observable space. Many real-world counts (e.g., overdispersed, zero-inflated) are poorly fit by Poisson-like tails. For the breast cancer example, could you plot the empirical distribution against a Poisson fit? 
- A plot of SPGM’s probability mass function would help build intuition, as the form reads as somewhat convoluted.
- It appears R and R0 are treated as constants (otherwise the constructions would not be exponential families). How are these chosen in practice? What guidance can you offer to practitioners regarding default choices, tuning, or data-driven selection?

Related work and positioning
- The literature review feels incomplete and occasionally inaccurate. In particular, the discussion of copulas suggests misunderstandings. Reference [8] explicitly targets multivariate modeling for arbitrary discrete data, including counts, and comes with a suite of MCMC and approximate inference tools. Please articulate more precisely how your approach differs from and improves upon discrete copula-based methods (e.g., in terms of conditional independence representation, scalability, identifiability, or tail behavior).
- The treatment of [11] is also unclear. The classic log-linear modeling literature shows how to parameterize discrete MRFs without parameter growth exploding with the number of variables. Please clarify what is and is not inherited from that body of work, and whether it offers alternatives or complementary approaches to your constructions (including any relevance to Poisson-like specifications).
- A substantial omission is the spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data have long been modeled using structured Gaussian random fields with Poisson (or related) observation models (e.g., CAR/ICAR structures, log-Gaussian Cox processes, and other hierarchical models). While many of these do not encode MRF-style conditional independences in the observed counts themselves (often the independences live in the latent Gaussian layer), they remain widely used, empirically effective, and supported by mature inference methods. Please position your contribution against these approaches, explaining when and why one should prefer an observed-space MRF over a latent-Gaussian-plus-Poisson measurement model.

Practical trade-offs
- From a practitioner’s perspective, the key question is the price–performance trade-off: what empirical advantages justify adopting your model class over standard structured Gaussian random fields with Poisson measurement? Concrete comparisons—predictive accuracy, calibration, tail fit, robustness, and computational cost—would be valuable.
- Since SPGM lacks closed-form conditionals, please clarify the practical consequences for inference and learning (e.g., algorithmic complexity, mixing, convergence diagnostics), and whether the loss of local conditional tractability materially affects scalability.

Empirical suggestions
- Provide: (i) pmf plots for SPGM and tail comparisons with Poisson and QPGM; (ii) sensitivity analyses for R and R0; (iii) empirical fits on datasets known to be overdispersed or zero-inflated; and (iv) comparisons to latent-Gaussian count models and discrete copula-based baselines.
- For the genomics application, please cite a reference more recent than [20] to support the statement that “counts of sequencing reads … are replacing microarrays,” or soften the claim.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a difficult and important problem and proposes constructions with clear theoretical interest and reasonably simple estimation procedures. However, some modeling choices (thinner tails in QPGM, lack of closed-form conditionals in SPGM) raise practical concerns, and the related-work discussion omits or mischaracterizes key strands in discrete copula modeling, log-linear discrete MRFs, and spatial statistics. With a more careful literature review, clearer positioning against established latent-Gaussian count models, practical guidance on hyperparameter selection, and stronger empirical evidence (including diagnostics and ablations), the contribution would be considerably strengthened.
