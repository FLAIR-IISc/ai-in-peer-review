The goal of designing multivariate models suitable for count data is important, and the authors correctly point out limitations of existing methods (with a caveat noted below).

Conceptually and theoretically, the paper has several strengths. Beginning with a proof that a previously proposed truncation does not yield a valid joint model, the authors introduce several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM are technically simple yet novel in their explicit use of the exponential-family base measure and sufficient statistics as levers to modify the Poisson law. These modifications are well motivated and appealing. However, much of the theoretical development follows directly from Yang et al., leaving us primarily with a menu of plausible model classes whose value ultimately hinges on empirical performance.

Given this, the empirical evaluation is underwhelming, especially since the stated aim is to overcome the practical shortcomings of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic study is suggestive, the data appear tailored to favor the proposed models. On real data, comparisons to copula-based alternatives are conspicuously absent. Moreover, the reported outcomes are primarily qualitative. Since all methods define joint distributions, a more objective assessment via held-out log-likelihood (or related proper scoring rules) is needed.

More importantly—bearing on whether new models are necessary—the copula competitors are deployed in an overly black-box fashion. Even with a Gaussian copula, one would typically pair it with sensible marginal models rather than the nonparanormal transform (which lacks a proper density and was chosen mainly for asymptotic properties rather than practical fidelity). The near-identical performance to Glasso is puzzling, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density marginals would perform substantially better. Similarly, for the real-data analysis, the (reasonable) choice R = 11 in TPGM/SPGM could be mirrored by an informed marginal choice within a copula framework. A thorough exploration is not required here, but a reasonable baseline is warranted.

The paper is generally well written. Although I believe the results are correct—especially given the identification of an error in prior work—I recommend including full proofs in the supplementary material. I also found the end of Section 3.1 overly detailed, and the bound did not materially strengthen the “Gaussian-esque” argument. By contrast, Section 3.2 is the core contribution and would benefit from a slower, more intuitive presentation; the key figure should appear in the main text rather than the supplement.

In summary: Building on the conditional exponential-family construction, the authors propose alternatives to the Poisson graphical model that enable mixed positive and negative dependencies. Positives include an appealing approach with some theoretical novelty; negatives include a limited and somewhat biased empirical evaluation. As it stands, the contribution reads as another Poisson-like multivariate family whose practical advantage remains uncertain.
