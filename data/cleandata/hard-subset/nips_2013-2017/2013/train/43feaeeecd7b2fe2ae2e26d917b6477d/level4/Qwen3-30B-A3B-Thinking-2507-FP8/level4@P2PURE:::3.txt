

The paper presents a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure of an undirected network. Constructing such distributions is generally challenging, as extensively documented in the literature since Besag's work over thirty years ago (including Arnold et al.'s *Conditional Specification of Statistical Models*, 1999, which extends related observations to distributions like conditionally-specified exponential families).  

The authors' proposal achieves certain relevant goals: it enables marginal count distributions and yields relatively straightforward estimation algorithms. However, significant drawbacks exist. QPGM exhibits marginally thinner tails, while SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions—a shortcoming that undermines the rationale for constructing a conditionally specified model. Indeed, I question whether SPGM can reasonably be termed a Poisson distribution (for QPGM, one might argue only the base measure is altered). This raises a fundamental concern: what is the primary motivation for modeling counts, given that the Poisson distribution itself rarely fits empirical count data well (though it remains a valuable building block in stochastic processes and latent variable models). For instance, could the authors plot the probability mass function of SPGM, which appears complex at first glance? Additionally, assuming R and R₀ are constants (as required for exponential family properties), how are these parameters selected, and what practical guidance do the authors provide to practitioners?  

That said, constructing multivariate count distributions respecting Markov independence constraints in Markov random fields (MRFs) is inherently difficult. I sincerely appreciate the authors' effort and acknowledge the theoretical interest of their results for NIPS. However, the literature review is notably incomplete and underappreciated. For example, the authors seem unfamiliar with copulas (e.g., [8]), which provide a framework for building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data—including counts—with established MCMC and approximate inference methods. The discussion of [11] also appears questionable, as that work focuses on avoiding exponential growth in parameters for discrete models via log-linear structures (though I cannot verify its specific treatment of Poisson distributions without the text). Most critically, the paper neglects the extensive spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been extensively studied for decades. While approaches like the sparse precision Gaussian copula model in [8] do not strictly model MRF independence constraints in the observable space, the theoretical contribution of this paper remains valid. Nevertheless, as a practitioner, I am unconvinced why one should adopt this model over the established simpler alternative: a structured Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model.  

Final note: I am not an expert in gene expression analysis, but I request a reference newer than [20] supporting the claim that "counts of sequencing reads ... are replacing microarrays."  

A method for constructing multivariate count distributions Markov with respect to undirected graphs, with inherent advantages and limitations. The literature review is incomplete.
