- Addresses an important problem (multivariate count distributions) but proposed solutions require careful evaluation.  
- Theoretical contributions are well-motivated: shows prior truncation methods fail and proposes three alternatives (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).  
- TPGM is conceptually straightforward and intuitively justified.  
- QPGM and SPGM introduce novel, simple modifications by specifying base measure and sufficient statistics within the exponential-family framework.  
- Much of the theoretical development follows Yang et al., leaving the practical benefits of the new variants unproven.  
- Experimental evaluation is a critical weakness and does not substantiate the paper’s practical claims.  
- Synthetic experiments appear designed to favor the proposed models.  
- Real-data experiments omit comparisons to copula-based alternatives, which are standard for multivariate counts.  
- Reported results are qualitative/anecdotal; objective metrics (e.g., test log-probability) are missing and necessary for fair comparison.  
- Inappropriate use of copula competitors: the non-paranormal marginal model (lacking a density) is used improperly as a practical baseline.  
- Near-identical performance to Glasso is suspicious; simple alternatives (e.g., Gaussian KDE) would likely perform better.  
- The choice of R=11 for the marginal copula is inadequately justified and no reasonable baseline reflecting that choice is provided.  
- Paper is generally well-written and corrects an error in prior work; all proofs should be included in the supplement.  
- Section 3.1 is overly detailed and could be condensed; Section 3.2 (core method) needs more emphasis and the key figure should be in the main text.  
- Overall: a theoretically appealing extension to Poisson graphical models, but limited, biased, and inconclusive experimental validation leaves practical contribution uncertain.
