The paper provides a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that obey the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building a multivariate distribution over unbounded counts is a challenging problem that has been studied extensively by Besag and others over the past 30 years. This problem is thoroughly discussed in textbooks such as Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models" (1999), which extends some of the authors' observations to other distributions, including conditionally-specified exponential distributions.

The proposal presented by the authors has both advantages and disadvantages. On the positive side, the constructions allow for marginal distributions over counts and lead to relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, the proposed models have some drawbacks. Specifically, the Quasi-Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) has marginally thinner tails, and the Sparse Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which somewhat defeats the purpose of building a conditionally specified model. Furthermore, it is unclear how the SPGM can be considered a Poisson distribution, as the Poisson itself may not be the best choice for modeling empirical data. While the Poisson distribution is a useful building block for many models, such as components of stochastic processes and latent variable models, it often fails to accurately fit real-world data. For instance, a plot of breast cancer data would likely not resemble a Poisson distribution.

It would be beneficial to include a plot of the probability mass function of the SPGM, as it appears somewhat convoluted at first glance. Additionally, it is assumed that R and R0 are considered constants, as they would otherwise not be exponential families. The authors should provide guidance on how to choose these parameters and offer advice to practitioners.

While constructing a multivariate distribution over counts that obeys the independence model of a Markov random field is indeed challenging, the authors' effort is appreciated, and the results are of theoretical interest to NIPS. However, the literature review appears to be somewhat incomplete. For instance, the authors seem to misunderstand the concept of a copula and misrepresent reference [8], which aims to build multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data, including count data. Moreover, the discussion of reference [11] appears to be unclear, as the main point of that book is to demonstrate how to build discrete models with log-linear parameters without exponential growth in the number of variables.

A significant omission is the neglect of the vast spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been conducted for a long time. Although some of these approaches, including the sparse precision Gaussian copula model of [8], do not model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space, the theoretical contribution of this paper remains valid. Nevertheless, as a practitioner, it is unclear why one should prefer this model over the simpler structured Gaussian random field + Poisson measurement model, which has been the standard approach for a long time.

Finally, a more recent reference than [20] would be appreciated to support the claim that "counts of sequencing reads... are replacing microarrays."
