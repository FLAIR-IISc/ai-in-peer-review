This paper addresses the critical limitation of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), which can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors aim to develop graphical models for count data capable of capturing both positive and negative dependencies. The paper's main contributions are: (1) a proof showing that a previously proposed "Winsorized" PGM is not a valid joint distribution; (2) the introduction of a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) with a different truncation scheme; and (3) the proposal of two novel models, the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which are derived by modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the underlying exponential family distribution. This work builds directly on the established framework of constructing graphical models from node-conditional distributions (Besag, 1974; Yang et al., 2012).

**Strengths of the paper:**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Modeling complex dependencies in multivariate count data is a pressing need in many domains where such data is now ubiquitous (e.g., genomics, social network analysis), and existing methods are often inadequate.
2.  **Originality and Conceptual Contribution:** The core idea of modifying the base measure (for the QPGM) and the sufficient statistics (for the SPGM) to circumvent the parameter restrictions of standard PGMs is both novel and elegant. The motivation for these new model classes is well-explained and theoretically sound.
3.  **Correction of Prior Work:** The proof that the previously proposed Winsorized PGM does not lead to a valid joint distribution is a valuable and important correction to the existing literature.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. It effectively communicates the limitations of prior work and provides clear motivation for the proposed solutions.

**Weaknesses of the paper:**

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The primary weakness is the empirical evaluation, which is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed models over strong alternatives.
    *   **Lack of Objective Metrics:** The experiments focus on network structure recovery and qualitative interpretation. Since the paper proposes new generative models, a quantitative comparison of held-out log-likelihood on test data is essential for an objective assessment of model fit. Without this, it remains unclear whether the new models provide a better explanation of unseen data.
    *   **Superficial Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to copula-based methods like the nonparanormal model is not compelling. These methods are used in a "black-box" manner, and their performance depends heavily on the marginal transformation. The reported performance, which is nearly identical to a standard Gaussian Graphical Model, suggests the default transformation was not well-suited for this count data. A more rigorous comparison would involve using more appropriate marginal distributions for the copula framework.
2.  **Incremental Theoretical Framework:** While the proposed model families are novel, the underlying theoretical machinery for constructing the joint distribution from node-conditionals follows directly from the framework established by Yang et al. (2012). The main contribution lies in the clever specification of new model forms rather than a fundamental theoretical advance.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Strengthen Experimental Validation:** To substantiate the paper's claims, the empirical section requires significant strengthening. I strongly recommend that the authors:
    *   Include a comparison of held-out log-likelihood on test data for all models (proposed and baselines) on both synthetic and real-world datasets. This would provide a crucial, objective measure of performance.
    *   Conduct a more thoughtful comparison against copula-based methods. This could involve exploring more suitable marginal transformations for count data, such as those derived from the empirical CDF or kernel density estimates.
2.  **Refine Presentation:**
    *   The end of Section 3.1, discussing the variational upper bound for the QPGM, feels overly detailed and does not add much to the core argument about its "Gaussian-esque" properties. This could be condensed or moved to the appendix.
    *   Conversely, Section 3.2, which introduces the SPGM, is the heart of the paper's novel contributions and feels too brief. I would encourage the authors to expand this section with more intuition and to move the figure illustrating the sub-linear sufficient statistic from the appendix into the main text to better explain its design.
3.  **Ensure Reproducibility:** Given that the paper begins by correcting an error in prior work, it is imperative that the authors include full, detailed proofs for all theorems in the supplementary material to allow for thorough verification of their claims.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

The paper presents an appealing and conceptually interesting set of solutions to a well-known problem in graphical models for count data. The ideas are creative and well-motivated. However, the experimental evaluation in its current form is limited and does not sufficiently validate the practical utility of these new models over strong, existing alternatives. The absence of an objective model comparison, such as test log-likelihood, is a significant omission. While the paper has clear conceptual merits, its contribution would be much more significant with a more rigorous empirical validation.
