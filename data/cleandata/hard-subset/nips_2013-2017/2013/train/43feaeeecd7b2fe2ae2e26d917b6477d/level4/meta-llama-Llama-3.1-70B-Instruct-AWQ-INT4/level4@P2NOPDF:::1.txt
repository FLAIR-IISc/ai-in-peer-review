The paper proposes novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the standard model, as described by Yang et al., is insufficient because it can only capture negative correlations to ensure normalization. To address this limitation, they introduce a series of modifications, including a new truncation method, a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove is necessary given the existing framework), and a sub-linear sufficient statistic. Experimental results are presented for both synthetic and real-world data.

This is a well-written paper that presents innovative solutions for modeling count data. The originality and significance of the results are notable. However, I found the discussion of the "big negatives" associated with the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unconvincing. Specifically, I can envision scenarios where counts are typically low, and the truncation model would be effective. Similarly, "Gaussian-esque thin tails" might be suitable in certain contexts, making the quadratic model more applicable than the authors suggest.

I was unfamiliar with the term "sparsistent," but it appears to be a term that has been used in the field for some time.

There are a few minor errors and suggestions for improvement. On page 3, above section 2.1.1, "graphical mode distributions" should be corrected to "graphical model distributions." On page 5, above section 3.1, the sentence "Note that the log..." might be rephrased as "Note that although the log..." for clarity.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a high-quality contribution to the field of modeling joint distributions over count variables.
