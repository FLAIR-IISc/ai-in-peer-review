

The paper introduces novel approaches to model joint distributions over count-valued variables using graphical models, addressing a significant limitation in existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that can only capture negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors build on the foundational work of Yang et al. (2013) who established PGMs as graphical models with univariate Poisson node-conditionals. They demonstrate that the standard PGM suffers from a severe restriction: its edge weights must be non-positive for the distribution to be normalizable, which limits its practical applicability to real-world count data that often exhibits both positive and negative dependencies.

The authors propose three distinct approaches to overcome this limitation. First, they investigate truncation methods and identify a valid alternative to the Winsorized Poisson approach (Kaiser & Cressie, 2007) which, as they prove in Theorem 1, cannot yield a consistent joint distribution. Their Truncated PGM (TPGM) uses a natural truncation that preserves exponential family properties, allowing both positive and negative dependencies but with a bounded domain. Second, they introduce a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with a quadratic base measure, proving in Theorem 3 that this scaling is necessary for normalizability with positive edge weights. Third, they propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with sub-linear sufficient statistics that maintains Poisson-like base measure while permitting a broader range of feasible parameters.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The theoretical development is rigorous, with careful proofs of normalizability conditions and distribution properties. The authors demonstrate how their models can be learned via neighborhood selection, with promising empirical results on both synthetic and real-world microRNA sequencing data. Their case study on breast cancer microRNA networks provides compelling evidence that their methods identify biologically meaningful relationships missed by existing approaches, including the original PGM and Gaussian graphical models.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making a complex technical topic accessible to readers. The authors provide thorough comparisons with related work, including recent advances in graphical models for count data. The experimental results effectively demonstrate the advantages of their proposed models, particularly in high-dimensional settings where standard approaches fail to capture the full dependence structure.

Regarding the paper's strengths, the theoretical contributions are significant: they establish necessary conditions for normalizability while enabling both positive and negative dependencies, which had previously been impossible with Poisson-based graphical models. The practical implications are substantial, as count data is increasingly prevalent in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains. The case study on breast cancer miRNA networks provides concrete evidence of the approach's utility, identifying biologically relevant hub miRNAs like mir-375 and mir-10b that were missed by previous methods.

The paper's main weaknesses relate to practical limitations of the proposed models. The TPGM approach requires specifying a truncation level R, which introduces a parameter that must be determined in practice. While the authors note that as R increases, the model approaches the original PGM with its negativity constraints, they could provide more guidance on selecting R for different applications. The QPGM's Gaussian-like thin tails might be undesirable in applications where heavy-tailed distributions are more appropriate, though the authors acknowledge this limitation. The SPGM addresses the tail issue but requires careful selection of the sub-linear sufficient statistic parameters R0 and R.

The term "sparsistent" appears in the paper without definition, though it has been used in the literature (e.g., Ravikumar et al., 2011). The authors should clarify this term for readers unfamiliar with the specific terminology. The review also notes a minor typo on page 3 (above 2.1.1) where "graphical mode distributions" should be "graphical model distributions."

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on graphical models for count data. While the proposed models have some practical limitations, they represent the first classes of Poisson-like graphical models that can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies between count-valued variables. The authors successfully address a major gap in the literature and provide a theoretically sound framework for modeling complex multivariate count data.

The significance of this work is evident from its potential applications in genomics (as demonstrated in the breast cancer case study), spatial statistics, and other domains where count data is prevalent. The paper advances the state of the art by resolving a fundamental limitation in Poisson graphical models that has constrained their use in real-world applications.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with thorough theoretical development and empirical validation. The experimental design is appropriate, and the results are well-presented. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach while demonstrating its advantages over existing methods.

The paper is original in its approach to overcoming the normalizability constraint in Poisson graphical models. The authors' theoretical insights about the necessary quadratic scaling of the base measure (Theorem 3) are novel contributions that clarify a fundamental constraint in this area. The three proposed models represent distinct, non-trivial extensions of the existing PGM framework.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with each section building logically on the previous one. The authors provide sufficient detail for readers to understand and potentially replicate their methods. The connection between theoretical results and practical applications is particularly strong, with the breast cancer case study effectively demonstrating the value of their approach.

Given the paper's theoretical contributions, practical significance, and careful presentation, I recommend acceptance. While the proposed models have some practical limitations, they represent a significant advancement in the modeling of count data using graphical models. The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature and provides a theoretically sound, practically useful framework that will likely be valuable to researchers working with count-valued data across multiple domains.
