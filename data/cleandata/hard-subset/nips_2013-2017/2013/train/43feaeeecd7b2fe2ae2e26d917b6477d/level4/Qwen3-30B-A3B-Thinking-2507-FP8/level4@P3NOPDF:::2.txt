

The paper addresses the important challenge of constructing multivariate distributions for count data, identifying limitations in existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs). The theoretical contributions are a strength: the authors correctly demonstrate that prior truncation approaches fail to yield valid models, and they propose three alternatives (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM). The TPGM offers a natural interpretation, while QPGM and SPGM introduce novel modifications to the Poisson base measure and sufficient statistics within exponential family framework. The motivations for these changes are compelling, though the underlying theory largely follows Yang et al., leaving the practical merit of the proposed models to be validated empirically.

The experimental evaluation, however, critically undermines the paper’s stated goal of resolving practical limitations of PGMs. The synthetic experiments are overly tailored to the proposed models, lacking generality. More significantly, the real-data analysis omits critical baselines: copula-based alternatives are entirely absent, and the comparison to the non-paranormal method is flawed. The non-paranormal approach uses a marginal model with no density (chosen for asymptotic properties rather than practicality), which is inappropriate for count data. This black-box treatment of copulas is unacceptable; a sensible marginal model (e.g., Poisson or negative binomial) should be used instead. The reported performance—matching Glasso’s results—is suspicious, as simpler methods like Gaussian kernel density estimates (KDEs) would likely outperform this misapplied baseline. Additionally, the choice of *R*=11 for the copula’s bandwidth (a hyperparameter) could be directly translated to the marginal model, yet this is not addressed. The absence of objective metrics (e.g., log-probability on test data) renders the results qualitative and unconvincing. A rigorous comparison against well-established, appropriately configured baselines is essential to justify the proposed models.

The paper is generally well-written, but minor improvements would enhance clarity. All proofs should be included in the supplementary material, as the theoretical claims are central to the contribution. Section 3.1’s detailed bound in the final paragraph is tangential to the Gaussian-esque argument and could be condensed. Conversely, Section 3.2—the core methodological contribution—deserves more careful exposition. The key figure should be presented in the main text (not supplementary) with a clearer explanation of its intuition to aid reader comprehension.

In summary, the theoretical framework is conceptually sound and offers a fresh perspective on extending PGMs. However, the experimental shortcomings are severe: missing baselines, inappropriate comparisons, and lack of objective evaluation prevent a clear demonstration of practical utility. The paper proposes another multivariate Poisson-like model, but without compelling evidence that it outperforms simpler, well-established alternatives, its contribution remains unclear. The authors must address the experimental gaps to justify the need for their approach. While the ideas are promising, the current evaluation does not substantiate the claim that these models resolve practical limitations of existing methods. The work would be significantly strengthened by a thorough empirical comparison against properly implemented baselines, including Gaussian KDEs and copulas with sensible marginals.
