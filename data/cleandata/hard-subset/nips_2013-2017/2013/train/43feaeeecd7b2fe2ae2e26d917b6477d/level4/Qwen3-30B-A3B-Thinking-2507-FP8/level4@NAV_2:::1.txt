

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Poisson graphical models for count data, where existing approaches can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors correctly identify that Yang et al.'s Poisson graphical model (PGM) construction [18, 19] inherently restricts edge weights to be non-positive, making it unsuitable for modeling count data with both positive and negative dependencies. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a novel truncation approach (Truncated PGM) that yields a valid joint distribution with more flexible dependencies; (2) a Quadratic PGM with quadratic base measure that enables both positive and negative conditional dependencies while maintaining normalizability; and (3) a Sublinear PGM with sub-linear sufficient statistics that provides heavier tails and broader parameter feasibility. The authors validate their methods through synthetic experiments and a breast cancer microRNA sequencing case study.

Strengths:
- Quality: The theoretical contributions are well-founded, with rigorous proofs establishing normalizability conditions (Theorems 1-4) and clear justification for each model variant. The experimental results demonstrate consistent advantages over existing methods for count data, particularly in recovering network structures with both positive and negative edges.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem identification to solution development. The derivations and proofs are presented with appropriate technical detail, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described.
- Originality: The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent novel extensions not previously considered in the literature. The authors effectively distinguish their work from prior approaches, including Kaiser and Cressie's Winsorized Poisson (which they prove cannot yield valid joint distributions) and other Poisson variants.
- Significance: The models address a critical gap in graphical modeling for count data, with clear applications in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains. The breast cancer microRNA case study demonstrates practical value, identifying biologically relevant hub miRNAs that existing methods miss.

Weaknesses:
- Quality: The discussion of limitations for the Truncated PGM (bounded domain) and Quadratic PGM (Gaussian-esque thin tails) could be more nuanced. The claim that "Gaussian-esque thin tails" is a drawback may be context-dependent, as this behavior might be desirable in certain applications. The authors could better acknowledge when these characteristics might be advantageous.
- Clarity: The paper contains minor typographical errors (e.g., "graphical mode distributions" on page 3, above 2.1.1 should be "graphical model distributions"). The term "sparsistent" is used without definition, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with the term.
- Significance: The paper could better articulate why their approach is superior to simpler alternatives like log-transforming count data or using non-parametric methods, particularly regarding the trade-offs between model complexity and interpretability.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that effectively solves a well-documented problem in graphical modeling for count data. The authors provide a technically sound, well-justified solution with clear practical implications. The paper is well-written, makes significant theoretical contributions, and demonstrates strong empirical validation. I recommend acceptance.
