

The paper introduces novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors identify a limitation in the default model from Yang et al., which requires negative correlations to ensure normalization. They propose several solutions: a new truncation method, a quadratic base measure statistic (proven necessary under fixed conditions), and a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors evaluate these models on synthetic and real-world count data.

This is a well-written paper presenting significant contributions to count data modeling. The proposed methods are original, and the results demonstrate clear empirical advantages over existing approaches.

However, two key drawbacks require further justification. First, the critique of the truncation method's "big negatives" appears less compelling. In many real-world applications (e.g., word counts in documents or event frequencies), counts naturally exhibit upper bounds, making truncation highly suitable and potentially superior. The authors should acknowledge this domain-specific applicability rather than treating truncation as inherently flawed.

Second, the dismissal of the quadratic model's "Gaussian-esque thin tails" assumption seems premature. For certain count distributions (e.g., those with strong central tendencies and rapid decay), Gaussian-like tail behavior could be a reasonable and effective modeling choice. The authors should discuss scenarios where this property would be advantageous, rather than implying it is universally undesirable.

The term "sparsistent" appears unexplained, though it is established in high-dimensional statistics literature (e.g., for consistent support recovery). Its omission here may confuse readers unfamiliar with the term; a brief clarification or citation would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear technical contributions. The core ideas are sound, and the experimental validation is thorough. Addressing the points above would significantly strengthen the paper's impact, particularly by better contextualizing the limitations of the proposed methods within practical data scenarios. The work makes a valuable step toward more flexible and normalized count distribution modeling.
