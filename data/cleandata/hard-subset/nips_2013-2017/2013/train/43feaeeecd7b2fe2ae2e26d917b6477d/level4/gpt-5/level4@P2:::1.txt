Summary
This paper introduces new undirected graphical models tailored to multivariate count data. The authors show that the standard Poisson graphical model (PGM) derived from node-conditional Poisson distributions is fundamentally limited: normalizability forces all pairwise parameters to be non-positive, so it can only capture competitive (negative) conditional dependencies. They first revisit “Winsorized” truncation and prove that this approach cannot yield a consistent joint distribution under the Besag/Yang construction. They then propose: (1) a Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) that preserves an exponential-family form on a bounded count domain and admits both positive and negative dependencies; (2) a Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) that retains linear sufficient statistics but uses a quadratic base measure, which the authors show is necessary (up to order) to allow positive dependencies while ensuring normalizability; and (3) a Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) that replaces the linear sufficient statistic with a sublinear one, recovering heavier tails and broad feasibility for pairwise parameters. Structure learning is done via ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection. Experiments on simulated data and on TCGA microRNA sequencing data indicate improved edge recovery and more biologically plausible networks compared to Gaussian-based baselines and the original PGM.

Evaluation
- Quality: The theoretical results are solid and clearly stated, including the impossibility of a Winsorized joint, the necessity of quadratic-scale base measures for linear statistics, and normalizability conditions for QPGM and SPGM. The learning approach is standard and appropriate. Empirical studies support the claims, though some additional baselines and sensitivity analyses would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. A few terms (e.g., “sparsistent”) and notational choices could be defined more clearly on first use. Minor typos are listed below.
- Originality: The work is original in identifying and proving the Winsorization inconsistency, and in proposing three tractable model families that permit both positive and negative conditional dependencies for counts, with distinct tail behaviors.
- Significance: Modeling conditional dependence in counts beyond negative-only constraints is important in many areas (genomics, spatial statistics, recommender systems). The proposed models are likely to be useful to practitioners and to stimulate further methodological work.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of a key shortcoming in existing PGMs for counts, with a rigorous impossibility result for a commonly suggested fix (Winsorization).
- Three complementary model families with distinct trade-offs: TPGM (bounded support, positive/negative edges), QPGM (Gaussian-like tails, variational connections), SPGM (heavier tails, unconstrained pairwise parameters).
- Practical estimation via penalized neighborhood selection and sensible variational bounds for node-wise likelihoods.
- Empirical results on both synthetic networks and a real sequencing dataset, with biologically interpretable findings (e.g., miRNA hubs).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- The paper argues that truncation (TPGM) and Gaussian-esque tails (QPGM) are “big negatives,” but this is not fully convincing. In many domains, counts are naturally bounded (by protocol, dynamic range, or preprocessing), and thin tails may be appropriate. Suggest adding guidance on when each model is preferable, with quantitative trade-offs (e.g., calibration, tail fit metrics).
- Hyperparameter sensitivity is underexplored. Please include sensitivity analyses for R and R0 in TPGM/SPGM, and discuss practical selection strategies beyond “set to maximum count.”
- For SPGM, the node-conditional log-partition lacks a closed form. Clarify how it is handled in optimization: exact summation truncation vs. variational bounds vs. importance sampling. Provide runtime and convergence diagnostics across models.
- Theory: you note that sparsistency likely extends from prior work. A brief theorem sketch or conditions (e.g., incoherence, tail assumptions) in the appendix would increase confidence.
- Empirics: consider adding copula-based baselines or other multivariate Poisson constructions (where feasible), and evaluate calibration (e.g., PIT histograms for node conditionals) and goodness-of-fit. Report computational costs and scalability with p and n.
- Case study: clarify preprocessing for miRNA counts and the choice “R = 11,” which seems low relative to typical RNA-seq counts unless heavy normalization was applied. Please explain normalization, filtering, and whether counts were rescaled or capped.

Minor comments and editorial notes
- Define “sparsistent” on first use (sparsity-consistent) and cite a canonical reference.
- Page 3, above 2.1.1: “graphical mode distributions” → “graphical model distributions.”
- Page 5, above 3.1: “Note that the log…” likely intended “Note that although the log…”.
- Provide brief intuition after Theorem 1 about why Winsorization breaks exponential-family compatibility.
- Where possible, standardize notation for domains (e.g., use N0 for {0,1,2,…}) and ensure consistency between W, X, and related symbols.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models with principled, theoretically justified alternatives.
- Clear, original contributions with practical learning procedures and compelling applications.
- Likely to impact multiple application areas dealing with counts.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited exploration of when truncation or Gaussian-like tails are actually problematic in practice; stronger guidance and sensitivity analyses would help.
- Missing theoretical guarantees for structure recovery for the new models in the current draft.
- Some implementation details (SPGM partition function handling) and scalability evidence are sparse.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original, and relevant contribution. With modest clarifications and additional empirical/theoretical support as suggested, it would make a strong NIPS paper.
