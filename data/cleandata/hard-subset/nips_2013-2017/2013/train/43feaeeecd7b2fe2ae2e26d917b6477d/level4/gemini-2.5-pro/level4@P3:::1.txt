### Summary

This paper addresses a significant limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), which, due to normalizability constraints over an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. The authors propose a series of novel graphical models for count data that can capture both positive and negative relationships.

The paper's contributions are developed in a clear, logical progression:
1.  The authors first analyze truncation-based approaches. They demonstrate that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional distribution (Kaiser and Cressie, 1997) does not lead to a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1). They then propose a new, valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) that does permit mixed dependencies, but note its limitations related to the choice of the truncation level, R.
2.  To overcome these limitations, they investigate more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. They prove that for a model with linear sufficient statistics (like the standard PGM), the base measure must scale at least quadratically to ensure normalizability with positive dependencies (Theorem 3).
3.  This insight leads to the proposal of the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure. While this model is flexible, its properties, such as thin "Gaussian-esque" tails, may not be suitable for all count data.
4.  Finally, they introduce the Sub-linear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear while retaining the standard `log(X!)` base measure. This model successfully allows for both positive and negative dependencies without the limitations of the TPGM or the tail-behavior of the QPGM.

The authors validate their proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM) via penalized neighborhood selection for structure learning, demonstrating strong performance on simulated networks and a compelling application to a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and rigorous piece of work. The theoretical contributions are a major strength; for instance, Theorem 1 formally disproves a claim in prior literature, and Theorem 3 provides a clear and principled motivation for developing the QPGM. The progression from one model to the next is well-justified, with the authors being careful to articulate the strengths and weaknesses of each proposed solution. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data effectively support the paper's claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is stated clearly in the introduction, and the subsequent sections logically build upon one another. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the narrative guides the reader through the authors' thought process, making a complex topic accessible.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While PGMs for count data exist, the problem of modeling both positive and negative dependencies in a tractable, high-dimensional setting has been a significant open challenge. This paper presents not just one, but a family of three novel and principled solutions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM). This represents a substantial and creative contribution to the literature on graphical models.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. Count data is ubiquitous in machine learning, from genomics and user ratings to document analysis. Standard tools like Gaussian graphical models are often ill-suited for such data. By providing a flexible and theoretically grounded framework for modeling complex dependencies in multivariate count data, this work offers a valuable new tool for researchers and practitioners. The demonstrated success in identifying biologically relevant hubs in a microRNA network underscores the practical importance of these new models.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The primary suggestion for improvement relates to the framing of the intermediate models. The paper presents the drawbacks of the TPGM and QPGM as major limitations that necessitate the development of the subsequent model. This narrative is effective, but it could be made more nuanced.

*   For the TPGM, the authors cite its bounded domain and behavior near the truncation level *R* as weaknesses. However, for many practical applications where counts rarely exceed a known, reasonable maximum, this model might be perfectly suitable and even preferable for its relative simplicity. A brief discussion of scenarios where the TPGM is a strong model in its own right would strengthen the paper.
*   Similarly, the "Gaussian-esque thin tails" of the QPGM are presented as a drawback motivating the SPGM. It would be valuable to acknowledge that for some datasets, this property might accurately reflect the underlying data-generating process, making it a feature rather than a bug.

Acknowledging that these models form a "toolkit" where different models might be appropriate for different data types would provide a more complete picture of the paper's contribution.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Page 3, first line of Section 2.1.1: "graphical mode distributions" should likely be "graphical model distributions".
*   Page 5, first paragraph of Section 3.1: The sentence beginning "Note that the log partition function A(θ)..." might be clearer as "Note that although the log partition function A(θ)...".
*   The term "sparsistent" appears to be established jargon in the relevant literature, but it is worth noting for broader readership.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and well-known limitation in an important class of graphical models.
*   Introduces three novel, theoretically sound, and well-motivated models for count data.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous, including a disproof of a previous claim in the literature.
*   The paper is very clearly written and logically structured.
*   Experimental validation is convincing, with strong results on both synthetic data and a significant real-world biological application.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper could present a more balanced view of the utility of the intermediate models (TPGM and QPGM), which may be valuable tools in their own right, not merely stepping stones to the final SPGM. This is a minor point and does not detract from the overall contribution.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant and timely contribution to the field. It is well-executed, clearly communicated, and addresses an important problem with novel and effective solutions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
