

The paper proposes a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building such distributions is a well-known challenge, as highlighted by Besag and others over the past three decades (e.g., Arnold et al., *Conditional Specification of Statistical Models*, 1999, which extends conditional specification to distributions like conditionally-specified exponential families).  

The authors achieve two key strengths: (1) constructions that yield marginal count distributions, and (2) relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, significant limitations remain. QPGM exhibits marginally thinner tails than Poisson, while SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions—directly undermining the premise of a conditionally specified model. This raises a fundamental question: how can SPGM be reasonably termed a "Poisson distribution" when QPGM at least modifies only the base measure? While the Poisson is a valuable building block in stochastic processes and latent variable models, it rarely fits empirical count data (e.g., breast cancer read counts); a plot of SPGM’s PMF would clarify its practical relevance. The authors treat \(R\) and \(R_0\) as constants (necessary for exponential family structure), but omit guidance on their selection or practical implementation advice.  

The theoretical contribution—constructing a multivariate count distribution Markov with respect to an MRF—is valid and of interest to NIPS. However, the literature review is inadequate. The work misrepresents [8] (which focuses on building multivariate discrete distributions, including counts, with scalable inference methods), suggesting a misunderstanding of copulas. Similarly, the critique of [11] lacks context: that work pioneers log-linear parameterizations for discrete models without exponential growth in variables, though its treatment of Poisson distributions is unclear without direct reference. Most critically, the paper neglects the vast spatial statistics literature on high-dimensional count analysis, despite its decades-long focus on this problem.  

While the theoretical advance is sound, I remain unconvinced of the practical necessity. A standard structured Gaussian random field with Poisson measurements (a well-established approach) offers greater simplicity and interpretability. Why adopt this model over existing practice? Finally, the claim that "counts of sequencing reads are replacing microarrays" requires a reference newer than [20].  

The paper presents a theoretically interesting construction but suffers from incomplete literature engagement, questionable model justification, and insufficient practical motivation.
