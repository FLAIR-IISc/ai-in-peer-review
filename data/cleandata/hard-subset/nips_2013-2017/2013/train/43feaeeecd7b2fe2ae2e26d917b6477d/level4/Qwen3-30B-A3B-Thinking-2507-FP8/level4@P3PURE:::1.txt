

The paper introduces novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors identify a limitation in Yang et al.'s "default" model, which requires negative correlations to ensure distribution normalization, and propose several refinements: a new truncation method, a quadratic base measure statistic (proven necessary under fixed conditions), and a sub-linear sufficient statistic. Experimental results are presented on both synthetic and real-world datasets.  

This is a well-written paper that offers original and significant contributions to count data modeling. The technical arguments are sound, and the results demonstrate meaningful advances.  

However, I find the "major drawbacks" of the truncation and quadratic models somewhat unconvincing. For truncation, it is plausible that domains with typically low counts would benefit significantly from this approach, making its applicability broader than suggested. Regarding the quadratic model, Gaussian-like thin tails could be appropriate in certain contexts, and the claim that this is inherently unsuitable requires stronger justification.  

Minor points:  
- Page 3, above 2.1.1: "graphical mode distributions" → "graphical model"  
- Page 5, above 3.1: "Note that the log..." should read "Note that although the log..."  
- The term "sparsistent" is unfamiliar but appears established in the literature.  

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with strong technical merit.
