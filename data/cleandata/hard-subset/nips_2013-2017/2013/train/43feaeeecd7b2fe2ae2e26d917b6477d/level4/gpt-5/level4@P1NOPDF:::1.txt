The paper introduces several new models for representing joint distributions over integer count variables. The authors argue that the “default” model arising from Yang et al. is unsatisfactory because it must enforce negative correlations to remain normalizable. They then develop a series of remedies: a truncation approach, a model using a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove is necessary given the rest of the setup), and, finally, a model with a sublinear sufficient statistic. They present experimental results on both synthetic and real data.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with solid contributions to modeling multivariate count data. The work appears original, and the results are significant.

That said, I find the “big negatives” associated with the truncation and quadratic models somewhat unconvincing. For truncation, there are many practical domains in which counts rarely grow large, in which case the truncation would not be problematic and the model could perform well. For the quadratic model, aren’t Gaussian-like thin tails sometimes appropriate? In such cases, the putative drawback may not be a drawback at all.

I am unfamiliar with the term “sparsistent,” although I recognize it has been used in the literature for some time.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper on modeling joint distributions over count variables, with thoughtful methodological advances and reasonable empirical support.
