The paper introduces several new models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors motivate their work by arguing that the default model proposed by Yang et al. is limited, as it can only represent normalized distributions with negative correlations. They address this limitation through a series of refinements, including a novel truncation method, the incorporation of a quadratic base measure statistic (for which they provide a necessity proof given the other components), and the development of a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The effectiveness of these models is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

This is a well-written paper presenting elegant solutions for modeling count data. I assess the work as original and the results as significant.

I found the arguments against the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unpersuasive. Regarding the truncation method, I anticipate that there are application domains where counts are naturally bounded, and this model would perform effectively. Similarly, for the quadratic model, “Gaussian-esque thin tails” may be a reasonable representation in certain scenarios. 
The term “sparsistent” is unfamiliar to me, though I acknowledge its established usage in the literature. 
On page 3, above section 2.1.1, “graphical mode distributions” should be corrected to “model.” On page 5, above section 3.1, the phrase “Note that the log…” would benefit from clarification – perhaps “Note that although the log…” Overall, this is a high-quality paper on the challenging problem of modeling joint distributions over count variables.
