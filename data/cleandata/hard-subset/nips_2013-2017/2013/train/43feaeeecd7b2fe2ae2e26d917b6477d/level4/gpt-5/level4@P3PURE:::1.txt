Summary:
The paper introduces several new models for representing a joint distribution over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the “default” model arising from Yang et al. is unsatisfactory because it can only model negative correlations in order for the distribution to be normalized. They then develop a series of fixes, including a new truncation method, the use of a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove is necessary with everything else fixed), and finally a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The paper presents experimental results on both synthetic and real data.

Strengths:
- Well written and clearly presented.
- Proposes a set of thoughtful solutions for modeling count data.
- Appears original, with results that are significant for the problem of joint modeling of counts.
- Empirical evaluation includes both synthetic and real datasets.

Concerns and questions:
- The “big negatives” that the paper attributes to the truncation and quadratic models are not entirely convincing. For truncation, many practical domains exhibit counts that rarely grow large; in such cases, a truncation-based approach could be entirely adequate and computationally appealing. For the quadratic model, “Gaussian-esque” thin tails may be appropriate in some applications; it would help to qualify the scenarios in which the tail behavior truly becomes a limiting factor versus those where it is acceptable or even desirable.
- I am unfamiliar with the term “sparsistent.” While I recognize that the term has been in use for some time, please provide a brief definition or citation to make the paper self-contained.

Minor comments and edits:
- Page 3, above 2.1.1: “graphical mode distributions” => “graphical model distributions” (or simply “model,” if that is what was intended).
- Page 5, above 3.1: “Note that the log ...” Consider revising to “Note that although the log ...” if you intend to signal a contrast or caveat at this point.

Overall evaluation:
This is a well written, high-quality paper on modeling a joint distribution over count variables. The contributions appear original and significant. Addressing the questions above—particularly by tempering the blanket negatives ascribed to truncation and quadratic models with clearer guidance about when they are or are not appropriate—would further strengthen the paper.
