

The paper presents a novel construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded count variables that respect the Markov property of undirected graphical models. This addresses a long-standing challenge in statistical modeling, as demonstrated by decades of work from Besag and others (e.g., Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models," 1999, which extends these concepts to conditionally specified exponential families).

The authors successfully develop three models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that enable the modeling of both positive and negative conditional dependencies in count data, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The strengths include: (1) the ability to model marginal count distributions while maintaining graphical structure, (2) relatively straightforward estimation procedures via neighborhood selection, and (3) empirical validation on both simulated and real microRNA-sequencing data. However, the paper has notable limitations: QPGM exhibits thinner tails than expected for count data, while SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which undermines the core advantage of conditionally specified models. The authors' claim that SPGM constitutes a "Poisson" variant is questionable, as the base measure and sufficient statistics have been substantially modified. Additionally, the motivation for modeling counts requires clarification—empirical count data rarely follows Poisson distributions, and the paper would benefit from visualizations of the SPGM's probability mass function and discussion of how R and R0 should be selected in practice.

The literature review is notably incomplete. The authors misrepresent [8] (The Nonparanormal), which focuses on building multivariate discrete distributions with tractable inference for arbitrary discrete data (including counts), and overlooks substantial work in spatial statistics where high-dimensional count data analysis has been extensively studied for decades. While the theoretical contribution is valid, the paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from established methods like the sparse precision Gaussian copula model or the standard approach of using structured Gaussian random fields combined with Poisson measurement models, which practitioners have relied on for years. The claim that "counts of sequencing reads...are replacing microarrays" should be supported by more recent references than [20]. Overall, the theoretical contribution is sound and of interest to NIPS, but the paper's practical relevance and literature context require significant strengthening.
