- Addresses building multivariate count distributions that respect undirected graphical model Markov properties.  
- Builds on Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2012) to overcome PGMs' inability to model positive conditional dependencies.  
- Proposes three new models: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  
- Theoretical contributions: identifies fundamental PGM issue (Prop.1) and shows Winsorized Poisson is invalid (Thm.1).  
- Presents a valid truncation-based joint distribution (Thm.2) and derives QPGM and SPGM via sufficient-statistic/base-measure analysis (Prop.2, Thm.4).  
- Shows models can be learned using neighborhood selection and validates them on simulated and breast cancer microRNA data.  
- Strength: strong theoretical rigor solving a known limitation of Poisson graphical models.  
- Strength: three model variants offer clear trade-offs between flexibility, tractability, and theory.  
- Strength: empirical validation on synthetic and real datasets demonstrates practical utility.  
- Weakness: QPGM has Gaussian-like thin tails, potentially mismatching heavy-tailed count data.  
- Weakness: SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining the conditional-specification motivation.  
- Weakness: Calling SPGM a “Poisson variant” is misleading—it alters sufficient statistics rather than the base measure.  
- Weakness: Literature review incomplete/inaccurate (misses copula treatments like Nonparanormal addressing discrete data and spatial count-statistics literature).  
- Weakness: Outdated citation for RNA-seq adoption; needs more recent references (e.g., Marioni et al., 2008).  
- Clarification needed: provide empirical evidence that target count data follow proposed distributions and include a plot of SPGM PMF.  
- Clarification needed: explain practical selection of truncation parameters R and R0.  
- Clarification needed: better articulate connections/comparisons to Gaussian copula and other existing approaches.  
- Significance: moderate—strong theory but unclear whether added complexity yields sufficient practical gains over simpler alternatives.
