This paper presents a method for constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that respect the Markov properties of an undirected graph. While this is a challenging and important problem, and the proposed constructions have some merits, the review identifies significant weaknesses in the motivation, practical justification, and engagement with relevant literature.

**Strengths and Contributions**

The paper addresses the challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions for unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected graphical model. This problem has been studied at length for over three decades (e.g., by Besag, and in textbooks such as Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models," 1999), underscoring its difficulty. The proposed constructions are notable for allowing marginal distributions over counts and leading to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms. Given the difficulty of this problem, this represents a valuable theoretical contribution that is of interest to the NIPS community.

**Weaknesses and Criticisms**

1.  **Model Properties and Motivation:** The proposed models have significant limitations. The QPGM exhibits marginally thinner tails, which may limit its applicability. More critically, the SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which arguably undermines the primary advantage of a conditionally specified model. The connection of the SPGM to the Poisson family is also unclear; whereas the QPGM can be seen as changing the base measure, the justification for calling SPGM a "Poisson" graphical model is not well-established.

    This raises a broader question about motivation. The Poisson distribution itself is often a poor fit for empirical count data, serving more effectively as a building block in larger models (e.g., within stochastic processes or latent variable models). The paper would be strengthened by a more compelling justification for using a Poisson-like framework directly, perhaps by including an empirical analysis (e.g., a simple plot) of the breast cancer data to assess its fit to a Poisson distribution.

2.  **Engagement with Literature:** A major weakness of the paper is its limited engagement with the existing literature. The discussion of related work feels cursory and, in some cases, may misrepresent prior contributions.
    *   The treatment of copulas seems underdeveloped. The description of [8] is particularly concerning, as that work provides a general framework for building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data (including counts) and is supported by a range of MCMC and approximate inference methods. The current paper's presentation does not accurately reflect the scope of this work.
    *   Similarly, the discussion of the pioneering book [11] is questionable, as its central theme is the construction of discrete log-linear models in a way that avoids exponential growth in the number of parameters.
    *   Perhaps the most significant omission is the failure to connect with the vast literature in spatial statistics, which has a long history of modeling high-dimensional count data.

3.  **Practical Justification:** From a practical standpoint, the paper does not convincingly argue why a practitioner should adopt these models over well-established alternatives. A common and effective approach is the latent variable model, such as a structured Gaussian random field with a Poisson observation model. While the authors' models have the advantage of directly enforcing MRF independence in the observable space—a valid theoretical distinction—the paper needs to demonstrate a clear practical advantage to justify the trade-offs involved.

**Questions and Minor Points**

Several points require clarification:
*   It would be very helpful to include a plot of the probability mass function for the SPGM, as its form is not immediately intuitive.
*   The parameters R and R0 appear to be treated as constants to ensure the models are exponential families. How should these be chosen in practice? What guidance can be offered to users?
*   The claim that "counts of sequencing reads ... are replacing microarrays" should be supported by a more recent reference than [20].
