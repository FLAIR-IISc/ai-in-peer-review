

The goal of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data is important, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches (a reservation on this is noted below).  

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. Starting with a proof that previously suggested truncation does not yield a valid model, the authors propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, though technically simple, are novel in that they explicitly present the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation as the means to modify the Poisson distribution. Furthermore, the proposed modifications are well motivated and appealing. That said, the underlying theory follows almost directly from Yang et al., leaving several model suggestions whose practical merit requires empirical evaluation.  

Consequently, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly given the paper’s declared aim to address practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiments demonstrate some potential, the data is specifically designed to favor the proposed models. In real-data experiments, comparisons to copula-based alternatives are not included. Moreover, the reported results are qualitative and anecdotal. Since all methods construct joint distributions, an objective evaluation via log-probability on test data is necessary.  

More critically, the paper fails to address whether the new model is needed, as copula competitors are treated as black boxes. Specifically, even with a Gaussian copula, using a sensible marginal model is preferable to the non-paranormal marginal (which lacks a density and was chosen primarily for asymptotic properties rather than practicality). The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious; I strongly suspect that simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform significantly better. Similarly, the sensible choice of R=11 for the real-data analysis could be translated to a marginal choice for the copula. While an in-depth exploration is not expected, a reasonable baseline comparison is warranted.  

The paper is generally well written. Although I believe all results are correct—particularly given the authors’ correction of an error in prior work—I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. Additionally, Section 3.1 ends with excessive detail, and the bound does not contribute to the Gaussian-like argument. Conversely, Section 3.2, which presents the core method, should be developed more slowly. The figure should not be deferred to the supplementary material but presented and better explained to convey its intuition.  

In summary, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model via a conditional exponential construction, aiming for flexible joint modeling with mixed positive and negative dependencies. While the approach is appealing and theoretically novel, the experimental evaluation is limited and biased. The bottom line is that the paper proposes yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical merit remains unclear.
