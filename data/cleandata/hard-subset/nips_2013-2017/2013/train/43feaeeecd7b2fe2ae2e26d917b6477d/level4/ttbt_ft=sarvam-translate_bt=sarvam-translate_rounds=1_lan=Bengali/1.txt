The paper describes several new models for representing a binomial distribution on a discrete numerical variable. The authors argue that the "default" model derived from Yang et al. is not satisfactory because it can only model negative relationships to normalize the distribution. They then consider some solutions for this, including a new pruning method, using a binomial basis estimation statistic (which they prove is necessary if everything else is fixed), and finally, a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors present experimental results on both synthetic and real data.

It is a well-written paper describing some elegant solutions for representing numerical data. I think it is original, and the results are significant.

I found the "big negative" aspects of the pruning and binomial models to be somewhat unreliable. For pruning, I can imagine that there will be many interesting domains whose counts are usually not very high, and the model will work well. For the binomial model, might a "Gaussian-esque thin tail" not be suitable sometimes?

I don't know what "sparsity" means, but I see that this term has been used for some time.
