The paper addresses the important problem of constructing multivariate distributions for count data and correctly identifies limitations in existing approaches (though with one reservation, discussed below).

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. The authors begin by proving that a previously proposed truncation method is invalid and proceed to offer several alternatives. The Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), while a straightforward correction, is justified by its natural interpretation. The Quadratic and Submodular Poisson Graphical Models (QPGM and SPGM), though technically simple, are novel in their approach of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation to alter the standard Poisson distribution. These modifications are well-motivated and intuitively appealing. However, a key limitation is that the underlying theory largely follows from the work of Yang et al. Consequently, the paper primarily offers several new model proposals whose practical utility remains to be demonstrated through empirical evaluation.

Given the theoretical framing, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, especially as the paper's stated goal is to address the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiments show some promise, the data generation process appears tailored to favor the proposed models. A major omission in the real-data experiments is the lack of comparison to copula-based alternatives. Furthermore, the reported results are presented in a qualitative and anecdotal manner. A more objective evaluation, such as the log-probability of test data, is necessary, given that all compared methods construct joint distributions.

More fundamentally, and touching upon the core motivation for the proposed models, the copula-based competitors are treated as black-box methods. For instance, when using a Gaussian copula, it would be more appropriate to employ a well-chosen marginal model for the count data rather than the non-paranormal transformation, which lacks a density and was selected for its asymptotic properties over practical performance. The resulting performance being nearly identical to Glasso is suspicious; I suspect that a simple baseline using Gaussian kernel density estimates for the marginals would perform significantly better. Similarly, for the real-data experiments, the choice of R=11 could inform a more sensible discrete marginal for the copula model. While an exhaustive exploration is not expected, the inclusion of a more thoughtfully configured and stronger baseline is necessary.

Finally, the paper is generally well written. However, given that the work begins by identifying an error in a previous publication, I strongly recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material for verification, even if the results appear correct. In terms of presentation, the end of Section 3.1 felt overly detailed, and the derived bound did not seem essential to the argument about Gaussian-like behavior. Conversely, Section 3.2, which introduces the core of the proposed method, would benefit from a slower, more detailed exposition. In particular, the key illustrative figure should be moved from the supplement into the main text and used to provide a clearer intuition for the method.

In summary, the authors use a conditional exponential construction to propose alternatives to the Poisson graphical model, enabling flexible joint modeling of count data with mixed positive and negative dependencies. While the proposed approach is appealing and demonstrates some theoretical novelty, the experimental evaluation is limited and potentially biased. As a result, the paper introduces another set of multivariate Poisson-like models whose practical advantages over existing methods remain uncertain.
