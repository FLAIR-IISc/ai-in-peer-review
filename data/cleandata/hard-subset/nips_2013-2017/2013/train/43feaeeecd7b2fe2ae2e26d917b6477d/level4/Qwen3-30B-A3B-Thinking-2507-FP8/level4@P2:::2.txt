

This paper addresses an important gap in graphical models for count data, extending previous work on Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that can only capture negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing approaches, particularly the Winsorized Poisson model proposed by Kaiser and Cressie, which they rigorously prove cannot yield a valid joint distribution. Building on Yang et al.'s framework for graphical models via univariate exponential families, they propose three novel variants: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each designed to capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies.

The theoretical contributions demonstrate significant merit. The authors provide a clear proof that the Winsorized approach fails, then develop TPGM through a natural truncation that maintains exponential family properties, followed by QPGM and SPGM that modify the base measure and sufficient statistics to achieve normalizability with flexible dependencies. The QPGM's quadratic base measure and SPGM's sub-linear sufficient statistics represent novel conceptual shifts from standard Poisson modeling. These modifications are well-motivated and technically sound, representing a meaningful extension of the theoretical framework established by Yang et al. The paper's clarity is generally good, with careful definitions and logical progression through the theoretical development.

The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation, which fails to adequately demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed models. The synthetic experiments are limited to data generated from the proposed models themselves, making it difficult to assess general performance. The comparison to relevant baselines is notably incomplete: the paper omits comparison with copula-based alternatives (e.g., non-paranormal), which are standard approaches for count data. The real-world application to microRNA sequencing data presents results only qualitatively, without quantitative metrics like log-likelihood on held-out data. The comparison to Gaussian graphical models (GGMs) is misleading since the authors use a Gaussian copula with an inappropriate marginal model (one without a density) rather than a sensible count-specific marginal model. This choice artificially limits the performance of copula-based methods, making the reported results questionable.

The experimental section would be strengthened by:
1) Including quantitative log-likelihood comparisons on held-out data
2) Using appropriate marginal models for copula-based baselines
3) Comparing to relevant count-data-specific baselines (e.g., the hierarchical Poisson models mentioned in the introduction)
4) Providing more detailed analysis of the biological significance of the inferred networks

The paper's significance is somewhat diminished by this experimental weakness. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the practical utility of the proposed models remains unclear without convincing empirical validation. The claim that the methods "can capture rich dependence structures" is not sufficiently supported by the presented results.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution addressing a well-identified limitation
- Rigorous proof that previous approach is invalid
- Novel modifications to base measure and sufficient statistics are well-motivated
- Good technical writing with clear mathematical development
- Practical application to microRNA data demonstrates potential utility

Weaknesses:
- Experimental evaluation lacks quantitative metrics and appropriate baselines
- Missing comparison to relevant count-data-specific methods
- Copula-based baselines are evaluated in an unrepresentative manner
- Results are primarily qualitative rather than quantitative
- Insufficient exploration of the practical implications of the proposed models

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of graphical models for count data, but the experimental evaluation does not adequately demonstrate the practical significance of these theoretical advances. The authors correctly identify a limitation in existing Poisson graphical models and propose theoretically sound alternatives, but the lack of rigorous empirical validation prevents a strong conclusion about their practical utility.

The theoretical novelty is evident, particularly in the SPGM's sub-linear sufficient statistics, which provide a novel solution to the normalizability challenge while maintaining heavier tails than the QPGM. However, the significance of these contributions remains somewhat speculative without stronger empirical evidence.

I recommend a borderline acceptance. The theoretical contribution is strong and well-executed, but the experimental section significantly undermines the paper's claim of addressing practical limitations. With stronger empirical validation, this would be a valuable contribution to the field. As currently written, the paper's practical merit is unclear, which is particularly problematic given the authors' stated goal of addressing practical limitations of existing Poisson graphical models.

The paper would benefit from additional experiments comparing to appropriate baselines with proper marginal models, quantitative log-likelihood evaluations, and more detailed analysis of the biological significance of the inferred networks. The supplementary material should include all proofs for completeness, and the SPGM figure should be included in the main text with better explanation of its intuition.
