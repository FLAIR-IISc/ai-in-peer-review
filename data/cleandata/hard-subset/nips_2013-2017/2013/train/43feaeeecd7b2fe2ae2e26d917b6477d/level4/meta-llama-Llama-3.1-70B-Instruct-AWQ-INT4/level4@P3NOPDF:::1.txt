This paper introduces novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the standard model, as proposed by Yang et al., is unsatisfactory due to its limitation in modeling only negative correlations to ensure normalization. To address this issue, they explore a series of modifications, including a new truncation method, a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove to be necessary given the existing framework), and a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors provide experimental results on both synthetic and real data, demonstrating the efficacy of their proposed models.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents innovative solutions for representing count data. The work appears to be original, and the results are significant. The authors' contributions to the field of modeling joint distributions over integer-count variables are substantial.

However, I found some of the criticisms raised against the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unconvincing. For instance, the truncation method may be suitable for domains where counts typically do not exceed a certain threshold, making it a viable option in such cases. Similarly, the quadratic model's "Gaussian-esque thin tails" may be appropriate in certain situations, and it is not entirely clear why this should be considered a limitation.

There is a minor typographical error on page 3, above section 2.1.1, where "graphical mode distributions" should be corrected to "graphical model distributions." Additionally, on page 5, above section 3.1, the sentence "Note that the log..." would be clearer if rephrased as "Note that although the log..."

One term that was unclear to me was "sparsistent," but a quick review of the literature revealed that it has been used in the field for some time.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes significant contributions to the field of modeling joint distributions over count variables. The authors' writing is clear, and their experiments are well-designed and effectively presented. With some minor revisions to address the aforementioned points, this paper has the potential to make a substantial impact in the field.
