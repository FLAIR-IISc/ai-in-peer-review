The paper presents a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building such a distribution is generally a challenging problem, as extensively studied by Besag and others over the past 30 years – including detailed treatment in textbooks like Arnold et al.’s “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models” (1999), which expands on observations made by the authors to encompass other distributions, such as conditionally-specified exponential distributions.

Ultimately, the authors’ proposal succeeds in several important respects. It offers constructions that allow for marginal distributions over counts and leads to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms. However, there are drawbacks: the QPGM exhibits marginally thinner tails, and the SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions – which somewhat undermines the purpose of developing a conditionally specified model. In fact, I question whether the SPGM can even be accurately described as a Poisson distribution (though for the QPGM, one could argue that only the base measure is altered). This raises a broader point about the motivation for modeling counts, as the Poisson distribution itself isn’t always a good fit for empirical data. (To be clear, the Poisson is a valuable building block for many models – components of stochastic processes and within latent variable models, for example – but could you provide a plot of your breast cancer data to illustrate its resemblance to a Poisson distribution?) A plot of the probability mass function for the SPGM would also be useful, as its formulation appears somewhat complex at first glance. I assume R and R0 are treated as constants (otherwise, they wouldn’t belong to the exponential family); how are these values chosen, and what guidance can you offer practitioners?

Despite these questions, constructing a multivariate distribution over counts that respects the independence model of a Markov Random Field is undeniably difficult. I genuinely appreciate the effort invested in this paper, and I believe the results are of theoretical interest to the NIPS community. My main concern lies with what I perceive as an underappreciation of relevant literature. For instance, the authors’ understanding of copulas appears limited. They also seem to misinterpret reference [8], which focuses on building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data (including count data) and provides a range of MCMC and approximate inference methods. This misunderstanding also casts doubt on the validity of their discussion of [11], as the core contribution of that seminal work is demonstrating how to build discrete models with log-linear parameters without exponential growth in the number of variables (though I confess I don't have the book to hand and don’t recall its specific treatment of Poisson distributions). Perhaps the most significant omission is the complete neglect of the extensive literature in spatial statistics, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been conducted for a considerable period.

It's worth noting that several existing approaches (including the sparse precision Gaussian copula model from [8]) don’t directly model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space. That said, the theoretical contribution of this paper remains valid. However, as a practitioner, I’m not yet convinced why I should adopt this model space over the simpler and well-established approach of a structured Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model. 

Finally, while I am not an expert in gene expression analysis, I would appreciate a more recent reference than [20] to support the claim that “counts of sequencing reads … are replacing microarrays.” 

In summary, this paper presents a method for constructing multivariate distributions for counts that is Markov with respect to undirected graphs. As with any nontrivial multivariate construction, it has both advantages and disadvantages. The literature review, however, feels incomplete.
