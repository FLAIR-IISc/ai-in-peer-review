

The goal of constructing multivariate distributions appropriate for count data is important, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches (a reservation on this point is noted below).  

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. Starting with a proof that previously suggested truncation does not yield a valid model, the authors propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while obvious, is warranted due to its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, though technically simple, are novel in that the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation are explicitly positioned as the means to modify the Poisson distribution. The proposed changes are well motivated and appealing. That said, the underlying theory follows almost directly from Yang et al., leaving us with several model suggestions whose practical merit requires evaluation.  

In light of this, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly since the paper's declared goal is to address practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiments show some potential, the data is tailored to the proposed models. In real-data experiments, the comparison to copula-based alternatives is glaringly absent. Moreover, the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal; since all methods involve constructing joint distributions, an objective evaluation via log-probability on test data is essential.  

More critically, the paper fails to address whether a new model is needed, as the copula competitors are treated as overly black-box. Specifically, even when using a Gaussian copula, it is more sensible to employ a reasonable marginal model rather than the one used in the non-paranormal (which has no density and was chosen primarily for asymptotic properties, not practical merits). The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious, and I strongly suspect that simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform significantly better. Similarly, the sensible choice of R=11 for the real-data application could be translated to a marginal choice for the copula. While in-depth exploration is not expected, a reasonable baseline is warranted.  

The paper is generally well written. Though I believe all results are correct—particularly since the authors begin by correcting an error in prior work—I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. Additionally, Section 3.1 ends with an overly detailed argument where the bound does not contribute to the Gaussian-like argument. Conversely, Section 3.2, which forms the core of the proposed method, should be presented more carefully: the figure should be included in the main text with better explanation of its intuition, rather than deferred to supplementary material.  

Overall, the conditional exponential construction presents appealing alternatives to the Poisson graphical model for flexible joint modeling with mixed positive and negative dependencies. While the approach has theoretical novelty, the experimental evaluation is limited and biased, leaving the paper as yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical merit remains unclear.
