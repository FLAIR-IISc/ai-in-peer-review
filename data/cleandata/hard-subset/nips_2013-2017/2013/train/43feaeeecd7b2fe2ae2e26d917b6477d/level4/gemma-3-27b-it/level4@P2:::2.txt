This paper addresses the important problem of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data, correctly identifying limitations of existing approaches. On a theoretical level, the work offers several merits. Beginning with a proof demonstrating that a previously proposed truncation method is invalid, the authors present several alternatives. The Truncated PGM (TPGM) truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its intuitive interpretation. The Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) are novel in their consideration of the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation as means to modify the Poisson distribution. These changes are well-motivated and appealing. However, much of the underlying theory directly extends work by Yang et al. [18, 19], leaving us with several model suggestions whose practical value remains to be established.

Consequently, the experimental evaluation is somewhat disappointing, particularly given the paper’s stated goal of overcoming the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiments show some promise, the data is tailored to favor the proposed models. The real-data analysis lacks comparison to relevant copula-based alternatives. Furthermore, the results are presented primarily qualitatively; a more objective evaluation based on the log-probability of test data is needed.

A critical point, touching on the fundamental necessity of these new models, is the overly “black-box” approach to competitor models. Even using a simple Gaussian copula, employing a more sensible marginal model would be preferable to the non-paranormal model (which lacks a density function) used here, chosen primarily for its asymptotic properties rather than practical merits. The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspect, and I strongly believe that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimation would yield superior results. Similarly, the choice of R=11, while reasonable, could be reinterpreted as a selection for the marginal distribution within a copula framework when applied to the real data. While an in-depth exploration isn’t expected, a more appropriate baseline comparison is warranted.

The paper is generally well-written. Although I believe the results are valid—particularly given the correction of an error in previous work—I suggest moving all proofs to the supplementary material. The end of section 3.1 is overly detailed, and the presented bound does not substantially strengthen the Gaussian-esque argument. Conversely, section 3.2, representing the core of the proposed method, should be expanded. The figure should be included in the main text, and its intuition better explained. 

In summary, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model, based on a conditional exponential construction, with the goal of enabling flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies. The approach is appealing and exhibits some theoretical novelty. However, the limited and somewhat biased experimental evaluation leaves the practical merit of this yet another multivariate Poisson-like model unclear.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in modeling count data.
*   Provides theoretical justification for novel model extensions.
*   Clear motivation for the proposed modifications to the Poisson distribution.
*   Demonstrates some potential in synthetic experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks comparison to strong baselines (particularly copula-based methods).
*   Real-data results are primarily qualitative.
*   Much of the theoretical work builds directly on existing work by Yang et al.
*   The practical benefits of the proposed models over simpler alternatives remain unconvincing.
