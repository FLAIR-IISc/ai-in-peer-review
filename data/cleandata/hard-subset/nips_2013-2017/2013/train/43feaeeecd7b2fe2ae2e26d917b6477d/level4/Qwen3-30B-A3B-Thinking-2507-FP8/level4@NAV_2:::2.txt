

The goal of constructing multivariate distributions appropriate for count data is significant, and the authors correctly identify limitations in existing approaches (with a reservation noted below).  

On the theoretical front, the paper has several strengths. Starting with a proof demonstrating that previously suggested truncation methods do not yield valid models, the authors propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while conceptually straightforward, is well-justified by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM approaches, though technically simple, are novel in their approach to modifying the Poisson distribution by reconfiguring the base measure and sufficient statistics within the exponential family framework. These modifications are well-motivated and appealing. However, the theoretical development largely follows from the work of Yang et al., leaving us with multiple model proposals whose practical value requires empirical validation.  

Regarding the experimental evaluation, it is disappointing, particularly given the paper's stated aim of addressing practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiments suggest some promise, the data generation process is specifically tailored to the proposed models. More critically, the real-data experiments lack comparison to copula-based alternatives, and the reported results are only qualitatively described. Given that all methods involve constructing joint distributions, a more objective evaluation using log-probability of test data would be warranted.  

Most significantly, the paper uses copula competitors in an overly black-box fashion. Even when employing a Gaussian copula, a sensible marginal model should be used rather than the non-paranormal approach (which has no density and was chosen primarily for asymptotic properties rather than practical utility). The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious, and I strongly suspect that simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform better. Similarly, when applying the methods to real data, the choice of R=11 could be translated to a sensible marginal choice for the copula. While an in-depth exploration is not expected, some reasonable baseline comparisons are necessary.  

The paper is generally well-written. While I believe all results are correct, particularly given the authors' correction of an error in prior work, I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material. Additionally, I found the end of section 3.1 to be overly detailed, with the bound not contributing meaningfully to the Gaussian-esque argument. Conversely, I suggest presenting section 3.2 more thoroughly as it represents the core of the proposed methodology, including the figure within the main text rather than deferring it to supplementary material, along with clearer explanation of its intuition.  

In summary, the conditional exponential construction presented by the authors offers alternatives to the Poisson graphical model with the goal of enabling flexible joint modeling with mixed positive and negative dependencies. While the theoretical approach is appealing and contains some novelty, the experimental evaluation is limited and potentially biased, leaving the overall merit of the new model unclear.
