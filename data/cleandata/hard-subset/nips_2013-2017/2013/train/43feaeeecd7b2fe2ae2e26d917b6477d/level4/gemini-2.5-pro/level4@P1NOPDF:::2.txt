The problem of constructing multivariate distributions for count data is an important one, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches (with one reservation noted below).

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. It begins with a valuable contribution: a proof that a previously suggested truncation method for Poisson graphical models does not lead to a valid probability distribution. The authors then propose several well-motivated alternatives. The Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) is a straightforward and natural correction. The Quadrature Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) and Spline Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM), while technically simple, are novel in their mechanism; they modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation to alter the underlying Poisson distribution. These modifications are well-motivated and intuitively appealing. That said, the theoretical development relies heavily on the framework of Yang et al., which means the primary contribution is a set of new model proposals whose practical merits must be carefully evaluated.

In light of this, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, especially since the paper's stated goal is to address the practical limitations of existing Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiments show some potential, the data-generating process appears tailored to favor the proposed models. In the real-data experiments, a comparison to modern copula-based alternatives is glaringly absent. Furthermore, the reported results are presented anecdotally and qualitatively. Since all methods under consideration are generative models, a more objective evaluation using the log-probability of held-out test data is necessary to make a convincing case.

More importantly, the limited comparison to competing methods raises core questions about the necessity of the proposed models. The copula-based competitors are treated in a black-box manner, which weakens the comparison. For instance, when using a Gaussian copula, it would be far more effective to pair it with a sensible marginal model for count data rather than the non-paranormal transformation, which lacks a density and was chosen for its asymptotic properties rather than its practical performance. The reported performance being essentially identical to Glasso is suspicious and suggests a suboptimal baseline; I strongly suspect that a simple Gaussian copula with marginals fit by kernel density estimates would perform much better. Similarly, in the real-data experiments, the choice to discretize the data (R=11) could have informed a more appropriate discrete marginal distribution for a copula-based baseline. While I do not expect an exhaustive exploration, a comparison against reasonable and well-configured baselines is warranted to demonstrate the value of the new models.

Finally, the paper is generally well-written. However, given that it begins by identifying an error in a previous work, it is particularly important for the authors to provide full proofs of their own theoretical claims; I suggest including these in the supplementary material. I also found the end of Section 3.1 to be overly detailed, and the bound presented there did not seem to add much to the core argument. In contrast, Section 3.2, which is the heart of the proposed method, feels rushed. This section should be expanded, and the key illustrative figure should be moved from the supplement into the main paper and used to better explain the model's intuition.

**Summary:**

Based on a conditional exponential construction, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model, aiming to create flexible joint distributions for count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies. The proposed approach is appealing and demonstrates some theoretical novelty. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and not entirely convincing, failing to establish the practical superiority of the new models. As a result, the paper introduces yet another set of multivariate Poisson-like models whose practical utility remains unclear.
