The paper introduces novel models for representing joint distributions of integer-count variables. The authors identify limitations in the default model proposed by Yang et al., specifically its inability to represent positive correlations while maintaining normalization. To address this, they explore several modifications, including a new truncation approach, the incorporation of a quadratic base measure statistic (demonstrated to be a necessary component), and the introduction of a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The effectiveness of these models is evaluated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

This is a clearly written paper presenting compelling solutions for modeling count data. The work appears original, and the results are noteworthy. 

I have some reservations regarding the justification for the truncation and quadratic models. Concerning the truncation method, it seems plausible that many practical applications involve count data with relatively low upper bounds, where this model could perform effectively. Similarly, the assumption of “Gaussian-esque thin tails” for the quadratic model may be appropriate in certain scenarios. 
The term “sparsistent” is unfamiliar, though I acknowledge its established usage within the field. 
On page 3, above section 2.1.1, “graphical mode distributions” should be revised to “model.” On page 5, above section 3.1, the phrase “Note that the log…” would benefit from clarification – perhaps “Note that although the log…” Overall, this is a high-quality paper focused on an important problem in modeling joint distributions over count variables.
