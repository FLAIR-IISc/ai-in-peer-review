Summary
The paper proposes several new models for representing joint distributions over integer-valued count variables. The authors argue that the “default” model derived from Yang et al. is unsatisfactory because normalizability constraints effectively limit it to negative correlations. To address this, they explore: (1) a truncation scheme for the support, (2) a quadratic base measure (which they prove is necessary under fixed remaining components), and (3) a sub-linear sufficient statistic. They evaluate these approaches on synthetic and real datasets.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: the paper identifies a concrete limitation of an established model and proposes principled remedies.
- Methodological contributions: the introduction of a quadratic base measure with a necessity result, and the use of sub-linear sufficient statistics to enlarge the feasible correlation structure, are both interesting and potentially impactful.
- Empirical evaluation: the experiments on synthetic and real data provide evidence that the proposed models can be effective in practice.
- Writing quality: the paper is generally well written and easy to follow.

Concerns and suggestions
- Truncation “big negative”: The critique of truncation as a modeling device is not fully convincing. In many real domains (e.g., word counts per document section, events within fixed time windows, transaction counts), counts rarely become very large. In such settings, truncation can be a pragmatic and accurate approximation. I encourage the authors to:
  - Quantify truncation error or provide bounds/diagnostics indicating when the approximation is acceptable.
  - Include sensitivity analyses showing performance as a function of the truncation threshold and true tail heaviness.
  - Discuss computational trade-offs (normalization and inference cost vs. truncation level).
- Quadratic base measure “big negative”: The argument that a quadratic base measure imposes undesirably thin (Gaussian-like) tails is context-dependent. Thin tails may be appropriate in some applications, especially where extreme counts are rare or measurement processes cap variability. It would strengthen the paper to:
  - Provide empirical evidence comparing tail fit across datasets with varying dispersion.
  - Clarify the conditions of the necessity result: precisely what aspects are held fixed (e.g., exponential-family form, interaction structure, linear vs. non-linear sufficient statistics), and whether the necessity pertains to normalizability, expressivity, or both.
  - Offer intuition for why quadratic terms become necessary, and discuss alternatives that retain heavier tails (e.g., sub-linear or piecewise-linear sufficient statistics, or mixing mechanisms).
- Correlation structure: One of the core motivations is to move beyond the negative-correlation constraint of the default model. It would help to include:
  - A simple analytic example or proposition characterizing when positive correlations are permitted under the sub-linear statistic, and the range of achievable correlations.
  - Empirical diagnostics explicitly measuring learned pairwise correlations versus ground truth, especially on synthetic data designed to include strong positive dependencies.
- Relation to alternative models: The paper would benefit from a clearer comparison to other multivariate count models that can capture positive dependence or flexible dispersion, such as Poisson log-normal models, negative binomial–based constructions, copula-based count models, or COM-Poisson variants. Even if direct graphical structure learning is not comparable, reporting predictive performance or correlation recovery against such baselines would contextualize the proposed approaches.
- Scalability and computation: Please provide more detail on:
  - Computational complexity of learning and inference for the proposed models, especially after introducing sub-linear statistics or truncation.
  - Behavior in higher dimensions (e.g., hundreds to thousands of variables), including time/memory usage and convergence diagnostics.
  - Any approximations (e.g., pseudo-likelihood, contrastive divergence) and their impact on bias/variance in parameter estimates.
- Theoretical clarity: The necessity proof for the quadratic base measure is interesting. Consider adding a brief, intuitive explanation of the mechanism (e.g., how base measure curvature interacts with interaction terms to ensure normalizability), and delineate whether the result is tight or if there are corner cases that admit other solutions.

Minor comments and typos
- Define “sparsistent” at first use. Although the term is known in the literature, a concise definition and conditions for sparsistency (assumptions, sample complexity regime) would improve accessibility.
- Page 3, above 2.1.1: “graphical mode distributions” should be “graphical model distributions” (or simply “graphical model”).
- Page 5, above 3.1: “Note that the log...” likely should read “Note that although the log...”.
- Consider adding brief guidance on choosing between the quadratic and sub-linear approaches in practice (e.g., based on tail behavior diagnostics or domain knowledge).

Overall assessment
This is a well written and original paper that offers meaningful contributions to modeling joint distributions over count variables, including both theoretical insights and practical modeling tools. While I find the stated drawbacks of truncation and quadratic models less compelling as general criticisms—since both can be appropriate depending on the application—the paper’s central message remains strong: the default formulation is too restrictive, and the proposed remedies are valuable additions to the toolbox.

I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the scope and implications of the necessity result, expanding comparisons to alternative multivariate count models, and adding empirical analyses on truncation sensitivity, achievable correlations, and tail fit. These revisions would further solidify the paper’s contributions and practical relevance.
