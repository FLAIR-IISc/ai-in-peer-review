

The paper addresses an important problem: constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data. While the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches, their proposed solutions require careful evaluation.  

The theoretical contributions are well-motivated. The authors establish that prior truncation methods fail to yield valid models, then propose three alternatives. The TPGM truncation, though conceptually straightforward, is justified by its intuitive interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, while technically simple, introduce novel modifications to the Poisson distribution by explicitly specifying the base measure and sufficient statistics within the exponential family framework. However, the theoretical development largely follows from Yang et al., leaving the practical merits of these model variants unproven.  

This leads to a critical weakness: the experimental evaluation fails to substantiate the paper's stated goal of addressing practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. The synthetic experiments, while suggestive, are designed to favor the proposed models. More significantly, the real-data experiments omit comparisons to copula-based alternatives, which are standard for multivariate count modeling. The reported results are qualitative and anecdotal; an objective metric like log-probability on test data would be essential for fair comparison.  

The most concerning issue is the inappropriate use of copula competitors. The authors treat Gaussian copula methods as black boxes, particularly by employing the non-paranormal marginal model—which lacks a density—solely for its asymptotic properties rather than practical utility. A more sensible marginal model should be used instead. The near-identical performance to Glasso is suspicious; even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would likely outperform this setup. Similarly, the choice of R=11 for the marginal copula (a parameter justified in the paper) could be directly translated to a marginal model choice, yet no reasonable baseline is provided. This omission undermines the evaluation’s credibility.  

The paper is generally well-written, and the core results appear sound—particularly given the authors’ correction of an error in prior work. I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material for completeness. Section 3.1’s ending is overly detailed; the bound does not advance the Gaussian-esque argument and could be condensed. Conversely, Section 3.2—which presents the core method—deserves greater emphasis. The key figure should be included in the main text with clearer intuition, rather than deferred to supplementary material.  

In summary, the proposed approach offers a theoretically appealing extension to Poisson graphical models. However, the experimental evaluation is limited, biased, and fails to demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods. Without rigorous validation against relevant baselines, the paper’s contribution remains uncertain. The work advances the theory but does not convincingly address the practical problem it sets out to solve.
