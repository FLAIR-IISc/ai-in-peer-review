This paper presents a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected graphical model. Constructing such distributions is a challenging problem, as extensively studied over the past three decades – notably by Besag and detailed in texts like Arnold et al.’s “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models” (1999), which extends these concepts to conditionally-specified exponential distributions. The authors’ proposal offers several benefits, including constructions that allow for marginal count distributions and relatively straightforward estimation procedures. However, it also presents limitations: the Quadratic Pair Gaussian Model (QPGM) exhibits marginally thinner tails, and the Sparse Pair Gaussian Model (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions, arguably diminishing the purpose of a conditionally specified model. Indeed, it’s unclear whether the SPGM can legitimately be considered a Poisson distribution (though for QPGM, one could argue that only the base measure is modified). This raises questions about the core motivation for modeling counts, given the frequent inadequacy of the Poisson distribution itself when fitting empirical data. While the Poisson serves as a valuable building block in many models – stochastic processes and latent variable models, for example – I would appreciate seeing a plot of the authors’ breast cancer data to assess its similarity to a Poisson distribution. A plot of the SPGM’s probability mass function would also be beneficial, as its formulation appears complex. I assume R and R0 are treated as constants, as otherwise they would not belong to an exponential family; how are these values chosen, and what practical guidance can be offered to users of this model?

Despite these points, constructing a multivariate distribution over counts that respects the independence structure of a Markov Random Field is a difficult task, and I appreciate the authors’ efforts. The results are of theoretical interest to the NIPS community. My primary concern is a seemingly limited engagement with relevant literature. The authors’ understanding of copulas, for example, appears incomplete. Reference [8] is presented inaccurately; its central contribution lies in building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data, including count data, and a range of MCMC and approximate inference methods exist for it. This misinterpretation casts doubt on the discussion of [11], whose pioneering work demonstrates how to construct discrete models with log-linear parameters that scale efficiently with the number of variables (though I concede I do not have the text readily available to confirm specifics regarding Poisson distributions). Perhaps the most significant omission is a complete disregard for the extensive literature in spatial statistics, where high-dimensional count data analysis has a long history. While many existing approaches – including the sparse precision Gaussian copula model in [8] – do not directly model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space, the paper's theoretical contribution remains valid. However, as a practitioner, I am not yet convinced that the complexities of this model justify its adoption over the simpler and well-established approach of combining a structured Gaussian random field with a Poisson measurement model. 

Finally, while the claim that counts of sequencing reads are superseding microarrays is made, a reference more recent than [20] would be appreciated to support this assertion. In summary, this work offers a method for constructing multivariate distributions for counts that are Markov with respect to undirected graphs. As with any nontrivial multivariate construction, it has advantages and disadvantages. The literature review, however, feels incomplete.
