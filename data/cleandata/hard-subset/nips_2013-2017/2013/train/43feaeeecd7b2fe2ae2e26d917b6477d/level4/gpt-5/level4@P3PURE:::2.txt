The goal of constructing multivariate distributions appropriate for count data is important, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches (with a reservation noted below). Based on a conditional exponential construction, the paper proposes alternatives to the Poisson graphical model that aim to allow flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies.

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. Starting with a proof that a previously suggested truncation does not lead to a valid model, the authors offer several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, though obvious, is warranted due to its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, while technically simple, are novel in that the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential-family representation are put on the table as the means to modify the Poisson distribution. These changes are well motivated and appealing. That said, much of the theory follows almost directly from Yang et al., leaving several possible model suggestions whose merit must be evaluated in practice.

In light of the above, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly given the declared goal of fixing practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some potential, the data are geared toward the proposed models. In the real-data experiments, comparison to copula-based alternatives is glaringly missing, and the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. Since all methods involve the construction of joint distributions, a more objective evaluation using the log-probability of held-out test data is needed.

More important—and this goes to the core question of whether a new model is needed—the copula competitors are used in an overly black-box manner. In particular, even when using only a Gaussian copula, it makes more sense to use a sensible marginal model rather than the nonparanormal (which has no density) that was chosen primarily for its asymptotic properties rather than practical merits. The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates for the marginals would do much better. Similarly, for the real-data analysis, the (sensible) choice of using R=11 can be translated into a corresponding choice for the copula marginals. I do not expect an in-depth exploration here, but some reasonable baseline is warranted.

The paper is generally well written. Though I believe all results are correct, particularly since the authors start by correcting an error in another work, I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. I felt that the end of Section 3.1 was overly detailed and that the bound did not contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. By contrast, Section 3.2 should proceed more slowly, as it is the heart of the proposed method; the figure should not be deferred to the supplementary material but presented in the main text with a clearer explanation of its intuition.

In sum, the approach is appealing and has some theoretical novelty. On the downside, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving the bottom line as yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical merit remains unclear.
