The goal of constructing multivariate distributions that are suitable for count data is an important one, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches. A reservation on this is noted below.

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. The authors begin by providing a proof that previously suggested truncation does not lead to a valid model, and then propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, although straightforward, is warranted due to its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, while technically simple, are novel in that they modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation to adjust the Poisson distribution. The changes made are well-motivated and appealing. However, the actual theory involved follows almost directly from the work of Yang et al, leaving us with several possible model suggestions whose merit needs to be evaluated in practice.

In light of the above, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly since the declared goal of the paper is to address the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some potential, the data is tailored towards the models suggested. In the real experiments, the comparison to copula-based alternatives is glaringly missing. Furthermore, the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. Since all methods involve the construction of joint distributions, a more objective evaluation using the log-probability of test data is needed.

More importantly, the fact that the copula competitors are used in an overly black-box manner raises concerns about whether there is a need for the new model. In particular, even when using only a Gaussian copula, it would make more sense to use a sensible marginal model rather than the one used in the non-paranormal (which has no density!) that was mainly chosen due to its asymptotic properties rather than practical merits. The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform much better. Similarly, if applied to the real-data, the (sensible) choice of using R=11 can also be translated to a choice for the marginal of the copula. While I do not expect an in-depth exploration, a reasonable baseline is warranted.

Finally, the paper is generally well-written. Although I believe all results are true, particularly since the authors start with an error in another work, I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. I also felt that the end of section 3.1 was overly detailed, and the bound did not contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. On the other hand, I would take section 3.2 more slowly, as it is the heart of the suggested method. In particular, I would not defer the figure to the supplementary material but rather present it and better explain its intuition.

Based on a conditional exponential construction, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model with the goal of allowing for flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies. On the positive side, the approach suggested is appealing and has some theoretical novelty. On the negative side, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving the bottom line as yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose merit is unclear.
