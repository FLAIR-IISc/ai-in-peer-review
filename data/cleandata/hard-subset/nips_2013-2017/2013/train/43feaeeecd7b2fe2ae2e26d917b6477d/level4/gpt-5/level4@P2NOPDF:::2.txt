This paper tackles the important problem of constructing multivariate models for count data and correctly highlights limitations of existing Poisson graphical models (with one reservation noted below). Conceptually, it has several strengths. The authors begin by proving that a previously proposed truncation does not yield a valid model, and then present alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its intuitive interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM are novel in that they explicitly modify the base measure and sufficient statistics within an exponential-family framework to produce flexible count models. These design choices are well motivated and appealing. However, much of the underlying theory closely follows Yang et al., leaving multiple plausible variants whose relative merits ultimately need to be established empirically.

Given that the stated aim is to overcome practical limitations of Poisson graphical models, the experimental evaluation is underwhelming. The synthetic study shows some promise but appears tailored to favor the proposed models. On real data, a direct comparison to copula-based methods is conspicuously absent. Moreover, the reported results are largely qualitative; since all approaches define joint distributions, a quantitative evaluation using held-out log probability (or similar proper scoring rules) is necessary to support the claims.

More critically, the copula competitors are treated as black boxes. Even when restricting to a Gaussian copula, one should pair it with a sensible marginal model rather than the nonparanormal transform, which lacks a proper density and was chosen primarily for asymptotic properties rather than practical performance. The near-identical results to Glasso are therefore hard to interpret; I suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates for the marginals would perform substantially better. Likewise, on the real-data experiments, the authors’ (reasonable) choice of R=11 could be mirrored by an informed choice of copula marginals. An exhaustive study is not required, but a fair and reasonable copula baseline is.

The paper is generally well written. Given that it starts by correcting an error in prior work, I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material for completeness. The end of Section 3.1 felt overly detailed, and the bound presented there did not seem to strengthen the Gaussian-like argument. In contrast, Section 3.2 merits a slower, more thorough exposition; the key figure should appear in the main text with a clearer explanation of its intuition rather than being deferred to the supplement.

In summary, the paper proposes alternatives to Poisson graphical models via a conditional exponential-family construction, aiming to accommodate both positive and negative dependencies. The approach is attractive and has some conceptual novelty. However, the empirical evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, so the practical benefits of yet another Poisson-like multivariate model remain unclear.
