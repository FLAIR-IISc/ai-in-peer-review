This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions for count data. The authors propose several novel modifications to Poisson graphical models, aiming to overcome the limitations of existing methods, particularly in modeling a mix of positive and negative dependencies. While the theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed models, leaving their overall merit unclear.

**Strengths and Theoretical Contributions**

The paper's primary strengths lie in its theoretical development. The authors begin by usefully proving that a previously proposed truncation method for Poisson graphical models does not result in a valid probability distribution. They then introduce several alternative constructions:

1.  The Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM), while a straightforward extension, is a sensible inclusion due to its natural interpretation.
2.  The Quadratic and Sub-linear Poisson Graphical Models (QPGM and SPGM) are technically simple but represent a novel approach. Their novelty lies in identifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation as the mechanisms for modifying the standard Poisson distribution.

The motivations behind these modifications are well-explained and conceptually appealing. However, it is worth noting that the underlying theoretical framework relies heavily on the work of Yang et al. (2012). Consequently, the paper primarily offers several new model proposals whose practical advantages must be established empirically.

**Weaknesses and Experimental Evaluation**

The main weakness of this work is its experimental evaluation, which is particularly disappointing given the paper's stated goal of addressing the *practical* limitations of existing models.

*   **Synthetic Data:** The synthetic experiments appear designed in a way that may unfairly favor the proposed models, which limits the generalizability of these results.
*   **Real-Data Experiments:** The evaluation on real data is lacking in rigor. A significant omission is the lack of comparison to modern copula-based alternatives, which are a strong class of competitors for this task. Furthermore, the reported results are presented in a qualitative, almost anecdotal, manner. A more objective and quantitative evaluation is necessary. Given that all competing methods construct joint distributions, a standard metric like the log-probability of held-out test data would provide a much-needed and objective comparison of model fit.

**Critique of Baseline Comparisons**

The weak comparison to alternatives raises a fundamental question about the necessity of the proposed models. The copula-based competitors that are included are not used to their full potential and appear to be treated as black-box baselines.

For instance, when using a Gaussian copula, a more sensible choice of marginal distribution would likely yield much stronger results than the rank-based transformation used in the non-paranormal method, which was developed for its asymptotic properties rather than its finite-sample performance. The observation that this baseline performs almost identically to Glasso is suspicious and suggests a suboptimal implementation. I suspect that even a simple Gaussian kernel density estimate for the marginals would provide a more competitive baseline. Similarly, the parameter choice (R=11) for the discrete marginals in the real-data experiments could inform a more appropriate marginal model for a copula-based approach. While an exhaustive search for the best baseline is not expected, a reasonably strong and well-configured set of competitors is essential to validate the paper's claims.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions**

The paper is generally well-written. However, given that the work begins by identifying an error in a previous publication, it would be prudent to include all proofs in the supplementary material to ensure full transparency and reproducibility.

Regarding the structure, Section 3.1 feels overly detailed, and the derivation of the bound does not seem essential to the main argument about creating a Gaussian-like model. Conversely, Section 3.2, which introduces the core methodology, would benefit from a more deliberate pace. In particular, the key illustrative figure should be moved from the supplement to the main text and used to provide a clearer intuition behind the proposed models.
