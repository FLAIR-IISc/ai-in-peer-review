This paper introduces novel models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors identify a limitation in the default model proposed by Yang et al. (2013), specifically its inability to represent positive correlations while maintaining normalization. To address this, they present a series of refinements: a new truncation method, the incorporation of a quadratic base measure statistic (proven necessary under certain conditions), and the introduction of a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The efficacy of these models is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Overall, this is a well-written and clearly presented paper that proposes elegant solutions to a challenging problem in modeling count data. The work appears original, and the reported results are significant. The authors convincingly demonstrate the limitations of existing approaches and offer practical improvements. 

I do have some reservations regarding the justification for the proposed models, particularly the truncation and quadratic variants. While the authors highlight the potential drawbacks of these approaches, I believe their presentation of these as “big negatives” is somewhat overstated. For the truncation model, it seems plausible that many real-world domains involve count variables with limited upper bounds, in which case the truncation approach could perform effectively. Similarly, for the quadratic model, distributions exhibiting characteristics akin to Gaussian distributions with thin tails might be appropriate for certain applications, and the model's ability to capture such patterns shouldn't necessarily be considered a shortcoming. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging potential use cases for these models would be beneficial.

A minor point of clarification: the term “sparsistent” is used throughout the paper, and while I recognize it has been previously established in the literature, a brief definition or pointer to its original introduction would improve accessibility for readers less familiar with the field. 

Finally, there are a few minor editorial suggestions: on page 3, above section 2.1.1, “graphical mode distributions” should be changed to “model.” And on page 5, above section 3.1, the phrase "Note that the log..." is slightly awkward; consider revising to “Note that although the log…” 

Despite these minor points, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of statistical modeling, particularly in the context of count data. I recommend acceptance.
