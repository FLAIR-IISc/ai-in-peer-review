This paper addresses the challenging problem of modeling multivariate count data, extending traditional graphical models to accommodate non-negative integer variables. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models – their inability to represent positive correlations due to normalizability constraints – and propose a series of novel approaches to overcome this. These include a refined truncation method, a Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) leveraging a quadratic base measure, and a Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) with a modified sufficient statistic. The theoretical contributions are complemented by empirical evaluation on both simulated and real (microRNA sequencing) data, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex set of ideas. It effectively builds upon previous work in graphical models, particularly the contributions of Besag and Yang et al., and clearly articulates the motivations behind each proposed modification. The theoretical analysis, including the proof regarding the necessity of a quadratic base measure for positive correlations, is rigorous and valuable. The experimental results provide compelling evidence for the practical utility of the proposed models, particularly the SPGM, and demonstrate performance improvements over existing approaches like Gaussian graphical models and the original Poisson graphical model. The inclusion of a real-world case study strengthens the paper’s impact.

A key strength of this work is its comprehensive approach. The authors don’t settle for a single solution but explore multiple avenues, carefully analyzing the trade-offs of each. The discussion of the limitations of the truncation and quadratic models, while presented as "negatives", highlights a thoughtful consideration of the applicability of each approach – acknowledging that "Gaussian-esque thin tails" might be appropriate in certain scenarios, and that truncation could be useful in domains with bounded count values. 

However, the potential limitations of the QPGM and TPGM, while acknowledged, could be further elaborated upon. While the authors mention the thin tails of the QPGM, a more detailed discussion of scenarios where this might be detrimental, and how the SPGM addresses this, would be beneficial. Similarly, a more in-depth analysis of the performance of the TPGM under different truncation levels (R) would be valuable. The term "sparsistent" is used without definition, and while common in the field, briefly explaining it would enhance clarity for a broader audience.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Originality:** The proposed models represent a significant advancement in the field of graphical models for count data, addressing a fundamental limitation of existing approaches.
*   **Significance:** The ability to model both positive and negative correlations in count data has broad applicability across diverse fields, including genomics, epidemiology, and social sciences.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The discussion of the limitations of the QPGM and TPGM, while present, could be more detailed.
*   The term "sparsistent" is used without definition.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

*   **Page 3, above 2.1.1:** Change "graphical mode distributions" to "graphical model".
*   **Page 5, above 3.1:** Change "Note that the log..." to "Note that although the log..."
*   Expand the discussion on the practical considerations for choosing between the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM based on the characteristics of the data.
*   Define the term “sparsistent” or provide a citation where it is defined.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The strengths of the work significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
