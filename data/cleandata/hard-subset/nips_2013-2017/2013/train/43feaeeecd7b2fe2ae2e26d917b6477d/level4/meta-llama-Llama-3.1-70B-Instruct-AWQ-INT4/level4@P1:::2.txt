The goal of constructing multivariate distributions that are appropriate for count data is an important one, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches. 

The paper has several merits on the theoretical and conceptual front. Starting with a proof that previously suggested truncation does not lead to a valid model, the authors suggest several alternatives. The Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) truncation, though obvious, is warranted due to its natural interpretation. The Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) and Sub-Linear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM), while technically simple, are novel in that the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation are put on the table as the means to modify the Poisson distribution. Further, the changes made are well motivated and appealing. That said, the actual theory involved follows almost directly from the work of Yang et al so that we are left with several possible model suggestions whose merit need to be evaluated in practice.

However, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly since the declared goal of the paper is to fix the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some potential, the data is geared toward the models suggested. In the real experiments, the comparison to copula-based alternatives is glaringly missing. Moreover, the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. Since all methods involve the construction of joint distributions, a more objective log-probability of test data evaluation is needed.

Furthermore, it is unclear whether there is a need for the new model, especially considering the copula competitors used in an overly black-box manner. In particular, even when using only a Gaussian copula, it makes more sense to use some sensible marginal model rather than the one used in the non-paranormal (and that has no density!) which was mainly chosen due to its asymptotic properties rather than practical merits. The essentially identical performance to Glasso is suspicious and it is strongly suspected that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would do much better. Similarly, if applied to the real-data, the (sensible) choice of using R=11 can also be translated to a choice for the marginal of the copula. While an in-depth exploration is not expected, some reasonable baseline is warranted.

The paper is generally well written. Although it is believed that all results are true, particularly since the authors start with an error in another work, it is suggested to include all proofs in the supplementary material. Also, the end of section 3.1 is overly detailed and the bound does not contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. On the other hand, section 3.2 should be explained more slowly as it is the heart of the suggested method. In particular, the figure should not be deferred to the supplementary material but rather presented and better explained in terms of its intuition.

Based on a conditional exponential construction, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model with the goal of allowing for flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies. On the good side, the approach suggested is appealing and has some theoretical novelty. On the bad side, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased so that the bottom line is yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose merit is unclear.

Arguments For Acceptance:
- The paper has several merits on the theoretical and conceptual front.
- The suggested models are novel and well motivated.
- The paper is generally well written.

Arguments Against Acceptance:
- The experimental evaluation is disappointing and limited.
- The comparison to copula-based alternatives is missing.
- The reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal.
- It is unclear whether there is a need for the new model.
- The experimental evaluation is somewhat biased.
