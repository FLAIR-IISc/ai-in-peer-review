The construction of multivariate distributions suitable for count data is a crucial objective, and the authors accurately identify the limitations of existing approaches, with some reservations noted below.

From a theoretical and conceptual standpoint, the paper has several strengths. It begins with a proof demonstrating that previously proposed truncation methods do not yield a valid model, followed by the introduction of alternative approaches. The TPGM truncation, although straightforward, is justified due to its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, while technically simple, offer novelty by placing the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation at the forefront as means to modify the Poisson distribution. The changes made are well-motivated and appealing. However, the underlying theory largely builds upon the work of Yang et al., resulting in multiple model suggestions that require practical evaluation to assess their merit.

In light of the above, the experimental evaluation is underwhelming, particularly given the paper's stated goal of addressing the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows promise, the data is tailored to the proposed models. The real experiments lack a comparison to copula-based alternatives, and the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. A more objective evaluation of log-probability of test data is necessary, as all methods involve constructing joint distributions.

Furthermore, the use of copula competitors in an overly simplistic manner raises concerns about the necessity of the new model. Specifically, even when using a Gaussian copula, it would be more sensible to employ a reasonable marginal model rather than the non-paranormal, which was chosen for its asymptotic properties rather than practical merits. The comparable performance to Glasso is suspicious, and I suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would yield better results. Similarly, applying the same reasoning to real data, the choice of R=11 could be translated to a choice for the marginal of the copula. While an in-depth exploration is not expected, a reasonable baseline is warranted.

Finally, the paper is generally well-written. Although I believe the results are true, I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. I also recommend revising section 3.1 to remove excessive detail and omitting the bound, as it does not contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. In contrast, section 3.2 should be expanded, as it is the core of the proposed method, and the figure should be presented and explained in greater detail.

Overall, the authors present an appealing approach with some theoretical novelty, aiming to allow for flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies using a conditional exponential construction. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, resulting in another multivariate Poisson-like model whose merit remains unclear.
