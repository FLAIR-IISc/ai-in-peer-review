This paper proposes a construction of multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that are Markov with respect to an undirected network. Building such distributions is generally difficult, as studied extensively by Besag and many others over the past 30 years (see, e.g., Arnold et al., “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models,” 1999, which extends some of the authors’ observations to other conditionally specified exponential families).

The proposal succeeds in several relevant ways. It offers constructions that yield proper marginal count distributions and lead to relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, there are notable downsides. QPGM has marginally thinner tails, and SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which undermines the rationale for using a conditionally specified model in the first place. In fact, I do not see how SPGM can be called a Poisson model at all (for QPGM one can at least argue that “only” the base measure is altered). That is acceptable in principle, but it raises the question of what the main motivation is for explicitly modeling counts here, given that the Poisson distribution itself often fits empirical count data poorly. The Poisson is undeniably useful as a building block in stochastic processes and latent variable models, but could you plot the breast cancer data and show whether it resembles a Poisson at all? It would also be very helpful to include a plot of the SPGM probability mass function, which appears somewhat convoluted at first glance.

I assume R and R0 are treated as constants (otherwise these would not define exponential families). How are these constants chosen in practice? What guidance can you offer to practitioners on their selection?

Constructing a multivariate count distribution consistent with an MRF independence structure is a hard and important problem, and I appreciate the effort in this paper. I believe the results are of theoretical interest to NIPS. What detracts from the paper, however, is an overly light engagement with the existing literature. For example, the discussion suggests the authors may not fully appreciate the role of copulas. Reference [8] appears to be misunderstood or at least misrepresented: its central goal is to construct multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data (including counts), and there already exists a range of MCMC and approximate inference methods for such models. This also makes me question whether the discussion of [11] is well grounded: that pioneering book is precisely about constructing discrete models with log-linear parameters in a way that avoids exponential growth in the number of variables (although I do not recall its specific treatment of Poisson models). Perhaps the most significant omission is the near-complete neglect of the spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data have been modeled for a long time.

It is true that several of these alternatives (including the sparse-precision Gaussian copula model in [8]) do not impose MRF-style independence constraints directly in the observable space. So the theoretical contribution of this paper—enforcing the MRF structure for counts—is valid. Nonetheless, from a practitioner’s perspective, I remain unconvinced about the practical benefit of restricting oneself to this model class, relative to using the simpler and well-established structured Gaussian random field plus Poisson measurement model, which has long been a standard approach.

Final comment: I am not an expert in gene expression analysis, but please provide a reference more recent than [20] to support the statement that “counts of sequencing reads … are replacing microarrays.”

In summary: This paper presents a method for constructing multivariate count distributions that are Markov with respect to undirected graphs. As with any nontrivial multivariate construction, there are clear advantages and shortcomings. The literature review feels incomplete and, in places, misleading.
