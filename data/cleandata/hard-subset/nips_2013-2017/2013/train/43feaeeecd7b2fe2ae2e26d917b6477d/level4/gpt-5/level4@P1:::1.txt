Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes new undirected graphical models for multivariate count data that overcome a core limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs): for normalizability, the standard PGM of Besag (1974) and its generalized form in Yang et al. (NIPS 2012; arXiv 2013) only permits non-positive edge parameters, effectively modeling only negative conditional dependencies. The authors first examine truncation-based remedies. They show that using Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals, as suggested by Kaiser and Cressie (1997) and Griffith (2006), cannot yield a consistent joint distribution in the sense of Besag/Yang (Theorem 1). They then introduce a different truncation that preserves the exponential-family form at the node level and prove it admits a valid joint model—the Truncated PGM (TPGM)—that allows both positive and negative dependencies.

Recognizing that truncation has drawbacks, they next modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the univariate Poisson exponential family. They prove that with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability with both positive and negative edges requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses linear sufficient statistics and quadratic base measures, and is normalizable when the induced quadratic form is negative definite or pointwise negative (Proposition 2). To avoid Gaussian-like thin tails, they then define a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) by replacing the linear sufficient statistic with a sublinear, saturating function, while keeping the Poisson base measure; they prove SPGM is normalizable for arbitrary pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). For learning, they adopt ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection for each model. Experiments on simulated data and a TCGA miRNA-seq dataset show improved structure recovery over Gaussian-based approaches and the original PGM.

Quality:
- Theory: The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals (Theorem 1) is important and clarifies a misconception in the spatial stats literature. The necessity of quadratic growth in the base measure with linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) is a clear structural insight. Normalizability conditions for QPGM and SPGM are well articulated; the Gaussian variational bound for QPGM node-conditionals is a practical touch.
- Empirics: The simulations use hub and scale-free graphs and compare against Gaussian, nonparanormal, and SKEPTIC baselines. On real miRNA-seq data, the learned networks highlight plausible biology and recover hubs (e.g., miR-375, miR-10b).
- Suggestions to strengthen: 
  - Provide intuition or a brief sketch for Theorem 1 (e.g., why the indicator-at-R term breaks the exponential-family compatibility conditions), and discuss the R > 3 assumption (what happens for small R?).
  - Clarify how the Gaussian variational bound for QPGM affects estimation: does using the bound in node-wise regression introduce bias or affect sparsistency?
  - Report additional metrics beyond ROC for structure recovery (e.g., precision at a fixed recall, sign accuracy of recovered edges, parameter error) and computational time.
  - Discuss selection of R (TPGM) and R0, R (SPGM): practical guidelines, sensitivity analyses, or cross-validation/stability procedures.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized. The construction from univariate exponential families to joint MRFs is summarized effectively, and the limitations of the standard PGM are explained. A few minor points:
- Define “sparsistent” on first use and cite representative references (e.g., Meinshausen & Bühlmann; Ravikumar et al.). As written, readers unfamiliar with the term may be confused.
- Page 3, above 2.1.1: “graphical mode distributions” → “graphical model distributions.”
- Page 5, above 3.1: “Note that the log...” should be “Note that although the log...”
- Consider adding a short paragraph contrasting your models with other multivariate Poisson constructions (e.g., sums of independent Poissons, Poisson-lognormal, and copula-based approaches), emphasizing tractability and the ability to model both positive and negative conditional dependencies.

Originality:
The paper makes several original contributions:
- A rigorous negative result ruling out Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals for a consistent joint model.
- A new truncation scheme (TPGM) that preserves exponential-family form and yields a valid joint MRF accommodating positive and negative dependencies.
- A structural necessity result on the base measure growth with linear sufficient statistics, and two concrete model classes (QPGM, SPGM) that address the thin-tail issue and allow unrestricted pairwise parameters (SPGM).
These advances go beyond prior NIPS work (e.g., generalized linear model-based PGMs) by enabling positive dependencies for count data while maintaining tractability.

Significance:
Count data are pervasive in genomics, text, and spatial incidence settings. Enabling positive as well as negative conditional dependencies in a principled MRF framework fills a real gap. The theoretical results are likely to influence how researchers design exponential-family graphical models for non-Gaussian data. The empirical evidence, especially on miRNA-seq, suggests these models can yield more biologically plausible networks than Gaussian-based alternatives.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical advances (impossibility, necessity, and normalizability results).
- Practically useful new models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) with tractable estimation strategies.
- Strong motivation and relevance to modern count data applications.
- Empirical gains over standard baselines; convincing real-data case study.

Cons and questions:
- Practical guidance on hyperparameters (R, R0) and sensitivity is limited.
- QPGM estimation relies on a variational bound; impact on guarantees and practice should be clarified.
- Evaluation could include additional baselines tailored to count data (e.g., Poisson log-normal) and more diagnostic metrics.
- Computational complexity and scalability (p, n) are not thoroughly discussed.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-written, original, and significant contribution to modeling multivariate count data. The theoretical insights and new models address a long-standing limitation of PGMs for counts. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the theoretical intuitions for Theorem 1, adding guidance on hyperparameter selection, and expanding the empirical evaluation as suggested above.
