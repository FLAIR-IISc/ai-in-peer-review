The aim of building multivariate distributions suitable for count data is important, and the authors correctly point out shortcomings of current methods (with a caveat noted below).

On the theoretical and conceptual side, the paper has several strengths. Beginning with a proof that a previously proposed truncation does not yield a valid model, the authors offer several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, though technically simple, are novel in treating the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential-family representation as the primary mechanisms for modifying the Poisson distribution. Moreover, the modifications are well motivated and attractive. That said, much of the theory follows quite directly from Yang et al., leaving us with multiple candidate models whose value must ultimately be established empirically.

Given this, the experimental evaluation is underwhelming, particularly since the stated objective is to address the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. The synthetic study shows some promise, but the data are tailored to the proposed models. In the real-data experiments, comparisons to copula-based alternatives are conspicuously absent. In addition, the reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. Since all methods construct joint distributions, a more objective assessment via test log-likelihood is needed.

More importantly—and this bears on whether the new model is necessary—the copula competitors are treated too much as black boxes. Even when restricting to a Gaussian copula, one should employ a sensible marginal model rather than the nonparanormal (which lacks a density) that was chosen mainly for asymptotic reasons rather than practical performance. The nearly identical results to Glasso are questionable, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform better. Likewise, for the real data, the (reasonable) choice of R=11 could be mirrored in the selection of the copula marginals. I do not expect an exhaustive exploration, but a reasonable baseline is warranted.

Overall, the paper is clearly written. While I believe the results are correct—especially given that the paper begins by identifying an error in prior work—I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material. I also found the end of Section 3.1 overly detailed, with the bound adding little to the Gaussian-like argument. Conversely, Section 3.2 should be presented more deliberately, as it is central to the proposed approach; the figure should not be relegated to the supplement but shown in the main text with clearer intuitive explanation.

Based on a conditional exponential construction, the authors propose alternatives to the Poisson graphical model aimed at flexible joint modeling with both positive and negative dependencies. On the positive side, the approach is appealing and exhibits some theoretical novelty. On the negative side, the empirical evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving us with yet another Poisson-like multivariate model whose practical value remains unclear.
