- Introduces three novel multivariate count graphical models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that permit both positive and negative conditional dependencies.  
- Proves that the previously proposed Winsorized Poisson conditionals do not yield a valid joint distribution.  
- Proposes the Truncated PGM (TPGM) as a valid alternative to truncation-based approaches.  
- Develops the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) using a quadratic base measure to ensure normalizability for linear sufficient statistics.  
- Introduces the Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with sub-linear sufficient statistics to allow heavier tails.  
- Provides a penalized neighborhood-selection framework for structure learning and demonstrates performance on simulations and microRNA data.  
- Strength: Tackles an important limitation of standard PGMs and offers principled, original constructions.  
- Strength: Technically sound with clear theoretical contributions, including Theorem 1 and analysis linking normalizability to base-measure choice.  
- Strength: High potential practical impact for high-dimensional count data; estimation fits into familiar neighborhood-selection methods.  
- Weakness: Lacks comparison to latent-variable approaches (e.g., latent Gaussian models with Poisson/Negative Binomial observations).  
- Weakness: Insufficient engagement with spatial statistics literature and only brief discussion of copula-based alternatives.  
- Weakness: QPGM may be inappropriate for overdispersed count data due to Gaussian-like thin tails.  
- Weakness: SPGM sacrifices tractability—node-conditionals lack closed form and require a variational upper bound, complicating inference.  
- Weakness: No clear guidance or principled methods for selecting new hyperparameters (truncation R for TPGM; R and R0 for SPGM).  
- Weakness: Empirical validation is limited—paper assumes Poisson-like suitability for microRNA data without exploratory checks and lacks a PMF figure for SPGM.  
- Summary: A valuable, novel theoretical contribution with strong potential impact; addressing comparisons to alternatives and practical/model-selection issues would strengthen the paper.
