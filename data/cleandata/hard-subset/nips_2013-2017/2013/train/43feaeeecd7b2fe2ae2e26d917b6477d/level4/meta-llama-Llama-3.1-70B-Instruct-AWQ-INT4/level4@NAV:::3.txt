The authors present a novel method for constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected network. This is a challenging problem that has been extensively studied over the past three decades, as exemplified by the work of Besag and others (e.g., Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models", 1999), which explores the conditional specification of statistical models, including conditionally-specified exponential distributions.

The proposed approach has both strengths and weaknesses. On the positive side, it allows for marginal distributions over counts and yields relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, the method also has some drawbacks. Specifically, the QPGM has thinner tails than expected, and the SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which somewhat undermines the purpose of using a conditionally-specified model. Furthermore, it is unclear how the SPGM can be considered a Poisson distribution, as it does not seem to exhibit the characteristic properties of a Poisson distribution.

In fact, the use of the Poisson distribution as a model for counts is questionable, as it often fails to accurately capture empirical data. While the Poisson distribution can be a useful building block for more complex models, such as stochastic processes and latent variable models, it may not be the most suitable choice for modeling counts. A plot of the probability mass function of the SPGM would be helpful in clarifying its properties.

Additionally, the authors' treatment of R and R0 as constants is not clearly explained, and it would be beneficial to provide guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice. The authors' effort to construct a multivariate distribution over counts that obeys the Markov structure of an undirected network is commendable, and the results are of theoretical interest to the NIPS community.

However, the literature review in the paper feels incomplete, and the authors seem to overlook important contributions in the field. For instance, the concept of copulas is not adequately addressed, and the authors' discussion of [8] appears to be inaccurate. Furthermore, the paper neglects to mention the vast spatial statistics literature, which has a long history of analyzing high-dimensional count data.

While the theoretical contribution of this paper is valid, it is unclear why practitioners should prefer this model over simpler alternatives, such as a structured Gaussian random field with a Poisson measurement model, which has been widely used in the field. Finally, a more recent reference than [20] would be appreciated to support the claim that "counts of sequencing reads... are replacing microarrays".
