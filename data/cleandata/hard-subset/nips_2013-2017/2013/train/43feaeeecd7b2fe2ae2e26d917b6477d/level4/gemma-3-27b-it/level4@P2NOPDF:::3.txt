This paper presents a novel construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure defined by an undirected graphical model. Constructing such distributions is a challenging problem, as highlighted by decades of research, including the work of Besag and detailed in texts like Arnold et al.’s (1999) “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models,” which extends these concepts to other distributions.

The authors’ proposal offers both benefits and drawbacks. The resulting distributions allow for tractable marginal distributions over counts and facilitate relatively straightforward estimation procedures. However, the Quasi-Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) exhibits marginally thinner tails than a standard Poisson distribution, and the Sparse Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions – a limitation that somewhat undermines the purpose of a conditionally specified model. Furthermore, the categorization of SPGM as a Poisson distribution is questionable; while QPGM modifies the base measure, the connection to the Poisson distribution is less clear for SPGM. This raises questions about the core motivation for modeling counts, given the frequent inadequacy of the Poisson distribution itself for empirical data. A visualization of the probability mass function for the SPGM would be particularly helpful, as its formulation appears complex. 

Regarding the model parameters R and R0, are these treated as fixed constants? If so, how are they selected, and what practical guidance can the authors offer to users of the model?

Despite these concerns, I acknowledge the inherent difficulty in constructing multivariate count distributions respecting Markov Random Field (MRF) independence constraints and appreciate the effort invested in this work. The theoretical contribution is of interest to the NIPS community. My primary concern lies with the paper’s limited engagement with relevant literature. The discussion of copulas, for example, feels superficial, and the understanding of reference [8] appears incomplete. This work builds multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data, including counts, and provides established inference methods. Consequently, the discussion of reference [11] also seems questionable, as that seminal work addresses efficient parameterization of discrete models to avoid exponential growth in complexity. 

Crucially, the paper overlooks the extensive literature in spatial statistics, which has long addressed the analysis of high-dimensional count data. While some existing approaches, such as the sparse precision Gaussian copula model [8], don’t directly model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space, their omission is significant. 

The theoretical contribution of this paper remains valid, but as a practitioner, I am not convinced that the complexities of this model justify abandoning simpler, well-established approaches like a structured Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model – a standard practice in many fields. 

Finally, the claim that sequencing read counts are “replacing microarrays” requires a more recent citation than [20].

In summary, this paper presents a method for constructing multivariate count distributions with Markovian properties. Like any novel approach, it has both advantages and disadvantages, and the literature review feels incomplete.
