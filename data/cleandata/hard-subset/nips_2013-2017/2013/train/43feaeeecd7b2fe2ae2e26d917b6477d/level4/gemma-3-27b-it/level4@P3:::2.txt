This paper addresses the important problem of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data, identifying limitations of existing approaches. The authors present a series of novel modifications to the Poisson graphical model, aiming to overcome the restriction to negative dependencies inherent in the standard formulation. 

The theoretical and conceptual contributions are significant. Starting with a clear and valuable proof demonstrating the invalidity of a previously proposed truncation approach [20], the authors systematically explore alternative strategies. The Truncated PGM, while conceptually straightforward, is a worthwhile addition due to its natural interpretation. More notably, the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) represent genuinely novel extensions, thoughtfully manipulating the base measure and sufficient statistics within the exponential family framework. The motivations behind these modifications are well-articulated and compelling. However, much of the underlying theory builds directly upon the work of Yang et al. [18, 19], leaving the core merit dependent on practical evaluation.

The experimental evaluation, unfortunately, is a weakness. While the synthetic experiments show some promise, the data generation process appears tailored to favor the proposed models. The real-data analysis suffers from a significant omission: a comparison against relevant copula-based alternatives. The reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. Given the focus on joint distributions, a more objective evaluation, such as comparing log-probabilities on held-out test data, is needed. 

Critically, the comparison to existing methods feels somewhat unfair. The employed copula approaches are treated as “black boxes”, and the choice of marginal distributions seems suboptimal. Specifically, even a Gaussian copula would benefit from a more sensible marginal model than the density-free one used in the Non-paranormal estimator [7], which was primarily chosen for its asymptotic properties. The similarity in performance to Glasso [1, 2] is also suspect, and it's highly probable that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would yield superior results. Similarly, the specific truncation choice of R=11, when applied to real data, could be framed as a selection of the marginal distribution within a copula framework. While an exhaustive exploration isn't expected, a more reasonable baseline comparison is warranted.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. While the authors convincingly demonstrate the correctness of their results – particularly the correction of an error in prior work – I recommend moving all proofs to the supplementary material to improve readability. The end of Section 3.1 feels overly detailed, and the discussed bound does not strongly support the stated "Gaussian-esque" argument. Conversely, Section 3.2, representing the core innovation of the SPGM, deserves more careful presentation. The figure illustrating the sublinear statistic should be included in the main text, and its intuition explained more thoroughly.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Novelty:** Introduces novel modifications to the Poisson graphical model, addressing a key limitation of existing approaches.
*   **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** Built upon existing work in graphical models and exponential families, with clear and rigorous derivations.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The modifications are well-motivated by the desire to capture both positive and negative dependencies in count data.
*   **Error Correction:** Identifies and corrects an error in a previously published work.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed models.
*   **Biased Comparisons:** The lack of comparison to relevant copula-based methods and the suboptimal choices in existing methods raise concerns about the fairness of the evaluation.
*   **Qualitative Results:** The real-data results are largely anecdotal and lack quantitative support.
*   **Overly Detailed Section:** Section 3.1 contains details that do not significantly contribute to the main argument.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in statistical modeling.
*   The correction of an error in a previous publication is valuable.
*   The proposed models offer potential for improved modeling of count data in various applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is weak and does not convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed models.
*   The lack of comparison to relevant alternative methods raises concerns about the validity of the conclusions.
*   The paper’s contribution, while novel, is largely theoretical, and its practical impact remains unclear.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**. While the theoretical contributions are valuable and the proposed models are interesting, the limited and potentially biased experimental evaluation is a significant concern. The authors should address the concerns regarding the evaluation and provide a more compelling demonstration of the practical benefits of their methods before final acceptance. I encourage the authors to submit a revised version addressing these points.
