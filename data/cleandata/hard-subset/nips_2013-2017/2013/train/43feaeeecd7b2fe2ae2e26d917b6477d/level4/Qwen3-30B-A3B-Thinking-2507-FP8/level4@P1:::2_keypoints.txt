- Addresses an important problem: extending graphical models to count data common in genomics and spatial statistics.  
- Correctly identifies limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): they can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability.  
- Contribution 1: Proof that the Winsorized Poisson approach of Kaiser and Cressie cannot yield a valid graphical model (Theorem 1).  
- Contribution 2: Introduces a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that permits both positive and negative dependencies within a bounded domain.  
- Contribution 3: Proposes two novel variants—Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that modify the Poisson base measure and sufficient statistics to allow richer dependencies.  
- Theoretical results are sound and well-motivated; correcting the Winsorized Poisson misconception is particularly valuable.  
- The TPGM is conceptually straightforward with a natural interpretation.  
- QPGM and SPGM are technically simple but novel in modifying the Poisson exponential family structure.  
- Properly situates work within Yang et al. framework, showing how models extend prior work.  
- Experimental evaluation is disappointing and fails to demonstrate practical value of proposed models.  
- Synthetic experiments only show expected performance when data are generated from TPGM/SPGM, not general superiority.  
- Baselines are inappropriate: comparisons use GGMs and non-paranormal methods that assume continuous data, not count data.  
- Real-data analysis (breast cancer microRNA) is problematic: lacks comparisons to suitable count-data competitors and uses qualitative, anecdotal results.  
- Methodological choices (e.g., R=11 for TPGM/SPGM) are unexplained and not justified relative to alternatives.  
- No objective evaluation metrics reported (e.g., held-out log-probability) to quantify performance.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized, though some technical sections are overly detailed and important visualizations are left to the supplement.  
- NIPS criteria: Quality—strong theory but weak experiments; Clarity—good overall; Originality—TPGM modest, QPGM/SPGM novel; Significance—important problem but undermined by weak empirical support.  
- Arguments for acceptance: identifies a key limitation in PGMs and proposes theoretically valid, novel extensions enabling richer dependencies.  
- Arguments against acceptance: inadequate experimental evaluation and missing comparisons to relevant baselines prevent assessment of practical benefits.  
- Recommendation: authors should provide more rigorous comparisons with appropriate count-data baselines and quantitative evaluation to establish practical significance.
