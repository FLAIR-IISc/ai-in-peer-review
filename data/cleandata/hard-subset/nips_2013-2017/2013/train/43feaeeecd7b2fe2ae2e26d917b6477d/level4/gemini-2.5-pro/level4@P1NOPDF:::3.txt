This paper proposes a method for constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that are Markov with respect to an undirected graph. This is a notoriously difficult problem, as explored over the last three decades by Besag and others. Much of this work, including the general theory of conditionally specified models (e.g., Arnold et al., 1999), is highly relevant to the authors' goals.

The proposed construction has clear advantages, namely that it allows for marginal distributions over counts and leads to relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, there are also notable drawbacks. The QPGM model has marginally thinner tails than a standard Poisson, while the SPGM model lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which arguably defeats the purpose of a conditionally specified model. Indeed, it is unclear why SPGM is referred to as a Poisson distribution at all; for QPGM, one could at least argue that only the base measure is altered.

This raises a broader question about the motivation for adhering so closely to the Poisson form, given that it is often a poor fit for empirical count data. While the Poisson is an invaluable building block within hierarchical models or as a component of stochastic processes, its direct application to real-world data is often problematic. For example, does the breast cancer data used in the experiments resemble a Poisson distribution? A plot of the data's marginal histograms would be informative. It would also be very useful to see a plot of the SPGM's probability mass function, as its formulation appears somewhat convoluted. Finally, the paper seems to assume that R and R0 are constants (a necessary condition for the models to be exponential families). How should these be chosen? What guidance can the authors provide to practitioners on setting these values?

Despite these issues, constructing a multivariate count distribution that satisfies the conditional independence structure of an MRF is a challenging task. The effort invested in this paper is commendable, and the results are of theoretical interest to the NIPS community. However, a major weakness is the superficial treatment of the existing literature. For instance, the discussion of [8] appears to be a misinterpretation. The central contribution of that work is to build multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data, including count data, for which a range of MCMC and approximate inference methods are available. The paper's characterization of this work is inaccurate. Similarly, the discussion of [11] is questionable; that book's primary focus is on constructing discrete models with log-linear parameters such that the parameter space does not grow exponentially with the number of variables. Perhaps the most significant omission is the failure to engage with the vast spatial statistics literature, which has a long history of analyzing high-dimensional count data.

Admittedly, many of these alternative approaches (including the sparse precision Gaussian copula model of [8]) do not explicitly model MRF-style conditional independencies in the space of the observed counts. Therefore, the theoretical contribution of this paper remains valid. From a practical standpoint, however, the paper does not make a compelling case for why one should adopt this new model class over the long-established and simpler alternative of a structured Gaussian random field with a Poisson measurement model.

Final comment: A more recent reference than [20] would be appreciated to support the claim that "counts of sequencing reads ... are replacing microarrays."

Summary: The paper proposes a method for constructing multivariate distributions over counts that are Markov with respect to an undirected graph. The construction is non-trivial and, like similar efforts, has both clear advantages and significant shortcomings. The review of related literature is incomplete and in some cases inaccurate.
