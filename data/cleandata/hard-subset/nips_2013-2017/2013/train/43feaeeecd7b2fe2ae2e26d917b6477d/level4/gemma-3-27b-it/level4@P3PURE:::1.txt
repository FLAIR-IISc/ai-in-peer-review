The paper describes a number of novel models for representing a joint distribution over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the default model proposed by Yang et al. is limited, as it can only model negative correlations to ensure normalization. They then consider a series of refinements to address this limitation, including a new truncation method, the utilization of a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove is necessary under certain conditions), and finally, a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors present experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets.

This is a well-written paper detailing effective solutions for modeling count data. I believe the work is original, and the results are significant. 

I found the arguments against the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unconvincing. Regarding the truncation model, I can readily envision domains where counts typically do not reach excessively high values, in which case the model would likely perform well. Similarly, for the quadratic model, could “Gaussian-esque thin tails” be an appropriate characterization in certain scenarios? 

I am unfamiliar with the meaning of “sparsistent,” though I observe that this term has been used previously in the literature. 

On page 3, above section 2.1.1, “graphical mode distributions” should be revised to “model.” On page 5, above section 3.1, the phrase “Note that the log…” would benefit from clarification; does this intend to convey “Note that although the log…”? Overall, this is a high-quality paper on modeling joint distributions over count variables.
