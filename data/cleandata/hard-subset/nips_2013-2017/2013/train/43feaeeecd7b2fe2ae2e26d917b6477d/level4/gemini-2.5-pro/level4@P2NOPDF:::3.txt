This paper presents a novel construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected graphical model. The problem of building such distributions is known to be challenging, a topic explored extensively over the past three decades by researchers like Besag. This history is well-documented in texts such as "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models" by Arnold et al. (1999), which details the difficulties and extends some of the authors' observations to other conditionally specified models, like exponential distributions.

The proposed method has several notable strengths. The constructions successfully yield valid marginal distributions over counts and lead to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms. However, the approach also has significant drawbacks. The first model, the Quasi-Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM), produces marginals with thinner tails than a standard Poisson. The second, the Subtractive-Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM), lacks closed-form conditional distributions. This is a considerable limitation, as the availability of simple conditional forms is often a primary motivation for developing conditionally specified models in the first place. Furthermore, the naming of these models is questionable. While one might argue that QPGM only alters the base measure of a Poisson, it is unclear in what sense SPGM can be considered a Poisson-based distribution.

This raises a broader question about the motivation for modeling counts with distributions that are so strictly tied to the Poisson form. The Poisson distribution itself is rarely a good fit for empirical count data, although it serves as an invaluable building block within more complex models, such as components of stochastic processes or latent variable models. The authors could strengthen their case by providing more empirical justification. For instance, a plot of the breast cancer dataset, comparing it to a fitted Poisson, would be illuminating. To better understand the proposed SPGM, a plot of its probability mass function would also be very helpful, as its formulation appears convoluted on first reading. Additionally, clarification is needed on the parameters R and R0. Are they treated as constants, as they must be for the models to remain in the exponential family? If so, how are they chosen, and what guidance can be offered to practitioners for setting these values?

While the paper's theoretical contributions are appreciated, its most significant weakness is the superficial treatment of the existing literature. The discussion suggests a potential misunderstanding of foundational concepts. For instance, the treatment of copulas is brief and imprecise. The characterization of reference [8] seems to miss its central point, which is to provide a general framework for building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data (including counts), complete with a suite of MCMC and approximate inference methods. Similarly, the discussion of the pioneering book [11] is questionable; a key contribution of that work was demonstrating how to construct discrete log-linear models without the number of parameters growing exponentially with the number of variables.

Perhaps the most glaring omission is the complete absence of any reference to the vast literature in spatial statistics, a field that has been a hub for high-dimensional count data analysis for decades. It is important to acknowledge that many of these alternative approaches (such as the sparse precision Gaussian copula model of [8]) do not enforce Markov-style independence constraints directly in the observable space. Therefore, the theoretical contribution of this paper—constructing a model that does—remains valid and interesting.

From a practical standpoint, however, the paper does not yet convince me why one should adopt this new model space. A practitioner might reasonably ask why they should accept the model's limitations instead of using a standard, well-understood alternative, such as a structured Gaussian random field coupled with a Poisson measurement model.

Finally, a minor point: the claim that "counts of sequencing reads ... are replacing microarrays" is supported by a reference [20] from 2008. Given the pace of development in genomics, a more contemporary reference would be more persuasive.

In summary, this paper tackles a difficult and important problem, and the results are of genuine theoretical interest to the NIPS community. The construction of a multivariate count distribution that is Markov with respect to an undirected graph is a non-trivial achievement. However, the practical applicability of the proposed models is hindered by their limitations, and the paper is significantly weakened by an incomplete and, at times, inaccurate review of relevant prior work.
