This paper presents a method for constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure defined by an undirected graphical model.

Constructing such distributions is a challenging problem, extensively studied for decades (e.g., Besag, and comprehensively covered in texts like Arnold et al., 1999, which broadens these concepts to distributions like conditionally-specified exponential families). 

The proposed approach offers valuable contributions, enabling marginal distributions over counts and facilitating relatively straightforward estimation procedures. However, it also exhibits limitations: the QPGM model demonstrates thinner tails than expected, and the SPGM model lacks closed-form conditional distributions—a significant drawback for conditionally-specified models. Furthermore, the classification of SPGM as a Poisson distribution is questionable (though the modification of the base measure in QPGM is more defensible). This raises questions about the primary motivation for modeling counts, given that the Poisson distribution often provides a poor fit to empirical data. A visual comparison of empirical data (e.g., the breast cancer example) with a Poisson distribution would be insightful. A plot of the SPGM probability mass function would also be helpful, as its formulation appears complex. Clarification regarding the treatment of R and R0—whether they are considered constants—and practical guidance for practitioners on their selection would be beneficial.

Despite these points, constructing multivariate count distributions compatible with Markov Random Field independence assumptions is inherently difficult, and the effort invested in this work is commendable. The theoretical results are of interest to the NIPS community. However, the paper’s engagement with existing literature feels somewhat cursory. The authors’ understanding of copulas appears limited, and their presentation of reference [8] is inaccurate; this work specifically addresses the construction of multivariate distributions for discrete data, including counts, with well-established MCMC and approximate inference methods. This oversight impacts the discussion of [11], which fundamentally concerns building discrete models with log-linear parameters that scale efficiently with the number of variables. Most notably, the paper overlooks the substantial body of work in spatial statistics focused on high-dimensional count data analysis. While some existing approaches (including the sparse precision Gaussian copula model in [8]) do not directly model MRF-style independence in the observed space, the omission is significant.

Ultimately, the paper presents a valid theoretical contribution. However, as a practitioner, the advantages of this model over established methods—such as a structured Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model—are not yet compelling. 

Finally, a more recent citation than [20] supporting the claim regarding the shift from microarrays to sequencing read counts would be appreciated. 

In summary, this work proposes a novel method with trade-offs inherent in any nontrivial multivariate construction. The literature review, however, appears incomplete.
