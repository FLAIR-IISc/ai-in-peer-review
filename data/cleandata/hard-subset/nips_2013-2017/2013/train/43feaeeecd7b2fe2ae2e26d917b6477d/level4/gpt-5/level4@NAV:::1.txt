The paper introduces a set of new models for representing a joint distribution over integer-valued count variables. The authors argue that the default model arising from Yang et al. is inadequate because normalization forces it to capture only negative correlations. They then examine several remedies, including a new truncation scheme, the use of a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove is necessary when the other components are held fixed), and ultimately a sublinear sufficient statistic. The authors provide empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets.

This is a clearly written paper that presents appealing solutions for modeling count data. I believe the work is original, and the results are significant.

I found the “big negatives” attributed to the truncation and quadratic models not entirely convincing. For truncation, there seem to be many settings where counts rarely become large, in which case the model could perform well. For the quadratic model, might “Gaussian-like thin tails” be appropriate in some cases?

I am not familiar with the term “sparsistent,” though I note it has been used in the literature for some time.
