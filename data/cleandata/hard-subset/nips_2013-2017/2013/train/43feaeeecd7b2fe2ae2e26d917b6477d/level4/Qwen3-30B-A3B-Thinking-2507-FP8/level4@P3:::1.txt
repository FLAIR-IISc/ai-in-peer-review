

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for modeling count data. The authors correctly identify that the standard PGM proposed by Yang et al. (2013) can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints, as formally proven in Proposition 1. This limitation severely restricts the applicability of PGMs to real-world count data, which often exhibits both positive and negative dependencies. The authors' central contribution is to develop three novel variants of Poisson graphical models that overcome this constraint: a Truncated PGM (TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models are theoretically grounded, with the authors proving necessary conditions for normalizability while permitting both positive and negative edge weights.

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the NIPS literature. Previous work on graphical models for count data has largely been limited to either univariate Poisson distributions or models that cannot capture positive dependencies (e.g., [20, 21] proposed Winsorized Poisson but failed to establish a valid joint distribution, as the authors correctly show in Theorem 1). The authors' systematic approach to modifying the Poisson distribution—first by truncation, then by altering the base measure and sufficient statistics—represents a significant theoretical advance. Their work builds upon the foundational framework of graphical models via exponential families established by Yang et al. (2013), while addressing a critical limitation in that framework.

The paper is technically strong and well-written. The theoretical derivations are clear and well-justified, with careful proofs of normalizability conditions for each model variant. The experimental results on synthetic data and a breast cancer microRNA network demonstrate practical utility. The TPGM and SPGM models outperform Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM on count data, with SPGM showing particular promise in recovering biologically meaningful network structures. The case study is compelling, identifying known breast cancer-related miRNAs (mir-375 and mir-10b) that were missed by Gaussian graphical models but captured by the authors' methods.

The paper excels in Quality. The theoretical arguments are sound, the experimental design is appropriate, and the authors carefully acknowledge limitations (e.g., TPGM's bounded domain, QPGM's Gaussian-like tails). The paper is also highly Clear in its presentation, with well-structured sections and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The minor grammatical error noted in the draft ("graphical mode distributions" on page 3) should be corrected.

The work demonstrates strong Originality. While previous approaches to count data modeling have been limited (e.g., hierarchical models [16], multivariate Poisson constructions [14, 15]), this paper systematically addresses the core limitation of existing PGMs. The authors correctly note that their three variants are distinct from prior work and provide the first classes of Poisson-like graphical models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies. This represents a meaningful advance over the NIPS 2013-2017 proceedings, where PGMs were previously constrained to negative dependencies.

The Significance of this work is substantial. Count data is increasingly prevalent in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains, and the ability to model both positive and negative dependencies is crucial for accurate representation of real-world phenomena. The case study on microRNA networks demonstrates clear practical impact, identifying biologically relevant network structures that were missed by existing methods. The authors' models provide a principled framework for analyzing high-dimensional count data that practitioners can directly apply.

The main weaknesses are limited to two aspects. First, the TPGM's requirement for a bounded domain may restrict its applicability to certain count data settings (e.g., where counts can be arbitrarily large). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they could more thoroughly discuss scenarios where this might be problematic. Second, the QPGM's "Gaussian-esque thin tails" (a valid observation) may not be desirable for all applications, though the authors note this is a trade-off for normalizability. The paper could more explicitly address when each model variant is most appropriate.

The draft reviewer's concern about the "unconvincing" negatives is understandable but somewhat misplaced. The TPGM's bounded domain is a clear limitation that the authors acknowledge, and it's reasonable to expect that in many applications (e.g., genomic sequencing), counts may frequently approach the truncation level R. Similarly, the QPGM's tail behavior is a trade-off for normalizability that the authors explicitly characterize, and it's not necessarily a flaw but a property of the model that could be appropriate in some contexts.

The paper is well-positioned for acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical contributions are significant, the experimental validation is convincing, and the practical impact is clear. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the literature on graphical models for count data.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear and well-justified contributions, compelling experimental results, significant practical impact, well-written.

Con: Limited applicability of TPGM to bounded count domains, QPGM's tail behavior may not suit all applications, more detailed discussion of model selection trade-offs would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of graphical models. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a meaningful advance over previous work. I recommend acceptance.
