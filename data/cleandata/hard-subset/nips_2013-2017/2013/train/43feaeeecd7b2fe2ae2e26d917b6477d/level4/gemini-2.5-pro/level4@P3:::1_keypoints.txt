- Addresses limitation of Poisson graphical models that only allow negative conditional dependencies by proposing models that capture both positive and negative relationships.
- Shows the previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional (Kaiser & Cressie, 1997) does not yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1).
- Proposes Truncated PGM (TPGM) that permits mixed dependencies but is limited by the choice of truncation level R.
- Proves that for linear sufficient statistics the base measure must scale at least quadratically to allow positive dependencies (Theorem 3).
- Proposes Quadratic PGM (QPGM) using a quadratic base measure; flexible but has thin, Gaussian-like tails that may be inappropriate for some count data.
- Introduces Sub-linear PGM (SPGM) that uses sub-linear sufficient statistics with the standard log(X!) base measure to allow both positive and negative dependencies without TPGM/QPGM drawbacks.
- Validates TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM via penalized neighborhood selection, showing strong performance on simulated networks and a microRNA sequencing dataset.
- Theoretical contributions are rigorous and significant, including a corrective disproof of prior work and principled motivation for new models.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and mathematically precise.
- Work is highly original, presenting a family of three novel, principled solutions to an important open problem.
- Results are significant for many count-data applications and demonstrate practical utility (e.g., biologically relevant microRNA hubs).
- Suggestion: discuss scenarios where TPGM is suitable and possibly preferable when counts are naturally bounded.
- Suggestion: acknowledge that QPGM’s thin tails may be appropriate for some datasets and thus could be a desirable property.
- Recommend framing the three models as a toolkit where different models suit different data types.
- Minor correction: change "graphical mode distributions" to "graphical model distributions" (p.3).
- Minor wording suggestion: clarify the sentence about the log partition function A(θ) in Section 3.1 (p.5).
- Note that the term "sparsistent" may be jargon unfamiliar to broader readers.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a critical limitation, introduces three novel models, provides rigorous theory, clear writing, and convincing experiments.
- Argument against acceptance: needs a more balanced presentation of the intermediate models’ utility (minor issue).
