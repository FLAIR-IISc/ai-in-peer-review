- Proposes Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) and two Poisson-like families (QPGM, SPGM) to allow both positive and negative dependencies in multivariate count MRFs.
- Shows Winsorized truncation of Poisson node-conditionals cannot yield a valid joint MRF (Theorem 1).
- Introduces an exponential-family truncation that yields a valid joint TPGM permitting positive and negative edges (Theorem 2).
- Proposes Quadratic PGM (QPGM): keeps linear sufficient statistics but replaces Poisson base measure with a quadratic term, enabling mixed-sign edges under negative-definiteness-like constraints (Proposition 2).
- Proposes Sublinear PGM (SPGM): retains Poisson base measure with sublinear sufficient statistics, achieving normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4).
- Learns graphs via ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection and demonstrates empirical performance on synthetic and miRNA sequencing data.
- Positions work relative to Besag’s auto-models, exponential-family graphical constructions, truncation in spatial stats, and multivariate Poisson sum-based models.
- Claims superiority over Gaussian and copula-based baselines for count data in experiments.
- Strength: clearly diagnoses why standard PGMs enforce negative interactions and provides constructive alternatives.
- Strength: strong theoretical contributions on non-existence/existence results, necessity of quadratic base measures, and normalizability/variational bounds.
- Strength: practical relevance—method integrates with standard high‑dimensional neighborhood selection workflows.
- Strength: clear presentation of factorization arguments and role of base measures and sufficient statistics.
- Weakness: SPGM node-conditionals lack closed-form partition function; variational approximations need more detail on optimization, quality, and impact on estimation.
- Weakness: need plots comparing univariate pmfs of SPGM/QPGM vs Poisson and marginal histograms for real data to justify tail-behavior choices.
- Weakness: unclear treatment and selection of hyperparameters R and R0 (global vs node-specific, selection method, sensitivity).
- Weakness: terminology—“Poisson-like” is preferred and authors should specify which Poisson properties are retained.
- Weakness: literature review on copula, log-linear, and latent Gaussian spatial models is incomplete/misleading and needs a more balanced comparison.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation should include latent Gaussian Poisson observation models and discrete copula approaches, plus runtime/scalability reporting.
- Weakness: claims about RNA-seq replacing microarrays need updated citations.
- Evaluation (quality): theory is solid but SPGM estimation relies on approximations needing more validation; sparsistency not shown.
- Evaluation (clarity): core constructions clear; literature review and practical guidance need expansion.
- Evaluation (originality): novel framework linking base measure/sufficient-statistic design to normalizability and dependence.
- Evaluation (significance): advances count MRF modeling, but practical impact depends on hyperparameter guidance, tractable SPGM inference, and stronger baselines.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses Poisson MRF limitation with theoretically grounded alternatives and shows improved recovery on counts.
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient empirical comparison to leading alternatives, incomplete literature discussion, and lacking reproducible guidance for SPGM inference/hyperparameters.
- Minor: clarify enforcement of QPGM parameter constraints during estimation, report runtime/iterations/convergence, and consider releasing code.
- Overall: theoretically interesting and potentially impactful but requires better literature accuracy, SPGM practical guidance, and stronger empirical baselines for a compelling acceptance case.
