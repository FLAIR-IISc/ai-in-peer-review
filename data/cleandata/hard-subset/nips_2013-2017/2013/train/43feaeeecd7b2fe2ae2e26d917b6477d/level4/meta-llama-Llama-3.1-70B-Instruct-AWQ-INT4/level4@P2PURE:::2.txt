The goal of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data is an important one, and the authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches, with one notable reservation. 

On the theoretical and conceptual front, the paper has several merits. The authors begin by providing a proof that previously suggested truncation does not lead to a valid model, and then propose several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, although straightforward, is justified due to its natural interpretation. In contrast, the QPGM and SPGM, while technically simple, are novel in that they modify the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation to adapt the Poisson distribution. The changes made are well-motivated and appealing. However, the underlying theory largely follows from the work of Yang et al, resulting in several possible model suggestions that need to be evaluated in practice to assess their merit.

In light of the above, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly given the paper's declared goal of addressing the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows some potential, the data is tailored to favor the proposed models. Furthermore, the real experiments lack a comparison to copula-based alternatives, and the reported results are only qualitatively anecdotal. Since all methods involve constructing joint distributions, a more objective evaluation based on the log-probability of test data is necessary.

More importantly, the use of copula competitors in an overly black-box manner raises concerns about the need for the new model. Specifically, even when using a Gaussian copula, it would be more sensible to employ a reasonable marginal model rather than the non-paranormal model (which lacks a density) used in the paper, mainly chosen for its asymptotic properties rather than practical merits. The similar performance to Glasso is suspicious, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would perform better. Similarly, the choice of using R=11 in the real-data experiment could also be translated to a choice for the marginal of the copula. While I do not expect an in-depth exploration, a reasonable baseline is warranted.

Finally, the paper is generally well-written. Although I believe all results are true, particularly since the authors correct an error in another work, I suggest including all proofs in the supplementary material. I also felt that the end of Section 3.1 was overly detailed, and the bound did not contribute to the Gaussian-esque argument. In contrast, I would elaborate on Section 3.2, as it is the core of the suggested method, and present the figure in the main text to better explain its intuition.

Overall, the authors present an appealing approach with some theoretical novelty, based on a conditional exponential construction, as an alternative to the Poisson graphical model, aiming to allow for flexible joint modeling with a mix of positive and negative dependencies. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat biased, leaving the merit of yet another multivariate Poisson-like model unclear.
