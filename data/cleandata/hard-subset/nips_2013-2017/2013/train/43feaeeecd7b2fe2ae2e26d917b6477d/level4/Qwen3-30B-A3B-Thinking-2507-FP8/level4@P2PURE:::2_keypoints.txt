- Problem importance: Constructing multivariate distributions for count data is important and authors correctly identify limitations of current approaches.  
- Theoretical contribution: Paper proves previously suggested truncation does not yield a valid model and proposes alternatives.  
- Model proposals: TPGM truncation is straightforward and naturally interpreted; QPGM and SPGM are novel by explicitly modifying base measure and sufficient statistics in an exponential-family Poisson framework.  
- Novelty caveat: Underlying theory largely follows directly from Yang et al., leaving practical utility of some model variants uncertain.  
- Experimental weaknesses: Empirical evaluation is disappointing and biased toward the proposed models.  
- Synthetic experiments: Synthetic data are specifically designed to favor the new models, limiting generality of results.  
- Missing baselines: Real-data experiments omit comparisons to copula-based alternatives and sensible marginal choices for copulas.  
- Evaluation metrics: Results are qualitative/anecdotal; objective evaluation (e.g., test-set log-probability) is necessary for joint-distribution methods.  
- Need for model: Paper fails to demonstrate that the new models are necessary versus well-chosen copula approaches or simple alternatives (e.g., Gaussian kernel density marginals).  
- Suspicious findings: Essentially identical performance to Glasso is concerning and suggests better baselines (e.g., kernel density) might outperform reported results.  
- Presentation and proofs: All proofs should be included in the supplement; Section 3.1 is overly detailed while Section 3.2 needs a slower, clearer development and its figure should be in the main text.  
- Overall assessment: The approach is theoretically appealing and novel, but practical merit remains unclear due to limited and biased experiments.
