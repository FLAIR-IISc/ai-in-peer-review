This paper describes several new models for representing the combined distribution on integer-counting variables. The author argues that the "default" model derived from Yang et al. is not satisfactory because it can only model negative correlations to normalize the distribution. They then consider several improvements for this, including a new truncation method, using a quadratic basis measure statistic (which they prove necessary when everything else is fixed), and finally, a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The author presents experimental results on both synthetic and real data.

This is a well-written paper that describes some good solutions for representing computational data. I believe it is original and the results are significant.

I found the "big negative" of truncation and the quadratic model to be somewhat unreliable. For truncation, I could imagine that there would be many interesting domains for which the computation would not usually be too high and the model would work well. For the quadratic model, couldn't a "Gaussian-esque tail" sometimes be suitable?

I don't know what "sparsified" means, but I think the term has been used for some time.
