This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions for count data that adhere to the Markov properties of an undirected graph. The primary motivation is the major limitation of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), which, due to normalizability constraints over an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. The authors propose three novel graphical models to overcome this limitation and capture richer dependence structures.

First, the paper examines truncated versions of the Poisson distribution. It provides a valuable contribution by demonstrating that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional distribution [20] cannot lead to a valid joint distribution. The authors then propose a new Truncated PGM (TPGM) based on a different truncation scheme that does yield a valid MRF. While the TPGM allows for both positive and negative dependencies, its parameters are sensitive to the choice of truncation level, R.

To address the limitations of truncation, the paper introduces two more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. The Quadratic PGM (QPGM) alters the base measure to be quadratic (i.e., C(Z) ∝ Z²), which ensures normalizability for both positive and negative dependencies, analogous to a Gaussian Graphical Model. This, however, results in a distribution with thin, Gaussian-like tails. The Sublinear PGM (SPGM) instead modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, which allows for heavier tails while still guaranteeing normalizability for arbitrary interaction parameters. The paper concludes with experiments on simulated data and a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset to demonstrate the performance of the proposed TPGM and SPGM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a significant and well-known problem. Developing flexible and tractable graphical models for multivariate count data is a valuable goal, as standard PGMs are too restrictive for many real-world applications where variables can be both complementary and competitive.
2.  **Originality:** The proposed solutions, particularly the QPGM and SPGM, are novel constructions. The theoretical insight that a quadratic base measure is necessary for normalizability with linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) is an interesting result. The paper's refutation of the validity of the Winsorized PGM construction is also a noteworthy clarification for the field.
3.  **Completeness:** The paper presents a coherent line of reasoning, starting from the failure of existing approaches and progressively developing more flexible models. Providing structure learning algorithms based on neighborhood selection makes the proposed models applicable in practice.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Literature Review and Context:** The review of related work feels incomplete and, in some cases, imprecise.
    *   **Spatial Statistics:** There is a vast literature in spatial statistics dedicated to modeling high-dimensional, spatially-correlated count data that has been largely overlooked.
    *   **Copula Models:** The characterization of copula-based approaches like [8] seems to misrepresent their purpose. These methods are specifically designed to construct flexible multivariate distributions for various data types, including counts, and represent a major alternative modeling strategy.
    *   **Hierarchical Models:** A standard and powerful approach for this problem is to use a hierarchical model, such as a latent Gaussian random field with a Poisson measurement model (e.g., log(λ) = Z, where Z ~ GGM). This is a common baseline in many application areas and its omission from both the discussion and experimental comparison is a significant weakness. As a practitioner, it is unclear why one should adopt the proposed models over this well-established and flexible alternative.

2.  **Model Justification and Practicality:**
    *   **Motivation for Poisson Form:** The paper's adherence to the Poisson functional form could be better motivated. The univariate Poisson distribution is often a poor fit for empirical count data, which typically exhibits overdispersion. It would strengthen the paper to include an empirical analysis (e.g., a simple plot or goodness-of-fit test) of the marginal counts in the breast cancer data to show whether they resemble a Poisson distribution. This would help justify the choice of `log(X!)` as the base measure.
    *   **SPGM Properties:** The SPGM is an interesting theoretical construct, but it has practical drawbacks. The lack of a closed-form conditional distribution complicates inference and arguably undermines the elegance of the conditionally-specified model framework. Furthermore, its connection to the Poisson distribution is tenuous. A plot of the SPGM's probability mass function for a few parameter settings would be very helpful for building intuition.
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper does not provide guidance on how to select the crucial hyperparameters R and R0 for the TPGM and SPGM. This is a critical detail for any practitioner wishing to use these models.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform Gaussian-based approaches on count data, which is expected. However, the evaluation would be much more convincing if it included comparisons to more appropriate and powerful baselines, particularly the latent Gaussian variable model mentioned above.

**Final Comments:**

This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by developing novel graphical models for count data that permit mixed-sign dependencies. The identification of the flaw in the Winsorized PGM and the development of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are valuable additions to the literature.

However, the paper's significance is diminished by an incomplete review of related work and a lack of comparison to strong, standard baseline models from hierarchical modeling and spatial statistics. The practical justification for the new models over these established alternatives is not yet convincing. I would encourage the authors to broaden their discussion of related work and, most importantly, to compare their methods against a latent Gaussian variable model in their experiments to better situate their contribution and demonstrate its practical utility.
