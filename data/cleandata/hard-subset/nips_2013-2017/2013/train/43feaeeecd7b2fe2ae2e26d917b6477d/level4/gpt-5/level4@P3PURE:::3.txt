This paper proposes a construction of multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that obey the Markov structure of an undirected network. Constructing such models is well known to be difficult, as emphasized by Besag and others over the past several decades (see also Arnold et al., “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models,” 1999, which extends some of the authors’ observations to other conditionally specified distributions such as exponential families). Within this challenging context, the proposal succeeds in several relevant ways.

Strengths:
- The constructions yield multivariate models with count-valued marginals and lead to relatively simple estimation procedures.
- The work offers a theoretically interesting contribution on building multivariate count distributions that respect an MRF independence structure.

Concerns and limitations:
- QPGM appears to have marginally thinner tails. SPGM does not admit closed-form conditional distributions, which to some extent defeats the purpose of pursuing a conditionally specified model. In fact, I do not see how SPGM can be called a Poisson distribution at all (whereas for QPGM one could argue that “only” the base measure is being changed).
- This raises the question of the primary motivation for modeling counts here, given that the Poisson distribution itself often fits empirical data poorly. The Poisson is certainly a valuable building block in stochastic processes and latent variable models, but as an empirical model for observed counts it is frequently inadequate. Could you plot the breast cancer data and show whether it resembles a Poisson at all?
- It would be very helpful to include a plot of the SPGM probability mass function, which feels somewhat convoluted at first sight.
- I assume R and R0 are treated as constants (otherwise these would not be exponential families). How are these constants chosen in practice, and what guidance can you offer practitioners?

Positioning relative to prior work:
- The paper’s treatment of the literature feels light. It almost reads as if the authors do not really know what a copula is. Reference [8] seems not to be correctly understood (or at least is presented in a misleading way): its core objective is to construct multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data (including count data), and there already exists a substantial toolbox of MCMC and approximate inference methods for such models.
- The discussion of [11] is also questionable. The central contribution of that pioneering book is precisely to show how to construct discrete models with log-linear parameters without incurring exponential growth in complexity with the number of variables (I do not recall its specific treatment of Poisson models, but the general point stands).
- Perhaps the most significant omission is the near-total neglect of the spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data have been analyzed for a long time. To be fair, several of these approaches—including the sparse-precision Gaussian copula model of [8]—do not impose MRF-style independence directly in the observable space. Still, this body of work is relevant for comparison.

Practical implications:
- While the theoretical contribution is valid, I remain unconvinced from a practitioner’s perspective about paying the price of staying within this model class instead of using the simpler and long-standard structured Gaussian random field plus Poisson measurement model.

Minor request:
- I am not an expert in gene expression analysis, but please provide a reference newer than [20] to support the claim that “counts of sequencing reads … are replacing microarrays.”

Overall assessment:
- A method for constructing multivariate distributions for counts that is Markov with respect to undirected graphs. Like any nontrivial multivariate construction, it has its advantages and shortcomings. The literature review feels incomplete.
