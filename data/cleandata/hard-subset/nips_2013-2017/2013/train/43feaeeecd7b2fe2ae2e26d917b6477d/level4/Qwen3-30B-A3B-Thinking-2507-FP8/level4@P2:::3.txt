

This paper addresses a significant challenge in statistical modeling: constructing multivariate distributions over count data that respect the Markov properties of undirected graphical models. The authors build upon the foundational work of Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2012) on Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which have a critical limitation—they can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors propose three novel approaches to overcome this limitation: a Truncated PGM (TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM). These models aim to capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies while maintaining the graphical structure.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution. The authors correctly identify the fundamental issue with existing Poisson graphical models (Proposition 1) and rigorously prove that the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie (2008) does not yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1). Their novel truncation method (Theorem 2) successfully produces a valid joint distribution, and their theoretical exploration of base measures and sufficient statistics leads to the QPGM (Proposition 2) and SPGM (Theorem 4) models. The authors demonstrate that these models can be learned using neighborhood selection, and they provide empirical validation on both simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA dataset.

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, it provides a theoretically rigorous solution to a well-known problem in graphical models for count data. Second, the three proposed models offer distinct trade-offs between model flexibility, computational tractability, and theoretical properties. Third, the empirical validation on both synthetic and real data demonstrates practical utility. The authors clearly articulate how their models differ from existing approaches, particularly addressing the limitations of PGMs and Winsorized Poisson variants.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that require attention. The QPGM model's Gaussian-esque thin tail (Section 3.1) is a notable limitation, as it may not capture the heavy-tailed nature of many real count datasets. The SPGM model, while theoretically sound, lacks closed-form conditional distributions (Section 3.2), which contradicts the primary motivation of conditionally specified graphical models. The paper's claim that SPGM is a "Poisson variant" is misleading—SPGM fundamentally modifies the sufficient statistics rather than the base measure, making the connection to Poisson distributions tenuous. The authors should clarify this distinction.

The literature review is incomplete and contains inaccuracies. The paper dismisses [8] (The Nonparanormal) as not modeling count data, yet this work explicitly addresses discrete data through copulas. The authors should engage more thoroughly with the extensive spatial statistics literature on count data modeling (e.g., Cressie, 1993; Griffith, 1987), which has developed high-dimensional approaches for count data for decades. The claim that "counts of sequencing reads are replacing microarrays" (Section 4) requires a more recent reference than [20] (Kaiser and Cressie, 2008), as the shift to RNA-seq has been well-documented in more recent work (e.g., Marioni et al., 2008).

The paper would benefit from several clarifications. First, the authors should provide empirical evidence that count data in their application domains (e.g., microRNA sequencing) actually follow the distributions they propose, rather than making assumptions about the data. A plot of the SPGM's probability mass function would help readers understand its behavior. Second, the authors should explain how to choose the truncation parameters R and R0 in practice, as these are critical to model performance but not addressed in the paper. Third, the connection between these models and existing approaches (e.g., the Gaussian copula model [8]) should be more clearly articulated.

The significance of this work is moderate. While the theoretical contribution is sound and addresses a genuine limitation in the literature, the practical advantages over simpler alternatives (e.g., Gaussian graphical models with Poisson measurement models) are not convincingly demonstrated. The paper's empirical results show improvements over Gaussian models on count data, but the authors do not establish whether the added complexity of their models is justified by significant performance gains.

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically valuable contribution to graphical models for count data by providing three novel approaches that overcome the negative dependency constraint of existing Poisson graphical models. The technical quality is strong, and the empirical validation is appropriate. However, the literature review requires substantial improvement, the connection to Poisson distributions for SPGM is questionable, and the practical motivation could be strengthened with better empirical evidence and clearer guidance for practitioners. The paper would benefit from addressing these issues before publication.

Strengths: Theoretical rigor in addressing a well-defined limitation of Poisson graphical models, three distinct model variants with clear trade-offs, sound empirical validation on both synthetic and real data.

Weaknesses: SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions (contradicting the conditional specification approach), QPGM has Gaussian-esque thin tails, literature review omits key spatial statistics and copula approaches, insufficient justification for count data modeling (no empirical evidence that data follows proposed distributions), unclear guidance for parameter selection (R and R0), outdated reference for RNA-seq adoption.
