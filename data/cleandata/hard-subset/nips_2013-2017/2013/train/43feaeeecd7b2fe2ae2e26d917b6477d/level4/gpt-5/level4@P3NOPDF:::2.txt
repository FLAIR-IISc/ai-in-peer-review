Summary
The paper proposes alternatives to the Poisson graphical model (PGM) for multivariate count data, using a conditional exponential family construction to allow both positive and negative dependencies. It begins by showing that a previously proposed truncation leads to an invalid model, then introduces three variants—TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM—based on truncation and modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution.

Strengths
- The problem is important: constructing flexible multivariate models for count data remains a core challenge.
- The identification and proof that a previously suggested truncation is invalid is valuable and clarifies the literature.
- The proposed variants are conceptually appealing. In particular, explicitly placing the base measure and sufficient statistics “on the table” as the levers for modification is a clean and useful perspective.
- The TPGM is straightforward and naturally interpretable; QPGM and SPGM, while simple, represent a novel and well-motivated way to expand the modeling space.

Concerns and weaknesses
- Incremental theory: Much of the theoretical development appears to follow directly from Yang et al., yielding several plausible model variants without a clear theoretical or empirical rationale for preferring one over another. The paper would benefit from sharper insights into when each variant should dominate (e.g., regimes of sparsity, dispersion, or dependency strength).
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient and, in places, biased toward the proposed models.
  - Synthetic data: The design seems tailored to favor the suggested models; broader scenarios (e.g., varying overdispersion, zero-inflation, mixed-sign dependencies with differing strengths) are needed.
  - Real data: Comparisons to copula-based methods are conspicuously absent, despite being a key class of competitors for flexible dependence modeling with count marginals.
  - Metrics: The reported results are largely qualitative. Because all methods define joint distributions, objective metrics such as held-out log-likelihood (or predictive log-score) should be central. Additional diagnostics like calibration and posterior predictive checks would further strengthen the case.
- Copula baselines are not treated fairly:
  - Using a Gaussian copula with the nonparanormal transformation is problematic here; the nonparanormal lacks a proper density and is typically chosen for asymptotic properties, not for modeling count marginals. This undermines the comparison.
  - The similarity in performance to Glasso is suspicious and likely reflects an ill-specified marginal model rather than a limitation of copula-based dependence.
  - Simple, more sensible alternatives (e.g., Gaussian copula with Poisson-Gamma/negative binomial marginals, discretized lognormal, or kernel-smoothed empirical CDFs) are likely to perform substantially better and should be included.
  - Choices made in your models (e.g., setting R=11) have direct analogs in copula marginal choices and should be translated to ensure parity in model capacity and hyperparameter tuning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide strong, fair baselines:
  - Gaussian copula with appropriate count marginals (negative binomial, zero-inflated variants, discretized continuous marginals), and a kernel-smoothed empirical transform where appropriate.
  - Evaluate both structure recovery (on synthetic data) and predictive performance (held-out log-likelihood) on real data.
- Expand the experimental design:
  - Systematically vary dispersion, zero-inflation, sparsity, and sign/magnitude of dependencies; test robustness to model misspecification.
  - Include ablations that isolate the impact of truncation (TPGM) vs. modified base measure/sufficient statistics (QPGM, SPGM).
  - Report sensitivity to key hyperparameters (including R in SPGM) with cross-validated selection where possible.
  - Add runtime and scalability comparisons, as these are often decisive in practice.
- Clarify when each variant should be preferred, theoretically or empirically. For example, provide conditions under which SPGM is expected to capture stronger negative dependence than QPGM, or when TPGM’s truncation induces undesirable bias.
- Presentation refinements:
  - Include all proofs in the supplementary material for completeness, especially given the correction of prior work.
  - The end of Section 3.1 feels overly detailed; the bound there does not substantially strengthen the Gaussian-like argument and could be tightened.
  - Section 3.2 is central to the contribution and should proceed more slowly; bring the deferred figure into the main text and expand the accompanying intuition.

Overall assessment
This is a timely and appealing line of work with clear conceptual contributions: correcting an error in prior truncation-based modeling, and cleanly framing modifications to the Poisson distribution through the base measure and sufficient statistics. However, the empirical evidence is not yet commensurate with the paper’s stated goal of fixing the practical limitations of PGMs. The missing and/or weak baselines—especially for copula-based approaches with appropriate marginals—and the reliance on largely qualitative evaluations make it difficult to assess the real-world merit of the proposed models. As it stands, the paper reads as proposing several promising Poisson-like alternatives whose advantages remain unproven.

Verdict
Promising theory and a clear modeling perspective, but the current empirical evaluation is too limited and biased to support strong claims. I lean toward a weak reject, with the expectation that a revised version including rigorous predictive evaluations and fair, well-specified copula baselines could make a compelling case.
