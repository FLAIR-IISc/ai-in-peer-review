This paper proposes several new models for representing joint distributions over integer-count variables. The authors begin by arguing that the default model derived from Yang et al. is unsatisfactory, as its normalization constraint permits only the modeling of negative correlations. To address this limitation, they introduce and analyze a series of remedies: a novel truncation method, the use of a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove is a necessary modification, all else being equal), and finally, a model based on a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The paper validates these approaches with experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

This is a well-written paper presenting original and significant contributions to the modeling of count data. The proposed solutions for representing count data are elegant and well-motivated.

However, I found the arguments against the truncation and quadratic models to be somewhat unconvincing. The authors frame these models as having significant drawbacks, but these limitations may not be universally problematic. For instance, in many domains where counts are naturally bounded or do not reach high values, the truncation method could be quite effective. Similarly, the "Gaussian-esque thin tails" of the quadratic model, presented as a negative, might be an appropriate and desirable property for certain types of data.

Minor points and corrections:
*   The term "sparsistent" was unfamiliar. While I see it has been used in prior work, a brief definition or citation would improve the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.
*   Page 3, above section 2.1.1: "graphical mode distributions" should likely be "graphical model distributions".
*   Page 5, above section 3.1: In the sentence beginning "Note that the log...", should this perhaps read "Note that although the log..." for clarity?
