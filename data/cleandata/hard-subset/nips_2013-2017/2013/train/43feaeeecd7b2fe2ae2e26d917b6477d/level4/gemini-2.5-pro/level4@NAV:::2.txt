The authors address the important goal of constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data, and they effectively identify shortcomings in existing approaches, with one caveat I will return to later.

The paper presents several theoretical and conceptual merits. It begins by proving that a previously proposed truncation method results in an invalid model, then proceeds to offer several alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while a straightforward idea, is a warranted inclusion due to its natural interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM models, though not technically complex, are novel in their strategy of modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation to alter the Poisson distribution. Furthermore, these modifications are well-justified and conceptually sound. That said, the theoretical foundation is a direct extension of the work by Yang et al., which means the paper primarily offers several plausible model suggestions whose practical value must be demonstrated empirically.

Given the above, the experimental evaluation is underwhelming, which is particularly concerning as the paper's stated objective is to resolve the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment shows potential, the data generation process appears tailored to favor the proposed models. In the real-data experiments, a comparison to copula-based alternatives is conspicuously absent. Moreover, the reported results are largely qualitative and anecdotal. A more objective evaluation, such as the log-probability of test data, is necessary since all methods involve the construction of joint distributions.

More fundamentally, and touching upon the core question of whether these new models are needed, the copula-based competitors are applied in a superficial, black-box fashion. Specifically, even when using a simple Gaussian copula, a more sensible marginal model should be chosen over the one from the non-paranormal method (which lacks a density), as the latter was selected for its asymptotic theory rather than its practical utility. The performance being nearly identical to Glasso is suspicious, and I strongly suspect that using simple Gaussian kernel density estimates for the marginals would lead to far better results. Similarly, for the real-data experiments, the sensible choice of R=11 could have been used to define a more appropriate marginal for the copula baseline. While I do not expect an exhaustive analysis, a reasonably implemented baseline is required for a meaningful comparison.

Finally, the paper is generally well-written. Although I believe the results are correct, I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material, especially since the paper begins by correcting an error in another publication. I also found the end of Section 3.1 to be overly detailed, with a bound that did not substantially contribute to the main argument. Conversely, Section 3.2, which contains the heart of the proposed method, should be presented more slowly. In particular, the key figure should be moved from the supplement to the main paper and be used to better explain the intuition behind the model.

In summary, the authors leverage a conditional exponential framework to introduce alternatives to the Poisson graphical model, designed to permit flexible modeling of both positive and negative dependencies. On the positive side, the proposed approach is appealing and possesses a degree of theoretical novelty. On the negative side, the experimental validation is limited and appears biased, leaving the reader with yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical merit remains uncertain.
