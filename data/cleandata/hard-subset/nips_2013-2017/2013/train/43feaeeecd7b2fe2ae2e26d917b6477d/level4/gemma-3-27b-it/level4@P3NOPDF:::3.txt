This paper presents a novel construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure defined by an undirected graphical model. Building such distributions is a long-standing challenge, extensively studied for decades – notably by Besag and detailed in texts like Arnold et al.’s “Conditional Specification of Statistical Models” (1999), which generalizes these concepts to other distributions including conditionally-specified exponential families.

The authors’ proposal offers some appealing features. It enables the creation of multivariate distributions over counts and leads to relatively tractable estimation algorithms. However, there are drawbacks. The Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) exhibits marginally thinner tails than a standard Poisson distribution, while the Sparse Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions, a limitation that somewhat undermines the benefits of a conditionally specified model. Indeed, the categorization of SPGM as a “Poisson” distribution is questionable; while QPGM alters the base measure, the modifications in SPGM are more substantial. This raises a broader question regarding the motivation for explicitly modeling counts, given the frequent inadequacy of the Poisson distribution itself when fitting empirical data. A visualization of the probability mass function for SPGM would be particularly helpful, as its formulation appears complex. 

The paper assumes R and R0 are constants, justified by their membership in the exponential family. Clarification regarding the selection of these parameters, and practical guidance for practitioners, would be valuable. 

Despite these points, constructing a multivariate distribution over counts consistent with a Markov Random Field (MRF) is a difficult task, and the authors should be commended for their effort. The theoretical contribution is of interest to the NIPS community. However, the paper's engagement with existing literature feels incomplete. The authors’ understanding of copulas appears superficial. Reference [8] is misrepresented; its core contribution lies in building multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data – including counts – accompanied by established MCMC and approximate inference methods. This casts doubt on the relevance of the discussion surrounding [11], which aims to demonstrate efficient parameterization of discrete models without exponential growth in dimensionality. Critically, the paper omits the extensive spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been a focus for some time. While many of these approaches (like the sparse precision Gaussian copula model in [8]) don’t directly model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space, their omission is notable. 

The theoretical contribution is valid, but as a practitioner, it’s unclear whether the advantages of this model justify the complexity compared to a simpler approach: a structured Gaussian random field combined with a Poisson measurement model, a standard approach for many years. 

Finally, while the claim that sequencing read counts are replacing microarrays is widely accepted, a more recent citation than [20] would strengthen this assertion.

In summary, this work provides a valuable theoretical contribution to the challenging problem of constructing multivariate count distributions. However, the paper would benefit significantly from a more comprehensive literature review, clarification regarding parameter selection, and a stronger justification for its practical advantages over existing methods.
