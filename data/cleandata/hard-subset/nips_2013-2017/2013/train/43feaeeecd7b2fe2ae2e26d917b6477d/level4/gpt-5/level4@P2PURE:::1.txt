The paper introduces several new models for representing joint distributions over integer-valued count variables. The authors argue that the default model derived from Yang et al. is unsatisfactory because normalization requires it to model only negative correlations. They then explore a series of remedies: a new truncation method; a quadratic base-measure statistic (which they prove is necessary when all other components are held fixed); and, finally, a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors present experimental results on both synthetic and real data.

This is a well-written paper that offers solid solutions for modeling count data. I believe the contributions are original, and the results are significant.

However, I am not fully convinced by the claimed “big negatives” of the truncation and quadratic models. For truncation, many practical domains have counts that do not grow too large, in which case the approach could work well. For the quadratic model, “Gaussian-like thin tails” may sometimes be appropriate.

I am not sure what “sparsistent” means, although I recognize that the term has been in use for some time.
