This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of defining multivariate distributions for count data that adhere to the conditional independence structure of an undirected graphical model. Standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) are severely limited because they can only model negative conditional dependencies. The authors propose three novel models to overcome this limitation—the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—each designed to accommodate both positive and negative dependencies while remaining amenable to neighborhood-based estimation methods.

The paper's main strength lies in its direct and thoughtful approach to constructing these models. The problem of building a valid joint distribution over unbounded counts from a set of conditional specifications is notoriously difficult, a topic studied extensively since Besag's foundational work. The authors' effort in this space is commendable, and the resulting constructions are of clear theoretical interest to the NIPS community. A significant practical advantage is that the proposed models lead to relatively simple estimation algorithms based on neighborhood selection, which is well-suited for high-dimensional settings.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed.

First, the proposed models introduce their own set of trade-offs and conceptual questions. The QPGM, with its quadratic base measure, results in Gaussian-like thin tails, which may not be appropriate for many real-world count datasets that exhibit heavy tails. More critically, the SPGM is defined in a way that its conditional distributions lack a closed-form expression. This seems to undermine a key motivation for building a conditionally specified model in the first place, and the authors should elaborate on the practical implications of this for estimation or sampling.

Furthermore, the connection to the Poisson distribution becomes tenuous, particularly for the QPGM and SPGM, where the base measure and sufficient statistics are fundamentally altered. While this is a valid modeling choice, it raises questions about the primary motivation for adhering to a "Poisson-like" framework. Real-world count data, such as the miRNA-sequencing data used in the case study, often exhibit overdispersion and other features not captured by a simple Poisson distribution. It would strengthen the paper to include an analysis (e.g., a plot of the marginal probability mass functions) of the dataset to motivate why these specific functional forms are preferable to other, perhaps more standard, models for counts. Similarly, a plot of the SPGM's PMF would be useful, as its functional form is not immediately intuitive. For practitioners, the paper also needs to provide clearer guidance on selecting the SPGM hyperparameters R and R0.

My main concern, however, is the paper's engagement with the existing literature, which feels incomplete and, in some cases, cursory.
1.  There is a significant omission of the extensive literature in spatial statistics, where modeling high-dimensional count data on a lattice has been a central topic for decades.
2.  The discussion of copula-based methods seems to mischaracterize their utility. For example, the work of Dobra and Lenkoski [8] is designed precisely to build multivariate distributions for various data types, including discrete and count data, via a latent Gaussian structure. The theoretical contribution of the present paper is different (direct modeling of dependencies in the observable space), but the comparison should be made more thoroughly.
3.  The characterization of Lauritzen's work [11] on log-linear models could be more nuanced. A key contribution of that work was to demonstrate how graphical model constraints prevent the number of parameters from growing exponentially, which seems contrary to the authors' claim.

This lack of context leads to a broader unanswered question: why should a practitioner prefer these novel models over well-established hierarchical alternatives, such as a latent Gaussian MRF with a Poisson observation model? Such models are a standard approach for multivariate count data and can capture both complex dependencies (via the latent field) and count-based marginals. A discussion of the relative advantages and disadvantages of the proposed direct modeling approach versus this common latent variable framework would significantly strengthen the paper's impact and clarify its contribution.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically interesting and novel approach to the difficult problem of constructing Markov random fields for count data, successfully addressing a key limitation of existing PGMs. However, the work is weakened by a limited engagement with related literature, particularly from spatial statistics and latent variable modeling, and by a need for clearer justification of the modeling choices compared to established alternatives. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, addressing these points would substantially improve the paper's quality and significance.
