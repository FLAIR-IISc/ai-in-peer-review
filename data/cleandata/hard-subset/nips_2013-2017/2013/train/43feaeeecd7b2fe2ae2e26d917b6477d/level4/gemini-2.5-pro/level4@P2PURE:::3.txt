This paper proposes a method for constructing multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that are Markov with respect to a given undirected graph. The construction of such distributions is a well-known and challenging problem, extensively studied over the past three decades by researchers including Besag. The literature, such as Arnold et al. (1999) on the conditional specification of statistical models, details these difficulties and extends some of the observations made by the authors to other distributions, like the conditionally-specified exponential family.

The proposed method has notable strengths. It provides a valid construction for marginal distributions over counts and leads to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms. However, there are significant drawbacks. The Quadrature Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) results in distributions with marginally thinner tails than might be desired. More critically, the Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM) lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which arguably defeats the primary motivation for developing a conditionally specified model.

The connection to the Poisson distribution is also tenuous for the SPGM; while the QPGM can be seen as altering the base measure, the same justification for SPGM is not immediately apparent. This raises a broader question about motivation. Given that the standard Poisson distribution often fails to capture the characteristics of empirical count data (e.g., the breast cancer data mentioned in the paper), the rationale for building a complex model around a Poisson-like structure needs further justification. To aid understanding, a plot of the SPGM's probability mass function would be very helpful, as its form is not immediately intuitive. Additionally, the paper should clarify the role of the constants R and R0. Are they treated as fixed? If so, practical guidance on how to select them is needed.

Despite these concerns, the paper's core theoretical contribution should be recognized. Constructing a multivariate count distribution that respects the conditional independence structure of an MRF is a difficult task, and the authors' effort is commendable. However, the paper is significantly weakened by an incomplete review of the relevant literature. The discussion of related work seems to miss several key areas and misinterprets some existing methods.

For example, the characterization of copula models, particularly reference [8], appears inaccurate. The point of [8] is precisely to build multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data (including counts), for which various inference methods are available. Similarly, the discussion of [11] is questionable; that pioneering book's central theme is how to construct discrete log-linear models without the number of parameters growing exponentially. Perhaps the most significant omission is the failure to engage with the vast literature on spatial statistics, where the analysis of high-dimensional count data is a well-established field.

To be fair, many of the aforementioned approaches (e.g., the sparse precision Gaussian copula of [8]) do not explicitly enforce MRF independence in the observable space. This distinction highlights the novelty of the paper's theoretical contribution. Nevertheless, from a practical standpoint, the paper does not convincingly argue why a user should adopt these models over a standard and simpler alternative, such as a structured Gaussian random field with a Poisson observation model, which has long been a standard approach.

Finally, while not a major point, the claim that "counts of sequencing reads ... are replacing microarrays" would be strengthened by a more recent citation than [20].
