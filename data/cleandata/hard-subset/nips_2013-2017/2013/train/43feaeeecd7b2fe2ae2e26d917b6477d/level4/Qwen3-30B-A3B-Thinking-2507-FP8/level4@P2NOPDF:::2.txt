

The paper addresses a significant challenge in constructing appropriate multivariate distributions for count data, correctly identifying limitations in existing approaches. The theoretical contributions are largely sound and well-motivated. The authors establish a key theoretical foundation by proving that prior truncation methods fail to yield valid models, then propose three alternatives: TPGM (truncated Poisson graphical model), QPGM (quadratic Poisson graphical model), and SPGM (spherical Poisson graphical model). While TPGM offers intuitive appeal through its natural interpretation, the novelty of QPGM and SPGM lies in explicitly treating the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential family representation as design parameters to modify the Poisson distribution. However, the theoretical development closely follows Yang et al., leaving the practical merit of these models unproven without empirical validation.

The experimental evaluation critically undermines the paper's stated goal of addressing practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. The synthetic experiments, while demonstrating conceptual potential, are overly tailored to the proposed models. More severely, the real-data analysis omits a necessary comparison to copula-based alternatives, which are standard for multivariate count data. The reported results rely on qualitative anecdotes rather than objective metrics; a log-probability evaluation on test data would be essential for fair comparison. Crucially, the copula competitors (particularly non-paranormal) are treated as black boxes. Even with a Gaussian copula, using an inappropriate marginal model (e.g., one lacking a density, chosen solely for asymptotic properties) invalidates the comparison. The identical performance to Glasso is suspicious; simple Gaussian kernel density estimates for marginals would likely outperform the current copula implementation. Similarly, the choice of R=11 in real-data experiments could be reasonably translated to the copula marginal, yet no such baseline is provided.

The paper is generally well-written, and all mathematical claims appear valid—particularly given the authors' correction of an error in prior work. Including all proofs in the supplementary material is recommended for completeness. Section 3.1 contains overly detailed exposition that does not advance the Gaussian-esque argument, while Section 3.2 (the core methodological contribution) requires clearer presentation. The key figure should be included in the main text with enhanced intuitive explanation rather than deferred to supplementary material.

In summary, the theoretical framework presents an appealing, novel approach to flexible joint modeling of counts with mixed dependencies. However, the experimental shortcomings—particularly the absence of rigorous, fair comparisons and reliance on non-standard baselines—prevent meaningful assessment of the model's practical value. The paper falls short of demonstrating that these alternatives offer tangible advantages over existing methods. The core contribution remains a technically sound but empirically unverified extension of Poisson graphical models, whose necessity for real-world applications remains unproven. The work would benefit from stronger empirical validation against sensible baselines before its practical relevance can be established.
