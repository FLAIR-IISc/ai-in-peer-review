This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of constructing multivariate distributions for count data that adhere to the Markov properties of an undirected graphical model. Standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) are limited to modeling only negative conditional dependencies. The authors propose three novel models—the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—to overcome this limitation and allow for both positive and negative dependencies. This line of inquiry builds upon a long history of research on conditionally specified models, notably the work of Besag, which aims to define valid joint distributions from a set of conditional distributions.

The primary strength of this work is its direct and novel approach to creating flexible graphical models for count data. Constructing valid multivariate distributions for unbounded discrete variables is a notoriously difficult task. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by proposing constructions that are normalizable and permit a rich dependency structure. Furthermore, the proposed models lend themselves to relatively straightforward estimation procedures based on penalized neighborhood selection, which is a significant practical advantage.

Despite the theoretical merits, there are several areas where the paper could be improved.

First, the proposed models come with significant trade-offs that warrant further discussion. The QPGM, with its quadratic base measure, results in thin, Gaussian-like tails, which may not be appropriate for many real-world count datasets that exhibit overdispersion. The SPGM is more flexible, but it does not have a closed-form conditional distribution, which seems to detract from the primary appeal of a conditionally specified model. Its connection to the Poisson family is also tenuous; the authors could clarify why this particular functional form for the sufficient statistic was chosen over others. A plot of the SPGM's probability mass function would be very helpful for building intuition.

Second, the practical application of these models needs more clarification. For the TPGM and SPGM, the parameters R and R0 are treated as fixed constants. How should a practitioner select these values? This is a crucial detail for the usability of the models. Additionally, the motivation for using a Poisson-based model could be strengthened with some exploratory analysis. For instance, a simple plot showing the marginal distribution of the breast cancer miRNA data would help justify whether a Poisson-like model is a reasonable starting point.

Third, the review of the existing literature feels incomplete and could be strengthened to better position the paper's contributions.
- The discussion of copula models, such as Dobra and Lenkoski [8], could be more nuanced. Copulas provide a very general and widely used framework for constructing multivariate distributions for arbitrary marginals (including count data), and a more thorough comparison is warranted.
- The reference to log-linear models [11] seems to mischaracterize their purpose; a key goal of that work was precisely to develop parameterizations for discrete models that do not grow exponentially with the number of variables.
- A significant omission is the vast literature on spatial statistics, where modeling high-dimensional count data has been a central topic for decades.
- Crucially, the paper does not compare its approach to a very common and powerful baseline: a latent Gaussian graphical model with a Poisson observation model (i.e., a hierarchical model). For a practitioner, it is not yet clear why one should adopt the proposed models, with their specific limitations, over this well-established alternative.

Finally, a minor point: the claim that sequencing reads "are replacing microarrays" would be more convincing with a citation more recent than Marioni et al. [22] from 2008.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically interesting and novel set of solutions to the difficult problem of modeling multivariate count data with MRFs. The contribution is valid and of potential interest to the NIPS community. However, its significance is currently limited by a narrow view of related literature, a lack of comparison to strong, practical baselines, and insufficient discussion of the inherent limitations and practical application of the proposed models.
