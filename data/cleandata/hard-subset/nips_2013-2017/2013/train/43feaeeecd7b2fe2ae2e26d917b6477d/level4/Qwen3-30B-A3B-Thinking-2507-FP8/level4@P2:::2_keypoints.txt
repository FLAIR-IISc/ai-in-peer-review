- Addresses an important gap: extends Poisson graphical models to capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies.
- Rigorously proves the Winsorized Poisson model (Kaiser & Cressie) cannot yield a valid joint distribution.
- Proposes three new models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) built on exponential-family graphical model framework.
- TPGM uses truncation to maintain exponential-family properties and normalizability.
- QPGM introduces a quadratic base measure to allow flexible dependencies.
- SPGM uses sub-linear sufficient statistics as a novel solution to normalizability while retaining heavier tails.
- Theoretical contributions are technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly presented.
- Mathematical development and writing are generally clear with careful definitions and proofs.
- Experimental evaluation is weak: synthetic experiments use data generated only from the proposed models.
- Missing key baselines: no comparison to copula-based alternatives (e.g., non-paranormal) or count-specific hierarchical Poisson models.
- Quantitative metrics are lacking: no log-likelihood comparisons on held-out data for real or synthetic sets.
- Copula-based baselines were evaluated with inappropriate marginal models, biasing comparisons.
- Real-world microRNA results are presented qualitatively without quantitative validation or biological analysis.
- Paper’s practical significance is unclear due to insufficient empirical validation despite strong theory.
- Recommendations for improvement: add held-out log-likelihoods, use appropriate copula marginals, include count-specific baselines, and deepen biological analysis.
- Additional suggestions: include all proofs in supplementary material and move/explain the SPGM figure into the main text.
- Overall judgment: technically strong and novel but experimentally under-validated; recommend borderline acceptance.
