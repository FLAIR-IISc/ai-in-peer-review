- Proposes methods (QPGM and SPGM) for constructing multivariate count distributions that are Markov wrt a given undirected graph.  
- Strength: provides a valid marginal construction for counts and leads to relatively straightforward estimation algorithms.  
- Weakness: QPGM yields marginally thinner tails than may be desirable for empirical count data.  
- Major weakness: SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, undermining the motivation for a conditionally specified model.  
- The connection between SPGM and the Poisson distribution is unclear, unlike QPGM where the base measure interpretation is more evident.  
- Motivation is weak: the paper does not justify why building complex Poisson-like models is better than simpler alternatives when Poisson often fails on real data.  
- Suggestion: include a plot of the SPGM probability mass function to aid intuition about its form.  
- Clarify treatment and selection of constants R and R0 (are they fixed, and how should they be chosen in practice?).  
- Core theoretical contribution is important and novel: constructing multivariate count distributions that enforce MRF conditional independence is difficult and commendable.  
- The related-work review is incomplete and misinterprets key prior work, weakening the paper.  
- Specific misinterpretations: reference [8] does aim to build multivariate discrete distributions (including counts) and offers inference methods; reference [11] focuses on constructing discrete log-linear models without exponential parameter growth.  
- Significant omission: failure to engage with extensive spatial statistics literature on high-dimensional count data.  
- Practical relevance questioned: paper does not convincingly argue why users should prefer these models over standard alternatives like structured Gaussian random fields with Poisson observations.  
- Minor: update the citation supporting the claim that sequencing reads are replacing microarrays with a more recent source.
