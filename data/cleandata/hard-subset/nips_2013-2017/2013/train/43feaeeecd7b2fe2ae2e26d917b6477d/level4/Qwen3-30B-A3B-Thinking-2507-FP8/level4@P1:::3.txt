

This paper proposes novel graphical models for count-valued data, addressing a significant limitation in existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that can only capture negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors introduce three new approaches: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), all of which can model both positive and negative dependencies. The work builds upon Besag's pairwise Poisson model [17] and Yang et al.'s generalization [18, 19], while addressing a major gap in the literature regarding count data.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it corrects a fundamental error in Kaiser and Cressie's Winsorized Poisson approach [20], proving that their construction cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1). Second, it introduces TPGM, a valid truncated model that relaxes the negative edge-weight constraint but still has bounded domain limitations. Third, it proposes QPGM with quadratic base measure (C(Z) = Z²) that permits both positive and negative edge weights while maintaining normalizability (Proposition 2), and SPGM with sublinear sufficient statistics that achieves even broader parameter flexibility (Theorem 4). The authors demonstrate how these models can be learned via neighborhood selection and validate them on simulated networks and a breast cancer microRNA dataset.

The paper excels in technical quality. The theoretical arguments are sound, with clear proofs of normalizability conditions and model properties. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real data (TCGA microRNA data) demonstrates practical utility. The connection to existing work is well-established, with careful comparison to Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM. The clarity of the mathematical presentation is generally good, though some technical details (e.g., the choice of R and R₀ for SPGM) could be better motivated.

The primary strengths of this work include its theoretical rigor in addressing the normalizability constraints of Poisson graphical models, the introduction of three novel model classes that collectively enable richer dependency structures, and the practical demonstration of their utility on real count data. The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of graphical models for count data, which is a well-motivated problem given the increasing prevalence of count-valued data in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. First, the SPGM's conditional distributions do not have closed forms (as noted in Proposition 3), which somewhat undermines the conditional specification framework that underpins the entire approach. While the paper correctly states that the log-partition function can be upper-bounded by the Poisson log-partition, the lack of closed-form conditional distributions makes the model less practical for direct inference. Second, the motivation for modeling counts with Poisson-like distributions needs strengthening. The paper claims counts "are increasingly ubiquitous," but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why standard Poisson assumptions are inappropriate for the data (e.g., by showing the breast cancer microRNA counts deviate substantially from Poisson distributions). A figure showing the empirical distribution of the TCGA data would greatly strengthen this argument.

The literature review, while generally adequate, omits important context. The paper mentions copula approaches [8, 9, 10] but fails to acknowledge how these methods differ from the authors' approach. The authors' dismissal of log-linear models [11] is misleading; the paper's claim that these "grow exponentially with the number of variables" is inaccurate for sparse log-linear models, which are commonly used for high-dimensional count data. The spatial statistics literature (e.g., [16, 21]) is also underappreciated, despite its relevance to count data analysis. The paper should clarify how their models differ from existing spatial count models.

The significance of this work is substantial for the theoretical foundations of graphical models for count data. The authors successfully address a fundamental limitation in the PGM framework, opening the door to more flexible modeling of count-valued networks. However, the practical utility for practitioners remains somewhat unclear. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why one should use these specialized models instead of established alternatives like Gaussian copulas [8] or hierarchical Poisson models [16]. The authors' claim that "counts of sequencing reads... are replacing microarrays" (reference [22]) needs updating with a more recent citation.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by resolving the normalizability constraint in Poisson graphical models, introducing three novel model classes that can capture both positive and negative dependencies. The technical quality is strong, with sound proofs and meaningful experimental validation. However, the practical motivation needs strengthening, and the literature review would benefit from more comprehensive engagement with existing approaches for count data. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as a theoretically rigorous contribution to graphical models, though it would be strengthened by addressing the practical considerations for practitioners.

Strengths: Theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental limitation in Poisson graphical models; sound proofs; clear demonstration of model utility on real data; novel model classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that collectively enable richer dependency structures.

Weaknesses: SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions; insufficient demonstration that count data deviates from Poisson assumptions; incomplete literature review (omits key spatial statistics and copula work); unclear practical advantages over existing approaches.
