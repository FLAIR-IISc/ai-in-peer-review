The paper presents a construction for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that adhere to the Markov structure of an undirected network. Building a multivariate distribution over unbounded counts is generally a challenging problem, as extensively studied by Besag and others over the past 30 years. This problem has been explored in various texts, including Arnold et al.'s "Conditional Specification of Statistical Models" (1999), which extends some of the authors' observations to other distributions, such as conditionally-specified exponential distributions.

The authors' proposal has both advantages and disadvantages. On the positive side, the construction allows for marginal distributions over counts and leads to relatively simple estimation algorithms. However, the proposed models have some drawbacks. The QPGM has marginally thinner tails, and the SPGM lacks closed-form conditional distributions, which somewhat defeats the purpose of building a conditionally specified model. Furthermore, it is unclear how the SPGM can be considered a Poisson distribution, as it does not seem to adhere to the traditional definition of a Poisson distribution. This raises questions about the primary motivation for modeling counts, given that the Poisson distribution itself may not be the best fit for empirical data.

It would be beneficial to include a plot of the probability mass function of the SPGM, as it appears convoluted at first glance. Additionally, it is assumed that R and R0 are considered constants; otherwise, they would not form exponential families. The authors should provide guidance on how to choose these parameters and offer advice to practitioners.

Despite some drawbacks, the paper's effort to construct a multivariate distribution over counts obeying the Markov structure of an MRF is appreciated. The results are of theoretical interest to NIPS. However, the literature review seems incomplete, and the authors appear to underestimate the existing literature. For instance, they seem to misunderstand the concept of copulas and the work presented in [8], which aims to build multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data, including count data. The discussion of [11] also seems misplaced, as the book focuses on building discrete models with log-linear parameters without exponential growth in the number of variables.

A significant omission is the neglect of the vast spatial statistics literature, where high-dimensional count data analysis has been conducted for an extended period. While some of these approaches may not model MRF-style independence constraints in the observable space, they are still relevant to the topic. As a practitioner, it is unclear why one should prefer this model space over simpler alternatives, such as structured Gaussian random fields with Poisson measurement models, which have been the standard for a long time.

Finally, a more recent reference than [20] would be appreciated to support the claim that "counts of sequencing reads... are replacing microarrays" in gene expression analysis.
