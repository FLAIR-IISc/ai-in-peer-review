The paper describes a number of new models for representing a joint distribution over integer-count variables. The authors argue that the standard model arising from the work of Yang et al. is not satisfactory because it can only model negative correlations to ensure the distribution is normalized. They then consider a series of modifications to address this, including a novel truncation method, using a quadratic base measure statistic (which they prove is necessary under certain conditions), and finally a sub-linear sufficient statistic. The authors present experimental results on both simulated and real data.

This is a well-written paper describing elegant solutions for representing count data. I believe the work is original, and the results are significant. 

I found the authors’ discussion of the limitations of the truncation and quadratic models somewhat unconvincing. For the truncation approach, I can imagine many domains where counts do not typically reach high values, and the model would perform well. For the quadratic model, “Gaussian-esque thin tails” could certainly be appropriate in some scenarios. 

I am unfamiliar with the term “sparsistent,” but I note it has been used in the literature.

Page 3, above section 2.1.1: “graphical mode distributions” should be “graphical model distributions.”

Page 5, above section 3.1: “Note that the log…” should read “Note that although the log…” 

Overall, this is a high-quality paper on modeling a joint distribution over count variables.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents several new approaches to modeling count data with graphical models, addressing a limitation of existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The work is well-grounded in theory, with clear justifications for the proposed modifications and proofs of key properties.
*   **Significance:** The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies in count data has the potential to be impactful in various applications, such as genomics and spatial statistics.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The authors demonstrate the performance of their methods on both simulated and real data, showing promising results.
*   **Clarity:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is generally clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practical Limitations:** The authors acknowledge limitations of their proposed models (e.g., truncation constraints, thin tails), and it's unclear how these limitations might affect performance in real-world applications.
*   **Discussion of limitations**: The arguments against the truncation and quadratic models are not compelling and seem to dismiss important possibilities.
*   **Complexity**: The models are complex and may require significant computational resources to implement and apply.
