Summary
The paper proposes two constructions for multivariate distributions over unbounded counts that are Markov with respect to an undirected graph. Constructing such models is notoriously challenging, as emphasized in the work of Besag and in texts such as Arnold et al. (1999) on conditional specification. The authors’ proposals—QPGM and SPGM—are promising in that they yield valid multivariate count models with relatively straightforward estimation procedures.

Strengths
- Addresses a difficult and important problem: building multivariate, unbounded-count distributions consistent with MRF conditional independence.
- Provides concrete constructions with tractable estimation algorithms.
- Theoretical contribution is relevant to NIPS and of independent interest.

Concerns and requests for clarification
- Tail behavior and empirical fit: QPGM appears to have thinner tails than Poisson. Because real count data often exhibit overdispersion, please discuss the practical implications. Do your models adequately capture heavy tails or zero-inflation when present? Empirical diagnostics (e.g., dispersion indices, QQ plots) would help.
- Conditional distributions for SPGM: The lack of closed-form conditionals seems at odds with the appeal of conditionally specified models and may complicate inference (e.g., Gibbs sampling). Please clarify how conditional or pseudo-conditional inference is performed in practice, the computational cost, and any accuracy implications.
- Terminology (“Poisson”): For SPGM, it is unclear in what sense the model is “Poisson” if the base measure and/or kernel deviate substantially from a Poisson form. For QPGM, one can arguably view the change as a modified base measure; for SPGM this is less clear. Please justify the terminology or consider reframing to avoid confusion.
- Role and selection of R and R0: The manuscript seems to treat R and R0 as fixed constants (otherwise the models would not be exponential families). How are they chosen? Are there identifiability, regularization, or robustness considerations? Please provide guidance for practitioners, including default choices and sensitivity analyses.
- Intuition and visualization: A plot of the SPGM probability mass function for representative parameters would be highly informative, as the specification is not immediately transparent. Comparing PMFs across SPGM, QPGM, and Poisson (showing mean/variance, tail behavior, and zero mass) would greatly aid interpretation.
- Practical motivation vs. alternatives: Please clarify when a directly specified count MRF is preferable to standard latent-Gaussian approaches (e.g., Gaussian random fields with Poisson observation models). What price is paid (in complexity, flexibility, or fit) for enforcing MRF conditional independencies in the observed count space? A side-by-side empirical comparison would strengthen the case.

Relation to prior work
- Conditional specification and discrete graphical models: The paper would benefit from a deeper and more precise connection to classical work on log-linear models and conditionally specified distributions (e.g., as in Arnold et al., and related graphical model literature). One of the key points in this literature is how to parameterize discrete graphical models without parameter growth that is exponential in the number of variables; please clarify how your approach compares and scales.
- Copula-based models for discrete/count data: The discussion of copulas, especially [8], feels incomplete and somewhat misleading. There exists a broad line of work using copulas (including Gaussian copulas with sparse precision matrices) to construct flexible multivariate distributions for arbitrary discrete data, with established MCMC and approximate inference schemes. Although such approaches typically do not enforce exact MRF constraints in the observed space, a balanced discussion of their strengths and limitations relative to your models is needed.
- Spatial statistics and high-dimensional count data: The manuscript overlooks a substantial body of spatial statistics addressing multivariate and high-dimensional count data (e.g., Poisson-lognormal models, CAR/ICAR-based models, and related hierarchical constructions). While many of these do not impose MRF independence directly on observed counts, they are widely used and provide competitive baselines and practical tools. Positioning your contribution within this landscape would improve the paper’s impact.

Empirical evaluation
- Beyond reporting estimation simplicity, please evaluate fit and predictive performance relative to strong baselines (e.g., Poisson-lognormal with Gaussian copula/latent Gaussian field). Diagnostics of marginal fit (mean-variance relationship, tail probabilities, zero mass) and dependence fit (pairwise/conditional associations) would be valuable.
- Discuss computational complexity and scalability: How do runtime and memory scale with node count and graph density? Are there convexity guarantees or convergence diagnostics for your estimation procedures?
- Provide practical guidance: How should practitioners choose between QPGM and SPGM? Under what data characteristics (e.g., dispersion levels, sparsity of the graph) does each model excel?

Minor comments
- The claim that “counts of sequencing reads … are replacing microarrays” should be supported with a reference more recent than [20]. Please update this citation or soften the claim.
- Clarify whether your constructions admit consistent structure learning procedures (e.g., penalized neighborhood selection) and whether standard tools apply despite the tail differences and non-closed-form conditionals.

Overall assessment
This paper tackles a genuinely difficult problem and presents constructions that are theoretically interesting and potentially useful. However, the current version underplays important related literatures (copulas, discrete/log-linear graphical models, spatial statistics) and leaves unclear the practical advantages over established latent-Gaussian approaches. Strengthening the literature review, clarifying the role of R and R0, justifying the “Poisson” nomenclature for SPGM, and adding empirical diagnostics and comparisons would substantially improve the paper. With these revisions, the contribution would be clearer and more compelling to both theorists and practitioners.
