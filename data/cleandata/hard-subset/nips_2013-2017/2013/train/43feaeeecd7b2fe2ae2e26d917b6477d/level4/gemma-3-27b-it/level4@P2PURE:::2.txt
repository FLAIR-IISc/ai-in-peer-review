Constructing multivariate distributions suitable for count data is an important problem, and the authors rightly identify limitations in existing approaches – although a caveat regarding this is noted below.

The paper offers several theoretical and conceptual strengths. Beginning with a proof demonstrating the invalidity of previously proposed truncation methods, the authors introduce a number of alternatives. The TPGM truncation, while straightforward, is justified by its intuitive interpretation. The QPGM and SPGM, though technically simple, are novel in their explicit consideration of the base measure and sufficient statistics of the exponential representation as a means to modify the Poisson distribution. Moreover, these modifications are well-motivated and conceptually appealing. However, the underlying theory largely follows from the work of Yang et al., leaving us with a set of potential models whose value remains to be established empirically.

Given the above, the experimental evaluation is disappointing, particularly considering the paper’s stated goal of addressing the practical limitations of Poisson graphical models. While the synthetic experiment indicates some potential, the data is tailored to favor the proposed models. The real-data experiments conspicuously lack comparison to copula-based alternatives. Furthermore, the reported results are primarily qualitative and anecdotal. A more objective evaluation, such as the log-probability of test data, is necessary given that all methods construct joint distributions.

More critically – and this addresses the fundamental question of whether these new models are needed – the copula-based competitors are used in a somewhat unprincipled manner. Specifically, even employing a simple Gaussian copula, it is preferable to use a sensible marginal model instead of the one used in the non-paranormal approach (which lacks a density function), which was selected primarily for its asymptotic properties rather than practical performance. The near-identical performance to Glasso is suspect, and I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates would yield superior results. Similarly, the sensible choice of R=11 in the real-data analysis could also inform the selection of a marginal distribution for the copula. While an in-depth investigation is not expected, a reasonable baseline comparison is warranted.

Finally, the paper is generally well-written. Although I believe the results are valid – especially given that the authors begin by identifying an error in a previous work – I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material. Additionally, the end of section 3.1 feels overly detailed, and the presented bound does not significantly enhance the Gaussian-like argument. Conversely, section 3.2 should be expanded, as it represents the core of the proposed method; in particular, the figure should be moved from the supplement to the main text and its underlying intuition better explained. 

In summary, the authors present alternatives to the Poisson graphical model, based on a conditional exponential construction, with the aim of enabling flexible joint modeling that allows for both positive and negative dependencies. The approach is appealing and possesses some theoretical novelty. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and potentially biased, resulting in yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical merit remains unclear.
