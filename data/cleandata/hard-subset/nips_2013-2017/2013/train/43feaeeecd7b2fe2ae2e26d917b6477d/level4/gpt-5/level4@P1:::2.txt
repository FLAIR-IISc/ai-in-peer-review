Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the important problem of constructing multivariate models for count data. Starting from the well-known limitations of Poisson graphical models (PGMs)—notably that normalizability forces non-positive edge parameters and hence only negative conditional dependencies—the authors revisit truncation schemes and propose alternative Poisson-like graphical models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.

They first prove that the Winsorized Poisson approach, previously proposed by Kaiser and Cressie, cannot yield a consistent joint distribution under the node-conditional construction of Besag and its generalization by Yang et al. Building on the exponential family framework of Yang et al., they propose: (i) a truncated Poisson graphical model (TPGM) that remains in the exponential family and avoids negativity constraints; (ii) a Quadratic Poisson Graphical Model (QPGM) that keeps linear sufficient statistics but replaces the Poisson base measure with a quadratic one, thereby allowing both positive and negative edges under a negative-definite quadratic form; and (iii) a Sublinear Poisson Graphical Model (SPGM), which retains the Poisson base measure but uses sublinear sufficient statistics to attain normalizability without restricting pairwise parameters. They advocate `1-penalized neighborhood selection for structure learning and provide experiments on simulated data and a microRNA-seq breast cancer dataset.

Quality:
On the theoretical side, the contributions are clear and mostly sound. The invalidity of the Winsorized conditional construction is an important and useful correction. The TPGM is a natural and well-motivated truncation that preserves the exponential family form. The QPGM and SPGM are technically simple but novel in spirit, as they explicitly modify the base measure and sufficient statistics to achieve richer dependence structures. Much of the construction and theory follows directly from Yang et al.’s exponential-family node-conditional framework, so the novelty lies more in model design than in new theory. I believe the results are correct, but given that the paper begins by identifying a mistake in prior work, I recommend including all proofs in the supplementary material.

On the empirical side, the evaluation is the weak point, especially given the paper’s stated goal of addressing practical limitations of PGMs. The synthetic experiments are somewhat tailored to the proposed models. In the real-data study, key copula-based competitors are missing. Results are presented only qualitatively; since all methods define joint distributions, a more objective evaluation using test log-likelihood would be appropriate.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written. However:
- The end of Section 3.1 feels overly detailed; the variational bound does not add much to the “Gaussian-esque” argument.
- Section 3.2 is central to the paper; it should be paced more slowly, with the figure for the sublinear sufficient statistic included in the main text and its intuition better explained.

Originality:
The work is original in proposing Poisson-like graphical models that relax the negative-dependence constraint by modifying the exponential-family components (base measure and sufficient statistics). The constructions themselves are straightforward once the Yang et al. framework is adopted, but they open up a useful design space for count-valued graphical models.

Significance:
If validated empirically, the proposed models could be impactful for multivariate count data. However, the current experiments do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over strong alternatives. In particular, the copula comparisons are treated in an overly black-box manner. Even within a Gaussian copula, it is more sensible to select appropriate marginals than to use the nonparanormal (which does not define a proper density and was motivated mainly by asymptotic properties rather than practical fit). The essentially identical performance to the graphical lasso is suspicious; I strongly suspect that even simple Gaussian kernel density estimates for the marginals would perform better. The choice R = 11 in the real-data analysis is reasonable for TPGM, but the analogous choice should also inform the marginal specification in copula models. While an exhaustive study is unnecessary, a reasonable baseline is warranted.

Pros:
- Correctly identifies and fixes a key issue with Winsorized conditionals.
- Proposes three intuitive and theoretically grounded alternatives (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
- Offers a clear path to learning via penalized neighborhood selection.
- Potentially broad applicability to count data with mixed positive/negative dependencies.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation is limited and biased toward the proposed models.
- Missing or weak baselines: no careful copula competitors with sensible marginals; reliance on the nonparanormal is problematic.
- No test log-likelihood or other principled out-of-sample metric.
- Some presentation issues (overly detailed bound in 3.1; underexplained core ideas in 3.2).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add copula-based baselines with reasonable marginal fits (e.g., Gaussian kernel density estimates or parametric marginals informed by the data; map the TPGM choice of R to analogous marginal choices).
- Report test log-likelihood (or pseudo-likelihood) on held-out data to provide an objective comparison across methods that define joint distributions.
- Include all proofs in the supplement.
- Streamline Section 3.1’s bound; expand and visualize Section 3.2’s sublinear statistic in the main text with clearer intuition.
- Discuss the practical trade-offs of TPGM as R increases (and its relation to the original PGM’s constraints) more explicitly in the context of model selection.

Conclusion:
Based on a conditional exponential construction, the paper proposes alternatives to the Poisson graphical model that allow flexible joint modeling with both positive and negative dependencies. The approach is appealing and has some theoretical novelty, but the empirical evaluation is limited and somewhat biased. As it stands, this feels like yet another multivariate Poisson-like model whose practical merit is not yet demonstrated. Strengthening the baselines and adding principled, quantitative evaluations would significantly improve the paper.
