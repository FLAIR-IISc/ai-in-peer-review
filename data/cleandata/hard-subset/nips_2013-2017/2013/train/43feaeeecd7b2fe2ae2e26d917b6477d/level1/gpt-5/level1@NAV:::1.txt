This paper tackles a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the normalizability constraint that forces non-positive edge parameters, hence only negative conditional dependencies (Besag, 1974; Yang et al., NIPS 2012/2013). The authors (i) prove that the commonly suggested “Winsorized” (hard-truncated) Poisson node-conditionals do not admit a valid joint MRF, correcting claims in the spatial statistics literature (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith); (ii) propose a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) based on an exponential-family truncation that admits both positive and negative dependencies; (iii) show that with linear sufficient statistics, any normalizable joint model allowing mixed-sign interactions must have at least quadratic base measure, leading to a Quadratic PGM (QPGM); and (iv) introduce a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that replaces the linear sufficient statistic by a sublinear transformation to regain heavier tails while allowing arbitrary pairwise parameters. They couple these constructions with ℓ1-penalized neighborhood selection and demonstrate improved ROC performance over Gaussian, nonparanormal, and SKEPTIC baselines on simulations and a TCGA microRNA network, recovering biologically plausible hubs.

Quality: The theoretical contributions are substantive. The impossibility result for Winsorization and the normalizability requirement C(z)=Ω(z^2) with linear statistics are well-motivated and useful. The new families are clearly defined; normalizability conditions are stated precisely. Estimation is practical via nodewise GLM-like likelihoods with variational bounds, though finite-sample/statistical guarantees are deferred.

Clarity: Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful contrasts among PGM/TPGM/QPGM/SPGM. Minor suggestions: clarify computational complexity and convergence for neighborhood selection under SPGM (partition function approximation), and provide guidance for choosing R and R0.

Originality: High. The negative result on Winsorization, the quadratic-base-measure necessity, and the SPGM construction are novel and clearly distinguished from prior work (GLM-based graphical models; copula and Poisson-sum constructions).

Significance: Strong, given the ubiquity of high-dimensional count data (sequencing, spatial incidence). These models fill a gap by enabling positive dependencies with tractable learning.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical correction and new existence results.
- Three practical model classes covering different tail/parameter regimes.
- Empirical gains on synthetic and real RNA-seq data with biological interpretability.

Cons:
- No sparsistency/consistency theory yet for QPGM/SPGM estimators.
- Sensitivity to truncation and tuning (R, R0) not systematically studied.
- Baselines omit popular latent-variable count models (e.g., Poisson log-normal, negative-binomial graphical alternatives).

Recommendation: Accept (weak). Strengthen by adding discussion/experiments vs. Poisson log-normal or NB-based graphical models, and providing heuristic rules for choosing R and R0.
