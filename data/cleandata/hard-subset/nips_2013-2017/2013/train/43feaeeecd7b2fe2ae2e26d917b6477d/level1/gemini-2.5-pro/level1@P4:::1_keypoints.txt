- Paper addresses Poisson graphical models' inability to capture positive dependencies and proposes three new classes for count data (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
- Proves the Winsorized Poisson graphical model from prior work is invalid (Theorem 1).
- Proposes Truncated PGM (TPGM), an exponential-family model that permits positive and negative dependencies via truncation.
- Notes TPGM limitations: bounded domain and sensitivity of parameter estimates to chosen truncation level.
- Introduces Quadratic PGM (QPGM), replacing log(X!) with cX^2 to ensure normalizability for all interactions but producing thin, Gaussian-like tails.
- Introduces Sublinear PGM (SPGM), keeping log(X!) but using sub-linear sufficient statistics to allow arbitrary real-valued interactions and potentially heavier tails than QPGM.
- Theoretical results (Theorems 2–4, Propositions 1–2) establish existence and normalizability properties for the proposed models.
- Experimental evaluation on simulated and microRNA sequencing data shows TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian and non-paranormal baselines for structure learning.
- Paper is technically sound with clear, well-supported theoretical contributions and correct derivations for each model.
- Focus is primarily on model specification; formal learning theory (e.g., sparsistency of the penalized neighborhood selection) is deferred to future work.
- QPGM is not directly evaluated experimentally, making its practical advantage over GGMs on transformed counts unclear.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear motivation, logical flow, consistent notation, and accessible explanations.
- The SPGM's specific piecewise sub-linear sufficient statistic feels somewhat engineered and could use more motivation.
- Contribution is original and significant: first tractable high-dimensional count graphical models supporting positive and negative dependencies and a useful disproof of prior work.
- Practical guidance on hyperparameter selection for TPGM (R) and SPGM (R0, R) is limited; heuristics used are based on observed maxima.
- Overall arguments for acceptance: solves a major limitation, introduces novel models, strong theory, high applicability, and clear presentation.
- Overall arguments for rejection: lacks theoretical guarantees for structure learning and provides limited hyperparameter selection guidance.
