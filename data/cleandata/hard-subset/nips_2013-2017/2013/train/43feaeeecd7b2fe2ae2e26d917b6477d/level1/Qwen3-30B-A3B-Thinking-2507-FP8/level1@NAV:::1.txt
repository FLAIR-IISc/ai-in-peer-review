

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors identify this as a key gap in the literature, building upon the foundational work of Yang et al. (NIPS 2012) who introduced PGMs, and extending the line of work on graphical models for count data that includes recent NIPS contributions like the nonparanormal (NIPS 2009) and Gaussian copula approaches (NIPS 2012). The paper makes three key contributions: (1) it demonstrates that the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie (2009) cannot yield a valid joint distribution, (2) it introduces a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that allows both positive and negative dependencies within a bounded domain, and (3) it proposes two novel variants—Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that overcome domain limitations while permitting richer dependence structures.

The paper excels in technical quality, with rigorous theoretical proofs establishing normalizability conditions and clear model specifications. The experimental validation on both simulated networks and real microRNA sequencing data demonstrates significant practical advantages over existing methods, particularly in capturing biologically meaningful network structures. The clarity of presentation is excellent, with well-organized mathematical derivations and effective use of theorems to build the argument. The originality is strong, as the authors successfully address a fundamental limitation that has persisted since the introduction of PGMs, while making novel contributions to the theoretical understanding of graphical models for count data.

The significance of this work is substantial, as count data modeling is increasingly important across diverse domains including genomics and spatial statistics. The paper makes a clear and demonstrable advance over prior work, particularly in enabling the modeling of positive conditional dependencies. A minor limitation is that the SPGM's log-partition function lacks a closed form, though the authors provide a useful variational upper bound.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that addresses a well-motivated problem with technical rigor and practical relevance. The authors have clearly demonstrated how their models differ from and improve upon previous work, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of their superiority. This paper advances the state of the art in graphical models for count data and should be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
