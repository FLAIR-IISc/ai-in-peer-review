This paper tackles the important problem of modeling multivariate count data, a common scenario in modern applications like genomics. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to represent negative correlations – and propose several novel approaches to address this. The paper is well-motivated and clearly articulates the problem and its significance.

The core contribution lies in proposing three new variants: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The analysis of truncation approaches is particularly strong, with a clear and rigorous proof demonstrating the incorrectness of a previously proposed method (Kaiser & Cressie, 2006). The derivation of the QPGM and SPGM, stemming from modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution, is logically sound and mathematically well-presented. The theoretical justifications for normalizability and the ability to model both positive and negative dependencies are convincing.  The paper relates well to prior work on graphical models (Meinshausen & Bühlmann, 2006; Yuan & Lin, 2007; Friedman et al., 2008) and extensions to count data (Yang et al., 2013, 2014).  The authors demonstrate practical applicability through simulations and a case study on microRNA expression data, which is a relevant and compelling application.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is thorough, the numerical experiments feel somewhat limited. The comparisons are primarily against a Gaussian graphical model and the original PGM, but a more extensive comparison with other count data models (e.g., hierarchical models, copula-based methods) would strengthen the evaluation. The discussion of the computational complexity of inference and parameter estimation is lacking. While the authors mention using a penalized likelihood approach, details regarding scalability and computational cost for large datasets are missing. The connection to variational inference, hinted at for the QPGM, could be expanded upon, potentially offering a more efficient inference strategy. The selection of parameters (e.g., R and R0 in SPGM) in the experiments isn’t thoroughly justified and a sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge limitations in the Truncated PGM, a more detailed discussion of when each approach (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) is most appropriate given the characteristics of the data would be valuable.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and a clear presentation of the proposed models. Claims are generally well-supported by theoretical analysis and preliminary experimental results.  It feels like a complete piece of work, though more extensive numerical validation would add confidence. The authors are careful to address limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the logical flow is easy to follow. Providing more implementation details would improve reproducibility.

**Originality:** The proposed models and the analysis of truncation approaches are novel and represent a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The extension beyond the limitations of traditional PGMs is a clear advance.

**Significance:** The results are important because they provide a more flexible and expressive framework for modeling multivariate count data. The potential applications in areas like genomics, epidemiology, and spatial statistics are significant.  The work addresses a difficult problem and advances the state of the art.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound models for count data.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing PGMs.
*   Clear and rigorous mathematical analysis.
*   Demonstrated applicability to a real-world problem (microRNA expression).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited numerical evaluation and comparison to alternative methods.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of computational complexity.
*   Insufficient guidance on model selection (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, specifically expanding the numerical evaluation and providing more details on computational aspects and model selection.
