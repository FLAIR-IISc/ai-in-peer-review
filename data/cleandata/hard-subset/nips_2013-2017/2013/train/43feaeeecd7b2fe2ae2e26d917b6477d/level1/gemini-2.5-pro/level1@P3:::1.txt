### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a critical and well-known limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) as defined by the construction of Besag (1974) and extended by Yang et al. (2012, 2013). Standard PGMs, which are derived from Poisson node-conditional distributions, can only model negative conditional dependencies (i.e., non-positive edge weights) due to the requirement that the joint distribution be normalizable over the infinite domain of count data. This severely restricts their practical applicability.

The authors propose three novel classes of "Poisson-like" graphical models that overcome this limitation, allowing for both positive and negative conditional dependencies.
1.  **Truncated PGM (TPGM):** The paper first analyzes truncation as a strategy. It provides an important negative result, proving that the previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson conditional by Kaiser and Cressie (1997) does not lead to a valid joint distribution. The authors then propose a different, valid truncation scheme where the resulting conditional is in the exponential family, which guarantees the existence of a proper joint MRF. This model, TPGM, allows for arbitrary edge weights but requires a pre-specified upper bound `R` on the counts.
2.  **Quadratic PGM (QPGM):** To avoid the need for truncation, the authors investigate modifying the PGM density more fundamentally. They prove that to allow positive edge weights with linear sufficient statistics (`X_s`), the base measure must grow at least quadratically (`Ω(X_s^2)`). This insight leads to the QPGM, which incorporates a quadratic term (`-c * X_s^2`) in the log-probability, analogous to a Gaussian Graphical Model (GGM), but defined on a count domain.
3.  **Sublinear PGM (SPGM):** Recognizing that the QPGM has Gaussian-like thin tails, the authors propose a third variant to allow for heavier tails while maintaining normalizability. Instead of modifying the base measure, they modify the sufficient statistic to be sub-linear. The proposed statistic `B(X)` is linear for small counts but becomes constant for large counts, effectively taming the quadratic interaction terms `B(X_s)B(X_t)` and ensuring normalizability for any real-valued edge weights, while retaining the original Poisson base measure (`log(X_s!)`).

The paper demonstrates how to learn the structure of these models via `l1`-penalized neighborhood selection and provides strong empirical results on simulated data and a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset, showing significant improvements over standard GGMs and the original PGM.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important open problem in the modeling of multivariate count data. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive dependencies ("cooperative" relationships) is a major practical roadblock. This work provides not one, but three principled and well-motivated solutions. The proposed models significantly expand the toolkit for researchers working with high-dimensional count data, such as in genomics or social sciences.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. Theorem 1, which formally refutes the validity of the Winsorized Poisson MRF proposed in prior literature, is a significant contribution in its own right, clarifying a point of confusion. The theoretical motivation for the QPGM (Theorem 3), linking the growth rate of the base measure to the sign of permissible interactions, is elegant and insightful. The derivations of the three models are sound and logically connected.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear statement of the problem, systematically critiques previous attempts, and then develops each of its novel contributions as a logical step to overcome the limitations of the last. This narrative structure makes the paper very easy to follow and understand.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The comparison against relevant baselines (GGMs, non-paranormal) on both synthetic and real-world data clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed methods for count data. The application to miRNA sequencing data is compelling, as the SPGM/TPGM methods uncover biologically relevant hubs and network structures missed by both GGMs and the original, overly-restrictive PGM.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The practical application of TPGM and SPGM relies on hyperparameters (`R` for TPGM, `R0` and `R` for SPGM). The paper sets these based on the maximum observed count value, which is a reasonable heuristic but lacks a more principled justification. A discussion of the sensitivity to these parameters or potential methods for their selection (e.g., cross-validation) would strengthen the practical utility of the proposed methods.
2.  **Inclusion of QPGM in Experiments:** The QPGM is well-motivated theoretically but is conspicuously absent from the numerical experiments. It would have been valuable to see how it performs relative to TPGM and SPGM. As presented, it serves primarily as a stepping stone to motivate SPGM, but its own practical merits remain unevaluated.
3.  **Theoretical Guarantees for Learning:** The paper states that sparsistency analysis of the neighborhood selection procedure is left for future work. While the framework builds on the work of Ravikumar et al. (2010) and Yang et al. (2013), where such guarantees exist, providing at least a high-level argument for why these guarantees should extend to the proposed models would make the work more complete.
4.  **Form of the Sublinear Statistic:** The piecewise function chosen for the sublinear sufficient statistic `B(x)` in the SPGM feels somewhat engineered. While it achieves the desired theoretical properties, a brief justification for this specific form over other possible sub-linear functions would be welcome.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. The contributions are novel, significant, and technically sound. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the presented research.

-   The proof that the Winsorized Poisson conditional does not lead to a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1) is an important corrective to the literature and highlights the subtleties of constructing MRFs from conditional distributions.
-   The connection made between the tail behavior of the base measure and the allowed parameter space is a key insight that could potentially be applied to the construction of graphical models for other data types.
-   The computational aspect of fitting the models is handled via proximal gradient ascent on the node-wise regressions. For the SPGM, the log-partition function of the conditional is intractable. The use of the Poisson log-partition function as a variational upper bound is a clever and practical solution, though a brief analysis of the tightness of this bound would be beneficial.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that provides a comprehensive and satisfying solution to a long-standing problem. It combines sharp theoretical analysis with strong empirical results, making a significant contribution to the NIPS community.
