- Addresses fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints.
- Proposes three novel solutions: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).
- Shows prior work failed to provide valid joint distributions for truncated Poisson models or was restricted to negative dependencies.
- Proves the Winsorized Poisson approach cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1).
- Introduces a new truncation method that yields a valid joint distribution (Theorem 2).
- Demonstrates that modifying the base measure (QPGM) or sufficient statistics (SPGM) enables modeling both positive and negative dependencies.
- Proves key normalizability constraints, including that the base measure must scale quadratically for linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3).
- Experimental validation includes simulations and a breast cancer microRNA dataset, showing empirical support.
- Acknowledges limitations of each approach (e.g., TPGM’s bounded domain, QPGM’s Gaussian-like tail).
- Notes practical complication: SPGM log-partition function has no closed-form solution.
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions and well-proven theorems.
- Writing is clear and well-organized with precise mathematical formulations.
- Would benefit from more detailed discussion of computational aspects and efficient computation of SPGM log-partition.
- Work is highly original, filling a significant gap by enabling positive dependencies in PGMs.
- Clearly differentiates from and improves upon prior methods, explaining why Winsorized Poisson fails.
- Significant practical value for count-data applications (e.g., genomic sequencing, microRNA networks).
- Strength: rigorous theoretical foundation with multiple well-proven theorems.
- Strength: clear demonstration of why previous approaches fail and presentation of three complementary solutions.
- Strength: strong empirical validation on simulated and real-world data.
- Weakness: limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability to very large graphs.
- Weakness: SPGM’s lack of closed-form log-partition may complicate implementation.
- Weakness: case study could include more detailed biological interpretation.
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a significant problem, offers three validated approaches, demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods, and is likely to be widely adopted.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited computational discussion and a less detailed case study.
- Overall recommendation: substantial contribution and recommendation for acceptance.
