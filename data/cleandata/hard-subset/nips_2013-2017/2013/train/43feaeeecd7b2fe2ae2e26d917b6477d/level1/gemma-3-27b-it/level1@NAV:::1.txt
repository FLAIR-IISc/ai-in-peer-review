## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses a significant limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to model positive conditional dependencies. The authors convincingly argue that standard PGMs, built upon Poisson node-conditional distributions, are restricted to representing competitive relationships due to normalizability constraints. To overcome this, they propose three novel variants: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), each building on different modifications to the underlying Poisson distribution. The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifies a crucial problem in the field, and proposes innovative solutions. 

The authors effectively review existing work on graphical models, from Gaussian and Ising models to earlier attempts at extending the Poisson distribution for multivariate modeling. They correctly position their work relative to Besag’s seminal work on PGMs and subsequent extensions by Yang et al. The theoretical justification for the proposed models, particularly the demonstration that quadratic base measures are required for both positive and negative dependencies, is rigorous.  The analysis of the truncated Poisson approach and its failure to yield a valid joint distribution is a valuable contribution. 

A strength of the paper lies in the breadth of its approach, exploring truncation, quadratic variations, and sublinear modifications.  The inclusion of simulation studies and a case study using microRNA sequencing data adds practical relevance.  The comparisons to Gaussian graphical models and existing PGM estimators strengthen the claims of the proposed models' benefits. 

However, the paper could be improved.  The connection between the SPGM's sublinear statistic and the resulting graphical model properties is not as intuitively explained as the QPGM.  The numerical experiments, while demonstrating improvements, lack detailed statistical comparisons (e.g., significance testing) beyond ROC curves. The computational complexity of estimating the models, particularly the SPGM, isn’t discussed, which is important for practical adoption. Finally, while mentioning sparsistency analysis is planned for future work, some discussion of its expected challenges and the current state of theory for similar models would be helpful. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant limitation in existing PGMs.
*   Novel and theoretically sound model extensions are proposed.
*   Demonstrated empirical performance on both simulated and real data.
*   Clear and well-written exposition of complex concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity.
*   Numerical experiments lack robust statistical comparison.
*   SPGM's motivations and properties aren't as clearly elucidated as the QPGM.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical models and offers promising new tools for analyzing count-valued data. Addressing the points raised above would further strengthen its impact.
