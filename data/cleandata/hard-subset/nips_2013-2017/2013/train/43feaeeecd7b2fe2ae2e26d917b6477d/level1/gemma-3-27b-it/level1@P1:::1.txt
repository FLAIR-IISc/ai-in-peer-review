This paper addresses the problem of modeling multivariate count data, a common scenario in modern applications like genomics and user behavior analysis. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to capture negative conditional dependencies – and propose several novel approaches to overcome this. They begin by analyzing truncation strategies for the Poisson distribution, showing that a previously proposed method is flawed, and introduce a Truncated PGM (TPGM). Recognizing limitations of the TPGM, they further explore modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution, leading to the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper includes theoretical justification for these models and demonstrates their performance on both simulated and real-world (microRNA sequencing) data.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles a significant problem in statistical modeling. The identified limitation of standard PGMs is correctly highlighted, and the proposed solutions are presented in a clear and logical manner. The theoretical analysis, particularly the proof that Kaiser and Cressie’s truncation approach is invalid (Theorem 1) and the exploration of base measure requirements (Theorem 3), is rigorous and contributes meaningfully to the field. The introduction of the QPGM and SPGM represents a novel contribution, offering alternative ways to model complex dependencies in count data. The experimental results, comparing the proposed models with existing methods on both simulated and real datasets, provide convincing evidence of their effectiveness. The connection to existing work in graphical models and the careful referencing of relevant literature are also strengths.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the explanation of the intuition behind the SPGM's modified sufficient statistic is somewhat lacking. The visual depiction in the appendix is helpful but could be elaborated on in the main text. A more detailed discussion of the computational challenges associated with the SPGM, particularly regarding the intractable log-partition function and the reliance on a Poisson approximation, would be beneficial. While stability selection is mentioned for the real data analysis, a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis with respect to regularization parameter choices could strengthen the results. The numerical experiments are a bit limited; exploring the performance of the models on a wider range of network structures and dataset sizes would be valuable. The discussion of the relationship between the QPGM and Gaussian Graphical Models (GGMs) feels a bit superficial. A more in-depth exploration of the connection, potentially through asymptotic analysis, could reveal further insights. Furthermore, while the comparison with the original PGM is noted, a quantitative comparison (e.g., in terms of likelihood or information criteria) would be helpful. Finally, the paper's length prevents in-depth exploration of certain concepts; for instance, the implications of the SPGM for specific applications beyond the breast cancer example are not fully discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM represent original contributions to the field of graphical models for count data.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed models and their properties.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the models on both simulated and real-world data.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a significant limitation of existing PGMs and provides new tools for modeling complex dependencies in count-valued data, which has broad applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making the concepts accessible to readers familiar with graphical models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The SPGM's computational challenges are not fully addressed.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments could be more extensive.
*   **Superficial Discussion:** Some discussions, such as the QPGM-GGM connection, lack depth.
*   **Intuition Gap:** The rationale behind the SPGM’s modified sufficient statistic could be better explained.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. The proposed models address a critical limitation of existing methods and offer a promising avenue for modeling complex dependencies in count data. While some aspects could be further elaborated on, the theoretical soundness, empirical validation, and novelty of the work justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with a minor revision request to address the points raised regarding computational complexity, experiment scope, and the SPGM's sufficient statistic intuition.
