This paper addresses the problem of modeling multivariate count data, a common occurrence in modern datasets but poorly addressed by existing graphical model frameworks. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – their inability to model negative dependencies – and propose several novel approaches to overcome this, including a Truncated PGM (TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper provides theoretical justification for each approach and demonstrates their performance on simulated and real microRNA sequencing data.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying a gap in the existing literature. The mathematical derivations are rigorous, and the proposed models are presented in a logical progression. The authors correctly point out the flaws in previous truncation attempts (Kaiser & Cressie) and provide a compelling argument for their new approaches. The empirical evaluation, while limited, provides encouraging results, showing that the proposed models outperform existing methods, including Gaussian graphical models, on both simulated and real datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent a significant contribution to the field, offering new tools for modeling multivariate count data with both positive and negative dependencies.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations and justifications for each model are rigorous and well-explained. The theorems and propositions are clearly stated and proved.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to microRNA sequencing data demonstrates the potential of the proposed models in a real-world setting.
*   **Thoroughness:** The authors acknowledge and address the limitations of their methods, such as the Gaussian-esque thin tail of the QPGM and the computational challenges associated with the SPGM.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not provide a detailed discussion of the computational cost of fitting the proposed models, particularly the QPGM and SPGM.  This is a significant concern for high-dimensional data.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. While the results are promising, they would be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison to other relevant methods, and more extensive parameter tuning, particularly given the sensitivity of graphical model estimation to regularization parameters.
*   **SPGM Parameter Choice:** The choice of R0 and R for the SPGM isn't fully justified.  A more systematic investigation of the impact of these parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Variational Upper Bound:** The reliance on a variational upper bound for the SPGM suggests potential limitations in accuracy. A more precise analysis of the approximation error would be valuable.
*   **Connections to other Literature:** While the paper cites relevant work, it could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of connections to recent advances in copula-based graphical models or other approaches for handling non-Gaussian data.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in statistical modeling.
*   The proposed models are novel and theoretically well-founded.
*   The empirical results are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of the proposed methods.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the proposed models is a concern.
*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited.
*   Some aspects of the model specification (e.g., SPGM parameter choice) require further justification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical modeling. The proposed models are novel, theoretically sound, and demonstrate promising performance on both simulated and real data. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.
