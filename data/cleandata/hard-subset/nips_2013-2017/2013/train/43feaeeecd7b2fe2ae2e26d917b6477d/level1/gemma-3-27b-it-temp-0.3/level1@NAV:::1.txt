## Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"

This paper addresses the limitations of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) – specifically their inability to model positive dependencies – and proposes several novel extensions to overcome this issue. The authors correctly identify the normalizability constraint as the root cause of this limitation and explore different strategies to circumvent it. They begin by analyzing a truncation approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie, demonstrating its inconsistency, and then introduce the Truncated PGM (TPGM). Recognizing the limitations of the TPGM, they further propose the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) by modifying the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution. The paper is well-motivated, tackling a significant problem in modeling count data, and the proposed models offer potential improvements over existing approaches.

A key strength of the paper lies in its thorough theoretical analysis. The authors rigorously demonstrate the inconsistencies of the Winsorized Poisson approach and provide clear justifications for their proposed modifications. The proofs are well-structured and contribute to a deeper understanding of the constraints and possibilities within Poisson graphical modeling. The numerical experiments, including both simulated data and a real-world miRNA expression dataset, provide empirical support for the proposed methods, showing improvements over standard Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical derivations are strong, the connection between the proposed models and practical applications beyond the miRNA dataset could be further elaborated. The discussion of computational complexity and scalability is limited, and it’s unclear how these models would perform with extremely high-dimensional data. The parameter tuning and model selection process in the experiments are not fully detailed, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the results. Additionally, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of the TPGM, a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between the different proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing PGMs.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous proofs.
*   Empirical validation on both simulated and real data.
*   Novel and potentially impactful contributions to the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Insufficient detail regarding parameter tuning and model selection.
*   Lack of a comprehensive comparison of the proposed models’ trade-offs.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of graphical modeling for count data. While some aspects could be further strengthened, the theoretical rigor, novel approach, and promising empirical results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
