- Problem: Standard Poisson graphical models are only normalizable with non-positive edge parameters, so they can model only negative conditional dependencies.

- Goal: Develop PGM-like models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies for multivariate count data.

- Contribution — Truncation (TPGM): Proves the Winsorized PGM is invalid (Theorem 1) and proposes a valid Truncated PGM that allows mixed dependencies but has a bounded domain and reimposes negativity for large truncation R.

- Contribution — Quadratic PGM (QPGM): Introduces a quadratic base measure (-c X^2) making the model normalizable for both positive and negative edges when the quadratic-parameter matrix is negative-definite; drawback is unrealistic thin/Gaussian-like tails.

- Contribution — Sub-linear PGM (SPGM): Replaces the linear sufficient statistic with a sub-linear B(X) that saturates, retaining log(X!) base measure, yielding normalizability for any edge parameters and heavier tails than QPGM (Theorem 4).

- Experiments: Simulations and a microRNA sequencing dataset show TPGM and SPGM recover mixed dependencies and outperform Gaussian methods and the original restrictive PGM.

- Strengths: High technical quality with rigorous proofs, clear exposition, honest discussion of limitations, strong originality, and high potential impact for modeling count data.

- Weaknesses: Learning/estimation discussion is brief; optimization challenges (intractable SPGM node-conditional partition function and reliance on a variational bound) are underexplored; no theoretical sparsistency guarantees.

- Arguments for acceptance: Solves a well-motivated unsolved problem, presents three novel theoretically sound models, corrects prior literature, and shows practical utility.

- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient treatment of learning algorithms and theoretical properties; reliance on approximations for SPGM partition function needs deeper analysis.

- Conclusion: The paper is an excellent, significant, and original contribution that substantially advances graphical modeling for count data and is well-suited for acceptance.
