- Tackles modeling multivariate count data and highlights practical relevance (e.g., genomics).  
- Correctly identifies limitation of existing Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs): inability to represent negative correlations.  
- Proposes three new variants: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  
- Strong, rigorous analysis of truncation approaches with proof showing a prior method (Kaiser & Cressie, 2006) is incorrect.  
- QPGM and SPGM derivations are logically sound, modifying Poisson base measure and sufficient statistics.  
- Theoretical justification presented for normalizability and for modeling both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Good relation to prior graphical-model and count-data literature.  
- Demonstrates practical applicability via simulations and a microRNA expression case study.  
- Numerical experiments are limited and comparisons are mainly to Gaussian graphical models and original PGM.  
- Lacks comparison to other count-data approaches (e.g., hierarchical models, copula methods).  
- Discussion of computational complexity, scalability, and estimation cost is insufficient.  
- Connection to variational inference (mentioned for QPGM) is underdeveloped and could offer more efficient inference.  
- Parameter selection for SPGM (R and R0) is not well-justified and needs sensitivity analysis.  
- Needs clearer guidance on when to choose TPGM vs QPGM vs SPGM depending on data characteristics.  
- Paper quality: technically sound with rigorous proofs; more extensive numerical validation would increase confidence.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized, but more implementation details would improve reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel models and truncation analysis represent a significant contribution.  
- Significance: advances expressiveness for multivariate count modeling with important applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound models; addresses key PGM limitation; rigorous analysis; real-world application shown.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited numerical evaluation, missing computational discussion, insufficient model-selection guidance.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to expand numerical evaluation and provide more computational and model-selection details.
