- Paper identifies a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): joint distributions only allow non-positive edge weights, preventing modeling of positive conditional dependencies.
- Corrects the literature by proving the Winsorized Poisson approach of Kaiser and Cressie is not a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1).
- Proposes three novel fixes: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) to permit both positive and negative dependencies.
- Provides rigorous theoretical justification and normalizability conditions for each approach (Theorems 2–4).
- Establishes connections between the proposed models and existing exponential family graphical models.
- Technical presentation is strong: clear mathematics, well-structured proofs, and thorough analysis of normalizability constraints.
- Empirical results on simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA dataset support the theoretical claims and show superiority over existing methods.
- Biological case study finds relevant hub miRNAs (e.g., mir-375, mir-10b) that other approaches miss, demonstrating practical value.
- Paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Strength: Correcting the Winsorized Poisson claim is a significant theoretical contribution.
- Strength: The three proposed models are well-motivated, theoretically sound, and address a longstanding limitation for count data.
- Strength: Experiments convincingly demonstrate advantages in both synthetic and real-world settings.
- Weakness: Computational trade-offs between proposed methods and existing approaches are not thoroughly discussed.
- Weakness: Practical limitations of each model (e.g., bounded domain for TPGM, Gaussian-like tails for QPGM) need more elaboration.
- Weakness: More analysis of parameter sensitivity (e.g., choice of R for TPGM and SPGM) is required.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically solid solutions to an important problem, supported by empirical evidence and relevant applications.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient treatment of computational complexity and practical limitations.
