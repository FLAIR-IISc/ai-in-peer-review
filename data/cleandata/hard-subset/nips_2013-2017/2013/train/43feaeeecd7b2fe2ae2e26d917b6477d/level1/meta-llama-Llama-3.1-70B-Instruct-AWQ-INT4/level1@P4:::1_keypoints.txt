- Paper introduces Truncated, Quadratic, and Sublinear Poisson Graphical Models to overcome PGMs' inability to model positive conditional dependencies.  
- Existing PGMs only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints; new models modify the Poisson sufficient statistic and base measure.  
- Builds on prior work framing PGMs as joint distributions with Poisson node-conditionals and relates to log-linear, copula, and multivariate Poisson approaches.  
- Novelty lies in modifying the Poisson sufficient statistic and base measure to enable richer dependence structures.  
- Strength: clear, concise exposition of limitations of existing PGMs and motivation for new approaches.  
- Strength: proposes three distinct model extensions (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) each with different trade-offs.  
- Strength: provides a thorough analysis of normalizability conditions for each proposed model.  
- Strength: demonstrates model performance with simulation studies and a breast cancer microRNA network case study.  
- Weakness: paper assumes strong background in graphical models and exponential families, limiting accessibility.  
- Weakness: limited discussion on interpretability of the proposed models in real-world applications.  
- Weakness: some mathematical derivations (e.g., proof of Theorem 3) are not fully presented, hindering verification.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed empirical or theoretical comparison with existing methods like Gaussian graphical models.  
- Pro-acceptance: introduces novel, well-motivated extensions that address a key limitation of PGMs.  
- Pro-acceptance: rigorous normalizability analysis strengthens theoretical validity.  
- Pro-acceptance: empirical results (simulations and case study) support practical utility.  
- Con-acceptance: accessibility concerns due to assumed technical background.  
- Con-acceptance: insufficient discussion of model interpretability for practitioners.  
- Con-acceptance: incomplete presentation of some mathematical proofs and derivations.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound with convincing analysis and valid proposed distributions.  
- Clarity assessment: well-organized and readable but assumes substantial prior knowledge.  
- Originality assessment: significant novel contribution by altering Poisson sufficient statistics and base measure.  
- Significance assessment: addresses a long-standing PGM limitation with potential impact across genomics, neuroimaging, and spatial statistics.
