

**Review: On Poisson Graphical Models**

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that prevents them from modeling both positive and negative conditional dependencies between count-valued variables. The authors build upon the work of Yang et al. [18, 19], who established PGMs where node-conditional distributions are Poisson, but note that the resulting joint distribution only permits non-positive edge weights due to normalizability constraints (Proposition 1). The paper correctly identifies that the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie [20] does not yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1), which is a significant correction to the literature.

The authors propose three novel approaches to overcome this limitation: (1) Truncated PGM (TPGM), which uses a truncated Poisson distribution to maintain exponential family properties; (2) Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which modifies the base measure to be quadratic; and (3) Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear. The paper provides rigorous theoretical justification for each approach, including normalizability conditions (Theorems 2-4) and connections to existing exponential family graphical models.

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness with clear mathematical formulations, well-structured proofs, and a thorough analysis of normalizability constraints. The authors correctly identify and address a major deficiency in previous work, providing a clear theoretical foundation for their novel contributions. The experimental section supports the theoretical claims, demonstrating the superiority of their methods over existing approaches (Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal, etc.) on both simulated data and a real-world breast cancer microRNA dataset. The case study shows that their methods identify biologically relevant hub miRNAs (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b) that existing approaches miss.

The paper is well-written, logically organized, and clearly explains the limitations of existing approaches while providing sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors adequately reference related work, including the key contributions of Yang et al. [18, 19] and the incorrect Winsorized approach by Kaiser and Cressie [20].

Strengths:
1. The identification and correction of the Winsorized Poisson approach (Theorem 1) is a significant theoretical contribution.
2. The three novel approaches (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are well-motivated, theoretically sound, and provide a practical solution to a long-standing limitation in graphical models for count data.
3. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed methods over existing approaches in both simulated and real-world settings.
4. The paper establishes clear connections to the broader literature on graphical models while making a distinct theoretical contribution.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could better elaborate on the computational trade-offs between the proposed methods and existing approaches, which is important for practical adoption.
2. The practical limitations of each model (e.g., bounded domain for TPGM, Gaussian-esque tail of QPGM) could be more thoroughly discussed.
3. The paper could provide more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity across different choices of R (for TPGM and SPGM).

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper identifies and correctly addresses a significant limitation in the existing PGM literature.
- The proposed approaches are novel, theoretically sound, and provide a solution to a practically important problem.
- The theoretical contributions are well-supported by empirical results.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of graphical models for count data, which is increasingly important in applications like genomics.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper does not sufficiently address computational complexity comparisons.
- The practical limitations of each model require more detailed exploration.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of graphical models by providing a theoretically sound framework for modeling multivariate count data with rich dependence structures. The authors' correction of the Winsorized approach (Theorem 1) is particularly valuable, as it corrects a misconception in the literature. The three novel approaches (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent a significant advancement in the field, addressing a clear gap in the existing literature.

This paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental theoretical problem with clear practical implications. The work is technically sound, novel, and significant, making it a strong candidate for acceptance. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches through both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation. The authors have successfully developed a framework for modeling count data that can capture both positive and negative dependencies, which has important applications in fields like genomics where count data is prevalent.
