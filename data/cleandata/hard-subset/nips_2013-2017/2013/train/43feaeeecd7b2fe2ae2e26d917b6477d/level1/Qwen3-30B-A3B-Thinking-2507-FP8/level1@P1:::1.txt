

**Review of "On Poisson Graphical Models"**

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which have been proposed as a natural extension of univariate Poisson distributions to model multivariate count data. The authors correctly identify that existing PGMs can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints, severely limiting their practical applicability. The paper makes three novel contributions: (1) a Truncated PGM (TPGM) that truncates the Poisson distribution to a finite domain {0, 1, ..., R}, (2) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) that modifies the base measure to be quadratic, and (3) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that uses sub-linear sufficient statistics. The authors rigorously prove the normalizability of their models and demonstrate their superiority over existing approaches on both simulated and real-world count data.

The paper begins by establishing the problem with existing PGMs, noting that the normalizability condition imposes non-positive edge weights. The authors then carefully analyze two approaches to truncating the Poisson distribution, showing that the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser and Cressie is invalid (Theorem 1), and proposing a valid truncated approach (Theorem 2). They further address the limitations of truncation by exploring fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution's base measure and sufficient statistics, leading to QPGM and SPGM. The theoretical development is thorough, with clear propositions and theorems that establish the conditions for normalizability while allowing both positive and negative dependencies.

The paper's main strength is its rigorous theoretical contribution, which fills a clear gap in the literature. The authors correctly identify and refute previous work, providing a solid foundation for their novel models. The experimental section demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed models, showing that they outperform existing approaches on count data in both simulated networks and a breast cancer microRNA-sequencing case study. The case study is particularly compelling, showing that the new models identify biologically meaningful hubs (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b) that were missed by Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM.

The paper is well-written and well-structured, with clear mathematical derivations and sufficient background for readers familiar with graphical models. The theoretical analysis is meticulous, with appropriate references to related work in NIPS proceedings (e.g., Yang et al. [18, 19] on graphical models via exponential families).

However, the paper has some limitations. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, with only two simulation scenarios (hub and scale-free networks) and a single case study. More extensive experiments comparing the computational efficiency of the proposed methods would strengthen the claims. Additionally, while the SPGM's sub-linear sufficient statistics are mathematically sound, the paper could better motivate why this particular form was chosen over other alternatives.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-supported claims. The experimental results are appropriate and support the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to address both strengths and limitations of their approaches.

The paper is highly original, making a clear contribution to the field. The three proposed models are distinct from previous work, and the identification of the invalidity of the Winsorized Poisson approach is particularly valuable. The paper's contributions are significantly different from those in recent NIPS proceedings, including Yang et al. [18, 19] which introduced the original PGM.

The significance of this work is substantial, as count data is ubiquitous in modern applications (genomics, spatial statistics, etc.). The proposed models have the potential to be widely adopted in these domains, and the case study demonstrates real-world applicability. The paper advances the state of the art by providing the first classes of Poisson-like graphical models that can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a clear, important limitation in existing Poisson graphical models
- Provides three novel, theoretically sound approaches to overcome this limitation
- Demonstrates practical utility through experiments on simulated and real data
- Well-written, well-structured, with rigorous theoretical analysis

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental evaluation (only one case study)
- Doesn't fully explore computational aspects of learning the models
- The SPGM's sub-linear sufficient statistics could be better motivated

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The theoretical development is rigorous, the experimental results support the claims, and the practical significance is clear. While the experimental section could be more comprehensive, the theoretical contributions are substantial and the paper should be accepted for NIPS. The work fills an important gap in the literature and has the potential to influence future research in graphical models for count data.
