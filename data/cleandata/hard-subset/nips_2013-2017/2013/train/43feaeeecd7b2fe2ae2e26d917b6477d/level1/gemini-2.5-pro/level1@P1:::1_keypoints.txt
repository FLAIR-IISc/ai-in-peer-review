- Paper addresses Poisson Graphical Models' inability to model positive conditional dependencies and its impact on real-world count data.  
- Reviews source of PGM parameter constraints and explains normalizability issues on infinite domain.  
- Proves Kaiser & Cressie (1993) Winsorized Poisson approach does not yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1).  
- Proposes Truncated PGM (TPGM) that preserves exponential family form and models both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Notes TPGM trade-offs: bounded domain and dependency between truncation level R and allowable parameters.  
- Introduces Quadratic PGM (QPGM) by modifying Poisson base measure to quadratic form, requiring a negative-definiteness condition for normalizability.  
- Introduces Sub-linear PGM (SPGM) by using sub-linear sufficient statistics with original Poisson base, allowing arbitrary dependencies and heavier tails than QPGM.  
- Demonstrates learning via penalized neighborhood selection adapted to TPGM and SPGM.  
- Empirical results on simulated hub/scale-free graphs and miRNA sequencing data show proposed models outperform Gaussian-based methods.  
- Theoretical contributions include constructive derivations for new models and proof correcting prior literature.  
- Paper is technically strong, with clear motivation, precise formalism, and careful discussion of model limitations.  
- Writing and organization are exceptionally clear, making contributions easy to follow.  
- Work is highly original, offering three principled solutions to a long-standing PGM limitation and novel refutation of a prior approach.  
- Significance is high given ubiquity of count data in domains like genomics and text analysis; provides a practical recipe for valid count graphical models.  
- Builds on Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2012, 2013) framework while substantially extending it for count data.  
- Main weakness: theoretical analysis of structure learning (sparsistency guarantees) is deferred to future work.  
- Practical weakness: hyperparameter tuning challenges (e.g., R, R0) are not deeply explored.
