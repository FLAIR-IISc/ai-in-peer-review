

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors identify and rigorously resolve this problem through three novel approaches: a Truncated PGM (TPGM), a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM). Previous work (e.g., [17, 18, 19, 20, 21]) had either failed to provide valid joint distributions for truncated Poisson models or was restricted to negative dependencies. The authors demonstrate that the Winsorized Poisson approach (suggested by Kaiser and Cressie [20]) cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1), and they propose a new truncation method that does (Theorem 2). They further show that modifying the base measure (QPGM) or sufficient statistics (SPGM) enables modeling both positive and negative dependencies.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. The authors prove key theorems about normalizability constraints, demonstrating that the base measure must scale quadratically for linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3). The experimental validation with simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA dataset provides strong empirical support. The paper acknowledges limitations of each approach (e.g., TPGM's bounded domain, QPGM's Gaussian-esque tail), demonstrating careful evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Mathematical formulations are precise, and the logical progression from problem statement to solution is easy to follow. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of computational aspects (e.g., efficient computation of the SPGM log-partition function), but the current presentation is sufficient for experts to understand and reproduce the methods.

**Originality:** This work is highly original. The authors identify a significant gap in the literature (the inability to model positive dependencies in PGMs) and provide three distinct, novel solutions. The paper clearly differentiates itself by showing why the Winsorized Poisson approach fails (Theorem 1) and by proposing fundamentally new variants (QPGM and SPGM) that build on the exponential family framework established in [18, 19].

**Significance:** This work addresses an important need for graphical models that can handle count data with rich dependence structures. The applications to genomic sequencing data (e.g., microRNA networks) demonstrate clear practical value. The paper advances the state of the art by resolving a fundamental limitation of existing PGMs, and the methods are likely to be widely adopted by practitioners working with count data. The results show clear advantages over existing approaches (Gaussian graphical models, original PGM), particularly in modeling both positive and negative dependencies.

**Strengths:**
1. Rigorous theoretical foundation with well-proven theorems
2. Clear demonstration of why previous approaches fail (Theorem 1)
3. Three novel approaches that collectively address the problem comprehensively
4. Strong empirical validation with both simulations and a real-world application
5. Clear differentiation from previous work with appropriate citations
6. Practical relevance to important applications in genomics and other fields

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability to very large graphs
2. The SPGM log-partition function has no closed-form solution, which may complicate practical implementation
3. The case study could have included more detailed biological interpretation of the findings

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Solves a significant, previously unaddressed problem in graphical models for count data
- Provides three theoretically sound and practically validated approaches
- Demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods in both simulations and a real-world application
- Advances the state of the art in a well-defined and important direction
- The work is likely to be widely adopted by researchers working with count data

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of computational aspects (though this may be a minor issue)
- The case study could have been more detailed

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of graphical models. The problem addressed is important, the solutions proposed are novel and well-justified, and the empirical validation demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. The theoretical contributions are substantial, and the practical applications are compelling. It represents a clear advance in the state of the art for graphical models of count data, which is increasingly important in modern data analysis. I recommend acceptance.
