- Paper addresses a key gap: classical exponential-family MRFs and canonical PGM cannot model positive conditional dependencies for counts.  
- Main contributions: (i) shows Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals do not admit a compatible joint (Theorem 1).  
- Main contributions: (ii) proposes Truncated Poisson Graphical Model (TPGM) as a valid exponential-family joint permitting positive and negative dependencies.  
- Main contributions: (iii) introduces Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM) as two non-truncation alternatives.  
- Theoretical insight (Theorem 3): linear sufficient statistics require at least quadratic base-measure growth to permit positive pairwise parameters.  
- QPGM implements quadratic base measure (or quadratic unaries) with normalizability via negative-definiteness of quadratic parameter matrix.  
- SPGM tempers sufficient statistics to be sublinear/saturating, retains Poisson base measure, and allows arbitrary pairwise parameters.  
- Structure learning via penalized nodewise neighborhood selection; simulations and TCGA miRNA study show improved edge recovery and richer networks than PGM.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on Besag’s auto-models and Yang et al.; contrasts with latent-sum multivariate Poisson and copula approaches.  
- Clarifies and corrects a misconception in spatial statistics by invalidating the Winsorized approach.  
- Theory quality is strong: careful impossibility result, existence/uniqueness for TPGM, and clear normalizability conditions for QPGM/SPGM.  
- Computational devices: variational bounds for conditional log-partitions (Gaussian bound for QPGM; Poisson bound for SPGM when θ≥0) are reasonable.  
- Empirics: simulations show ROC gains over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines; TCGA case study offers biologically plausible findings.  
- Empirics limitations: no comparison to other count-specific baselines (latent-sum, copulas), no held-out predictive likelihoods, and minimal runtime/scalability analysis.  
- Methodological gaps: sparsistency guarantees deferred; would benefit from sketches paralleling Yang et al.  
- Practical estimation gaps: computing D(θ,R0,R) for SPGM may require infinite sums and is unspecified when θ<0; unclear if QPGM experiments used exact or variational objectives.  
- Optimization concerns: need clarity on convexity, numerical stability, and handling piecewise sublinear B(·) in SPGM.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; recommend a compact summary table of models and expanded implementation/approximation details for reproducibility.  
- Originality: high — impossibility result, necessity of quadratic base measures, and the QPGM/SPGM constructions are novel and insightful.  
- Significance: substantial — enables modeling both positive and negative conditional dependencies for counts with practical applications (genomics, text, spatial).  
- Pros for acceptance: identifies a flaw in prior work, proposes three principled model classes, offers strong conceptual theorems, and shows scalable neighborhood-selection compatibility with promising empirical gains.  
- Cons for acceptance: estimation and computational details under-specified, no sparsistency or finite-sample guarantees, limited baselines/metrics, and little guidance on hyperparameter selection (R, R0).  
- Suggested revisions: clarify which objectives were optimized, detail how infinite sums are approximated (especially for θ<0), provide runtime/scalability results, add predictive held-out evaluations and comparisons to copula/latent-sum methods, and include sensitivity analyses and a sparsistency sketch.  
- Overall assessment: strong, original theoretical contributions with promising but preliminary empirical validation; lean toward acceptance pending strengthened experimental and methodological details.
