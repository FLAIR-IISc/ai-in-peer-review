This paper tackles an important gap in graphical modeling of multivariate count data. Classical exponential-family MRFs (Gaussian, Ising, multinomial) are ill-suited for counts; the canonical Poisson graphical model (PGM) allows only nonpositive pairwise parameters due to normalizability on an infinite support, thus capturing only negative conditional dependencies. The paper makes three main contributions: (i) it shows that a widely cited “Winsorized” truncation of Poisson node-conditionals (Kaiser & Cressie) does not admit a compatible joint (Theorem 1); (ii) it proposes a principled truncated Poisson graphical model (TPGM) that remains in the exponential family and yields a valid joint capable of positive and negative dependencies; (iii) it develops two non-truncation alternatives by modifying the Poisson base measure and/or sufficient statistics: a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM). A key theoretical insight (Theorem 3) is that if one keeps linear sufficient statistics, any normalizable joint allowing positive pairwise parameters requires a base measure with at least quadratic growth. QPGM implements this with a quadratic base measure (or equivalently quadratic unaries), while SPGM instead tempers the sufficient statistic to be sublinear/saturating, retaining the Poisson base measure and allowing arbitrary pairwise parameters. Structure learning is done via penalized nodewise neighborhood selection; simulations and a TCGA microRNA case study suggest improved edge recovery over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines and substantially richer networks than the original PGM.

Relation to prior work: The work builds on Besag’s auto-models and recent NIPS work framing graphical models from univariate exponential families (Yang et al.), and contrasts with multivariate Poisson constructions via sums of independent Poissons (only positive dependence, intractable) and copula-based models. The invalidation of the Winsorized approach clarifies a misconception in the spatial statistics literature.

Quality:
- Theory: The impossibility result for Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1, for R>3) is valuable and carefully stated. The existence/uniqueness of a joint for the TPGM follows cleanly from the exponential-family conditional construction. The necessity of quadratic (or heavier) base measure to permit positive dependencies with linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) is a strong conceptual result. The QPGM normalizability condition via negative definiteness of the quadratic parameter matrix is clear and interpretable. The SPGM normalizability for all pairwise parameters is appealing; the variational bounds for conditional log-partitions (Gaussian bound for QPGM; Poisson bound for SPGM when θ≥0) are reasonable computational devices.
- Empirics: Simulations demonstrate ROC improvements over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines in regimes where count-specific models should help. The TCGA case study offers qualitative biological plausibility (hub miRNAs, chromosome 19 cluster). However, empirical validation is limited: no comparison to other count-model baselines (e.g., multivariate Poisson via latent-sum constructions, copulas), no predictive held-out likelihood/performance, and minimal runtime/scalability analysis.
- Methodology gaps: The paper defers sparsistency guarantees to future work; while understandable, some sketch of conditions paralleling Yang et al. would strengthen claims. For SPGM, computing D(θ,R0,R) generally requires infinite sums; the practical estimation strategy is not fully specified when θ<0 (the provided Poisson bound applies to θ≥0). For QPGM, it is unclear whether experiments optimize the exact node-conditional likelihood or the Gaussian upper bound; the consequences for estimator bias are not discussed. Clarifying convexity and numerical stability of nodewise objectives (particularly with piecewise sublinear B(·) in SPGM) would improve the methodological completeness.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear motivation and theorems. The distinction between the two truncation schemes and the reason the Winsorized approach fails are well explained. Notational choices are mostly standard; a compact summary table of models (sufficient statistics, base measures, parameter constraints) would further help. Implementation details (optimization objective used in practice, stopping criteria, how infinite sums are truncated/approximated) should be expanded for reproducibility.

Originality:
- High. The impossibility of Winsorized conditionals admitting a joint, the necessity of quadratic base measures for linear sufficient statistics, and the concrete QPGM/SPGM constructions are, to my knowledge, new and insightful. They shift the design space from ad hoc truncation to principled modification of exponential-family components.

Significance:
- Significant. Modeling positive as well as negative conditional dependencies in counts is a long-standing practical need (genomics, text, spatial incidence). The proposed families broaden the toolkit beyond the restrictive PGM and could stimulate further methodological and theoretical work. The current empirical evidence is promising but would benefit from broader comparisons and quantitative downstream benefits.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear identification and proof of a flaw in prior truncation-based approaches.
- Three principled model classes that overcome the negative-dependence limitation.
- Conceptually strong theorem on base-measure growth for positive dependencies.
- Compatible with scalable neighborhood selection; initial empirical gains on counts.
- Relevant and timely application area with compelling qualitative results.

Cons for acceptance:
- Estimation procedure and computational details (exact vs variational objectives; handling θ<0 in SPGM) insufficiently specified and evaluated.
- No sparsistency or finite-sample guarantees at present; theory section is largely about normalizability rather than learning.
- Limited baselines and metrics; no predictive evaluation, ablations, or sensitivity to R and R0.
- Practical guidance for hyperparameter selection (R, R0) and robustness is minimal.

Suggestions:
- Clarify which objective is optimized for QPGM/SPGM in experiments, and discuss the impact of variational bounds on estimates.
- Provide computational complexity, convergence behavior, and scalability results; include runtime comparisons.
- Add predictive log-likelihood or held-out deviance and compare against copula-based and latent-sum multivariate Poisson methods.
- Include sensitivity analyses for R and R0 and guidance for choosing them from data.
- Sketch conditions under which neighborhood selection is sparsistent for SPGM/QPGM.

Overall assessment: Strong and original theoretical contributions that open a promising path to flexible count graphical models, with encouraging but preliminary empirical validation. With strengthened experimental and methodological details, this would be a very impactful NIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
