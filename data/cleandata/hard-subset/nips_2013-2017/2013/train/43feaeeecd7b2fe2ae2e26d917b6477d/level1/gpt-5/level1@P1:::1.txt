Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs), namely that normalizability on the infinite count domain forces all pairwise parameters to be nonpositive (Besag, Yang et al.), which prevents modeling positive conditional dependencies. The authors first revisit truncation: they prove that Kaiser & Cressie’s Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals cannot correspond to any consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1), correcting a claim in that literature. They then propose a valid exponential-family truncation that yields a proper MRF with Poisson-like node-conditionals and a finite partition function (TPGM; Theorem 2), enabling both positive and negative dependencies on a bounded domain.

To overcome drawbacks of truncation and preserve an unbounded count domain, the authors introduce two new Poisson-like exponential families by modifying either the base measure or the sufficient statistics. They show a necessary condition that, with linear sufficient statistics, the base measure must grow at least quadratically to allow both signs of dependencies while ensuring normalizability (Theorem 3). This motivates the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), with linear sufficient statistics and quadratic terms in the exponent; they give sufficient conditions for normalizability via a negative-definite quadratic form (Proposition 2) and relate the node-conditional log-partition to a Gaussian variational bound. Finally, they define a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with a piecewise sublinear sufficient statistic B(x; R0, R) and Poisson base measure; they show normalizability for all real pairwise parameters (Theorem 4) and bound its node-conditional log-partition by the Poisson one when θ ≥ 0 (Proposition 3).

Learning is via penalized neighborhood selection on node-conditional likelihoods (as in prior NIPS work on graphical models via univariate exponential families). Experiments include simulations (hub/scale-free graphs; data from TPGM or sums-of-independent-Poissons) and a TCGA breast cancer miRNA-seq case study. TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines and recover biologically plausible hubs and a chromosome 19 cluster; the original PGM recovers mainly negative edges.

Quality
– Theory: The negative result for Winsorization (Theorem 1) is important and clarifies a misconception; the condition R > 3 and the general functional form E(XV\s) are clearly stated, but a proof sketch or counterexample would make the argument more transparent. The constructive truncated model (Theorem 2) is clean and fits the Besag/Yang framework. The necessity result (Theorem 3) is a strong and insightful statement about base measure growth; a precise definition of Ω(Z^2) and assumptions (pairwise MRF, linear sufficient statistics) are clear. QPGM normalizability via a negative-definite quadratic form is standard; the Gaussian variational upper bound on node conditionals is useful for learning. The SPGM construction is elegant; Theorem 4’s claim of normalizability for all real θst is strong—more intuition, proof outline, and conditions on R0, R would strengthen confidence. Overall, the theoretical claims are well-motivated and plausible within the exponential-family MRF framework.
– Algorithms: The learning method (proximal gradient on penalized node-wise likelihoods) is appropriate, but practical details are sparse: how are partition functions and gradients evaluated for SPGM (infinite sums; truncation strategy, error control)? How is the Gaussian variational bound used in optimization for QPGM, and does it bias estimation? No complexity/runtime or convergence diagnostics are reported.
– Empirics: Simulations show ROC improvements over GGM/nonparanormal (reasonable baselines given their popularity at NIPS), and the case study offers qualitative biological validation. However, comparisons to other count models (e.g., Poisson log-normal/latent Gaussian, copula-based count models, sum-of-Poissons constructions) are missing, and quantitative goodness-of-fit (held-out likelihood) and sensitivity to hyperparameters R, R0 are not reported. Sparsistency is deferred.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The distinction between the two truncation strategies is clear, and the construction via exponential-family node-conditionals is consistent with prior NIPS work. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., define W and Wp explicitly when used; consistently define domains for Xs in each model). Provide intuition and (short) proof sketches for key theorems (especially Theorems 1, 3, 4). The figures are referenced appropriately, but more detail on experimental setup (regularization grids, proximal step sizes, stopping criteria, partition function approximations) would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The paper makes several novel contributions: the impossibility of Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals yielding a valid joint; a valid truncated exponential-family PGM; the necessity of quadratic (or heavier) base measure with linear sufficient statistics; and two new Poisson-like MRFs (QPGM and SPGM) enabling both signs of dependence on the infinite count domain. To my knowledge, these results were not present in prior NIPS proceedings, and they extend the Besag/Yang exponential-family MRF framework in a meaningful way.

Significance
Count-valued multivariate data are ubiquitous (genomics, spatial incidence, recommender systems). The inability of classical PGMs to capture positive conditional dependencies has been a serious limitation. This work offers principled alternatives with theoretical guarantees of normalizability and practical learning strategies. If accompanied by stronger empirical validation and clearer guidance on hyperparameters and computation, these models could be adopted by practitioners and spur further methodological work (e.g., consistency, scalable inference). The significance is high.

Pros
– Clear identification and rigorous resolution of a long-standing limitation of Poisson MRFs.
– Three new model classes with complementary trade-offs (bounded but simple TPGM; unbounded QPGM/SPGM).
– Insightful necessary condition on base measures; variational links to Gaussian models facilitate learning.
– Promising empirical results on simulations and a real miRNA-seq dataset with plausible biological findings.

Cons
– Limited algorithmic detail and practical guidance (partition function computation, variational optimization).
– No sparsistency or finite-sample guarantees; empirical evaluation lacks comparisons to other count-specific models.
– Sensitivity to R and (R0, R) not explored; choice of R = max count is data-dependent and may bias TPGM.
– SPGM bound DPois(θ) applies only when θ ≥ 0; handling θ < 0 in learning is unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
– Include proof sketches or intuitive counterexamples for Theorem 1; clarify why R > 3 is required and what happens for small R.
– Provide more detail on computation: how are infinite sums truncated; error bounds; effect on gradients and convergence; runtime.
– Add sensitivity analyses for R and (R0, R), and practical heuristics for choosing them.
– Compare against Poisson log-normal or latent Gaussian graphical models and copula-based count GMs where feasible.
– Report predictive log-likelihood or held-out perplexity, and edge sign recovery, in addition to ROC.
– Outline sparsistency conditions for TPGM/QPGM/SPGM following Yang et al., even if proofs are deferred.

Overall recommendation
A strong and timely contribution with solid theoretical advances and promising empirical results. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying proofs for key theorems and adding practical computational details.
