Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the classical auto-Poisson/Poisson MRF constructed from Poisson node-conditionals (Besag; Yang et al.) is only normalizable when pairwise parameters are non-positive, thus capturing only negative conditional dependencies. The authors first analyze truncation-based fixes. They show that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditional proposed in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith), which concentrates all probability mass above a threshold R at X=R, cannot be made compatible with a joint MRF consistent with conditional distributions of the Besag/Yang type (Theorem 1). They then introduce an alternative exponential-family truncation that redistributes the tail mass across the truncated domain, yielding a valid MRF (TPGM) with finite state space and no sign constraints on edge parameters (Theorem 2), while noting its practical limitations as R grows.

Motivated by the normalizability mechanism, they consider more fundamental modifications to the univariate exponential family. They prove that with linear sufficient statistics B(X)=X, allowing both positive and negative interactions in a normalizable joint distribution necessitates a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This leads to a Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which augments the Poisson-like model with quadratic node terms and permits positive and negative interactions under a negative-definiteness-type condition on the quadratic parameter matrix (Proposition 2). The authors note that QPGM exhibits Gaussian-like tails and derive a Gaussian variational upper bound on the node-conditional log-partition to enable tractable node-wise estimation.

To obtain heavier tails without a quadratic base measure, they propose a Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which retains the Poisson base measure log(x!) but replaces B(x) with a sublinear, saturating sufficient statistic that is linear up to R0, then diminishes to a plateau at R. This ensures normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4) and yields a Poisson-based variational upper bound for nonnegative canonical parameters (Proposition 3). For learning, they use `1-penalized neighborhood selection for all proposed models. Simulations (data from TPGM and sums-of-Poissons constructions) indicate that TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines on ROC recovery, and a TCGA miRNA-seq case study suggests more plausible biological structure than classical PGM and GGM.

Quality (soundness and empirical support)
- Theory: The negative result for Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1) addresses a long-standing folk-construction; the statement is precise (R>3, general functional form of the canonical parameter), and, if the proof is correct, convincingly rules out that approach. The TPGM construction is a straightforward application of exponential-family node-conditionals consistent with a finite-state MRF and is standardly sound (Theorem 2). The necessity of a quadratic-or-heavier base measure for linear sufficient statistics (Theorem 3) is an insightful contribution; the statement is clear (Ω(Z^2) growth), though it would benefit from explicit regularity assumptions and a brief proof sketch in the main text. The QPGM normalizability condition (Proposition 2) is reasonable and parallels Gaussian negative-definiteness; sufficient conditions are clearly stated. The SPGM normalizability (Theorem 4) leverages the saturation of B(x); the result appears plausible and useful.
- Algorithms: The node-wise estimation programs are sketched rather than fully developed. For QPGM and SPGM, node-conditional log-partitions involve infinite sums; the paper suggests variational upper bounds (Gaussian for QPGM; Poisson for SPGM with θ≥0). More detail is needed on how these bounds are used in optimization (e.g., tightness, bias in gradients, convergence guarantees) and how negative θ are handled in SPGM. The absence of formal sparsistency results for the new models is noted as future work.
- Experiments: Simulations are sensible and support the claims that Poisson-like models outperform Gaussian/copula baselines on count data, with TPGM best when the data are from TPGM. The real-data case study is illustrative, and the identified hubs have biological support, though no gold-standard network is available and quantitative evaluation is limited. Runtime and scalability are not discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a helpful progression from negative results to constructive alternatives. Notation is consistent with prior exponential-family graphical model literature. Some technical results (especially Theorem 3, SPGM optimization details) would benefit from additional intuition and brief proof outlines in the main text. Practical guidance on choosing R (TPGM) and (R0,R) (SPGM) is limited.

Originality
The paper makes several original contributions: a rigorous impossibility result for Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals in MRFs; a principled truncated Poisson MRF (TPGM); a necessary growth condition on the base measure to allow positive interactions with linear sufficient statistics; and two new families (QPGM, SPGM) that broaden the feasible dependence structures for count-valued MRFs. These extend and complement prior work on auto-Poisson models and exponential-family graphical models.

Significance
Modeling conditional dependence among counts is important in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains. The proposed families substantially expand the modeling toolkit beyond the classical negative-dependence-only PGM, and the theoretical insights (especially Theorem 3) help chart the design space. While estimation theory and some computational aspects remain underdeveloped, the work is likely to spur follow-on research and practical adoption for network inference from count data.

Pros
- Clear identification and resolution of a fundamental limitation of auto-Poisson MRFs.
- Strong and useful negative result on Winsorization; clarifies misconceptions in the literature.
- Introduction of three concrete model families covering finite-state truncation (TPGM), quadratic base measure (QPGM), and saturating sufficient statistics (SPGM).
- Theoretical normalizability conditions are explicit and interpretable.
- Empirical evidence that Poisson-like models outperform Gaussian/copula baselines on count data; relevant real-data case study.

Cons
- Learning procedures for QPGM/SPGM rely on variational bounds; optimization details, approximation quality, and convergence guarantees are insufficiently specified.
- No sparsistency or finite-sample guarantees for structure learning with the new models.
- Practical guidance for selecting truncation or saturation parameters (R, R0) is limited; sensitivity analyses are absent.
- Comparisons omit some alternative approaches for counts (e.g., copula-based count models or negative binomial variants), although such methods may be less directly comparable.
- Global consistency of node-wise learned parameters (symmetry enforcement, conditions ensuring a valid joint) is not discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide proof sketches or intuition for Theorem 1 and Theorem 3 in the main text, and clarify assumptions.
- Elaborate on optimization: how the variational bounds are incorporated, handling θ<0 in SPGM, gradient computation, and any convergence guarantees.
- Discuss parameter selection for TPGM (R) and SPGM (R0,R), with sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify how symmetry across node-wise regressions is enforced to ensure a coherent joint model.
- Include runtime/scalability results and, if possible, additional comparisons with alternative count-based dependencies.
