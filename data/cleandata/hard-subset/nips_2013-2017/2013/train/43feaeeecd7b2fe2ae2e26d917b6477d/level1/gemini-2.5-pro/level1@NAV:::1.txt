This paper addresses a critical limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs), namely their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints on their infinite domain. This work builds directly upon the foundational PGM framework established by Besag (1974) and generalized in prior NIPS proceedings by Yang et al. (2012, 2013). The authors make several key contributions: they first prove that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson approach is invalid for constructing a joint distribution. They then introduce three novel and valid graphical models for count data that permit both positive and negative dependencies: the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The performance of these models is demonstrated through structure recovery on simulated networks and a compelling application to microRNA sequencing data.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper solves a well-known and highly restrictive problem, substantially increasing the practical applicability of PGMs to real-world count data where variables can be both complementary and competitive. This is a significant advance for modeling in fields like genomics and social sciences.
*   **Originality & Quality:** The contributions are highly original. The disproof of the Winsorized PGM is an important technical correction to the literature. The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are elegant, well-motivated, and theoretically sound. The authors clearly analyze the trade-offs of each approach, such as the thin tails of the QPGM versus the heavier tails of the SPGM. The experimental results convincingly show the superiority of the proposed methods over Gaussian-based approaches and the original PGM.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly explains the deficiency in existing models before methodically presenting and justifying each of its novel solutions.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The TPGM and SPGM models introduce new hyperparameters (R and R0) whose selection is critical. While the paper uses a reasonable heuristic in the experiments (setting R to the maximum observed count), a more detailed discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters and guidance for their selection would strengthen the practical utility of the methods.
*   While the focus is on model specification, the theoretical guarantees (e.g., sparsistency) for the proposed neighborhood selection learning procedure are noted as future work. Including such analysis would make the paper a more complete contribution for the NIPS audience.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper provides a clear and significant solution to a fundamental limitation of an important class of graphical models. The proposed models are novel, well-supported by theory, and validated with strong empirical results.
*   **Con:** The discussion on hyperparameter selection is brief, and theoretical guarantees for the learning algorithm are deferred.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial and valuable contribution to the literature on graphical models for count data.
