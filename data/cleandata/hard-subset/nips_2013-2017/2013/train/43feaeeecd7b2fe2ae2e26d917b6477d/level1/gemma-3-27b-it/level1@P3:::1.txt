This paper tackles a significant problem: extending graphical models to effectively handle count data, a growing modality in many scientific fields. The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard Poisson graphical models (PGMs) – their inability to model negative correlations – and propose several novel approaches to overcome this restriction. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a comprehensive investigation of different modifications to the Poisson distribution for use in graphical modeling.

The core contribution lies in demonstrating the impossibility of directly applying truncation (Winsorization) as suggested by Kaiser & Cressie (20), and then introducing the Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The theoretical analysis demonstrating the need for at least quadratic base measures to achieve both positive and negative correlations is particularly strong and insightful. The exploration of different sufficient statistic modifications in the SPGM is also a valuable addition.  The empirical validation on both simulated data and a real-world miRNA dataset further strengthens the paper's claims.  The comparison with existing methods (Gaussian GGM, nonparanormal) and the original PGM estimator provides a useful benchmark.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. While the theoretical foundation is solid, some aspects lack sufficient detail. For instance, the justification for the specific form of the sublinear sufficient statistic in the SPGM feels somewhat arbitrary. A more detailed discussion of alternative functional forms and their implications would be beneficial.  Also, the connection between the variational upper bound used in the QPGM learning and the actual performance of the model isn’t fully elaborated. How well does this approximation hold in practice, and how does it affect the accuracy of the learned graph structure?  

The numerical experiments, while comprehensive, could be strengthened by a more rigorous statistical comparison. Reporting p-values alongside the ROC curves would allow for a more definitive assessment of the differences between the methods. Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis regarding the choice of regularization parameters and the truncation level (R) in the TPGM and SPGM would be valuable. The specific choice of R0 in the SPGM is mentioned but lacks justification; how does varying R0 affect the performance?

The discussion of computational complexity is also missing.  While the authors mention using proximal gradient ascent, they don't discuss the scalability of their methods to very high-dimensional datasets. Given the increasing size of count-data datasets (e.g., single-cell RNA sequencing), this is an important consideration.  

Finally, while the authors appropriately cite relevant work, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship to other recent advances in modeling count data, such as generalized additive models for count data or newer copula-based approaches.  The limited discussion of alternatives outside the graphical model framework feels like a missed opportunity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM offer genuinely novel approaches to modeling multivariate count data with graphical models, overcoming a key limitation of existing PGMs.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed models, including a proof of the impossibility of using simple truncation and a clear rationale for the required modifications to the Poisson distribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on both simulated and real-world data, outperforming existing alternatives.
* **Significance:**  The ability to model both positive and negative correlations in count data has significant implications for a wide range of applications in genomics, epidemiology, and other fields.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* **Lack of Detail:** Some aspects of the model specification (e.g., the choice of sublinear function in SPGM) lack sufficient justification.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The paper doesn't address the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed methods.
* **Incomplete Comparison:** The numerical comparison could be strengthened by more rigorous statistical analysis and a more comprehensive discussion of alternative approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of graphical modeling. The authors address a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach, supported by compelling empirical results.  I believe the paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, with a request for the authors to address the points raised above regarding the justification of model choices, scalability discussion, and statistical rigor in the experimental evaluation.  I would rate this a **strong accept**, contingent on minor revisions.
