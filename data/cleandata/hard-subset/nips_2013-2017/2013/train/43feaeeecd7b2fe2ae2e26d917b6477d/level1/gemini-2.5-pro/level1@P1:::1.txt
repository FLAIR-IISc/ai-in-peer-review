This paper addresses a critical and well-known limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs): their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints on their infinite domain. This restriction severely limits their application to real-world count data where variables often exhibit cooperative relationships. The authors propose three novel classes of "Poisson-like" graphical models that overcome this limitation.

The paper begins by reviewing the standard PGM and the source of its parameter constraints. It then analyzes a previously proposed solution based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution by Kaiser and Cressie (1993), proving in Theorem 1 that this approach does not lead to a valid joint distribution, a significant corrective contribution. The authors then introduce their first novel model, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), which uses a different truncation scheme that preserves the exponential family form and thus yields a valid graphical model capable of representing both positive and negative dependencies. They thoughtfully discuss the trade-offs of this approach, namely its bounded domain and the interaction between the truncation level `R` and the allowable parameter range.

Recognizing the limitations of truncation, the paper then explores more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. This leads to the second and third contributions: the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and the Sub-linear PGM (SPGM). The QPGM modifies the base measure of the Poisson to be quadratic (akin to a Gaussian), which ensures normalizability for arbitrary edge weights, provided a negative-definiteness condition holds. The SPGM modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear while retaining the original Poisson base measure, which also allows for arbitrary dependencies and results in a model with heavier tails than the QPGM. Finally, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of the TPGM and SPGM by applying a penalized neighborhood selection algorithm to learn network structures from both simulated data and real-world microRNA sequencing count data, showing superior performance compared to Gaussian-based methods.

This work builds directly upon the foundational framework for constructing graphical models from conditional exponential family distributions, established by Besag (1974) and extended by Yang et al. (2012, 2013). By identifying and solving a core problem within this framework for count data, the paper makes a substantial contribution.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs (for the invalidity of the Winsorized approach) and constructive derivations for the new models. The motivation for each model is sound, progressing logically from the failure of one approach to the development of a more sophisticated one. The authors are careful to discuss the limitations and characteristics of each proposed model (e.g., the trade-offs of TPGM, the Gaussian-like tail of QPGM). The experimental validation is solid, using relevant simulation settings (hub, scale-free graphs) and a compelling real-world application in genomics. The results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed models outperform standard methods like Gaussian graphical models on count data.

A minor weakness is that the theoretical analysis of the structure learning algorithm (i.e., sparsistency guarantees for neighborhood selection applied to these new models) is mentioned as future work. While the empirical results are strong, providing these guarantees would have made the contribution more complete.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The problem statement is crisp, and the narrative guides the reader smoothly from the limitations of existing models to the proposed solutions. The mathematical formalism is precise without being overly dense. The structure is logical, dedicating sections to each major idea (truncation, modifying the base measure, modifying the sufficient statistics). This organization makes the paper's novel contributions easy to identify and appreciate. The experimental section is also clearly presented, with figures that effectively communicate the results.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While PGMs are not new, the problem of modeling both positive and negative dependencies has been a long-standing and significant challenge. The paper's main contribution is a set of three novel and principled solutions to this problem. The refutation of the previously proposed Winsorized Poisson approach is itself a novel and important finding. The conceptual leaps to modify the base measure (QPGM) and sufficient statistics (SPGM) are particularly innovative and move beyond simple fixes like truncation. While the use of penalized neighborhood selection for learning is a standard technique, its application to these new model classes is novel and appropriate. The paper is well-situated in the literature and clearly distinguishes its contributions from prior work.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. Count data is ubiquitous in machine learning applications, from genomics (RNA-seq) and document analysis to social network event counts and spatial statistics. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive dependencies has been a major barrier to their widespread adoption. This paper provides a suite of theoretically-grounded and practically-viable models that directly address this bottleneck. Researchers and practitioners in various fields are likely to find these models useful and build upon them. The demonstration on miRNA sequencing data highlights the immediate impact on computational biology. By providing a clear "recipe" for constructing valid graphical models for count data with rich dependencies, this work significantly advances the state of the art.

**Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Solves a fundamental, long-standing problem in graphical models for count data.
*   Provides three distinct, novel, and theoretically sound model classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
*   Corrects a flawed approach in the prior literature (the Winsorized PGM).
*   Exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically structured.
*   Strong empirical results on both simulated and important real-world biological data.
*   High potential for impact in multiple application domains where high-dimensional count data is prevalent.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis for the learning procedure (structure recovery guarantees) is deferred to future work.
*   The practical challenges of hyperparameter tuning for the new models (e.g., `R`, `R0`) are not deeply explored.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution to the modeling of high-dimensional count data. The strengths in originality, quality, and significance far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It addresses a clear need in the community with elegant and effective solutions.
