Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles a well-known limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) as defined via node-conditionals in the exponential family [17,18,19]: normalizability forces nonpositive pairwise parameters, hence only negative conditional dependencies are allowed. The authors (i) formally refute prior proposals to use Winsorized (point-mass-at-R) Poisson node-conditionals in auto-Poisson models [20,21], proving that such node-conditionals cannot be compatible with any joint MRF (Theorem 1); (ii) propose a valid truncated exponential-family variant (TPGM) based on the Poisson conditioned to {0,…,R}, which admits both positive and negative edges (Theorem 2); (iii) show a necessary condition for any Poisson-like EF-MRF with linear sufficient statistics to allow positive edges while remaining normalizable: the base measure must scale at least quadratically (Theorem 3); (iv) develop a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with linear sufficient statistics and quadratic base measure, normalizable under a negative-definiteness condition on the quadratic form (Proposition 2), hence allowing both signs of dependency; and (v) introduce a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with bounded, sublinear sufficient statistics B(·;R0,R), preserving the Poisson base measure, yielding heavier tails and normalizability for all real pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They outline nodewise ℓ1-penalized conditional likelihood estimation with proximal gradient, provide variational upper bounds (Gaussian for QPGM, Poisson for SPGM under θ≥0), and demonstrate ROC performance on simulated data and a TCGA miRNA network, where TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-based baselines and the original PGM.

Quality:
- Theory: The direction is well-motivated and the main technical claims are plausible and valuable. The incompatibility of Winsorized node-conditionals (Theorem 1) addresses a persistent misconception in spatial statistics; however, the statement is quite strong (“no joint for any E(·)”) and hinges on R>3. Please provide full proofs or at least a constructive counterexample and intuition (e.g., via Brook’s lemma/Hammersley–Clifford) in the appendix; also clarify the exact regularity assumptions on E(·).
- Theorem 3 (C(z)=Ω(z^2) necessity) is an elegant and impactful insight; it would benefit from a precise, self-contained statement: over which set of θ does A(θ)<∞ need to hold (existence of some positive edges? a nonempty open set with θst>0?), and are there corresponding sufficiency results when C(z)≈z^2?
- QPGM normalizability (Proposition 2): the condition XTΘX ≤ −c||X||^2 seems sufficient; please clarify whether Θ comprises both diagonal θss and off-diagonal θst and whether negativity of Θ is required or negative-definiteness suffices (you state both). Also discuss the practical implications: for high-degree graphs, ensuring Θ≺0 may be restrictive; give examples where positive edges are feasible under this constraint.
- Computation: For QPGM and SPGM, node-conditional log-partitions are infinite sums. The Gaussian integral bound for QPGM is reasonable, but the optimization then targets an upper bound, introducing bias; discuss the effect on consistency and whether tighter truncation-based approximations are used in practice. For SPGM, the Poisson upper bound applies only for θ≥0; how is the general case handled? The paper should specify how log-partitions and gradients are computed (exact sums with truncation heuristics? dynamic bounds?) and complexity.
- Empirics: Simulations compare against Gaussian/nonparanormal methods and PGM. Given the application domain, comparison to Poisson log-normal or negative binomial latent variable models (common for sequencing data) would strengthen the case (even if joint likelihoods are intractable, there are pseudolikelihood or variational implementations). Also, given SPGM is a primary contribution, it is odd not to show recovery when data are generated from SPGM. Please add experiments assessing sensitivity to R and R0, and show calibration (edge sign recovery, parameter error) and computational cost.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Some notational issues should be fixed: define W and Wp consistently; define Θ precisely and separate diagonal vs off-diagonal components. The description of SPGM’s B(·;R0,R) would benefit from intuition and a clearer plot. The miRNA case study states R=11 as the maximum count; this seems inconsistent with typical RNA-seq counts—please clarify preprocessing (e.g., normalization, filtering, binning) and justify chosen R,R0.

Originality:
The combination of (i) formal refutation of Winsorized node-conditionals, (ii) valid truncated EF construction (TPGM), (iii) a necessary quadratic-growth condition for base measures, and (iv) two concrete models (QPGM, SPGM) that lift the negativity constraint is novel and timely. The sublinear sufficient statistic construction is particularly creative. Related work is broadly cited, but discussion of negative-binomial-based models and Poisson log-normal approaches could be expanded.

Significance:
Allowing both positive and negative conditional dependencies in count-valued MRFs is important for many applications (genomics, spatial incidence). The theoretical insights (Theorem 3) can guide future EF-MRF design beyond Poisson. Practical impact hinges on robust, scalable estimation; the presented algorithms are promising but need more computational detail and stronger empirical validation against competitive count models.

Pros:
- Addresses a well-known and important limitation of PGMs with clear theoretical contributions (Theorems 1–4).
- Provides three concrete, tractable classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) enabling positive dependencies.
- Demonstrates improved graph recovery over Gaussian-based baselines; compelling case study with biologically plausible hubs.

Cons:
- Some key theoretical claims need fuller proofs/conditions and tighter statements.
- Computational aspects (evaluation of log-partitions, convergence, complexity) are under-specified; variational bounds may bias estimation.
- Experimental comparisons omit strong count-data baselines; sensitivity to hyperparameters (R, R0) and generation from SPGM are missing.
- Practical constraints for QPGM (Θ≺0) may limit applicability; guidance is needed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include full proofs or extended sketches for Theorems 1 and 3; clarify assumptions and scope.
- Detail optimization: how node-conditional log-partitions are computed/approximated, truncation strategies, error control, and runtime scaling.
- Add comparisons to Poisson log-normal and negative binomial models; include SPGM-generated data and sensitivity analyses for R,R0.
- Provide guidance on setting R and R0 (e.g., via quantiles, cross-validation) and discuss trade-offs.
- Clarify preprocessing in the miRNA study and reconcile the reported maximum count.
- Discuss identifiability and concavity of nodewise problems for SPGM/QPGM (conditions ensuring convex objectives with ℓ1).

Recommendation:
This is a strong and original paper with meaningful theoretical and modeling advances for count graphical models. With strengthened proofs and computational/empirical details, it would be a valuable NeurIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the above concerns.
