- Addresses key limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): existing PGMs can only represent negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints.
- Corrects prior work by proving the Winsorized Poisson approach (Kaiser & Cressie [20]) cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1).
- Proposes three new graphical-model families that allow both positive and negative dependencies: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).
- Provides rigorous theoretical development with normalizability proofs (Theorems 3–4) that establish validity of the proposed models.
- Shows how to learn these models via neighborhood selection and outlines practical estimation procedures.
- Demonstrates empirical effectiveness on simulations, with TPGM particularly strong in high-dimensional settings compared to Gaussian-based methods.
- Case study on breast cancer microRNA sequencing finds biologically meaningful hub miRNAs (e.g., mir-375, mir-10b) that original PGM and Gaussian models miss, showing practical utility.
- Paper is original and significant, filling an important gap in modeling count data dependencies and representing a substantial theoretical advance.
- Theoretical arguments and proofs are sound, well-constructed, and build logically on prior work.
- Writing and organization are clear and accessible to the NIPS audience; introduction and mathematical presentation are precise.
- Experimental evaluation is focused and convincing but could include more comparative baselines (e.g., fuller comparison to non-paranormal SKEPTIC [10]).
- SPGM’s sufficient statistic (Equation 7) is mathematically complex and would benefit from a clearer intuitive explanation of its "soft" linear behavior.
- Major limitation: paper omits discussion of computational complexity and scalability for very large graphs (e.g., p ≥ 1000).
- Theoretical learning guarantees (sparsistency) for the proposed models are only briefly mentioned and need fuller development.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, original contribution with strong theoretical and empirical results; recommends acceptance despite scope for future work on scalability and theoretical guarantees.
