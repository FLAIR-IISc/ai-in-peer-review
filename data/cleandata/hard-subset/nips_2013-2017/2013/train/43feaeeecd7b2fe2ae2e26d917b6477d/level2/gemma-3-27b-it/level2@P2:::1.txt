This paper addresses the problem of modeling multivariate count data using graphical models, a task for which standard approaches like Gaussian graphical models are ill-suited. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models – their inability to model negative dependencies – and propose several novel extensions to overcome this. They explore truncation strategies, ultimately finding that a simple truncation approach fails, and then introduce the Truncated PGM (TPGM), the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper provides theoretical justification for these models and demonstrates their performance on both simulated and real (microRNA sequencing) data.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles an important problem. The detailed analysis of the shortcomings of existing Poisson graphical models and the careful derivation of the proposed alternatives are strengths. The mathematical treatment appears rigorous, with clear propositions and theorems supporting the claims. The exploration of multiple approaches (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) is commendable, showing a thorough investigation of the problem space. The inclusion of both simulation studies and a real-world application strengthens the evaluation. The comparison against existing methods (Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal models, original PGM) is helpful and demonstrates the advantages of the proposed models, particularly the SPGM.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the mathematical exposition is generally clear, some sections, particularly those involving the derivations of the QPGM and SPGM, are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The justification for the specific choices made in the SPGM’s sub-linear sufficient statistic (R0 and R) isn’t fully explained, and the impact of these parameters on model performance isn't thoroughly investigated beyond their mention in the experiments. The simulation studies, while present, lack detailed discussion of parameter choices and sensitivity analysis. For example, the network structures used (hub and scale-free) are standard, but a wider variety of structures or more realistic data generating processes might provide a more robust evaluation. The experimental results section is somewhat brief; a more in-depth analysis of the performance differences between the proposed models and the baselines (beyond simply stating superiority) would be valuable. The discussion on computational complexity, while implicit in the mention of using proximal gradient ascent, is not explicit. Specifically, it's unclear how the computational cost scales with the problem size (p, the number of variables). While the authors acknowledge limitations related to the log-partition function in SPGM, a more detailed discussion of approximation strategies or alternative inference methods would be beneficial. Related work on multivariate count data modeling beyond graphical models (e.g., latent variable models) could also be briefly discussed to provide a broader context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound extensions to Poisson graphical models that address a significant limitation of existing methods.
*   Clear mathematical derivation and justification of the proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM).
*   Demonstrated performance improvements on both simulated and real-world datasets compared to existing methods.
*   Addresses an important problem with increasing relevance in fields dealing with count data (genomics, epidemiology, etc.).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Mathematical derivations in some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   Limited in-depth analysis and sensitivity analysis of experimental results.
*   Lack of a thorough discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   The rationale for specific parameter choices (e.g., in SPGM) isn't fully explained.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. Despite some minor weaknesses in presentation and experimental detail, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and demonstrated performance of the proposed models are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on clarifying the mathematical derivations, providing a more detailed discussion of the experimental results, and addressing the computational complexity aspects.
