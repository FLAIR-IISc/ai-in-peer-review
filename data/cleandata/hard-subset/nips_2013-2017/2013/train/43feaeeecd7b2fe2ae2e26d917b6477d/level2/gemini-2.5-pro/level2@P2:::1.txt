This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), namely their inability to model positive conditional dependencies. The authors correctly identify that the normalizability condition of the classical PGM distribution forces all pairwise interaction parameters to be non-positive, which severely restricts its applicability to real-world count data where variables can be both positively and negatively correlated. The paper makes several significant contributions to overcome this problem.

First, the authors analyze a previously proposed solution based on a "Winsorized" Poisson distribution and prove rigorously (Theorem 1) that this approach does not lead to a valid joint distribution, correcting a misconception in the literature. This is a valuable contribution in its own right.

Second, the paper proposes three novel classes of "Poisson-like" graphical models capable of capturing rich dependency structures:
1.  **Truncated PGM (TPGM):** A new truncation scheme is introduced that, unlike the Winsorized version, results in a valid joint distribution belonging to the exponential family. This model allows for both positive and negative dependencies but requires pre-specifying a truncation level `R`.
2.  **Quadratic PGM (QPGM):** This model addresses the infinite domain by modifying the base measure of the Poisson distribution to be quadratic. This ensures normalizability under conditions similar to Gaussian Graphical Models (e.g., a negative-definite parameter matrix), but results in a distribution with thin, Gaussian-like tails.
3.  **Sub-linear PGM (SPGM):** To allow for heavier tails while maintaining a flexible parameter space, this model modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear for large counts. This clever construction bounds the interaction terms, ensuring the distribution is normalizable for any set of real-valued pairwise parameters.

Finally, the authors demonstrate the utility of their proposed models (TPGM and SPGM) through simulations and an application to learning a microRNA expression network from sequencing data, showing superior performance over Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and important open problem in the modeling of multivariate count data. Providing a theoretically sound framework for graphical models of counts with mixed dependencies is a major advance for the field and has clear applications in areas like genomics, neuroscience, and social sciences.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The contributions are highly original. The disproof of the Winsorized PGM's validity is a key theoretical result. The three proposed models are novel, well-motivated, and elegant solutions that arise from a deep understanding of why the original PGM fails. The theoretical analysis of normalizability for each model appears sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the progression from analyzing the problem to proposing a series of increasingly sophisticated solutions is logical and easy to follow. The trade-offs associated with each proposed model (e.g., bounded domain for TPGM, thin tails for QPGM) are honestly discussed.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The proposed TPGM and SPGM models introduce new hyperparameters (`R` for TPGM; `R0` and `R` for SPGM) that are critical to their performance. The paper sets these based on the maximum observed count in the data, which is a reasonable heuristic. However, the work would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion on the sensitivity of the models to these choices. A discussion of potential data-driven methods for selecting these parameters, perhaps via cross-validation, would make the methods more accessible to practitioners.
2.  **Details of Learning Algorithm:** The paper mentions using proximal gradient ascent for `l1`-penalized neighborhood selection. While this is a standard approach, more detail would be beneficial. For the SPGM in particular, the node-conditional log-partition function is intractable. The paper provides a variational upper bound (Proposition 3), but it is not specified whether this bound was used for optimization in the experiments or if a numerical approximation was employed. Clarifying the exact objective function being optimized would improve the paper's reproducibility.
3.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments compellingly show the advantage of TPGM and SPGM over existing methods. However, the QPGM is introduced theoretically but not evaluated empirically. While the authors argue it has Gaussian-like properties, including it in the comparisons, even if just to confirm this intuition, would provide a more complete picture of the proposed family of models.
4.  **Theoretical Guarantees for Learning:** The authors correctly note that their models fit within the framework of Yang et al. [18, 19] and state that sparsistency analysis is left for future work. While a full theoretical analysis is likely beyond the scope of a single conference paper, a brief discussion or sketch of how existing consistency proofs for neighborhood selection could be adapted to these new conditional likelihoods would bolster confidence in the learning procedure.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the field of graphical models. It clearly identifies and solves a major deficiency in existing models for count data. The proposed models are novel, theoretically sound, and practically relevant. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily concern implementation details and hyperparameter sensitivity, which do not detract from the core conceptual and theoretical contributions of the work. The paper is of high quality, clarity, originality, and significance, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
