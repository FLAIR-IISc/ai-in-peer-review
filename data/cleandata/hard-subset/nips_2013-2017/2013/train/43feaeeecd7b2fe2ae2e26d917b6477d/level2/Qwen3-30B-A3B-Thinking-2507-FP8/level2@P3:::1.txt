

The paper addresses a significant limitation in existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which can only represent negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors propose three novel approaches—Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that enable modeling both positive and negative dependencies in count-valued data. This work extends Yang et al.'s NIPS 2012 framework for Poisson graphical models by rigorously analyzing the mathematical requirements for normalizability and dependency flexibility.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through four key theorems. Theorem 1 correctly identifies the flaw in Kaiser and Cressie's Winsorized Poisson approach, showing it cannot yield a consistent joint distribution. Theorem 2 establishes TPGM as a valid model with a finite domain, while Theorems 3 and 4 prove that QPGM (with quadratic base measure) and SPGM (with sublinear sufficient statistics) achieve normalizability with unrestricted edge parameters. The theoretical development is comprehensive, connecting to exponential family properties and providing clear proofs of normalizability conditions. The empirical validation on simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA network demonstrates practical utility, with SPGM and TPGM outperforming Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM on count data.

Clarity is well-served by the logical structure: problem identification (Section 2), theoretical foundations (Section 3), and empirical validation (Section 4). The paper clearly differentiates the proposed models from prior work, particularly clarifying why truncation approaches must be carefully designed to preserve exponential family properties. The connection to existing NIPS work (e.g., Yang et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2012) is appropriately contextualized, though the discussion of related work could more explicitly contrast with non-paranormal approaches used in count-data applications.

Originality is a clear strength. The paper identifies a fundamental gap in Poisson graphical models that has persisted since Besag's initial work, and the three proposed variants offer distinct mathematical pathways to resolve it. The QPGM's quadratic base measure and SPGM's sublinear sufficient statistics represent novel constructions not previously explored in graphical models for count data. The work builds on but significantly extends Yang et al.'s framework, moving beyond the restrictive negative-edge constraint.

Significance is demonstrated through both theoretical advancement and practical impact. The ability to model positive dependencies expands the applicability of graphical models to count data in genomics, spatial statistics, and user behavior analysis. The microRNA network case study provides concrete evidence of improved biological insights: TPGM and SPGM recover known breast cancer-related miRNAs (mir-375, mir-10b) and a functionally relevant chromosome 19 clique that the original PGM misses. This suggests real-world utility beyond theoretical novelty.

Constructive suggestions for improvement include: (1) More detailed discussion of practical considerations for choosing truncation parameter R in TPGM, particularly how R should scale with data size or domain characteristics; (2) Expansion of computational details for SPGM learning, as the log-partition function's infinite summation may pose implementation challenges; (3) A brief analysis of when each model (TPGM/QPGM/SPGM) would be preferable based on tail behavior or dependency patterns. The current discussion of R=11 in the case study lacks justification for this specific choice.

The paper's main limitation is that it focuses exclusively on model specification without fully addressing computational scalability for very large p (e.g., p>1000). While neighborhood selection is mentioned, the computational complexity of learning SPGM/QPGM compared to Gaussian or PGM methods isn't quantified. This is a reasonable omission given space constraints but would strengthen the significance claim.

Overall, the paper makes a substantial contribution to graphical models for count data. The theoretical results resolve a long-standing limitation, and the empirical results validate practical utility. The three proposed models collectively provide a comprehensive solution space for count-valued graphical modeling, with SPGM offering particularly flexible tail behavior. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretically grounded, high-impact methods for complex data. The only notable gap is the lack of comparison to alternative count-data models like negative binomial graphical models, though this may be beyond the paper's scope. The quality of theoretical development, clarity of presentation, and demonstrated significance strongly support acceptance.
