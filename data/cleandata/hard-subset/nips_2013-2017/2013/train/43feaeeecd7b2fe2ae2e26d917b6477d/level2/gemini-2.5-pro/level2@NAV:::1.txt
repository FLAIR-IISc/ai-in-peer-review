This paper addresses a significant and well-known limitation of Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs): their inability to model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors make several key contributions to overcome this issue. First, they rigorously analyze truncated Poisson distributions, formally proving that a previously proposed "Winsorized" approach is invalid for constructing a joint distribution. They then introduce a novel, valid Truncated PGM (TPGM). Recognizing the limitations of truncation, the paper proposes two more fundamental and powerful modifications: the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which alters the base measure, and the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which uses sub-linear sufficient statistics. This work builds directly upon the foundational framework of Besag (1974) and Yang et al. (2012) but provides a crucial extension for modeling the rich, mixed-dependency structures often found in real-world count data.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a critical open problem. The proposed QPGM and SPGM are novel, well-motivated, and provide the first general framework for PGMs that can model both positive and negative dependencies. This represents a substantial advancement for modeling ubiquitous count data in fields like genomics.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical contributions are sound and clearly presented. The proof discrediting the Winsorized PGM approach is a strong contribution in its own right. The derivations for the proposed models and the analysis linking the base measure to normalizability (Theorem 3) are insightful and technically solid.
*   **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, and the progression from simple truncation to more fundamental model modifications is logical. The inclusion of experiments on both simulated and real-world miRNA sequencing data effectively demonstrates the practical utility and superior performance of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper defers a formal theoretical analysis of structure recovery (sparsistency) to future work. While understandable given space constraints, a brief discussion on how existing theoretical guarantees might extend to these new models would be beneficial.
*   Practical guidance on setting the hyperparameters for the new models, especially R0 and R for the SPGM, is limited. A more detailed discussion on their selection or a data-driven approach would enhance the models' applicability.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant and well-supported contribution. The arguments for acceptance are strong: it solves a major problem with novel and theoretically grounded models, supported by convincing empirical evidence. I strongly recommend acceptance.
