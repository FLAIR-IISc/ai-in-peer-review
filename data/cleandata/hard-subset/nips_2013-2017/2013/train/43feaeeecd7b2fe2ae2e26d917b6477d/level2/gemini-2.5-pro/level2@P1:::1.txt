This paper addresses a critical limitation of standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs), which, due to normalizability constraints on an infinite domain, can only model negative conditional dependencies. This severely restricts their applicability to real-world count data where relationships can be both positive and negative. The authors propose three novel classes of graphical models for count data that overcome this issue.

The paper first investigates truncation-based approaches. It makes the important negative finding that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson graphical model is, in fact, not a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1). The authors then propose a new, valid truncation approach, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), which uses an exponential family formulation on a finite domain {0, ..., R}. This model can capture both positive and negative dependencies but has limitations related to the choice of the truncation level R.

To move beyond truncation, the paper explores more fundamental modifications to the Poisson distribution. The authors provide a key insight (Theorem 3) that for models with linear sufficient statistics (like the standard PGM), the base measure must grow at least quadratically to ensure normalizability with positive dependencies. This motivates their second model, the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which uses a quadratic base measure, resulting in a Gaussian-like model for counts that can handle arbitrary dependencies provided the parameter matrix is negative definite. Finally, to allow for heavier tails than the QPGM, the authors propose a third model, the Sub-linear PGM (SPGM), which retains the original Poisson base measure but modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear and bounded. This clever construction ensures the model is normalizable for any real-valued pairwise parameters. The paper concludes with experiments on simulated and real-world microRNA sequencing data, demonstrating that the TPGM and SPGM outperform methods based on Gaussian assumptions for network recovery from count data.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions are sound and well-supported by theorems and propositions. The identification of the flaw in the previously proposed Winsorized PGM is a valuable correction to the literature. The motivation for the QPGM and SPGM is built on a clear theoretical argument about the interplay between the base measure, sufficient statistics, and normalizability, which is insightful. The experimental evaluation, while primarily illustrative of the new models, is well-executed. It demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed methods on both synthetic and real datasets, showing clear improvements over relevant baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the core problem with existing PGMs and logically develops its solutions, progressing from simple truncation to more fundamental modifications. Each new model is well-motivated, and the theoretical results are stated clearly. The narrative is easy to follow, making a complex topic accessible.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) are novel contributions to the literature on graphical models for count data. While building on the general framework of constructing graphical models from exponential families, the specific constructions and the analysis leading to them are new. Correcting the record on the Winsorized PGM is also a novel and important contribution. The paper correctly positions itself relative to prior work and clearly distinguishes its contributions.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are very significant. Modeling multivariate count data is a fundamental problem in many fields, including genomics, social sciences, and spatial statistics. The inability of standard PGMs to model positive dependencies has been a major roadblock. By providing several principled, flexible, and tractable models that overcome this limitation, this work opens the door to more realistic and accurate modeling of complex count data. Researchers and practitioners in various domains are likely to find these models useful and build upon them. The work represents a clear advance in the state of the art for this problem domain.

**Strengths:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and well-known limitation in an important class of models.
*   Provides three novel and well-motivated graphical models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) for count data capable of capturing both positive and negative dependencies.
*   Includes a significant negative result, demonstrating that a previously proposed model (Winsorized PGM) is invalid.
*   The theoretical analysis providing the conditions for normalizability is insightful and guides the model design.
*   The paper is very clearly written and structured.
*   Experimental results on both simulated and real-world data demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed models.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The practical application of the TPGM and SPGM introduces new hyperparameters (R for TPGM; R0 and R for SPGM). The paper provides limited guidance on how to set these parameters, which could be sensitive and crucial for performance in practice.
*   The Quadratic PGM (QPGM) is theoretically motivated but not included in the experimental evaluation. While the authors suggest it behaves like a Gaussian graphical model, a direct empirical comparison would have strengthened the paper.
*   While the paper demonstrates structure learning via neighborhood selection, it leaves a formal analysis of the statistical consistency (e.g., sparsistency) of these estimators for future work. Including such results, even briefly, would have made the contribution more complete.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to an important problem. It is theoretically sound, clearly presented, and empirically validated. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to practical details and extensions for future work, and they do not detract from the importance of the core contributions. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
