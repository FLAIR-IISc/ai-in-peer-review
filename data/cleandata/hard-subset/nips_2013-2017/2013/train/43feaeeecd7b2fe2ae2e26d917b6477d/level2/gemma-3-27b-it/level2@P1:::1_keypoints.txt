- Tackles modeling multivariate count data and limitations of Gaussian graphical models for this task.  
- Identifies inability of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) to model positive dependencies.  
- Analyzes truncation strategies and proves a previously suggested Winsorized Poisson approach is invalid.  
- Introduces a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM) as an alternative truncation approach.  
- Proposes two modified Poisson-based models: Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  
- Provides rigorous mathematical analysis of the proposed models and their properties.  
- Demonstrates empirical performance on simulations and a real microRNA sequencing dataset.  
- Clearly connects to and cites related graphical model literature ([17,18,19]).  
- Paper is well-motivated and presents novel, theoretically grounded model extensions.  
- Mathematical derivations (especially QPGM and SPGM) are dense and hard to follow, hurting clarity.  
- Lacks intuitive justification for the quadratic base measure in QPGM.  
- SPGM’s sufficient statistic B(x;R0,R) is introduced without sufficient motivation or theoretical properties.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited: simulations compare mainly TPGM and SPGM, missing broader baselines (e.g., negative binomial graphical models).  
- Real-data analysis uses only one dataset and offers weak biological interpretation of findings (e.g., hub miRNAs).  
- The variational upper bound for QPGM feels peripheral and doesn’t substantially strengthen the analysis.  
- Neighborhood selection claim for structure learning lacks implementation details and discussion of challenges.  
- Sparsistency and other theoretical guarantees are deferred, with only brief discussion of expected estimator properties.  
- Originality is strong in the specific model constructions, though the general idea of modifying Poisson to allow positive/negative dependencies is not entirely novel.  
- Practical significance is moderate and depends on computational feasibility and comparisons to simpler alternatives.  
- Acceptance arguments: important problem, novel/theoretically sound approaches, rigorous analysis, promising real-data results.  
- Rejection arguments: poor clarity, limited empirical validation, weak justification for some modeling choices, and unresolved computational issues.  
- Overall recommendation: Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after improving clarity, empirical validation, and practical discussion.
