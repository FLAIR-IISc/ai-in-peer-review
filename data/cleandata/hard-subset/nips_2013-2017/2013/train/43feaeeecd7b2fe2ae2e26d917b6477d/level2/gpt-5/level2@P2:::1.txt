Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a long-standing limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): normalizability forces nonpositive pairwise parameters, restricting conditional dependencies to be negative. The authors first re-examine truncation-based fixes. They show that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals used in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie, 1997; Griffith, 2003) cannot be compatible with any joint MRF in the Besag/Yang framework (Theorem 1). They then propose a different exponential-family truncation (TPGM) whose node-conditionals lead to a valid joint MRF (Theorem 2), allowing both positive and negative dependencies, albeit on a finite domain.

Moving beyond truncation, they study when exponential-family count MRFs with linear sufficient statistics can admit positive edges while remaining normalizable. They prove that the base measure must grow at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates (i) QPGM, which retains linear sufficient statistics but adds quadratic node terms; normalizability holds if the quadratic parameter matrix is negative definite, permitting mixed-sign dependencies (Proposition 2), and (ii) SPGM, which preserves the Poisson base measure but replaces the linear sufficient statistic with a sublinear, saturating B(x; R0, R), yielding heavier tails and normalizability for all pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They sketch practical neighborhood-selection with ℓ1 regularization using variational bounds for intractable nodewise log-partitions.

Empirically, they compare TPGM and SPGM to Gaussian, nonparanormal, and SKEPTIC estimators on simulated hub and scale-free graphs and on TCGA miRNA-seq data, showing improved ROC behavior on counts and qualitatively richer biological networks than the original PGM (which only finds negative edges).

Strengths
- Clear identification and formal resolution of a longstanding issue with Poisson MRFs; Theorem 1 appears novel and corrects common practice in parts of the spatial statistics literature.
- Constructive alternatives: a valid truncated exponential-family model (TPGM), a principled necessity result (Theorem 3) motivating QPGM, and a sublinear-sufficient-statistic model (SPGM) that allows mixed-sign dependencies with heavier tails.
- Sound use of the Besag/Yang exponential-family conditional-to-joint construction; the proposed families are transparent and interpretable.
- Empirical evidence that count-aware models outperform Gaussian/copula baselines on count data; real-data network results are plausible and biologically motivated.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical gaps around learning and computation:
  - Convexity/concavity of the penalized node-conditional problems is not discussed. For QPGM and SPGM, are the conditional log-likelihoods concave in the nodewise parameters? If not, what are guarantees for proximal gradient ascent?
  - Sparsistency is deferred; while [Yang et al. 2012, 2013] suggests a path, extending their conditions to QPGM/SPGM (with different log-partitions and tail behavior) requires nontrivial work. Even a theorem statement outlining sufficient conditions would strengthen the paper.
- Practicality of SPGM:
  - The sublinear sufficient statistic B(x; R0, R) saturates. How are R0 and R selected from data? If many counts exceed R0, B carries little incremental information, potentially harming identifiability. The paper sets R0=5, R=11 in the case study without justification or sensitivity analysis.
  - Nodewise log-partition for SPGM lacks a closed form; the Poisson upper bound DPois(θ)=exp(θ) applies only when θ≥0. How is inference handled when θ can be negative? What is the impact of the bound’s looseness on estimation bias?
- TPGM limitations are acknowledged but under-explored empirically. Setting R to the observed maximum induces boundary effects that can create spurious hubs; this is visible in the case study. A sensitivity analysis over R and a check for boundary inflation are needed.
- Comparisons:
  - The original PGM is mentioned “results not shown.” Even if only negative edges are recoverable, including its ROC (over signed detection) would be informative.
  - Relevant alternatives for count networks (e.g., Poisson lognormal models, negative binomial or PLN-based graphical models, log-linear network models for sequencing data) are not empirically compared. Given [Allen & Liu, 2012] is cited, a direct quantitative comparison on the TCGA data would be appropriate.
- Clarity and completeness:
  - Theorem 3 is a central contribution. Its statement needs precise conditions (e.g., growth conditions on C(·), tail regime, role of graph size) and a proof sketch to convey the mechanism (why Ω(Z^2) is necessary).
  - Proposition 2 uses a matrix Θ collecting pairwise and quadratic terms; clarifying the exact mapping from θst and θss to Θ, and giving concrete examples of mixed-sign edge patterns under Θ≺0, would help.
  - Computational details (gradient, proximal steps, step-size, convergence, runtime) are minimal; reproducibility would benefit from these.

Quality
The modeling contributions are technically sound and well-motivated; the negative result (Theorem 1) and necessity result (Theorem 3) are significant. However, the learning methodology requires more theoretical and practical detail, and some empirical choices (SPGM parameters, TPGM R) are not justified.

Clarity
Generally well written with good intuition and references. Some proofs and algorithmic details are missing from the main text; several notational definitions (e.g., Wp) should be clarified.

Originality
High. The impossibility of Winsorized conditionals, the necessity of quadratic base measure for linear sufficient statistics, and the SPGM construction are, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
High potential. Enabling mixed-sign dependencies for count MRFs fills a real gap in graphical modeling for modern count data. With stronger learning guarantees and broader comparisons, this could become a standard toolkit.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise statement and proof sketch for Theorem 3; include intuition about the competition between quadratic interactions and base measure growth.
- Analyze convexity of nodewise problems; state conditions ensuring a unique optimum, or describe the optimization of variational surrogates and its impact on consistency.
- Detail estimation for SPGM when θ can be negative; propose tighter bounds or truncation-sum approximations with error control.
- Include sensitivity analyses for R and (R0, R); report boundary frequencies and their effects on degree distribution.
- Add comparisons to Poisson lognormal/negative binomial copula/PLN-based methods and to your prior log-linear sequencing network model on the TCGA data, with quantitative metrics (held-out likelihood or stability, not just visuals).
- Report runtimes and convergence behavior.
- Include PGM baseline curves, even if limited to negative-edge recovery.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important modeling limitation with principled new families and meaningful theoretical insights.
- Negative-result contribution (Theorem 1) corrects prior practice.
- Empirical results on counts are promising and relevant to NIPS.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete development of estimation theory and optimization details; unclear practical guidance for SPGM hyperparameters.
- Limited empirical comparisons and sensitivity analyses; some baselines omitted.

Overall, I lean toward acceptance if the authors can strengthen the learning/optimization details and empirical evaluation as suggested.
