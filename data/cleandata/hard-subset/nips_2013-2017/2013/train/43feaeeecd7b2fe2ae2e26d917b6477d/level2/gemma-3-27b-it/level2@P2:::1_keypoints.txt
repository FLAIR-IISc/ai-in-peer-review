- Addresses modeling multivariate count data with graphical models and the inadequacy of Gaussian approaches for counts.
- Identifies the key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models: inability to model negative dependencies.
- Proposes three novel extensions: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).
- Finds simple truncation approaches fail and develops TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM as alternatives.
- Provides theoretical justification with clear propositions and theorems supporting the proposed models.
- Demonstrates model performance on simulated data and real microRNA sequencing data.
- Compares proposed models against Gaussian graphical models, non-paranormal models, and original PGM, highlighting SPGM’s advantages.
- Well-motivated problem with rigorous mathematical treatment and multiple explored approaches.
- Inclusion of both simulations and a real-world application strengthens empirical evaluation.
- Some derivations, especially for QPGM and SPGM, are dense and need more intuitive explanation.
- Rationale for specific SPGM sub-linear sufficient statistic choices (R0 and R) is not fully explained.
- Impact of SPGM parameter choices on performance is not thoroughly investigated beyond experiments.
- Simulation studies lack detailed discussion of parameter choices, sensitivity analysis, and broader network structures.
- Experimental results section is brief and lacks in-depth analysis of differences between models and baselines.
- Computational complexity and scalability (dependence on p) are not explicitly discussed.
- Limitations related to the SPGM log-partition function are acknowledged but approximation/inference strategies are not detailed.
- Related work on multivariate count modeling beyond graphical models (e.g., latent variable models) is missing broader context.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound extensions addressing a significant limitation with demonstrated empirical improvements.
- Arguments against acceptance: some mathematical exposition needs intuition, limited sensitivity/experimental analysis, unclear scalability, and unexplained parameter choices.
- Overall assessment: significant contribution worth acceptance with minor revisions to clarify derivations, expand experimental detail, and address computational complexity.
