- Addresses limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) that only capture negative conditional dependencies.  
- Proposes three novel PGMs that allow both positive and negative dependencies: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM).  
- Proves the Winsorized Poisson approach from prior work is invalid for constructing joint distributions (Theorem 1).  
- Provides rigorous theoretical proofs showing TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM are normalizable and can model positive and negative dependencies.  
- Highlights importance of base measure scaling (Theorem 3) and design of sufficient statistics (notably for SPGM).  
- Empirical simulations show TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian graphical models on count data.  
- Real-data application to breast cancer microRNA sequencing finds biologically meaningful hub miRNAs missed by Gaussian models.  
- Strengths: clear problem identification, solid theoretical contributions, and good positioning relative to prior work.  
- Novelty: original and meaningful extension of exponential-family graphical models to handle richer count dependencies.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation limited to only two network structures (hub and scale-free), lacking varied topologies and sparsity patterns.  
- Weakness: lacks sparsistency/consistency analysis for structure learning, weakening claims about learnability from data.  
- Weakness: missing direct empirical comparisons to other count-modeling approaches (multivariate Poisson via sums, copula methods).  
- Clarity: generally well-organized with consistent notation, but some explanations (QPGM Gaussian-like tails, SPGM heavier tails) need more intuition.  
- Missing discussion of trade-offs between models (computational complexity, parameter sensitivity).  
- Scalability concern: paper does not address performance or feasibility in very high-dimensional settings (e.g., p > 1000).  
- Quality: theoretical work is sound, but empirical validation and computational analysis should be strengthened.  
- Overall: significant and original contribution with strong theory and promising empirical results; would benefit from broader experiments, comparisons, and scalability analysis.
