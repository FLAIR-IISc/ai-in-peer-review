- Problem: Standard Poisson graphical models cannot model positive conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints, limiting applicability to real-world count data.

- Contribution 1: Proves the previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson construction is invalid for node-conditional specification, correcting the literature.

- Contribution 2: Proposes the Truncated PGM (TPGM) with a different truncation that preserves exponential-family form and can model both positive and negative dependencies, with limitations tied to truncation level.

- Contribution 3: Introduces the Quadratic PGM (QPGM), using a quadratic base measure to allow arbitrary interaction parameters but yielding thinner (Gaussian-like) tails.

- Contribution 4: Introduces the Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which uses sub-linear sufficient statistics to retain Poisson-like heavy tails while permitting both positive and negative dependencies.

- Learning & Experiments: Demonstrates neighborhood selection procedures for TPGM and SPGM and provides simulation and real miRNA-seq experiments showing feasibility and utility versus continuous-data methods.

- Quality: Paper is technically strong, with rigorous theorems (notably Theorem 1) and well-supported claims about normalizability and model properties.

- Clarity: Exceptionally well-written and organized; mathematical details and model descriptions are precise and accessible to experts.

- Originality: Highly original work that addresses a long-standing problem and offers three novel classes of count graphical models plus a corrective theoretical result.

- Significance: Significant contributions with broad applicability to domains with count data (genomics, text, social sciences) and likely strong impact on future research.

- Suggestion 1: Add guidance on model selection among TPGM/QPGM/SPGM and hyperparameter choice (e.g., R, R0), including sensitivity analysis or data-driven selection methods.

- Suggestion 2: Include empirical evaluation of QPGM to complete comparative analysis and assess its performance relative to GGM.

- Suggestion 3: Provide a brief sketch of theoretical guarantees for neighborhood-selection learning (sparsistency) to bolster learning claims.

- Suggestion 4: Discuss computational complexity for each model and potential effects of SPGM's variational partition-function approximation on estimation accuracy.
