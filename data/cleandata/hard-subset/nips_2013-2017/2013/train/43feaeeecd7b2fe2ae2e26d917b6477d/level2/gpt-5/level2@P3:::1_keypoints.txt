- Addresses Poisson graphical model (PGM) limitation that classic EF-GM constructions only normalize with non-positive pairwise parameters, preventing positive conditional dependencies.  
- Revisits truncation approaches and proves Winsorized Poisson node-conditionals cannot be consistent with any Besag/Yang joint (Theorem 1).  
- Proposes an exponential-family truncation (TPGM) that preserves EF structure and proves existence of a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 2).  
- Shows that with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability without sign constraints requires base-measure growth at least quadratic (necessity result, Theorem 3).  
- Introduces Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure motivated by Theorem 3.  
- Introduces Sublinear PGM (SPGM) using a sublinear/saturating sufficient statistic and log-factorial base to allow unrestricted edge signs (Theorem 4).  
- Provides variational upper bounds for node-wise estimation applicable to QPGM and SPGM.  
- Empirical results: TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines in simulations and recover plausible structure in microRNA sequencing data.  
- Theoretical contributions are clear, well-motivated, and correct long-standing assertions in spatial statistics.  
- Definitions, organization, and intuition are generally clear, though minor notation switches and typos exist.  
- Practical guidance on choosing truncation/saturation parameters (R, R0) is under-specified.  
- Originality: novel negative result on Winsorized conditionals, necessity of quadratic base measures, and SPGM design are nontrivial and original.  
- Significance: enables modeling of both positive and negative dependencies for ubiquitous count data, filling a substantive modeling gap.  
- Strength: provides two complementary model families (QPGM for thin tails, SPGM for heavier tails) with neighborhood-selection estimation.  
- Weakness: lacks statistical guarantees (sparsistency) for neighborhood selection; only deferred discussion.  
- Weakness: tuning of R and R0 is insufficiently specified and can induce boundary effects if set poorly.  
- Weakness: computational details for SPGM (infinite sums, truncation, runtimes) are not fully quantified, impacting scalability assessment.  
- Weakness: comparisons omit other practical count-model baselines (e.g., negative-binomial surrogates, latent-factor Poisson, Gaussian-copula Poisson).  
- Weakness: QPGM interpretability vs Gaussian GGM and identifiability/parameter-constraint guidance in high dimensions need clearer discussion.  
- Essential suggestion: add theorem sketch or appendix with high-level conditions for neighborhood-selection consistency for TPGM/SPGM.  
- Essential suggestion: provide practical procedures and sensitivity analysis for choosing R and R0.  
- Essential suggestion: report computational costs and implementation details (truncation schemes, runtimes, convergence).  
- Optional suggestion: include an additional baseline like Gaussian copula with Poisson marginals or negative-binomial surrogates.  
- Optional suggestion: clarify QPGM vs Gaussian surrogates and provide guidance on when to prefer QPGM vs SPGM.  
- Minor suggestion: unify notation and fix typographical errors.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong theory resolving a known limitation, usable models permitting both edge signs, and relevant empirical validation.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing statistical guarantees, limited hyperparameter guidance and computational detail, and narrower baseline comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: solid, timely contribution that advances count graphical modeling; with modest additions on theory presentation and practical guidance it is a strong NIPS candidate.
