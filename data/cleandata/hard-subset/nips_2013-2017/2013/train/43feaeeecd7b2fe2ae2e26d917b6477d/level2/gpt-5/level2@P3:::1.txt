Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses a key limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs): the classical auto-Poisson/EF-GM construction (Besag, 1974; Yang et al., NIPS 2012; arXiv 2013) yields normalizable joint distributions only when pairwise parameters are non-positive, restricting conditional dependencies to be competitive. The work examines whether modifying the univariate Poisson family can yield valid joint MRFs supporting both positive and negative dependencies.

First, it revisits truncation. It shows that the “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditionals proposed in spatial statistics (Kaiser & Cressie, 1997; Griffith, 2010) cannot be compatible with any joint distribution in the Besag/Yang construction (Theorem 1). It then proposes an alternative exponential-family truncation that preserves EF structure and proves the existence of a consistent joint MRF (TPGM; Theorem 2). Next, it analyzes fundamental modifications to the Poisson family. It proves that, with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability without sign constraints requires a base measure that scales at least quadratically (Theorem 3). This motivates a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with quadratic base measure and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that retains the log-factorial base measure but replaces the linear sufficient statistic with a sublinear, saturating function, yielding heavier tails and no constraints on pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). Variational upper bounds for node-wise estimation are provided for QPGM and SPGM. Empirically, TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian and nonparanormal baselines on simulations and reveal plausible structure in microRNA sequencing data, while the classical PGM recovers predominantly negative edges.

Quality:
The theoretical contributions are clearly stated and largely well-motivated. Theorem 1 corrects a long-standing assertion in applied spatial statistics; Theorem 2 provides a principled EF truncation yielding a tractable joint; Theorem 3 offers an illuminating necessity result on base-measure growth; and Theorem 4 establishes normalizability for SPGM with unrestricted edge signs. The claims are supported by formal statements and by intuitive discussion (e.g., tail behavior and normalizability). The estimation strategy via neighborhood selection with variational bounds is reasonable, though statistical guarantees are deferred.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized, with clear separation of truncation-based and EF-modification approaches. Definitions and notation are mostly consistent, though there are minor notational switches (e.g., W vs X) and occasional typographical roughness. Practical guidance on choosing truncation/saturation parameters (R, R0) could be clearer.

Originality:
The negative result on Winsorized node-conditionals, the necessity of quadratic base-measure scaling, and the design of SPGM with sublinear sufficient statistics appear novel and nontrivial. The QPGM and SPGM constructions provide new, interpretable families that extend EF-GMs to count data with rich dependencies.

Significance:
Count-valued multivariate data are ubiquitous (sequencing, incidence, site visits). Enabling both positive and negative conditional dependencies while retaining EF-GM structure directly impacts structure learning practice. The work fills a substantive modeling gap and is likely to be used and extended.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and resolution of a core limitation of PGMs for counts.
- Rigorous corrective result about Winsorized Poisson conditionals (Theorem 1).
- Conceptually elegant necessity result (Theorem 3) guiding model design.
- Two practically distinct alternatives (QPGM vs SPGM) covering thin- vs heavy-tail regimes.
- Neighborhood selection pipeline leveraging EF node-conditionals.
- Empirical results demonstrate advantages over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines and provide a relevant biological case study.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Statistical guarantees are deferred. Given reliance on neighborhood selection, even a sketch of conditions for sparsistency (in the style of Yang et al., 2012; Ravikumar et al., 2010) would strengthen the contribution.
- Practical tuning of R and R0 is under-specified. Setting R to the observed maximum can induce boundary effects; guidance via, e.g., cross-validated likelihood, quantiles, or prior-informed caps would be useful. Sensitivity analyses are limited.
- Computational aspects are not fully quantified. Node-wise estimation for SPGM involves infinite sums or variational bounds; details on truncation schemes, error control, and runtimes for p=262 would help assess scalability.
- Comparisons omit other count models used in practice (e.g., negative binomial graphical surrogates, latent-factor multivariate Poisson constructions, copula Poisson variants). Even if joint likelihoods are intractable, including a strong baseline such as a Gaussian-copula with Poisson marginals would contextualize gains.
- QPGM interpretability vs Gaussian GGM: when quadratic terms dominate, QPGM may behave similarly to GGMs; a more explicit discussion of regimes where QPGM provides distinct advantages would clarify its niche.
- Identifiability and parameter constraints for QPGM (negative definiteness vs elementwise negativity) deserve more practical guidance, especially in high dimensions.

Suggestions for improvement:
Essential:
- Add a brief theorem sketch or appendix pointer stating high-level conditions under which neighborhood selection is consistent for TPGM/SPGM (dependencies, tail conditions, incoherence, sample scaling).
- Provide practical procedures for choosing R and R0, with a short sensitivity analysis on simulations.
- Report computational costs and implementation details (summation truncation for SPGM, convergence criteria, per-node runtimes).

Optional:
- Include an additional baseline (e.g., Gaussian copula with Poisson marginals or a negative-binomial-based surrogate).
- Clarify the relationship between QPGM node-conditionals and Gaussian surrogates; give guidance on when to prefer QPGM vs SPGM.
- Minor editorial pass to unify notation and fix typos.

Pros and cons for acceptance:
Pros:
- Strong, well-motivated theory resolving a known limitation.
- Introduction of practically usable models with both positive and negative dependencies.
- Empirical benefits on simulated and real count data.
- Relevance to a wide range of NIPS topics.

Cons:
- Missing statistical guarantees in the current version.
- Limited guidance on hyperparameter selection and computational details.
- Comparisons could be broader vis-à-vis alternative count-modeling baselines.

Overall assessment:
A solid and timely contribution that advances graphical modeling for counts beyond the restrictive auto-Poisson framework. With modest additions on theory presentation and practical guidance, the paper would be a strong fit for NIPS.
