Summary and relation to prior work: This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the necessity of non-positive edge weights for normalizability on an infinite count domain. The authors (i) prove that “Winsorized” node-conditionals from Kaiser & Cressie cannot yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1), (ii) introduce a valid exponential-family truncation that yields a Truncated PGM (TPGM) supporting both positive and negative dependencies, and (iii) show that, with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability with mixed-sign edges requires a base measure growing at least quadratically (Theorem 3). Motivated by this, they propose the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) with piecewise sublinear sufficient statistics, both admitting positive and negative conditional dependencies; they derive normalizability conditions and variational bounds enabling neighborhood selection. Empirically, on simulations and a TCGA microRNA case study, TPGM/SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods and the original PGM. The work is well-situated relative to Besag’s auto-models and Yang et al.’s exponential-family graphical models, and clarifies misconceptions in prior truncation attempts.

Quality: The theoretical contributions are substantive and mostly sound, particularly the impossibility result for Winsorization and the necessity of quadratic base measures. The learning procedure via penalized node-conditional likelihood is standard and appropriate. However, sparsistency is deferred; inference and computational complexity for SPGM (infinite sums) are not fully characterized, and empirical evaluation lacks statistical significance tests and broader baselines (e.g., Poisson log-normal, negative binomial/counted GLM networks, copula-based count models).

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Some parts (e.g., SPGM’s piecewise statistic and choice of R0,R) would benefit from more intuition, guidance on hyperparameter selection, and computational details.

Originality: High. The negative result for Winsorization, the Ω(Z^2) base-measure requirement, and the SPGM construction are novel and impactful.

Significance: Strong. Allowing positive and negative dependencies for counts fills an important gap for genomics and other domains, and the methods appear practically useful.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear theoretical advances (Theorems 1–4) resolving a long-standing limitation.
- Three concrete model classes with learning recipes and encouraging results.
- Relevance to NIPS communities on graphical models and high-dimensional inference.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Missing sparsistency and finite-sample guarantees for the new models.
- Limited comparisons and ablation on hyperparameters; scalability and runtime not quantified.
- Case study lacks objective ground-truth evaluation.

Constructive suggestions: Add theoretical guarantees for structure recovery; include comparisons to Poisson log-normal and negative-binomial-based graphical models; provide runtime/memory benchmarks and sensitivity to R0,R; clarify estimation for SPGM’s partition function (bounds/approximations); release code and describe stability-selection settings.
