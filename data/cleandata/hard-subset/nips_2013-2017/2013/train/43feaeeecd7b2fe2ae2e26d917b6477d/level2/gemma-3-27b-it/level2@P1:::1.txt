This paper addresses the problem of modeling multivariate count data, a task for which standard graphical models like Gaussian graphical models are ill-suited. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) – their inability to model positive dependencies – and propose several novel approaches to overcome this. They begin by analyzing truncation strategies, finding that a previously suggested approach is flawed and then introduce a valid Truncated PGM (TPGM). Recognizing limitations of the TPGM, they further explore modifications to the Poisson distribution itself, leading to the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The paper concludes with empirical evaluation on simulated and real microRNA sequencing data.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles an important problem. The analysis of the Winsorized Poisson approach and the proof that it doesn’t lead to a valid joint distribution are valuable contributions. The introduction of the TPGM, QPGM and SPGM represents a significant effort to extend the applicability of PGMs to more general dependency structures. The numerical experiments, while limited in scope, demonstrate the potential of the proposed methods and their advantages over existing approaches.  The connection to existing work on graphical models, particularly [17, 18, 19], is clearly established and well-referenced.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. Clarity is a major issue. The mathematical derivations, especially those related to the QPGM and SPGM, are dense and difficult to follow without significant effort.  More intuitive explanations of the rationale behind the proposed modifications to the Poisson distribution would be helpful. For example, the motivation for the quadratic base measure in the QPGM isn't fully articulated. The leap from wanting a non-quadratic base measure to using a quadratic one feels somewhat arbitrary. The introduction of the SPGM, with its rather involved sufficient statistic B(x;R0, R), feels abrupt and lacks sufficient justification.  Why is this specific sublinear function chosen? What are its theoretical properties?

The empirical evaluation is also limited. The simulation studies only compare TPGM and SPGM and do not include a thorough comparison to other count data modeling techniques beyond those mentioned (e.g., negative binomial graphical models, which allow for overdispersion). The real data analysis focuses on a single dataset, and the interpretation of the biological findings could be strengthened. The paper mentions advantages of the TPGM finding more “hub” miRNAs, but doesn't elaborate enough on the biological significance of these findings.  The connection to the variational upper bound for the QPGM feels somewhat tacked on and doesn't significantly strengthen the analysis. The claim that the network structure can be learned via neighborhood selection lacks detailed discussion of the implementation and potential challenges.  While the authors state they leave a sparsistency analysis for future work, some discussion of the expected properties of the estimator would be beneficial.

Originality is strong in terms of the proposed models themselves. However, the core idea of modifying the Poisson distribution to allow for both positive and negative dependencies isn't entirely novel, though the specific approaches taken are.  The significant contribution lies in the rigorous mathematical analysis and the development of tractable graphical model distributions. 

Significance is moderate. The models presented offer a potential advance in the modeling of multivariate count data, which is increasingly prevalent in many fields. However, the practical impact will depend on the computational feasibility of inference in these models and their performance compared to simpler alternatives.  The success in the microRNA data analysis is promising, but further validation on larger and more diverse datasets is needed.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem in statistical modeling.
* Novel and theoretically sound approaches to extending Poisson graphical models.
* Rigorous mathematical analysis of the proposed models.
* Demonstrates potential for improved performance on real data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Clarity of presentation is lacking, particularly in the mathematical derivations.
* Limited empirical evaluation.
* Justification for some of the modeling choices (e.g., the specific form of the sublinear statistic) is weak.
* Practical computational challenges for inference are not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field but requires significant improvements in clarity, empirical validation, and discussion of practical considerations. The core ideas are promising, but the paper needs to be more accessible and convincing to a broader audience.
