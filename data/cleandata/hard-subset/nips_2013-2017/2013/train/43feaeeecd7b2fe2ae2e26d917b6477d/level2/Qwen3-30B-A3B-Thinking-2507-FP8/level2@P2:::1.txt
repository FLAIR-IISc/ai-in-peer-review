

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which are commonly used for modeling count data but can only capture negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors propose three novel variants of Poisson graphical models that can capture both positive and negative dependencies: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM). The main contributions include showing that the Winsorized Poisson approach suggested in prior work is invalid for constructing joint distributions (Theorem 1), and then developing three new models that overcome this limitation.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding, with rigorous proofs showing that TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM can accommodate both positive and negative dependencies while remaining normalizable. The authors provide clear theoretical justifications for their approaches, particularly highlighting the importance of base measure scaling (Theorem 3) and the design of sufficient statistics (SPGM). The empirical evaluation on simulated data shows that TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian graphical models on count data, and the real-world application to breast cancer microRNA sequencing data demonstrates practical utility, with SPGM and TPGM identifying biologically meaningful hub miRNAs that Gaussian models miss.

The paper's strengths include a clear identification of a significant limitation in existing work, rigorous theoretical contributions, and compelling empirical validation. The authors effectively situate their work within the literature, showing how their models differ from prior approaches like Winsorized Poisson [20, 21] and the original PGM [17, 18, 19]. The theoretical framework for extending exponential family distributions to graphical models [18, 19] is well leveraged, and the three novel models represent a meaningful advancement in the field of graphical models for count data.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. First, while the authors claim their models can capture "rich dependence structures," the empirical evaluation is limited to only two network structures (hub and scale-free) and doesn't test how the models perform under more varied conditions, such as different sparsity patterns or network topologies. Second, the paper states that extensions of sparsistency analysis would confirm network structure learning but doesn't provide this analysis themselves, which weakens the claim that the models can be reliably learned from data. Third, the paper lacks a thorough comparison with other recent approaches to count data modeling, such as the multivariate Poisson models based on sums of independent Poissons [14, 15] or copula-based methods [12]. While the authors reference these approaches, they don't explicitly compare their models' performance against them.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections and consistent notation. However, some explanations could be more intuitive—particularly regarding why the quadratic base measure in QPGM leads to Gaussian-like tails, and why the SPGM's "heavier tails" are practically advantageous. The paper would benefit from more discussion of the trade-offs between the three models, such as computational complexity or sensitivity to parameter choices.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses an important problem in modeling count data, which is increasingly common in applications like genomics and spatial statistics. The demonstration on real microRNA data showing identification of biologically relevant hubs (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b) is compelling and suggests practical utility. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss how these models might scale to very high-dimensional problems (e.g., p > 1000), which is a common scenario in modern genomic applications.

The originality of the work is strong, as the authors clearly identify and resolve a fundamental limitation in existing Poisson graphical models. The three proposed models represent distinct approaches to the problem, each with different theoretical properties and practical advantages. The paper effectively differentiates itself from prior work, particularly by showing the invalidity of the Winsorized Poisson approach.

In terms of quality, the theoretical contributions are sound, but the empirical evaluation could be strengthened by more comprehensive comparisons with relevant prior methods and more varied experimental conditions. The paper would benefit from including computational complexity analysis to address scalability concerns.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The three proposed models represent a significant advancement beyond existing approaches, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the paper provides a solid foundation for future work in modeling count-valued data with graphical models. The authors have identified a meaningful problem and provided a rigorous solution that addresses a key limitation in current methodology. The paper would be strengthened by more thorough empirical comparisons and discussion of practical limitations, but as it stands, it represents a significant contribution to the field that should be considered for acceptance at NIPS.
