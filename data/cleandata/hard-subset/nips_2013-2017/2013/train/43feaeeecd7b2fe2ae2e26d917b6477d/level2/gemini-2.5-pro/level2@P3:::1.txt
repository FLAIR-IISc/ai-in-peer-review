This paper addresses a critical limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) as defined in the framework of Besag [17] and Yang et al. [18, 19]: their inability to model positive conditional dependencies. This restriction arises from the normalizability condition of the joint distribution over an infinite, countable domain, which forces all pairwise interaction parameters to be non-positive. This severely limits their applicability to real-world count data, where variables often exhibit both competitive and cooperative relationships.

The work makes several novel contributions to overcome this challenge. First, it investigates truncation as a potential solution. It provides a formal proof that a previously proposed "Winsorized" Poisson distribution [20] cannot be used to form a valid joint distribution through node-conditional specification, correcting a misconception in the literature. Second, it proposes a valid alternative, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), based on a different truncation scheme that preserves the exponential family form. While the TPGM can model both positive and negative dependencies, it is shown to have limitations tied to the choice of truncation level. Third, the paper introduces two novel classes of graphical models for count data by modifying the underlying univariate distribution more fundamentally. The Quadratic PGM (QPGM) employs a quadratic base measure, which ensures normalizability for arbitrary interaction parameters at the cost of Gaussian-like thin tails. The Sublinear PGM (SPGM) modifies the sufficient statistics to be sub-linear, allowing the model to retain the original Poisson base measure (and thus heavier tails) while still permitting both positive and negative dependencies. Finally, the paper demonstrates a neighborhood selection approach for learning the structure of the TPGM and SPGM and evaluates their performance on simulated data and a real-world microRNA sequencing dataset.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions are sound and rigorously presented. Theorem 1, which disproves the validity of the Winsorized PGM construction, is a particularly valuable and clear contribution. The development of the three new models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) is well-motivated, and their normalizability properties are established through formal theorems and propositions. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis. The experimental section, while primarily focused on demonstrating feasibility, provides convincing evidence for the utility of the proposed models compared to methods designed for continuous data.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation for the problem, systematically reviews prior work, and logically progresses through its proposed solutions. Each new model is introduced as a response to the limitations of the previous one, creating a compelling narrative. The mathematical details are presented with precision. The description of the models and the learning procedure is sufficient for an expert to understand the core ideas.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. It tackles a fundamental, long-standing problem in the graphical modeling literature. The proposed solutions—the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM—are novel classes of distributions for count data. While they build upon the established framework of constructing graphical models from node-conditional exponential families, the specific designs of these families are new and cleverly constructed to circumvent the positivity constraint. The refutation of a prior approach is also an important and original corrective contribution.

**Significance:** The contributions of this paper are significant. Count data is ubiquitous in many machine learning application domains, including genomics, text analysis, and social sciences. The lack of a flexible, tractable graphical model for this data type that can capture rich dependency structures has been a major gap. This work provides not one, but three potential solutions, each with different trade-offs in terms of tail behavior and parameterization. These models are likely to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners and will form a strong basis for future work in this area. The application to miRNA network inference demonstrates the potential for immediate real-world impact.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is strong, but its practical impact could be further enhanced with a few additions, which could be considered for a future extended version of this work.

1.  **Model and Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper introduces a valuable new suite of models. A discussion on how a user might select among TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM for a given dataset would be beneficial. Furthermore, guidance on setting the hyperparameters (e.g., R for TPGM, R and R0 for SPGM) would be helpful. The experiments use a reasonable heuristic of setting R to the maximum observed count, but exploring the sensitivity to this choice or suggesting a more data-driven selection method (e.g., via cross-validation) would strengthen the practical methodology.

2.  **Experimental Comparison of QPGM:** The QPGM is theoretically motivated and derived, but it is absent from the numerical experiments. Including the QPGM in the comparative analysis would provide a more complete picture of the proposed methods. Its performance, particularly in relation to the GGM given their conceptual similarities, would be of great interest.

3.  **Theoretical Guarantees for Learning:** The paper suggests that sparsistency results for neighborhood selection can be extended from prior work but defers the analysis. While understandable given space limitations, the claim could be bolstered by including a brief, high-level sketch of the proof argument, outlining how the conditions required by the theory of [18] are met by the new models.

4.  **Computational Considerations:** A brief analysis of the computational complexity of the learning procedure for each model would be a useful addition. For the SPGM, which relies on a variational upper bound for its intractable node-conditional partition function, some discussion on the potential impact of this approximation on estimation accuracy would be informative.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory and practice of graphical models for count data. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a problem of high significance. The proposed models are elegant, well-motivated, and demonstrably effective.
