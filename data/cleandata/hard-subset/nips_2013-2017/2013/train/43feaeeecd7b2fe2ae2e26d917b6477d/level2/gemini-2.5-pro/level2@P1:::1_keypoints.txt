- Problem: Standard Poisson Graphical Models (PGMs) can only model negative conditional dependencies, limiting applicability to real-world count data.  
- Main contribution: Proposes three new count graphical model classes (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) that allow both positive and negative dependencies.  
- Negative theoretical finding: Shows the previously proposed Winsorized PGM is not a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1).  
- TPGM: Introduces Truncated PGM on finite domain {0,...,R} that is valid and models positive/negative dependencies but depends on truncation level R.  
- Theoretical insight: Proves that for linear sufficient-statistic models, the base measure must grow at least quadratically to normalize with positive dependencies (Theorem 3).  
- QPGM: Proposes Quadratic PGM using a quadratic base measure, yielding a Gaussian-like count model that handles arbitrary dependencies if parameters are negative definite.  
- SPGM: Proposes Sub-linear PGM that keeps the Poisson base measure but uses bounded sub-linear sufficient statistics to ensure normalizability for any pairwise parameters.  
- Experiments: Empirical evaluation on simulated and microRNA sequencing data shows TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian-based methods for network recovery.  
- Quality: Theoretical contributions are sound, well-supported, and the experimental evaluation is well-executed.  
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and makes complex theory accessible.  
- Originality: Models and the correction of the Winsorized PGM are novel and distinct contributions to the literature.  
- Significance: Advances modeling of multivariate count data by removing a major limitation of standard PGMs; relevant across domains.  
- Strength: Provides insightful normalizability analysis that guides model design.  
- Strength: Offers practical models (TPGM, SPGM) with demonstrated empirical benefits.  
- Weakness: TPGM and SPGM introduce hyperparameters (R, R0) with limited guidance on selection in practice.  
- Weakness: QPGM is not included in experiments, leaving its empirical behavior vs. theory untested.  
- Weakness: Leaves statistical consistency analysis of neighborhood-selection estimators (e.g., sparsistency) to future work.  
- Overall verdict: Strong, original, and significant work with minor practical and empirical gaps; recommended for acceptance.
