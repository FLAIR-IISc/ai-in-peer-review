

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Poisson graphical models (PGMs), which can only model negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The authors propose three novel approaches to overcome this limitation: Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), all of which can capture both positive and negative conditional dependencies between count-valued variables. The paper makes strong theoretical contributions by proving the normalizability of these models under appropriate conditions (Theorems 1-4) and demonstrates their practical utility through simulations and a breast cancer microRNA network analysis.

The main strengths of the paper include: (1) clear identification of a significant gap in the literature; (2) elegant theoretical derivations that properly address the normalizability constraint; (3) well-designed experiments showing superior performance on count data compared to Gaussian-based methods; and (4) meaningful application to a real-world biological dataset where the authors identify biologically relevant miRNA clusters. The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of related work, including the foundational PGM work by Besag and Yang et al., and properly situates their contributions.

The primary weakness is the limited discussion of computational complexity for the proposed methods, particularly for the SPGM which involves infinite summations in the log-partition function. While the paper mentions using the Poisson log-partition as a variational upper bound, more details about implementation challenges would strengthen the practical contribution. Additionally, the paper could better articulate the trade-offs between the three proposed models in different application scenarios.

The significance of this work is substantial as count data modeling is increasingly important in genomics, spatial statistics, and other domains. The ability to model both positive and negative dependencies opens up new applications for graphical models in these fields. The paper's theoretical advances are clearly presented and well-supported, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed methods.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in graphical models for count data. The paper's theoretical rigor, practical relevance, and clear presentation make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The minor limitations noted do not detract from the paper's significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the computational complexity discussion.
