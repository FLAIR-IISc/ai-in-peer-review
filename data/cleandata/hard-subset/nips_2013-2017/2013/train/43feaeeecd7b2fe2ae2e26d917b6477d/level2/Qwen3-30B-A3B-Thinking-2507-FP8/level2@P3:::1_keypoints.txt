- Problem: existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) can only represent negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints.  
- Proposal: three novel models—Truncated PGM (TPGM), Quadratic PGM (QPGM), and Sublinear PGM (SPGM)—that allow both positive and negative dependencies for count data.  
- Framework: extends Yang et al. (NIPS 2012) by rigorously analyzing mathematical normalizability requirements and dependency flexibility.  
- Theorem 1: proves Kaiser & Cressie’s Winsorized Poisson approach cannot produce a consistent joint distribution.  
- Theorem 2: establishes TPGM as a valid exponential-family model on a finite (truncated) domain.  
- Theorems 3 & 4: show QPGM (quadratic base measure) and SPGM (sublinear sufficient statistics) are normalizable with unrestricted edge parameters.  
- Technical quality: comprehensive theoretical development with clear proofs and connections to exponential family properties.  
- Empirical results: simulations and a breast cancer miRNA network show TPGM and SPGM outperform Gaussian graphical models and the original PGM on count data.  
- Clarity: logical structure (problem → theory → experiments) and clear differentiation from prior work; related-work discussion could better contrast non-paranormal methods.  
- Originality: novel model constructions (QPGM, SPGM) that resolve a long-standing limitation since Besag and significantly extend prior frameworks.  
- Significance: enables modeling positive dependencies, expanding applicability in genomics, spatial statistics, and behavioral data; case study recovers biologically relevant miRNAs and a chromosome-19 clique.  
- Suggested improvements: (1) discuss practical choice and scaling of truncation parameter R in TPGM; (2) expand computational details for SPGM learning (handling infinite log-partition sums); (3) provide guidance on when to prefer TPGM vs QPGM vs SPGM based on tails/dependency patterns.  
- Limitation: paper omits quantified analysis of computational scalability for very large p (e.g., p>1000) and complexity comparisons to Gaussian/PGM methods.  
- Comparison gap: lacks comparison to alternative count-data models such as negative binomial graphical models.  
- Recommendation: strong accept—theoretical novelty, clarity, and empirical validation make a substantial contribution to count-valued graphical modeling.
