Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses a key limitation of Poisson graphical models (PGMs) for multivariate count data: the standard auto-Poisson/Poisson MRF construction (Besag 1974; Yang et al. NIPS 2012) is only normalizable when all pairwise parameters are non-positive, hence it can capture only negative conditional dependencies. The authors (i) prove that a commonly suggested “Winsorized Poisson” node-conditional approach (Kaiser & Cressie; Griffith), which truncates by moving all tail mass to a finite cap R, cannot yield a consistent joint MRF (Theorem 1); (ii) propose a different truncated exponential-family construction that does admit a consistent joint MRF, the Truncated PGM (TPGM), which supports both positive and negative dependencies on a finite support; (iii) show that with linear sufficient statistics, normalizability with mixed-sign edges requires a base measure that grows at least quadratically (Theorem 3); this motivates (iv) a Quadratic PGM (QPGM) with a quadratic base measure and negative-definite quadratic form, admitting mixed-sign interactions; and (v) a Sublinear PGM (SPGM) that keeps the Poisson base measure while replacing the linear sufficient statistic by a sublinear, saturating statistic B(·; R0, R), which yields heavier tails and allows all real pairwise parameters (Theorem 4). They outline penalized neighborhood selection for structure learning and present experiments on simulated networks and miRNA-seq data, comparing to Gaussian and semiparametric Gaussian copula approaches.

This work builds directly on the framework for graphical models via univariate exponential families (Yang et al., NIPS 2012; subsequent arXiv) and connects to classical auto-models (Besag 1974), multivariate Poisson constructions via sums-of-Poissons (Holgate; Karlis), and copula-based methods. To my knowledge, the negative result for the Winsorized conditional model and the sufficient growth condition for the base measure are new and important.

Quality:
- Theory: The counterexample for the Winsorized conditionals (Theorem 1) and the construction/uniqueness for TPGM (Theorem 2) are valuable and address a confusion in the literature. The necessity result (Theorem 3) clarifies why linear statistics plus a factorial base cannot accommodate positive edges on an unbounded domain. SPGM normalizability (Theorem 4) is also useful. The variational bounds relating QPGM to Gaussian and SPGM to Poisson are reasonable and practically motivated.
- Methods/estimation: Neighborhood selection via node-conditional likelihood is natural in this framework. However, for SPGM, the node-conditional log-partition involves an infinite sum; the paper mentions an upper bound but it is unclear whether the bound is used as a surrogate objective during optimization, whether truncation is used, and how this impacts bias/consistency. For QPGM, reliance on a Gaussian variational bound is reasonable, but then one expects Gaussian-like behavior; the tradeoff merits more discussion.
- Empirics: Simulations demonstrate advantages of TPGM/SPGM over Gaussian/nonparanormal baselines on count data; the design includes hub and scale-free graphs, moderate/high dimensions, and generation via TPGM or sum-of-Poissons. The miRNA case study is compelling, with biological interpretation and stability selection. Some critical details are missing (see below).

Clarity:
Overall well-written, with clear motivation and contributions. A few places would benefit from added precision:
- Explicitly state assumptions behind Theorem 3 (formal definition of Ω(·) in this context; whether it is pointwise or asymptotic in Z→∞).
- For SPGM, include the exact expression of D(θ,R0,R) used in optimization or the approximation strategy.
- Explain selection of R and R0 (and sensitivity) and whether they can be learned.
- In the case study, justify R=11 (were counts binned/normalized?) and report basic count distributions.

Originality:
High. The impossibility result for Winsorized conditionals and the identification of a necessary quadratic growth of the base measure for positive edges with linear sufficient statistics are novel. The SPGM construction with saturating statistics is also original in the graphical modeling context.

Significance:
The inability of classical PGMs to model positive conditional dependence in counts is a serious practical limitation across genomics, spatial statistics, and recommender/count applications. Providing multiple, theoretically grounded alternatives (finite-support TPGM, quadratic-tail QPGM, and heavy-tailed SPGM) is valuable and likely to influence subsequent work on count graphical models.

Strengths:
- Clear articulation of a long-standing limitation and principled remedies.
- Important negative result correcting prior claims on Winsorized Poisson conditionals.
- New families (TPGM/QPGM/SPGM) with theoretical normalizability guarantees and practical learning strategies.
- Solid empirical comparisons on relevant structures; real data case study with domain insights.
- Connections to known frameworks (GLM-based neighborhood selection) enabling adoption.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Estimation details for SPGM and QPGM:
  - How exactly is the node-conditional likelihood computed/optimized for SPGM (truncation level for the infinite sum, use of the Poisson variational bound as a surrogate, error control)?
  - For QPGM, is the Gaussian variational bound used in the objective or only for analysis? Any gap characterization?
- Hyperparameters R, R0:
  - How to choose R and R0 in a principled way (e.g., via likelihood, AIC/BIC, cross-validation, or stability-based criteria)?
  - Sensitivity analyses w.r.t. R and R0 (both simulations and real data) would strengthen conclusions.
- Global parameter coherence:
  - Node-wise estimation yields separate parameter vectors; what symmetrization rule is used? Are there conditions under which local QPGM/SPGM fits are jointly consistent with a global negative-definite Θ (QPGM)?
- Comparisons:
  - Consider including comparisons to Poisson lognormal or related hierarchical count models (widely used for overdispersed counts) and to the GLM-based Poisson graphical model estimator from Yang et al. (even if limited to negative edges, a quantitative comparison helps).
- Evaluation metrics:
  - Report AUCs with confidence intervals; provide calibration/sparsity at selected operating points.
- Case study:
  - Provide preprocessing details (library size normalization, filtering) and rationale for R=11/R0=5; report network sizes and stability thresholds; include overlap with known miRNA interaction resources, if available.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important modeling gap with clear theoretical advances.
- Provides practically learnable models that admit positive and negative dependencies.
- Corrects misconceptions in prior literature.
- Demonstrates empirical benefits on both synthetic and real data.

Cons against acceptance:
- Some estimation details and hyperparameter selection procedures are under-specified.
- No sparsistency or statistical guarantees for the new models (left to future work).
- Limited comparative baselines beyond Gaussian/semi-parametric methods.
- Sensitivity analyses and ablations (e.g., effect of R/R0) are missing.

Overall assessment:
This is a thoughtful and substantive contribution to graphical modeling for count data. Despite some open questions on estimation details and hyperparameter selection, the theoretical insights and practical potential are strong. I lean toward acceptance, and I encourage the authors to add implementation specifics, sensitivity analyses, and expanded comparisons to further strengthen the paper.
