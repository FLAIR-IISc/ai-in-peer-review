This paper addresses the problem of modeling multivariate count data, a task for which standard graphical models like Gaussian graphical models are ill-suited. The authors identify a key limitation of existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) – their inability to capture negative dependencies – and propose several novel modifications to overcome this. They begin by analyzing truncation strategies for the Poisson distribution, demonstrating that a previously suggested approach is flawed and presenting a valid truncated PGM (TPGM). Recognizing limitations of the TPGM, they then introduce the Quadratic PGM (QPGM) and Sublinear PGM (SPGM), based on modifications to the base measure and sufficient statistics of the Poisson distribution. The paper concludes with empirical evaluation on simulated and real microRNA sequencing data, demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed methods.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a clear gap in the literature. The derivation of the TPGM, QPGM, and SPGM is mathematically sound, and the authors provide clear justifications for each step. The theoretical analysis, particularly the theorems regarding normalizability and the conditions for capturing both positive and negative dependencies, is a significant contribution. The experimental section is also commendable, with comparisons to existing methods on both simulated and real data. The use of a biological dataset adds practical relevance.

However, the paper could benefit from several improvements. While the mathematical derivations are rigorous, they are dense and could be made more accessible with additional explanations and intermediate steps. The presentation of the sublinear Poisson sufficient statistics is particularly challenging to follow without careful scrutiny of the equations. It would be helpful to include a visual representation of B(x) alongside the description.

The experimental section, while thorough, lacks detailed discussion of the parameter tuning process for each method. Providing sensitivity analyses or guidelines for choosing appropriate regularization parameters would enhance reproducibility and practicality. Furthermore, the performance comparisons on the real dataset are presented primarily through visual inspection of network structures. Quantifying the performance using metrics such as precision and recall, or comparing the biological significance of the identified networks, would strengthen the conclusions. 

The discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed methods is limited. Estimating the computational cost of learning the graph structure, particularly for the QPGM and SPGM, would be valuable.  Although the authors mention using proximal gradient ascent, details of implementation are lacking.

A potential concern is the practical applicability of the QPGM and SPGM. While theoretically sound, the quadratic base measure and sublinear sufficient statistics introduce complexities that may hinder their adoption. A more in-depth discussion of these limitations and potential solutions could be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and mathematically sound extensions to Poisson graphical models.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing methods for modeling count data.
*   Comprehensive theoretical analysis and empirical validation.
*   Clear presentation of the problem and motivation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Mathematical derivations are dense and potentially inaccessible.
*   Limited discussion of computational complexity and parameter tuning.
*   Performance evaluation on real data could be more quantitative.
*   Practical limitations of the proposed models need further discussion.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of graphical models for count data. The proposed methods are novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While the paper has some areas for improvement, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. The work is of high quality and is likely to be of interest to researchers in statistics, machine learning, and related fields. A revision addressing the points raised above would further strengthen the paper and make it even more impactful.
