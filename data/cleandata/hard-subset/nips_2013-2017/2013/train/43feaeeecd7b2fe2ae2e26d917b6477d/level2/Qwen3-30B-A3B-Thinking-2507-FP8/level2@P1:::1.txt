

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical models for count data. The authors identify that existing Poisson graphical models (PGMs) suffer from a fundamental limitation: they can only represent negative conditional dependencies due to normalizability constraints. The paper makes three key contributions to overcome this limitation, proposing novel graphical model distributions that can capture both positive and negative dependencies.

The paper begins by rigorously showing that the Winsorized Poisson approach proposed by Kaiser & Cressie [20] cannot yield a valid joint distribution (Theorem 1), correcting an important error in prior work. The authors then introduce three new approaches: (1) Truncated PGM (TPGM), which uses a bounded domain Poisson distribution; (2) Quadratic PGM (QPGM), which modifies the base measure to quadratic form; and (3) Sublinear PGM (SPGM), which employs sublinear sufficient statistics. The theoretical development is strong, with clear proofs of normalizability conditions (Theorems 3-4) that establish the mathematical foundation for these models. The authors demonstrate how these models can be learned via neighborhood selection, and provide compelling empirical results on both simulated data and a breast cancer microRNA sequencing dataset.

The paper excels in originality and significance. The problem of modeling count data with rich dependence structures has been acknowledged as important (e.g., in [11, 12, 14, 15, 18]), but no prior work has successfully resolved the normalizability issue to permit both positive and negative dependencies. The authors' three distinct approaches represent a substantial theoretical advance. Their case study on microRNA networks demonstrates clear practical value: both TPGM and SPGM identify biologically meaningful hub miRNAs (e.g., mir-375 and mir-10b) that the original PGM and Gaussian models miss, providing strong evidence of the method's utility in real-world applications.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The theoretical arguments are sound, with careful attention to normalizability conditions that are crucial for graphical models. The proofs are well-constructed and build logically on prior work (e.g., [17, 18, 19]). The experimental section is appropriately focused on demonstrating the core claims of the paper, though it could be strengthened by including more comparative baselines (e.g., the non-paranormal SKEPTIC [10] is mentioned but not fully compared in the microRNA analysis). The simulation studies effectively show that the proposed models outperform Gaussian-based approaches on count data, with TPGM showing particular advantages in high-dimensional settings.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical contributions accessible to the NIPS audience. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and related work, and the mathematical presentation is precise. One minor weakness is that the SPGM sufficient statistic (Equation 7) is complex without sufficient intuitive explanation; a brief verbal description of how it "softly" bends the linear behavior would improve clarity.

The most significant limitation is that the paper focuses solely on model specification without addressing computational challenges for large-scale data. While the authors note that neighborhood selection can be used for structure learning, they do not discuss computational complexity or scalability to very large graphs (e.g., p=1000+), which is an important consideration for real-world applications. Additionally, the theoretical analysis of sparsistency for the proposed models (beyond the brief mention in Section 4) would strengthen the paper's claims about learning guarantees.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original contribution that addresses a well-motivated problem in graphical models. The paper makes a clear theoretical advance with practical implications for analyzing count data across multiple domains. The three proposed models (TPGM, QPGM, SPGM) represent a significant step forward from existing Poisson graphical models, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate their advantages. While the computational aspects and scalability could be further explored, these are reasonable extensions for future work rather than critical flaws.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a genuine contribution beyond prior work (Originality), and addresses an important problem with demonstrated impact (Significance). I strongly recommend acceptance.
