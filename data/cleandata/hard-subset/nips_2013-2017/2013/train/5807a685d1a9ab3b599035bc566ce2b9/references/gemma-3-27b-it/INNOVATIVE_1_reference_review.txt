This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem: understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation. The authors skillfully connect Warner's early work on randomized response with the more recent framework of differential privacy and minimax theory. The results are, frankly, quite striking – demonstrating a significant degradation in sample complexity due to privacy constraints, and a surprising shift in convergence rates for density estimation. I highly value the boldness of framing this problem within a rigorous minimax framework and the sharpness of the derived bounds. The connection to randomized response being *optimal* is a particularly compelling result, highlighting the longevity and fundamental nature of that approach. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This work presents a genuinely novel synthesis of disparate fields – privacy, statistics, and information theory. The application of minimax theory to differentially private estimation is a substantial contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically sound, building on established results in differential privacy and minimax estimation. The proofs (as referenced to the accompanying paper [9]) appear solid.
* **Impactful Results:** The identified trade-offs are significant. The reduction in effective sample size and the altered convergence rate for density estimation have clear implications for practical privacy-preserving data analysis.
* **Connection to Classical Work:** The demonstration that Warner’s randomized response is optimal in this setting is a valuable historical and practical insight.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The core ideas are well-motivated and explained, although some sections (especially the background on minimax theory) are dense and assume significant prior knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density Estimation Complexity:** While the theoretical results for density estimation are intriguing, the practical implications are less clear. The proposed estimator relies on efficiently sampling from a complex distribution defined by the privacy mechanism and, as acknowledged, may not be easily implementable. Further discussion of computational challenges and alternative estimators would strengthen this section.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses almost entirely on theoretical bounds. While these are valuable, some limited empirical validation (even on small-scale simulations) would bolster confidence in the derived rates and demonstrate the practical impact of the findings.
* **Background Density:** The background material, particularly on minimax theory, is quite dense. While complete self-containment isn’t essential, a bit more intuitive explanation would make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
* **Notation:** The notation is generally good, but occasionally cumbersome. For example, the extended definition of Mnν could be streamlined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically grounded framework for analyzing the privacy-utility trade-off in probability estimation.
* The derived minimax rates are sharp and provide valuable insights into the limitations of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* The connection to randomized response is a significant historical and practical result.
* The work has the potential to influence future research in privacy-preserving machine learning and data analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The density estimation results, while theoretically interesting, may be difficult to translate into practical algorithms.
* The lack of empirical validation limits the immediate impact of the findings.
* The dense background material may hinder accessibility for some readers.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the impactful results outweigh the minor weaknesses. While empirical validation and a more accessible presentation would be beneficial, the paper’s core contributions are compelling and warrant acceptance. I’m particularly impressed by the boldness of tackling such a fundamental problem with a rigorous theoretical framework. I believe this work will stimulate further research in this important area and is a strong candidate for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
