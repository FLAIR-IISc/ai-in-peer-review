

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between differential privacy and minimax decision theory, providing sharp convergence rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors characterize the fundamental trade-off between privacy (measured by α-local differential privacy) and statistical efficiency for two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation.

The work builds on Warner's randomized response (1965) for survey sampling and connects it to modern differential privacy frameworks. While prior work (e.g., Duchi et al. [9]) established information-theoretic bounds for privacy-constrained estimation, this paper provides sharp minimax rates for specific estimation problems, demonstrating that Warner's classical mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation. For density estimation, they show a fundamental change in the convergence rate exponent under privacy constraints, which differs from classical non-private rates.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor and Sharpness**: The paper provides tight minimax bounds for both multinomial and density estimation problems, with matching upper bounds achieved by efficiently implementable algorithms. This is a significant theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature.

2. **Novel Insights for Density Estimation**: The finding that privacy changes the convergence rate exponent for density estimation (from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2)) is particularly insightful. This demonstrates a fundamental difference between private and non-private estimation that wasn't previously established.

3. **Practical Relevance**: By showing that Warner's randomized response achieves the optimal rate for multinomial estimation, the paper provides a theoretically grounded justification for a mechanism that's been in use for nearly 50 years but hadn't been proven optimal.

4. **Technical Sophistication**: The authors skillfully combine information-theoretic bounds (Fano's inequality, Le Cam's method) with differential privacy constraints to derive their results. The proofs are well-structured and rigorous.

5. **Clear Algorithmic Contributions**: The paper doesn't just provide theory but also demonstrates how to achieve the optimal rates with practical algorithms, including the privatized histogram estimator and orthogonal series estimator.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Other Privacy Mechanisms**: While the paper shows that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation, it doesn't thoroughly compare their results to other privacy mechanisms (e.g., the perturbation approach of Dwork et al. [11]). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the practical relevance of their optimality claim.

2. **Density Estimation Sampling Strategy**: The sampling strategy for the orthogonal series estimator (equation 19) is non-intuitive and requires careful implementation. The paper could better explain why this particular noise mechanism is necessary and how it compares to simpler alternatives.

3. **Practical Implications**: The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the practical trade-offs between privacy level α and statistical efficiency in real-world applications. For example, how does the rate change for α = 0.1 vs. α = 1 in typical survey settings?

4. **Related Work Coverage**: While the paper cites key works, it doesn't adequately engage with recent advances in private statistical estimation (e.g., Chaudhuri and Hsu [5], Smith [23]). A more thorough discussion of how their results relate to these works would strengthen the paper's positioning.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes significant progress in understanding the fundamental limits of statistical estimation under local privacy constraints. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The results for multinomial estimation are particularly elegant and satisfying, while the density estimation results reveal a fundamental change in the convergence rate that has important theoretical implications.

The main weaknesses are somewhat marginal given the paper's theoretical focus. The practical implications could be expanded, and a more thorough comparison with other privacy mechanisms would be valuable. However, these do not undermine the paper's core theoretical contributions.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical foundations of privacy-preserving statistical estimation. The results are sharp, the proofs are rigorous, and the connection between differential privacy and minimax theory is well-motivated. The paper demonstrates both theoretical novelty (particularly in the density estimation results) and practical relevance (by showing Warner's mechanism is optimal). While there are minor areas for improvement in the discussion of related work and practical implications, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

The paper advances the state of the art in understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, which is a central concern in the growing field of privacy-preserving machine learning. The results will be valuable to both theoretical statisticians and practitioners working on privacy-preserving data analysis.
