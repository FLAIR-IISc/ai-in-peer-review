This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building upon the work of Warner [23] and recent advances in differential privacy [11, 1]. The authors provide sharp minimax lower and upper bounds for multinomial and density estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The work is a strong contribution, formally connecting classical statistical decision theory with the modern framework of differential privacy. It rigorously analyzes the penalty incurred by guaranteeing privacy and demonstrates, for instance, that the effective sample size in multinomial estimation reduces to nα²/d.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically sound, employing minimax theory and careful analysis of information-theoretic bounds. The proofs appear solid and well-structured (based on the provided excerpts and references to [9]).
* **Sharp Results:** The derived minimax rates are sharp, meaning the upper and lower bounds match, providing a complete characterization of the optimal convergence rates under privacy constraints. This is a significant achievement.
* **Connection to Classical Work:** The paper thoughtfully connects to the early work of Warner [23] on randomized response, showing that this classical method is, in fact, optimal under the given privacy constraints.
* **Novel Insights:** The analysis reveals that the penalty for privacy in density estimation is different than a simple reduction in sample size. The alteration in the polynomial rate of convergence is a noteworthy finding.
* **Practical Implications:** The paper provides insights into the design of privacy-preserving estimators, identifying conditions under which existing techniques (like randomized response and perturbation) are optimal or require modification.
* **Clear Presentation (for a theoretical paper):** Despite the technical nature of the subject matter, the introduction and motivation are clearly articulated. The notation is standard and consistently used.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Examples:** The paper focuses primarily on multinomial and density estimation. While these are important examples, the generalizability of the results to other statistical problems is not fully explored.  It would be beneficial to briefly discuss potential applications or challenges in other contexts.
* **Dependence on [9]:**  A substantial portion of the technical results relies on Proposition 1 from a previous paper [9]. While referencing prior work is standard, the extensive reliance makes it difficult to fully assess the novelty and completeness of *this* paper without access to [9]. The review would benefit from a more detailed summary of the key ideas in [9] within the main body.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim efficiency of some estimators, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of implementing these algorithms, particularly for high-dimensional problems, would be valuable. For instance, the projection step in the histogram estimator (Section 4.2) could become expensive.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper is very theoretical. A brief discussion of practical challenges in implementing these privacy-preserving estimators in real-world settings (e.g., choosing appropriate parameters, dealing with noisy data) would enhance its impact.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical validation of the theoretical results. While not strictly necessary for a theoretical paper, including some simulations to demonstrate the performance of the proposed estimators would strengthen the conclusions.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* The results are mathematically rigorous and establish sharp bounds on the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and utility.
* The connection to classical work and the identification of novel insights make this a valuable addition to the NIPS literature.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The strong reliance on a previous paper [9] makes it difficult to fully evaluate the originality and completeness of this work.
* The limited scope of examples and the lack of empirical validation weaken the practical impact of the research.
* The discussion of computational complexity and practical considerations is insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution that is *borderline* acceptable for NIPS. While the theoretical rigor and novelty are commendable, the strong dependence on a prior paper, limited scope, and lack of empirical evaluation raise some concerns. 

**Recommendations:**

If accepted, I recommend the authors consider:

* Providing a more detailed summary of the key ideas from [9] within the main body of the paper.
* Discussing the generalizability of the results to other statistical problems.
* Including a brief discussion of the computational complexity and practical challenges of implementing the proposed estimators.
* Adding some simulations to validate the theoretical results.

If rejected, I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and consider submitting the revised version to a future NIPS or related conference. The core ideas are strong and deserve further development.
