### Summary

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors use minimax decision theory to derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds on the estimation error for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation.

For estimating a d-dimensional multinomial, the authors demonstrate that imposing α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to `nα²/d`. A key insight is that Warner's classical randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal in this modern privacy framework.

For nonparametric density estimation of functions within a Sobolev class with smoothness `β`, the paper reveals a more dramatic consequence of local privacy. The minimax rate of convergence changes from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This is not merely a constant factor degradation but a fundamental change in the polynomial rate of convergence. The authors provide constructive, computationally efficient estimators (a private histogram for `β=1` and a private orthogonal series estimator for `β > 1`) that achieve these new optimal rates.

The work connects the foundational ideas of Warner on randomized response with the modern, rigorous definition of differential privacy, and extends classical minimax theory to this locally private setting, providing a precise, quantitative characterization of the cost of privacy.

### Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)

1.  **Fundamental Significance:** The paper addresses a question of core importance at the intersection of privacy, statistics, and machine learning: What are the fundamental limits of statistical inference under strong privacy guarantees? By providing sharp minimax rates, the authors give a definitive answer for two central problems. This work provides a solid theoretical foundation that future research can build upon. The results are likely to be widely cited and influential for researchers studying private data analysis.

2.  **High Originality and Novelty:** To my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to provide a complete and sharp minimax analysis for these problems under local differential privacy. The findings are highly insightful. The connection drawn between Warner's 50-year-old randomized response and minimax optimality under local DP is elegant and surprising. Furthermore, the discovery that privacy fundamentally alters the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a major conceptual contribution.

3.  **Excellent Technical Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical command, skillfully combining tools from statistical decision theory (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) with information-theoretic bounds tailored to the local privacy constraint. The fact that the lower bounds are complemented by matching upper bounds from achievable, efficient algorithms makes the results particularly strong and complete.

4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem setting is formally and precisely defined, and the main results are stated cleanly. The authors do an excellent job of contextualizing their contributions with respect to both classical statistics and the contemporary privacy literature.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The most significant weakness is that all proofs, as well as the key information-theoretic bounds in Proposition 1, are deferred to a companion arXiv paper [9]. While this is sometimes necessary due to page limits, it prevents a thorough verification of the paper's claims based on the submitted manuscript alone. For a conference submission, it is crucial that the work is self-contained and verifiable. I strongly urge the authors to include at least proof sketches or the statements and proofs of the most critical lemmas in an appendix for the camera-ready version. This would substantially strengthen the paper.

2.  **Clarity of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The mechanism proposed in Section 4.3 for density estimation is sophisticated and its description is quite dense. While technically sound, its intuition is not immediately obvious. A few sentences explaining *why* this particular two-stage randomization (sampling `τ̃` then `Z`) is necessary to achieve the optimal rate would be very helpful. Furthermore, the constant `ck` in eq. (20) is vaguely described as "O(1) and bounded away from 0". Providing a more concrete form or characterization of this constant would improve the reproducibility and clarity of the proposed estimator.

3.  **Discussion of the High-Utility Regime (large α):** The analysis primarily focuses on the high-privacy regime where α is small (e.g., α ∈ [0, 1]). In the multinomial rate `min{1, d/(nα²)}`, the dependence on α suggests that for a fixed `n` and `d`, as `α` grows, the bound eventually becomes worse than the trivial bound of 1. While the `min` operator handles this formally, a brief discussion of the behavior for larger `α` (the low-privacy, high-utility regime) and the phase transition point would provide a more complete picture of the privacy-utility trade-off.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent and important paper that makes a fundamental contribution to our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results are novel, significant, and technically strong. The discovery of the change in convergence exponents for density estimation is a particularly impactful finding. The clarity of the presentation is commendable.

The primary drawback is the paper's reliance on an external manuscript for all of its proofs, which hinders the review process. However, assuming the proofs in the companion paper are correct, the contributions are of a very high caliber and are perfectly suited for NIPS. I recommend acceptance, with the strong condition that the authors make the paper more self-contained by adding key proof elements to an appendix.

**Decision:** Accept.
