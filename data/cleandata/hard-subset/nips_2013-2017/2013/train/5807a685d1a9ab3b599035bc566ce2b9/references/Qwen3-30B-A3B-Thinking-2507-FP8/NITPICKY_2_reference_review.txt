

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, addressing two main problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. The authors provide lower bounds and matching upper bounds, demonstrating that local privacy imposes a penalty on statistical efficiency, with the effective sample size reduced from n to nα²/d for multinomial estimation and a different convergence rate for density estimation.

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by connecting minimax decision theory with differential privacy, offering sharp rates for these problems. The results are significant as they quantify the trade-off between privacy and statistical utility, with Warner's randomized response shown to be optimal for multinomial estimation.

However, the paper contains multiple minor technical inconsistencies that undermine its quality and precision. These issues are particularly concerning given the paper's focus on precise mathematical bounds.

**Major Issues:**

1. **Critical Notation Error (Equation 1):** The definition of α-local differential privacy is written as "sup { Q(S | Xi = x) Q(S | Xi = x′) | ... } ≤ exp(α)", which is missing a division sign. It should be "sup { Q(S | Xi = x) / Q(S | Xi = x′) | ... } ≤ exp(α)". This is a fundamental error in the paper's core definition.

2. **Formatting Inconsistencies in Exponents:** Multiple equations (8, 9, 14, 17, and Proposition 3) contain inconsistent exponent formatting. For example, in equation (14), the exponent "- 2β 2β+2" should be "-2β/(2β+2)" for clarity. Similarly, in equation (17), "- 12" should be "-1/2". These formatting errors could cause serious misinterpretation of the mathematical results.

3. **Confusing Randomized Response Description (Equation 10):** The description of the randomized response mechanism states "[Z]j = xj with probability exp(α/2)/(1+exp(α/2)) and -xj with probability 1/(1+exp(α/2))". Since xj ∈ {0,1} (as it's a basis vector indicator), the "-xj" is confusing and technically incorrect. The paper should clarify that the mechanism adds noise to the indicator, not negates it.

4. **Ambiguous Minimax Notation (Equation 2):** The minimax rate is defined with "EP,Q", which is ambiguous. The expectation should be written as EP[EQ|P] to clearly indicate the double expectation over data and privacy mechanism.

**Minor Issues:**

5. **Reference Formatting Inconsistency:** Several references contain extraneous commas (e.g., "Proceedings of the 26th ACM Symposium on Principles of Database Systems, , ,").

6. **Typographical Error in Abstract:** "nα2/d" should be "nα²/d" for mathematical clarity.

7. **Slightly Overstated Claim in Introduction:** The statement that "the optimal privacy-preserving strategy for problems such as survey collection has been known for almost 50 years" could be more precisely phrased to emphasize that the minimax optimality result is novel, rather than the strategy itself.

8. **Missing Proof Details:** While the paper references a longer version [9] for the proof of Theorem 2, it does not provide a sketch of the key ideas or sufficient detail to assess the proof's validity.

**Strengths:**
- The paper connects minimax theory with differential privacy in a meaningful way.
- The results for multinomial estimation are particularly elegant, showing Warner's randomized response is optimal.
- The density estimation results demonstrate a novel departure from classical minimax rates under privacy constraints.
- The paper provides efficiently implementable algorithms that match the lower bounds.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistical estimation, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies and formatting errors significantly undermine its quality. The critical notation error in the definition of local privacy is particularly concerning, as it's the foundation of the entire analysis. In a paper that focuses on precise mathematical bounds, such errors are unacceptable.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too numerous to overlook, despite the paper's theoretical merits. The authors should carefully revise the paper to fix these technical inconsistencies before it can be considered for publication. I recommend rejection due to these substantial (though minor) technical flaws that compromise the paper's rigor.
