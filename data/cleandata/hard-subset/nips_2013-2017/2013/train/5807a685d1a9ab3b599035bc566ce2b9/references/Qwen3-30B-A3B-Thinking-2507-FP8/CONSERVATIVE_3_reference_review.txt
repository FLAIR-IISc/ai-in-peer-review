

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, focusing on multinomial estimation and density estimation. The authors derive lower and upper bounds for these problems, claiming that local privacy reduces the effective sample size in multinomial estimation from n to nα²/d, and for density estimation, they show a different convergence rate of (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2) compared to the classical rate of n⁻²β/(2β+1). The paper claims Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation and proposes new density estimation methods that achieve the derived rates.

The paper is well-structured and theoretically sound, with proper application of minimax theory and differential privacy concepts. The mathematical arguments appear rigorous, and the paper builds upon established techniques in statistical decision theory. However, the paper suffers from significant limitations that undermine its contribution to the field.

First and foremost, the paper is purely theoretical with no empirical validation. Despite claiming to provide "sharp rates" and "efficiently implementable algorithms," the authors provide no numerical experiments or comparisons to established methods. For a paper claiming to demonstrate optimal privacy-utility trade-offs, the absence of empirical evidence is a major weakness. How do these theoretical rates translate to practical performance in real-world applications? How do the proposed methods compare to existing privacy-preserving estimators in terms of both utility and computational efficiency?

Second, the paper's claim that Warner's randomized response is optimal is problematic. While the paper demonstrates that it achieves the derived minimax rate, it fails to acknowledge that Warner's method has been known to be suboptimal for decades in modern privacy contexts. The paper cites Warner's original work [23] but doesn't address more recent developments in privacy-preserving estimation, such as the work by Dwork et al. [11] on the exponential mechanism or the more sophisticated approaches in Chaudhuri et al. [6]. The paper claims "it is interesting to note that even with the recent focus on privacy and statistical inference, the optimal privacy-preserving strategy for problems such as survey collection has been known for almost 50 years," but this ignores the fact that Warner's method is typically suboptimal compared to more modern techniques.

Third, the paper lacks adequate comparison to established baselines. The authors derive minimax rates but don't demonstrate how their proposed methods compare to existing privacy-preserving estimators in terms of practical performance. For example, in the density estimation section, they claim their orthogonal-series estimator achieves the optimal rate, but they don't compare it to other methods like the Laplace perturbation approach (which they acknowledge is suboptimal) or other established privacy-preserving density estimators.

Fourth, the paper makes no mention of computational complexity for high-dimensional problems. The proposed methods involve projections onto probability simplices and orthogonal series estimators, but the authors don't discuss how these methods scale with dimensionality or sample size. For large-scale applications, computational efficiency is crucial, yet the paper treats this as an afterthought.

The paper's theoretical contribution is somewhat incremental. While the specific rates for multinomial and density estimation under local privacy are novel, the approach is essentially an application of standard minimax techniques to a well-studied problem. The paper doesn't provide significant new theoretical insights beyond what could be derived using established methods in the literature. The connection to Warner's original work is interesting historically but doesn't represent a major advancement in the field.

The significance of the paper is limited by its purely theoretical nature. While the paper identifies a fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical utility, it doesn't provide practical guidance for practitioners. The paper would be significantly stronger with empirical validation showing how the derived rates translate to real-world performance improvements.

In summary, while the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of privacy-preserving estimation, its lack of empirical validation, inadequate comparison to established methods, and limited practical implications significantly undermine its contribution to the field. The paper would benefit substantially from adding numerical experiments that demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

Strengths:
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-structured
- Proper application of minimax theory to privacy problems
- Clear presentation of mathematical results

Weaknesses:
- No empirical validation of theoretical results
- Inadequate comparison to established privacy-preserving methods
- Overstates the practical significance of Warner's randomized response
- Ignores computational complexity considerations
- Limited discussion of practical implications for real-world applications

Given these limitations, I recommend rejection of this paper. While the theoretical results are interesting, the lack of empirical validation and failure to demonstrate practical relevance make it difficult to justify acceptance at NIPS, where we expect papers to provide both theoretical and practical contributions to the field.
