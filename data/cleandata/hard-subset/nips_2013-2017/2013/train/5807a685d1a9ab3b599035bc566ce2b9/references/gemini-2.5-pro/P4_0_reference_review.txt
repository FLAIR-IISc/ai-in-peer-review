This paper provides a minimax analysis of two fundamental statistical estimation problems—multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation—under the constraint of local differential privacy. The central contribution is a precise characterization of the trade-off between the level of privacy, parameterized by α, and the optimal statistical convergence rate. The authors connect the modern framework of differential privacy with classical statistical decision theory, leveraging information-theoretic tools to derive sharp lower bounds on the minimax risk for any locally private mechanism. These lower bounds are complemented by constructive upper bounds achieved by computationally efficient algorithms.

For multinomial estimation in d dimensions, the paper demonstrates that the minimax mean-squared error scales as d/(nα²), where n is the sample size. This implies that local privacy induces a reduction in the effective sample size from n to approximately nα²/d. A notable finding is that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism [23], originally proposed nearly 50 years ago, is minimax optimal in this modern privacy framework.

For nonparametric density estimation of functions with β derivatives (within a Sobolev class), the paper reveals a more severe penalty for privacy. While the classical minimax rate is n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹), the authors show that under α-local privacy, the optimal rate degrades to (nα²)⁻²β/(²β⁺²). This is not merely a constant-factor loss in sample size but a fundamental change in the polynomial rate of convergence. The paper presents private histogram and orthogonal series estimators that achieve these new optimal rates. This work builds upon and extends the recent literature connecting differential privacy and statistical inference (e.g., Wasserman & Zhou [24], Smith [21]), but distinguishes itself by focusing on the stringent local privacy model and providing sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for population parameter estimation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. It adeptly combines concepts from minimax theory (Le Cam's and Fano's methods), information theory (KL divergence, mutual information), and differential privacy. The approach of using information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1, from their work in [9]) to control the terms in the classical minimax lower bounds is powerful and cleanly executed. The analysis is thorough, covering both lower bounds (impossibility results) and upper bounds (achievability via specific algorithms).

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The primary contribution—deriving sharp minimax rates for fundamental estimation problems under *local* differential privacy—is highly original. The discovery that privacy changes the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate for density estimation is a particularly striking and novel insight. This result demonstrates that the "price of privacy" can be qualitatively different for different problem classes (parametric vs. nonparametric). Furthermore, the formal proof of optimality for Warner's randomized response under the modern definition of differential privacy is an elegant and important connection between historical and contemporary work in the field. The paper also correctly points out that simple perturbation mechanisms, such as adding Laplace noise to raw data points, are suboptimal for density estimation, necessitating the more sophisticated mechanisms proposed.

3.  **Significance and Impact:** The results presented are of fundamental importance to the fields of private machine learning, statistics, and data analysis. By providing a precise, quantitative understanding of the cost of local privacy, this work lays a theoretical foundation that researchers and practitioners can build upon. The findings are likely to influence the design and analysis of future privacy-preserving algorithms. The clear distinction between the impact of privacy on parametric and nonparametric problems is a significant conceptual advance that will guide further research.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the main results are stated precisely, and their implications are discussed with insight. The authors do an excellent job of placing their contributions within the context of related work, clarifying how their focus on population estimation under local privacy differs from and strengthens previous results.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on External Manuscript:** The proofs for the key information-theoretic bounds in Proposition 1 and the main lower bound for density estimation (Theorem 2) are deferred to a companion arXiv paper [9]. While this is understandable given space constraints, it hinders the self-containedness of the manuscript and makes a complete verification of the claims challenging for a reviewer. Including brief proof sketches for these crucial results in an appendix would substantially strengthen the paper.

2.  **Practicality of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The sampling strategy proposed for the optimal orthogonal series estimator (Section 4.3) is more complex than standard mechanisms like randomized response or Laplace addition. While its theoretical properties are well-established in the paper, a brief discussion of its practical implementation and computational complexity (e.g., regarding the sampling from a constrained uniform distribution) would be beneficial for readers interested in applying the method.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Makes a fundamental, novel, and significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of privacy-preserving statistical estimation.
    *   Provides sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for two canonical statistical problems under local differential privacy.
    *   The result on the change of the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a major finding.
    *   Technically strong and exceptionally well-written.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The paper is not fully self-contained, as it relies on a separate manuscript for key proofs.

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It addresses a core problem at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy with rigor and clarity, yielding fundamental insights that are likely to have a lasting impact on the field. The work represents a significant advance in our theoretical understanding of the costs of privacy.
