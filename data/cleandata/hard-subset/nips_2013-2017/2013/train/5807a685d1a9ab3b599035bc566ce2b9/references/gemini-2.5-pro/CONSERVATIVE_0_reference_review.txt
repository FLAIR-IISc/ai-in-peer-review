This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors apply the tools of minimax decision theory to characterize the optimal convergence rates for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, the paper demonstrates that α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα²/d`. For density estimation over Sobolev classes, it shows a more severe penalty, changing the minimax rate's exponent from `2β/(2β+1)` to `2β/(2β+2)`. The authors provide matching lower and upper bounds, establishing the sharpness of these rates, and propose computationally efficient mechanisms that achieve them. A notable finding is that a variant of Warner's original randomized response is minimax optimal for the multinomial case.

**Strengths:**

The paper is technically sound and presents a rigorous, well-executed theoretical analysis. The application of classical minimax theory (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) to the modern local differential privacy setting is a natural and powerful combination. Deriving sharp, matching lower and upper bounds is a significant technical achievement and provides a definitive characterization of the statistical cost of privacy for the problems studied.

The clarity of the paper is commendable. It provides the necessary background on both minimax theory and local privacy, making the work accessible. The problem formulation is precise, and the main theorems are stated clearly. The connection drawn to Warner's 1965 work is particularly elegant, grounding this modern theoretical treatment in the historical origins of the field.

The results are of significant theoretical interest. Quantifying the fundamental limits of estimation under strong privacy constraints is an important contribution. The discovery that local privacy alters the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation, rather than just constants or logarithmic factors, is a key insight. Furthermore, the analysis showing that simple additive noise mechanisms (like Laplace) are suboptimal for density estimation is a valuable and non-obvious finding.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness of this work lies in its purely theoretical nature and the consequent lack of empirical validation for its claims. While minimax rates provide crucial insight into asymptotic behavior, their relevance in finite-sample, practical settings is not explored. The paper makes strong claims about the optimality of its proposed mechanisms and the sub-optimality of others (e.g., Laplace perturbation for density estimation), but these claims are not substantiated with any experimental results. For a conference like NIPS, which values practical applicability, the absence of experiments comparing the proposed "optimal" estimators against more established or simpler baselines is a significant omission. It is difficult to assess whether the asymptotic gains translate into meaningful real-world performance improvements.

Second, the practicality of the proposed mechanism for density estimation is questionable. The orthogonal series estimator in Section 4.3 relies on a rather complex sampling strategy (Eq. 19). While shown to be theoretically optimal, its complexity could be a barrier to implementation and adoption compared to more straightforward techniques like privatized histograms or direct noise addition. The paper does not discuss the computational or implementation overhead of this method. An established, simpler method that is theoretically suboptimal by a polynomial factor might still be preferable in practice if the constants are favorable or the implementation is significantly easier. The endorsement of a new, unconventional mechanism requires more compelling evidence of its practical advantages.

Finally, while the paper tackles two fundamental problems, the scope remains somewhat limited. The analysis provides a blueprint for this type of investigation, but the broader implications for other common statistical and machine learning tasks (e.g., regression, classification, mean estimation in high dimensions) are left to future work. As it stands, the paper presents a complete analysis of two specific cases rather than a broadly applicable methodology.

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

**Pros:**
*   Technically rigorous analysis providing sharp, matching minimax bounds.
*   Addresses the important and timely problem of quantifying the privacy-utility trade-off.
*   Provides a clear and fundamental insight: local privacy can fundamentally change the convergence rate of nonparametric estimators.
*   The connection to, and validation of, Warner's randomized response is an elegant and insightful result.

**Cons:**
*   The work is entirely theoretical, with no empirical validation to support its asymptotic claims in practical, finite-sample regimes.
*   The proposed "optimal" mechanism for density estimation is complex and its practical utility compared to simpler, established baselines is unproven.
*   The paper's claims about the sub-optimality of other methods (e.g., those based on Laplace noise) are not supported by any experimental comparison.

In conclusion, this paper represents a solid piece of theoretical work that contributes to our understanding of the fundamental limits of private statistical estimation. However, its claims of optimality for new, unconventional methods are not sufficiently validated against established approaches in a practical context. The lack of any experimental results makes it difficult to endorse the proposed methods for real-world use and leaves the practical significance of the theoretical findings in question. While the theoretical contributions are sound, the paper would be substantially stronger if it provided empirical evidence to ground its asymptotic analysis and demonstrate the claimed superiority of its proposed estimators.
