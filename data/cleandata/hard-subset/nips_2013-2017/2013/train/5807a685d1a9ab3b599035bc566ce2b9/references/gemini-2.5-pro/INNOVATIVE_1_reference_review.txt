This paper undertakes a rigorous theoretical investigation of the fundamental trade-off between statistical utility and privacy, specifically within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors apply the tools of minimax decision theory to characterize the optimal convergence rates for two canonical probability estimation problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation over `d` categories, they find that α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to `nα^2/d`. A notable consequence is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal. For nonparametric estimation of β-smooth densities, the authors uncover a more dramatic penalty: local privacy fundamentally alters the polynomial rate of convergence, degrading it from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. The paper supports these sharp minimax lower bounds with matching upper bounds, achieved by computationally efficient and novel privacy-preserving mechanisms. This work builds a crucial bridge between the classical statistical literature on privacy (Warner) and the modern computer science framework of differential privacy, providing sharp, non-asymptotic answers to fundamental questions about the cost of privacy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel synthesis of two powerful theoretical frameworks: minimax statistical decision theory and local differential privacy. While prior work has explored differential privacy, it has often focused on sample-level accuracy or provided looser bounds. This paper asks a more fundamental question: what is the *best possible* estimation error for a given population, under a given privacy constraint? The answers it provides are both sharp and, in the case of density estimation, quite surprising. The discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate is a major conceptual contribution that advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of private data analysis. This is a foundational paper that other researchers will likely build upon for years to come.

2.  **Bold and Insightful Results:** The findings are not merely incremental. The characterization of the `n -> nα^2/d` sample size reduction for multinomials provides a clean, intuitive, and quantifiable cost of privacy. Even more impressively, the result for density estimation reveals that the cost of privacy can be more severe than a simple reduction in sample size. This forces a re-evaluation of how we think about the privacy-utility trade-off in complex, infinite-dimensional problems. The connection showing Warner's 50-year-old method to be optimal is an elegant and satisfying piece of intellectual history and theory combined.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Completeness:** The work is technically excellent. The authors not only derive tight lower bounds using sophisticated information-theoretic arguments (Le Cam's and Fano's methods adapted for the private setting) but also demonstrate that these bounds are achievable. This is a complete piece of theoretical work. The construction of novel, non-trivial privacy mechanisms—particularly the orthogonal series estimator for β>1 smooth densities—is a significant technical achievement in its own right. It demonstrates that simple noise-addition schemes (like adding Laplace noise to raw data) are suboptimal and that more creative mechanisms are necessary to achieve optimal rates.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly introduces the concepts of local privacy and the minimax framework, states its main results cleanly in Theorems 1 and 2, and then dedicates separate sections to proving their attainability. The motivation is clear, the notation is consistent, and the flow of logic is easy to follow for a reader familiar with statistical theory.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Concurrent Work:** The core information-theoretic lemmas used to prove the lower bounds (Proposition 1) are presented without proof and cited from the authors' own concurrent paper [9]. While this is a common practice, it means that a full verification of the lower bounds requires consulting another manuscript. This is a very minor point, as the focus of the current paper is on the application of these tools to derive the sharp rates for specific statistical problems, but it slightly reduces the self-contained nature of the contribution.

2.  **Complexity of the Optimal Mechanism:** The proposed optimal mechanism for density estimation (Section 4.3) is quite sophisticated, involving sampling from discrete sets based on inner products. While its theoretical importance is undeniable, its complexity may present a barrier to immediate practical application compared to simpler (though suboptimal) methods like Laplace noise addition. This is less a flaw of the paper and more an inherent feature of the problem's difficulty, but it is worth noting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper makes a fundamental, highly original contribution by bridging minimax theory and local differential privacy.
*   The results are significant and surprising, particularly the discovery of a change in the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation.
*   It provides a complete theoretical picture by presenting sharp, matching upper and lower bounds.
*   The work is technically deep, including the design of novel, non-trivial mechanisms that achieve the optimal rates.
*   It elegantly connects modern privacy concepts to the historical foundations of the field (Warner's randomized response).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The key technical lemmas for the lower bounds are imported from another paper by the same authors, making the work not entirely self-contained.
*   The optimal mechanism for the nonparametric case is complex, which might limit its direct practical uptake.

**Recommendation:**

Strong Accept.

This paper is an outstanding example of fundamental theoretical research that NIPS should champion. It addresses a core problem—the ultimate statistical cost of privacy—with rigor, depth, and creativity. Its central results are novel, significant, and likely to influence future research in privacy-preserving machine learning and statistics. The discovery that local privacy can alter the fundamental difficulty of an estimation problem (by changing the convergence exponent) is a bold and important finding. While one could point to minor issues like the reliance on a companion paper for key lemmas, these are vastly outweighed by the paper's conceptual novelty and significance. This is precisely the kind of work that defines the state of the art and opens up new avenues for investigation.
