This paper addresses the fundamental problem of statistical estimation under privacy constraints, specifically utilizing local differential privacy. The authors connect Warner’s early work on privacy-preserving surveys with modern differential privacy and minimax theory, providing theoretical bounds on the convergence rates for multinomial and density estimation. The core contribution lies in quantifying the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, demonstrating a clear penalty for providing local privacy.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the mathematical framework. The connection to Warner’s work is a nice historical touch, and the authors correctly position their work within the broader literature on differential privacy and statistical decision theory. The minimax analysis is rigorous, and the derivation of the convergence rates appears sound. The inclusion of achievable upper bounds, demonstrating the tightness of the lower bounds, is a definite strength.

However, despite the technical correctness, I remain unconvinced of the overall significance of this work for NIPS. The core idea – that privacy comes at a cost – is not novel. This has been established in the differential privacy literature for some time. While the authors provide sharp rates, the *results* largely confirm what is intuitively expected: stronger privacy (smaller α) leads to worse statistical performance. The results for multinomial estimation, showing a reduction in effective sample size, feel somewhat incremental.  The more interesting departure in density estimation, with a different polynomial rate of convergence, is also not entirely surprising given the known limitations of perturbation-based approaches. 

My main concern lies with the lack of a compelling, new *insight*. The paper feels more like a careful application of existing tools (minimax theory, differential privacy) to a well-understood problem, rather than a breakthrough that significantly advances the field. The practical implications are also not fully explored. While the authors mention the optimality of Warner’s randomized response, the discussion remains largely theoretical. There’s little guidance for practitioners on *how* to best navigate the privacy-utility trade-off in real-world scenarios beyond stating that a smaller α is worse.

Furthermore, while the authors compare their results to existing literature, they do not adequately discuss alternative approaches to privacy-preserving statistical estimation *beyond* simple perturbation. For example, more sophisticated mechanisms involving data aggregation or feature selection are not considered. 

**Strengths:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and derivation of minimax rates.
*   Clear presentation of the mathematical framework.
*   Tightness of bounds (matching upper and lower bounds).
*   Connection to Warner’s foundational work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of substantial novelty – the main result (privacy degrades performance) is largely expected.
*   Limited practical guidance or discussion of real-world implications.
*   Narrow focus on perturbation-based approaches, neglecting alternative privacy mechanisms.
*   The density estimation result, while interesting, doesn't represent a substantial shift in understanding. 



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous analysis of a fundamental problem.
*   The results are formally correct and provide useful bounds on estimation error.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks significant novelty and does not offer a substantial advance over existing literature.
*   The practical implications are limited, and the paper does not provide clear guidance for practitioners.
*   The scope feels somewhat narrow given the broader field of privacy-preserving machine learning.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **Reject**. While the paper is technically competent, it does not meet the NIPS criteria for originality and significance. The results are largely expected and, while mathematically sound, do not represent a substantial contribution to the field. The paper would be more appropriate for a more theoretically-focused statistics or privacy conference. I would encourage the authors to consider exploring more practical aspects of their work or investigating more sophisticated privacy mechanisms to generate more impactful results.
