- Paper derives sharp minimax lower and upper bounds for multinomial probability and nonparametric density estimation under local differential privacy.  
- Shows local privacy penalizes effective sample size for multinomial estimation and alters density estimation convergence rates.  
- Proves Warner’s randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation and gives efficient estimators achieving the bounds for density estimation.  
- Bridges prior work: connects Warner’s randomized response, modern differential privacy, and classical minimax estimation, building on the authors’ earlier divergence bounds.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: rigorous use of differential privacy and minimax theory with well-justified derivations.  
- Sharp results: matching upper and lower bounds demonstrate tightness of claimed rates.  
- Practical relevance: recovery of randomized response optimality and efficiently implementable estimators increase applied significance.  
- Clear presentation overall: standard notation and good motivation, though some sections are dense.  
- Addresses an important and timely problem on privacy–utility trade-offs.  
- Density estimation method may be computationally complex and less practical than simpler alternatives.  
- Analysis limited to Sobolev-type smoothness classes; broader density classes or alternative smoothness notions are not explored.  
- Minor clarity issues: dense notation in places and need for more intuitive explanations of key lemmas/propositions.  
- Evaluation scores: Quality 9/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous sharp results, fundamental problem relevance, and practical confirmation of randomized response optimality.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potential computational impracticality of density estimator and limited scope of density classes.  
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and discuss density estimation practicality.
