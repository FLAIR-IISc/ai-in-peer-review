## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation under the framework of local differential privacy. Building upon recent work connecting differential privacy and minimax theory, the authors derive sharp minimax convergence rates for estimating multinomial probabilities and nonparametric densities when data is subject to local privacy constraints. They demonstrate a reduction in effective sample size for multinomial estimation and a modified convergence rate for density estimation compared to the non-private setting. Importantly, they provide matching upper bounds, showing the achievability of their lower bounds, and highlight the optimality of Warner's randomized response procedure.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis based on well-established concepts from minimax theory and differential privacy.  The use of divergence bounds and hypothesis testing arguments is sound and clearly presented.
* **Sharp Results:** The derived minimax rates are sharp, establishing a precise understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off. This is a significant contribution as it goes beyond simply showing a penalty for privacy and quantifies it accurately.
* **Practical Implications:** The analysis of Warner’s randomized response mechanism and its optimality is particularly valuable. Demonstrating that a decades-old technique remains optimal in this modern setting is a compelling result.  The discussion of efficient algorithms to achieve the upper bounds is also helpful.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper clearly situates its contributions within the broader context of privacy and statistical inference, referencing relevant literature and highlighting how it builds upon prior work (e.g., Duchi et al. [9], Warner [23]).
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, although some sections (particularly the background material) are dense.  The notation is standard and clearly defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density Estimation Complexity:** While the results for density estimation are theoretically interesting, the practical implications aren't as immediately clear as in the multinomial case. The perturbation-based estimators mentioned appear sub-optimal, and a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges in achieving the theoretical rates would be beneficial. The statement that the perturbation-based estimators can only achieve a rate of (nα2)− 2β β+3 is somewhat buried and could be given more emphasis, as it motivates the orthogonal series approach.
* **Background Material Density:**  The background section, while thorough, is quite dense and might be difficult for readers unfamiliar with both minimax theory and differential privacy to navigate.  More intuitive explanations or pointers to introductory resources could improve accessibility.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the focus is theoretical, a small-scale simulation study demonstrating the impact of α on the estimation error (even for the multinomial case) would strengthen the paper and provide a more concrete understanding of the trade-offs.
* **Missing Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be expanded. For example, the connection to private density estimation methods based on kernel density estimation (KDE) could be discussed.




**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** Very Strong. The technical analysis is rigorous, the claims are well-supported, and the paper represents a complete and significant piece of work. The authors are careful in their analysis and acknowledge the limitations of their results.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written, but the density of the background material and some technical details could be improved for broader accessibility.
* **Originality:** Strong. The combination of minimax theory, differential privacy, and the derivation of sharp rates for probability estimation is novel and makes a significant contribution to the field.
* **Significance:** Strong. The results have the potential to impact the design of privacy-preserving statistical methods and inform policy decisions related to data privacy.  The clear quantification of the privacy-utility trade-off is particularly valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper provides a rigorous and insightful analysis of a fundamental problem in privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* The results are sharp and theoretically significant, offering a precise understanding of the trade-offs involved.
* The connection to Warner's randomized response procedure adds practical relevance.
* The work builds upon and extends existing research in both differential privacy and minimax theory.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The density estimation results, while theoretically sound, lack clear practical implications and could benefit from further discussion.
* The background material is dense and may be challenging for some readers.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The strengths of the paper outweigh the minor weaknesses, and I believe it is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.  I recommend the authors consider incorporating a small-scale simulation study and providing more intuitive explanations of the background material to further enhance the paper's accessibility.
