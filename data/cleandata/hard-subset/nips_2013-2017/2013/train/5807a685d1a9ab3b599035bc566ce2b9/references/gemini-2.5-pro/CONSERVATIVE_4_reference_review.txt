This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and local differential privacy, framed within the classical minimax decision-theoretic setting. The authors aim to characterize the fundamental limits of estimation for two canonical probability estimation problems: d-dimensional multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes.

The main contributions are the derivation of sharp minimax rates of convergence under α-local differential privacy. For multinomial estimation, the paper establishes that the mean-squared error scales as d/(nα²), which corresponds to a reduction in effective sample size from n to nα²/d. Notably, the authors show that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is sufficient to achieve this optimal rate. For nonparametric estimation of a density with β derivatives, the results are more striking. The paper demonstrates that local privacy alters the polynomial rate of convergence, degrading it from the standard n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹) to (nα²)⁻²β/(²β⁺²). To prove the attainability of these rates, the authors provide corresponding upper bounds achieved by specific, computationally efficient estimators: a privatized histogram for Lipschitz (β=1) densities and a novel orthogonal series estimator for the general case (β > 1).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Rigor and Soundness:** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous application of classical minimax decision theory to the modern problem of local differential privacy. The analysis is built upon a solid foundation of well-established statistical tools, including Le Cam's and Fano's methods for deriving lower bounds. The derivation of matching lower and upper bounds (up to universal constants) provides strong evidence for the correctness and tightness of the results. This represents a high-quality, complete piece of theoretical work.

2.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper addresses a significant and fundamental question: what is the ultimate statistical price of guaranteeing local privacy? By providing sharp minimax rates, the authors offer a precise, quantitative answer for two important classes of problems. This moves the field beyond the analysis of specific algorithms and toward a complete understanding of the inherent limitations, which is a crucial step for any mature field.

3.  **Significant and Insightful Results:** The findings are both novel and important. The result that Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal is a particularly elegant and compelling conclusion, connecting a foundational technique in survey statistics with the modern framework of differential privacy. Furthermore, the discovery that local privacy changes the exponent in the convergence rate for density estimation is a non-obvious and highly significant insight, demonstrating that the privacy-utility trade-off can be more severe in nonparametric settings.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The analysis is entirely theoretical. While establishing sharp asymptotic rates is a valuable contribution, the paper provides no empirical results to demonstrate how the proposed estimators perform in finite-sample regimes or to validate the predicted scaling laws. Without experiments, it is difficult to assess the practical implications of the derived rates. For instance, the behavior of the hidden constants and the relevance of the asymptotic regime for practical dataset sizes (n) and privacy levels (α) remain unknown.

2.  **Practicality of Proposed Mechanisms:** While randomized response is a simple and established method, the orthogonal series estimator for general density estimation appears somewhat complex and specifically engineered to achieve the theoretical lower bound. The paper rightly points out that simpler methods like adding Laplace noise to samples are suboptimal, but it fails to provide any quantitative comparison. A discussion or, ideally, an experiment comparing the proposed optimal estimator to a simpler, standard (but suboptimal) method would have substantially strengthened the paper's practical relevance. As it stands, the practical utility of the proposed estimator is not well-established.

3.  **Reliance on a Concurrent Preprint:** The core technical lemmas for the lower bounds (Proposition 1), which bound the relevant information-theoretic quantities, are stated without proof and cited from the authors' own arXiv preprint [9]. While this is common practice, it means that a central part of the technical novelty resides in another manuscript, which complicates a fully self-contained assessment of the paper's technical innovation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper provides a strong, foundational theoretical contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistics. It is well-written, methodologically sound, and delivers significant, sharp results on an important problem. The connection to classical statistical methods is a major plus, and the insights into how local privacy affects estimation rates are novel and likely to be influential.

The primary weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation, which limits the immediate impact for practitioners. I am generally skeptical of purely theoretical work that does not ground its claims in at least some form of empirical reality. However, the quality and fundamental nature of the theoretical results presented here are exceptional. The paper establishes a benchmark against which future private estimation algorithms must be measured. While I would have strongly preferred to see an experimental section, the theoretical contribution is significant enough to stand on its own. The work advances the state of the art in our understanding of private statistical inference in a demonstrable way.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
