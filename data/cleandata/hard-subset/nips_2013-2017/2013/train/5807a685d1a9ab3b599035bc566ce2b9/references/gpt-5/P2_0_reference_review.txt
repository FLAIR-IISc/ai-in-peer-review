Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), focusing on minimax risks. The setup is non-interactive (memoryless) channels Q mapping each Xi to a privatized Zi with α-LDP. The authors bring together minimax decision-theoretic tools (Le Cam, Fano) with information-contraction bounds under LDP to derive sharp rates, and provide matching algorithms.

Two main contributions:
- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the α-private minimax risk in ℓ2 scales as min{1, d/(nα2)} (and in ℓ1 as min{1, d/√(nα2)}), showing an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d when α is a small constant and d is fixed. They also prove attainability via a slight variant of Warner’s randomized response and via simple perturb-then-project estimators, establishing optimality of randomized response.
- Density estimation: For Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1], the non-private minimax rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2). They give two achieving mechanisms: a privatized histogram (β=1) and a bounded orthogonal-series scheme with a carefully constructed privatization channel that preserves expectations of basis coefficients while satisfying LDP. They also argue that simple Laplace perturbation of raw Xi leads to a fundamentally worse deconvolution rate, hence cannot attain the optimal private rate.

The work builds on and explicitly cites the authors’ prior technical development [9], from which they restate key information bounds (KL and mutual information contraction under LDP). It also connects to work on DP in databases and learning (e.g., Dwork et al., Kasiviswanathan et al., Wasserman & Zhou), while focusing on local (user-level) privacy rather than curator-side (global) DP.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Claims are well-supported by standard minimax lower bound techniques (Le Cam/Fano) combined with sharp information bounds under LDP. The upper bounds are constructive with computationally efficient procedures (projection onto the simplex in O(d), histogram bins, and a practical albeit nontrivial orthogonal-series sampling scheme). The separation between mechanisms that perturb Xi directly and those tailored to preserve basis expectations is clearly justified (via deconvolution rates). Assumptions (non-interactive LDP, α ∈ [0,1], bounded bases) are stated, and the main results are sharp up to universal constants. Proofs are deferred to the longer version [9], which is reasonable for NeurIPS but makes the present submission somewhat proof-light.
- Completeness: The paper covers lower and upper bounds for two canonical problems and gives implementable algorithms. Some practical considerations (choice of k, constants ck in the series mechanism, computational burden of rejection sampling) could be further elaborated.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, well-organized, and uses standard notation. The minimax framework and testing reductions are succinctly summarized. The mechanism descriptions (randomized response variant, histogram with Laplace on indicators, orthogonal-series sampling) are explicit enough to reproduce.
- Minor clarity issues: In Eq. (10), using “−xj” for flipping a Bernoulli bit when Z ∈ {0,1}d is slightly confusing; consider stating explicitly that the bit is flipped with specified probability. In Section 4.3, the role and values of ck and how B is chosen could be clarified; a brief note on the acceptance rate of the rejection sampler would help. It would also help to state more explicitly the parameter ranges where the “effective n” heuristic n → nα2/d holds (small α, fixed d).

Originality
- The conceptual combination—precise minimax rates under local privacy with matching mechanisms—is novel and important. Showing that randomized response is sample-complexity optimal for multinomial estimation is a compelling and concrete message connecting classical survey methodology to modern LDP.
- The lower bound technique relies on information-contraction inequalities under LDP previously developed by the same authors ([9], restated here). The density estimation result—polynomial rate change to 2β/(2β+2) with sharp mechanisms—appears to be new and nontrivial. The mechanisms for achieving optimality (especially the orthogonal-series channel) are technically interesting.
- A potential concern is overlap with the authors’ arXiv [9], which the paper cites as containing all proofs. The NeurIPS paper serves as a polished, focused exposition of those results; the authors should clearly delineate which results first appear here versus [9].

Significance
- High. The results establish fundamental trade-offs between utility and privacy under LDP with sharp rates and practical mechanisms. They are likely to influence both theory (lower bound techniques under privacy constraints) and practice (guiding mechanism choice, e.g., validating randomized response and discouraging naive Laplace-on-data for density estimation). The change in the nonparametric rate is conceptually impactful.

Strengths
- Sharp matching lower and upper bounds; clear takeaways (effective sample size; exponent degradation).
- Practical, efficient mechanisms with rigorous guarantees; optimality of randomized response.
- Clean, unified minimax-information framework adaptable to other problems.
- Well written and well situated in related work.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on [9] for proofs; novelty relative to [9] should be clarified.
- Focused on non-interactive LDP; no results for interactive channels where rates might improve.
- No empirical illustrations; some constants and implementation details (e.g., ck, acceptance rates) are opaque.
- Assumes known smoothness β and uses fixed k; adaptivity to unknown β is not discussed.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Clarify the novelty beyond [9]. Which theorems/mechanisms first appear here?
- Discuss whether interaction (sequentially adaptive LDP channels) can improve rates for these problems.
- Provide guidance on selecting k adaptively and on practical constants in the series mechanism.
- Consider adding a brief experiment demonstrating the rates and the suboptimality of Laplace-on-X.
- Tighten the randomized response description to avoid confusion with {0,1} vs {−1,1} encodings.

Pro/con for acceptance
Pros:
- Foundational, sharp theoretical contributions with practical implications.
- Elegant, well-written, and well-justified results and mechanisms.
- High relevance to NeurIPS communities working on privacy, statistics, and learning.

Cons:
- Substantial overlap with an existing arXiv manuscript; proofs not in the submission.
- Scope limited to non-interactive LDP; no empirical validation.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The work is technically strong, novel in its core results (especially for density estimation), clearly presented, and significant for the field. Minor revisions to clarify novelty relative to [9] and to improve a few presentation details would further strengthen the paper.
