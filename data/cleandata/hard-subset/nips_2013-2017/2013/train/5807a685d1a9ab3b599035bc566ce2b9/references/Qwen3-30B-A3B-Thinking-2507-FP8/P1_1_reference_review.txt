

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary

This paper studies the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation problems, bridging the classical privacy framework of Warner [23] with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors establish sharp minimax convergence rates for two fundamental statistical problems under α-local differential privacy constraints: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, they prove that the effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d, with Warner's randomized response mechanism achieving optimal rates. For density estimation with β continuous derivatives, they show the minimax rate shifts from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2), demonstrating that privacy constraints fundamentally alter convergence rates. The paper provides both tight lower bounds and efficiently computable estimators achieving these bounds.

This work builds on Duchi et al. [9] (which the authors cite) by extending their privacy-constrained minimax analysis to provide sharp rates for specific estimation problems. It connects to the broader literature on differential privacy for statistical inference [11, 13, 16, 24], but makes the novel contribution of establishing precise trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency through minimax analysis.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically rigorous and well-executed. The authors provide complete theoretical proofs for both lower and upper bounds, with careful attention to the mathematical details. The minimax framework is correctly applied, and the proofs of the key theorems are sound and well-structured. The paper demonstrates excellent theoretical understanding of the interplay between privacy constraints and statistical estimation, and the authors are appropriately careful in discussing the limitations of their approach (e.g., noting that direct Laplace perturbation of samples is suboptimal for density estimation).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly defined. The authors provide thorough explanations of the background concepts (local privacy, minimax rates, Sobolev spaces) before presenting their results. The paper is structured logically, with clear transitions between sections. The main results are presented with appropriate precision, and the proofs are sufficiently detailed for experts to follow.

**Originality:** The paper makes several significant original contributions. First, it demonstrates that Warner's randomized response procedure (introduced nearly 50 years ago) is optimal for multinomial estimation under local privacy, a result that has been overlooked in the recent differential privacy literature. Second, it reveals a fundamental change in the convergence rate for density estimation under local privacy (from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2)), which is a novel theoretical insight. Third, it provides a unified framework for analyzing privacy-constrained estimation using minimax decision theory, which is a significant contribution to the literature.

**Significance:** The results have substantial theoretical and practical significance. The paper provides a precise quantitative understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off in statistical estimation, which is valuable for practitioners designing privacy-preserving data collection mechanisms. The finding that Warner's simple randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly noteworthy and has important practical implications. The density estimation results reveal a fundamental difference between private and non-private estimation that had not been previously characterized.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong in theoretical contribution, there are a few areas for improvement:

1. **Lack of empirical validation:** As a theoretical paper, this is understandable, but the absence of any numerical examples or simulations to illustrate the practical implications of the theoretical results is a missed opportunity. For instance, it would be valuable to see how the convergence rates translate to practical sample size requirements for achieving a given level of estimation error.

2. **Limited discussion of practical parameter choices:** The paper focuses on theoretical rates but doesn't address how α (the privacy parameter) should be chosen in practice. It would be helpful to discuss the trade-offs between privacy level and statistical utility in more concrete terms, particularly for the density estimation setting where the rate change is more dramatic.

3. **Narrow scope of density estimation:** The paper focuses on density estimation on [0,1] with the trigonometric basis. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss how the results might extend to higher-dimensional settings or different basis functions, as this is a common direction for future work in density estimation.

4. **Connection to recent NIPS work:** While the paper cites relevant literature, it could better position itself relative to recent NIPS papers on privacy and statistical learning (e.g., Chaudhuri & Hsu [5], Lei [18], Smith [21]). A more explicit comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution statement.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. The results are novel, rigorously proven, and have both theoretical importance and practical relevance. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions. While the lack of empirical validation is a minor drawback for a NIPS paper (which often values both theoretical and practical contributions), this is understandable for a purely theoretical work.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results are sharp, well-supported, and provide valuable theoretical insights that will inform future work in the area. The connection to Warner's historical work on randomized response is particularly noteworthy and adds historical perspective to the modern differential privacy literature.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the privacy parameter α might be chosen in practice, perhaps with a small example showing the trade-off between privacy level and estimation error.

2. Include a short section comparing the theoretical rates to practical sample size requirements for achieving a given level of accuracy (e.g., how many more samples are needed to achieve the same accuracy with α = 0.1 versus α = 0.5).

3. Add a brief discussion of how the density estimation results might extend to higher dimensions, as this is a natural next step for future work.

4. Explicitly connect the results to recent NIPS papers on privacy and statistical learning to better position the contribution within the conference's recent literature.

This paper represents an important contribution to the theoretical understanding of privacy-constrained statistical estimation, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
