**Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors apply the tools of minimax decision theory to derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds on the estimation error for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation over `d` categories, the authors demonstrate that α-local privacy incurs a penalty equivalent to reducing the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα^2/d`. They show that a mechanism similar to Warner's classic randomized response is minimax optimal. For nonparametric density estimation of functions in a Sobolev class with smoothness `β`, the paper reveals a more severe penalty: the minimax rate degrades from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, a change in the polynomial exponent. The authors complement these lower bounds with computationally efficient estimation procedures (a private histogram and an orthogonal series estimator) that provably achieve these new optimal rates.

**Strengths**

The paper addresses a problem of significant contemporary importance: quantifying the precise statistical cost of strong, local privacy guarantees. The connection between classical minimax theory and the modern cryptographic notion of differential privacy is elegant and provides a powerful lens for analysis. The primary strengths of this work are its theoretical depth and the sharpness of its results.

1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic characterization of the price of local privacy for fundamental estimation tasks. This moves beyond simply proposing private algorithms to understanding the fundamental limits of what is possible.
2.  **Novel Technical Results:** The discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation (rather than just constants or log factors) is a significant and surprising finding. This highlights a fundamental structural change in the difficulty of the problem under privacy constraints.
3.  **Completeness of Theory:** The work is laudable for providing matching upper and lower bounds. This demonstrates a complete understanding of the minimax rates for the problems studied. The proposed algorithms that achieve the lower bounds are constructive and computationally efficient.
4.  **Clarity of High-Level Message:** The "effective sample size" interpretation for the multinomial case is insightful and provides a clear, memorable takeaway for practitioners and theorists alike.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the clear significance of the contributions, the manuscript suffers from a number of flaws in its execution, ranging from minor formatting issues to a major structural problem regarding its self-containment. As a reviewer, I found these issues substantial enough to undermine my confidence in the work as a complete and polished contribution ready for publication at NIPS.

1.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** This is the most critical issue. The proofs for the main lower bounds (Proposition 1, Theorem 1, and Theorem 2) are not included in the paper or its appendix. Instead, the authors repeatedly and explicitly state that the proofs are in a separate arXiv manuscript [9]. A conference submission must be reviewable on its own merits. Deferring the core technical arguments that support the paper's central claims to an external, unreviewed manuscript is not acceptable practice. It is impossible to verify the paper's correctness without access to these proofs.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:**
    *   The mechanism for the orthogonal series estimator in Section 4.3 is presented without sufficient intuition. The two-stage sampling process in (19) is complex, and the paper does not explain why this specific design is necessary to achieve the optimal rate, in contrast to simpler noise-addition schemes which are shown to be suboptimal.
    *   The constant `ck` in equation (20) is vaguely defined as being `O(1)` and bounded away from zero. A more precise characterization of its dependence on `k`, if any, is needed for a rigorous verification of the subsequent variance analysis.
    *   The analysis of the randomized response estimator in Section 3.2 uses a Taylor approximation (`e^x - 1 ≈ x`) to get the `1/α^2` dependence, but claims the result holds for `α ∈ [0, 1]`. While the approximation is reasonable here, this lack of precision appears in several places and detracts from the overall rigor.

3.  **Inconsistent and Incomplete Formatting:**
    *   The bibliography is in a poor state. Multiple references (e.g., [2], [17]) are incomplete, containing placeholder commas (", , ,"). Other references have inconsistent formatting. This suggests a lack of careful preparation and proofreading.
    *   The notation `an . bn` to mean `an = O(bn)` is defined but highly non-standard and hinders readability. Standard `O(·)` notation is preferred.
    *   The sentence ending Section 2, "we provide proofs of all results in [9]," appears to be a copy-paste error from the introduction of manuscript [9] itself and is confusing in this context.

4.  **Minor Imprecisions:**
    *   The paper claims optimality for "Warner’s classical randomized response mechanism," but the mechanism analyzed in (10) is a multi-dimensional generalization, not the original binary response mechanism. The claim should be phrased more precisely.
    *   In the analysis of the histogram estimator (Proposition 2), the bound contains two terms. The text claims the first term dominates for fixed `α`, which is true asymptotically in `n`, but a more detailed discussion of the trade-off would be welcome.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a highly important and timely problem.
*   The main theoretical results, if correct, are novel, significant, and likely to be influential.
*   The work provides a complete minimax characterization with matching, constructive upper bounds.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is not self-contained, with the core proofs deferred to an external manuscript, making it impossible to properly review and verify.
*   The manuscript exhibits a lack of care in its preparation, evidenced by numerous formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography.
*   Several key technical descriptions lack the clarity and detail required for full understanding and reproducibility.
*   Minor but repeated instances of imprecision detract from the paper's overall quality.

**Decision**

While the ideas presented in this paper are promising and potentially of high impact, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication. The lack of self-containment is a fatal flaw for a rigorous peer review process. This, combined with a significant number of issues regarding clarity, precision, and formatting, leads me to recommend **rejection**. The authors should be encouraged to produce a revised, self-contained version of the manuscript that addresses these substantial shortcomings for submission to a future venue.
