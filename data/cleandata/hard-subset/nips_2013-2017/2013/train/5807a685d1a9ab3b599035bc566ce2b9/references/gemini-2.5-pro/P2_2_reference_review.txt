This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and local differential privacy for two fundamental probability estimation problems: multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The authors connect the modern framework of α-local differential privacy with classical minimax theory to derive sharp, non-asymptotic lower and upper bounds on the estimation error. For multinomial estimation on `d` categories, they show that α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to `n * α^2 / d`. Remarkably, they demonstrate that Warner's original randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal. For nonparametric density estimation of functions with `β` degrees of smoothness, they establish a more severe penalty: the optimal convergence rate changes from the classical `n^(-2β / (2β+1))` to `(n * α^2)^(-2β / (2β+2))`, a degradation in the polynomial exponent. The authors provide constructive, computationally efficient algorithms (a private histogram and a novel orthogonal series estimator) that achieve these optimal rates.

### Strengths:

1.  **Significance:** This is a foundational and highly significant contribution. The paper addresses a central question in private data analysis: what is the fundamental price of privacy? By providing sharp minimax rates, the authors move beyond the analysis of specific algorithms to characterize the best possible performance for *any* algorithm under a given privacy constraint. This provides a precise, quantitative understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off and establishes a theoretical benchmark for future work.

2.  **Originality:** The combination of minimax decision theory with the constraints of local differential privacy is novel and powerful. While prior work has studied utility in DP, it often focused on the accuracy of estimating a sample quantity (e.g., empirical risk) rather than the true population parameter. By focusing on population-level estimation error, this work provides stronger and more statistically meaningful guarantees. The result that simple additive Laplace noise is suboptimal for density estimation and that a more sophisticated mechanism is required is a novel and important insight.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically outstanding. The derivations of the lower bounds, which leverage the authors' information-theoretic bounds from a companion paper [9], are elegant and appear sound. Crucially, the paper doesn't stop at lower bounds; it provides matching upper bounds with explicit, non-trivial algorithms. The design of the orthogonal series estimator for smooth density functions is particularly impressive, as it requires a clever randomization scheme to achieve both local privacy and the optimal statistical rate. This demonstrates a deep command of both privacy mechanisms and statistical estimation theory.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and clear. It does an excellent job of motivating the problem, defining the formal setting (local privacy, minimax risk), and crisply stating its main contributions and results. The comparison between the classical rates and the new privacy-constrained rates is stark and easy to understand, effectively highlighting the "cost" of privacy in each setting. The logical flow from the finite-dimensional multinomial problem to the infinite-dimensional density estimation problem is very effective.

### Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:

1.  **Reliance on External Proofs:** The core technical lemmas used for the lower bounds (Proposition 1) are stated without proof and deferred to a longer arXiv preprint [9]. While this is a common and acceptable practice for a conference paper due to space constraints, it means the paper is not entirely self-contained for a reviewer seeking to verify every step of the argument.

2.  **Practicality of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The sampling strategy for the orthogonal series estimator (Section 4.3, Scheme 19) is quite sophisticated. While theoretically optimal and computationally efficient in principle, a brief discussion on its practical implementation or intuition behind its design could be beneficial for readers less familiar with these advanced techniques. This is a minor point, as the theoretical contribution is the primary focus.

3.  **Scope of Problems:** The paper provides a definitive analysis for two canonical problems. A natural question is how these techniques and insights generalize to other fundamental statistical tasks like regression, classification, or parameter estimation in more complex graphical models. While outside the scope of this paper, a brief mention of potential extensions or challenges in the discussion would be welcome.

### Summary and Recommendation:

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy. It establishes a rigorous connection between local differential privacy and minimax theory, providing sharp, definitive answers about the optimal statistical rates achievable for two core estimation problems. The technical quality is very high, the results are significant, and the presentation is exemplary. The weaknesses are minor and typical of the conference format. This work is likely to become a cornerstone reference for researchers studying the theoretical limits of private statistical inference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Fundamental Contribution:** Provides a rigorous, quantitative answer to the privacy-utility trade-off for core statistical problems.
*   **Technical Depth and Novelty:** Combines two major theoretical frameworks in a novel way, yielding deep results and non-trivial new algorithms.
*   **High Impact:** Establishes benchmarks that will guide the design and analysis of future privacy-preserving algorithms.
*   **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is beautifully written and accessible despite its technical nature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   (None of significance)

I strongly recommend acceptance for this paper. It represents a major advance in our understanding of private data analysis and is of the quality expected for an oral presentation at NIPS.
