## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, specifically focusing on multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors ground their analysis within the framework of differential privacy (specifically, local privacy) and minimax decision theory.  They derive sharp lower and upper bounds on the minimax risk for both problems under local privacy constraints, revealing a penalty in convergence rates directly tied to the level of privacy (α). A key finding is a different rate of convergence for density estimation compared to the classical non-private case, and they demonstrate optimality of Warner's randomized response procedure for multinomial estimation.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon a rich history of research in privacy-preserving statistical inference, citing foundational work by Warner (1965) on randomized response and more recent advances in differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006). It extends the authors’ previous work (Duchi et al., 2013) by applying their divergence bounds to concrete statistical estimation problems.  The paper nicely connects differential privacy with classical minimax theory, a combination not thoroughly explored before.  It also connects back to classical results on density estimation (e.g., Tsybakov, 2009), highlighting how privacy alters the fundamental rates of convergence.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, utilizing minimax theory and differential privacy guarantees to derive tight bounds. The use of information-theoretic arguments and KL-divergence bounds are well-executed.
*   **Sharp Results:** The convergence rates derived are remarkably sharp, demonstrating a clear understanding of the inherent trade-offs.  The differing rate for density estimation is a particularly insightful result.
*   **Practical Implications:** The demonstration of optimality for Warner's randomized response is valuable and reinforces the efficacy of this classic technique. The authors also provide efficiently implementable algorithms for density estimation.
*   **Clear Connections to Existing Literature:** The paper effectively positions itself within the broader landscape of privacy and statistical inference, citing relevant work and contrasting their findings.
*   **Well-Organized and Mostly Clear Writing:** The paper is structured logically, and the mathematical notation is generally well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density Estimation Specifics:** While the density estimation results are significant, the connection between the β-smoothness assumption and the resulting convergence rate could be clarified further. It's not immediately obvious *why* the privacy constraint alters the rate in that specific way beyond the general information-theoretic argument.
*   **Practical Considerations:** While the algorithms are efficiently implementable in theory, a discussion of the computational cost of these algorithms for large datasets and high-dimensional problems would be helpful. The complexity of the projection step in the histogram estimation could be a bottleneck.
*    **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is heavily theoretical.  While this is acceptable for a NIPS paper focused on fundamental bounds, including a small-scale simulation study demonstrating the impact of privacy on estimation accuracy could strengthen the presentation.
* **Notation Heavy:** The paper contains a lot of notation, making it hard to digest for people not deeply familiar with the field. While comprehensive, some simplifying could make it easier to follow.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound, with claims well-supported by rigorous analysis. It represents a complete and substantial contribution.
*   **Clarity:** Good.  The writing is generally clear, but the density estimation section could benefit from more intuition and explanation. The heavy notation can be daunting.
*   **Originality:** Excellent. The combination of differential privacy and minimax theory to derive sharp bounds for probability estimation is novel and valuable. The differing density estimation rate is a significant finding.
*   **Significance:** Excellent. The results are important for understanding the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical inference.  The insights will likely be useful to researchers and practitioners working in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides fundamental theoretical understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs.
*   Derives sharp, provably optimal bounds for two important statistical problems.
*   Connects disparate areas of research (differential privacy, minimax theory, statistical estimation).
*   Offers practical insights into the design of privacy-preserving estimators.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The highly theoretical nature might not appeal to all NIPS attendees.
*   Limited empirical validation.
*   Density estimation section could benefit from clearer explanation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.  The theoretical results are rigorous and insightful, and the connection to practical problems (like surveys) is valuable. While a few minor clarifications and the inclusion of empirical results would further strengthen the work, I believe it is well-deserving of acceptance to NIPS.

**Score:** 8.5/10 (Strong Accept)
