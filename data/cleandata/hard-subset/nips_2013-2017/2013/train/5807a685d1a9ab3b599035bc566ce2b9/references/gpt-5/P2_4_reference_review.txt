Summary
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). Individuals privatize their own data through a memoryless channel Q satisfying α-local DP, and the statistician only observes privatized samples Zi. The authors bring minimax decision-theoretic tools to this setting and derive sharp rates:

- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the minimax risks under ℓ2-squared and ℓ1 losses scale as d/(nα2) and d/√(nα2), respectively (up to constants), showing an effective sample size reduction from n to nα2/d for small α.
- Density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1]: The optimal L2 risk degrades from the classical n−2β/(2β+1) to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), i.e., privacy changes the polynomial exponent. Matching upper bounds are achieved by practical mechanisms: (i) a privatized histogram for β=1 and (ii) an orthogonal series estimator with a carefully designed locally private channel for β≥1.

Methodologically, the paper leverages information-theoretic lower bounds adapted to LDP—uniform KL and mutual information bounds (stated from the authors’ prior work)—to drive Le Cam/Fano reductions, and then constructs simple, efficient mechanisms (randomized response variants and a bounded, sign-based privatization for series coefficients) to match the lower bounds.

Relation to prior work
The work connects Warner’s randomized response to modern LDP and shows its optimality for multinomials. It complements differential privacy literature (central DP and LDP) by giving population minimax rates rather than sample-query accuracy. It also links to classical nonparametric minimax theory (e.g., Tsybakov) and deconvolution limitations (Carroll–Hall) to motivate why naive Laplace perturbation of raw data is suboptimal for density estimation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds follow a clear recipe: construct well-separated packings, control divergences via LDP-specific KL and MI inequalities, and invoke Fano/Le Cam. The achievability constructions are explicit and computationally efficient. The density estimation mechanism is nontrivial and convincingly argued to be optimal. One minor concern: Equation (8) appears to have a possible typo in the lower bound term min{1, sqrt(nα2), d/(nα2)} for ℓ2-squared risk; the sqrt(nα2) term is dimensionally unexpected. Clarification would help.
- Completeness: Many proofs are deferred to the cited technical report [9]. While space is limited, the main ideas are presented, and the paper is internally coherent. Still, a brief proof sketch for the key lower-bound steps specialized to each problem would strengthen self-containment.

Clarity
The paper is well-written and organized. Definitions (LDP, minimax risk, Sobolev classes) and the lower-bound machinery are presented crisply. The mechanisms are described concretely, with implementation notes (e.g., simplex projection). Notational consistency is mostly good; a careful pass could fix small notational glitches (e.g., the potential typo in (8), and clearer specification of the constant ck and how B is set in the orthogonal-series mechanism). A short intuitive explanation of the exponent change in density estimation (why privacy “adds one” to the effective smoothness denominator) would improve accessibility.

Originality
The main conceptual novelty is establishing sharp minimax rates under LDP for multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, including the qualitative change in nonparametric exponents and optimality of simple mechanisms. The mutual information variational bound for LDP (stated from [9]) is a useful contribution enabling these results. The combination of lower-bound techniques with carefully tailored local channels for optimality is original and impactful. The overlap with the authors’ tech report [9] should be clarified: which results are first appearing here versus restated? If [9] contains full proofs and some statements verbatim, an explicit delineation would help the program committee assess novelty.

Significance
The results provide fundamental, broadly relevant guidance on the privacy-utility tradeoff in the stringent LDP model. Showing that randomized response is optimal for multinomials is both practically and pedagogically valuable. The change in nonparametric rates under LDP is a landmark finding likely to influence subsequent theoretical and algorithmic work. The mechanisms are implementable and demonstrate that optimality sometimes requires nontrivial privatization (beyond adding Laplace noise to raw data).

Pros
- Sharp, matching lower and upper bounds in both finite- and infinite-dimensional problems under LDP.
- Clear articulation of effective sample size loss (n → nα2/d) and exponent degradation in density estimation.
- Elegant, simple, and efficient mechanisms (randomized response; privatized histograms; bounded-series privatization).
- Bridges DP and minimax theory with reusable information bounds.

Cons
- Significant reliance on an external technical report for proofs; limited proof sketches in the main text.
- Scope restricted to non-interactive (memoryless) channels; no discussion of whether interaction can improve rates.
- No empirical demonstrations; while not necessary, small simulations could illustrate finite-sample behavior and constants.
- Practical adaptivity: the orthogonal series estimator assumes known β; data-driven model selection under LDP is not discussed.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Clarify the novelty relative to [9]: which theorems and proofs are new here?
- Fix or explain the lower-bound expression in (8).
- Discuss adaptivity to unknown β (e.g., privatized Lepski or cross-validation) and whether optimal rates are achievable adaptively under LDP.
- Comment on potential gains from interactive channels vs. the non-interactive setting studied.
- Provide a brief intuition for the exponent shift in density estimation and the role of deconvolution hardness under LDP.

Recommendation
Strong accept. This paper makes foundational, technically sharp contributions to learning under local differential privacy, with clear mechanisms and broad impact despite minor presentation and scope limitations.

Confidence
High.
