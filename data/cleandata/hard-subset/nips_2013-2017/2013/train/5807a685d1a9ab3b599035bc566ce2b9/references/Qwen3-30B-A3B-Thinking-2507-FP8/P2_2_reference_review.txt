

# Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation problems, specifically using the framework of local differential privacy. The authors establish sharp minimax convergence rates for two key problems: multinomial estimation (probability estimation in discrete distributions) and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation with d categories, they show the effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response mechanism [23] is minimax optimal. For density estimation of β-smooth densities, they prove the minimax rate degrades from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (α²n)^(-2β/(2β+2)), which is different from the classical rate. The paper complements these lower bounds with efficient algorithms that achieve the optimal rates, making the results practically relevant.

This work builds upon the foundational local privacy framework of Evfimievski et al. [13] and the minimax decision theory framework, while connecting Warner's classic 1965 work on randomized response to modern differential privacy. It provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the cost of privacy in statistical estimation that goes beyond the standard "privacy loss" metrics.

## Strengths

**Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper excels in technical quality. The authors provide rigorous proofs for both lower and upper bounds, with careful attention to the details of the minimax framework under privacy constraints. The use of information-theoretic tools (Fano's inequality, Le Cam's method) to establish lower bounds is particularly elegant. The paper clearly demonstrates the sharpness of their bounds by providing matching upper bounds with efficient algorithms.

**Novelty and Contribution:** The paper makes several important contributions. First, it formalizes the trade-off between privacy and statistical utility in a minimax framework, which is a significant theoretical advance over previous work that focused on specific estimation problems or privacy mechanisms. Second, the finding that Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation is both surprising and important, as it shows that optimal privacy-preserving strategies for certain problems have been known for decades. Third, the density estimation results reveal a fundamental difference in the rate of convergence under local privacy compared to the non-private case, which is not merely a multiplicative factor but a different polynomial rate.

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written for a theoretical paper. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the main results are clearly stated with precise bounds. The authors effectively build up the necessary background before presenting their main results, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with statistical theory and differential privacy.

**Practical Relevance:** The paper doesn't just provide theoretical results—it demonstrates that efficient algorithms exist that achieve the optimal rates, making the results practically relevant. The authors show that Warner's randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation, which is both simple and explainable, making it a strong candidate for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

The paper is exceptionally strong theoretically, and I find few weaknesses that would significantly impact its contribution. The most minor limitation is that the results are focused on specific estimation problems (multinomial and density estimation), so the framework doesn't directly address other statistical problems like regression or classification. However, this is a common limitation in foundational theoretical papers, and the authors explicitly state that their framework could be extended to other problems.

The paper doesn't explore the practical implementation challenges of the proposed mechanisms in high-dimensional settings or with non-i.i.d. data. However, this is not a major shortcoming for a theoretical paper at NIPS, which is primarily concerned with establishing fundamental limits and optimal algorithms.

## Significance

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. It provides a formal framework for understanding the trade-off between privacy and statistical utility, which is crucial for designing privacy-preserving data collection and analysis systems. The results show that for some problems (like multinomial estimation), the cost of privacy is a manageable reduction in effective sample size, while for others (like density estimation), the cost is a more severe degradation in convergence rate.

The paper's findings have direct implications for practitioners: it demonstrates that Warner's randomized response, which has been known for 50 years, is in fact optimal for certain privacy-preserving estimation problems. This provides theoretical justification for using this simple mechanism rather than more complex ones that may not be optimal.

## Recommendation

This paper is an outstanding theoretical contribution that meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. It is technically sound, well-written, and makes significant advances in understanding the fundamental limits of statistical estimation under privacy constraints. The results are novel, important, and well-supported by rigorous analysis. The paper clearly demonstrates how to bridge classical statistical decision theory with modern privacy frameworks, providing a foundation for future work in privacy-preserving statistics.

I believe this paper should be accepted at NIPS. The theoretical results are sharp, the proofs are rigorous, and the implications for both theory and practice are substantial. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on differential privacy and statistical inference, and it advances the field in a demonstrable way.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro (Accept):**
- Provides sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, which is a fundamental question in privacy-preserving statistics
- Demonstrates that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation, resolving a long-standing question
- Establishes a new framework for understanding the trade-off between privacy and statistical utility
- Provides efficient algorithms that achieve the optimal rates
- Well-written and rigorous mathematical presentation

**Con (Reject):**
- Limited to specific estimation problems (multinomial and density estimation) rather than general statistical problems
- Does not address practical implementation challenges in real-world systems
- (Minor) Some readers might find the density estimation results less intuitive than the multinomial results

However, the "pro" arguments significantly outweigh the "con" arguments for this theoretical paper. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to a core area of statistical privacy that will be of interest to the NIPS community. The results have both theoretical importance and practical relevance, making them well-suited for publication at NIPS.
