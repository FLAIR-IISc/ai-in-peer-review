

# Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary
This paper bridges minimax decision theory with differential privacy to establish sharp convergence rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that local differential privacy fundamentally alters estimation rates: for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, while for density estimation with β smoothness, the convergence rate changes from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (α²n)^(-2β/(2β+2)). The paper demonstrates that Warner's randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation and provides efficient algorithms achieving these sharp rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems.

## Quality
The paper exhibits exceptional technical quality. The authors rigorously establish minimax lower bounds using information-theoretic tools (KL divergence, mutual information) and complement them with matching upper bounds via efficiently computable algorithms. The proofs are sound, with careful attention to constants and dependencies on α and problem parameters. The paper makes a clear distinction between their privacy setting (local differential privacy) and previous work on central differential privacy, which is important for establishing the novelty of their contribution. The theoretical framework is well-motivated, connecting Warner's classical work with modern differential privacy and minimax theory.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem, relates it to historical work (Warner's randomized response), and states the contributions. The notation is consistent and well-defined, and the paper provides sufficient background on both differential privacy and minimax theory. The main theorems are clearly stated with precise conditions, and the connection between the theoretical results and practical implications (e.g., "effective sample size") is well-explained. The proofs follow a logical structure that makes the arguments accessible to readers familiar with statistical theory and information theory.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by formally connecting minimax decision theory with differential privacy, a connection that has not been previously established in this rigorous way. The specific results on how privacy constraints alter estimation rates are novel: the paper demonstrates that local privacy changes the fundamental convergence rate in density estimation, not just the constant factor. The finding that Warner's randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation is both theoretically interesting and historically significant. The paper carefully situates its work within the literature, citing relevant NIPS and related work (e.g., Dwork et al. on differential privacy, Tsybakov on minimax estimation).

## Significance
The results provide fundamental insights into the trade-off between privacy and statistical utility. The paper establishes the exact price of privacy in terms of convergence rates for two fundamental statistical problems. This is highly significant for both theoretical and practical applications: it provides a quantitative foundation for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures, showing precisely how much statistical efficiency is lost due to privacy constraints. The results will inform practitioners about the limitations of privacy-preserving estimation and guide the development of better privacy mechanisms. The work bridges two important fields, creating a new theoretical framework for understanding privacy in statistical estimation.

## Strengths
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with sharp lower and upper bounds
- Clear connection between historical work (Warner) and modern privacy theory
- Practical implications for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures
- Well-motivated problem formulation and careful attention to privacy definitions
- Efficient algorithms that achieve the theoretical bounds

## Weaknesses
The paper is purely theoretical, which is appropriate for its contribution, but it could have included a brief discussion of implementation considerations for the proposed mechanisms. The focus on local differential privacy (as opposed to central differential privacy) is well-justified in the context, but the paper could have more explicitly addressed why local privacy is the appropriate setting for their problem rather than central privacy.

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution at the intersection of statistics and privacy. It establishes sharp rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy, providing both fundamental theoretical insights and practical guidance for privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in the field. It advances the state of the art in understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility.

I recommend **acceptance** at NIPS. The paper is a clear example of high-quality theoretical work that will be valuable to both the privacy and statistics communities. The results provide a foundation for future work on privacy-preserving statistical estimation and will likely be cited as a key reference in this growing field.

The paper's contributions are sufficiently distinct from prior NIPS work (e.g., Dwork et al. on differential privacy, Chaudhuri et al. on private empirical risk minimization) to warrant publication. The theoretical results are novel, the analysis is rigorous, and the paper provides significant insights into the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility.
