This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building upon the framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner’s randomized response procedure and provide sharp minimax convergence rates for multinomial and density estimation under local privacy constraints. While the core ideas are strong and the technical analysis appears rigorous (based on references to their prior work [9]), the paper suffers from a plethora of minor flaws – inconsistencies in notation, a sometimes-convoluted writing style, and an overreliance on referencing rather than including detailed proofs – that ultimately detract from its overall impact and make it difficult to fully assess the contribution.

**Summary of Main Ideas:** The paper establishes formal minimax lower bounds for estimation accuracy when data is collected under local differential privacy. For multinomial estimation, the effective sample size is reduced by a factor of α²/d, while for density estimation, the convergence rate differs from the classical minimax rate. The authors demonstrate that Warner’s randomized response achieves optimal sample complexity and provide efficiently implementable algorithms for both problems, achieving the derived bounds. The central contribution is a quantification of the privacy-utility trade-off.

**Strengths:**
* **Important Problem:** The trade-off between privacy and utility is a critical concern in modern data analysis, and this paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding it.
* **Sharp Bounds:** The derived minimax rates are tight, demonstrating a clear understanding of the fundamental limits of private estimation.
* **Connections to Classical Results:** The paper effectively connects modern differential privacy concepts with classic statistical decision theory.
* **Practical Implications:** The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response and the proposed algorithms are practically relevant.

**Weaknesses:**
* **Notation:** The notation is inconsistent and occasionally confusing. For example, the definition of ‘an . bn’ is unusual and not immediately clear. The use of both ‘α’ for the privacy parameter and as an exponent within formulas creates ambiguity.
* **Writing Clarity:** The writing is frequently dense and could be significantly improved with more accessible explanations.  Phrases like "the likelihood ratio also controls the error rate in tests for the presence of points x in the data [24]" feel disconnected and lack context.  Many assertions are followed by references rather than explanations.
* **Missing Details:** The paper relies heavily on referencing to prior work ([9] in particular) for key proofs and technical details. While referencing is appropriate, the lack of self-contained proofs makes it difficult to verify the correctness and originality of the results. Specifically, the proofs of Propositions 1 and 2, crucial for the main results, are relegated to [9], hindering a complete evaluation.
* **Redundancy:** Some of the background material (e.g., the definition of KL divergence and total variation distance) is standard and could be presented more concisely.
* **Minor Errors:** Several minor typographical errors and inconsistencies were observed throughout the text, indicating a lack of careful proofreading (e.g., “sup P∈σ(X ) |P (S)−Q(S)|” should likely be “sup_{S∈σ(X)} |P(S)−Q(S)|").
* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge the limitations of perturbing samples directly for density estimation, the discussion feels somewhat brief and could be expanded.
* **Formatting:** The formatting is occasionally inconsistent (e.g., spacing around equations).

**Specific Comments:**
* Page 1, line 16: “The literature has stopped short, however…” - This is unnecessarily verbose.
* Page 3, equation (1): The meaning of the σ-field σ(Z) is unclear without more explanation.
* Page 4: The use of “e.g.” is excessive.
* Page 6: The comparison to prior work on differential privacy is somewhat superficial. A more detailed discussion of the differences and relationships would be helpful.
* Page 8: The use of cℓ and cu to denote lower and upper bounds respectively is not standard and could be confusing.
* Page 10, Proposition 2: The claim that the proposed estimator achieves the optimal rate needs to be substantiated more convincingly with details of the convergence analysis (currently relegated to the longer paper).

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**
* The paper addresses a fundamentally important and timely problem.
* The theoretical results are sharp and provide valuable insights into the privacy-utility trade-off.
* The work builds upon and extends existing literature in both differential privacy and statistical decision theory.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**
* The numerous minor flaws – inconsistencies in notation, writing clarity, lack of detailed proofs, and formatting issues – detract from the overall quality of the paper.
* The heavy reliance on referencing to prior work hinders a complete evaluation of the contribution.
* The paper, despite its strengths, feels somewhat incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS publication.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core ideas of this paper are valuable, the numerous shortcomings prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper’s potential impact is diminished by the lack of clarity and self-containedness. A major revision, addressing the issues outlined above – particularly by including more details of the proofs and improving the writing – would be necessary for reconsideration. Currently, I recommend **rejection**.  A score of 5/10 feels appropriate given the conceptual strength coupled with the execution's deficiencies.
