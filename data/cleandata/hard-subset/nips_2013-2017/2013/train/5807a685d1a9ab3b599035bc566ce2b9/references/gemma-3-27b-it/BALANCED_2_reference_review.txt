This paper investigates fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building on the concepts of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner's original randomized response framework and provide tight bounds on the convergence rates for estimating multinomial probabilities and nonparametric densities under local privacy constraints. This is a well-motivated and technically strong contribution that bridges theoretical computer science, statistics, and privacy. The paper is particularly valuable for providing concrete, quantifiable trade-offs, rather than merely establishing the feasibility of privacy-preserving inference.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the problem setting is clearly defined and motivated, building upon a rich history of work on privacy and statistical disclosure limitation. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, citing relevant papers from diverse fields. Second, the technical analysis appears rigorous and well-executed. The derivation of minimax lower and upper bounds, along with the establishment of optimality for Warner’s randomized response mechanism, are convincing. The use of Lemma 1 and Propositions 1 and 2 from their earlier work [9] is effective in streamlining the presentation. The clear separation of results for multinomial estimation and density estimation allows for a focused analysis in each case. Third, the achievability results are strong. Demonstrating that the proposed estimators (randomized response for multinomials, and orthogonal series estimators for densities) *attain* the derived bounds is crucial for practical relevance. The discussion of the limitations of Laplace perturbation for density estimation is insightful. Finally, the writing is generally clear and concise, and the notation is consistent throughout.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. While the writing is generally clear, the level of technical detail in some sections (particularly those presenting the lower bounds) might be daunting for readers not deeply familiar with minimax theory. A more intuitive explanation of the key ideas behind the lower bound derivation could broaden the paper’s appeal. For example, explaining *why* the privacy constraint necessitates a reduction in effective sample size would be helpful. Second, the connection between the theoretical results and practical implications could be further strengthened. While the authors mention the use of randomized response in surveys, a more thorough discussion of how these theoretical results inform the design of privacy-preserving statistical systems in real-world scenarios would be valuable. Specifically, the effect of the α parameter in different settings and the considerations for choosing appropriate values of α are not discussed. Third, the discussion of the limitations of the perturbation approach for density estimation feels somewhat brief. A more detailed explanation of *why* this approach fails to achieve optimality would be appreciated. Fourth, the paper assumes a known orthonormal basis for the density estimation problem. While this is a standard assumption in nonparametric statistics, the sensitivity of the results to the choice of basis could be briefly discussed.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Technical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous and novel theoretical results on minimax rates under local privacy.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem is well-motivated and clearly defined, bridging multiple research areas.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The analysis of Warner’s randomized response and the development of optimal estimators have practical implications for privacy-preserving data analysis.
*   **Tight Bounds:** Establishing both lower and upper bounds demonstrates a deep understanding of the trade-offs involved.
*   **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, facilitating comprehension.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   **Technical Complexity:** The level of technical detail may be challenging for some readers.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** The connection to real-world applications could be further strengthened.
*   **Brief Treatment of Perturbation Limitations:** The discussion on the limitations of Laplace perturbation could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The technical analysis is rigorous, the results are insightful, and the paper is well-written. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above. Specifically, I would suggest adding some intuitive explanations to the lower bound derivation and expanding the discussion of practical implications. The paper is suitable for NIPS due to its theoretical rigor, novelty, and potential impact on the field.
