

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, bridging Warner's classic randomized response work with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors characterize the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical utility, demonstrating that local privacy imposes a substantial penalty on estimation rates.

The main novelty lies in the rigorous characterization of minimax convergence rates under local privacy. For multinomial estimation, they show that the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d (Theorem 1), proving Warner's randomized response is optimal (a 50-year-old technique that has been overlooked in modern privacy research). For density estimation, they demonstrate a fundamental change in convergence rates from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to n^(-2β/(2β+2)) (Theorem 2), which is a substantial theoretical insight. The paper complements these lower bounds with efficiently computable algorithms that achieve the optimal rates (Propositions 2 and 3), providing a complete minimax theory for these problems.

The paper stands out for its clear theoretical framework that connects two important but previously disconnected areas: the classical survey sampling literature (Warner [23]) and modern differential privacy (Dwork et al. [11]). The authors demonstrate remarkable technical skill in deriving sharp bounds using information-theoretic tools, and they successfully address a fundamental question about the cost of privacy in statistical estimation.

Strengths:
- Establishes sharp minimax rates for two fundamental statistical problems under local privacy
- Demonstrates Warner's randomized response is optimal, connecting historical work with modern theory
- Shows a fundamental change in convergence rates for density estimation under privacy
- Provides efficiently computable algorithms that achieve the theoretical bounds
- Clear and well-organized presentation with rigorous proofs
- Addresses a theoretically important problem with practical implications for privacy-preserving statistics

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges beyond the theoretical guarantees
- Could provide more concrete examples of how these bounds would affect real-world statistical analysis
- The connection to other privacy mechanisms (beyond randomized response) is somewhat limited

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results are not incremental but establish new foundational knowledge about the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The paper's theoretical insights are significant for both the privacy and statistics communities, as they provide a precise quantitative understanding of how privacy constraints affect estimation quality.

The authors successfully address a question that has been lurking in the background of privacy research: what is the fundamental cost of privacy in statistical estimation? Their answer is both elegant and surprising—the penalty for privacy is not merely a multiplicative factor but fundamentally changes the convergence rates for nonparametric problems.

This paper represents a bold theoretical contribution that deserves publication at NIPS. It exemplifies the kind of work that pushes the field forward by establishing fundamental limits and connecting historical work with modern theory. The paper's theoretical depth and novelty significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses.

The significance of this work is substantial—it provides the theoretical foundation for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation, which is crucial for designing privacy-preserving data collection systems. The results will inform both theoretical research and practical applications in privacy-preserving statistics.

In conclusion, this paper makes a major theoretical contribution that is both novel and significant. The sharp minimax rates established here represent a fundamental advance in our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The connection between Warner's classical work and modern differential privacy is particularly elegant and insightful.

I recommend acceptance. This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution that will be widely cited and influential in both the privacy and statistics communities. The authors have successfully established a new direction for understanding the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation.
