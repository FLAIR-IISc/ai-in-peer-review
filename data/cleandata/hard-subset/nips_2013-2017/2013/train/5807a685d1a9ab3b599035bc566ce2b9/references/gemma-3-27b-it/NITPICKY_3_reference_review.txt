This paper attempts to bring together differential privacy, minimax decision theory, and probability estimation. While the core idea of formally analyzing privacy-utility tradeoffs within a minimax framework is valuable, the paper suffers from a relentless focus on minor details and a lack of broader perspective, ultimately leading to a negative assessment despite containing genuinely interesting results.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem, although the historical context feels somewhat lengthy. The authors correctly identify the gap in the literature regarding formal treatment of disclosure risk within a decision-theoretic framework. The notation is generally clear, but the frequent use of “≍” and “.” (for asymptotic equivalence) feels unnecessarily terse for a theoretical paper intended for a broad audience.

My primary concern lies with the relentless pursuit of minor technicalities at the expense of appreciating the larger contributions. For instance, the authors dedicate significant space to detailing Proposition 1 (from their previous work [9]), which, while relevant, feels overly verbose and detracts from the main flow of the paper. The proof sketches and references to [9] are insufficient; crucial details are omitted, making it difficult for a reader unfamiliar with the prior work to fully grasp the arguments.

The theoretical results themselves are interesting. Theorem 1 demonstrates a clear penalty for local privacy in multinomial estimation, quantifying the reduction in effective sample size. The observation that Warner’s randomized response is optimal is a nice historical connection. However, the authors then immediately temper this result by pointing out the limitations, and their insistence on meticulously outlining every possible corner case feels pedantic. The derivation of the convergence rate for density estimation, and the departure from classical minimax rates due to privacy constraints, is a more substantial contribution, but again, the presentation is dense and difficult to follow. 

The exposition regarding histogram estimators (Section 4.2) is overly detailed. While the technical justification is present, the discussion feels unnecessarily bogged down in implementation details. The claim of optimality hinges on Proposition 3, but the explanation of the sampling strategy (19) and its justification (especially the choice of B) are insufficiently clear. The dependence of B on k is problematic and requires more careful explanation and justification. Why is this choice optimal? What are the implications of a different choice? These questions are left unanswered. 

Furthermore, several stylistic and formatting issues detract from the paper's readability. The use of multiple notations for asymptotic equivalence (an . bn and an ≍ bn) is confusing. The frequent use of LaTeX commands within sentences (e.g., “σ(Z)”) disrupts the flow. The overall writing style is dry and lacks elegance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important problem at the intersection of privacy and statistical inference.
*   The theoretical results on minimax rates with local privacy are novel and potentially impactful.
*   The connection to Warner’s randomized response is a valuable historical insight.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is overly detailed and bogged down in technical minutiae.
*   The presentation is dense, unclear, and lacks elegance.
*   Proofs are often incomplete, relying heavily on references to a prior work without providing sufficient context.
*   The writing style is dry and unengaging.
*   Numerous stylistic and formatting inconsistencies detract from readability.
*   The authors seem more focused on identifying minor flaws than on highlighting the broader significance of their contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the inherent interest of the topic and the presence of some genuinely novel results, I recommend **rejection**. The paper's excessive focus on detail, unclear presentation, and numerous stylistic issues overshadow its contributions. A major revision, with a significantly streamlined presentation, clearer explanations, and more attention to writing quality, would be required for reconsideration. The paper, as it stands, does not meet the standards of clarity, originality, and significance expected of a NIPS publication. It feels like a technically correct but ultimately uninspiring contribution that prioritizes meticulousness over insight.
