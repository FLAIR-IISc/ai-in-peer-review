Summary
The paper develops a decision-theoretic framework for statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP) and derives sharp minimax rates for two canonical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The authors combine information-theoretic lower bounds (via Le Cam/Fano) with new divergence/mutual information contractions induced by α-LDP channels to quantify the price of privacy. For d-dimensional multinomial estimation, they show the minimax MSE scales as min{1, d/(nα^2)} and ℓ1 risk as min{1, d/√(nα^2)}, implying an “effective sample size” n → nα^2/d for small α. They further show that (a slightly modified) randomized response achieves the lower bounds, as do simple perturb-then-project estimators.

For density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1], the nonprivate rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (nα^2)−2β/(2β+2). They give matching upper bounds via two practical constructions: (i) for β=1, a privatized histogram with Laplace perturbations of bin indicators and projection onto the simplex; (ii) for general β, an orthogonal-series estimator with a carefully designed locally private mechanism that preserves unbiasedness of truncated coefficients while controlling variance, avoiding the suboptimality of naive additive Laplace noise on raw samples (which would incur deconvolution penalties).

Relation to prior work
The work situates classical randomized response (Warner) within the modern LDP framework (Evfimievski et al.; Kasiviswanathan et al.) and connects to differential privacy’s decision-theoretic treatments (Wasserman & Zhou; Smith). The lower bound technique leverages information contraction under LDP channels akin to strong data processing inequalities. The density estimation results deliberately contrast with deconvolution theory (Carroll & Hall), arguing that privatizing the coefficients rather than the raw samples is necessary to attain optimal rates. The paper extends and unifies strands from privacy, information theory, and minimax nonparametrics.

Strengths
- Technical depth and sharpness: The paper gives matching upper and lower bounds with clear dependence on α, n, d, β. The exponent change in density estimation is a nontrivial and important phenomenon.
- Conceptual clarity: It cleanly separates estimation under LDP from previous “sample statistic” accuracy notions, focusing on population minimax risk.
- Mechanism-design insights: Shows that classical randomized response is rate-optimal for multinomials, and that naive additive noise on X is suboptimal for density estimation; provides alternative mechanisms that are simple and computationally efficient (projection-based, histogram, orthogonal series).
- General tools: The mutual information and KL contraction bounds under LDP are broadly usable.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Potential typo in randomized response mechanism (Eq. 10): The text says set [Z]j = −xj with probability 1/(1+eα/2), but Z is stated to be in {0,1}d and later E[Z|x] mixes x and 1−x. This suggests the intended operation is bit-flipping (1−xj), not negation. Please fix and recheck the privacy ratio argument accordingly.
- Dependence on a companion/longer paper for proofs: Several key technical results (e.g., the density lower bound, the constant ck in the orthogonal-series mechanism, the information bounds) are deferred or cited to [9]. While acceptable, a more self-contained appendix with proof sketches and explicit constants would strengthen the submission and aid reproducibility.
- Practical implementability details: The orthogonal-series mechanism samples from halfspaces of {−B,B}k with rejection sampling; bounds on acceptance rates, runtime, and the magnitude/role of ck should be stated explicitly, along with guidance for choosing k and B in practice. Also, the requirement B≥B0√k(eα+1)/(eα−1) can lead to large dynamic range; numerical stability and clipping deserve discussion.
- Adaptivity: Procedures and rates are stated for known β. It would be valuable to discuss adaptivity to unknown smoothness under LDP (e.g., Lepski-type or model-selection approaches) and whether the private mechanisms can support adaptivity without losing the rate.
- Scope limitations: Results are for non-interactive (memoryless) LDP. It would help to discuss whether interaction could improve rates (particularly for density estimation), or why the proved lower bounds already preclude such improvements.
- Empirical validation: Although theory-driven, a small simulation study contrasting naive Laplace-perturbation of X versus the proposed mechanisms, and illustrating the α^2 dependence, would make the contributions more concrete.

Quality
The paper is technically strong and, modulo the noted typo and deferred proofs, appears sound. The lower bounds follow from well-crafted information contractions tailored to LDP, and the upper bounds are constructive with attention to computational feasibility.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with clear statements of settings and results. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., consistently define Z’s support; define ck precisely; ensure all constants and parameter ranges are specified). Bringing at least proof outlines into the main or supplementary material would improve clarity.

Originality
High. The precise minimax characterization under local privacy, especially the altered exponent in density estimation and the optimality of randomized response for multinomials, are significant and nontrivial. The mechanisms for orthogonal-series privatization are novel in this context.

Significance
High. The results will influence how the community reasons about the statistical cost of LDP, guide mechanism design in practice, and provide a foundation for future work on private nonparametrics and distributed/private sensing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct Eq. (10) and re-derive expectations accordingly.
- Include proof sketches for Theorem 2 and the design/analysis of the orthogonal mechanism, with explicit constants and computational guarantees.
- Discuss adaptivity to unknown β and possible interactive LDP extensions or limits.
- Provide brief experiments highlighting the key phenomena (n→nα^2/d effect, exponent change in density estimation).

Recommendation
Accept. This is a theoretically strong and impactful paper that provides sharp, conceptually important results and practical mechanisms for estimation under local differential privacy.
