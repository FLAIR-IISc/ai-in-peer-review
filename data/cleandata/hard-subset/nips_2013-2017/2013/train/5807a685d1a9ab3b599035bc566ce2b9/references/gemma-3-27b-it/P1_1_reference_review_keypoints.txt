- Paper derives minimax lower and upper bounds for locally private probability and density estimation, quantifying the accuracy–privacy trade-off.  
- Shows multinomial estimation effective sample size shrinks with privacy (nα²/d) and density estimation rates change non-trivially under local privacy.  
- Provides computationally feasible algorithms (randomized response, orthogonal series) that achieve the optimal bounds.  
- Connects classical statistical decision theory and randomized response (Warner 1965) to modern differential privacy frameworks.  
- Builds on and sharpens prior work in differential privacy and minimax theory, including the authors’ earlier results.  
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with tight minimax optimality proofs.  
- Novel formal treatment of privacy–utility trade-offs in probability estimation.  
- Sharp, interpretable scaling results (e.g., nα²/d) that illuminate the cost of privacy.  
- Practical algorithms that match theoretical rates, linking theory to applicability.  
- Reaffirms the optimality of Warner’s randomized response in the local privacy setting.  
- Clear and well-presented exposition with standard, well-defined notation.  
- Density estimation focus is narrow—restricted to smoothness classes with bounded derivatives.  
- Lacks detailed computational-complexity analysis, particularly for the orthogonal series estimator and k-selection.  
- Assumes known sample space and channel distributions, which may be restrictive in practice.  
- Limited empirical evaluation to demonstrate algorithm performance and α’s practical impact.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem, provides rigorous novel theory, and offers achievable algorithms.  
- Cons for acceptance: narrower density scope, insufficient computational and empirical detail, and somewhat restrictive assumptions.  
- Overall: strong, significant contribution with minor limitations; recommendation to accept.  
- Reviewer rating: 8/10 (Strong Accept); confidence: 9/10.
