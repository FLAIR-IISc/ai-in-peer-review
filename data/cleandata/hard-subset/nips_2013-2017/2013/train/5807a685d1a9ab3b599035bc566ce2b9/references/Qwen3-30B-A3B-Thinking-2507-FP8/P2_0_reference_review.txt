

# Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors bridge minimax decision theory with differential privacy to provide sharp convergence rates for two core problems: multinomial estimation (e.g., survey response) and nonparametric density estimation. The paper makes significant theoretical contributions by characterizing how local privacy affects estimation rates, showing that for multinomial estimation the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, and for density estimation with β smoothness, the convergence rate changes from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to n^(-2β/(2β+2)).

## Quality

The paper demonstrates exceptional technical quality. The authors rigorously establish both lower and upper bounds for the minimax rates under local privacy constraints, providing matching theoretical guarantees. Their proofs are well-structured, building on established techniques in minimax theory while extending them to the privacy setting. The paper carefully distinguishes between the privacy constraints (local vs. central) and shows that their lower bounds are stronger than those in prior work. For instance, they properly address the distinction between estimating population parameters (θ(P)) versus sample quantities (θ(x₁:ₙ)), which is a critical nuance in the privacy literature. The authors also provide concrete, efficiently computable algorithms (e.g., randomized response for multinomial estimation, histogram and orthogonal series estimators for density estimation) that achieve the derived rates. The paper is a complete theoretical contribution with no apparent gaps in the analysis.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors carefully define all necessary notation and build the theoretical framework incrementally. The connection between Warner's classic randomized response and modern differential privacy is particularly well-explained, making the historical context accessible to modern readers. The main results are presented with precision (Theorems 1 and 2), and the proof sketches for the main theorems are sufficiently detailed without overwhelming the reader. The paper effectively balances mathematical rigor with readability, making it accessible to readers familiar with minimax theory and differential privacy. The section on density estimation, particularly the distinction between histogram and orthogonal series estimators, is especially well-organized.

## Originality

This work makes several significant original contributions. The most notable is the characterization of how local privacy fundamentally changes the minimax rate for density estimation (from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to n^(-2β/(2β+2))), which differs from the classical setting in a non-trivial way. The authors also establish that Warner's randomized response (a method proposed nearly 50 years ago) is actually optimal for multinomial estimation under local privacy, which is a surprising and insightful connection between classical survey methodology and modern privacy theory. The paper builds on Duchi et al. [9] but extends it significantly by providing sharp rates for specific estimation problems rather than just general privacy mechanisms. The work is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers on differential privacy (e.g., [11, 1, 6, 18]) by focusing on minimax rates rather than specific estimation procedures.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides the first sharp characterization of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility for core estimation problems. The results quantify precisely how much statistical efficiency is lost when privacy is imposed, which is crucial for practitioners designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The paper demonstrates that for density estimation, the privacy constraint fundamentally changes the convergence rate, which has important implications for the design of privacy-preserving nonparametric methods. The authors also provide practical algorithms that achieve the optimal rates, bridging the gap between theory and practice. This work provides a theoretical foundation that will inform future research on privacy-preserving statistical methods.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few minor weaknesses. The authors focus exclusively on local privacy (where each data point is privatized independently), which is an important but limited setting compared to central privacy (where a trusted curator applies privacy mechanisms to the entire dataset). While this is justified in the paper's context, a brief discussion of how their results might extend to central privacy would strengthen the paper. Additionally, while the theoretical results are compelling, the paper could more thoroughly discuss the practical implications of the quantitative trade-offs (e.g., how much privacy loss is acceptable in different application domains).

## Conclusion

This is a high-quality, well-written paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The paper provides sharp minimax rates for two fundamental estimation problems under local privacy constraints, establishes the optimality of classical methods like randomized response, and demonstrates a fundamental difference in the density estimation rate compared to the non-private case. The work is original, technically sound, and addresses a problem of significant importance to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Pro Acceptance:** Rigorous theoretical results with sharp bounds, clear connection between historical survey methodology and modern privacy theory, significant theoretical contributions to understanding privacy-utility trade-offs.

**Con Acceptance:** Limited to local privacy setting (central privacy not addressed), minimal discussion of practical implications beyond theoretical rates.
