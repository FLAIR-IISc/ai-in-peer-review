This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building on the framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner’s original problem of estimating population fractions with privacy constraints and extend the analysis to nonparametric density estimation. While the core ideas are valuable and the connection between disparate fields (privacy, statistics, and information theory) is well-motivated, the paper suffers from a meticulous level of detail that, paradoxically, obscures the broader significance of the results and introduces numerous minor flaws.

The introduction effectively motivates the problem and contextualizes it within the existing literature. However, the extensive referencing feels somewhat excessive for an introduction, and the numerous citations (e.g., “[e.g., 23, 14, 10, 15]”) create a choppy reading experience. 

The mathematical formalism is generally sound, and the definitions of local privacy and minimax risk are presented clearly. However, the notation is occasionally inconsistent. For instance, the use of both ‘Zn’ and ‘Zi’ to represent both individual private views and the entire set of private views is confusing.  Moreover, the repetition of definitions (e.g., KL-divergence and total variation distance) in Section 1.1, which are standard and readily known to the NIPS audience, feels unnecessarily verbose.

The core result for multinomial estimation (Theorem 1) is interesting, demonstrating a reduction in effective sample size due to privacy. However, the presentation is somewhat dry and lacks intuitive explanation. The claim regarding Warner's randomized response being optimal is presented without sufficient detail on *how* the analysis confirms optimality – is it simply a matching upper bound, or is there a deeper connection? The discussion of the projection operator in the proof of optimality (11) feels rushed and lacks justification for its use. 

The density estimation section is more problematic. The derivation of the lower bound (Theorem 2) is stated without a clear roadmap for how Proposition 1 is applied. The justification for the claim that Laplace perturbation is suboptimal is insufficient; a citation to Lei [19] is provided, but the connection to the authors' analysis is not clearly articulated. The presentation of Propositions 2 and 3, detailing the achievability results with histogram and orthogonal series estimators respectively, is overly detailed. While mathematical rigor is appreciated, the extensive notation and lengthy calculations obscure the key ideas.  For example, the justification for choosing B = B0 √ k(eα + 1)/ck(e α − 1) in Proposition 3 lacks clarity.  What is the role of the constant ck, and why is this choice of B optimal? 

Several minor issues detract from the overall polish of the paper:

*   Equation (1) lacks context – what is σ(Z)? It’s defined in the preceding text, but a brief reminder would be helpful.
*   The phrase “an . bn” and “an ≍ bn” are non-standard notation and should be replaced with O(bn) and Θ(bn) respectively.
*   The closing acknowledgements are excessive in length for a theoretical paper.
*   Numerous typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies are present throughout the manuscript (e.g., "differently private" instead of "differentially private").

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamentally important problem at the intersection of privacy and statistics.
*   The theoretical framework is sound, and the authors demonstrate a strong command of the relevant tools.
*   The results provide quantitative trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is overly detailed and verbose, obscuring the key ideas and making it difficult to follow.
*   The presentation lacks clarity and intuitive explanation.
*   Numerous minor flaws (notational inconsistencies, typos, grammatical errors) detract from the overall polish of the paper.
*   The originality, while present, feels incremental rather than transformative. The core ideas build heavily on existing work, and the extensions are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
*   The significance of the results is somewhat diminished by the level of detail and lack of broader discussion.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper presents a technically sound analysis, the excessive detail, lack of clarity, and numerous minor flaws outweigh the strengths. The paper feels more like a technical report than a polished NIPS contribution. A significant rewrite, focusing on clarity, conciseness, and intuitive explanation, is needed before it would be suitable for NIPS. The authors should also consider streamlining the notation and addressing the minor flaws mentioned above.
