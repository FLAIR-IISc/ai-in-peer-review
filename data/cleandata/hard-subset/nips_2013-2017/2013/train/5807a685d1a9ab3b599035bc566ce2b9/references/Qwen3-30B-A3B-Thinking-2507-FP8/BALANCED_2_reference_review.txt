

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of differential privacy and statistical estimation theory. The authors bridge Warner's classical survey design work with modern differential privacy, using minimax decision theory to characterize optimal estimation under local privacy constraints. The paper provides sharp minimax rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation under α-local differential privacy.

The main technical contributions are twofold: (1) For multinomial estimation, the paper shows the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. (2) For density estimation with β smoothness, the paper establishes a novel convergence rate of (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2), which differs from the classical n⁻²β/(2β+1) rate. The authors complement these lower bounds with efficiently computable upper bounds using histogram and orthogonal series estimators.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Rigor and Completeness:** The paper provides both sharp lower bounds (using minimax reduction to hypothesis testing and information-theoretic arguments) and matching upper bounds (via explicit algorithms). The theoretical development is thorough, with careful attention to the connections between privacy constraints and statistical efficiency.

2. **Historical Insight:** The paper's demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation under local privacy is particularly noteworthy, connecting 50-year-old survey methodology with modern privacy theory.

3. **Novel Results:** The departure from standard minimax rates for density estimation (changing the exponent from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2)) is a significant theoretical contribution that has not been previously established.

4. **Practical Relevance:** The authors provide efficient algorithms that achieve the theoretical bounds, making their results applicable to real-world privacy-preserving statistical estimation.

5. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions, well-motivated results, and sufficient technical detail for experts to follow the arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Practical Trade-offs:** While the paper establishes sharp rates, it doesn't sufficiently address how these rates translate to practical privacy-utility trade-offs in real-world applications. A discussion of the computational cost of the proposed algorithms (beyond "efficiently computable") would strengthen the practical relevance.

2. **Narrow Scope of Privacy Mechanisms:** The paper focuses exclusively on α-local differential privacy, without exploring how their results might extend to other privacy notions (e.g., ε-differential privacy in the central model) or how their bounds compare to other privacy mechanisms.

3. **Density Estimation for Higher Smoothness:** The orthogonal series estimator for β > 1 is theoretically sound but lacks comparison to other privacy-preserving density estimation methods. A brief discussion of how the proposed method compares to existing approaches would provide better context for the significance of the result.

4. **Limited Discussion of Small α Regime:** The paper focuses on α as a "sufficiently small constant," but doesn't address the practical implications when α is very small (close to 0), which is often the case in real privacy applications.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical estimation. The results are technically sound, novel, and well-motivated, with clear connections to both historical work (Warner's randomized response) and modern privacy research. The minimax rates provide fundamental insights into the trade-off between privacy and statistical utility.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper meets all the key criteria for NeurIPS: it's of high quality, clearly written, original, and significant. The results will be of interest to researchers working at the intersection of privacy, statistics, and machine learning.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Expand the discussion of practical implications, particularly regarding how the theoretical rates translate to real-world applications with limited computational resources.

2. Consider adding a brief comparison to other privacy-preserving density estimation methods to better contextualize the significance of the orthogonal series estimator.

3. Include a short discussion of the behavior when α approaches 0 (e.g., for very strong privacy guarantees), which would be relevant for practitioners.

4. Clarify the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms beyond the statement that they are "efficiently computable."

5. Briefly mention how their results might extend to other privacy models (e.g., central differential privacy) to highlight the broader relevance of their theoretical framework.

Overall, this is a well-executed theoretical paper that makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The results are sharp, the proofs are rigorous, and the connection to historical work is particularly insightful.
