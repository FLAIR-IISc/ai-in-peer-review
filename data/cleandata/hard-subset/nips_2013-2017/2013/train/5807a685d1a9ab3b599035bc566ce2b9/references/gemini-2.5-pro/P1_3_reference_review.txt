### Paper Review

**Summary**

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and privacy. It focuses on the setting of local differential privacy (LDP), where each data point is privatized before collection. The authors use the framework of minimax theory to derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds on the estimation error for two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation.

The main contributions are twofold. First, for estimating a d-dimensional multinomial parameter, the authors show that the minimax squared error rate is `Θ(d / (nα^2))`, demonstrating that LDP effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `nα^2/d`. They prove that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal under this modern privacy definition. Second, for nonparametric density estimation of functions with β derivatives (Sobolev class `Fβ`), they establish a more dramatic cost of privacy. While the classical minimax rate is `n^(-2β/(2β+1))`, the LDP minimax rate is shown to be `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent reveals a fundamental alteration in the problem's statistical difficulty. The authors complement these lower bounds with computationally efficient algorithms (a privatized histogram for β=1 and a novel orthogonal series estimator for general β) that achieve these optimal rates. The paper connects classical ideas in survey sampling (Warner), modern concepts in privacy (Differential Privacy), and core principles of statistical theory (minimax estimation).

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a question of fundamental importance: what is the inherent statistical cost of guaranteeing privacy? By providing sharp, quantitative answers for canonical estimation problems, this work provides a foundational understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off. These results are likely to be influential for both theoreticians studying the limits of private data analysis and practitioners seeking to understand the feasibility of deploying privacy-preserving methods.

2.  **Technical Quality and Completeness:** The theoretical contributions are of very high quality. The authors derive matching upper and lower bounds for the minimax rates, which represents a complete theoretical characterization. The analysis is rigorous, building upon the tools of Le Cam and Fano, and adapting them to the local privacy setting via novel information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1). The discovery that the convergence rate's exponent changes for density estimation is a deep and surprising insight.

3.  **Originality and Novelty:** This work is highly original. While prior work had studied differential privacy, this paper is one of the first to apply the full power of minimax decision theory to the *local* privacy model and to focus on estimating population parameters rather than finite-sample quantities. The result that simple mechanisms like adding Laplace noise to data points are suboptimal for density estimation is a crucial and novel finding, highlighting the need for carefully designed privacy mechanisms tailored to the statistical task. The construction of the optimal orthogonal series estimator is also a novel contribution.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides a clear background on local privacy and the minimax framework before presenting its main results. The motivation is clear, the theorems are stated precisely, and the implications of the results are discussed thoughtfully. The connection drawn between Warner's 50-year-old randomized response and the modern definition of differential privacy is both elegant and insightful.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on External Proofs:** The proofs of the main results (Proposition 1, Theorems 1 and 2) are deferred to a longer manuscript [9]. While this is a common and understandable practice for conference submissions, it prevents a fully self-contained verification of the paper's technical claims. The review's assessment of technical soundness relies on trusting the correctness of the cited work.

2.  **Practicality of the Optimal Mechanism:** The sampling strategy for the orthogonal series estimator (Section 4.3, scheme 19) is quite complex. While theoretically optimal, a more detailed discussion of its practical implementation, computational complexity (e.g., rejection sampling efficiency), and potential numerical stability issues would be beneficial. For instance, the bound `B` grows with `sqrt(k)`, which in turn grows with `n`; the implications of this for the variance of the estimator could be elaborated upon.

**Detailed Comments**

*   The distinction made in Section 2.1 between estimating a population parameter `θ(P)` versus a sample quantity `θ(x1:n)` is important and strengthens the paper's claims. It correctly points out that lower bounds on the former are stronger. A brief sentence explicitly comparing this to a specific result from prior work (e.g., [16, 8]) could make this point even more concrete for the reader.
*   The analysis primarily focuses on the `α ∈ [0, 1]` regime. This is the most relevant setting for strong privacy guarantees. However, the rates are presented with `min{1, ...}` terms, suggesting a saturation effect. A brief comment on the behavior for large `α` (weak privacy) and how the rates smoothly recover the non-private case would be a welcome addition.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and highly relevant problem at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy.
*   Provides strong, novel, and complete theoretical results (sharp minimax rates).
*   The insights (e.g., change in convergence exponent, optimality of randomized response) are significant and likely to inspire follow-up work.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The technical core of the proofs resides in a separate paper, which complicates the review process. (This is a minor point given the norms of the field).

In conclusion, this is an outstanding theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to our understanding of private statistical inference. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is a perfect fit for NIPS and should be accepted.
