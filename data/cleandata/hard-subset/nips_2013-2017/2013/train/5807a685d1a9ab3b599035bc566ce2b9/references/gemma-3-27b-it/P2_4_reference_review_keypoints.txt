- Investigates trade-offs between local differential privacy and statistical efficiency, giving tight minimax bounds for probability estimation.  
- Derives sharp upper and lower bounds for multinomial and nonparametric density estimation under local privacy.  
- Shows local privacy changes the convergence rate for density estimation compared to non-private settings.  
- Proves the optimality of Warner’s randomized response and provides efficient algorithms achieving the bounds.  
- Strong theoretical foundation using Le Cam, Fano, and information bounds.  
- Minimax rates are tight (upper and lower bounds match), giving precise performance characterization.  
- Results are practically relevant for privacy-preserving statistical inference and estimator design.  
- Clear and well-organized exposition despite theoretical density.  
- Novel application of minimax theory to local privacy and discovery of new convergence rates.  
- Paper is highly technical, limiting accessibility to non-experts.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited; more simulations would strengthen the paper.  
- Some assumptions (smoothness, bounded basis functions) are not fully discussed regarding relaxation impact.  
- Scope is narrow, focused on multinomial and density estimation without broader application examples.  
- Quality: technically sound with correct proofs and acknowledged limitations.  
- Clarity: reasonably clear but mathematically dense; could use more intuition.  
- Originality: high, connecting classical randomized response to modern differential privacy via minimax analysis.  
- Significance: important contribution to understanding privacy-accuracy trade-offs with sharp bounds.  
- Pros for acceptance: rigorous analysis, novelty, practical insights on randomized response.  
- Cons for acceptance: high complexity, limited empirical work, narrow application scope.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution; strengths outweigh minor drawbacks.
