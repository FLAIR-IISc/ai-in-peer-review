Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local (non-interactive) differential privacy (LDP). It frames estimation risk via a private minimax criterion and derives sharp rates for two canonical problems: (i) multinomial probability estimation over a d-simplex, and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. The main technical tool is an information-contraction bound under LDP that controls KL divergence and mutual information after privatization, enabling Le Cam/Fano-based lower bounds. For multinomials, the effective sample size degrades from n to roughly nα^2/d (for small α), and the authors show randomized response achieves the upper bound, establishing sharpness. For density estimation with smoothness β, the classical non-private n^{-2β/(2β+1)} rate becomes (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under LDP; the paper gives matching upper bounds via a privatized histogram for β=1 and a privatized orthogonal series estimator for general β≥1. The work connects classical minimax theory (Le Cam, Fano, Yang–Barron, Tsybakov) with LDP (Evfimievski; Dwork et al.; Kasiviswanathan et al.), and builds on the authors’ prior information bounds.

Strengths
- Clear, principled problem formulation: private minimax risk over all α-LDP channels and estimators.
- Sharp theory: matching lower and upper bounds for both multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, with explicit dependence on α, n, d, β.
- Conceptual clarity: makes precise the “effective sample size” reduction under LDP and shows that classical randomized response attains the multinomial rate.
- Methodological soundness: lower bounds via tight information-contraction under LDP and Fano-type arguments; upper bounds via carefully designed privatization mechanisms and projections.
- Significance: provides a rigorous benchmark for what is and isn’t possible under local privacy, a setting of growing practical relevance (surveys, telemetry, distributed data).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Overlap with prior work: The key information bounds and some results are restated from the authors’ earlier work [9]. While the current paper applies them to specific estimation problems and completes matching upper bounds, the novelty is partly incremental relative to the cited arXiv version. Please clarify what is strictly new here (e.g., exact constants/rates, new mechanisms, stronger optimality statements).
- Practicality of mechanisms: 
  - Multinomial upper bound uses a d-dimensional randomized response (coordinate-wise flips). This has high communication and computation overhead compared to standard k-ary randomized response. A discussion proving that a k-ary RR variant achieves the same rate (and perhaps better constants) would strengthen the applicability.
  - The orthogonal-series privatization scheme relies on a sign-based sampling with constants c_k and a bound B that depend on unknown basis bounds and appear only up to O(1). The feasibility and tuning in practice (and sampling efficiency) are not fully addressed.
- Lack of empirical validation: There are no simulations illustrating finite-sample constants, stability of the projection steps, or sensitivity to mis-specification of β and α. While theory papers are welcome, some empirical evidence would help assess the practicality of the proposed mechanisms, especially for the more elaborate density estimator.
- Assumptions and adaptivity:
  - The density estimators require knowing β (and implicitly C) to set k. Adaptivity under LDP (e.g., privately choosing k or cross-validation with privacy budget accounting) is not discussed.
  - The histogram result for β=1 is reassuring, but boundary bias and binning effects under privatization could be discussed.
- Clarity/typos: In Definition 1, the constraint appears as sum j^{2β} φ_j^2 ≤ C^2, which seems to be a typo for sum j^{2β} θ_j^2 ≤ C^2. Please correct. Some constants (e.g., c_ℓ, c_u, c_β, c_k) are introduced without enough guidance on their magnitudes or how they affect practical choices.
- Scope: The paper restricts to pure LDP (δ=0), non-interactive channels. A short discussion of how results might change under (ε,δ)-LDP or interaction would be valuable context.

Quality
Technically solid and careful. The lower bounds follow standard, sound techniques with LDP-specific contraction. Upper bounds are constructive and match rates. However, important proofs are deferred to [9], and some mechanisms’ practical details are under-specified.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with clear motivation and takeaways. Proof sketches for the central theorems would make the paper more self-contained. Fix the noted typo and better explain the sampling scheme in Section 4.3, including computational complexity and parameter choices.

Originality
Moderate to high. The private minimax framing is known, and some pieces appeared in [9]. The contribution here is packaging the framework to deliver sharp rates and matching constructions for multinomial and Sobolev density estimation. The orthogonal-series privatization achieving the optimal exponent under LDP is a notable addition.

Significance
High for the theory of LDP: establishes benchmark rates and shows standard mechanisms (randomized response, histograms, orthogonal series) can be tuned to be minimax-optimal under LDP. This should guide both future theoretical work and principled algorithm design.

Questions for the authors
- Please delineate precisely what is new relative to [9] and other cited works (e.g., Wasserman–Zhou, Lei).
- Can you provide a k-ary randomized response mechanism with the same rate and better communication than the coordinate-wise scheme?
- How sensitive are the density estimators to mis-specified β? Can you propose or discuss a privacy-preserving adaptive selection of k?
- What are the constants hidden in B, c_k, and how do they affect sample sizes needed to see the asymptotic rates?
- Any empirical results (even small-scale) demonstrating finite-sample behavior?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, rigorous, and sharp theoretical results; resolves fundamental questions about LDP minimax rates.
- Conceptually clarifies the price of local privacy across classical problems.
- Methods are rooted in established theory; upper bounds use familiar estimators adapted to LDP.

Cons for acceptance
- Partial overlap with prior work; limited novelty in techniques.
- Practical aspects of proposed mechanisms (especially orthogonal-series privatization) are under-explored; no experiments.
- Some clarity issues and dependence on unspecified constants.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning). This is a solid, principled contribution establishing sharp rates under LDP for important problems. Addressing clarity, practical considerations, and a brief empirical illustration would further strengthen the paper.

Confidence: High.
