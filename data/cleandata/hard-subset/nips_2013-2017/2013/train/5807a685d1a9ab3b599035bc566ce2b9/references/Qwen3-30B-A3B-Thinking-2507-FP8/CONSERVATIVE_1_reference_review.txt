

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints using minimax decision theory. The authors provide theoretical bounds for multinomial and density estimation under local privacy, showing that local privacy imposes a penalty on statistical efficiency. For multinomial estimation, they show the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, and for density estimation with β smoothness, the convergence rate changes from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2). They demonstrate that Warner's randomized response mechanism achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation and provide efficient algorithms for density estimation that match their lower bounds.

**Strengths:**
The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of minimax rates. The connection between classical minimax theory and differential privacy is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The paper provides a thorough theoretical treatment of privacy-constrained estimation, establishing sharp rates for two important statistical problems. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient mathematical detail for experts to follow the arguments.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the strong theoretical contributions, the paper suffers from significant limitations that undermine its impact. Most critically, it provides no empirical validation of the proposed algorithms. The authors claim their methods achieve optimal rates but offer no experimental results demonstrating this in practice. For a paper claiming to establish "sharp" rates, the absence of empirical evidence is a major shortcoming, especially in a field like machine learning where theoretical guarantees should be complemented with practical validation.

The paper's novelty claim is questionable. The authors demonstrate that Warner's randomized response (proposed in 1965) achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation. While the theoretical characterization is novel, the mechanism itself is not new, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its theoretical contribution from prior work on privacy and minimax rates. The connection to Warner's work is interesting, but the "new" aspect is the formal minimax characterization, not the mechanism itself.

The paper makes strong claims about the necessity of "non-trivial sampling strategies" but doesn't provide comparative analysis with existing privacy-preserving methods. The discussion of why Laplace perturbation fails is theoretically sound but lacks empirical support. Without experimental results showing how the proposed methods compare to established privacy mechanisms in practice, the paper fails to demonstrate the practical significance of its theoretical results.

**Quality Assessment:**
While the theoretical framework is sound and the proofs appear rigorous, the paper lacks the empirical validation expected in modern machine learning research. The absence of experiments to validate the theoretical claims against established baselines significantly weakens the contribution. The paper would be stronger with empirical evidence showing how the proposed mechanisms perform relative to existing privacy-preserving methods.

**Originality and Significance:**
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the intersection of privacy and minimax estimation. However, the significance is limited by the lack of practical validation and the fact that the optimal mechanism (Warner's randomized response) is not new. The main contribution is the precise characterization of the rates under local privacy, but this doesn't translate to practical improvements without empirical validation.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the theoretical work is sound, the lack of empirical validation is a critical weakness for a NeurIPS paper. The field of privacy-preserving machine learning increasingly demands both theoretical and empirical validation. Without demonstrating that the proposed methods achieve the theoretical rates in practice, the contribution lacks the necessary impact for NeurIPS. The paper would benefit from extensive empirical comparisons with existing privacy mechanisms before being considered for publication.

**Suggested Improvements:**
1. Add comprehensive empirical results comparing the proposed algorithms with existing privacy-preserving methods
2. Provide a more detailed comparison with prior work on privacy and minimax rates
3. Discuss practical implementation challenges and limitations of the proposed methods
4. Consider whether the theoretical results actually translate to meaningful improvements in practical privacy settings

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution, but its lack of empirical validation and limited practical significance prevent it from meeting NeurIPS standards for publication.
