### Summary

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors apply minimax decision theory to derive sharp convergence rates for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation over `d` categories, they show that `α`-local privacy effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `nα²/d`. A key consequence is that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal. For density estimation over a Sobolev class with smoothness `β`, the authors reveal a more dramatic effect: the minimax rate degrades from the classical `n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹)` to `(nα²)⁻²β/(²β⁺²)`, changing the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate. The paper complements these information-theoretic lower bounds with analyses of computationally efficient algorithms (modified randomized response, private histograms, and a private orthogonal series estimator) that achieve these optimal rates. This work bridges the gap between the classical privacy literature initiated by Warner and the modern framework of differential privacy, providing a rigorous, quantitative understanding of the cost of privacy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality

The paper is theoretically ambitious and appears to tackle a problem of fundamental importance. The primary results, particularly the change in the convergence rate for density estimation, are striking and significant. The approach of using information-theoretic tools from minimax theory to bound the performance of any privacy-preserving algorithm is powerful and well-founded.

However, the manuscript suffers from a number of quality issues that undermine its technical soundness as a standalone piece of work.

1.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** This is the most critical flaw. All of the paper's lower bounds hinge on Proposition 1, which provides bounds on the KL-divergence and mutual information. The proofs for this proposition, as well as for the main Theorems 1 and 2, are deferred to an external arXiv paper [9]. A conference paper must be reviewable on its own merits. Without the core proofs, a reviewer cannot verify the correctness of the central claims. This transforms the submission from a complete scientific paper into an extended abstract or an announcement of results, which is not appropriate for NIPS.

2.  **Methodological Ambiguity:** The descriptions of the achievable mechanisms contain several gaps and imprecisions.
    *   In Section 3.2, the randomized response mechanism is confusingly specified. It states `Z ∈ {0, 1}d`, but the sampling procedure involves `xj` and `-xj`. If `x` is a standard basis vector `ej`, then `xj` is 0 or 1, but `-xj` could be -1, which contradicts the stated range of `Z`. This must be clarified.
    *   The variance bound `E[‖Z − E[Z]‖²₂] < d/4+1` in the same section appears without any derivation. While likely correct, such assertions should be justified.
    *   The sampling strategy for the orthogonal series estimator in Section 4.3 is complex. The claim that it is "efficiently computable (for example by rejection sampling)" is made without any analysis of the acceptance rate or computational complexity.
    *   Crucially, the analysis of this estimator relies on a constant `ck` in equation (20) which "may depend on k but is O(1)". This constant is never defined or analyzed, yet the choice of the parameter `B` and the entire subsequent rate analysis depends on it. This is a significant omission that makes the upper bound analysis unverifiable.

#### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent context, and the problem setup is clear. However, the clarity is marred by notational and presentational sloppiness.

1.  **Non-standard Notation:** The notation `an . bn` to denote `an = O(bn)` is highly non-standard and potentially confusing. This is used in the final line of Section 3.2, for instance. Standard big-O notation should be used consistently.
2.  **Formatting Errors:** The bibliography is poorly formatted, with numerous stray commas (e.g., in the Barak et al. and Kasiviswanathan et al. citations). This suggests a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
3.  **Vague Claims:** Statements like "the constant `ck` ... is O(1)" without further explanation detract from the paper's clarity and rigor.

#### Originality

The combination of local differential privacy and minimax theory to derive sharp rates is a novel and powerful research direction. While prior work has studied differential privacy and statistical estimation, this paper is among the first to provide a complete characterization of the minimax rates for these specific and fundamental problems in the *local* privacy model. The discovery that Warner's mechanism is optimal is a beautiful result connecting old and new ideas.

However, the originality of this specific manuscript is somewhat diluted by its heavy reliance on the companion paper [9]. The core technical tools (Proposition 1) are not original to this paper, which positions it more as an application of a previously developed theory rather than a self-contained work with its own foundational contributions.

#### Significance

The results are highly significant. Quantifying the precise statistical cost of local privacy is crucial for practitioners and theorists. It allows for an informed decision on the level of privacy to employ based on the desired statistical accuracy and available sample size. The results for multinomials are directly applicable to survey design, while the density estimation results have broad implications for nonparametric inference on private data. The finding that simple perturbation is suboptimal for density estimation provides important guidance for designing better private mechanisms. This work lays a strong theoretical foundation that future research will likely build upon.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem at the intersection of privacy, statistics, and machine learning.
*   The main theoretical results are novel, sharp, and insightful, particularly the characterization of how the convergence rate's exponent changes for density estimation.
*   The connection drawn to Warner's classical work is elegant and provides a satisfying sense of closure.

**Arguments against:**
1.  **Not self-contained:** The paper is fundamentally un-reviewable as its core proofs are located in a separate manuscript. This is a violation of standard peer review practice.
2.  **Methodological gaps:** The descriptions of the algorithms that achieve the upper bounds contain ambiguities, unjustified steps, and undefined constants, making the claims difficult to verify.
3.  **Lack of polish:** The manuscript suffers from numerous minor issues, including non-standard notation and significant formatting errors in the bibliography, suggesting a rushed preparation.

### Recommendation

**Reject.**

While the ideas presented in this paper are of high quality and significance, the manuscript itself is not ready for publication at a top-tier conference like NIPS. The lack of self-containment due to the offloading of all proofs to a separate paper is a disqualifying flaw. A reviewer cannot be expected to read and vet an entirely different manuscript to assess the correctness of the one under review. Furthermore, the accumulation of methodological ambiguities, undefined constants, and general sloppiness in notation and formatting falls short of the high standard expected. The paper presents a promising research direction, but it requires a major revision to include its proofs, clarify its methods, and polish its presentation before it can be fairly evaluated.
