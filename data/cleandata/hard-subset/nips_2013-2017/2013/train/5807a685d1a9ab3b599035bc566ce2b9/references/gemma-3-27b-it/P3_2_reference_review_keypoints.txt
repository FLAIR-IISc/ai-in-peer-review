- Paper studies trade-offs between local differential privacy and statistical efficiency, giving minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation and showing achievable algorithms.  
- Shows local privacy imposes a sample-size penalty for multinomial estimation and changes convergence rates for density estimation.  
- Demonstrates that Warner’s randomized response is optimal in this framework.  
- Connects classical randomized-response surveys (Warner) with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory.  
- Positions work as stronger than prior DP literature by focusing on population estimation rates; builds on the authors’ prior divergence bounds.  
- References are comprehensive, spanning cryptography, statistics, and ML.  
- Theoretical framework is rigorous, providing matching lower and upper minimax bounds.  
- Results are sharp, establishing optimality of proposed mechanisms.  
- Combination of local DP with minimax estimation is novel and significant.  
- Proposed algorithms (histograms, orthogonal series) are computationally efficient and practically implementable.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and uses standard notation; introduction motivates the problem effectively.  
- Density estimation analysis is limited to Sobolev smoothness, which may restrict applicability to other smoothness classes.  
- More detailed computational-complexity analysis is needed, especially for the orthogonal series estimator (scaling with n and α).  
- Lacks empirical validation or simulations to confirm practical performance and theoretical rates.  
- Minor unconventional notation ("an . bn" for "an = O(bn)") should be replaced with standard asymptotic notation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, sharp bounds, strong connections to literature, and the notable optimality of Warner’s method.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation and narrow focus on Sobolev spaces for density estimation.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address complexity and smoothness-class coverage.  
- Score: 8/10 (Strong Accept).
