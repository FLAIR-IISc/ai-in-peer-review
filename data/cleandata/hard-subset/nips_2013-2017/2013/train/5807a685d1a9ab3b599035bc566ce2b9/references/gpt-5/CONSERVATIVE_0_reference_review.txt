Summary
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It frames population risk via a private minimax criterion and derives sharp rates and matching algorithms for two canonical problems:
- Multinomial estimation (θ ∈ Δd): Establishes lower and upper bounds showing MSE scales as Θ(d/(nα^2)) (and ℓ1-error as Θ(d/√(nα^2))) when α is a small constant. This corresponds to an “effective sample size” nα^2/d. A simple variant of randomized response and a Laplace-perturbation approach attain the rate, implying Warner’s procedure is minimax optimal up to constants.
- Density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1]: Shows an intrinsic rate change under LDP, from the classical n−2β/(2β+1) (non-private) to (α^2 n)−2β/(2β+2). Provides two constructive and efficient estimators that match the lower bound: (i) a privatized histogram for β=1, and (ii) a privatized orthogonal series estimator for β≥1 using a tailored noise mechanism that is not simple additive Laplace.

The lower bounds combine Le Cam/Fano reductions with LDP-specific information inequalities (KL and mutual information bounds under α-LDP). The achievability results use explicit mechanisms that satisfy α-LDP and yield tight variance-bias tradeoffs.

Relation to prior work
- Differential privacy and LDP: Builds on Dwork et al. (TCC’06), Evfimievski et al. (PODS’03), and Wasserman & Zhou (JASA’10). Distinguishes population minimax risk from the (easier) sample-estimation focus in much of the DP literature. The information bounds used are restated from the authors’ prior arXiv [9]; Hardt–Talwar, De, Smith also relate to lower bounds/generic noise calibration but not in this local-population minimax setting.
- Classical minimax: Le Cam, Fano, Assouad, Yang–Barron, Tsybakov provide the non-private toolkit; the paper adapts these with LDP-specific divergences. The histogram and orthogonal-series constructions align with standard nonparametric estimators (Scott; Efromovich; Tsybakov) but require new privatization to be rate-optimal.
- Deconvolution: The negative result for additive Laplace is justified via Carroll–Hall’s rates.

Quality
- The theoretical results are technically sound and carefully linked to precise privacy constraints. The lower bounds are sharp and the upper bounds constructive. The multinomial section is clean; the density section appropriately handles Sobolev classes using trigonometric bases.
- However, several points would benefit from clarification:
  - Practicality of the orthogonal-series mechanism (Eq. 19): The sampling is described as “efficient,” but generating Z uniformly over a half-hypercube requires more detail. What is the expected runtime, and how does it scale with k ~ (nα^2)1/(2β+2)?
  - Constants and regimes: The results often assume α ∈ [0,1]; it would help to specify precisely where small-α asymptotics kick in, and whether constants are uniform in d and β.
  - Multinomial mechanism (Eq. 10): The definition appears to have a typographical error (“−xj” should likely be “1−xj”), while the subsequent expectation formula matches bit flips.
  - Growth of d: The multinomial rates are given for fixed d; statements about effective sample size would be clearer with explicit conditions on d vs n (e.g., d = o(nα^2)).
  - Boundary effects for Sobolev classes: The periodicity assumptions (f(k)(0)=f(k)(1)) should be highlighted as essential; discuss whether boundary correction or compactly supported wavelets could yield the same rates without periodicity.

Clarity
- The paper is well written, with good background and clear high-level arguments. Proofs are deferred to [9]; for NeurIPS, providing at least proof sketches of the key lemmas (e.g., Proposition 1) would improve self-containment.
- Minor notational issues (as above) should be fixed.

Originality
- The combination of LDP with population minimax theory and the identification of sharp rates for multinomial and density estimation under LDP is novel and impactful. The finding that randomized response is rate-optimal for multinomials is conceptually satisfying and practically relevant. The change in nonparametric rates under LDP and the necessity of nontrivial noise mechanisms (beyond additive Laplace) are new and important.
- A portion of the technical underpinning (information inequalities) appears in the authors’ arXiv [9]. The present submission applies them to concrete problems and provides matching algorithms; this contribution seems sufficiently distinct, but the paper should clearly delineate what is new relative to [9].

Significance
- The results are likely to be influential: they quantify the “price of local privacy” in classical tasks and provide practically implementable mechanisms that are provably optimal. These insights guide practitioners away from naive Laplace perturbation for density estimation and justify the continued use of randomized response in categorical settings.
- The lack of empirical demonstrations leaves open questions about finite-sample constants and practical performance.

Pros
- Sharp, clean minimax characterization under LDP for two central problems.
- Matching, efficient mechanisms; clear message on when classical randomized response is optimal.
- Solid placement in both DP and statistical minimax literatures.

Cons
- Practicality details for the orthogonal-series privatization are thin; runtime and implementation nuances need elaboration.
- No empirical evaluation to assess constants and finite-sample performance.
- Some reliance on prior arXiv for key lemmas; more self-contained proofs would strengthen the submission.
- Minor clarity/typo issues and unaddressed growth regimes (d with n).

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide complexity bounds (and a simple algorithm) for sampling Z in Eq. (19), and discuss scalability for k up to, say, a few hundred?
2) How do the mechanisms behave when α is moderate/large? Do constants make randomized response competitive with k-ary randomized response variants?
3) Can the density results be extended to nonperiodic Sobolev classes (e.g., via boundary-corrected wavelets) with the same rates?
4) Do interactive LDP mechanisms improve rates for either problem?
5) Any simulations illustrating finite-sample behavior and constants?

Recommendation: Accept (strong theory), with minor revisions to address clarity, implementation details, and to better delineate contributions relative to [9].
