

This paper studies minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, bridging classical statistical decision theory with modern privacy frameworks. The authors provide sharp lower bounds and matching upper bounds for two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation under α-local differential privacy.

The main contribution is the characterization of the penalty imposed by privacy constraints on statistical efficiency. For multinomial estimation, the authors show that the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, with Warner's randomized response mechanism achieving the optimal rate. For density estimation of β-smooth densities, they establish that the minimax rate degrades from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2), with the paper providing efficient algorithms (histogram and orthogonal series estimators) that achieve these rates.

The paper builds on previous work by Duchi et al. [9] which established information-theoretic bounds for privacy-constrained estimation, extending this to sharp minimax rates. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature on differential privacy (Dwork et al. [11], Evans et al. [13]) and classical minimax theory (Tsybakov [22], Yu [25]), demonstrating how their results extend and refine previous understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs.

**Strengths**:
1. Technical soundness: The paper provides rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds, with careful attention to the mathematical details of the privacy constraints. The authors demonstrate how to apply information-theoretic techniques (Le Cam's method, Fano's inequality) to derive sharp minimax rates under privacy constraints.
2. Novelty: The paper establishes new results for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy, with the density estimation result being particularly novel in showing a different polynomial rate than classical minimax results. The optimality of Warner's randomized response mechanism for multinomial estimation is a significant historical insight.
3. Practical relevance: The paper provides efficient algorithms (with computational complexity analysis) that achieve the theoretical bounds, making the results applicable to real-world privacy-preserving statistical estimation.
4. Clarity: The paper is well-organized with clear notation and definitions, making the theoretical contributions accessible to readers familiar with statistical learning theory.

**Weaknesses**:
1. The paper focuses exclusively on the local privacy model (where each individual's data is perturbed before collection), which may limit applicability to settings where a trusted curator collects data and applies privacy mechanisms. The authors could have briefly discussed how their results might extend to central privacy models.
2. While the paper demonstrates the necessity of non-trivial sampling strategies for density estimation, the orthogonal series approach for β > 1 requires a complex sampling mechanism that might be less intuitive than the histogram approach for Lipschitz densities (β = 1).
3. The paper doesn't explore how their results might generalize to other statistical problems beyond probability estimation, though this is a minor limitation given the paper's focused scope.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments**:

Pro:
- Provides sharp minimax rates for fundamental statistical problems under local privacy, filling a significant theoretical gap.
- Demonstrates that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation, connecting historical work to modern theory.
- Establishes a new convergence rate for density estimation that differs from classical results, with matching efficient algorithms.
- Well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

Con:
- Focuses on a specific privacy model (local) that may not apply to all practical scenarios.
- The density estimation results for higher smoothness (β > 1) require more complex implementation than the β = 1 case.
- Doesn't explore extensions to other statistical problems or privacy models.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. The results are technically sound, novel, and well-motivated, with clear implications for practical privacy-preserving statistical inference. The authors provide sharp minimax rates with matching upper bounds for both finite-dimensional (multinomial) and nonparametric (density estimation) problems, which advances the state of the art in this area.

The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS as it combines theoretical machine learning with privacy, addresses a fundamental problem in the field, and provides both theoretical insights and practical algorithms. The contribution is significant and original, with clear connections to both statistical theory and privacy literature. The quality of the technical content, clarity of presentation, and novelty of the results make this paper a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.
