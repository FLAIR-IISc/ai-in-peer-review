This paper investigates fundamental limits on probability estimation under local differential privacy, bridging classical statistical decision theory with modern privacy concerns. The authors build upon their prior work establishing a framework for analyzing privacy-utility tradeoffs using minimax rates and successfully apply this framework to both multinomial and density estimation problems. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important and timely problem. The connection to Warner’s original work on randomized response is particularly insightful.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper rigorously derives minimax lower and upper bounds for estimation under local privacy. The use of differential privacy as a formalization of disclosure risk provides a solid theoretical basis.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The authors present a complex topic with commendable clarity. The notation is well-defined, and the structure of the paper is logical.
*   **Novel Contributions:**  The identification of differing convergence rates for density estimation under privacy constraints, specifically the change from  n<sup>-2β/(2β+1)</sup> to (nα<sup>2</sup>)<sup>-2β/(2β+2)</sup>, is a significant contribution. Establishing the optimality of Warner’s randomized response procedure is also noteworthy.
*   **Connections to Prior Work:** The paper effectively situates its contributions within the broader context of privacy-preserving statistical inference, referencing relevant work in cryptography, statistics, and theoretical computer science.
*   **Practical Implications:** The results have practical implications for designing privacy-preserving surveys and data analysis pipelines, providing guidance on the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.
*   **Completeness:** The paper includes a detailed overview of necessary background, careful definitions, and thorough proofs in a related publication [9], making it a complete piece of work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** The analysis of density estimation is restricted to the Sobolev class Fβ.  While this is a reasonable starting point, it would be valuable to discuss the limitations of this assumption and potential extensions to other function classes.
*   **Computational Complexity of Optimal Estimators:** The paper mentions achieving optimal rates, but doesn’t deeply analyze the computational complexity of the proposed estimators, particularly in higher dimensions or for complex density estimation scenarios. While efficiently implementable algorithms are mentioned, a more detailed discussion of practical computational costs would be useful.
*   **Assumptions on Orthonormal Basis:** The analysis in the density estimation section relies heavily on the choice of orthonormal basis. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion on the sensitivity of the results to different bases could strengthen the paper.
*   **Missing Discussion of Real-World Data:** The paper is largely theoretical. While the theoretical results are valuable, discussing how these results translate to performance on real-world datasets would strengthen the practical impact of the work.
*   **Notationally Dense:** While clear overall, the paper’s reliance on a large amount of notation can sometimes make it challenging to follow the derivations.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem at the intersection of privacy and statistical inference.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous and provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The results have potential practical implications for designing privacy-preserving data analysis systems.
*   The contributions are novel and build upon a strong foundation of prior work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scope of the density estimation analysis is somewhat limited.
*   The computational complexity of the proposed estimators is not fully addressed.
*   The lack of empirical evaluation on real-world datasets limits the practical impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The theoretical results are rigorous and insightful, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding computational complexity and the scope of density estimation. Specifically, the authors could consider adding a section discussing the computational costs of their proposed estimators and acknowledging the limitations of the Sobolev space assumption in the density estimation setting.



**Confidence:** 8/10
