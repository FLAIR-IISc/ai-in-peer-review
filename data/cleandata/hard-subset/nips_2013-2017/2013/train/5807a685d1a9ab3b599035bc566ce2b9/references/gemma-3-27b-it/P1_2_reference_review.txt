## Peer Review for "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a strong theoretical contribution to the growing field of privacy-preserving machine learning. It rigorously connects differential privacy with minimax optimality, providing sharp convergence rate bounds for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a significant problem with clear implications for practical applications like surveys and density estimation. While the core ideas build on existing work in differential privacy and minimax theory, the combination and application to these specific problems, along with the resulting tight bounds, are novel and valuable. I recommend acceptance.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper investigates the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical accuracy in probability estimation. It establishes a formal framework utilizing α-local differential privacy and minimax decision theory.  The key contribution lies in deriving tight upper and lower bounds on the convergence rates for estimating multinomial probabilities and density functions when data is subject to local privacy constraints.  Specifically, they show that local privacy introduces a penalty on the effective sample size (for multinomial estimation) and alters the convergence rate for density estimation compared to the non-private setting. They also demonstrate the optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism and provide efficiently implementable algorithms achieving these bounds. The paper builds on the work of Dwork et al. on differential privacy and connects it to classical minimax theory.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is built on a solid foundation of differential privacy and minimax theory. The proofs appear to be carefully constructed and leverage existing results effectively.  The use of Lemma 1 and Propositions 1 from [9] is elegant and demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant theoretical tools.
* **Sharp Bounds:** Deriving tight upper and lower bounds is a significant achievement.  These bounds provide valuable insights into the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving probability estimation.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem of privacy-preserving probability estimation is highly relevant in various applications, including surveys, medical data analysis, and social science research.
* **Optimality Result:** The demonstration of optimality for Warner’s randomized response procedure is a noteworthy finding, highlighting the longevity and continued relevance of this classic technique.
* **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the theoretical developments. The notation is standard and the key definitions are clearly presented.
* **Comprehensive Background:** The introduction provides a good overview of the motivating factors for privacy in statistical inference and the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** While the density estimation results are insightful, they focus solely on smoothness measured by Sobolev classes. Exploring the impact of other density characteristics (e.g., support, modality) might provide a more comprehensive picture.
* **Computational Complexity of Estimators:**  While the authors mention that their algorithms are efficiently implementable, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, especially for higher-dimensional problems or complex density models, would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions on Orthonormal Basis:** The analysis in the density estimation section relies on the choice of an orthonormal basis. Discussing the sensitivity of the results to the specific choice of basis would strengthen the analysis.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical bounds. A brief discussion of the practical implications of the α parameter (e.g., choosing appropriate values based on the sensitivity of the data and desired privacy levels) would be useful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to a growing and important field.
* The results are novel, rigorous, and provide valuable insights into the trade-off between privacy and accuracy.
* The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response procedure is a significant finding.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scope of the density estimation results could be broader.
* A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the estimators would be valuable.



**Specific Comments & Questions:**

* **Page 3 (Likelihood Ratio):** It would be helpful to briefly mention the connection between the likelihood ratio bound and epsilon-differential privacy.
* **Page 5 (Theorem 1):**  The notation “nα2/d” feels a little loose; it might be more precise to indicate that this scaling applies when α is small.
* **Page 7 (Randomized Response):** The description of the optimization procedure and discussion around projections onto the simplex could be elaborated to enhance clarity.
* **Page 12 (Proposition 3):** The constant *c* is mentioned as depending on C and B0. Providing a more precise statement of the dependence would be helpful.
* **General Comment:** While the paper consistently refers back to [9], providing a slightly more detailed summary of the key results from [9] within the main body of the paper would improve readability for readers unfamiliar with that work.

**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points outlined above.**
