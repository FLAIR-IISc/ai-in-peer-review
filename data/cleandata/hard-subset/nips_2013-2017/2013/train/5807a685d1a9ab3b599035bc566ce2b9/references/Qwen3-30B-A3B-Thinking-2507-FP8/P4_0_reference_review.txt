

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in the context of local differential privacy, combining minimax decision theory with differential privacy frameworks. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for two key problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation under local differential privacy constraints. They demonstrate that providing α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d for multinomial estimation, and alters the convergence rate for density estimation from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2) for densities with β continuous derivatives. The paper also shows that Warner's randomized response procedure achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation, and provides efficient algorithms for density estimation that match the lower bounds.

The paper makes significant contributions to the literature on privacy-preserving statistical inference. Previous work on differential privacy has primarily focused on utility guarantees for specific estimators or sample complexity for particular problems, but this paper provides a unified framework for understanding the fundamental limits of statistical estimation under privacy constraints. The work builds on Duchi et al.'s [9] previous contributions on statistical inference under differential privacy, but extends it by providing minimax rates that characterize the optimal trade-off between privacy and statistical utility.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish lower bounds using standard minimax techniques (Le Cam's two-point method and Fano's inequality) combined with information-theoretic bounds on differential privacy. The proofs are well-structured and the mathematical arguments are sound. The paper provides matching upper bounds with computationally efficient algorithms, which is a significant strength. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach, such as the necessity of non-trivial sampling strategies for optimal density estimation. The technical content is complete and well-supported.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to practical algorithms. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The key results are clearly stated with appropriate mathematical precision. The connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation is well-explained, making it accessible to experts in the field. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates under local differential privacy constraints. The results differ from previous work in two key ways: (1) they provide a unified framework for understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility, rather than focusing on specific estimators or problems; (2) they demonstrate that the classical randomized response procedure is optimal for multinomial estimation, which is an interesting historical connection. The density estimation results showing a different convergence rate (2β/(2β+2) vs. 2β/(2β+1)) represent a significant departure from classical minimax results, which is novel. The paper properly cites relevant prior work and clearly distinguishes its contributions.

**Significance:** The results have important implications for understanding the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical inference. By characterizing the sharp rates, the paper provides a quantitative understanding of the trade-off between privacy and statistical utility that can inform the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The results are broadly applicable to survey design, data collection, and privacy-preserving machine learning. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a theoretical framework that can be extended to other statistical problems under privacy constraints. The practical algorithms that achieve the theoretical bounds make the results immediately applicable to real-world privacy-preserving statistical inference.

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with sharp lower and upper bounds
- Clear demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation
- Practical algorithms that achieve the theoretical bounds
- Unified framework for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of practical applications beyond the theoretical framework
- Minimal empirical validation of the proposed algorithms
- The paper is highly technical, which may limit accessibility to some readers
- Limited discussion of connections to other privacy frameworks beyond local differential privacy

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy
- Establishes a fundamental theoretical framework for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs
- Demonstrates that classical privacy techniques (Warner's randomized response) are optimal
- Provides efficient algorithms that match the theoretical bounds
- Makes significant contributions to both statistical decision theory and differential privacy

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The paper's high technical level may limit its accessibility
- Limited empirical validation of the proposed methods
- Could have provided more discussion of practical implications

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing sharp minimax bounds for statistical estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The theoretical results are rigorous, the algorithms are efficient, and the paper advances our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The work is of high quality, original, and significant, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
