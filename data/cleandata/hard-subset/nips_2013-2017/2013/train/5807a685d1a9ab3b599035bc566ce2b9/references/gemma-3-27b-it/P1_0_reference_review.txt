## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building on the framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors provide tight minimax lower and upper bounds for multinomial and density estimation under the constraint of α-local differential privacy.  They demonstrate a specific penalty in sample complexity (n -> nα²/d for multinomial estimation) and a modified convergence rate for density estimation (scaling as (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2)). Notably, they show that Warner's randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation and derive efficiently implementable algorithms achieving the established bounds.  The work connects classical notions of privacy (Warner's randomized response) with modern differential privacy and statistical decision theory, offering a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding these trade-offs.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the work of Duchi et al. (2013) [9] on statistical inference under differential privacy, leveraging their information-theoretic bounds. It also draws from classical minimax theory (e.g., Tsybakov, 2009 [22]) and previous work on differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006 [11], Chaudhuri et al., 2012 [6]).  The authors effectively bridge the gap between the cryptographic/database focus of differential privacy and the statistical decision-theoretic framework of minimax estimation.  The connection to Warner’s work [23] provides historical context and validates its optimality in a modern theoretical framework. The work is related to, but distinct from, research on private empirical risk minimization and differentially private M-estimators [19, 24] by focusing on sharp minimax rates rather than specific estimation procedures.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides a solid mathematical foundation for analyzing the privacy-utility trade-off, utilizing differential privacy and minimax theory in a principled way.
* **Sharp Bounds:** The derived minimax rates are tight, offering valuable insights into the fundamental limits of private probability estimation.
* **Practical Implications:** The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response is significant, demonstrating that a decades-old technique remains state-of-the-art under rigorous theoretical analysis. The proposed algorithms are efficiently implementable.
* **Novel Combination of Techniques:**  Successfully combining differential privacy, minimax theory, and information-theoretic bounds is a significant contribution.
* **Clear Exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the theoretical concepts, although some background knowledge is assumed (addressed by restating key results from [9]).
* **Addresses an important problem:** The work tackles a foundational question regarding the costs of privacy, which is of increasing relevance in a data-driven world.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** While the density estimation results are interesting, the focus on smoothness (β) and the restriction to the trigonometric basis might limit the broader applicability of the results.  Exploring other basis functions or density classes could be valuable.
* **Practicality of Histogram Estimator:** Although theoretically optimal, the histogram estimator in the locally private setting may require a large number of bins (k = (nα²)^(1/(2β+2))), potentially leading to practical challenges in high dimensions or with limited data.
* **Assumptions on Basis Functions:**  The orthogonal series estimation relies on the assumption of bounded basis functions.  While reasonable, this constraint should be acknowledged as a potential limitation.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks empirical validation of the derived bounds. Including simulations to demonstrate the performance of the proposed algorithms and verify the theoretical results would strengthen the work.
* **Notationally Dense:** While generally clear, the paper uses a considerable amount of notation, which can occasionally make it difficult to follow the derivations.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper provides a fundamental and rigorous theoretical contribution to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation.
* The sharp minimax bounds are valuable for designing and evaluating privacy-preserving algorithms.
* The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response is significant and provides practical guidance.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* The limited scope of the density estimation results and the potential practicality issues with the histogram estimator.
* The lack of empirical validation, which would help to demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The rigorous analysis and sharp bounds provide important insights into the fundamental limits of private probability estimation. While the lack of empirical validation and some limitations in the scope of the density estimation results are weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is a worthy addition to NIPS and recommend acceptance.

**Confidence:** 8/10

**Recommendation:** Accept.  Minor revisions suggested:  consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the assumptions made, and strongly encourage the inclusion of simulations to validate the theoretical results.
