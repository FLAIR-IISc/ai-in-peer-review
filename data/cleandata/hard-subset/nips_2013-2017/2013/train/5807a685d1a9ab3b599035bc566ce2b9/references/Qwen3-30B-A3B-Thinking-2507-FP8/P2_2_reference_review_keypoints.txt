- Studies trade-offs between local differential privacy and statistical utility, providing sharp minimax rates for probability estimation.  
- For multinomial estimation (d categories), effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy.  
- Warner's randomized response is shown to be minimax optimal for multinomial estimation.  
- For β-smooth density estimation, the minimax rate changes from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (α²n)^(-2β/(2β+2)).  
- Provides matching lower and upper bounds with rigorous proofs using information-theoretic tools (Fano, Le Cam).  
- Presents efficient algorithms that achieve the optimal privatized estimation rates.  
- Novel contribution: formalizes privacy–utility trade-off in a minimax framework and links classical randomized response to modern differential privacy.  
- Paper is technically rigorous, well-organized, and clearly written with consistent notation.  
- Results are practically relevant because they identify simple, optimal mechanisms (e.g., randomized response).  
- Limitation: focus is on specific problems (multinomial and density estimation), not on regression/classification.  
- Limitation: does not explore practical implementation challenges in high-dimensional or non-i.i.d. settings.  
- Significance: quantifies when privacy cost is a manageable sample-size reduction versus when it causes a fundamentally worse convergence rate.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — outstanding theoretical contribution with substantial implications for privacy-preserving inference.  
- Pro arguments: sharp minimax rates, optimality of randomized response, new framework, efficient algorithms, rigorous exposition.  
- Con arguments: limited problem scope, lack of practical implementation discussion, some density results may be less intuitive.
