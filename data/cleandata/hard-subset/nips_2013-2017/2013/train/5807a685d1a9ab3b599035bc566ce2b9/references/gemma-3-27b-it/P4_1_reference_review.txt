This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in probability estimation, building upon the formal framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner’s original problem of estimating population strata proportions and extend it to nonparametric density estimation. They derive minimax convergence rates under local differential privacy, demonstrating a penalty in statistical efficiency due to privacy constraints. Specifically, they show that for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size is reduced by a factor of α²/d, and for density estimation, the convergence rate degrades from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2). They also provide efficiently implementable algorithms (randomized response for multinomial estimation and orthogonal series estimation for density estimation) that achieve these optimal rates.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly connecting the historical roots of privacy in statistical surveys (Warner, 1965) with modern differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006). The introduction effectively situates the work within the broader literature on privacy and statistical inference. The technical presentation is generally sound, employing standard minimax theory tools and carefully defining the required concepts. The paper successfully brings together concepts from disparate fields – statistics, information theory, and privacy – and provides a rigorous analysis of the resulting trade-offs. The achievability results, demonstrating that the derived lower bounds are indeed attainable, are a significant strength. The clear articulation of the penalty on the convergence rates and the explanation of why simple perturbation approaches are sub-optimal (for density estimation) are particularly valuable.

However, the paper’s length and density make it challenging to follow in detail.  The jump between the general background material (Section 2) and the specific results for multinomial and density estimation feels abrupt. More intuitive explanations of the key technical steps, particularly those involving the information bounds (Proposition 1), would enhance clarity. While the authors reference their previous work [9], readers unfamiliar with that paper will find themselves needing to consult it to fully grasp the derivations. The notation is also quite dense and could benefit from more careful introduction and explanation.  The proofs themselves are relegated to a separate paper, which hinders reproducibility and makes it harder to assess the validity of the claims directly within the submission.  While the authors demonstrate optimality, a discussion of the practical implications of the α² reduction in effective sample size for multinomial estimation is missing. How large does α need to be for the loss of statistical power to become significant in real-world applications?

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing the trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in probability estimation, building on differential privacy and minimax theory.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis is mathematically well-founded, with clear derivations of minimax rates and accompanying upper bounds.
*   **Significance:**  The results are important for understanding the limitations of privacy-preserving statistical methods and for designing optimal estimators in privacy-sensitive settings.
*   **Clarity (overall):** The introduction and motivation are clear. The organization is logical, progressing from general concepts to specific problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity (details):** The technical details are dense and challenging to follow without prior familiarity with the referenced work.
*   **Completeness:** The proofs are in a separate paper, hindering reproducibility and assessment.
*   **Practical Implications:** Limited discussion of the practical significance of the findings.
*   **Abrupt Transitions:** The transition from background to specific results could be smoother.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistics.
*   The derived minimax rates provide a fundamental benchmark for evaluating privacy-preserving estimators.
*   The achievability results demonstrate that the theoretical bounds are tight and attainable.
*   The paper builds on and extends previous work in both differential privacy and minimax theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The density and technical complexity may make the paper inaccessible to a broad audience.
*   The lack of complete proofs in the submission hinders reproducibility and thorough evaluation.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a rapidly growing area of research. Addressing the clarity and completeness concerns (e.g., providing more intuitive explanations of key steps, potentially including key proof sketches in the appendix) would substantially enhance the paper's impact. While the technical density is a drawback, the novelty and significance of the results justify acceptance, provided the authors are willing to address the reviewers' concerns regarding clarity and completeness.
