Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP) constraints using a minimax decision-theoretic framework. It formalizes the α-local privacy condition (non-interactive channels) and defines the private minimax risk over estimators and privacy mechanisms. The main technical tools are information-theoretic inequalities that relate divergences between privatized distributions to pre-privacy distances between the underlying data distributions. Using Le Cam/Fano techniques together with new bounds (KL and mutual information) tailored to LDP, the paper derives sharp lower bounds and matching upper bounds for two canonical problems:

1) Multinomial probability estimation: For θ ∈ Δd and observations privatized via α-LDP channels, the minimax mean-squared error scales as min{1, d/(nα^2)} (up to constants), and the ℓ1-risk scales as min{1, d/√(nα^2)}. This implies an effective sample size reduction from n to nα^2/d for small α. A variant of randomized response (Warner 1965) achieves the lower bound, as does a Laplace-perturb-then-project estimator.

2) Nonparametric density estimation on [0,1] in Sobolev classes Fβ: Under LDP, the minimax L2-risk exhibits a different exponent from the non-private case, scaling as (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)} instead of n^{-2β/(2β+1)}. For β=1 (Lipschitz), a privatized histogram with k ≍ (nα^2)^{1/4} bins achieves the rate; for general β≥1, an orthogonal series estimator combined with a carefully designed locally private mechanism over k ≍ (nα^2)^{1/(2β+2)} coefficients achieves the rate.

The work connects classical randomized response (Warner), differential privacy foundations (Dwork et al.), and information-theoretic minimax lower bounds (Le Cam, Fano; Yang and Barron). It emphasizes the distinction between prior DP optimality results focused on sample-level accuracy and the present population minimax risks. The information bounds used (Proposition 1) are stated as results from the authors’ prior work (arXiv:1302.3203), and are central to the lower bound proofs.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Lower bounds: The reductions use standard testing frameworks. The KL/mutual information upper bounds for LDP channels (scaling with (e^α−1)^2 and TV distances) are plausible and cited to prior work by the authors. Given these, the packing and variance–bias trade-offs produce the stated rates for both multinomial and Sobolev classes. The density lower bound correctly reflects that under LDP the effective noise inflation depends on k, changing the optimal bias–variance balance and the exponent.

- Upper bounds: The multinomial estimator via a modified randomized response mechanism is shown α-LDP and unbiased up to a linear transformation, followed by projection; variance calculations yield the stated rates. For histograms (β=1), adding Laplace noise to one-hot encodings and projecting to the simplex is α-LDP (L1 sensitivity argument) and achieves the rate with appropriate k. For general β, the orthogonal series mechanism that samples Z ∈ {±B}^k conditioned on a privatized sign pattern is α-LDP and calibrated for unbiasedness, yielding variance ~k/(nα^2) and optimal rates. The achievability arguments are consistent with standard non-private analyses with the variance term inflated by α and k as dictated by the mechanism.

- Proof completeness: Some proofs are deferred to [9]. For a conference paper, this is acceptable if the main ideas and key steps are clear, which they are. It would strengthen the submission to include proofs of the most central lemmas or a more detailed sketch of Proposition 1’s derivation within this paper.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and precise, with clear problem statements, definitions of LDP, minimax risk, and relevant divergences. The multinomial mechanism is described explicitly, including its DP guarantee. The density section clearly distinguishes β=1 (histograms) from β>1 (orthogonal series). Minor clarity issues:
- In Section 3.2, clarify that x,x′ are basis vectors when using the L1≤2 argument; or state the general bound but specialize to the multinomial case.
- Provide more detail on the computational procedure for sampling Z in the orthogonal-series mechanism (complexity in k and acceptance rates).
- Some references have incomplete bibliographic metadata; fixing this would aid readers.

Originality
The combination of LDP constraints with sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, including matching mechanisms, is novel and impactful. The key information bounds are attributed to the authors’ prior work; the paper transparently restates them. The application to density estimation with a provably optimal LDP mechanism beyond simple additive noise appears to be a substantive contribution beyond standard Laplace perturbation approaches (and is supported by deconvolution impossibility arguments).

Significance
The results provide fundamental understanding of the cost of local privacy in statistical estimation, with exact dependence on α, n, d, and β. They identify optimal mechanisms, including the optimality of randomized response for multinomials and nontrivial privatization for orthogonal series estimators. These insights are likely to influence both theory and practice in privacy-preserving data analysis and clarify the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

Pros
- Sharp, matching upper and lower minimax rates under LDP for two canonical problems.
- Clear identification of effective sample size penalties and exponent changes due to LDP.
- Constructive, efficiently implementable mechanisms achieving the bounds.
- Sound information-theoretic lower bound methodology tailored to LDP.
- Relevance to NeurIPS topics in privacy, statistical theory, and learning.

Cons
- Key information bounds and some proofs are deferred to an external manuscript; the paper relies on prior work for crucial steps.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and constants for the orthogonal-series privatization; practical implementation details could be expanded.
- No empirical demonstrations or finite-sample evaluations to complement the theory.
- Scope limited to non-interactive LDP; discussion of potential improvements via interaction is brief.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include more detailed proof sketches of Proposition 1 or an appendix summarizing its derivation, to make the paper more self-contained.
- Elaborate the sampling algorithm for the orthogonal-series mechanism, with runtime/communication analysis.
- Add a brief experimental section or simulations illustrating finite-sample behavior and comparing mechanisms (e.g., simple Laplace perturbation vs. proposed schemes).
- Clarify the multinomial randomized response mapping and DP guarantee, explicitly stating the assumptions on x.
- Update and complete reference entries.

Overall, the paper provides a technically sound, clear, and significant contribution to understanding minimax rates under local differential privacy, with well-motivated mechanisms that achieve the derived bounds.
