Summary
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It formalizes an α-LDP minimax risk (population risk) and provides information-theoretic lower bounds that translate LDP constraints into limits on achievable accuracy. Two main applications are analyzed:

- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the minimax ℓ2-squared risk scales as min{1, d/(nα2)} (and ℓ1 risk as min{1, d/√(nα2)}). A slight variant of Warner’s randomized response attains the rate, as do simple perturbation schemes on the one-hot representation followed by projection.

- Density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1] in L2: The classical rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2). Histogram estimators achieve the β=1 rate; for β≥1, an orthogonal-series estimator with a carefully designed locally private mechanism for privatizing basis coefficients attains the lower bound. The paper explains why direct Laplace perturbation of raw samples (deconvolution) is suboptimal.

The technical backbone is a pair of information bounds relating KL/mutual information to total variation under α-LDP channels, combined with standard testing reductions (Le Cam/Fano).

Relation to prior work
The work connects Warner’s randomized response with modern DP (Dwork et al.), and minimax testing tools (Yang & Barron; Yu). It contrasts with earlier DP optimality that targets sample queries rather than population minimax risk. It refines insights of Wasserman–Zhou on private density estimation and leverages Carroll–Hall’s deconvolution limits to argue suboptimality of additive-noise approaches. The information bounds are drawn from the authors’ prior technical report.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall framework and rates are plausible and align with known phenomena in LDP (effective sample size reduction by α2). The lower bounds via information contraction, and upper bounds via concrete mechanisms, appear correct at a high level.

- However, substantial proofs are deferred to an external manuscript [9]. For NeurIPS, the submission should be self-contained (proofs in the main or supplement). Some derivations (e.g., properties and complexity of the orthogonal-series privatization, exact constants/normalization in Eq. (20)) are only sketched.

- Potential error: Theorem 1’s ℓ2-squared lower bound includes a term “√(nα2)” inside a min; this increases with n and is dimensionally inconsistent for a risk bound. I suspect a typo; the intended lower bound is likely c min{1, d/(nα2)}. Please correct/clarify.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized with clear high-level narrative. Notation and background are standard.

- Some mechanism descriptions are confusing. In Eq. (10), Z∈{0,1}d but “[Z]j = −xj” is not meaningful when xj∈{0,1}; this seems to intend bit-flipping, but the formulation should be rewritten with explicit conditional probabilities P(Zj=1|xj).

- For the orthogonal-series scheme, the construction via τ̃, the Bernoulli T, and the selection of Z on the hypercube is clever but under-explained. A brief algorithmic description with expected sampling complexity (e.g., rejection rate) would help.

- Bibliography entries are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages). Please fix.

Originality
- The problem formulation (population minimax under LDP) and emphasis on sharp rates with matching mechanisms are timely and valuable. The change in the nonparametric exponent under LDP is an important insight.

- Some components (randomized response optimality, histogram rates) are expected, but the information bounds tailored to LDP and the orthogonal-series privatization achieving optimal rates are technically novel and nontrivial.

Significance
- These results provide a foundational understanding of the cost of LDP in both parametric and nonparametric settings and identify optimal mechanisms. They will likely influence both theory and practice (e.g., choice of mechanism beyond naive Laplace noise).

- The paper focuses on the non-interactive (memoryless) setting; discussing whether interaction can improve rates would increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling connection between DP and minimax theory.
- Sharp lower bounds with matching, efficiently implementable mechanisms.
- Illuminates when classical randomized response is optimal and when more sophisticated privatization is necessary.
- Identifies suboptimality of naive additive-noise mechanisms for densities.

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) Self-containment: Move the core proofs (or detailed sketches) into the supplement; relying on [9] is insufficient for review.

2) Correctness/typo: Fix Theorem 1’s suspicious √(nα2) term and ensure all rate statements are dimensionally consistent.

3) Mechanism clarity: Rewrite Eq. (10) with proper conditional probabilities for Zj|xj. Clarify the orthogonal-series mechanism, including:
- Proof of α-LDP with explicit constants.
- Choice of B and ck, and how to compute/estimate them.
- Expected computational cost and failure probabilities.

4) Valid density: For the series estimator, discuss whether f̂ is projected to a proper density (nonnegativity, unit integral) or justify why this is unnecessary for L2-risk while still meaningful.

5) Empirical illustration: While theory is primary, a small synthetic study comparing randomized response, Laplace perturbations, and the proposed series mechanism would make the claims more convincing and inform constants.

6) Scope and parameters: Most results assume α∈[0,1]. Comment on behavior for moderate/large α, and whether constants remain reasonable.

7) References: Complete and standardize the bibliography; add missing related LDP learning results (e.g., Kasiviswanathan et al.) in context.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide a lower bound ruling out any additive noise mechanism (beyond Laplace) from achieving the optimal density rate under LDP?
- Does interaction help for either multinomial or density estimation rates?
- What is the expected runtime/sample complexity of the series sampling procedure as a function of k and α?

Recommendation
Borderline accept leaning to accept. The contribution is significant and, if the noted clarity/proof issues and the apparent typo are fixed, merits inclusion.
