This paper addresses the fundamental problem of probability estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy, connecting Warner’s early work with modern privacy definitions and minimax theory. The authors provide lower and upper bounds on the convergence rates for multinomial and density estimation, highlighting the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. While the combination of these areas is valuable and the theoretical results are well-established, the paper relies heavily on existing results and offers relatively incremental contributions.

**Summary:** The core idea is to leverage differential privacy guarantees to derive minimax optimality results for probability estimation. For multinomial estimation, the authors show a reduction in effective sample size due to privacy constraints. For density estimation, they demonstrate a different polynomial rate of convergence compared to classical nonparametric estimation. The paper carefully builds on their prior work [9] and provides efficiently implementable algorithms that match their lower bounds.

**Strengths:**
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is built on solid theoretical ground, utilizing minimax theory and differential privacy in a rigorous manner. The proofs appear correct and follow logically from established results.
* **Clear Connection to Prior Work:** The authors explicitly connect their work to Warner’s initial motivation and recent developments in differential privacy. They cite relevant literature appropriately and place their contributions within the existing body of knowledge.
* **Sharp Bounds:** The derived minimax rates are, according to the authors, sharp, demonstrating a clear understanding of the fundamental limits of private estimation.
* **Practical Algorithms:** The paper doesn't just present theoretical limits but also provides algorithms achieving those limits. This is a positive aspect, showing the results aren't merely academic.

**Weaknesses:**
* **Incremental Contribution:** The paper largely *applies* existing theory (minimax, differential privacy) to a specific problem. The new insights, while correct, are not groundbreaking. The application, while solid, feels somewhat expected given the established frameworks.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any substantial empirical validation. While theoretical bounds are presented, there's no demonstration of the practical impact of these bounds on real-world datasets. A simulation study, even a modest one, would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Heavy Reliance on Previous Work:** The reliance on the authors’ previous work [9] is substantial. While citing one's own work is standard, the current paper feels like a direct extension with limited independent novelty. A reader unfamiliar with [9] will struggle to fully grasp the contributions.
* **Density Estimation Results are Narrow:** The density estimation results are specifically tied to smoothness assumptions (β derivatives) and a particular orthonormal basis. A discussion of the robustness or limitations of these assumptions would be beneficial. The claim that perturbation-based estimators are sub-optimal feels somewhat hand-wavy without more detailed analysis.
* **Notationally Dense:** While generally clear, the paper is quite notationally dense, which might hinder accessibility for readers not deeply familiar with the field.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
* The paper presents a technically sound analysis of a fundamental problem in privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* The results are mathematically rigorous and contribute to a better understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and utility.
* The provided algorithms are efficiently implementable and achieve optimal convergence rates.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
* The paper's contribution is relatively incremental, building primarily on existing theoretical frameworks.
* The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact and relevance of the results.
* The strong dependence on prior work [9] raises concerns about the novelty of the presented contributions.



**Overall Assessment:** I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically correct and well-presented, it doesn't meet the bar for acceptance at NIPS. The contributions are incremental, the empirical evaluation is lacking, and the reliance on previous work is too strong. The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, but it doesn't offer enough new insights or practical advancements to justify inclusion in the conference. A significant revision with empirical results and a more clearly articulated novelty would be needed for reconsideration. The problem is important and the theoretical treatment is sound, but the paper reads more like a journal article than a NIPS paper.
