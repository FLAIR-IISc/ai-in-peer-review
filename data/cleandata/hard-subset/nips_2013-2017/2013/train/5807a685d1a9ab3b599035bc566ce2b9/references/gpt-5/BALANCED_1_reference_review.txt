Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), combining a minimax decision-theoretic framework with formal privacy guarantees. The setting is non-interactive LDP: each user privatizes Xi via a memoryless channel Q that satisfies α-local DP (Evfimievski et al. [13]; Kasiviswanathan et al. [17] on local learning). The authors leverage information-theoretic contraction bounds for private channels (KL and mutual information bounds depending on total variation distances; restated here as Proposition 1, derived in their prior work [9]) together with Le Cam/Fano methods to obtain sharp private minimax lower bounds.

Two concrete estimation problems are analyzed:

- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the minimax risk under LDP scales as d/(n α^2) (in ℓ2 risk, truncated at 1), and d/√(n α^2) in ℓ1. This implies an “effective sample size” reduction n → n α^2/d for small α. Matching upper bounds are achieved by simple mechanisms: a slight variant of Warner’s randomized response [23] and Laplace perturbation on one-hot encodings followed by projection.

- Density estimation on [0,1] in Sobolev classes Fβ: The non-private rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} degrades under LDP to (n α^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)}. The lower bound is shown via packing and the information bounds; achievability is demonstrated by a privatized histogram for β=1 and an orthogonal series estimator with a tailored privatization mechanism for general β ≥ 1. The authors argue that naive additive noise on the raw Xi cannot attain the optimal rate due to deconvolution limits (Carroll and Hall [4]).

This work connects classical statistical lower bound techniques (Yang and Barron [24], Yu [25], Tsybakov [22]) with DP (Dwork et al. [11]; Wasserman and Zhou [24]; Hardt and Talwar [16]) and shows precise utility-privacy tradeoffs under LDP, including optimality of randomized response. It complements prior DP optimality results that target sample quantities rather than population risk, and extends beyond contingency table or ERM analyses (e.g., [6], [5]) by providing sharp population minimax rates.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds rest on clear information-contraction inequalities for LDP channels and standard hypothesis testing reductions; the upper bounds use explicit, computationally simple mechanisms with risk analyses that match the lower bounds up to constants. The argument that direct additive noise on Xi is suboptimal for density estimation is well supported by known deconvolution theory.
- Completeness: Many proofs are deferred to the authors’ longer paper [9]. While acceptable for space reasons, it would strengthen the submission to include key proofs or full details in a supplementary appendix to ensure self-containment for NeurIPS.
- Minor issue: Equation (8)’s lower bound includes a term sqrt(n α^2) inside a min over dimensionless quantities; that seems inconsistent dimensionally and likely a typo. Clarification is needed.

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally clear and well structured, with good background and careful statement of assumptions. The description of the orthogonal-series privatization (Sec. 4.3) could benefit from algorithmic pseudocode and an explicit complexity bound for the sampling scheme (e.g., how to implement the uniform sampling over {±B}^k with the halfspace constraint efficiently).
- The role and bounds for constants (e.g., ck, B, B0, dependence on k and α) could be made more explicit. It would also help to state high-probability versions of the risk bounds, not only in expectation.
- Some references have typos or missing years; please clean the bibliography.

Originality:
- The integration of local DP with minimax population risk, the sharp rates for multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, and the demonstration that different mechanisms are required to attain optimal rates (beyond simple Laplace noise on data) are novel and valuable. The change in the density estimation exponent under LDP is particularly insightful.
- The information bounds are drawn from [9], but the application here to concrete problems with matching algorithms is substantive.

Significance:
- High. The results quantify the precise price of LDP in fundamental settings, validate long-standing heuristics (optimality of randomized response), and introduce mechanism design ideas necessary for optimal nonparametric estimation under privacy. The work is likely to influence both theory and practice in private data collection and analysis.
- Scope: Results are for univariate densities and non-interactive LDP. Discussion of extensions to higher-dimensional densities and to interactive local protocols would broaden impact.

Pros:
- Sharp private minimax lower bounds with matching, efficient mechanisms.
- Clear conceptual contribution connecting privacy and classical minimax theory.
- Illuminates when standard noise-addition mechanisms are suboptimal and provides better alternatives.
- Strong relevance to NeurIPS topics (learning under constraints, information theory, statistics).

Cons:
- Proofs largely deferred to [9]; limited self-containment for a conference paper.
- No empirical illustrations; even small simulations could validate constants and compare mechanisms.
- Restricted to non-interactive LDP and 1D density; limited discussion of higher dimensions or interactive gains.
- Minor clarity/typo issues (e.g., Eq. (8)), and algorithmic details in Sec. 4.3 could be streamlined.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include a detailed supplementary proving the main theorems and propositions in this submission.
- Add small-scale experiments comparing randomized response, Laplace perturbation, and the proposed orthogonal-series mechanism under varying α, n, d, and β.
- Provide pseudocode and implementation guidance for the Sec. 4.3 sampling scheme, and discuss data-driven selection of k under privacy (e.g., private cross-validation or Lepski-type methods).
- Clarify the behavior for α > 1 and provide explicit constants or non-asymptotic bounds.
- Discuss potential extensions: interactive local protocols, multivariate density estimation, other loss metrics (e.g., Hellinger/TV), and adaptivity to unknown smoothness.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper offers rigorous and sharp results on a timely and important topic, with clear advances over prior work. A stronger supplement and minor clarifications would further improve the submission.
