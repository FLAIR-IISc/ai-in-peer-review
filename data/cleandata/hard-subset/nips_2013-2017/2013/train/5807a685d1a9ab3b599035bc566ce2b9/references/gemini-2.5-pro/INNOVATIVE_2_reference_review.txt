### Review

**Summary**

This paper establishes a deep and fundamental connection between the modern computer science notion of local differential privacy and the classical framework of statistical minimax decision theory. The authors' goal is to precisely characterize the inherent statistical cost of guaranteeing local privacy. They derive sharp minimax lower bounds on the estimation error for any procedure satisfying α-local differential privacy, and complement these with matching upper bounds achieved by explicit, computationally efficient algorithms.

The paper presents two main results. First, for the problem of estimating a d-dimensional multinomial distribution, it is shown that local privacy incurs a penalty that reduces the effective sample size from `n` to `n * α^2 / d`. A striking consequence of this result is that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal. Second, for the nonparametric problem of estimating a smooth probability density (belonging to a Sobolev class with smoothness β), the paper demonstrates a more severe penalty. Local privacy changes the polynomial rate of convergence from the classical `n^(-2β / (2β+1))` to `(n * α^2)^(-2β / (2β+2))`. This degradation of the exponent itself, not just the constant factor, represents a fundamental shift in the statistical difficulty of the problem. The authors construct novel privacy mechanisms, including a private histogram and an orthogonal series estimator, to show these new rates are achievable.

**Strengths**

This is an exceptional paper that makes a foundational contribution. Its primary strength lies in its novelty and the bold synthesis of ideas from seemingly disparate fields.

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The paper's most significant contribution is the creation of a rigorous framework for quantifying the price of privacy. By bringing the powerful tools of minimax theory to bear on differential privacy, the authors move beyond the typical analysis of specific private algorithms and instead ask a much more fundamental question: what are the ultimate limits of statistical inference under a given privacy constraint? This provides a powerful new lens through which to understand the privacy-utility trade-off and is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.

2.  **Significant and Surprising Results:** The specific findings are both technically deep and remarkably insightful.
    *   Proving the minimax optimality of Warner's randomized response is a beautiful and elegant result. It connects the very first work on survey privacy from 1965 to the cutting edge of privacy theory, providing a satisfying and definitive answer to a long-standing question.
    *   The discovery that local privacy alters the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a profound insight. It demonstrates that for complex, infinite-dimensional problems, the cost of privacy is not merely a reduction in "effective sample size" but a fundamental change in the problem's statistical character. This is a crucial, non-obvious finding that significantly advances our understanding.

3.  **Technical Sophistication:** The paper demonstrates a masterful command of the technical machinery from both statistics and theoretical computer science. The adaptation of Le Cam's and Fano's methods via novel information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) that cleanly incorporate the privacy parameter α is clever and powerful. The construction of optimal mechanisms, particularly the non-trivial orthogonal series estimator for density estimation, shows that the lower bounds are not artifacts of the proof technique but reflect real statistical barriers.

**Weaknesses**

The paper is overwhelmingly strong, and any weaknesses are minor in comparison to the magnitude of the contribution. The primary focus is on establishing a new theoretical direction, and in this, it succeeds completely.

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is appropriate given its foundational nature, some simple simulations illustrating the derived rates, especially for the more complex density estimation problem, could have helped build intuition. However, the work's value does not depend on experiments; it is defining the theoretical benchmarks that future empirical work should be measured against.

2.  **Complexity of Optimal Mechanisms:** The proposed sampling strategy for the orthogonal series estimator is somewhat complex. While its existence is crucial for proving the achievability of the lower bound, its direct practical applicability might be less immediate than simpler (though suboptimal) mechanisms like Laplace noise addition. This is less a flaw and more an interesting consequence of the theory: achieving optimality under strong privacy constraints may require non-obvious and sophisticated data randomization procedures.

3.  **Reliance on External Proofs:** Many of the detailed proofs are deferred to a longer manuscript [9]. This is standard practice for conference submissions but makes a complete, self-contained verification of the results challenging. Nevertheless, the presented proof sketches and the logic of the main theorems are clear and convincing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Establishes a novel and highly significant connection between minimax theory and local differential privacy, opening a new research direction.
*   **Pro:** Delivers deep, surprising, and fundamental results on the statistical cost of privacy for both parametric and nonparametric estimation.
*   **Pro:** The finding that Warner's classic randomized response is minimax optimal is an elegant and impactful result.
*   **Pro:** The paper is technically superb, demonstrating a sophisticated synthesis of advanced statistical and information-theoretic tools.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Con:** The work is purely theoretical with no experiments. (In my view, this is not a significant weakness for a paper of this type.)
*   **Con:** Some of the proposed optimal mechanisms are complex.

**Recommendation**

This is a landmark paper that I would enthusiastically champion for acceptance. It addresses a fundamental problem—the ultimate statistical cost of privacy—with a bold and highly original approach. The results are not incremental; they establish new, sharp limits and reveal surprising truths, such as the optimality of a 50-year-old technique and the fundamental change in convergence rates for nonparametric problems. This work has the clear potential to become a classic in the field, defining the conversation around private statistical inference for years to come. It perfectly embodies the kind of ambitious, concept-driven research that NIPS should highlight.
