**Summary:**
This paper provides a foundational minimax analysis of statistical estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. It forges a crucial link between the modern, computer-science-derived notion of differential privacy and the classical framework of statistical decision theory. The authors derive sharp, non-asymptotic minimax rates for two fundamental statistical problems: estimating the parameters of a multinomial distribution and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, they show that privacy incurs a penalty equivalent to reducing the sample size from `n` to `n * alpha^2 / d`, and remarkably demonstrate that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For density estimation of functions with `beta` derivatives, they establish a more dramatic result: local privacy changes the polynomial rate of convergence from the classical `n^(-2beta/(2beta+1))` to `(n * alpha^2)^(-2beta/(2beta+2))`. This reveals that the cost of privacy can be more severe than a mere reduction in effective sample size. For both problems, the authors complement their lower bounds with computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these optimal rates.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and conceptual significance. It masterfully bridges the gap between two major fields, providing a rigorous statistical decision-theoretic foundation for understanding privacy. Rather than proposing another ad-hoc private algorithm, this work asks a much deeper question: what are the fundamental limits of statistical inference under local privacy? The answers it provides are both elegant and insightful. This is exactly the kind of bold, foundational work that NIPS should champion.

2.  **Fundamental Theoretical Insights:** The results are not merely incremental; they are fundamental. The finding that local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a striking and important insight. It demonstrates that the "price of privacy" is not a universal constant but is problem-dependent, and in some cases, it can fundamentally alter the statistical complexity of a task. This is a profound contribution that will undoubtedly shape future research in this area.

3.  **Elegant Connection to Classical Work:** The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is, in fact, minimax optimal for multinomial estimation is a beautiful result. It provides a satisfying link between the historical origins of privacy in statistics and its modern, formal treatment under differential privacy, showing that the optimal solution has been known for decades, even if its optimality within this rigorous framework was not.

4.  **Completeness of Analysis:** The work is theoretically complete and convincing. The authors don't just provide lower bounds, which can sometimes be loose, but also provide matching upper bounds achieved by concrete, computationally efficient procedures. This "sandwiching" of the true minimax rate gives the results a great deal of authority and provides practitioners with clear, optimal strategies.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

1.  **Limited Practical/Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the proposed algorithms are described as "efficiently implementable," there is no empirical validation or discussion of their performance in practice. For instance, the orthogonal series estimator in Section 4.3 involves a somewhat complex sampling scheme (19) whose practical implementation and computational overhead are not explored. However, for a paper of this theoretical nature, the primary goal is to establish the existence of *some* optimal mechanism, which the authors succeed in doing. The lack of experiments is a minor omission given the strength and importance of the theoretical contribution.

2.  **Reliance on Companion Paper for Proofs:** Many of the detailed proofs are deferred to a longer companion paper [9]. While this is standard practice for conference submissions due to space constraints, it means that a full, self-contained verification of all technical claims is not possible from the manuscript alone.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation:**

This is an exceptional paper that represents a significant advance in our understanding of the interplay between privacy and statistical inference. Its main contribution is conceptual: it provides the definitive theoretical framework for analyzing the minimax costs of local privacy. The results are sharp, the insights are deep, and the connection forged between classical statistics and modern privacy is both novel and important.

While a different type of paper might be criticized for its lack of experiments, here it is not a major flaw. This work is about establishing fundamental limits, and it does so with rigor and elegance. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's core contribution. This work addresses a difficult and important problem, provides unique theoretical conclusions, and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. It is highly likely to be influential and inspire a great deal of follow-up research.

I strongly recommend acceptance, and I believe this paper is of sufficient quality and significance to be considered for an oral presentation.
