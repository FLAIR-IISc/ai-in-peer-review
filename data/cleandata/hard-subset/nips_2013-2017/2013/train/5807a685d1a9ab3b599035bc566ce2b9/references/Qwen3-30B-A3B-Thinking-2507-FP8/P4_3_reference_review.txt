

# Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

This paper establishes a theoretical framework for characterizing the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation problems. The authors connect minimax decision theory with differential privacy to derive sharp convergence rates for two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation under local differential privacy constraints.

The paper builds on Warner's classic work on randomized response [23] and Evfimievski et al.'s formulation of local differential privacy [13]. It extends the work of Duchi et al. [9] on statistical inference under differential privacy by providing a rigorous minimax analysis that characterizes the exact penalty for privacy constraints. The authors establish that for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, and demonstrate that Warner's randomized response procedure achieves this optimal rate. For density estimation, they show a different convergence rate (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) compared to the classical rate n^(-2β/(2β+1)), which is a significant departure from standard minimax results.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds. The authors provide a clear minimax framework (equation 2) and leverage information-theoretic tools (Proposition 1) to derive tight bounds. The connection between the privacy constraint (equation 1) and the statistical estimation problem is well-motivated, and the proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 are thorough. The paper demonstrates that the theoretical bounds are achievable with efficiently computable algorithms (equation 11, Propositions 2 and 3), which strengthens the technical contribution. The authors carefully address the strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly noting that direct perturbation of samples (e.g., Laplace noise) cannot achieve the optimal rates.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide clear definitions of all key concepts (local privacy, minimax framework, Sobolev spaces) and establish a logical flow from problem formulation to results. The connection between classical minimax theory and differential privacy is well-explained, with sufficient background for readers unfamiliar with either field. The notation is consistent and well-chosen, and the main results (Theorems 1 and 2) are stated precisely with clear implications. The paper is self-contained and provides enough detail for experts to reproduce the results.

**Novelty**
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy. The finding that Warner's randomized response procedure is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly novel and surprising, given that it was proposed nearly 50 years ago. The different convergence rate for density estimation (compared to classical results) represents a meaningful departure from standard minimax analysis. While the paper builds on prior work in differential privacy [9, 11, 24], it establishes new theoretical results that are distinct from previous NIPS papers. The paper thoroughly references related work, including Duchi et al.'s earlier work on statistical inference under differential privacy, and clearly articulates how this work advances the field.

**Significance**
The paper provides a foundational theoretical framework for understanding the privacy-utility trade-off in statistical estimation, with clear implications for survey design and privacy-preserving data analysis. The quantitative characterization of the penalty for privacy (effective sample size reduction from n to nα²/d) is valuable for practitioners. The results demonstrate that privacy constraints have more severe effects in nonparametric settings than in finite-dimensional problems, which is an important insight for researchers developing privacy-preserving statistical methods. The paper's theoretical framework can be extended to other statistical problems, as the authors suggest in the discussion, making it a significant contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical framework connecting minimax decision theory with differential privacy
- Rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds with matching rates
- Demonstrates optimality of Warner's randomized response for multinomial estimation
- Provides efficient algorithms achieving the theoretical bounds
- Establishes a novel convergence rate for density estimation under local privacy
- Well-written and well-organized with clear notation

**Weaknesses:**
- Focuses on specific problems (multinomial and density estimation) without broader discussion of applicability to other statistical problems
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges beyond theoretical efficiency
- Does not compare with other privacy models (e.g., central differential privacy)

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. It is well-motivated, technically sound, and provides sharp rates that are both interesting and useful for the field. The results are significant for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of privacy-preserving statistical methods. The paper is clearly in scope for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental theoretical problem at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy.
