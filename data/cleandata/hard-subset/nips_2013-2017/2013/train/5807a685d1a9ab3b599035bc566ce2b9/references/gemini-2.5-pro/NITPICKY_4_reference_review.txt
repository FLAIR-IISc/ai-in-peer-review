**Summary:**
This paper establishes a connection between the information-theoretic framework of local differential privacy and classical minimax decision theory. The authors aim to derive the fundamental limits on statistical estimation accuracy under local privacy constraints. The work focuses on two core probability estimation problems: (1) multinomial parameter estimation and (2) nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation over `d` categories, the authors show that `α`-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `n * α^2 / d`. They demonstrate that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For density estimation of functions with `β` derivatives, they show that local privacy changes the minimax rate of convergence from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(n * α^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, a more severe degradation. The paper presents lower bounds based on information-theoretic arguments and provides matching, computationally efficient upper bounds via specific private mechanisms.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a question of fundamental importance: quantifying the precise statistical cost of local privacy. Providing sharp minimax rates offers a principled way to understand and navigate the trade-off between privacy and utility. This work has the potential to be highly influential in both the privacy and statistics communities.
2.  **Novelty and Insight:** The primary results are novel and provide deep insights. The finding that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response is optimal in this modern framework is an elegant and powerful conclusion. The discovery that privacy induces a change in the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation, rather than just a constant factor penalty, is a significant and surprising theoretical contribution.
3.  **Strong Conceptual Framework:** The use of minimax decision theory provides a rigorous and powerful lens through which to analyze privacy-preserving algorithms. By focusing on population parameters rather than finite-sample quantities, the authors derive strong lower bounds that apply to any possible estimation procedure.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, despite its significant conceptual contributions, suffers from a substantial number of flaws in clarity, technical detail, and presentation that, in aggregate, undermine its quality.

1.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The paper's core theoretical results are not proven within the manuscript. The proofs for Proposition 1 (the key information bounds) and Theorem 2 (the density estimation lower bound) are deferred to the authors' own unpublished arXiv preprint [9]. This makes a proper review of the paper's central claims impossible. A submission to a top-tier conference must be self-contained and allow for the verification of its results.

2.  **Critical Methodological Ambiguity and Errors:**
    *   **Multinomial Mechanism:** The description of the randomized response mechanism in Section 3.2 appears to be contradictory. Equation (10) defines `[Z]j` to take values `x_j` or `-x_j`. Since `x_j` is either 0 or 1, `[Z]j` can be -1. However, the text explicitly states that `Z ∈ {0, 1}^d`. This is a direct contradiction that makes the mechanism and the subsequent analysis unclear and potentially incorrect.
    *   **Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The sampling strategy in Section 4.3 is opaquely described. The constant `ck` in Equation (20) is vaguely defined as "O(1) and bounded away from 0," which is not a precise definition. Since the choice of the crucial parameter `B` depends on `ck`, the entire procedure is not reproducible. Furthermore, the claim that the mechanism is "efficiently computable" via rejection sampling is unsubstantiated, lacking any analysis of the acceptance probability.

3.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:**
    *   The manuscript contains several instances of imprecise or incorrect writing. For example, a sentence in Section 3.2 is an incomplete fragment: "we abuse notation and represent samples x as either when designing estimation strategies."
    *   Notation is at times confusing. `X_j` is used to denote both a partition set and a random variable. The notation `an . bn` for `O(bn)` is non-standard. The use of an index `i` on the distributions `P_{ν,i}` in Proposition 1 is confusing in an i.i.d. setting and could be simplified.

4.  **Poor Manuscript Preparation:** The paper exhibits a general lack of care. The bibliography is in a state of disarray, with numerous references being incomplete (e.g., [2], [4], [7], [11], [13], [16], [26]), missing essential information like publication venues or years, and containing formatting errors (e.g., double commas in [2]). This level of carelessness is not acceptable for a formal submission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a very important and timely problem.
*   The high-level ideas and main results (assuming they are correct) are of significant interest and advance the state of the art in private statistical analysis.
*   The connection drawn between classical statistics and modern privacy is elegant and insightful.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is not self-contained, preventing verification of its main theoretical claims. This is a fatal flaw.
*   The descriptions of the proposed algorithms, which are central to the attainability results, contain critical ambiguities and apparent contradictions.
*   The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of minor errors in writing, notation, and formatting, which collectively suggest a rushed submission and fall below the quality standards of NIPS.

**Recommendation:**
While the ideas presented in this paper are promising and potentially of high impact, the execution is severely lacking. The lack of self-contained proofs is, on its own, grounds for rejection. This, combined with the critical ambiguities in the proposed methods and the overall poor preparation of the manuscript, leads me to recommend **rejection**. The authors should be encouraged to substantially revise the paper to address these issues—by including proofs (perhaps in an appendix), carefully and precisely defining their algorithms, and meticulously proofreading the text and references—before resubmitting to a future venue.
