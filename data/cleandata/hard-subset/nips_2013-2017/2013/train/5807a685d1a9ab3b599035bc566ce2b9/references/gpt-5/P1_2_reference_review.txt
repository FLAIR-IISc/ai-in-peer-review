Summary
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), unifying classical minimax decision theory with the formal privacy guarantees of differential privacy. The setting is non-interactive (memoryless channels): each user privatizes their datum Xi via an α-LDP channel Q to produce Zi, and the statistician estimates population parameters from Z1:n. The authors develop information-contraction bounds under LDP (via KL/TV and mutual information inequalities) to derive sharp minimax lower bounds, and provide matching upper bounds via efficient privatization/estimation schemes.

Two problems are treated:

- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, they show the mean-squared and ℓ1 minimax risks under α-LDP scale as min{1, d/(nα2)} and min{1, d/√(nα2)}, respectively, for α ∈ [0,1]. Thus, LDP reduces the effective sample size from n to nα2/d. They further show that (a slightly modified) randomized response achieves the optimal rates, as do simple Laplace-perturbed count schemes, hence recovering Warner’s classic mechanism as minimax-optimal under LDP.

- Density estimation on [0,1] in Sobolev classes Fβ: The non-private minimax L2 risk scales as n−2β/(2β+1). Under α-LDP, the lower bound changes the exponent to (α2 n)−2β/(2β+2), exhibiting a genuine degradation in the polynomial rate (e.g., β=1 degrades from n−2/3 to n−1/2). They provide matching, implementable estimators: (i) for β=1, a privatized histogram with Laplace noise on bin indicators followed by projection; (ii) for β≥1, an orthogonal series estimator coupled with a tailored privatization scheme that yields unbiased privatized basis evaluations and achieves the optimal rate.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects Warner’s randomized response with modern LDP, and leverages differential privacy literature (Dwork et al.; Hardt–Talwar; Wasserman–Zhou; Kasiviswanathan et al.). The information bounds invoked are from the authors’ prior work [9], and here are specialized to concrete estimation problems with sharp rates and optimal mechanisms. The deconvolution barrier is used to argue suboptimality of naively adding Laplace noise to raw samples.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds follow standard minimax reductions (Le Cam/Fano) paired with LDP-specific information contraction (KL and mutual information bounds). The upper bounds are constructive and computationally efficient. The multinomial analysis is clean, and the histogram/series estimators in the density case are carefully designed to respect LDP and achieve the target bias-variance tradeoff.
- Rigor/completeness: Many proofs are deferred to [9]; while acceptable for space, including brief proof sketches (especially for the mutual information variational bound and the key steps in Propositions 2–3) would strengthen the submission. Assumptions (non-interactive LDP, α ∈ [0,1], bounded basis functions) are clearly stated.
- Minor issues:
  - Equation (10): Sampling “−xj” when xj ∈ {0,1} yields values in {0,−1}, yet Z is said to be in {0,1}d and later treated as Bernoulli. This likely should be “1−xj.” Please clarify/correct.
  - The variance bound for Z is quoted tersely; a short derivation or appendix reference would help.
  - The constant c_k in (20) and how it depends on k is not fully explained; giving a concrete bound (and its effect on B) would improve readability.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized, with a clear minimax framework, problem statements, and main theorems. The notation is standard and the narrative is coherent. Suggestions:
- Provide brief proof sketches of Theorems 1–2 and Propositions 2–3 to make the submission more self-contained.
- Add intuition about why LDP changes the nonparametric rate exponent (information contraction per sample under LDP) to help readers.
- Explicitly contrast non-interactive vs interactive LDP (the paper focuses on non-interactive) and comment on whether interactivity could improve rates.

Originality
The work offers new, sharp minimax characterizations under LDP for both multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, including the identification of optimal privatization mechanisms. The change in nonparametric rate exponent under LDP is a notable and novel insight. While the information bounds build on the authors’ prior work, their application to derive tight rates and mechanisms in these canonical problems is original and significant.

Significance
The results articulate a precise privacy–utility trade-off: an effective sample size penalty for finite-dimensional problems and a fundamental degradation of rates in nonparametrics. These findings are likely to influence both theory and practice in private data analysis, guiding mechanism design (e.g., validating randomized response; discouraging naive sample perturbation for density estimation) and setting benchmarks for future work. The paper fits well within NIPS’s theoretical ML scope.

Pros
- Sharp, matching minimax lower/upper bounds under LDP for central estimation problems.
- Clear identification of optimal mechanisms (randomized response; tailored series privatization).
- Conceptually important finding: privacy can change nonparametric rate exponents.
- Methods are computationally efficient and practically interpretable.

Cons
- Reliance on an external technical report [9] for key proofs; limited proof detail in the submission.
- Non-interactive LDP only; no discussion of potential gains from interactivity.
- Minor clarity issues/typos (e.g., randomized response mechanism definition).
- No empirical illustration; small simulations could help confirm constants and practical performance.

Questions for authors
- Can any interactive LDP protocol improve the exponents or only constants? Are there lower bounds ruling out gains from interactivity in these problems?
- Is adaptive selection of k (bins or series truncation) under LDP possible while retaining optimal rates?
- How robust are the mechanisms to misspecification of α or to heavy-tailed basis functions?

Minor comments/typos
- Eq. (10): likely “1−xj” instead of “−xj”; reconcile domain of Z.
- Define Πk and kΔk when first used; ensure consistent notation for norms in Mn(·, ·, α).
- Proposition 2: clarify the role of the second term √α n−3/4 and when it is dominated.

Recommendation
Accept. This paper delivers rigorous, sharp, and practically relevant results on minimax estimation under local differential privacy, advancing the theoretical foundations and informing mechanism design. Despite minor clarity issues and reliance on an external technical report for proofs, the contribution is strong and of broad interest to the NIPS community.
