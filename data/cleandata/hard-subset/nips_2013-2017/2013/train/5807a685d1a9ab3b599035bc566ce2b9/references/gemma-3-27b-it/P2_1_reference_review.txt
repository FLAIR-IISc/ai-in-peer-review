## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, bridging differential privacy with minimax decision theory. The authors provide sharp minimax lower and upper bounds for estimating multinomial probabilities and density functions under the constraint of α-local differential privacy.  They demonstrate a reduction in effective sample size for multinomial estimation and a change in the rate of convergence for density estimation compared to the non-private setting. Notably, they show Warner's randomized response procedure is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation and develop efficient algorithms achieving optimal rates for both problems.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in differential privacy (Dwork et al., Chaudhuri et al.) and minimax statistical estimation (Tsybakov, van der Vaart). It connects these fields, offering a rigorous theoretical treatment of privacy-preserving statistical inference, something previously lacking. The work extends their prior work (Duchi et al. [9]) by applying the developed divergence bounds to specific estimation problems.  It also draws connections to Warner’s early work on randomized response [23], positioning it within a long history of privacy-preserving statistics.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous minimax lower and upper bounds, firmly establishing the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving probability estimation. The use of local differential privacy and minimax theory is a strong and appropriate combination.
*   **Sharp Results:**  The derived convergence rates are sharp, demonstrating the optimality of certain estimation procedures (e.g., randomized response). This is a significant contribution, providing a clear understanding of the trade-offs involved.
*   **Practical Implications:** The results have clear implications for the design of privacy-preserving statistical systems, guiding the choice of estimation methods and the allocation of privacy budgets.
*   **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, logically structured, and provides sufficient background information. The notation is clearly defined.
*   **Comprehensive Coverage:** The paper addresses two important problems - multinomial estimation and density estimation – showcasing the applicability of the theoretical framework.
* **Efficient Algorithms:** The authors provide efficiently implementable algorithms that attain the optimal rates, demonstrating the practical relevance of the theory.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Density:** The paper is mathematically demanding and may be inaccessible to readers without a strong background in statistical decision theory and differential privacy. While clarity is generally good, some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:**  While density estimation is covered, the analysis focuses on specific Sobolev classes. Exploring other density classes or more general settings could broaden the applicability of the results.
* **Assumptions on Orthonormal Basis:** The analysis in the density estimation section relies heavily on the properties of the chosen orthonormal basis.  More discussion of how sensitive the results are to the choice of basis would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks empirical evaluation. Demonstrating the performance of the proposed estimators in simulations would strengthen the practical impact of the work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in privacy-preserving statistical inference.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous, sharp, and provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between privacy and utility.
*   The work is well-connected to existing literature and builds upon previous research in a significant way.
*   The efficient algorithms offered provide practical guidance for implementing privacy-preserving estimation procedures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The high level of technical detail may limit the paper’s accessibility to a broad audience.
*   The lack of empirical evaluation leaves open questions about the practical performance of the proposed estimators.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results are rigorous, sharp, and have clear implications for the design of practical systems. While the paper is technically demanding and could benefit from empirical validation, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:** **Strong Accept**. This paper is well-suited for NIPS given its theoretical rigor, novel results, and potential impact on the field.  I encourage the authors to consider adding a brief empirical validation section in a future version to further strengthen the work.
