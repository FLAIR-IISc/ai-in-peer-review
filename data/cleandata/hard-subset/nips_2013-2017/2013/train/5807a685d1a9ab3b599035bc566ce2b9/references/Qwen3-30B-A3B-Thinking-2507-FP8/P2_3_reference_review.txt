

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary
This paper makes a significant contribution by bridging minimax decision theory with local differential privacy to establish sharp rates for probability estimation under privacy constraints. The authors provide two main results: (1) for multinomial estimation, they show the effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy, and prove Warner's randomized response is optimal; (2) for density estimation, they demonstrate a fundamental change in convergence rates from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) under local privacy, along with efficient algorithms achieving these rates. The paper builds on foundational work in differential privacy (Dwork et al. [11, 9]) and minimax theory (Tsybakov [26], Yu [27]), but makes novel theoretical connections between these fields.

## Quality
The paper is technically rigorous and well-executed. The authors establish tight minimax lower bounds using clever applications of Le Cam's two-point method and Fano's inequality, complemented by matching upper bounds via carefully constructed algorithms. The proofs are detailed and well-motivated, with clear connections between the information-theoretic bounds and the statistical estimation problems. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., noting that Laplace perturbation fails for density estimation) and provide thorough comparisons to prior work. The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution, not merely a position paper.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by connecting Warner's early work to modern differential privacy. The formalism is introduced precisely and consistently throughout. The main results are stated with appropriate mathematical precision, and the connection between the theoretical bounds and practical algorithms is particularly well-explained. The exposition of the randomized response mechanism and its optimality is especially clear. The notation is consistent and well-defined, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with minimax theory and differential privacy.

## Originality
This paper makes several novel contributions. The connection between minimax theory and local differential privacy is new, providing a theoretical framework for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs. The density estimation result is particularly original—the change in the convergence rate exponent from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2) under local privacy is a fundamental theoretical insight not present in prior work. The paper also establishes the optimality of Warner's randomized response (a 50-year-old technique) for multinomial estimation, which is a nice historical and theoretical insight. The authors' demonstration that standard Laplace perturbation fails for density estimation under local privacy, and the development of novel sampling strategies to achieve optimal rates, represents significant methodological innovation.

## Significance
The results have substantial significance for both theory and practice. The paper establishes fundamental limits on statistical estimation under privacy constraints, providing practitioners with precise understanding of the price of privacy. The results for multinomial estimation are particularly important for survey design, where Warner's randomized response is commonly used. The density estimation results reveal a more severe penalty for privacy than previously understood, which has implications for applications like private nonparametric statistics. The paper provides optimal algorithms for both problems, making the theoretical results immediately applicable. The work advances the state of the art in privacy-preserving statistics by establishing sharp rates for fundamental problems.

## Strengths
- Establishes tight minimax bounds for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy
- Demonstrates optimality of Warner's randomized response (a historical technique) for multinomial estimation
- Provides novel theoretical insight into density estimation under local privacy (rate changes from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2))
- Develops efficient algorithms achieving the theoretical bounds
- Connects foundational work in differential privacy with minimax decision theory

## Weaknesses
The paper is exceptionally strong, but one minor weakness is that it focuses exclusively on the local privacy model (where each individual's data is perturbed before being shared with the statistician). While this is a natural starting point, the authors might briefly discuss how their results might extend to the central privacy model (where a trusted curator applies privacy mechanisms to the entire dataset), which is more common in practice.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistics. It provides sharp rates for fundamental estimation problems under local privacy, connects two important areas of research (minimax theory and differential privacy), and offers practical algorithms. The results are both theoretically elegant and practically relevant. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear advance over prior work. I strongly recommend acceptance.

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, as it advances our theoretical understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in a fundamental way. The results have clear implications for practitioners designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures, and the theoretical framework established could be extended to other problems in privacy-preserving statistics. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level.
