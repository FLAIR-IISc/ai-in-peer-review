### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of minimax decision theory. The authors focus on the setting of α-local differential privacy (LDP), where each individual's data is privatized before being sent to a central aggregator. They analyze two canonical statistical problems: (1) parametric estimation of a d-dimensional multinomial probability vector and (2) nonparametric estimation of a probability density function belonging to a Sobolev smoothness class.

The main contribution is the derivation of sharp, non-asymptotic minimax rates of convergence for these problems under LDP constraints. For multinomial estimation, the authors show that the minimax squared error scales as `d/(nα^2)`, which can be interpreted as a reduction of the effective sample size from `n` to `nα^2/d`. They demonstrate that a modified version of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal.

For the more challenging problem of nonparametric density estimation over a class of functions with `β` derivatives, the paper reveals a more severe penalty for privacy. The classical minimax rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` is shown to degrade to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This is not merely a change in the constant factor but a fundamental change in the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate. The authors provide constructive and computationally efficient procedures—a private histogram for `β=1` and a novel orthogonal series estimator for general `β>1`—that achieve these new optimal rates. The technical approach for the lower bounds relies on information-theoretic inequalities (Fano's and Le Cam's methods) coupled with novel bounds on the KL-divergence and mutual information under local privacy constraints, which are leveraged from the authors' companion work.

### Strengths

1.  **Fundamental and Significant Contribution:** The paper addresses a foundational question at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy: what is the ultimate statistical price of ensuring local differential privacy? By providing sharp minimax rates, the authors give a precise, quantitative answer for two central problems in probability estimation. This work elegantly connects the classic ideas of Warner [23] with the modern, rigorous framework of differential privacy and statistical decision theory.

2.  **High Technical Quality and Completeness:** The work is technically very strong. It provides both lower bounds (what is impossible) and matching upper bounds (what is achievable with explicit algorithms). This "sharp" characterization is the gold standard for theoretical work of this nature. The results are clean, interpretable, and insightful.

3.  **Novel and Surprising Results:** The result for density estimation is particularly striking. The fact that local privacy changes the exponent of the convergence rate is a non-obvious and important finding. It demonstrates that the impact of privacy can be more profound than a simple reduction in effective sample size. Furthermore, the paper correctly points out that naive privacy mechanisms, such as adding Laplace noise directly to the data points, would lead to sub-optimal rates, highlighting the necessity of designing bespoke mechanisms for specific statistical tasks. The proposed orthogonal series mechanism is a novel and non-trivial construction to achieve the optimal rate.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins with a clear motivation, precisely defines the problem setting, and cleanly separates the results for the two main problems. The background section provides the necessary context on the minimax framework, making the paper relatively self-contained for a reader familiar with statistical theory.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on an External Paper for Key Lemmas:** The proofs of the lower bounds hinge on Proposition 1, which provides crucial information-theoretic bounds. These results are cited from an arXiv preprint by the same authors [9]. While this is common practice, it means that the full technical machinery for the lower bounds is not presented in this paper, making a full technical verification reliant on an external (non-peer-reviewed) source. This is a minor weakness, as the main conceptual contribution lies in the application of these tools to derive the final rates.

2.  **Complexity of the Optimal Mechanism for Density Estimation:** The sampling strategy (19) for the orthogonal series estimator is clever but also quite complex compared to more standard mechanisms like randomized response or the Laplace mechanism. While it is shown to be optimal and efficiently implementable, a brief discussion on its practical feasibility or potential challenges in implementation (e.g., the efficiency of the uniform sampling step for large `k`) would have strengthened the paper.

3.  **Limited Scope of `α`:** The analysis and results are primarily focused on the small `α` regime (explicitly `α ∈ [0, 1]` in the theorems). While this is often the most interesting regime for privacy, the behavior for large `α` is not fully characterized, though the rates are correctly capped by the non-private risk.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant and lasting contribution to our understanding of private statistical inference. It tackles a fundamental problem with rigor and clarity, delivering sharp, insightful, and somewhat surprising results. The work bridges classical statistical theory with modern computer science concepts of privacy in a compelling way. The strengths—particularly the significance of the problem, the sharpness of the results, and the clarity of the exposition—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is a perfect fit for NIPS and is likely to be highly influential, inspiring further research into minimax optimal private estimation for other statistical models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem.
*   Provides sharp, matching upper and lower bounds (minimax optimal rates).
*   Reveals a novel and important phenomenon: privacy can change the polynomial convergence rate for nonparametric problems.
*   Constructs novel and efficient algorithms that achieve the optimal rates.
*   Exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core technical lemmas for the lower bounds are cited from a companion paper, making the work not entirely self-contained.
*   The practical implementation of the optimal mechanism for high-smoothness density estimation could be complex.

**Recommendation: Accept.** This paper represents a high-quality, complete, and significant piece of theoretical research that advances the state of the art in privacy-preserving machine learning.
