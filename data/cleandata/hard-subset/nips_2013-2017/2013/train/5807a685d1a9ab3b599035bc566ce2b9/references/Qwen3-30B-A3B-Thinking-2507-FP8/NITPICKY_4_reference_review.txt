

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, bridging differential privacy with statistical decision theory. The authors establish sharp lower and upper bounds for multinomial and density estimation problems, showing that local privacy imposes a fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation and deriving a novel convergence rate for density estimation under local privacy.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis that effectively bridges differential privacy and minimax decision theory.
- The results for multinomial estimation are particularly strong, with the authors establishing that Warner's randomized response achieves the optimal rate.
- The density estimation results are novel, showing a different convergence rate than classical non-private settings (n^(-2β/(2β+1)) vs. (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical Notation Error in Privacy Definition:** Equation (1) defines α-local differential privacy as "sup { Q(S | Xi = x) Q(S | Xi = x′) | ... } ≤ exp(α)". This is fundamentally incorrect - the definition should involve the ratio Q(S|Xi=x)/Q(S|Xi=x'), not the product. This critical error undermines the entire theoretical foundation of the paper and must be corrected immediately.

2. **Incorrect Derivation of Randomized Response:** In the derivation of the expected value for the randomized response mechanism (equation 10), the paper claims E[Z | x] = e^(α/2)/(e^(α/2)+1) x + 1/(1+e^(α/2)) 1. However, the correct calculation yields E[Z_j | x] = x_j (e^(α/2) - 1)/(e^(α/2) + 1), which does not match the paper's expression. This error invalidates the optimality claim for Warner's mechanism.

3. **Typographical Error:** In Proposition 2, the bound (17) states "5(α2n)− 12" which should be "5(α²n)^(-1/2)". The exponent is missing the slash and should be -1/2, not -12.

4. **Insufficient Justification for Claims:** The claim that "no estimator based on Laplace perturbation can attain the rate (14)" is not fully supported. The reference to Carroll and Hall [4] discusses deconvolution, which is a different problem than the one considered here. The authors need to provide a more direct justification for this claim.

5. **Inconsistent Notation:** The paper uses "d" for dimensionality in some places (e.g., "d-dimensional multinomial") and "k" for dimensionality in others (e.g., "k∆k" in section 4.2), creating confusion for readers.

6. **Incomplete Citation:** The paper cites Duchi et al. [9] but provides only the arXiv version without full publication details. A complete citation should include the journal or conference where the work was published.

7. **Missing Key Related Work:** The paper does not cite the seminal work by Kasiviswanathan et al. [17] on "What can we learn privately," which is highly relevant to the topic of private statistical estimation.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper addresses an important problem with promising results, it contains critical theoretical errors that undermine its main claims. The fundamental mistake in the definition of local differential privacy (equation 1) is particularly severe, as it invalidates the entire theoretical framework. The incorrect derivation for the randomized response mechanism further weakens the paper's optimality claims. These errors, combined with the typographical issues and inconsistent notation, make the paper difficult to trust.

The topic is important and the general approach is promising, but the paper as written does not present a sound technical contribution. The authors should correct these fundamental errors before considering publication.

**Recommendation: Rejection**

The paper's main theoretical claims are undermined by critical errors, and the minor flaws compound the issue. While the topic is significant, the paper does not present a technically sound contribution in its current form.
