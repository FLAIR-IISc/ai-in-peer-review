## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy (specifically, local differential privacy) and statistical efficiency in probability estimation. It builds upon recent work connecting differential privacy with minimax decision theory, providing sharp upper and lower bounds on the convergence rates for estimating multinomial probabilities and nonparametric densities under local privacy constraints. The authors demonstrate that local privacy incurs a penalty on sample complexity, reducing the effective sample size in multinomial estimation and altering the convergence rate in density estimation. Interestingly, they show that Warner's randomized response procedure is optimal for multinomial estimation and provide efficiently implementable algorithms to achieve the derived bounds.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper effectively connects several strands of research: Warner's early work on privacy in surveys, the development of differential privacy in computer science, and minimax optimal estimation theory in statistics. It builds upon the authors' previous work [9] establishing information-theoretic bounds for privacy-preserving inference and extends these results to specific statistical problems.  It also acknowledges connections to work on privacy-preserving data analysis (e.g., Chaudhuri et al., Dwork et al.) and nonparametric estimation (e.g., Tsybakov). The authors appropriately position their work within the broader literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a strong theoretical foundation, employing minimax optimality arguments and information-theoretic bounds to derive precise convergence rates. The proofs (referenced to [9]) seem solid.
* **Sharp Results:** The derived bounds are shown to be tight, meaning the proposed estimators achieve optimal performance. This is a significant accomplishment.
* **Practical Implications:** The analysis not only provides theoretical insights but also identifies optimal mechanisms (like randomized response) for practical implementation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions.  The notation is standard and well-defined.
* **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The trade-off between privacy and utility is central to many real-world applications, making this a highly relevant and impactful research area.
* **Novelty**: The combination of differential privacy with minimax theory to provide *sharp* rates for probability estimation problems appears novel, especially the altered convergence rate observed in density estimation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:**  The technical details and reliance on background knowledge from multiple fields (statistics, information theory, cryptography) may make the paper inaccessible to readers not already familiar with these areas. While the authors do attempt to provide necessary background, it could be expanded in certain areas (e.g. the Sobolev space definition).
* **Density Estimation Practicality:** While the authors demonstrate achievable rates for density estimation, the practical implementation of the orthogonal series estimator with the proposed privacy mechanism might be complex and computationally demanding. More discussion of computational costs would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** The density estimation results are focused on smoothness classes defined using a specific orthonormal basis. Exploring the robustness of the results to different bases or alternative smoothness assumptions would strengthen the work.
* **Discussion of other privacy mechanisms**: The paper mainly focusses on Laplace perturbation but doesn't offer a comprehensive discussion about other potential mechanisms or their drawbacks.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* The results are sharp and provide a clear understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.
* The identified optimal mechanisms have practical implications for real-world applications.
* The paper is well-written and rigorously analyzed.
* The originality of combining minimax analysis with local differential privacy is strong.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical complexity might limit the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.
* The practical implementation of the density estimation scheme could be challenging.
*  The scope of the density estimation results could be broadened.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The theoretical rigor, sharpness of the results, and practical implications outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.  The authors convincingly demonstrate that this work is significantly different from previous contributions and advances the state of the art.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I would suggest the authors consider adding a section discussing potential computational challenges of the proposed density estimation method and perhaps explore the implications of different basis choices.  Also, a more detailed explanation of the Sobolev spaces would be helpful for readers less familiar with this concept.
