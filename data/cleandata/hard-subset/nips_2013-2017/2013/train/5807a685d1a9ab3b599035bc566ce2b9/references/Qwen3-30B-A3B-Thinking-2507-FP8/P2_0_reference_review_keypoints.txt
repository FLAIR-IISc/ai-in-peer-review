- Studies trade-offs between local differential privacy and statistical utility, giving sharp rates for multinomial and nonparametric density estimation.  
- Shows multinomial effective sample size drops from n to n·α²/d; density estimation rate changes from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to n^{-2β/(2β+2)}.  
- Provides rigorous matching lower and upper minimax bounds under local privacy with well-structured proofs.  
- Properly distinguishes population-parameter estimation from sample-quantity estimation and strengthens prior lower bounds.  
- Presents concrete, efficiently computable algorithms (randomized response, histogram, orthogonal series) that attain the rates.  
- Exceptionally clear writing and organization, with precise theorems and accessible proof sketches; connects Warner’s randomized response to modern DP.  
- Original: characterizes how local privacy fundamentally alters minimax rates and shows randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation.  
- Extends prior work (e.g., Duchi et al.) by deriving sharp rates for specific estimation problems rather than general mechanisms.  
- Significant: first sharp characterization of privacy–utility trade-offs for core estimation tasks, informing design of privacy-preserving methods.  
- Weaknesses: limited to local privacy setting; lacks discussion of extensions to central privacy and more on practical implications of quantitative trade-offs.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept—high originality, technical quality, and significance.  
- Pro/Con summary: Pro—rigorous results and historical-to-modern connection; Con—restricted to local privacy and minimal practical discussion.
