

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper makes a significant contribution by bridging minimax decision theory with differential privacy to establish sharp convergence rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors tackle two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation, providing both lower and upper bounds under α-local differential privacy.

The most striking contribution is the demonstration that local privacy fundamentally changes the statistical trade-offs in estimation. For multinomial estimation, the paper shows that the effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d, and surprisingly proves that Warner's randomized response (proposed nearly 50 years ago) achieves this optimal rate. For density estimation, the paper reveals a more severe effect: the convergence rate changes from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)), which is a qualitatively different polynomial rate than classical results. This is a bold theoretical insight that fundamentally changes our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs.

The paper's novelty lies in its theoretical approach to connecting these two domains (minimax theory and differential privacy) to establish sharp bounds rather than just asymptotic rates. The authors provide a clean theoretical framework that characterizes the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation, which is a significant step beyond previous work that focused on constructing specific private algorithms without establishing tight bounds.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results and practical implications. The mathematical treatment is rigorous, and the authors carefully distinguish their work from prior contributions (notably Duchi et al. [9]). The connection to Warner's randomized response is particularly elegant, demonstrating that a 50-year-old method is actually optimal under local privacy constraints.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility, which is critical for practitioners designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The results are not just incremental improvements but represent a new theoretical lens through which to view privacy-preserving statistics. The paper also identifies a key insight that direct perturbation of samples (e.g., Laplace noise) is suboptimal for density estimation under local privacy, suggesting the need for more sophisticated privacy mechanisms.

While the paper is theoretically rigorous, it does not include empirical validation. However, given the nature of the theoretical results and the fact that the authors provide efficiently computable algorithms that achieve the bounds, this omission is acceptable for a theoretical paper at NIPS. The paper is complete in its theoretical contribution and carefully evaluates its own strengths and limitations.

The paper addresses a gap in the literature by providing a formal treatment of disclosure risk that permits decision-theoretic tools to characterize trade-offs between privacy and utility. Previous work on differential privacy focused on constructing specific private algorithms without establishing tight bounds on the fundamental limits, whereas this paper establishes these limits as a foundation for future work.

In terms of novelty, this paper significantly advances the field by introducing a minimax framework for privacy-preserving statistical inference. While there is related work on differential privacy and statistical inference (e.g., Duchi et al. [9]), this paper is the first to establish sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, which is a major theoretical contribution.

The paper's main weakness is that it doesn't explore extensions to more complex statistical models (e.g., regression, classification), but this is understandable given the focused theoretical contribution. The paper is not attempting to be comprehensive but rather to establish a foundational theoretical result.

This paper stands out for its theoretical depth, elegance, and significant contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistics. It provides a new framework for analyzing privacy-utility trade-offs that will likely influence future work in the field. The results are not just incremental but represent a significant conceptual advance in our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference.

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper's theoretical novelty and significance far outweigh any minor limitations in empirical validation, which is not expected for a theoretical contribution of this nature. The work provides a new lens through which to view privacy-preserving statistical inference and establishes fundamental limits that will guide future research in this area.
