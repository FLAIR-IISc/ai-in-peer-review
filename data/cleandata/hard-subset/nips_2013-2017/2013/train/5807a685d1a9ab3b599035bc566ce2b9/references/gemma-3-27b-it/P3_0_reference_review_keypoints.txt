- Overall: Strong theoretical contribution giving sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation under local differential privacy; recommend accept with minor revisions.

- Summary & related work: Studies probability estimation under α-local differential privacy using minimax decision theory to derive tight lower/upper bounds; situates work among classical DP and minimax literature and links to Warner’s randomized response and the authors’ prior results.

- Strength: Strong theoretical foundation—minimax framework yields rigorous, sharp bounds on privacy–accuracy trade-offs.

- Strength: Novel and significant results, notably differing convergence rates in density estimation under local privacy.

- Strength: Clear, well-organized presentation with consistent notation and self-contained exposition.

- Strength: Identification of optimal mechanisms—randomized response is rate-optimal for multinomial estimation; development of privately-optimal density estimators.

- Strength: Comprehensive analysis covering both multinomial and density estimation, demonstrating generality.

- Weakness: Highly theoretical focus with limited exploration of practical implications, algorithms’ computational complexity, and scalability.

- Weakness: Dependence on assumptions (e.g., bounded derivatives in density estimation) without discussion of sensitivity to those assumptions.

- Weakness: Limited empirical validation—no experiments to demonstrate practical performance of proposed estimators.

- Weakness: Introduction is somewhat verbose and could be more concise.

- Detailed comment: Provide an intuitive explanation for why effective sample size decreases by a factor of α^2/d.

- Detailed comment: Clarify the Laplace noise analysis—how the choice of noise scale B affects convergence rates.

- Detailed comment: Briefly remind readers of the meaning of the notation “≍” when used frequently.

- Arguments for acceptance: Rigorous proofs, significant advancement in understanding privacy–utility trade-offs, clear presentation, and demonstration of optimality of existing techniques.

- Arguments against acceptance: Primarily theoretical nature, lack of empirical validation, and reliance on possibly restrictive assumptions.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—add discussion of computational complexity, sensitivity to assumptions, and a small-scale empirical evaluation.
