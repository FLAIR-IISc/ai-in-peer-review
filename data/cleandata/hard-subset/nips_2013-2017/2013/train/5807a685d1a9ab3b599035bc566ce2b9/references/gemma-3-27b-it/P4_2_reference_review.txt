This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, specifically revisiting Warner’s original randomized response problem and extending it to nonparametric density estimation within the framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors establish minimax convergence rates under local privacy constraints, demonstrating a quantifiable penalty for privacy. The work builds upon prior research in differential privacy [11, 1, 13, 9] and classical minimax theory [26, 25, 22] and aims to bridge the gap between theoretical privacy guarantees and statistical utility.

The main contributions are sharp lower and upper bounds on the minimax risk for multinomial estimation and density estimation under α-local privacy. For multinomial estimation, they demonstrate an effective sample size reduction of n to nα²/d, and show that Warner's randomized response achieves this optimal rate. For density estimation, they establish a different rate of convergence than that attained in the non-private case, and provide efficiently implementable algorithms that attain sharp upper bounds.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation by leveraging differential privacy, minimax theory, and information-theoretic bounds. The derivation of the minimax rates is well-motivated and technically sound.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of differential privacy with minimax analysis to quantify the privacy-utility trade-off is a novel contribution. The identification of a differing convergence rate for density estimation under privacy constraints is particularly insightful.
*   **Optimality Results:** Demonstrating the optimality of Warner's randomized response is a valuable and practical finding. The development of efficient algorithms that match the lower bounds enhances the paper's impact.
*   **Clarity (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written, with a clear introduction and logical flow. The notation is mostly standard and well-defined.
* **Comprehensive Background:** The inclusion of background material on minimax theory and relevant prior work (e.g. [9]) facilitates understanding for readers unfamiliar with all aspects of the topic.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density Estimation Complexity:** While the results for density estimation are novel, the discussion and implementation details of the proposed estimators appear less fleshed out compared to the multinomial case. The explanation of the sampling strategy (19) and its connection to the optimal rate could be more intuitive.  The justification for the choice of B appears ad-hoc.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results. Some limited empirical validation, even on simple examples, would strengthen the claims and demonstrate the practical implications of the trade-offs.
* **Notation Overload:** The paper uses a significant amount of notation, some of which (e.g. the various rate notations like ‘an . bn’) is not strictly necessary and may slightly hinder readability.
* **Referencing could be improved:** While the references are relevant, some key papers in nonparametric statistics (e.g., those on kernel density estimation) could be included to provide a more complete context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental and timely problem in privacy-preserving data analysis.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous, novel, and provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between privacy and utility.
*   The optimality results for Warner's randomized response are significant and have practical implications.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The density estimation results, while novel, are less detailed and could benefit from further elaboration and potentially empirical validation.
*   The paper is highly theoretical and lacks empirical support to demonstrate the practical relevance of the findings.
*   The abundance of notation slightly complicates the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. While the density estimation part could be more detailed and empirical validation is lacking, the overall quality of the work is high, and the results are novel and impactful. I believe the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
