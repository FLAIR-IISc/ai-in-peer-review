This paper tackles the fundamental problem of probability estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy, bridging classical statistical decision theory with modern privacy notions. The authors revisit Warner's original motivation for privacy – protecting respondent confidentiality – and provide rigorous theoretical guarantees on the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper establishes minimax lower and upper bounds for multinomial and density estimation, demonstrating optimal rates for Warner’s randomized response procedure and deriving new rates for density estimation under local privacy.

The strengths of this work are numerous. Firstly, it addresses a significant and increasingly relevant problem. The need for privacy-preserving statistical analysis is growing, and a theoretical understanding of the inherent limitations is crucial. Secondly, the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, leveraging minimax theory and differential privacy in a cohesive manner. The careful derivation of lower bounds, coupled with matching upper bounds and achievable algorithms, demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem. The connection to Warner’s original work provides historical context and reinforces the relevance of the study. The detailed analysis of both multinomial and density estimation showcases the versatility of the developed framework. The inclusion of efficient algorithms, particularly for the multinomial case, adds practical value. The detailed exposition, including background material and clear notation, makes the paper relatively accessible. The proofs appear to be meticulously constructed and are referenced to a companion paper for full details.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The notation is quite dense, particularly in Section 2, and could benefit from further simplification or more intuitive explanations. While the authors acknowledge the complexity of the information bounds, making them more approachable would enhance clarity. The discussion of the density estimation results, while technically sound, feels somewhat less compelling than the multinomial results. The fact that the rate changes fundamentally (different polynomial dependence on *n*) is stated, but the implications of this departure from the classical rate are not fully explored beyond stating it is a "departure". Specifically, what kinds of density estimation tasks are *particularly* impacted by this change? Could the new rate imply specific choices of basis functions or estimators are needed in practice? The treatment of histogram estimators could have been expanded; the connection to existing literature on privacy-preserving histograms isn't thoroughly discussed.  The discussion of the limitations of Laplace perturbation for density estimation is good, but a more in-depth explanation of why the proposed orthogonal series approach circumvents those limitations would be beneficial. The paper lacks a discussion section explicitly outlining open problems and future research directions. Identifying specific areas where the developed framework could be extended would strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper rigorously establishes minimax rates under local privacy, a challenging problem.
*   **Novelty:** The results provide new insights into the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy in probability estimation.
*   **Practical relevance:** The analysis of Warner's randomized response and the development of efficient algorithms have practical implications.
*   **Clear presentation (overall):** Despite some density in notation, the paper is generally well-written and logically organized.
*   **Addresses a critical problem:** The paper tackles a problem of increasing importance in data analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Dense notation:** The mathematical notation can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field.
*   **Limited discussion of density estimation implications:** The paper doesn’t fully explore the practical implications of the altered rate for density estimation.
*   **Lack of future work discussion:** A dedicated discussion section outlining open problems would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Limited connection to related histogram literature:** The privacy-preserving histogram discussion could be richer.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and theoretically valuable paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While the dense notation and somewhat limited discussion of density estimation implications are weaknesses, the strengths of the paper – the rigorous theoretical framework, novel results, and practical relevance – outweigh these concerns.  I believe the paper is a good fit for NeurIPS, but would benefit from minor revisions addressing the clarity concerns and expanding on the implications of the results.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. Specifically, I recommend:

1.  Simplifying the notation where possible, particularly in Section 2.
2.  Expanding the discussion of the density estimation results, focusing on the practical implications of the altered rate.
3.  Adding a dedicated section outlining open problems and future research directions.
4.  Providing more context regarding existing literature on privacy-preserving histograms.
