## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, specifically under the framework of *local* differential privacy. Building upon Warner’s initial work on privacy-preserving surveys, the authors provide tight minimax lower and upper bounds for both multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation when data is subject to local privacy constraints.  A key contribution is demonstrating that local privacy introduces a different convergence rate for density estimation than traditionally observed in non-private settings. They also show the optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism and provide efficiently implementable algorithms matching the derived bounds.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper rigorously combines differential privacy with minimax decision theory, providing a formal and comprehensive analysis of the privacy-utility trade-off.  The use of established techniques like Le Cam’s method and Fano’s inequality, combined with their prior work on information bounds, provides a solid theoretical underpinning.
* **Sharp Results:**  The derived minimax rates are tight, meaning the upper and lower bounds match, providing a precise characterization of the achievable performance under privacy constraints.
* **Practical Relevance:** The analysis directly addresses a core concern in modern data analysis – protecting individual privacy while still enabling useful statistical inference. The re-examination of Warner’s randomized response and development of new estimators are practically valuable.
* **Clear Exposition:** While the topic is theoretically dense, the paper is generally well-written and organized.  The introduction provides good motivation and context. The notation is generally standard and well-defined.
* **Novelty:** The extension of minimax analysis to the locally private setting, and the identification of a different rate of convergence for density estimation, represent significant contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The paper is highly technical and requires a strong background in statistical decision theory, differential privacy, and information theory to fully appreciate. This might limit its accessibility to a wider audience within NIPS.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  While the authors demonstrate achievability through algorithms, there is a lack of extensive empirical validation. Including simulations to corroborate the theoretical results and compare the performance of different estimators would strengthen the work. (However, the authors do acknowledge the computational challenges of extensive empirical evaluation).
* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on certain assumptions (e.g., the smoothness of densities in the density estimation section, the boundedness of the basis functions) that are not always fully discussed.  A more detailed discussion of the impact of relaxing these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Applications:** While the focus on multinomial and density estimation is a good starting point, exploring the implications of these results in other statistical settings (e.g., regression, hypothesis testing) would broaden the paper's impact.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by rigorous analysis.  The proofs appear correct, and the authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach. It presents a complete piece of work within the defined scope.
* **Clarity:** The paper is reasonably clear, given its complexity.  However, the density of mathematical notation could be a barrier for some readers.  A few more intuitive explanations of key concepts might improve accessibility.
* **Originality:**  The work is highly original in applying minimax theory to the locally private setting and in discovering new convergence rates. The connection between classical privacy work and modern differential privacy is also valuable. The references appear comprehensive.
* **Significance:**  The results are significant because they provide a fundamental understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.  This understanding is crucial for designing practical privacy-preserving data analysis systems. The sharpness of the bounds is particularly valuable.


**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Rigorous theoretical analysis with tight bounds.
* Novel application of minimax theory to locally private settings.
* Addresses a critical problem in modern data analysis.
* Offers practical insights (optimality of randomized response).

**Con:**

* High technical complexity might limit accessibility.
* Lack of extensive empirical evaluation.
* Relatively narrow scope of application (focus on two specific estimation problems).




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The theoretical results are strong, the analysis is rigorous, and the work addresses an important problem. While the technical density and limited empirical evaluation are minor drawbacks, the strengths of the paper far outweigh these weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting the paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the assumptions made (e.g., smoothness assumptions for density estimation).
2.  Explore the possibility of including some illustrative simulations, even if they are limited in scope, to validate the theoretical findings.
3. Consider adding a few more intuitive explanations or examples of key concepts to increase accessibility for a broader audience.
