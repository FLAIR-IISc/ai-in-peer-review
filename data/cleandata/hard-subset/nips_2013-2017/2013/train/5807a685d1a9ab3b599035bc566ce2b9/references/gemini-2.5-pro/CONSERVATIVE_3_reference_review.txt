This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and privacy under the framework of local differential privacy. The authors employ minimax decision theory to derive sharp convergence rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, the work shows that local privacy induces a reduction in the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `n * alpha^2 / d`. A key finding is that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal in this setting. For nonparametric estimation of a `beta`-smooth density, the authors demonstrate a more severe degradation, where the minimax rate changes from the classical `n^(-2*beta / (2*beta + 1))` to `(n * alpha^2)^(-2*beta / (2*beta + 2))`. The paper complements these lower bounds by proposing computationally efficient estimators that achieve these rates.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The primary strength of this work lies in its rigorous, decision-theoretic approach. By grounding the analysis in the well-established minimax framework, the authors provide a principled and quantitative characterization of the cost of local privacy. The use of information-theoretic tools like Fano's and Le Cam's methods is sound and appropriate for deriving fundamental limits.

2.  **Sharpness of Results:** The paper successfully derives matching lower and upper bounds for the convergence rates. This provides a complete theoretical characterization for the problems studied, leaving little ambiguity about the optimal rates achievable under local privacy constraints. This level of completeness is commendable.

3.  **Connection to Classical Methods:** The result showing that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal is elegant. It connects a foundational technique in privacy-preserving surveys to the modern, formal framework of differential privacy, validating an established method through a new theoretical lens.

4.  **Clarity of the Core Message:** The paper clearly articulates the "price of privacy" for the statistical problems it considers. The derived rates, particularly the change in the polynomial exponent for density estimation, offer a stark and insightful look into how privacy constraints can fundamentally alter the difficulty of a statistical task.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Technical Tools:** The core analytical framework relies on standard minimax theory. The key information-theoretic bounds used to incorporate the privacy constraint (Proposition 1) are directly cited from the authors' previous work [9]. As such, the present manuscript is primarily an application of a previously developed framework to two specific, albeit important, statistical problems. This reduces the originality of the contribution to the specific derivations for these settings.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the asymptotic analysis is valuable, the absence of any simulations or experiments is a significant shortcoming. Empirical results could validate the theoretical rates in finite-sample regimes and provide insight into the magnitude of the constants hidden by the asymptotic notation. Without such validation, the practical relevance of these bounds is difficult to assess. For instance, the proposed orthogonal series estimator for density estimation is quite complex; simulations would be crucial to demonstrate that it is not only theoretically optimal but also practically viable.

3.  **Practicality of Proposed Mechanisms:** The estimator proposed for density estimation with `beta > 1` (Section 4.3) is non-trivial. The sampling strategy (19) is intricate, and the claim of efficient computation via rejection sampling is not substantiated with a formal complexity analysis. Furthermore, the optimal choice of the tuning parameter `k` (the number of basis functions) depends on `n` and `alpha`, which is a common feature in theoretical analyses but a practical challenge. The paper does not address how one might select `k` in a data-driven manner.

4.  **Limited Scope:** The paper focuses on two classical, low-dimensional problems. While foundational, it is not immediately clear how these results and techniques extend to the high-dimensional, complex models (e.g., structured prediction, deep learning) that are of significant interest to the NIPS community. The discussion section claims to lay groundwork, but the paper would be stronger if it offered more perspective on the challenges of extending this analysis to more modern machine learning settings.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper provides a technically sound, rigorous, and complete minimax analysis for two fundamental problems under local privacy. The results are sharp and offer clear, fundamental insights into the privacy-utility trade-off. The connection to Warner's established method is a notable contribution.

*   **Against Acceptance:** The core methodology is not novel to this paper but imported from prior work. The work is purely theoretical, lacking any empirical validation to support its claims, which is a significant weakness. The practicality of the proposed high-order density estimator is questionable and not sufficiently explored. The scope is limited to classical problems with little discussion of relevance to more contemporary machine learning.

In conclusion, this is a solid piece of theoretical work that rigorously applies established statistical theory to the important problem of local privacy. However, its contribution is more of a careful application than a development of new foundational techniques. The lack of any experimental results to accompany the theoretical claims and the questions surrounding the practicality of the proposed estimators are major concerns. The paper establishes an important theoretical benchmark but falls short of being a fully convincing and complete contribution for the NIPS audience.
