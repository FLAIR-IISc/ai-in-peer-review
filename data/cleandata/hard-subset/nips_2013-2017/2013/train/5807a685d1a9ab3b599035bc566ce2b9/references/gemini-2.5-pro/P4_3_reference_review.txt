### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of minimax decision theory. The authors focus on the setting of α-local differential privacy, where each data point is individually privatized before being observed by a statistician. The primary contribution is the derivation of sharp minimax convergence rates for two canonical statistical estimation problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation.

For the d-dimensional multinomial estimation problem, the paper establishes that the minimax mean-squared error rate scales as `d/(nα^2)`, a significant degradation from the classical `1/n` rate. This result implies that local privacy reduces the effective sample size by a factor of approximately `α^2/d`. The authors demonstrate that this lower bound is achievable, showing that a slightly modified version of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal.

For the problem of estimating a probability density belonging to a β-smooth Sobolev class, the paper reveals a more severe impact of local privacy. While the classical minimax rate is `n^(-2β/(2β+1))`, the authors prove that under α-local privacy, the optimal rate becomes `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This is not merely a constant-factor degradation but a fundamental change in the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate. To complement this lower bound, the authors construct explicit, computationally efficient estimators that achieve this new rate: a privatized histogram estimator for Lipschitz densities (β=1) and a novel orthogonal series estimator for general β > 1.

The technical approach builds on classical minimax lower bound techniques (e.g., Le Cam's and Fano's methods) but adapts them to the local privacy setting by leveraging new information-theoretic bounds that control the divergence between output distributions of private channels. This work connects the foundational ideas of randomized response [Warner, JASA 1965] with the modern formalism of differential privacy, providing a rigorous, decision-theoretic characterization of the price of local privacy.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The central claims—the sharp minimax rates—are supported by both lower bounds on the risk of any estimator and the construction of specific estimators that achieve these bounds. This provides a complete and compelling theoretical picture. The analysis is rigorous, employing standard but powerful tools from statistical decision theory and information theory. The authors are careful to distinguish their setting (population parameter estimation under local privacy) from related work on sample quantity estimation under central privacy, correctly noting that their lower bounds are stronger.

A potential weakness regarding quality is that the proofs of the main theorems and the key information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) are deferred to a longer companion paper [9]. While this is a common practice for conference submissions, it prevents a full and independent verification of the technical derivations by a reviewer. The review must trust the correctness of these foundational results presented without proof.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation, connecting the problem to its historical roots and modern context. The problem formulation is precise, and the main results (Theorems 1 and 2) are stated cleanly and are easy to interpret. The authors do an excellent job of comparing their privacy-constrained rates to the classical non-private rates, which effectively highlights the "cost of privacy" in each setting. The structure logically flows from problem setup to the two main application areas, with each section presenting lower bounds followed by matching, constructive upper bounds.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original and significant contributions. While there is a growing body of work on differential privacy and statistical inference, this paper is one of the first to provide a complete and sharp minimax characterization for fundamental estimation problems in the *local* privacy model. The most striking and novel result is the discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate for nonparametric density estimation. This is a deep insight that distinguishes it from the multinomial case, where the rate's dependence on `n` is unchanged. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates originality in mechanism design by showing that naive mechanisms (e.g., adding Laplace noise directly to data points) are suboptimal for density estimation and by constructing a more sophisticated, optimal orthogonal series-based mechanism.

**Significance:** The results are of high significance to both the machine learning and statistics communities. The paper addresses a fundamental question: what is the ultimate statistical price for a given level of privacy? The sharp rates provide a precise, quantitative answer for two core problems. This formal characterization of the privacy-utility trade-off is crucial for practitioners and theorists seeking to understand the limits of private data analysis. Researchers are likely to build on these results and apply the developed analytical framework to other statistical problems. The finding that Warner's 50-year-old method is optimal is also a significant and elegant conclusion. The work provides clear guidance on which types of privacy mechanisms are suitable for different problems, advancing the state of the art in private statistical inference.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper provides a definitive answer to a fundamental question about the cost of local privacy for two core statistical problems.
2.  **Novel and Surprising Results:** The discovery of a change in the polynomial rate for density estimation is a significant and non-obvious theoretical finding.
3.  **Technical Strength:** The work demonstrates a high level of technical sophistication, providing sharp, matching upper and lower bounds, which is the gold standard for this type of analysis.
4.  **Clarity and Impact:** The paper is clearly written, and its results are easy to understand and have broad implications for the fields of privacy, statistics, and machine learning. It provides both theoretical insight and constructive, optimal algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The reliance on an external paper for key proofs makes it difficult to fully assess the technical correctness of the core arguments within the confines of the submission.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, significance, and technical quality—far outweigh the weakness related to self-containment. The contributions represent a major advance in our understanding of private statistical inference.
