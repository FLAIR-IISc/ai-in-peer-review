**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper provides a foundational and rigorous analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors use minimax decision theory to derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds on the convergence rates for two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The work connects the classical notion of privacy in surveys, initiated by Warner's randomized response, with the modern, cryptographically-motivated definition of differential privacy.

The core technical contribution is the application of information-theoretic tools to derive lower bounds on the minimax risk for any locally private mechanism. These bounds are then matched with upper bounds achieved by specific, computationally efficient private algorithms. For multinomial estimation, the paper demonstrates that the effective sample size is reduced by a factor of roughly `α^2/d`, and remarkably shows that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal. For nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes, the paper reveals a more severe penalty: local privacy changes the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate, degrading it from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`.

This work builds upon the growing literature at the intersection of privacy and machine learning (e.g., [11, 6, 24]), but distinguishes itself by focusing on the stringent *local* privacy model and by providing sharp *population risk* bounds, which are stronger than the sample risk bounds considered in much of the prior work (e.g., [2, 16, 8]). The methodology of using information-theoretic inequalities (like Fano's) to prove minimax bounds is standard in statistics [26, 25], but its application in the context of local privacy, by bounding information flow through the private channel, is a key contribution of this work and its companion paper [9].

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Impact:** This is a landmark paper that formalizes and quantifies the fundamental price of local privacy for statistical estimation. The results are not just theoretical; they have direct implications for practitioners. For example, the finding that randomized response is optimal for surveys provides strong justification for its continued use. The new, slower rates for density estimation are a crucial warning that privacy can fundamentally alter the difficulty of nonparametric problems. These results are likely to be widely cited and built upon.

*   **Originality and Quality:** The paper is highly original in its synthesis of minimax theory and local differential privacy to derive sharp rates. The technical quality is outstanding. The lower bounds are powerful, and the authors provide constructive, non-trivial algorithms to prove the upper bounds are achievable. The insight that simple data perturbation (e.g., adding Laplace noise directly to samples `Xi`) is suboptimal for density estimation is particularly deep and important, highlighting the need for more sophisticated privacy mechanisms tailored to the estimator, as developed in Section 4.3.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Despite the technical density of the topic, the authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, defining the concepts (like the α-private minimax rate), and clearly stating their main theorems. The distinction between their population-level risk analysis and prior sample-level analysis is articulated well. The narrative flows logically from the general framework to the specific applications in multinomial and density estimation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Complexity of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The proposed sampling strategy for the optimal density estimator (Eq. 19) is quite complex. While it is shown to be optimal, its practical implementation is not immediately obvious (e.g., uniform sampling from the intersection of a half-space and a hypercube may require rejection sampling). A brief discussion of the computational complexity or practical considerations of this mechanism would strengthen the paper's contribution to practitioners.

*   **Reliance on Companion Paper:** Many of the key proofs and the foundational information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) are deferred to the authors' longer companion paper [9]. While this is understandable given conference page limits, it makes the paper less self-contained and harder to verify completely on its own. The core contributions presented here (the application of those bounds and the construction of matching upper bounds) are still substantial, but the reliance is worth noting.

*   **Scope of α:** The analysis in Theorem 1 is stated for `α ∈ [0, 1]`. While this is a reasonable regime for small privacy loss, differential privacy is often applied with `α > 1`. The behavior of the rates for larger `α` is not fully characterized, though the bounds likely saturate. A brief comment on this would be helpful for completeness.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper of high quality and significant impact. It addresses a fundamental problem at the intersection of statistics and privacy with rigor and depth. The results are novel, the analysis is sound, and the conclusions are important for both theorists and practitioners in the field. The paper is a clear and compelling contribution that advances the state of the art in understanding privacy-preserving statistical inference. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first sharp minimax rates for fundamental statistical problems under local differential privacy.
*   The results are both theoretically deep and practically relevant.
*   Connects classical statistical privacy methods with the modern differential privacy framework in a beautiful way (e.g., the optimality of Warner's randomized response).
*   Exceptionally well-written and clear, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper's technical core relies on lemmas and proofs from a companion paper, making it not fully self-contained.
*   The practical implementation of the most sophisticated proposed mechanism (for density estimation) could be elaborated upon further.
