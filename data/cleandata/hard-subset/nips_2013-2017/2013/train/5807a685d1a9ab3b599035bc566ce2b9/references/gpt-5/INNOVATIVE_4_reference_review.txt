Summary
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). The authors place LDP (α-local privacy) within a minimax decision-theoretic framework and derive sharp rates—matching lower and upper bounds—for two canonical problems: (i) multinomial (discrete probability) estimation and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. They show:

- Multinomial estimation: Under LDP with parameter α, the effective sample size degrades from n to nα^2/d (for small α), yielding minimax risks Mn(Δd, ℓ2^2, α) ≍ min{1, d/(nα^2)} and Mn(Δd, ℓ1, α) ≍ min{1, d/√(nα^2)}. A slightly modified randomized response mechanism is shown minimax optimal.

- Density estimation: For f in a Sobolev class Fβ, the classical rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} degrades under LDP to (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)}—a genuine change in the polynomial exponent. The paper exhibits private histogram estimators achieving the sharp rate for β=1 and a new locally private orthogonal series mechanism (with a nontrivial privatization scheme) that achieves the sharp rate for all β≥1. It also shows that direct Laplace perturbation of samples is provably suboptimal (via deconvolution limits).

The lower bounds rely on Le Cam/Fano reductions together with LDP-specific information inequalities bounding KL/mutual information in terms of total variation separations; the upper bounds are constructive and computationally simple.

Relation to prior work
Differential privacy has a rich literature (Dwork et al.; Hardt & Talwar; De; Wasserman & Zhou). Most prior lower bounds emphasize global DP or accuracy for sample statistics; local DP has been studied (e.g., Kasiviswanathan et al.), but sharp population minimax rates are far less developed. The paper builds on the authors’ general information bounds for LDP and brings them to bear on concrete estimation problems, delivering tight rates and mechanisms. For nonparametric density estimation, prior DP works are known to be suboptimal under LDP; this paper closes that gap and clarifies why naive Laplace perturbation fails by invoking classical deconvolution limits (Carroll & Hall).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds follow a standard yet strong recipe (packing + LDP-adapted info bounds). The achievability uses explicit mechanisms: (a) a carefully parameterized randomized response for multinomials, and (b) privatized histograms and a novel hypercube-sign mechanism for orthogonal series that maintains unbiasedness and controls variance under α-LDP. The deconvolution barrier convincingly rules out a common but suboptimal strategy.

- Completeness: Some proofs are deferred to a longer version [9]. While acceptable for space, the conference paper would benefit from including at least outlines of key proofs (e.g., the mutual information variational bound and the core steps of the orthogonal-series mechanism analysis) in an appendix.

- Reproducibility: Algorithms are simple and implementable; parameter choices (e.g., k ≍ (nα^2)^{1/(2β+2)}) are explicit. The series mechanism introduces constants (ck, B0) whose computation/estimation could be clarified, but this is minor.

Clarity
The paper is well organized, with clear problem statements and precise rates. Suggestions:
- Consolidate and highlight the main theorems early with informal interpretations (e.g., “effective sample size n→nα^2/d”).
- Provide pseudo-code for the orthogonal-series privatization (the hypercube sampling) and discuss computational cost for large k.
- Fix a few reference formatting issues and minor typos.

Originality
High. The identification of a rate-exponent change under LDP for nonparametric density estimation is novel and impactful. Showing that randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomials under LDP is a crisp, satisfying result. The privatization mechanism for orthogonal series is nontrivial and, to my knowledge, new in this precise form.

Significance
High. The paper establishes foundational, sharp trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in the local model. These rates will likely serve as benchmarks for future work on private learning and motivate mechanism design beyond simple noise addition. The constructive nature (simple estimators) enhances practical relevance.

Pros
- Conceptually bold and novel: unifies minimax theory with LDP and reveals a genuine phase change in nonparametric rates.
- Sharp lower and upper bounds with simple, implementable mechanisms.
- Clear message with practical implications (optimality of randomized response; limits of Laplace perturbation).

Cons
- Reliance on a separate, longer paper for full proofs; include more proof sketches in the supplement.
- The orthogonal-series privatization mechanism could use more implementation detail (constants, runtime, acceptance rates if using rejection sampling).
- No discussion of adaptivity to unknown smoothness β under LDP; selection of k under privacy constraints is nontrivial.
- Focuses on non-interactive channels; it would be helpful to comment whether interactivity could improve rates.

Questions for authors
- Can the rates be improved with interactive LDP mechanisms, or are your lower bounds already valid for interactive protocols?
- Is there a privacy-preserving, data-driven choice of k (e.g., private cross-validation) that attains the optimal rate adaptively over β?
- How sensitive are the constants/variance of the orthogonal-series mechanism to k in practice?

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper introduces a significant new conceptual and technical direction with sharp results and practical mechanisms, outweighing minor presentation and completeness issues.

Confidence
High.
