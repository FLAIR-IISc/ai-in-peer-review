### Summary of the paper

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors connect classical minimax statistical theory with the modern definition of α-local differential privacy to derive fundamental limits on estimation accuracy. They focus on two canonical probability estimation problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation.

The main contributions are twofold:
1.  **Multinomial Estimation:** For estimating a d-dimensional probability vector, the authors establish that the minimax squared error rate scales as `d / (nα^2)`. This provides a precise characterization of the cost of privacy, showing that it effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `nα^2/d`. A significant finding is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal, achieving this lower bound.

2.  **Nonparametric Density Estimation:** For estimating a density function with β degrees of smoothness, the paper demonstrates a more severe penalty for privacy. While the classical minimax rate is `n^(-2β/(2β+1))`, the authors prove that under α-local privacy, the optimal rate degrades to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent represents a fundamental shift in the problem's statistical difficulty. They provide constructive, efficient estimators (a private histogram for β=1 and a private orthogonal series estimator for β>1) that achieve these new rates, proving the lower bounds are sharp.

The paper leverages information-theoretic tools, such as Fano's and Le Cam's methods, and adapts them to the locally private setting using divergence bounds derived in their companion work.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** This work is of fundamental importance. It builds a strong, formal bridge between the fields of privacy-preserving data analysis and classical statistical decision theory. While previous work has studied utility in differentially private mechanisms, this paper is one of the first to establish sharp, non-asymptotic minimax rates for statistical estimation under *local* privacy constraints. The results are not just incremental; they precisely quantify the cost of privacy and reveal interesting phenomena, such as the change in convergence exponents for nonparametric problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and technically sound. The application of minimax theory to the private setting is elegant and powerful. By providing both lower bounds (on any possible private algorithm) and matching upper bounds (achieved by specific, efficient algorithms), the authors deliver a complete and compelling story. The finding that simple data perturbation (e.g., adding Laplace noise to samples) is suboptimal for density estimation is a subtle and important insight.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation, connecting to the historical origins of privacy in statistics (Warner's work). The problem formulation is precise, and the main results are stated clearly and interpreted effectively. The distinction between their population-level risk analysis and prior work on sample-level risk is important and well-articulated.

4.  **Impact:** The results are likely to have a significant impact on both the theory and practice of private data analysis. The finding that randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation provides strong theoretical justification for a simple, widely understood mechanism. The sharp rates for density estimation set a clear benchmark for future algorithm development and highlight that designing optimal mechanisms for local privacy requires non-trivial, problem-specific approaches. Researchers in both machine learning and statistics will likely build on these foundational results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on External Proofs:** The paper defers the proofs of its key technical results (Proposition 1, Theorem 1, Theorem 2) to a longer companion paper [9]. While this is understandable given conference page limits, it means that the self-contained technical novelty of this specific submission is somewhat limited. The core information-theoretic bounds that power the analysis must be taken on faith by the reader.

2.  **Intuition for Optimal Mechanisms:** The orthogonal series estimator for density estimation (Section 4.3) is technically sophisticated but the paper provides limited intuition for why this specific, somewhat complex, sampling strategy (19) is necessary for optimality. A brief, high-level explanation of why a simpler approach like coefficient perturbation fails and how their proposed mechanism overcomes this would strengthen the paper's clarity and pedagogical value.

3.  **Practical Implications of Constants:** The analysis focuses on rates, which is appropriate for a theory paper. However, a brief discussion of the constants could be insightful. For multinomial estimation, the effective sample size reduction by a factor of `α^2/d` is drastic. For small `α` (strong privacy) or large `d` (many categories), the data requirements become immense. A sentence contextualizing this would help ground the theoretical results.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem at the intersection of privacy, statistics, and machine learning.
*   **Pro:** The results are novel, significant, and establish sharp minimax rates, which is the gold standard in statistical theory.
*   **Pro:** It connects a classic 50-year-old technique (randomized response) to the modern theory of differential privacy and proves its optimality.
*   **Pro:** The work is of high technical quality and is written with exceptional clarity.
*   **Con:** Key proofs are deferred to an external manuscript, making it difficult to fully vet the technical machinery within this paper alone.
*   **Con:** The intuition behind the more complex optimal mechanism could be better developed.

### Conclusion

The weaknesses are minor and typical of conference submissions that summarize a larger body of work. The strengths are substantial. This paper makes a foundational contribution to our understanding of the fundamental limits of what can be learned under local privacy constraints. It is technically deep, clearly articulated, and its results are of significant importance to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
