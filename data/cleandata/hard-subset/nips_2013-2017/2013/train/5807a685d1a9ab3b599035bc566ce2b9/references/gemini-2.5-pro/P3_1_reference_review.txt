### Summary

This paper establishes a rigorous connection between local differential privacy and classical minimax decision theory to characterize the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy. The authors derive sharp, non-asymptotic minimax rates for two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For d-dimensional multinomial estimation, they show that α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα^2/d`. For estimating a density with β derivatives, they reveal a more dramatic effect: the optimal convergence rate changes from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, a degradation in the polynomial exponent. Crucially, the paper complements these information-theoretic lower bounds with computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these rates, demonstrating their sharpness. For multinomials, they show that a variant of Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For density estimation, they develop novel, non-trivial private mechanisms based on histograms and orthogonal series that attain the new optimal rates.

### Quality

The paper is of exceptional technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The methodology correctly adapts powerful tools from minimax theory (Le Cam's method, Fano's inequality) to the setting of local privacy. The key technical step is the use of information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) that precisely quantify the information loss due to the privacy mechanism in terms of the privacy parameter `α`. This provides a solid foundation for the subsequent lower bounds.

The work is complete. It doesn't just provide lower bounds, which would be a significant contribution in itself, but it also provides matching upper bounds via constructive and efficient algorithms. This demonstrates that the derived limits are not artifacts of the proof technique but are fundamental to the problem. The authors are also careful in their analysis, for instance, by highlighting why simpler privacy mechanisms like adding Laplace noise directly to the data are suboptimal for density estimation, a subtle but important point that showcases their deep understanding of the problem.

### Clarity

The paper is extremely well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent narrative, connecting the historical motivation from Warner's work to the modern formalism of differential privacy and the analytical power of minimax theory. The problem setting, notation, and the central object of study—the α-private minimax rate—are defined clearly. Each section builds logically on the previous one. The main theorems are stated precisely, and their implications are immediately discussed in an intuitive way (e.g., the "effective sample size" interpretation). While some of the proofs are deferred to a longer version, the paper provides enough detail and intuition for an expert to grasp the core arguments, particularly in the construction of the rate-optimal estimators. The descriptions of the randomized response and histogram estimators are crystal clear. The orthogonal series mechanism is more complex, but the authors provide a sufficient sketch of the sampling strategy and the resulting estimator.

### Originality

This work is highly original and represents a significant conceptual advance. While others have studied accuracy in differential privacy, this paper is one of the first to bring the full power of minimax decision theory to bear on the problem under *local* privacy constraints. The key original contributions are:

1.  **Framing the Problem:** Formally defining the private minimax risk and establishing a general framework for deriving lower bounds via information-theoretic inequalities.
2.  **The Density Estimation Result:** The discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate is a major and surprising finding. It shows that the cost of privacy can be more fundamental than a mere constant-factor loss in sample size.
3.  **Optimal Mechanism Design:** The paper demonstrates that achieving optimal rates requires careful design of the privacy mechanism itself. The insight that simple data perturbation is insufficient for density estimation and the subsequent construction of a novel, optimal orthogonal series estimator is a substantial and non-obvious contribution.
4.  **Vindication of a Classic:** Showing that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal nearly 50 years after its invention is a beautiful and elegant result that bridges the classical and modern privacy literature.

The authors clearly situate their work with respect to prior art, correctly pointing out that much previous work focused on the accuracy of sample estimates rather than population parameters, making their lower bounds stronger and more fundamental.

### Significance

The results in this paper are of fundamental importance to both the machine learning/statistics and privacy communities.
*   It provides definitive, quantitative answers to the question: "What is the statistical cost of local privacy?" This trade-off is now characterized precisely for two canonical estimation problems.
*   The findings have direct practical implications. The optimality of randomized response provides strong theoretical justification for its continued use in settings like private surveys.
*   The work opens up a new research direction in designing optimal privacy mechanisms. The discovery that naive mechanisms are suboptimal for complex problems will spur further research into developing sophisticated, problem-specific mechanisms that achieve the best possible accuracy for a given level of privacy.
*   By providing a rigorous decision-theoretic foundation, this work elevates the study of privacy-preserving machine learning and statistics, moving beyond ad-hoc algorithm design towards a more principled understanding of fundamental limits.

Researchers are very likely to build on these ideas, applying the framework to other statistical problems (e.g., regression, classification, high-dimensional inference) to understand the ultimate limits imposed by local privacy.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper addresses a core theoretical question at the intersection of privacy, statistics, and information theory, providing sharp and insightful answers.
*   **Novel and Surprising Results:** The finding that the convergence rate exponent changes for density estimation is a significant and non-intuitive discovery.
*   **Technical Excellence:** The analysis is rigorous, complete (providing both lower and upper bounds), and demonstrates a deep command of the relevant literature.
*   **Clarity and Impact:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and its results are likely to be highly influential and widely cited. It provides a foundational reference for understanding the statistical cost of local privacy.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   The technical proofs for some key results (e.g., Proposition 1, Theorem 2) are deferred to a longer arXiv paper. While this is common practice for conference submissions, it means some core technical details are not fully contained within the paper. However, the provided details and arguments are sufficient to make the results credible and their implications clear.

This is a minor point in the face of the paper's overwhelming strengths. This work is a landmark contribution and represents an exemplary NIPS paper.
