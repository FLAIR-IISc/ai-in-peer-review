

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary

This paper bridges classical privacy concepts with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory to provide sharp theoretical bounds on statistical estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors establish minimax rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, they prove the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d under α-local differential privacy, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response mechanism is optimal. For density estimation with β smoothness, they show the convergence rate changes from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)), and provide efficient algorithms achieving these rates. The paper connects Warner's 1965 work on randomized response with modern differential privacy, offering a quantitative framework for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs.

The work builds on Duchi et al. (2013) and other recent differential privacy literature but makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates rather than just upper bounds. It extends classical statistical decision theory to the privacy-constrained setting, providing a more complete understanding of the cost of privacy.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates exceptional technical rigor with well-supported theoretical claims. The minimax lower bounds are carefully derived using information-theoretic tools and Le Cam's two-point method, while the upper bounds are complemented by efficiently computable algorithms. The authors provide clear connections between their results and previous work, including Warner's randomized response and recent differential privacy literature. The paper is complete and self-contained, with all proofs and technical details presented in a logical manner.

**Originality:** This is a highly original contribution that establishes a new theoretical framework for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs. The key insight that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation under local privacy is particularly noteworthy. The paper also identifies a fundamental difference in convergence rates for density estimation under privacy constraints compared to classical results, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

**Significance:** The results provide a foundational understanding of how privacy constraints affect statistical estimation, with clear implications for survey design, data collection, and privacy-preserving machine learning. The sharp rates allow practitioners to quantify the exact cost of privacy in different settings. The paper establishes a framework that can be extended to other statistical problems, potentially guiding future research in privacy-preserving statistical inference.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear notation and logical flow. The main results are stated precisely, and the connection between classical privacy concepts and modern differential privacy is well-explained. The authors provide helpful examples (e.g., the randomized response mechanism) that make the theoretical results more accessible.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few limitations:

1. The focus on the non-interactive setting (memoryless channels) might limit applicability to certain privacy scenarios where interactive mechanisms could be more efficient. While the authors acknowledge this, it would be helpful to explicitly discuss the implications of this restriction.

2. The paper doesn't provide empirical validation of the theoretical rates. While theoretical minimax rates are the focus, some numerical examples would strengthen the practical relevance of the results.

3. The computational complexity of the proposed algorithms (particularly the orthogonal series estimator) isn't thoroughly analyzed. For high-dimensional problems, the O(d) projection in the multinomial case and the O(k) complexity in the density estimation case might become problematic.

4. The paper doesn't address the case where privacy level α varies across data points or depends on the data itself, which could be relevant in adaptive privacy mechanisms.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes significant advances in our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. The paper establishes sharp minimax rates for two fundamental problems under local differential privacy, connecting classical privacy concepts with modern theoretical frameworks. The results are original, significant, and well-supported by rigorous mathematical analysis.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to both privacy researchers and statisticians. The demonstration that Warner's randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly compelling and adds historical context to the modern privacy literature.

While there are minor limitations in scope (non-interactive setting, lack of empirical validation), these do not detract from the paper's substantial theoretical contribution. The results provide a foundation for future work on privacy-preserving statistical inference and would be of interest to both the statistics and machine learning communities.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The results are of high quality, original, and significant to the field. The paper is clearly written and provides a solid foundation for future research on privacy-preserving statistical inference.

The connection between Warner's classic work and modern differential privacy is particularly valuable, demonstrating that some optimal privacy-preserving strategies have been known for decades. This historical perspective, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis, makes the paper both insightful and practical for researchers working at the intersection of statistics and privacy.

The paper's contributions are clearly differentiated from previous work, with careful attention to the distinctions between local privacy and other privacy notions. The minimax framework provides a rigorous foundation for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs that will be valuable for both theoretical and applied researchers.
