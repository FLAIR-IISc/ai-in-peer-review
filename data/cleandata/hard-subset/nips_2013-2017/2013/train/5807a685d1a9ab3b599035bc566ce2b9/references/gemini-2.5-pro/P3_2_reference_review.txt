**Summary of the Paper**

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and privacy under the constraint of α-local differential privacy. The authors apply the minimax framework to two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. By deriving matching lower and upper bounds on the minimax risk, they precisely characterize the "price of privacy." For estimating a d-dimensional multinomial, they show that local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα²/d`. For estimating a β-smooth density, they demonstrate a more severe penalty: the optimal convergence rate's exponent changes from `2β/(2β+1)` in the non-private setting to `2β/(2β+2)`. The paper further shows these rates are achievable with computationally efficient mechanisms, notably connecting their result to Warner's classic randomized response for the multinomial case and proposing novel histogram and orthogonal series estimators for the density estimation case.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Fundamental Contribution and Significance:** This work is of very high significance. It bridges the gap between the modern, formal definition of local differential privacy and the classical, powerful framework of minimax statistical decision theory. This connection provides a principled way to answer a fundamental question: "What is the best possible statistical accuracy we can achieve for a given level of privacy?" The results are not incremental; they establish fundamental limits of estimation and will likely be a cornerstone for future work in private statistical inference.

2.  **Sharp, Interpretable Results:** A major strength is the sharpness of the results. The authors provide matching (up to constants) upper and lower bounds on the convergence rates. This is the gold standard in theoretical statistics, as it proves the proposed estimators are optimal and the analysis is complete. Furthermore, the results are highly interpretable: the `n → nα²/d` effective sample size reduction for multinomials is a clean, memorable takeaway. The change in the polynomial rate for density estimation is a subtle but profound insight into the cost of privacy in nonparametric settings.

3.  **Technical Quality and Novelty:** The technical quality is excellent. The authors skillfully adapt classical minimax lower-bounding techniques (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) to the private setting by developing information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) that directly link KL-divergence and mutual information to the privacy parameter α. On the achievability side, the paper makes two important contributions:
    *   It formally proves that Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal for multinomial estimation under local privacy. This is a beautiful and satisfying connection between the historical origins of the field and its modern formalization.
    *   For density estimation, it correctly identifies that simple perturbation schemes (e.g., adding Laplace noise to data points) are suboptimal and proposes more sophisticated, non-trivial mechanisms (especially the orthogonal series estimator) that are necessary to achieve the minimax rate. This is a crucial insight for algorithm design in private statistics.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation, precisely defines the problem setup, and presents its main theorems with insightful discussion comparing them to the classical non-private rates. The structure logically flows from problem formulation to lower bounds and then to matching upper bounds via constructive algorithms. This makes a technically dense topic accessible.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Reliance on External Paper for Proofs:** The core technical lemmas for the lower bounds (Proposition 1) and the full proofs for the main theorems are deferred to a longer companion paper [9]. While this is a common and often necessary practice for conference submissions due to space limitations, it makes the paper less self-contained. A brief proof sketch for one of the key results, perhaps the multinomial lower bound, in an appendix could have strengthened the submission by giving reviewers more insight into the mechanics of the argument without needing to consult another source.

2.  **Practicality of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The sampling strategy for the orthogonal series estimator (Eq. 19) is clever but complex. The paper asserts it is "efficiently computable (for example by rejection sampling)," but a bit more detail would be helpful. For a high-dimensional projection `k`, rejection sampling could be inefficient. A brief discussion on the practical computational complexity of this mechanism, especially as `k` grows with `n`, would be valuable.

3.  **Scope of α:** The analysis and discussion primarily focus on the regime where `α` is a small constant (e.g., `α ∈ [0, 1]`). This is the most relevant regime for strong privacy. However, a brief comment on how the bounds behave for large `α` (i.e., weak privacy) would be welcome, presumably showing a smooth transition back to the classical non-private rates.

**Detailed Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality. The claims are rigorously supported by theoretical analysis. It is a complete piece of work that establishes sharp, fundamental limits.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The writing is clear, the problem is well-motivated, and the results are presented with lucid interpretations.
*   **Originality:** High. While building on classical statistical theory and recent work in differential privacy, the synthesis is novel and powerful. It is one of the first works to provide a complete minimax characterization for local privacy in these fundamental settings. The finding that the convergence rate exponent changes for density estimation is a major original insight.
*   **Significance:** Very high. This work addresses a central problem in private data analysis. Researchers and practitioners seeking to understand the fundamental costs of implementing local privacy will find these results invaluable. The paper lays a theoretical foundation upon which a great deal of future research in private machine learning and statistics can be built.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an outstanding paper that makes a foundational contribution to the intersection of statistics and privacy. It is technically deep, highly significant, and exceptionally well-presented. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's core strengths. I strongly recommend acceptance and would consider it a candidate for an oral or spotlight presentation.
