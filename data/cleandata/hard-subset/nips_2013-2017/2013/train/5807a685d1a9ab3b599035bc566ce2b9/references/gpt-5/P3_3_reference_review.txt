Summary
The paper develops a decision-theoretic foundation for statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It defines an α-private minimax risk over channels satisfying α-LDP and derives sharp lower and matching upper bounds for two canonical problems:
- Multinomial probability estimation over d categories: the mean-squared error scales as Θ(min{1, d/(nα^2)}), and the ℓ1-risk as Θ(min{1, d/√(nα^2)}). This implies an effective sample size reduction from n to nα^2/d when α is a small constant. A slight variant of Warner’s randomized response attains the bounds.
- Nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1] under integrated squared error: the minimax rate degrades from the classical n−2β/(2β+1) to (nα^2)−2β/(2β+2). The paper gives constructive, efficient mechanisms: a privatized histogram achieving the β=1 rate, and an orthogonal series mechanism with a carefully designed locally-private encoder that achieves the bound for all β≥1.

Technically, the lower bounds rely on an information-contraction analysis for LDP channels (KL and mutual information bounds) previously developed by the authors, combined with Le Cam/Fano testing reductions. The upper bounds are achieved by explicit mechanisms and estimators with variance-bias tradeoffs that match the lower bounds.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself relative to classical randomized response (Warner, 1965), centralized differential privacy, and prior work on private estimation (e.g., Wasserman & Zhou; Smith; Hardt & Talwar; Kasiviswanathan et al.). Distinctively, it studies LDP—not centralized DP—with minimax population risk rather than sample accuracy, and it provides sharp rates with matching procedures. It also clarifies why naïve Laplace perturbation of raw data is suboptimal for density estimation (via deconvolution lower bounds).

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The minimax framework and information-contraction lemmas are appropriate and powerful. The rates are sharp, with matching constructions. The multinomial mechanism (a modified randomized response) and the orthogonal-series mechanism appear carefully calibrated to the LDP constraint; the variance analyses and choice of truncation level k yield the stated rates.
- Completeness: Many proof details are deferred to the authors’ prior paper; the main text provides the key lemmas and intuitions but not full proofs. For NeurIPS, a more self-contained supplement with proofs of the new bounds (especially the density estimation lower bound and Proposition 3’s variance calculation) would strengthen the submission.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The minimax formulation, the role of Qα channels, and the testing-based lower bounds are well explained. The orthogonal-series mechanism is nontrivial; the construction via τ→τ̃→Z and calibration of B to ensure both α-LDP and unbiasedness is insightful. Minor clarity issues:
- In Eq. (10), “[Z]j = −xj with probability …” looks like a typo; for xj∈{0,1}, “1−xj” is presumably intended.
- Assumptions for the density class (periodic boundary conditions, bounded basis) could be stated more prominently; noting how to handle non-periodic Sobolev classes would help.
- The choice of k and constants in Propositions 2–3 could be summarized to show the bias-variance decomposition explicitly.

Originality
The work is original in framing LDP estimation within minimax decision theory and in delivering sharp, matching rates with explicit locally-private mechanisms. The discovery that LDP changes the nonparametric exponent (2β/(2β+1) → 2β/(2β+2)) is particularly novel and important. The optimality of (modified) randomized response for multinomial estimation under LDP is also a valuable and clarifying result.

Significance
High. LDP has become a widely adopted privacy model; understanding the fundamental sample complexity penalties is crucial. The results provide tight guidance on how privacy impacts efficiency, and the constructions are implementable. The negative result on Laplace perturbation for density estimation and the positive result via a specialized encoder provide actionable insights for mechanism design.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope: The paper focuses on the non-interactive (memoryless) LDP setting. Some discussion of whether interaction can improve the rates (or not) would be valuable; even a brief argument or citation clarifying known limits would help bound the generality of the claims.
- Practicality/experiments: No empirical validation is provided. Simulations comparing Laplace-perturbed histograms/series, standard randomized response, and the proposed mechanisms (risk vs n, α, d, β) would make the results more accessible and quantify constants.
- Generality of density setting: The analysis is for 1D, periodic Sobolev classes and L2 loss. A brief discussion of extensions to higher dimensions, other smoothness models, and other losses (e.g., Hellinger, TV) would broaden impact.
- Parameter selection: The procedures depend on α and β (e.g., the choice of k). Practical methods for selecting k under LDP constraints (e.g., private cross-validation) could be mentioned.
- Minor technicalities: The multinomial ℓ1-rate scales as d/√(nα^2), which is significantly worse than the non-private √(d/n). A short comment on tightness under other loss functions (e.g., ℓ2 vs χ2, or entropy) and whether any structure (e.g., sparsity) improves dependence on d would be interesting.

Pros
- Sharp minimax lower bounds under LDP with matching, efficient algorithms.
- Clear identification of how LDP changes nonparametric rates.
- Conceptual unification of privacy and classical decision theory.
- Optimality of (modified) randomized response is both theoretically and practically relevant.

Cons
- Proof details deferred; limited self-contained technical depth in the main paper.
- No experiments; limited discussion of adaptivity, higher dimensions, other losses.
- Some presentation typos/clarity issues in mechanism definitions.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper makes a fundamental, well-argued theoretical contribution with clear implications for practice in LDP. Adding a supplementary with full proofs of the new results and a small empirical study would further strengthen an already compelling submission.
