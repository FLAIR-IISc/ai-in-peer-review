**Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical efficiency and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. Using tools from minimax decision theory, the authors derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds on the estimation error for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, they show that α-local privacy incurs a penalty equivalent to reducing the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα^2/d`, and they demonstrate that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For density estimation over Sobolev classes, they reveal a more severe penalty: privacy alters the polynomial rate of convergence, degrading the mean integrated squared error from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. The authors provide computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these new optimal rates.

**Strengths**

1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous and insightful connection between two important fields: statistical decision theory and differential privacy. By characterizing the minimax rates, it establishes fundamental limits on estimation accuracy under local privacy constraints, moving beyond the analysis of specific algorithms.
2.  **Sharp and Interpretable Results:** The derived rates are shown to be sharp, with matching upper and lower bounds. The results are clean and interpretable; for instance, the concept of an "effective sample size" reduction in the multinomial case is very intuitive. The discovery that privacy changes the exponent in the convergence rate for density estimation is a deep and significant finding.
3.  **Constructive Upper Bounds:** The paper complements its lower bounds with practical and computationally efficient estimators that achieve the optimal rates. The demonstration that a 50-year-old mechanism (randomized response) is optimal for multinomial estimation is an elegant and noteworthy result.

**Weaknesses**

While the high-level ideas and main results are compelling, the manuscript suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its technical presentation, clarity, and self-containment, which collectively undermine its quality as a complete scientific contribution.

1.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The paper's most significant failing is that it is not self-contained. The proofs for all the main results (Theorems 1 and 2) are deferred to an external arXiv preprint by the same authors [9]. Similarly, the core technical lemmas for deriving the lower bounds (Proposition 1) are merely stated and cited from the same preprint. A top-tier conference paper must provide sufficient detail for a knowledgeable reader to verify its claims. Outsourcing all technical derivations to a non-peer-reviewed manuscript is unacceptable and makes a rigorous review of the paper's core contributions impossible.

2.  **Gaps in Technical Arguments:** Even in the arguments that are present, there are notable gaps.
    *   In Section 3.2, the derivation of the upper bound for randomized response concludes that the MSE is `min { 1, d / (nα^2) }`. This relies on an approximation of `(e^(α/2) + 1 / e^(α/2) - 1)^2` that holds for small `α`, but Theorem 1 is stated for all `α ∈ [0, 1]`. This is an inconsistency between the informal derivation and the formal theorem statement.
    *   The proposed orthogonal series estimator in Section 4.3 is not adequately specified or analyzed. The paper claims the sampling strategy (19) is "efficiently computable (for example by rejection sampling)" without providing any analysis of the acceptance probability or its dependence on the dimension `k`. The constant `ck` in equation (20) is crucial for the estimator's unbiasedness but is left undefined, described only as "O(1) and bounded away from 0." The choice for the bound `B` depends on this undefined `ck`. The entire derivation leading to the rate in Proposition 3 is omitted, leaving the reader unable to assess the validity of the claim.

3.  **Inconsistent and Imprecise Writing:** The manuscript displays a lack of meticulous care in its writing and presentation.
    *   The introduction states the effective sample size becomes `nα^2/d` "whenever α is a sufficiently small constant," but Theorem 1 makes a claim for `α ∈ [0, 1]` without this caveat.
    *   The notation is occasionally inconsistent. The `.` notation is defined for `O(.)` but is barely used, while standard `O(.)` and `≍` are used elsewhere. The notation for the probability simplex appears as `∆d ⊂ Rd` in one section and `∆k ⊂ Rk+` in another.
    *   The distinction between population estimation (this paper's focus) and sample estimation (prior work) is a key point but is mentioned only briefly in Section 2.1. This important contribution should be highlighted more prominently in the introduction.

4.  **Formatting and Referencing Issues:** The reference list is poorly and inconsistently formatted, with numerous errors such as double commas, missing publication venues, and incomplete author lists. While a minor point, it reflects a general lack of polish and attention to detail that is unbecoming of a submission to a premier venue.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a highly significant problem at the intersection of privacy and machine learning.
*   The main theoretical results, particularly the change in the rate for density estimation, are novel and of fundamental importance.
*   The connection to and validation of Warner's classic work is elegant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is not self-contained, with all proofs and key lemmas deferred to an external preprint, making verification impossible. This is a critical flaw.
*   The technical descriptions of the proposed upper-bound estimators are incomplete and contain unsubstantiated claims (e.g., computational efficiency, value of constants).
*   The manuscript contains several inconsistencies, minor technical gaps, and a poorly formatted reference list, indicating a lack of careful preparation.
*   Given that the core technical machinery (Proposition 1) and all proofs are in [9], the novelty of this specific submission as a standalone contribution is diminished.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the research direction is excellent and the claimed results are highly significant, the manuscript in its current form falls well short of the standards for NIPS. The complete lack of proofs and the significant technical gaps in the analysis of the proposed estimators make it impossible to properly vet the paper's core claims. The work is promising, but the authors must present it in a self-contained, complete, and meticulously prepared manuscript.
