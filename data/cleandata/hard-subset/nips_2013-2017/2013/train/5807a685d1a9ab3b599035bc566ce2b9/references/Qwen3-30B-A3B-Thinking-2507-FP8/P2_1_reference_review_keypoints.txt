- Connects minimax decision theory with local differential privacy to derive sharp convergence rates for probability estimation.  
- Shows local DP reduces effective sample size for multinomial estimation from n to nα²/d.  
- Derives that density estimation with β smoothness changes rate from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to (α²n)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under local DP.  
- Proves Warner’s randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation and gives efficient algorithms achieving the bounds.  
- Exceptional technical quality: rigorous minimax lower bounds via information-theoretic tools and matching upper bounds.  
- Proofs carefully track constants and dependencies on α and problem parameters.  
- Clearly distinguishes local differential privacy from central DP, supporting the novelty claim.  
- Exceptionally well-written and organized with consistent notation and accessible proofs for the target audience.  
- Main theorems and practical implications (e.g., “effective sample size”) are clearly stated and motivated.  
- Original contribution: first rigorous formal connection between minimax theory and differential privacy showing privacy can change convergence rates.  
- Significant: quantifies the privacy–utility trade-off and provides a foundation for designing privacy-preserving estimators.  
- Strengths: rigorous analysis, historical linkage to Warner, practical implications, well-motivated formulation, and efficient algorithms.  
- Weaknesses: purely theoretical—lacks implementation/empirical discussion and could better justify focusing on local vs central DP.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept for NIPS; technically sound, novel, and impactful.
