Paper summary
This paper develops a decision-theoretic framework for statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), deriving sharp minimax rates and matching (efficient) procedures for two canonical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The key ingredients are:

- Privacy model: non-interactive α-LDP channels Qi(Z|X), with guarantees in likelihood ratio form.
- Lower bound machinery: Le Cam/Fano reductions combined with new information contraction inequalities under LDP that upper bound KL and mutual information after privatization in terms of pre-privacy total variation differences across hypotheses.
- Multinomial estimation: Theorem 1 shows that for d-dimensional multinomials, the minimax MSE scales as min{1, d/(nα^2)} (and analogous ℓ1 risk), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα^2/d for small α. A (slightly modified) randomized response mechanism achieves the upper bound, proving Warner’s classical mechanism is minimax optimal for this task under LDP.
- Density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ: Theorem 2 proves a qualitative change in rate due to privacy: the nonprivate n−2β/(2β+1) becomes (nα^2)−2β/(2β+2). For β=1 (Lipschitz), a privatized histogram with Laplace perturbation on bin indicators plus simplex projection is minimax optimal. For β>1, a novel privatized orthogonal series estimator achieves the lower bound, using an α-LDP sampling strategy over the corners of a hypercube that preserves unbiasedness and controls variance with B=Θ(√k/α).

The work ties classical minimax theory with LDP and gives quantitative privacy–utility trade-offs with sharp exponents and explicit, implementable mechanisms.

Relation to prior work
Differential privacy and LDP have been studied in TCS and statistics, but most prior work optimizes sample statistics or focuses on global DP. This paper (building on the authors’ prior technical report) provides population-level minimax lower bounds under LDP, contraction inequalities tailored to LDP channels, and matching procedures. It clarifies when simple perturbation (e.g., Laplace on raw samples) is suboptimal for density estimation, and it recovers randomized response as optimal for multinomials.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean, decision-theoretic bridge between LDP and minimax estimation, yielding sharp, interpretable rates and the fundamental nα^2 scaling.
- Sharpness: Matching lower and upper bounds for both finite-dimensional (multinomial) and infinite-dimensional (Sobolev) settings, including the striking change in exponent for density estimation.
- Mechanism design: Practical, simple mechanisms (randomized response, privatized histograms) where optimal; and a clever orthogonal-series privatization strategy that avoids the deconvolution barrier of naive Laplace noise.
- Clarity of the main ideas: The setup, information bounds, and estimator constructions are clearly presented; the non-interactive LDP focus is explicit.
- Relevance: LDP is central in modern data collection (e.g., telemetry), so population-risk rates under LDP are significant for both theory and practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope limited to non-interactive LDP: The paper does not discuss whether interaction could improve rates (there is evidence interaction can help in some LDP tasks). A brief comparison or impossibility/possibility discussion would strengthen the contribution.
- Proof availability: Several proofs are deferred to the longer version. Including proof sketches for the key lower bound and the variance analysis of the orthogonal-series mechanism would aid reproducibility.
- Practical guidance and constants: The orthogonal-series mechanism uses parameters (B0, ck) and rejection sampling over half-cubes. More detail on computational complexity, acceptance rates, and recommended defaults would help practitioners.
- Adaptivity to unknown β: The procedures require knowing β (choice of k). A discussion of adaptive selection (e.g., Lepski, cross-validation) under LDP and whether optimal adaptation is possible would be valuable.
- Empirical validation: Even small-scale simulations illustrating the predicted scaling and comparing naive Laplace perturbation vs. the proposed mechanism would improve accessibility and bolster the claims.
- Overlap with the authors’ technical report: The submission reuses key information bounds and defers many proofs to [9]. Please clarify precisely what is novel here relative to [9] (e.g., new achievability mechanisms, sharper constants, generalized lower bounds) within the main text.

Questions for the authors
- Can interaction or public randomness improve the density estimation rate under LDP, or do your lower bounds extend to interactive channels?
- Is there an adaptive version of the orthogonal-series estimator that attains the optimal rate without knowing β?
- What are the computational and sampling costs of the half-cube mechanism in high k (acceptance rates, running time)? Any practical heuristics?
- How sensitive are the rates to the small-α assumption? Can you characterize the transition for moderate/large α?
- Could the information contraction bounds be extended to other losses (e.g., Hellinger) or other function classes beyond Sobolev?

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Strong. Theoretical claims are well-supported by lower/upper bounds and careful reductions. Some proofs are deferred; adding sketches would help.
- Clarity: Good to very good. The exposition is generally clear. Minor improvements suggested above.
- Originality: High. The change in exponent under LDP and the optimality of randomized response in a formal minimax sense are conceptually fresh and important.
- Significance: High. Establishes foundational rates and mechanisms for LDP estimation; likely to influence subsequent work in privacy-aware statistics.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). This paper offers a bold and foundational advance by precisely quantifying minimax costs of local privacy and by designing mechanisms that achieve sharp rates, including a qualitatively new phenomenon for density estimation. Minor clarifications and a brief empirical illustration would further strengthen an already compelling contribution.

Confidence
High.
