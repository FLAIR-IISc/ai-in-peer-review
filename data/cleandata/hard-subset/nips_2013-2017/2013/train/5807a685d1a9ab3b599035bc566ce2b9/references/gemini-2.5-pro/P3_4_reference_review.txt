### Summary

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of statistical estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors bridge the fields of differential privacy and classical minimax statistical theory to derive sharp (i.e., asymptotically tight) convergence rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation in `d` dimensions, they show that α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to roughly `nα²/d`. A key finding is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal. For nonparametric estimation of β-smooth densities, the authors uncover a more dramatic effect: local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate of the mean squared error from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This demonstrates that privacy can fundamentally alter the statistical difficulty of a problem beyond a simple sample size reduction. The paper establishes these lower bounds using information-theoretic tools (Fano's and Le Cam's methods) adapted to the local privacy setting and proves their attainability by constructing and analyzing computationally efficient private algorithms.

### Strengths

1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** This work establishes a foundational link between local differential privacy and minimax statistical theory. By characterizing the optimal rates of convergence, it provides a precise, quantitative understanding of the inherent trade-off between statistical utility and privacy. This is a significant conceptual contribution that advances both fields.

2.  **Sharp, Complete Results:** The paper delivers a complete theoretical treatment by providing both minimax lower bounds and matching, constructive upper bounds. This "sharpness" is the gold standard for this type of analysis and leaves little ambiguity about the fundamental limits of estimation under local privacy for the problems studied.

3.  **Novel and Important Findings:** The result for density estimation is particularly striking and insightful. The fact that the exponent in the rate of convergence changes is a non-obvious and profound finding. It shows that for complex, nonparametric problems, the cost of privacy is not merely equivalent to having less data but can change the intrinsic statistical nature of the problem. The paper also provides a clear explanation for why simple perturbation mechanisms (like adding Laplace noise to the data points) are suboptimal for this task, motivating the need for more sophisticated privacy mechanisms.

4.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The problem setup is clear, the theorems are stated precisely, and the main ideas of the proofs are communicated effectively. The authors do an excellent job of placing their work in the context of both the classical statistics literature (Warner, Tsybakov) and the modern privacy literature (Dwork et al., Wasserman & Zhou).

5.  **Constructive and Practical Mechanisms:** The authors don't just prove existence; they provide explicit and computationally efficient algorithms that achieve the optimal rates. The analysis of randomized response for the multinomial case and the development of private histogram and orthogonal series estimators for density estimation are valuable contributions in their own right.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

The paper is of very high quality, and any weaknesses are minor.

1.  **Dependence on High Dimensions (`d`):** In the multinomial case, the rate `d/(nα²)` implies that utility degrades rapidly with the number of categories `d`. For `d` on the order of `nα²` or larger, the error bound is trivial, suggesting estimation is impossible. While this is a correct and important consequence of the theory, a brief discussion on the severe implications for high-dimensional discrete data would strengthen the paper's impact.

2.  **Practicality of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The sampling strategy proposed in Section 4.3 for the orthogonal series estimator is elegant but complex. It involves several steps, including sampling a random vector `τ̃` and then sampling `Z` conditioned on the sign of an inner product. A short discussion on the practical implementation, particularly the efficiency of sampling from the conditional uniform distribution in high dimensions (`k`), would be beneficial for readers interested in applying the method.

3.  **Scope of `α`:** The analysis and rate dependencies (e.g., `α²`) are most meaningful for small `α` (e.g., `α ≤ 1`), which is the typical regime of interest for strong privacy guarantees. The paper states this assumption, but it would be helpful to briefly comment on the behavior for large `α` and how the rates smoothly recover the non-private rates as `α → ∞` (which they appear to do, though the bounds might not be sharp in that limit).

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is technically flawless. The application of information-theoretic lower bound techniques to the locally private channel is rigorous and correct. The analyses of the proposed estimators for the upper bounds are sound. The work is a complete and polished piece of theoretical research.

**Clarity:** The paper is a model of clarity. The introduction perfectly motivates the problem, connecting historical work by Warner with modern differential privacy. The mathematical formalism is introduced cleanly, and the structure logically separates the two main problems being addressed.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While others have studied differential privacy from a statistical lens (e.g., Wasserman & Zhou, Smith), this paper's focus on *local* privacy and its complete, sharp characterization of minimax rates for these fundamental problems is novel. It significantly advances the line of work on the statistical foundations of privacy by providing much stronger and more general lower bounds (on population parameters) than much of the prior literature, which often focused on sample quantities.

**Significance:** This paper is of landmark significance. It provides definitive answers to fundamental questions about the cost of local privacy. The results will be a crucial reference point for anyone designing locally private algorithms or analyzing their utility. The finding that the convergence rate exponent can change is a deep insight that will likely spur further research into which statistical problems exhibit this behavior. Researchers and practitioners now have a clear benchmark against which to measure their locally private estimation algorithms. This work lays the groundwork for a broader research program of minimax analysis for a wide range of statistical tasks under privacy constraints.
