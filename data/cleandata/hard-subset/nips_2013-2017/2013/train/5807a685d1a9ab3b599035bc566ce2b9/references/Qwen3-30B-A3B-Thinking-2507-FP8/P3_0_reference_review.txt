

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary
This paper bridges classical privacy techniques (Warner's randomized response) with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory to characterize the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The authors derive sharp minimax rates for two key problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation under α-local differential privacy (LDP). For multinomial estimation, they show that LDP reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d, with Warner's randomized response achieving this optimal rate. For density estimation of β-smooth functions, they prove the rate degrades from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)), with efficient histogram and orthogonal series estimators matching these rates. The paper makes a compelling case that Warner's 50-year-old technique is optimal for probability estimation under LDP.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical rigor and novelty**: The paper provides sharp minimax lower and upper bounds, a significant advancement over prior work that typically offered only upper bounds. The characterization of the density estimation rate change (from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2)) is particularly insightful and reveals a fundamental difference between discrete and continuous estimation under LDP.

2. **Historical connection**: The paper elegantly connects Warner's seminal 1965 work on randomized response to modern differential privacy, demonstrating that his technique is optimal for multinomial estimation. This historical perspective adds significant value to the theoretical contribution.

3. **Practical relevance**: The authors provide efficiently computable algorithms (including projection-based estimators) that achieve the optimal rates, making their theoretical results applicable to real-world privacy-preserving statistical inference.

4. **Clear exposition**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with careful development of the necessary theoretical machinery (information bounds, minimax risk bounds) and clear presentation of results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on two specific statistical problems (multinomial and density estimation) without addressing more complex settings like regression, classification, or high-dimensional statistics. While these are important foundational problems, the scope is narrower than the broader implications suggested in the introduction.

2. **Lack of empirical validation**: Despite providing theoretically optimal algorithms, the paper lacks any empirical evaluation on synthetic or real datasets to validate the theoretical rates. This is particularly surprising given the practical implications of the results.

3. **Limited comparison with alternative mechanisms**: For density estimation, the paper notes that direct Laplace perturbation cannot achieve the optimal rate, but doesn't thoroughly compare with other privacy mechanisms that might be practical for implementation.

4. **Assumption of α-local privacy**: The paper assumes α-local privacy (a strong constraint) but doesn't explore how results might scale with more relaxed privacy guarantees (e.g., ε-differential privacy in the central model).

5. **Practical implementation considerations**: While the paper mentions that estimators are "efficiently computable," it doesn't address practical implementation challenges in large-scale settings, such as communication constraints in distributed LDP.

## Significance and Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution to the growing literature on statistical inference under differential privacy. It advances the field by providing sharp minimax rates rather than just upper bounds, which is a substantial theoretical improvement. The connection between Warner's randomized response and modern LDP is particularly valuable, as it shows that optimal privacy mechanisms for basic estimation problems have been known for decades.

The paper stands out from previous work (e.g., Chaudhuri & Hsu, 2013; Duchi et al., 2013) by providing tight minimax rates rather than just upper bounds. While Smith (2011) also studied minimax rates for private estimation, this paper provides sharper bounds and addresses a wider range of problems.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. While the scope is somewhat limited to specific estimation problems, the theoretical results are sharp and the connection to historical work is compelling.

The main limitation is the lack of empirical validation, which would have strengthened the practical relevance of the results. However, as a theoretical contribution to the specific problems addressed, this paper is of high quality and significance.

I recommend acceptance to NIPS. This paper provides a fundamental theoretical contribution that will inform future work on privacy-preserving statistical inference. The sharp minimax rates derived here establish important benchmarks for evaluating privacy mechanisms in estimation problems.

## Final Recommendation
**Recommendation**: Accept

**Strengths**: Theoretical rigor, historical connection, sharp minimax rates, clear exposition.

**Weaknesses**: Limited scope, lack of empirical validation, limited comparison with alternative mechanisms.

**Overall**: This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. The paper's main results are novel and significant, and the connection between classical and modern privacy techniques is particularly valuable. While the lack of empirical results is a minor drawback, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
