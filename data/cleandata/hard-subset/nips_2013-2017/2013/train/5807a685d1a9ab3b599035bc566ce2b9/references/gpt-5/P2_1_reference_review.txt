Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), focusing on population (not sample) estimation. It develops a minimax decision-theoretic framework for LDP and derives sharp rates with matching algorithms for two canonical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The key technical tool is an information-contraction bound under LDP that controls KL/mutual information in terms of total variation between underlying distributions; combined with Le Cam/Fano, this yields lower bounds that hold uniformly over all α-LDP channels. For multinomials in d dimensions, the minimax mean-squared error scales like min{1, d/(nα^2)}, implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα^2/d for small α; analogous sharp ℓ1 rates are also given. The classical randomized response mechanism (with a slight modification) is shown to be minimax optimal, and simple Laplace perturbation also attains the rate.

For density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1], the paper shows a qualitative change in the polynomial rate: the non-private n^{-2β/(2β+1)} becomes (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under LDP. It proves this lower bound and provides matching upper bounds via: (i) privatized histograms for β=1 (Lipschitz densities), and (ii) a novel orthogonal-series mechanism that privatizes basis coefficient vectors without simple additive noise, achieving the optimal rate for all β≥1. The paper also explains why naive additive Laplace noise on observations or series coefficients is suboptimal in this setting (via deconvolution limitations).

This work connects and extends prior lines: Warner’s randomized response, the DP literature (e.g., Dwork et al., Hardt–Talwar, Wasserman–Zhou), local privacy and learnability (Kasiviswanathan et al.), and information-theoretic minimax lower bounds (Yang–Barron, Yu). It is, to my knowledge, the first to give sharp population minimax rates under LDP and to identify the rate-exponent degradation for nonparametric density estimation, with matching mechanisms.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds rest on clear information-contraction inequalities specialized to LDP and standard testing reductions. The bounds track α and problem geometry (via TV distances or variance of indicator sets) and are used carefully to build packings that yield sharp rates. The achievability results are constructive and computationally reasonable: randomized response for multinomials; privatized histograms; and a carefully designed privatization of orthogonal coefficients that scales B = O(√k/α) to maintain unbiasedness and control variance. The argument that additive Laplace on raw samples cannot reach the lower bound is well-motivated via deconvolution results.
- Completeness: Main proofs are deferred to a longer version; within NeurIPS norms for theory papers, this is acceptable, though including more proof sketches (especially for the mutual information variational bound and the series mechanism) would strengthen the submission.
- Assumptions and scope: Focus is on non-interactive (memoryless) LDP channels; this is a meaningful and widely used regime, but it should be made explicit that interactive protocols might alter constants or possibly rates. The density results assume a bounded orthonormal basis and periodic boundary conditions (via trigonometric basis); this is standard in nonparametrics but worth discussing vis-à-vis other bases and boundary corrections. The series estimator currently requires knowledge of β to set k; adaptivity under LDP is an interesting open direction.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem setup, risk definition, and privacy model are precise. The statements of the main theorems communicate the dependence on n, d, α, and β. The construction and analysis of mechanisms are presented with useful intuition (e.g., why series privatization differs from Laplace). Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Provide brief intuition for the variational mutual information bound and where the supremum over sets S comes from.
- Summarize all obtained rates (non-private vs LDP) in a small comparison table to aid readers.
- Include pseudocode for the orthogonal-series privatization and discuss its runtime and sampling efficiency explicitly, especially how to sample uniformly from the constrained hypercube efficiently as k grows.
- Discuss the regime of α (small vs moderate) where the asymptotics kick in, and constants in the d/(nα^2) term for multinomials.

Originality
High. The paper introduces a general route to minimax lower bounds under LDP, derives sharp rates for multinomial and density estimation, and designs new locally private mechanisms achieving the bounds. The identification of the exponent change for nonparametric density under LDP, and the optimality of a nontrivial series-privatization mechanism, are new and insightful. The demonstration that randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomials underlines an elegant link between classical privacy ideas and modern DP.

Significance
High. The results are foundational for understanding statistical efficiency under LDP. They quantify precise privacy–utility tradeoffs and will influence mechanism design in practical local-privacy systems. The work provides benchmarks and lower bounds that subsequent research can build upon, and mechanisms that are implementable.

Pros
- Sharp minimax rates under LDP for central problems, with matching algorithms.
- Clear characterization of sample complexity degradation: nα^2/d for multinomials; exponent shift for densities.
- Conceptually novel and practically relevant mechanisms beyond additive noise.
- Bridges classical randomized response with modern DP and decision theory.

Cons
- No empirical demonstrations to illustrate constants or practical performance.
- Non-interactive restriction; no discussion whether interaction could improve rates.
- Series estimator requires knowledge of β; adaptivity not addressed.
- Some technical steps deferred; more intuition/proof sketches would aid accessibility.
- Implementation details (efficiency, acceptance rates) of the series sampling scheme are not fully spelled out.

Questions for authors
- Can any interaction in the local model improve the rates for these problems, or are these lower bounds interaction-robust?
- Is there a privacy-preserving method to adaptively choose k (e.g., via private Lepski or cross-validation) that preserves optimal rates?
- Can you quantify the computational complexity and sampling efficiency of the orthogonal-series mechanism for large k?

Minor comments
- Clarify constants cℓ, cu and the range of α where the approximation e^α−1 ≈ α is used in rate summaries.
- Explicitly state the acceptance probability or provide an efficient sampler for the constrained hypercube set in the series mechanism.
- A brief discussion of boundary effects for densities on [0,1] would be helpful.

Recommendation
Strong accept. This is a high-quality, original, and significant theoretical contribution that establishes core limits and optimal methods for learning under local differential privacy. My confidence is high.
