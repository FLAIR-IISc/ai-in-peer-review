

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary

This paper bridges minimax decision theory with differential privacy to characterize the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation problems. The authors study minimax convergence rates under α-local differential privacy constraints, which formalizes Warner's original motivation for privacy in survey collection. They provide sharp lower bounds and matching upper bounds for two key problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. For multinomial estimation, they show that privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d, with Warner's randomized response mechanism achieving optimal rates. For density estimation, they demonstrate a surprising departure from classical minimax rates, showing that privacy changes the convergence rate from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (α²n)^(-2β/(2β+2)). The paper builds on Duchi et al. [9] while making significant new contributions to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide both tight lower and upper bounds, with careful attention to the mathematical details. The proofs are complete and well-motivated, with the authors explicitly addressing the limitations of previous approaches (e.g., why direct Laplace perturbation fails for density estimation). The paper presents a complete theoretical treatment rather than a partial result, with all claims properly supported.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant original contribution by applying minimax theory to local privacy constraints, which hasn't been done before in this rigorous way. The characterization of the privacy penalty as an effective sample size reduction (n → nα²/d) for multinomial estimation is novel and surprising. The discovery that density estimation under privacy has a different convergence rate exponent (2β/(2β+2) instead of 2β/(2β+1)) is particularly insightful and contrasts with classical results. The historical note that Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism is actually optimal for this problem adds an interesting dimension to the work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and carefully defined. The main results are presented clearly with well-structured theorems and propositions. The paper efficiently balances technical depth with accessibility for the target audience. The connection between the theoretical results and practical algorithms (e.g., the randomized response mechanism) is particularly well-explained.

**Significance:** This work provides the first complete theoretical characterization of the privacy-utility trade-off in probability estimation problems. The results have direct implications for the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures in practice, showing that simple mechanisms like randomized response can be optimal. The paper establishes a formal framework for understanding how privacy constraints fundamentally change statistical estimation rates, which will guide future research in privacy-preserving statistics.

## Weaknesses

**Limited scope:** While the paper covers two important problems (multinomial and density estimation), the scope is somewhat narrow. The authors focus on specific classes of distributions (multinomial and Sobolev spaces) without discussing how their framework might extend to other statistical models. A brief discussion of how their results might generalize to other settings would strengthen the paper's broader impact.

**Algorithmic details:** While the paper provides theoretical algorithms that achieve the optimal rates, it doesn't discuss computational complexity beyond "efficiently computable." For the orthogonal series estimator, the sampling strategy (19) might be computationally intensive for large k, which could be a practical limitation for high-dimensional problems.

**Comparison to related work:** The paper references Duchi et al. [9] but could more thoroughly compare its results to other recent work on privacy and statistical estimation. For example, there's a recent paper by Chaudhuri and Hsu [5] on convergence rates for differentially private empirical risk minimization that might provide useful context.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistics by providing the first sharp minimax analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The theoretical results are rigorous, the connections to historical work (Warner's randomized response) are insightful, and the practical implications are clear. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a fundamental question in privacy research.

I recommend **acceptance** for NIPS. The paper's significance lies in its formal characterization of the privacy-utility trade-off, which provides a theoretical foundation for designing optimal privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The results have immediate relevance for practitioners working on privacy-preserving data analysis, and they open up new theoretical directions for future research.

The minor weaknesses noted (limited scope, computational details) do not detract from the paper's core contribution, which is both novel and important. The paper would be strengthened by slightly more discussion of how the framework might extend beyond the specific problems studied, but this is a minor point for an already strong paper.

This work represents a significant step toward a unified theoretical understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference, and it aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical advances with practical implications. The paper is well-positioned to influence both theoretical and applied research in privacy-preserving statistics.
