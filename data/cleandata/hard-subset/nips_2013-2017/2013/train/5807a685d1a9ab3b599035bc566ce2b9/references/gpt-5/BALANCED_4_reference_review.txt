Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), focusing on population-level minimax risk. The authors formalize an α-local privacy constraint via a likelihood ratio bound (Evfimievski et al.) and develop lower bounds on private minimax risks by combining Le Cam/Fano techniques with information bounds that translate privacy constraints into tight KL/MI contraction (building on their prior technical results). Two core problems are analyzed:

- Multinomial probability estimation (θ ∈ Δd): They show that under LDP the effective sample size degrades from n to nα2/d (for small α), yielding sharp rates in both ℓ2 and ℓ1 loss. They also demonstrate that (a slight modification of) Warner’s randomized response attains the minimax rate, as does simple Laplace perturbation of one-hot encodings.

- Nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ: classical non-private rates n−2β/(2β+1) degrade under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), i.e., privacy changes the polynomial exponent. They give matching, efficiently implementable upper bounds via privatized histograms (β=1) and an orthogonal series estimator with a carefully designed unbiased privatization mechanism (not just naive Laplace noise), establishing sharpness.

This work connects and extends strands in privacy (Dwork et al.; Wasserman & Zhou; Kasiviswanathan et al.; Hardt & Talwar), classical minimax theory (Le Cam, Fano, Tsybakov, Yang & Barron), randomized response (Warner), and deconvolution (Carroll & Hall). A notable contribution is moving beyond accuracy of sample functionals to population minimax risk under LDP, and showing that the optimal mechanisms can be simple and interpretable.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The paper provides a clean decision-theoretic framework for LDP and shows how to translate privacy constraints into information bounds that drive minimax lower bounds.

- Sharp results: For both multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, lower and upper bounds match (up to constants), including the striking exponent change for nonparametric problems.

- Mechanism design: The identification of (modified) randomized response as optimal for multinomials, and the novel unbiased privatization for orthogonal series, are both elegant and practically relevant. The deconvolution argument clarifies why naive additive Laplace noise cannot be optimal for density estimation.

- Breadth and impact: The results quantify privacy–utility trade-offs in central tasks and are likely to influence subsequent work on LDP statistics and learning.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity/typos in multinomial mechanism: In Eq. (10), [Z]j is said to equal xj with some probability and “−xj” otherwise, yet later Z is treated as Bernoulli and θ̂ is projected onto the simplex. If xj∈{0,1}, outputting −xj produces values in {0,−1}. This looks like a typo for 1−xj (bit-flip). Please correct and ensure consistency of the domain of Z and the variance calculation. Similarly, the lower bound (8) contains a term “√(nα2)” inside a min for an MSE rate; this appears dimensionally inconsistent and likely a typo.

- Precise constants and regimes: The results are stated for α∈[0,1]. It would help to explicitly discuss the behavior for larger α (interpolation to non-private rates), and to separate constant and small-α regimes. For Proposition 2, the second term √(α n−3/4) looks dimensionally odd; please check constants and exponents.

- Algorithmic details: For the orthogonal-series privatization (Sec. 4.3), give bounds on the expected runtime/acceptance rate of the rejection sampling and specify the constant ck and its dependence on k more explicitly. A brief proof sketch of the α-LDP property of (19) would also aid readability.

- Interactive mechanisms: The analysis assumes non-interactive (memoryless) channels. Please comment on whether interactivity could improve the minimax rates in these problems, or cite results that show no improvement (if known). This will situate the optimality claims more clearly.

- Adaptivity and model selection: For density estimation, can k be selected adaptively (e.g., via private cross-validation) while maintaining the optimal rate? A short discussion or a corollary would broaden applicability.

- Beyond pure LDP: Briefly discuss extensions to approximate LDP ((α,δ)) or Rényi/zCDP variants and whether the rates or mechanisms change materially.

- High-probability guarantees: Most results are in expectation. High-probability versions (with explicit tail bounds) would be valuable for practitioners.

Quality
Technically strong, with careful use of information-theoretic tools to obtain sharp lower bounds and matching constructive upper bounds. Some minor inconsistencies/typos should be fixed, and a few proof details could be expanded (or pointers to the longer version emphasized).

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized. The main ideas are clearly conveyed. The noted typos and a few notational glitches should be corrected. Adding a concise overview figure/table comparing non-private vs LDP rates would help readers.

Originality
High. The integration of LDP with population minimax decision theory and the discovery that privacy changes the nonparametric exponent are novel and insightful. The optimality of randomized response in this formal sense is also a valuable perspective.

Significance
High. The results provide a foundation for understanding statistical efficiency under LDP and will likely inform both theory and practice in privacy-preserving data analysis.

Arguments for acceptance
- Sharp, conceptually clean theory linking LDP and minimax risk.
- Matching lower/upper bounds; identification of optimal mechanisms.
- Novel and important insight that LDP alters nonparametric convergence exponents.
- Relevance to core NeurIPS topics (privacy, statistical learning, information theory).

Arguments against acceptance
- Some presentation issues (typos/inconsistencies) that may hinder reproducibility.
- Limited discussion of interactive protocols, approximate privacy, and adaptive procedures.
- Empirical illustrations are absent; small simulations could aid intuition.

Recommendation
Accept after minor revisions. The paper makes a substantial and technically solid contribution to the theory of learning under local privacy, with clear implications and well-chosen exemplar problems. Addressing the clarity issues and adding brief discussions as suggested would further strengthen the manuscript.
