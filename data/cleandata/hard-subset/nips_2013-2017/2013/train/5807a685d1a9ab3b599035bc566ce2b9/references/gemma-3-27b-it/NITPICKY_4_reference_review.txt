This paper attempts to bridge the gap between classical statistical estimation, differential privacy, and minimax theory, providing convergence rate analyses for multinomial and density estimation under local privacy constraints. While the motivations are sound and the topic is timely, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a generally overly meticulous approach to presentation that ultimately detracts from its overall impact.

The introduction is adequate, though verbose. Referencing Warner’s original motivation is helpful for context, but the extended discussion feels somewhat tangential to the core technical contributions. The transition to differential privacy is standard.

The formal definitions are generally correct, but the notation is inconsistent. For example, the use of both *Z* and *X* to represent different sets of random variables is confusing. The definition of α-local differential privacy (1) is standard, but a more concise and commonly used formulation exists. The use of σ-fields feels unnecessarily cumbersome given the context.

Section 2, “Background and Problem Formulation,” is excessively detailed and verbose. While referencing related work is important, citing a long list of papers (e.g., [17, 25, 26, 22]) without specific comment on their relevance feels like padding. Restating theorems from Duchi et al. [9] within the paper, while intended to make the presentation self-contained, contributes to the paper’s length without adding substantial value. Lemma 1 is presented with multiple variations (5a, 5b) that are not clearly differentiated in terms of applicability. The KL-divergence and total variation distance definitions are standard but feel unnecessary for a NIPS audience.

The analysis in Section 3 is mathematically correct, but the presentation is clunky. Theorem 1 provides useful bounds, but the constants *cℓ* and *cu* are introduced without explanation or justification. The claim that Warner's randomized response is optimal is interesting, but the discussion is brief and lacks a thorough comparison to alternative mechanisms. The notation *an . bn* and *an ≍ bn* is non-standard and should be replaced with standard big-O notation. The discussion regarding the optimality of randomized response feels somewhat overstated; while it achieves the optimal rate, its practical usability might be limited in certain scenarios.

Section 4 on density estimation suffers from similar issues. The discussion of Sobolev spaces feels excessive and the connection to the broader literature on density estimation could be more clearly articulated. The proposed sampling scheme (19) appears complex and lacks a clear justification for its efficiency compared to simpler alternatives. The claim that perturbing samples directly is suboptimal is somewhat vague and requires more detailed explanation. The use of Proposition 3 to justify the optimal convergence rate is sound, but the overall presentation feels unnecessarily complicated.

Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor formatting issues, such as inconsistent use of boldface and italics. The notation is often heavy and could be simplified. The writing style is overly formal and lacks conciseness. The paper also lacks a clear discussion of the limitations of the proposed methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The topic is relevant and timely, addressing an important intersection of privacy and statistical inference.
*   The theoretical results are mathematically correct and provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.
*   The paper demonstrates a thorough understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is excessively detailed and verbose, making it difficult to follow the main ideas.
*   The notation is inconsistent and often cumbersome.
*   The presentation is clunky and lacks conciseness.
*   The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting issues and stylistic flaws.
*   The overall contribution, while technically sound, does not represent a significant advance over existing work.
*   The meticulous attention to minor details overshadows the broader significance of the results.

**Overall assessment:**

Despite the technical correctness of the results, the numerous flaws and inconsistencies throughout the paper lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying theory, the presentation is simply too cumbersome and lacking in clarity to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The paper feels like a collection of technically correct, but poorly integrated, ideas rather than a cohesive and impactful research contribution. A substantial rewrite addressing the issues outlined above is necessary before this paper would be suitable for publication.
