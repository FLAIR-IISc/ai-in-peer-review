This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem: understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation. The authors elegantly connect Warner’s original motivations for privacy with the modern framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory, yielding sharp rates for both multinomial and density estimation. The results are technically sound, and the paper is generally well-written, although dense. The novelty of framing this problem within a rigorous minimax framework, combined with the surprising implications (e.g., the different rate for density estimation), makes this a strong submission.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of differential privacy, minimax theory, and classical statistical estimation problems is a novel and valuable contribution. The differing convergence rate for density estimation under privacy is particularly surprising and insightful.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides clear definitions, rigorous proofs (detailed in the linked work [9]), and sharp bounds. The authors convincingly demonstrate the optimality of certain mechanisms, like randomized response.
*   **Foundational Importance:** This work lays the groundwork for a more principled understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. It provides a quantitative framework for analyzing trade-offs, which is crucial for practical applications.
*   **Connection to Classical Work:** The authors effectively bridge the gap between early work on privacy (Warner’s randomized response) and modern theoretical frameworks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density Estimation Complexity:** While the theoretical result for density estimation is compelling, the practical implications aren't fully explored. The reliance on orthogonal series might limit applicability to some real-world scenarios. The explanation of the need for specific, non-standard, noise mechanisms is somewhat buried.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical results. While this is understandable given the complexity of the analysis, some limited empirical validation (even simulations) could strengthen the claims and provide intuition for the practical impact of the results. Specifically, for density estimation, illustrating the impact of α on the estimated density would be beneficial.
*   **Dense Writing:** The paper is quite dense and requires careful reading. Some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations and examples. The notation is substantial, which can be a barrier for readers unfamiliar with the field.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of knowing the basis functions in the density estimation portion is somewhat restrictive.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
*   The results are sharp and well-supported by rigorous analysis.
*   The work has the potential to influence future research in this area and provide a foundational framework for designing privacy-preserving statistical methods.
*   The connection to classical work adds historical context and highlights the enduring relevance of early ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical evaluation weakens the practical impact of the theoretical results.
*   The density estimation results, while theoretically interesting, might be difficult to apply in practice due to the reliance on orthogonal series and the complex noise mechanisms.
*   The dense writing style could make the paper inaccessible to some readers.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend acceptance. The novelty and theoretical significance of this work outweigh the limitations. The paper addresses a fundamental problem with a rigorous and insightful approach, and the results have the potential to significantly impact the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While the lack of extensive empirical validation and dense writing are concerns, they are not sufficient to outweigh the substantial contributions of this paper. I would categorize this as a high-quality submission that pushes the boundaries of the field and is worthy of publication at NIPS. The authors have identified a meaningful tradeoff, provided a rigorous analysis of its bounds, and shown that existing methods can achieve optimal performance. This work is likely to inspire further research and practical applications.
