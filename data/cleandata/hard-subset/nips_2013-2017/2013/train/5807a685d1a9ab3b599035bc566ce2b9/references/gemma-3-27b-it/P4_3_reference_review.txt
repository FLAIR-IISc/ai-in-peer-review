This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building on the framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner’s classic randomized response mechanism and provide sharp convergence rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems under local privacy constraints. They establish a connection between the amount of privacy (quantified by the parameter α) and the achievable estimation accuracy, demonstrating a reduction in effective sample size for multinomial estimation and a modified convergence rate for density estimation. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important problem in the intersection of statistics and privacy.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper rigorously applies minimax theory and differential privacy to establish fundamental limits on estimation accuracy under privacy constraints. The use of KL-divergence and mutual information bounds is appropriate and well-executed.
*   **Sharp Results:** The convergence rates derived are shown to be optimal (or nearly so) for both multinomial and density estimation, establishing a tight characterization of the privacy-utility trade-off. The attainability results for Warner’s randomized response are particularly compelling.
*   **Clear Exposition:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible. The notation is consistent, and the proofs are logically structured (though detailed proofs are relegated to a separate work, which is standard).
*   **Relevance to Existing Literature:** The paper appropriately situates its contributions within the existing literature on differential privacy, statistical decision theory, and privacy-preserving data analysis, referencing key works in each area. The connection to Warner’s original work is well-highlighted.
*   **Practical Implications:** The results have practical implications for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures in various applications, such as surveys, data collection, and machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dependency on Prior Work:** A significant portion of the theoretical machinery (Lemmas and Propositions) is referenced from a prior work [9]. While referencing prior work is common, the reliance on [9] is substantial, and the current paper does not provide complete self-contained proofs. Reviewing the prior work is necessary to fully assess the validity of the results.
*   **Density Estimation Complexity:** While achieving optimal rates for density estimation is a significant result, the practical implementation of the proposed estimator (based on orthogonal series) appears relatively complex compared to simpler approaches like kernel density estimation, even with privacy mechanisms. The computational cost may be a concern in high-dimensional settings.
*   **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** The paper focuses primarily on densities with bounded derivatives and uses a trigonometric basis. Exploring other basis functions or density classes (e.g., unbounded densities) could broaden the applicability of the results.
*   **Notation could be improved:** The notation, while consistent, is dense. More frequent reminders of the definitions of symbols (e.g., Fβ, Mn) could improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental question in privacy-preserving statistical inference with strong theoretical rigor.
*   The results are novel and provide sharp bounds on the privacy-utility trade-off.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The work has the potential to influence the design of practical privacy-preserving statistical procedures.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The substantial reliance on prior work [9] makes it difficult to fully evaluate the paper without reviewing that work.
*   The practical complexity of the proposed density estimation scheme may limit its applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of privacy and statistical efficiency. The theoretical results are strong, the exposition is clear, and the work is relevant to a growing area of research. While the reliance on prior work and the potential complexity of implementation are minor concerns, they do not outweigh the significant strengths of the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (e.g., adding more detailed explanations or reminders of key notations).
