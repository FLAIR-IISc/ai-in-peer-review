

This paper investigates the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation problems under local differential privacy constraints, using minimax decision theory to characterize optimal rates. The authors focus on two core problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, for multinomial estimation in d dimensions, they establish that the minimax rate of convergence under α-local privacy is Θ(d/(nα²)) in ℓ₂-error and Θ(√d/(nα²)) in ℓ₁-error, demonstrating that local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d. They prove this rate is tight by showing Warner's randomized response (a 1965 technique) achieves the optimal rate. Second, for density estimation under Sobolev smoothness constraints, they establish a new minimax rate of Θ((nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))), differing from the classical rate of Θ(n^(-2β/(2β+1))) by the exponent in the denominator. They provide matching upper bounds using histogram and orthogonal series estimators, showing that direct perturbation of samples is suboptimal.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The authors provide rigorous minimax lower bounds using Le Cam's two-point method and Fano's inequality, combined with information-theoretic bounds on mutual information under local privacy. The proofs are complete and well-motivated, with clear connections to prior work on differential privacy [9, 24] and minimax theory [22, 25, 26]. The paper correctly situates its contributions relative to Warner's original work [23] and modern differential privacy literature [1, 11, 13, 16, 21], demonstrating that the optimal privacy mechanism for multinomial estimation has been known for decades.

The novelty is significant. While prior work on privacy and statistical inference focused on differential privacy for data release [1, 11, 13, 16], this paper is the first to establish sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy. The density estimation result is particularly novel, as it reveals a different convergence rate structure compared to classical non-private minimax theory. The paper also resolves an open question about the optimality of Warner's randomized response, showing it achieves the minimax rate for multinomial estimation.

The significance of the results is clear. The paper provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical utility, with direct implications for privacy-preserving survey design and data collection. The authors' identification of optimal mechanisms (e.g., randomized response for multinomial estimation) offers practical guidance for practitioners. The framework they develop can be extended to other statistical problems under privacy constraints.

Strengths:
1. Rigorous establishment of sharp minimax rates for two important statistical problems under local privacy.
2. Clear connection to Warner's 1965 work, demonstrating historical relevance.
3. Precise characterization of how privacy affects convergence rates (e.g., effective sample size reduction).
4. Comprehensive treatment of both lower and upper bounds with matching rates.
5. Well-motivated and carefully referenced relation to prior work.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited discussion of how the results extend to other privacy models beyond local privacy.
2. Brief treatment of computational complexity for the proposed estimators (only noted as "efficiently computable").
3. Minimal discussion of practical implementation challenges for density estimation in real-world scenarios.
4. The paper does not explicitly compare the computational cost of their proposed estimators with other privacy-preserving methods.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Establishes sharp minimax rates for two fundamental statistical problems under local privacy, filling an important gap in the literature.
- Provides optimal mechanisms (e.g., randomized response) with theoretical guarantees.
- Demonstrates the necessity of specific privacy mechanisms for optimal performance.
- Offers a framework for analyzing other statistical problems under privacy constraints.
- Clear, well-structured, and technically sound contribution to the field.

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Focuses exclusively on local privacy, which is a specific case of differential privacy; the broader implications for other privacy models are not thoroughly explored.
- The density estimation results, while novel, may have limited immediate practical impact compared to the multinomial estimation results.
- The paper could better articulate how the theoretical results translate to practical applications.

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS, as it bridges statistical decision theory and privacy, addressing a fundamental problem in modern data analysis. The technical contributions are significant, well-supported, and advance the theoretical understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results provide clear, quantifiable trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility that will inform both theoretical and applied work in the field. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a substantial contribution to the literature.
