## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

**Summary:**

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of statistical estimation under the constraints of *local* differential privacy. The authors build upon recent work linking differential privacy with minimax decision theory to establish sharp convergence rate bounds for two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation.  A key contribution is demonstrating a trade-off between privacy (controlled by the parameter α) and statistical efficiency, expressed as a reduction in effective sample size or a degradation in convergence rate. The work effectively bridges classical privacy concepts (Warner's randomized response) with modern differential privacy frameworks, offering both theoretical guarantees and practical algorithmic implementations.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within a growing body of research on privacy and statistical inference, citing key works from Warner (1965) to more recent advances in differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006; Chaudhuri et al., 2012). It builds directly on the authors' prior work (Duchi et al., 2013) in establishing information-theoretic bounds for privacy-preserving inference. It distinguishes itself from existing differential privacy literature by focusing on *local* privacy – where privacy is guaranteed on an individual data point level – and providing tight minimax optimality results. The connection to classical minimax theory (Le Cam's method, Fano's inequality) is well articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper employs a solid mathematical framework, leveraging differential privacy, minimax theory, and information-theoretic inequalities to derive precise convergence rate bounds. The proofs (referenced to the accompanying technical report) appear well-structured.
* **Sharpness of Bounds:**  The authors achieve *sharp* minimax rates. This is a significant accomplishment, as demonstrating both lower and matching upper bounds is considerably more challenging than establishing just one.
* **Practical Relevance:** The analysis isn't purely theoretical. The authors show that Warner's randomized response method achieves optimal convergence in the multinomial estimation problem and provide efficiently implementable algorithms for density estimation. This connects the theory to real-world applications.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the broader context of privacy and statistics. The notation is standard and well-defined.
* **Novel Insights:** The observation that local privacy impacts density estimation differently than multinomial estimation (leading to a different rate exponent) is a significant and non-trivial result.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density Estimation Complexity:** While the theoretical analysis for density estimation is strong, the proposed algorithm, relying on orthogonal series, feels less immediately practical. The selection of basis functions (trigonometric) and the parameter *k* (number of series terms) could be discussed more fully. A comparison to kernel density estimation with privacy guarantees, even if sub-optimal, would be useful.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any empirical evaluation.  While the focus is on theoretical guarantees, some simulations demonstrating the degradation in performance with varying α would significantly strengthen the work and help assess the practical impact of the derived bounds.
* **Technical Depth Barrier:**  Sections 2 and 3 contain a lot of definitions and notation (KL-divergence, total variation distance, minimax framework, etc.). While these are standard to the field, a reader unfamiliar with these concepts may find the initial sections dense and difficult to follow. More intuitive explanations alongside the formal definitions would be helpful.
* **Discussion of Limitations:**  While the paper acknowledges the differences in how privacy impacts density estimation versus multinomial estimation, a broader discussion of the limitations of local privacy (e.g., sensitivity to the choice of channel *Q*) would be valuable.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* The sharpness of the derived minimax rates is a strong indication of the quality of the analysis.
* The work bridges important concepts from differential privacy, minimax theory, and classical statistical estimation.
* The connection to Warner's randomized response provides historical context and validates existing privacy-preserving techniques.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation is a noticeable omission.
* The proposed density estimation algorithm feels less immediately practical.
* The technical density of the initial sections could be a barrier to a broader audience.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to NIPS. The theoretical results are rigorous and insightful, and the connection to practical algorithms is valuable. While the lack of empirical validation is a drawback, the strength of the theoretical analysis outweighs this concern. The paper addresses a difficult and important problem, and it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend that the authors consider adding a section with some simulations illustrating the theoretical results and briefly discussing the limitations of local privacy.  Some clarification regarding the practical considerations of the density estimation algorithm would also be beneficial.
