

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation problems under local differential privacy constraints. The authors employ minimax decision theory to establish sharp lower and upper bounds on estimation rates for two key problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. Their main contribution is characterizing how privacy constraints degrade estimation performance, showing that for multinomial estimation the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, and for density estimation with β continuous derivatives, the convergence rate changes from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2).

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with rigorous mathematical proofs. The authors build upon established theoretical frameworks from minimax estimation and differential privacy, which lends credibility to their approach. The connection between Warner's classical randomized response mechanism and the optimal privacy-preserving strategy for multinomial estimation is a particularly interesting insight, though it's somewhat surprising that this was not previously established.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates for privacy-constrained estimation.
2. The authors provide both lower and matching upper bounds, which is a significant strength in theoretical work.
3. The paper connects two important fields (privacy and minimax estimation) in a meaningful way.
4. The mathematical treatment is thorough and rigorous, with appropriate references to related work.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results. While theoretical work is valuable, NIPS generally expects at least some experimental evidence to support claims about the practical implications of the theory. Without this, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical bounds.

2. The authors' claim that "Warner's randomized response procedure [23] enjoys optimal sample complexity" is overstated. Warner's method has been known for decades, and the paper doesn't provide significant new insights into why it's optimal beyond the theoretical bounds. This is not a novel contribution but rather a confirmation of an existing method.

3. The paper doesn't sufficiently address alternative privacy mechanisms or compare its results to other approaches in the literature. For instance, the paper mentions that "Laplace perturbation" is suboptimal but doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art privacy-preserving estimation techniques.

4. The significance of the results is somewhat limited by the lack of practical guidance. The paper establishes theoretical rates but doesn't discuss how these rates translate to practical privacy-utility trade-offs, which is crucial for practitioners.

5. The paper doesn't adequately distinguish its contributions from prior work on minimax rates under privacy constraints. For example, the paper cites Duchi et al. [9] but doesn't sufficiently explain how their work differs from or extends previous minimax analyses in differential privacy.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the literature on privacy-preserving statistical estimation, but it falls short of NIPS's expectations in several key areas. While the theoretical results are rigorous and interesting, the lack of empirical validation, the limited novelty of the main insight (Warner's method is optimal), and the insufficient differentiation from prior work weaken the paper's overall contribution.

The paper would benefit significantly from:
1. Empirical validation of the theoretical bounds
2. A more thorough comparison with alternative privacy mechanisms
3. A clearer articulation of how the results advance the state of the art beyond prior work
4. More practical discussion of the privacy-utility trade-offs

The paper is technically sound but doesn't present a compelling case for why it should be accepted at NIPS over other theoretical papers in the field. The theoretical results are valuable but not sufficiently novel or impactful to justify acceptance without empirical support or more substantial practical implications.

**Summary of pro/con points:**

Pro:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with both lower and upper bounds
- Clear connection between classical privacy methods (Warner) and modern privacy frameworks
- Well-written and well-organized theoretical exposition
- Addresses an important problem at the intersection of privacy and statistics

Con:
- Lacks empirical validation of theoretical results
- Overstates novelty of Warner's method being optimal (it's a known result)
- Insufficient comparison with alternative privacy mechanisms
- Limited practical guidance on implementing the theoretical results
- Doesn't clearly distinguish from prior minimax work under privacy constraints

Given these considerations, I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a theoretically valid contribution to the literature, it doesn't meet NIPS's standard for significance and novelty without empirical validation and a clearer articulation of how it advances the field beyond existing work.
