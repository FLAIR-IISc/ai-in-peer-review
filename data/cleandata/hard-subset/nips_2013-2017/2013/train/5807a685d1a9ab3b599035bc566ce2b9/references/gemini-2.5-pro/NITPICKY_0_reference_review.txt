### Review

**Summary**
This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of minimax decision theory. The authors focus on the setting of α-local differential privacy, where each data point is privatized independently. They derive sharp minimax convergence rates for two classical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation on `d` categories, they show that α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα^2/d`, and demonstrate that a variant of Warner's original randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For estimating β-smooth densities, they establish a more dramatic result: the optimal rate degrades from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, a change in the polynomial exponent of the rate. The authors provide computationally efficient algorithms—a private histogram and an orthogonal series estimator—that achieve these new optimal rates, thereby proving their lower bounds are sharp. The work connects the classical statistical literature on privacy (e.g., Warner [23]) with the modern computer science notion of differential privacy, providing a rigorous, quantitative analysis of the price of privacy.

**Strengths**
1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a highly important and fundamental question: what is the ultimate statistical cost of ensuring local differential privacy? By providing sharp minimax rates, the authors give a precise and definitive answer for two canonical estimation problems. This is a significant contribution to the burgeoning literature at the intersection of privacy and statistical machine learning.
2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The results are novel and technically strong. Establishing matching upper and lower bounds in minimax theory is challenging, and the authors accomplish this successfully. The finding that the convergence rate for density estimation changes its polynomial exponent, rather than just suffering a constant-factor degradation, is a particularly insightful and non-trivial discovery. It highlights that the impact of local privacy can be more severe in nonparametric settings.
3.  **Clarity of High-Level Ideas:** The paper's main narrative is compelling. It elegantly bridges classical minimax theory with local differential privacy, providing a clear theoretical framework for analyzing privacy-utility trade-offs. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work with respect to both historical and recent literature.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the significance of the contributions, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, ranging from notational inconsistencies and typos to unclear technical descriptions. This lack of precision and polish detracts from the overall quality and undermines confidence in the results.

1.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The paper relies critically on an external, unpublished manuscript ([9], an arXiv preprint by the same authors) for the proofs of its main information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) and the central lower bound for density estimation (Theorem 2). While common for conference submissions, this makes the paper's core technical claims impossible to verify from the text provided, rendering it an incomplete scientific document.

2.  **Clarity and Precision Issues:**
    *   **Inconsistent Mechanism Description (Sec 3.2):** The description of the randomized response mechanism for multinomial estimation is confusing. Equation (10) describes a procedure where `[Z]_j` takes values in `{-1, 0, 1}`. However, the subsequent privacy proof sketch refers to `z ∈ {0, 1}^d`. This is a direct contradiction. Furthermore, the likelihood ratio provided in the proof sketch appears to correspond to a channel of the form `Q(z|x) ∝ exp(-α/2 ||z-x||_1)`, which is not the channel described by the sampling procedure in Eq. (10). This section is in need of a major rewrite for clarity and correctness.
    *   **Anomalous Lower Bound (Thm 1):** In Equation (8), the lower bound for the `L2^2` risk is given as `c_l * min{1, sqrt(nα^2), d/(nα^2)}`. The `sqrt(nα^2)` term is not present in the matching upper bound and its origin is not explained. It seems dimensionally inconsistent with an estimation error and is likely a typo, but as written, it creates a gap between the lower and upper bounds.
    *   **Notational Errors:** At the end of Section 3.2, the paper concludes its rate analysis with `... . min{1, d/(nα^2)}`. The notation `.` was defined as `O()`, but the context implies it should be `≍` or `O()`. This is a small but telling error.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** In Section 4.3, the sampling strategy (19) is claimed to be "efficiently computable (for example by rejection sampling)" without any supporting analysis of complexity or acceptance probability. The constant `ck` in Eq. (20) is also defined implicitly, which is opaque.

3.  **Formatting and Presentation:**
    *   The bibliography is inconsistently and poorly formatted. For example, [1] is missing venue information, page numbers are present for some entries but not others, and the use of "et al." is erratic. This lack of attention to detail is surprising for a paper of this technical caliber.
    *   Minor notational choices are unconventional, such as `∆k ⊂ Rk+` instead of the more standard `∆k ⊂ R_+^k`.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper provides fundamental and sharp results on the minimax rates for estimation under local differential privacy.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant and likely to influence future work in private statistical analysis.
*   The connection between classical and modern privacy frameworks is elegant and important.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is not self-contained, as key proofs are relegated to an unpublished manuscript.
*   The manuscript suffers from numerous clarity issues, inconsistencies, and minor errors that collectively undermine its credibility. The description of the optimal mechanism in Section 3.2 is particularly problematic.
*   The overall lack of polish suggests a rushed submission that has not been subjected to careful proofreading.

**Recommendation:**
While the core ideas and results presented in this paper are strong and of high potential impact, the execution is unfortunately flawed. The sheer number of minor errors, inconsistencies, and clarity issues, coupled with the paper's lack of self-containment, prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The work is promising, but it requires a thorough revision to correct these deficiencies. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should be strongly encouraged to revise the manuscript to address the issues raised and resubmit to a future venue.
