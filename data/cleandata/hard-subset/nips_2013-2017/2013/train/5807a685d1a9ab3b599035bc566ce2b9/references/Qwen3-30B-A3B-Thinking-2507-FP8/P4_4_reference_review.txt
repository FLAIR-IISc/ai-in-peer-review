

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors combine minimax decision theory with differential privacy to establish sharp rates for two canonical problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. The main contributions include lower bounds showing the optimal rates under local privacy constraints, and matching upper bounds demonstrating the existence of computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these rates.

The paper builds upon Warner's classic randomized response method [23] and extends it to a minimax framework. The authors establish that for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy, and that Warner's mechanism is optimal. For density estimation, they show that the classical minimax rate of n^(-2β/(2β+1)) for β-smooth densities degrades to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) under local privacy constraints. The authors provide both histogram and orthogonal series estimators that achieve these rates, demonstrating that direct perturbation of samples is suboptimal.

The paper connects to previous NIPS work on differential privacy (e.g., [9, 11, 16, 24]) and minimax estimation (e.g., [22, 25, 26]), while making clear distinctions from prior results. The authors properly acknowledge Warner's 1965 work on randomized response, which they show is optimal under the local privacy framework. They also correctly distinguish their work from prior studies that focused on sample estimation rather than population estimation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds for two key statistical problems under local privacy. The authors carefully derive minimax rates using the framework of Le Cam's two-point method and Fano's inequality, and they connect these to information-theoretic bounds on mutual information. The proofs are complete and well-structured, with clear connections to prior work.

2. **Novelty:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates under local privacy constraints. The identification of the different convergence rates for density estimation (compared to classical minimax rates) is particularly novel. The authors also demonstrate that Warner's randomized response mechanism from 1965 is optimal, which is an interesting historical connection that had been overlooked in recent privacy literature.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide thorough background on both minimax theory and differential privacy, making the paper accessible to NIPS readers. The main results are stated precisely and proven rigorously, with appropriate connections to the broader literature.

4. **Significance:** The results provide a fundamental understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The sharp rates derived in the paper can guide the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures, and the authors clearly articulate the implications of their results for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the paper provides theoretical results, it could better connect these to practical applications. The authors mention that Warner's mechanism is "easy to use and explainable," but don't provide concrete examples of how this might be implemented in real-world survey settings.

2. **Assumptions on Privacy Model:** The paper focuses exclusively on local privacy, but doesn't explore how the results might extend to other privacy models (e.g., central privacy or hybrid models). This limits the generalizability of the results.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical, with no empirical validation of the proposed estimators. While this is common in theoretical NIPS papers, a brief empirical demonstration of the rates might strengthen the paper's significance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides sharp minimax rates for two fundamental statistical problems under local privacy constraints
- Demonstrates the optimality of Warner's randomized response mechanism
- Identifies a fundamental difference in convergence rates for density estimation under local privacy
- Connects classical minimax theory with differential privacy in a novel way
- Provides computationally efficient algorithms that achieve the theoretical bounds

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results
- Limited discussion of practical applications and implementation challenges
- Focuses exclusively on local privacy without discussing broader privacy frameworks
- The results, while theoretically sound, may not offer significant practical advantages over existing privacy-preserving methods for specific applications

The paper is technically sound, makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs, and is well-written. The results are novel and extend the theoretical foundation of privacy-preserving statistics. While the lack of empirical validation is notable, this is not uncommon in theoretical NIPS papers, and the authors have provided sufficient theoretical justification for their claims.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses fundamental theoretical questions in privacy and statistical learning. The work builds upon and extends recent NIPS papers on differential privacy [9, 11, 16, 24] while making clear contributions to the field.

The paper's main contribution is establishing sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, which provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. This work advances the state of the art in privacy-preserving statistics in a demonstrable way, as it provides precise quantitative characterizations of the cost of privacy.

Given the paper's technical soundness, novelty, and significance, I recommend acceptance. The authors have made a clear and important contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
