- Paper studies trade-offs between local differential privacy and statistical efficiency, giving sharp upper and lower bounds for multinomial and nonparametric density estimation.  
- Shows local privacy reduces effective sample size for multinomial estimation and changes convergence rates for density estimation.  
- Demonstrates Warner’s randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation and provides implementable algorithms achieving the bounds.  
- Effectively connects prior work: Warner’s survey methods, differential privacy literature, and minimax statistical theory, extending the authors’ earlier information-theoretic results.  
- Acknowledges related work in privacy-preserving data analysis (e.g., Chaudhuri, Dwork) and nonparametric estimation (e.g., Tsybakov).  
- Theoretical rigor: strong minimax and information-theoretic analysis with solid proofs.  
- Sharpness: derived bounds are tight and achieved by proposed estimators.  
- Practical implications: identifies optimal mechanisms with real-world applicability.  
- Presentation: generally well-written, organized, and clearly motivated with standard notation.  
- Addresses a fundamental and impactful problem in privacy-utility trade-offs.  
- Novelty: combining differential privacy with minimax theory to obtain sharp rates, notably altered density-estimation rates.  
- Limitation: technical complexity may limit accessibility to readers without cross-disciplinary background.  
- Limitation: practical implementation and computational cost of the orthogonal series estimator under the privacy mechanism are potentially high.  
- Limitation: density estimation results focus on a specific orthonormal basis and smoothness class; robustness to other bases or assumptions is not explored.  
- Limitation: discussion concentrates on Laplace perturbation and lacks a broader comparison of other privacy mechanisms.  
- For acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, sharp results, practical optimal mechanisms, and originality warrant publication.  
- Against acceptance: complexity and implementation challenges may restrict impact; scope could be broadened.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept, with suggested additions on computational challenges, alternative basis choices, and clearer Sobolev-space explanation.
