Summary
This paper develops a decision-theoretic framework for statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), deriving sharp minimax rates and matching, computationally efficient procedures. Two core problems are treated: (i) multinomial probability estimation over the d-simplex and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. The main technical engine is an information-contraction result under LDP (KL and mutual information bounds in terms of total variation), which, combined with classical Le Cam/Fano techniques, yields lower bounds. Matching upper bounds are provided via simple mechanisms: a slightly modified randomized response scheme for multinomials and histogram/orthogonal series estimators with carefully designed local privatization for densities. The results quantify the sample complexity penalty of LDP and, in the density case, reveal a change in the convergence exponent relative to the non-private case.

Novelty and relation to prior work
- Connects modern LDP (Evfimievski; Kasiviswanathan et al.; Dwork et al.) with minimax decision theory (Yang–Barron; Tsybakov) to produce rate-sharp characterizations under privacy constraints, filling a gap in prior work that focused largely on global DP or sample-level accuracy.
- Shows optimality (up to constants) of randomized response for multinomials, explaining a 50-year-old heuristic’s optimality in modern private estimation.
- For nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev Fβ, establishes that LDP changes the rate exponent from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} (non-private) to (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, and designs private mechanisms (not simple Laplace noise on raw samples) that achieve this rate.
- Relates impossibility of achieving the optimal density rate via direct additive noise to deconvolution lower bounds (Carroll–Hall).

Quality
- The theoretical results are carefully stated, with lower bounds derived via clean information inequalities under LDP and standard testing reductions. The upper bounds use explicit, efficiently implementable mechanisms and estimators (projection to the simplex; privatized histograms; a new privatization scheme for orthogonal series based on randomized signs and halfspace conditioning).
- The claims are well supported and technically plausible; the insight that naïve Laplace perturbation of raw samples is suboptimal for density estimation is compelling and grounded in known deconvolution theory.
- Some proofs are deferred to the authors’ prior work [9], which may limit self-containment. A bit more intuition around the constants (e.g., the “ck bounded away from 0” in Section 4.3) would be helpful, though this is likely resolvable in a longer appendix.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and reads clearly for a theory audience. The scope (non-interactive/memoryless LDP) is stated upfront. The interplay between privacy constraints and information contraction is explained cleanly.
- Minor suggestions: give more intuition for Proposition 1 (why TV^2 emerges under LDP), include a brief algorithmic sketch for sampling uniformly on {±B}^k with the sign constraint (easy via sign flip, but worth stating), and add a small table contrasting non-private vs LDP rates across settings.

Originality
- High. The unification of minimax analysis and local DP with sharp rates and matching mechanisms is a substantial conceptual step. Identifying the exponent change for nonparametric density estimation under LDP and designing a mechanism to achieve it is particularly novel. The optimality of (modified) randomized response for multinomials is both elegant and impactful.

Significance
- High. The results provide foundational guidance for practitioners and theorists: they quantify the statistical cost of local privacy and indicate which mechanisms are rate-optimal. The density results correct a common misconception that simple Laplace mechanisms suffice, steering future mechanism design. This work is likely to be widely cited and extended (e.g., to regression, classification, interactive LDP, other losses).

Strengths
- Clear, sharp minimax characterizations under LDP with matching efficient algorithms.
- Conceptually unifies privacy constraints with information contraction and classical testing-based lower bounds.
- Reveals and explains structural changes in nonparametric rates under LDP.
- Elegant optimality of randomized response for multinomial estimation.

Weaknesses / limitations
- Scope restricted to non-interactive (local) privacy; a discussion on whether interaction can improve rates would strengthen the paper.
- Some dependence on an external manuscript [9] for proofs; fuller appendices would improve self-containment.
- No empirical validation; while not necessary here, small simulations illustrating constants and finite-sample behavior would broaden impact.
- Minor notational density; a brief “mechanism cookbook” summary could help non-experts.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can interaction in LDP improve the exponents (especially in density estimation), or only constants? A brief discussion or citation would be valuable.
- In Section 4.3, can you provide a succinct proof or reference establishing that ck is bounded away from zero uniformly in k in the sampling scheme?
- Could you comment on other losses (Hellinger, L1) for density estimation under LDP?
- Any lower bounds for adaptive bin-width histograms or wavelet-based mechanisms under LDP?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution with clear impact and insight.
- Matching upper/lower bounds; mechanisms are simple and practical.
- Clarifies optimality of classical randomized response and limitations of naïve Laplace perturbation.

Cons for acceptance
- Proof details partly external; focus limited to non-interactive LDP; no experiments.

Overall recommendation: Accept (strong)
Confidence: High (4/5)
