**Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"**

**Summary**

This paper establishes a fundamental connection between the modern computer science notion of local differential privacy and the classical statistical framework of minimax decision theory. The authors aim to characterize the optimal statistical efficiency (in terms of convergence rates) achievable for probability estimation problems under the constraint of α-local differential privacy.

The paper presents two main results:
1.  For d-dimensional multinomial probability estimation, the minimax squared error rate is shown to be `min(1, d / (n * α^2))`. This implies that privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to `n * α^2 / d`. A key finding is that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal in this setting.
2.  For nonparametric density estimation of a function with β derivatives (within a Sobolev class), the authors show that local privacy changes the fundamental convergence rate. The rate degrades from the classical `n^(-2β / (2β+1))` to `(n * α^2)^(-2β / (2β+2))`. This is a more severe penalty than a simple reduction in effective sample size, as it alters the polynomial exponent of `n`.

To achieve these results, the authors employ information-theoretic lower bounding techniques (Le Cam's method, Fano's inequality) and leverage their own prior work on bounding statistical divergences under local privacy constraints. Crucially, they complement their lower bounds with explicit, computationally efficient algorithms (a private histogram and a private orthogonal series estimator) that achieve these rates, proving that the bounds are sharp.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of exceptionally high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and builds upon powerful, standard tools from information theory and statistical decision theory. The main contribution is not just deriving lower bounds, but proving their sharpness by constructing matching upper bounds. This provides a complete and satisfying characterization of the problem. The authors are careful in their problem formulation, distinguishing their goal of estimating population parameters from prior work that often focused on sample quantities, correctly noting that their lower bounds are stronger.

**Clarity:**
The paper is extremely well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation, tracing the history from Warner's randomized response to modern differential privacy. The introduction lucidly lays out the paper's setting and contributions. The structure of the main sections is logical, with each presenting the problem, the minimax lower bound, and a constructive mechanism that achieves the bound. The notation is standard, and the background on the minimax framework is concise yet sufficient for an expert reader to follow the arguments.

**Originality:**
This work is highly original and represents a significant conceptual advance. While the trade-off between privacy and utility is a known theme, this paper is one of the first to apply the full machinery of minimax theory to provide a definitive, quantitative characterization of this trade-off for local privacy. The key original insights are:
1.  The formal proof of optimality for Warner's randomized response within the modern differential privacy framework is an elegant and surprising result that connects historical and contemporary work.
2.  The discovery that privacy fundamentally alters the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a novel and profound finding. It demonstrates that the "cost of privacy" can be more complex than a simple effective sample size reduction.
3.  The paper clearly delineates its contributions from related work (e.g., Wasserman & Zhou [24], Lei [19]), highlighting the focus on the stringent *local* privacy model and the achievement of sharp, optimal rates. The discussion of why simple additive Laplace noise is suboptimal for density estimation is particularly insightful.

**Significance:**
The significance of this paper is very high. It provides a foundational theoretical benchmark for the field of private statistical inference.
1.  **Guidance for Algorithm Design:** Any future algorithm for private probability or density estimation can now be measured against the minimax rates established here. An algorithm failing to achieve these rates is provably suboptimal.
2.  **Quantifying the Price of Privacy:** The results give practitioners a concrete formula for the cost of privacy. For instance, the `d/α^2` factor in multinomial estimation provides a clear recipe for how much more data is needed to achieve a desired accuracy for a given privacy level and problem dimension.
3.  **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper successfully marries two major fields, providing a common language and set of tools for researchers in both privacy and statistics to analyze fundamental limits. This work is likely to inspire a new line of research applying minimax analysis to other statistical problems under privacy constraints.

**Weaknesses:**
The weaknesses of the paper are minor and do not detract significantly from its overall quality and impact.
1.  **Reliance on External Proofs:** The detailed proofs of the main information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) and the theorems are deferred to a longer manuscript [9]. While this is a common practice for conference submissions due to space constraints, it means the paper is not entirely self-contained, and a full verification of the claims requires consulting an external source.
2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** As a purely theoretical paper, it contains no experiments. While not strictly necessary for a contribution of this nature, simple simulations showing that the proposed estimators achieve the predicted rates in practice would have strengthened the paper and made the results more tangible.
3.  **Complexity of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The proposed sampling strategy for the optimal density estimator (Section 4.3) is somewhat complex and less intuitive than randomized response or Laplace noise addition. A brief discussion of its practical implementation challenges or computational complexity would have been beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a foundational, minimax-optimal characterization of the trade-off between local privacy and statistical accuracy.
*   Presents novel and significant theoretical results, including the change in the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation.
*   The work is technically sound, clear, and very well-situated within the literature.
*   The results are of high importance to both the machine learning and statistics communities and will serve as a benchmark for future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The full proofs are located in a separate preprint, making the paper not fully self-contained.
*   The lack of any experimental results may limit its accessibility to a more applied audience.

**Recommendation:**
This is an outstanding paper that makes a fundamental and lasting contribution to the theory of private statistical analysis. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It bridges two important fields, provides sharp, definitive answers to central questions, and sets the agenda for future work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
