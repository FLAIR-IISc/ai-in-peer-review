

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, focusing on multinomial estimation and density estimation problems. The authors apply minimax decision theory to characterize the trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency, providing lower and upper bounds for both settings. For multinomial estimation, they show the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, and for density estimation, they establish a different convergence rate (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2) compared to the classical n⁻²β/(2β+1). The paper demonstrates that Warner's randomized response mechanism achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation.

## Strengths

The paper is technically sound and well-written, with clear exposition of the theoretical framework. The authors properly contextualize their work within the existing literature on differential privacy (citing Dwork, Evfimievski, and others) and minimax theory. The results for multinomial estimation are particularly compelling, as they formally establish the optimality of Warner's classical randomized response procedure, which is an interesting historical connection. The paper also provides clear, efficiently implementable algorithms for both estimation problems, which is valuable for practical applications.

## Weaknesses

Despite its technical soundness, this paper fails to meet NIPS' standards for significance and novelty. The main contribution appears to be applying established minimax techniques to a new setting rather than introducing fundamentally new methods or insights. The authors' lower bounds for density estimation (Theorem 2) are consistent with known results in the literature, such as those by Duchi et al. [9] and others, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from these prior works.

The most significant weakness is the complete absence of empirical validation. NIPS papers are expected to provide either theoretical novelty with clear implications for practice, or strong experimental results demonstrating the practical value of the proposed methods. This paper is purely theoretical with no experiments to validate the proposed mechanisms or illustrate their practical performance. The authors claim their mechanisms are "efficiently implementable," but without empirical evidence, it's impossible to assess whether these methods would actually outperform existing approaches in practice.

Furthermore, the paper's claim that their density estimation approach is "minimax-optimal" for Lipschitz densities (Proposition 2) is overstated. The rate they achieve is consistent with known results for private density estimation, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that their proposed histogram estimator offers any practical advantages over simpler approaches like those based on Laplace noise, which are already well-established in the literature.

## Critical Evaluation

The paper's main contribution—characterizing the minimax rates under local privacy constraints—is valuable, but it doesn't represent a significant advance over prior work. The authors cite Duchi et al. [9] as the foundation for their information-theoretic bounds, and their results appear to be a straightforward application of these techniques to specific estimation problems. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why these specific bounds are substantially different or more useful than what has already been established in the literature.

For instance, the degradation in the effective sample size for multinomial estimation (from n to nα²/d) is consistent with prior results in the differential privacy literature. The paper doesn't provide evidence that this degradation rate is different from what was previously known or that their analysis offers new insights into the privacy-utility trade-off.

The density estimation results are similarly derivative. The authors show that privacy changes the convergence rate exponent from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2), but they don't provide sufficient justification for why this particular change is meaningful or how it differs from what could be inferred from existing work. The paper's claim that "no estimator based on Laplace perturbation can attain the rate" (Section 4.1) is questionable, as prior work has established similar bounds.

## Recommendation

This paper is technically sound but lacks the novelty and significance required for NIPS. While the theoretical results are correct and well-presented, they represent an incremental application of established techniques rather than a substantial contribution to the field. The complete absence of empirical validation is particularly concerning for a NIPS submission, as it fails to demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More thorough comparison with prior work to clearly articulate what's new
2. Empirical results demonstrating the practical value of the proposed methods
3. Discussion of how the theoretical results translate to real-world applications

Without these elements, this paper appears to be a straightforward extension of existing work rather than a significant contribution to the field. While the results are interesting from a theoretical perspective, they don't advance the state of the art in a way that would make them compelling for NIPS readers.

**Decision: Reject**
