

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by bridging Warner's classic 1965 randomized response approach with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors establish sharp minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, addressing a fundamental gap in understanding the privacy-utility trade-off.

The paper's main novelty lies in its theoretical framework that quantifies how local privacy affects statistical estimation. For multinomial estimation, the authors show that local privacy at level α reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d, with the remarkable finding that Warner's randomized response procedure is actually optimal for this problem. For density estimation, they demonstrate a fundamental departure from classical results: the convergence rate changes from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (α²n)^(-2β/(2β+2)), which is a significant theoretical insight.

The paper's significance is substantial. It provides the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, which has been an important but largely unquantified constraint in privacy-preserving statistics. The connection between historical survey techniques and modern privacy theory is particularly compelling - showing that Warner's 50-year-old approach is actually optimal under modern theoretical frameworks.

The work is technically sound with rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds, complemented by efficiently implementable algorithms that achieve the optimal rates. The authors properly contextualize their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers on differential privacy and minimax estimation. The theoretical framework they develop is general enough to apply to various probability estimation problems, enhancing its broader significance.

What makes this paper particularly valuable is its bold theoretical approach. While many papers in this area focus on practical implementations or incremental improvements, this work makes a clear, significant contribution by establishing fundamental limits and optimal strategies. The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is elegant and surprising - it shows that sometimes the historical approaches were correct all along, and modern theory has finally caught up.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with the complex theoretical content presented accessibly to readers familiar with minimax theory and differential privacy. The authors properly address the strengths and limitations of their work, including noting that direct perturbation of samples cannot achieve the optimal rates they establish.

The only minor weakness is that the paper doesn't extensively discuss practical applications of the theoretical results, but this is understandable given the paper's theoretical focus. The work is primarily a contribution to the theoretical foundations of privacy-preserving statistics, and the authors make this clear.

In terms of the NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically excellent with rigorous proofs and well-constructed theoretical framework
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with proper contextualization of the work
- Originality: The connection between Warner's work and modern minimax theory under local privacy is highly novel
- Significance: The work provides fundamental insights into privacy-utility trade-offs that will influence future research in the field

This paper represents a major theoretical advance in privacy-preserving statistics. It establishes sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, demonstrating that the privacy-utility trade-off has a different mathematical structure than previously understood. The paper's novelty, theoretical depth, and significance make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The fact that it shows Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly compelling evidence of the paper's importance.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing the first sharp rates for this problem, and it opens up new directions for research in privacy-preserving statistical inference. I strongly recommend acceptance.
