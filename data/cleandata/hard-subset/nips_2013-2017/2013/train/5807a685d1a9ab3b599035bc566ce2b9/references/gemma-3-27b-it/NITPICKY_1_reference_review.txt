This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building upon the frameworks of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner’s randomized response mechanism and provide sharp convergence rate bounds for multinomial and density estimation under local privacy constraints. The connection to classical minimax theory and the optimality results for randomized response are particularly noteworthy. However, despite the interesting theoretical contributions, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, issues that ultimately detract from its overall quality and readability.

The introduction is somewhat verbose and could benefit from more concise phrasing. While establishing context is important, the historical motivation feels disproportionately emphasized compared to the core technical contribution. The notation is introduced gradually, but becomes dense quickly, particularly in Section 2. There are several instances of ambiguous language; for example, the phrase “efficiently implementable algorithms” in the introduction lacks specificity. 

Section 2, presenting background, is arguably the weakest part of the paper. The inclusion of definitions for KL-divergence, total variation distance, and mutual information, while technically correct, feels unnecessary and disrupts the flow. These are standard concepts for the NIPS audience. More critically, simply *restating* theorems from Duchi et al. [9] without substantial discussion or justification feels lazy and increases the paper's length without adding value. The authors state they do this to keep the presentation self-contained, but the repetition is excessive and weakens the narrative. Furthermore, the notation within these restated theorems is inconsistent with the earlier definitions. For example, 'EP' is used without prior definition, and the space over which integration occurs is not always explicitly stated.

Section 3, focusing on multinomial estimation, provides interesting results demonstrating the reduction in effective sample size due to privacy constraints. However, the claim of optimality for randomized response is somewhat overstated; while the convergence rate is achieved, the constant factors may differ. A more nuanced discussion is needed. Additionally, the presentation of the estimator (11) lacks detail – how is the projection onto the simplex actually implemented? Is it computationally efficient?

Section 4, addressing density estimation, is significantly more complex. While the derivation of the minimax lower bound is a valuable contribution, the subsequent discussion of achievable rates feels rushed. The motivation for using the trigonometric basis is not adequately explained. The sampling strategy (19) is complicated, and the explanation of why it achieves privacy is not entirely clear. There's a lack of a concrete algorithmic description—how exactly is the rejection sampling implemented in practice? The discussion of why directly perturbing samples doesn’t achieve optimal rates is intriguing but could be expanded upon. The constant 'ck' appearing in Proposition 3 is defined as 'dependent only on C and B0', yet no explicit relationship is provided, hindering assessment of the bound’s tightness.

Throughout the paper, numerous minor formatting and typographical errors exist (e.g., inconsistent use of “Eq.” vs. “equation”, missing punctuation, and instances of poor spacing). While individually minor, they contribute to a perception of carelessness. The quality of the mathematical writing is inconsistent – some derivations are clear and concise, while others are convoluted. The use of inequalities like “an . bn” and “an ≍ bn” feels antiquated and should be replaced with standard big-O notation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of minimax theory to privately-constrained probability estimation.
*   Sharp convergence rate bounds for multinomial and density estimation.
*   Demonstration of optimality for Warner’s randomized response mechanism.
*   Identification of a new trade-off between privacy and convergence rate in density estimation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Weak background section with excessive repetition and inconsistent notation.
*   Numerous minor formatting and typographical errors.
*   Lack of implementation details for proposed estimators.
*   Insufficient justification for algorithmic choices (e.g., trigonometric basis).
*   Overstated claims of optimality.
*   Inconsistent mathematical writing.
*   Lack of clarity and conciseness in several sections.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the interesting theoretical contributions, the paper’s numerous flaws—primarily the pervasive lack of polish and attention to detail—lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper presents a valuable line of inquiry, but its current state is not suitable for NIPS. The authors would significantly benefit from a thorough revision addressing the issues outlined above. A more focused presentation, clearer explanations, and stricter attention to detail are essential for elevating this work to a publishable standard. The core ideas are strong, but the execution falls short.
