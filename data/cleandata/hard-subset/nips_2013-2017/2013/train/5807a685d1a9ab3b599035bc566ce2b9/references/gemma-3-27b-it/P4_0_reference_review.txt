This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building on the concepts of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner’s original problem of estimating population fractions while accounting for respondent privacy concerns, extending it to nonparametric density estimation. They derive minimax convergence rates under local differential privacy constraints, demonstrating a penalty in estimation accuracy due to privacy preservation. The work provides both lower and matching upper bounds, showcasing the optimality of certain existing techniques like randomized response.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates it within the broader context of privacy and statistical inference. The authors demonstrate a strong grasp of the relevant literature, referencing key works in differential privacy, statistical decision theory, and nonparametric estimation. The notation is consistently used, and the mathematical derivations appear sound. The detailed background section (Section 2) is particularly valuable, laying out the necessary theoretical tools (minimax framework, information bounds) before presenting the main results. The organization into multinomial and density estimation subsections enhances clarity. The inclusion of acknowledgements and a comprehensive list of references is appreciated.

The primary strength of this work lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. Establishing sharp minimax rates under local differential privacy is a significant contribution. The demonstration that randomized response achieves the optimal rate for multinomial estimation is noteworthy, lending theoretical justification to a widely used practical technique. The distinct convergence rate derived for density estimation (different polynomial scaling compared to the non-private case) is also a significant finding. The achievability results, coupled with the lower bounds, establish a tight characterization of the privacy-utility trade-off.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications could be further elaborated.  For example, the authors mention that the penalty on the effective sample size is substantial for small values of α. A more detailed discussion of how to choose α in practice, considering the trade-off between privacy and accuracy for specific applications, would be beneficial. While the authors touch on efficient algorithms for the density estimation case, detailing implementation considerations or computational complexity would strengthen the results. The discussion of how the results extend to more complex models or data types is limited. Furthermore, while the related work section is comprehensive, a more nuanced discussion of connections to the broader literature on privacy-preserving machine learning (beyond just statistical estimation) would be valuable. Finally, the paper assumes a certain level of mathematical sophistication from the reader; a more intuitive explanation of some of the key concepts (e.g., KL divergence, mutual information) could broaden its accessibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical analysis with clear proofs (references to a longer paper are provided).
*   **Novelty:** Establishing sharp minimax rates under local differential privacy is a novel and important contribution.
*   **Significance:** The results have significant implications for the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures and provide theoretical justification for existing techniques.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and uses consistent notation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** The paper lacks detailed discussion on practical considerations for choosing privacy parameters or implementing the proposed algorithms.
*   **Scope:** While technically sound, the focus is very specific (probability estimation). Expanding the discussion of broader applicability would increase the impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of privacy and statistical inference. The technical rigor, novelty, and significance of the results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
