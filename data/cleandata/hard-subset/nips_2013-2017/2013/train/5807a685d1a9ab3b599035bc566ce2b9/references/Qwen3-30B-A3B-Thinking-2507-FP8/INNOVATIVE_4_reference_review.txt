

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by bridging minimax decision theory with differential privacy, providing sharp rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors address the fundamental question of how privacy constraints impact statistical efficiency, a crucial gap in the literature. The work is novel in its theoretical approach, connecting Warner's 1965 randomized response method with modern differential privacy frameworks to derive minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems.

The paper's main contributions are threefold. First, it establishes that for d-dimensional multinomial estimation, local privacy at level α reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d. The authors prove this is tight by showing Warner's randomized response (a 50-year-old technique) achieves this optimal rate. Second, for density estimation with β-continuous derivatives, the paper demonstrates a surprising departure from classical minimax rates: the convergence rate shifts from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (α²n)^(-2β/(2β+2)), with the exponent changing from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2). Third, the paper provides efficient algorithms (histogram and orthogonal series estimators) that achieve these sharp rates, resolving the question of practical implementation.

The theoretical framework is sound and well-executed. The authors develop appropriate minimax bounds using information-theoretic techniques, carefully handling the privacy constraints. The lower bounds are complemented by matching upper bounds, making the results particularly compelling. The connection to Warner's work is insightful and adds historical depth to the theoretical contribution.

The paper excels in originality. While previous work has studied privacy constraints on statistical estimation, this is the first to provide sharp minimax rates under local differential privacy, revealing fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The finding that the classical randomized response mechanism is optimal is particularly striking and valuable. The authors also make a strong case that direct perturbation approaches (like adding Laplace noise) are suboptimal for density estimation, requiring more sophisticated privacy mechanisms.

In terms of significance, this work provides a theoretical foundation for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. It establishes a clear mathematical framework that can guide the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The results have immediate implications for survey design, data collection, and privacy-preserving machine learning, where understanding the precise cost of privacy is critical.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The theoretical development is accessible to experts in statistical learning theory, though it assumes familiarity with minimax theory and differential privacy. The notation is consistent and well-chosen. The authors properly contextualize their work against previous literature, including the foundational work of Warner and recent advances in differential privacy.

Minor weaknesses include the lack of empirical validation (the paper is purely theoretical), which is understandable given the focus on theoretical guarantees but would have strengthened the paper. Some readers might find the technical details challenging, though this is unavoidable given the depth of the theory.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are sharp, the framework is novel, and the insights are profound. The paper successfully connects historical privacy work with modern theory, revealing fundamental trade-offs that will guide future research.

The paper is clearly in scope for NIPS, addressing a core problem in statistical learning with privacy constraints. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing the first sharp minimax rates under local differential privacy for these problems. The results have the potential to influence both theoretical and applied work in privacy-preserving statistics.

I recommend acceptance for the following reasons:

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical framework connecting minimax theory with differential privacy
- Sharp, tight bounds for both multinomial and density estimation
- Surprising result that Warner's 1965 randomized response is optimal
- Practical algorithms that achieve the theoretical rates
- Clear demonstration of fundamental privacy-utility trade-offs

**Weaknesses (that don't outweigh the strengths):**
- Limited empirical validation (understandable for a theoretical paper)
- Some technical depth may be challenging for non-specialists

This paper represents a substantial theoretical advance that will likely influence future work in privacy-preserving statistics. The novelty, significance, and theoretical rigor make it a strong candidate for NIPS. I am particularly impressed by the elegance of the connection between classical and modern privacy concepts, and the sharp characterization of the privacy-utility trade-off.
