

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary
This paper bridges statistical decision theory with differential privacy by establishing sharp minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors derive fundamental trade-offs between privacy (measured by α) and statistical utility for two key problems: multinomial estimation (finite-dimensional) and density estimation (nonparametric). Their key contributions include showing that for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, and for density estimation with β continuous derivatives, the convergence rate changes from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2). They demonstrate that Warner's randomized response mechanism achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation and provide efficient algorithms for density estimation that match their lower bounds.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish both lower and upper bounds for their estimation problems, with careful attention to the mathematical details of the privacy constraints. The proofs build logically on established results in differential privacy (Duchi et al. [9]) and minimax theory. The paper is a complete theoretical contribution, not a position paper, and the authors are careful to distinguish their results from prior work (e.g., noting that their population estimation bounds are stronger than previous work on sample estimation). The analysis of the density estimation problem under local privacy is particularly impressive, as it reveals a different convergence rate structure than classical results.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear notation and logical flow. The connection between the theoretical results and practical algorithms is well-explained, and the authors provide sufficient detail for experts to follow the derivations. The paper effectively uses standard minimax framework terminology while making it accessible to readers familiar with statistical theory. The exposition of Warner's randomized response mechanism and its optimality is particularly clear.

## Originality
This work makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of privacy in statistical estimation. While differential privacy has been extensively studied, the paper's focus on *local* privacy with minimax rates is novel. The connection between Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism and modern privacy guarantees is insightful and demonstrates the enduring relevance of classical statistical techniques. The density estimation results are particularly original, as they reveal a fundamental change in the convergence rate structure under local privacy constraints that differs from both classical results and previous work on differential privacy.

## Significance
The results provide fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility that will be valuable for practitioners designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The sharp rates establish the theoretical limits of what can be achieved under local privacy constraints, which is crucial for understanding the fundamental costs of privacy. The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal provides historical continuity to modern privacy research. The efficient algorithms they propose for density estimation under local privacy make the theoretical results practically relevant.

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**
1. The paper establishes sharp minimax bounds that match achievable rates, providing a complete theoretical characterization of the privacy-utility trade-off.
2. The connection between Warner's 1965 work and modern privacy theory is both insightful and well-executed.
3. The density estimation results reveal a fundamental change in convergence rates that had not been previously established.
4. The paper carefully distinguishes between local privacy and other forms of differential privacy.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper focuses exclusively on the non-interactive (memoryless) setting, which is a limitation. It would be valuable to discuss how the results might extend to interactive privacy mechanisms, which are increasingly common in practice.
2. The practical implications could be strengthened by discussing how to choose α in real-world applications or how the results scale with large d (for multinomial estimation) or high-dimensional data.
3. While the algorithms are theoretically efficient, the paper doesn't fully analyze their computational complexity in terms of the dimensionality d or the number of basis functions k.
4. The connection to recent work on differentially private statistical estimation (e.g., Bassily et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2015) could be more explicitly addressed.

## Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes significant original contributions to the field. The results provide valuable theoretical guidance for privacy-preserving statistical estimation. While the focus on non-interactive privacy is a limitation, the paper's theoretical depth and sharp bounds make it a valuable addition to the literature.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper addresses a fundamental question in privacy-preserving statistics with rigorous theoretical analysis, and the results are likely to be influential for future work in the field. The connection to Warner's 1965 work is particularly elegant and demonstrates the enduring relevance of classical statistical techniques.

**Strengths:** Rigorous minimax analysis, sharp bounds, insightful connection to historical privacy techniques, novel density estimation results.

**Weaknesses:** Limited to non-interactive setting, insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges, computational complexity analysis could be expanded.

The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and would be a significant addition to the NIPS proceedings.
