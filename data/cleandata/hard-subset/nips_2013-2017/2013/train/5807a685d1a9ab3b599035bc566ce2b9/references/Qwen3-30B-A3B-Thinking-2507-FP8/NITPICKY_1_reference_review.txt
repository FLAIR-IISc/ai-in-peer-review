

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper connects minimax decision theory with differential privacy to characterize optimal estimation under privacy constraints. The authors provide sharp lower bounds and matching upper bounds for multinomial and density estimation under local differential privacy, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation and establishing a new convergence rate for density estimation. The work bridges classical statistical theory with modern privacy frameworks.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by establishing precise minimax rates under local privacy constraints. The connection between Warner's classical randomized response and modern privacy guarantees is particularly insightful. The authors provide efficient algorithms for both multinomial and density estimation that match the lower bounds, demonstrating practical relevance.

## Critical Issues

1. **Fundamental Notational Error**: The definition of local privacy in equation (1) contains a critical error. The paper states "sup { Q(S | Xi = x) Q(S | Xi = x′) | ... } ≤ exp(α)" but this should be a ratio, not a product. The standard definition uses Q(S|x)/Q(S|x') ≤ e^α. This is a serious notational error that undermines the entire privacy framework.

2. **Inconsistent Effective Sample Size**: The paper claims "the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α causes a reduction in the effective sample size of n → nα²/d" (Section 3.1). However, the rate in equation (8) is d/(nα²), which implies an effective sample size of nα²/d, not nα²/d as written. This is a significant inconsistency that requires correction.

3. **Confusing Minimax Notation**: In equation (2), the minimax risk is defined as "inf θ̂,Q∈Qα sup P∈P...", but Q is a channel, not an estimator. The infimum should be over estimators θ̂ and channels Q, but the notation suggests Q is being minimized over, which is confusing.

4. **Unjustified Constant in Density Estimation**: In Section 4.3, the authors claim "B = O(B0 √k/α)" but then specify B = B0 √k(e^α + 1)/c_k(e^α - 1) without defining c_k. They state c_k is "O(1) and bounded away from 0" but provide no justification for this claim, which is critical for the privacy guarantee.

5. **Inconsistent Rate in Proposition 2**: The bound in Proposition 2 has "5(α²n)^(-1/2) + √(αn^(-3/4))", which contains inconsistent exponents on n (n^(-1/2) vs n^(-3/8)). This is likely a typo that needs correction.

6. **Mischaracterization of Local Privacy**: The paper states "any individual providing data guarantees his or her own privacy—no further processing or mistakes by a collection agency can compromise one's data." This is inaccurate. Local differential privacy guarantees protection against the data collector's processing, but doesn't prevent mistakes in processing from compromising privacy.

7. **Reference Formatting Issue**: The reference "Privacy, accuracy, and consistency too: A holistic solution to contingency table release, B. Barak, K. Chaudhuri, C. Dwork, S. Kale, F. McSherry, K. Talwar, Proceedings of the 26th ACM Symposium on Principles of Database Systems, , ," has missing conference name information, which is a formatting inconsistency.

## Minor Issues

- The paper uses "differentially private" and "local differential privacy" interchangeably in some places, though they are distinct concepts.
- The claim that "Warner's randomized response procedure enjoys optimal sample complexity" should be qualified to "for multinomial estimation under local privacy constraints" rather than presented as a universal truth.
- The reference to Duchi et al. [9] as "arXiv:1302.3203 [math.ST]" is fine, but the paper should clarify it's a preprint.

## Overall Assessment

This paper addresses an important problem at the intersection of statistical theory and privacy. However, the fundamental notational error in defining local privacy (equation 1) is particularly concerning as it undermines the entire theoretical foundation. The inconsistent effective sample size description and the unverified constant in the density estimation procedure further weaken the paper's technical contributions.

While the theoretical results are interesting, the technical errors and inconsistencies prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper would be much stronger with careful attention to these details, particularly the critical definition of local privacy. The authors should carefully revise the notation and clarify all technical claims before submission.

The paper has significant potential, but the current version contains too many technical inconsistencies to be considered a complete and accurate piece of work. I recommend rejection due to these serious technical flaws that affect the paper's core contributions.
