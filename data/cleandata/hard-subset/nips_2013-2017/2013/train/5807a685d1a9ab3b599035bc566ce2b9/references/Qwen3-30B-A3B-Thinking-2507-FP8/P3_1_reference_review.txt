

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary
This paper bridges minimax decision theory with local differential privacy to characterize the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for two core problems: multinomial estimation (d-dimensional probability simplex) and density estimation with smoothness constraints. For multinomial estimation, they show the effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d, with Warner's randomized response achieving the optimal rate. For density estimation with β continuous derivatives, they prove a rate of (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)), differing from the classical n^(-2β/(2β+1)) rate. The work connects Warner's 1965 randomized response to modern differential privacy, demonstrating its optimality.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor and Sharpness**: The paper provides tight minimax lower and upper bounds for both problems, demonstrating the sharpness of their results. The use of Le Cam's two-point method combined with information-theoretic bounds for differential privacy is elegant and well-executed.

2. **Historical Insight**: The connection to Warner's randomized response (1965) is insightful and shows that the optimal privacy-preserving strategy for survey collection has been known for decades. This historical perspective enriches the theoretical contribution.

3. **Novel Insights for Density Estimation**: The paper reveals a fundamental difference between finite-dimensional and infinite-dimensional problems under privacy constraints. The different convergence rate (2β/(2β+2) vs. 2β/(2β+1)) for density estimation is particularly significant and not previously established.

4. **Practical Algorithms**: The authors provide efficiently computable estimators (histogram and orthogonal series) that match the lower bounds, making the theoretical results applicable to real-world privacy-preserving statistics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Practical Discussion**: While the paper establishes theoretical optimality, it doesn't adequately discuss the practical implications. For instance, how does the α parameter translate to real-world privacy guarantees? What are the computational costs of the proposed estimators compared to non-private alternatives?

2. **Density Estimation Mechanism**: The paper claims that direct Laplace perturbation is suboptimal for density estimation (citing Carroll and Hall), but the explanation could be clearer. The paper should more explicitly connect the characteristic function decay of Laplace noise to the specific rate degradation observed.

3. **Limited Scope of Privacy Definition**: The work focuses solely on α-local differential privacy, without discussing how results might extend to other privacy models (e.g., ε-differential privacy with varying parameters). This limits the applicability of the results.

4. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper references Smith (2011) and Wasserman and Zhou (2010), but doesn't fully position its results against more recent work on private density estimation (e.g., Lei 2011, Chaudhuri and Hsu 2012). A more thorough comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistics. The minimax analysis is technically sound, and the results are clearly presented. The connection to Warner's work is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates that optimal privacy-preserving strategies have existed for decades.

However, the paper's significance would be enhanced by addressing the practical implications of these theoretical results. For example, in real-world applications like medical surveys, what does an α = 0.1 privacy guarantee mean for the quality of the statistical estimates? How do these theoretical rates translate to specific applications?

The density estimation results are particularly compelling, but the paper could better explain why the proposed orthogonal series estimator (21) is necessary. The claim that "no estimator based on Laplace perturbation can attain the rate" (Theorem 2) is important but could be better justified.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of statistical inference under local privacy constraints. The results are sharp, the proofs are rigorous, and the connection to historical work on randomized response is insightful.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental question at the intersection of statistics and privacy, with clear theoretical contributions that advance the field. The technical depth and novelty of the results meet NIPS standards.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Pro arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides sharp minimax rates for two fundamental statistical problems under local privacy
- Demonstrates the optimality of Warner's randomized response
- Reveals a fundamental difference between finite and infinite-dimensional problems under privacy constraints
- Provides efficiently computable estimators that match lower bounds

**Con arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of practical implications and real-world trade-offs
- Could better position results against recent work in private density estimation
- Needs clearer explanation of why direct perturbation fails for density estimation

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. The paper's clarity, technical depth, and novel insights make it a valuable addition to NIPS.
