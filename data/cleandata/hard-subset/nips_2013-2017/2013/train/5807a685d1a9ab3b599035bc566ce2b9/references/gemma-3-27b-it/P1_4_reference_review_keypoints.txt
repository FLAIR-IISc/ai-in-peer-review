- Studies trade-offs between local differential privacy and statistical efficiency for multinomial and density estimation, deriving sharp minimax lower and upper bounds.  
- Shows the privacy level α induces a convergence-rate penalty and that density estimation rates differ from the non-private case.  
- Demonstrates optimality of Warner’s randomized response for multinomial estimation and provides efficiently implementable density-estimation algorithms.  
- Extends prior work (Duchi et al. 2013) by applying divergence bounds to concrete estimation problems and linking DP with minimax theory and classical density-estimation results.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis using information-theoretic and KL-divergence techniques.  
- Strength: produces sharp, provably tight convergence rates; the differing density-estimation rate is a notable insight.  
- Strength: practical implications—validates a classic technique (randomized response) and offers implementable methods.  
- Strength: situates results clearly within existing literature; overall organization and notation are mostly clear.  
- Weakness: connection between β-smoothness and the altered density-estimation rate needs clearer intuition beyond information-theoretic arguments.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of computational cost and scalability; projection step in the histogram method may be a bottleneck for large/high-dimensional data.  
- Weakness: limited empirical evaluation—would benefit from simulations showing privacy’s impact on accuracy.  
- Weakness: heavy notation makes the paper harder to follow for readers not expert in the field.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound and substantial contribution (excellent quality).  
- Clarity assessment: generally clear but density section could use more intuition; notation is dense.  
- Originality assessment: novel and valuable combination of differential privacy and minimax theory (excellent originality).  
- Significance assessment: important for understanding fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical inference (excellent significance).  
- Arguments for acceptance: fundamental theoretical contribution, sharp optimal bounds, cross-disciplinary connections, and practical estimator insights.  
- Arguments against acceptance: very theoretical focus may limit audience appeal, lacks empirical validation, and needs clearer exposition in density section.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper deserving acceptance with minor clarifications and added empirical results.  
- Score: 8.5/10 (Strong Accept).
