

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary
This paper investigates the theoretical trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation problems under local differential privacy constraints. The authors establish sharp minimax convergence rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation (probability estimation in survey data) and nonparametric density estimation. They demonstrate that local privacy at level α reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d for multinomial estimation, and changes the density estimation rate from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)). The paper shows that Warner's randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation and provides efficient algorithms achieving the lower bounds. This work connects classical privacy concepts (Warner, 1965) with modern differential privacy frameworks (Dwork et al., 2006) and statistical decision theory.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Rigor and Novelty**: The paper provides sharp minimax bounds for two important statistical problems under local privacy constraints, filling a significant gap in the literature. The results are theoretically sound with careful proofs and tight bounds.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The demonstration that Warner's randomized response (a 50-year-old method) is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation is both elegant and practically significant. This bridges historical and modern privacy approaches.

3. **Algorithmic Contribution**: The paper provides efficient algorithms (histogram and orthogonal series estimators) that achieve the lower bounds, making the theoretical results practically applicable.

4. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and logical flow. The connection between minimax theory and privacy constraints is presented accessibly.

## Weaknesses
1. **Density Estimation Interpretation**: The change in the convergence rate exponent (from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2)) for density estimation is mathematically precise but somewhat counterintuitive. A more intuitive explanation of why the privacy constraint changes the exponent in this specific way would strengthen the paper.

2. **Limited Practical Discussion**: While the paper provides theoretical results, it lacks detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges (e.g., computational complexity for large d or k, sensitivity to parameter choices in real-world applications).

3. **Basis Choice for Density Estimation**: The lower bound for density estimation uses a specific trigonometric basis. It would be valuable to discuss whether the rate is dependent on the basis choice or if it holds for more general bases.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically excellent, with rigorous proofs and careful theoretical analysis. The authors provide both lower and upper bounds that match, establishing sharp rates. The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution, not merely a position paper. The authors are appropriately cautious in their claims, noting the limitations of their results.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, the proofs are clear, and the main results are precisely stated. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The only minor issue is that the density estimation results could be more intuitively explained.

**Originality**: The work represents a significant conceptual advance, connecting minimax decision theory with differential privacy. While Duchi et al. (2013) established foundational results in local privacy, this paper extends those results to provide sharp rates for specific estimation problems, revealing novel trade-offs. The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal is particularly original.

**Significance**: The results provide a fundamental theoretical understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off in statistical estimation. This work will be valuable to researchers in privacy, statistics, and machine learning, as it establishes precise bounds for two fundamental problems. The practical implications for survey design and data collection under privacy constraints are substantial. The paper advances the state of the art in privacy-preserving statistical inference in a demonstrable way.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. It provides sharp theoretical bounds for two important problems, demonstrates the optimality of a classic privacy mechanism, and provides efficient algorithms. The results are both theoretically rigorous and practically relevant.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear advancement in the field. While the density estimation results could benefit from more intuitive explanation, this does not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.

I recommend **acceptance** for NeurIPS.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro (Accept):**
- Provides sharp minimax bounds for two fundamental statistical problems under local privacy
- Demonstrates optimality of Warner's randomized response mechanism
- Establishes precise privacy-utility trade-offs with theoretical guarantees
- Provides efficient algorithms achieving the lower bounds
- Connects historical privacy concepts with modern differential privacy framework

**Con (Reject):**
- Density estimation rate change could be more intuitively explained
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges
- Basis choice for density estimation might limit generalizability

Overall, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The paper makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation, with clear theoretical and practical implications.
