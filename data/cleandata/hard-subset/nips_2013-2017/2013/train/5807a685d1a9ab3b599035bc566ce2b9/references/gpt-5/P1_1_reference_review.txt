Summary
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), connecting classical minimax theory with privacy constraints. The setup is non-interactive (memoryless channels): each Zi is drawn from a channel Q(· | Xi) that satisfies α-local DP, and the statistician only observes Z1:n. The core technical tool is an information-contraction bound under LDP (KL/mutual-information upper bounds in terms of TV distances), which, combined with Le Cam/Fano, yields sharp minimax lower bounds. Two estimation problems are treated:

- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the α-private minimax risk in ℓ2 and ℓ1 norms scales like d/(nα2) (up to constants and truncation at 1). Hence, LDP reduces the effective sample size from n to nα2/d for small α. A slightly modified randomized response mechanism achieves the lower bound, establishing its optimality.

- Density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1]: The non-private minimax rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), i.e., the exponent worsens due to privacy. The paper gives constructive estimators: a privatized histogram (β=1) and an orthogonal series estimator (general β) based on a novel locally private mechanism that randomizes signs over a hypercube and preserves unbiasedness of basis coefficients. It also shows that naive sample perturbation (e.g., Laplace noise on Xi) is information-theoretically suboptimal via deconvolution lower bounds.

Relation to prior work is thorough: Warner’s randomized response; LDP as per Evfimievski et al.; global DP and geometry (Dwork et al., Hardt–Talwar); population vs sample risk (Wasserman–Zhou, Smith); and classical nonparametric minimax theory (Tsybakov, Efromovich). The paper builds on the authors’ technical bounds in a companion work, using them here to derive sharp rates and achievable mechanisms in concrete problems.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds follow a standard but nontrivial path (packing + information bounds under LDP). The contraction inequalities (KL and mutual information) are appropriate and powerful. Upper bounds are constructive and match lower bounds in order. The observation that additive Laplace noise to samples is suboptimal for density estimation is well supported (Carroll–Hall deconvolution rates).

- Completeness: Many proofs are deferred to [9]; while acceptable for NIPS, some key steps (especially the unbiasedness/variance calculation and privacy proof for the orthogonal-series mechanism) would benefit from brief sketches or pointers to precise lemmas. The multinomial achievability argument is concise and clear.

- Correctness/typos: A few notational slips should be fixed. In Definition 1, the constraint should be ∑j j2β θ2j ≤ C2 (θ, not ϕ). In randomized response (Eq. 10) “− xj” should be “1 − xj” for xj ∈ {0,1}. Minor constants in variance bounds and the claim “linear time” simplex projection (typically O(d log d), though selection-based O(d) exists) should be clarified with the cited algorithm.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized, with a clear minimax framework (definitions of TV, KL, mutual information, private minimax risk), and a good high-level explanation of the statistical costs of LDP. The density estimation section is necessarily technical; adding a short, self-contained proof sketch of privacy and unbiasedness for the sign-aggregation mechanism (Eq. 19–21), pseudocode, and a discussion of computational cost would improve readability. Clarify assumptions on α (often α ∈ [0,1]) and constants ck in Eq. (20). A brief figure or synthetic experiment illustrating the new exponent change for β=1 vs β>1 would aid intuition.

Originality
The work provides new, sharp minimax bounds under local DP, including the identification of randomized response as rate-optimal for multinomial estimation and a new, nontrivial, locally private orthogonal-series mechanism achieving the optimal nonparametric rate with a strictly different exponent from the non-private case. The information contraction under LDP and its use to derive population-level minimax rates seem novel and impactful relative to prior DP optimality results that mostly target sample quantities.

Significance
Highly significant. Local DP is increasingly relevant in practice; quantifying the exact statistical price of LDP and giving optimal, implementable mechanisms should influence both theory and applications. The discovery that privacy changes the nonparametric rate exponent is particularly noteworthy and likely to spur follow-up work (e.g., adaptation, other functionals, interaction effects).

Pros
- Sharp, matching lower/upper bounds for two canonical problems under LDP.
- Identifies optimality of randomized response for multinomials; clear “effective sample size” degradation.
- Novel locally private mechanism for orthogonal series; explains why naive noise addition fails.
- Solid integration of information-theoretic bounds with minimax testing.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on the companion paper for proofs; some constructions (Eq. 19–21) deserve more in-paper detail.
- Scope limited to non-interactive LDP; the potential benefit of interaction is not addressed.
- No empirical illustrations; while theory-centric, small simulations could validate constants and practical performance.
- Minor typos/notation issues could confuse readers.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can the results extend to interactive local channels? Are there known gaps between interactive and non-interactive rates here?
- Is adaptation to unknown β possible under LDP without losing rates? Any lower bounds for adaptive procedures?
- Please provide a concise proof or lemma for the LDP guarantee of the sign-aggregation mechanism and bounds on its variance; include pseudocode.
- Clarify the constants (ck) and the choice of B in Eq. (20) and give computational complexity for the sampling.
- Consider adding a small simulation comparing histogram/orthogonal-series mechanisms vs naive Laplace-X perturbation.

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. This is a high-quality, original, and impactful theoretical contribution that clarifies the fundamental trade-offs between local privacy and statistical efficiency and provides optimal algorithms. With minor clarifications and typos fixed, it is well suited for NIPS.
