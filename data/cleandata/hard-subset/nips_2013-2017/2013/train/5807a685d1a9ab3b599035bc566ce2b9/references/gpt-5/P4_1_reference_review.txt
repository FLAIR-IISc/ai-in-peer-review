Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each data point is privatized before any interaction using an α-private channel. It integrates differential privacy and minimax decision theory to derive sharp rates for two core problems: (a) multinomial probability estimation and (b) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. The authors establish lower bounds on α-private minimax risk via information-theoretic tools (Le Cam/Fano) combined with newly stated divergence bounds specific to LDP channels, and present matching, efficiently computable mechanisms.

For multinomial estimation in dimension d, the paper shows the mean-squared error rate scales as d/(nα2) (up to constants and truncation), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d for small α. It further shows that a suitably parameterized variant of Warner’s randomized response achieves this rate, thereby identifying classical randomized response as optimal under LDP.

For density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1], the authors prove that LDP changes the minimax exponent from n−2β/(2β+1) (non-private) to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2). They provide two constructions that attain the lower bounds: (i) a privatized histogram estimator achieving the optimal rate for Lipschitz densities (β=1) and (ii) a locally private orthogonal series estimator with an α-private mechanism that calibrates noise to preserve unbiasedness of basis coefficients. The paper also argues that direct additive noise on the raw samples (e.g., Laplace) cannot attain the optimal rate due to deconvolution limits (Carroll and Hall), underscoring the need for tailored privatization strategies.

This work connects to Warner’s randomized response, the DP literature initiated by Dwork et al., and related statistical formulations (e.g., Wasserman and Zhou). It extends prior learning-under-privacy frameworks (e.g., Kasiviswanathan et al.) by giving sharp minimax rates and matching mechanisms specifically for LDP in both parametric and nonparametric settings.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The lower bounds are derived via standard minimax reductions combined with LDP-specific information bounds, yielding clear, dimension- and α-dependent rates. These appear tight, as matching upper bounds are provided by computationally tractable estimators.
- The multinomial results are concrete and the optimality of randomized response is rigorously argued, including α-privacy verification and risk analysis.
- The nonparametric lower bound is standard in form but crucially exhibits the altered exponent under LDP; the constructions (histogram for β=1, orthogonal series for β≥1) are carefully designed to satisfy LDP and match the lower bounds.
- The paper relies on previously established divergence/mutual-information upper bounds under LDP (restated here), which are pivotal to the lower bounds. Proof details are deferred to a longer version; the claims and techniques are plausible and consistent with the cited references.
- Assumptions (non-interactive channels, bounded orthonormal bases, α∈[0,1]) are clearly stated.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized, with a clear setup of the minimax framework, precise LDP definition, and consistent notation.
- Statements of main theorems and propositions are explicit, with interpretations contrasting non-private and private regimes.
- The practical mechanisms are described concretely (randomized response, Laplace-perturbed histograms with projection, and the orthogonal-series sampling scheme), including parameter choices and privacy verification sketches.
- Some components (e.g., constants ck and the exact sampling/rejection steps in the series mechanism) could benefit from more intuition and guidance on implementation, but the key ideas are conveyed.

Originality
- The paper provides sharp α-LDP minimax rates with matching algorithms for multinomial estimation and density estimation. Identifying the change in the nonparametric convergence exponent under LDP and exhibiting optimal locally private estimators constitute novel and substantive contributions.
- The information bounds specialized to LDP and their use in deriving lower bounds represent a significant methodological step beyond earlier DP optimality results that focus on sample accuracy rather than population minimax risk.

Significance
- Establishing tight trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in the local model is of foundational importance for both theory and practice, especially where data must remain on-device or untrusted aggregators are assumed.
- The identification of randomized response as optimal for multinomials and the demonstration that naive perturbation can be suboptimal in nonparametric settings provide actionable guidance for private data collection and estimator design.
- The results and techniques should be broadly useful and influential for subsequent work on private statistical inference.

Strengths
- Sharp, matching lower and upper bounds under LDP for two canonical problems.
- Clear comparison to non-private rates and precise quantification of privacy-induced penalties.
- Practical, efficient mechanisms with provable optimality (randomized response; private histograms/series).
- Careful integration of information-theoretic tools into minimax analysis under privacy constraints.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Several key technical proofs are deferred to an external longer paper; including proofs or detailed sketches in supplementary material would strengthen self-containment.
- The work focuses on non-interactive (memoryless) channels; a discussion of whether interactivity can improve rates (and by how much) would contextualize the scope of optimality.
- The orthogonal series mechanism depends on bounds B0 and knowledge of β (for k selection). Discussion of adaptivity to unknown smoothness under LDP and the cost of privacy-preserving model selection would increase practical relevance.
- Clarify constants and regimes where the “effective sample size reduction” heuristic is precise, and discuss finite-sample performance.
- Briefly compare with central DP rates to highlight distinctions between privacy models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Advances the theoretical foundations of LDP with sharp minimax rates and optimal mechanisms.
- High significance and broad relevance across statistics, privacy, and machine learning.
- Well written, technically solid, and provides constructive, implementable procedures.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on prior technical results and a longer version for proofs reduces self-containment.
- Limited discussion of interactive protocols and adaptivity to unknown smoothness.
- Some implementation details (constants, rejection sampling efficiency) could be elaborated.
