## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a strong theoretical contribution to the intersection of differential privacy and statistical estimation. The authors provide sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy constraints, bridging classical statistical decision theory with modern privacy guarantees. The results are well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly presented. While the theoretical nature of the work may limit its immediate practical impact, the insights gained are valuable and the paper is a significant step forward in understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper investigates the problem of estimating probabilities (in the multinomial and density estimation settings) while guaranteeing local differential privacy. It builds upon Warner’s early work on privacy-preserving surveys, formalizing the privacy notion using the framework of α-local differential privacy. Crucially, the authors leverage minimax decision theory to derive lower and upper bounds on the convergence rates of estimators under privacy constraints. This approach provides a rigorous characterization of the “price of privacy” in terms of statistical accuracy. 

The paper correctly situates itself within the growing literature on privacy and statistical inference, highlighting the gap in providing formal treatments of disclosure risk that allow for decision-theoretic analysis.  It appropriately cites key works in differential privacy (Dwork et al., McSherry et al.) and minimax theory (Tsybakov, van der Vaart).  The connection to Warner’s randomized response is insightful, demonstrating that even a decades-old technique achieves optimal performance under the proposed framework.  The authors also acknowledge relevant work from their own previous paper [9] and leverage results from it effectively.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The use of minimax theory provides a robust and rigorous framework for analyzing the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy. The derived bounds are sharp and informative.
* **Novelty & Significance:**  The results are a significant contribution to the field, offering a formal characterization of the impact of local privacy on estimation rates. The identification of a differing convergence rate in density estimation (compared to classical results) is particularly noteworthy.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are explained clearly. The authors make a conscious effort to keep the presentation self-contained by restating relevant results from their previous work.
* **Optimal Mechanisms:** Demonstrating that Warner’s randomized response attains the optimal rate for multinomial estimation is a valuable result, highlighting the practical relevance of the theory.  The development of privately-optimal estimators for density estimation is also a strength.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper tackles two different probability estimation problems, showcasing the generality of their approach.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Focus:** The paper is highly theoretical, and the practical implications of the results are not fully explored. While the authors mention efficient algorithms, a more detailed discussion of their computational complexity and scalability would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on assumptions such as bounded derivatives in the density estimation setting.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the absence of empirical validation (even on relatively simple datasets) weakens the paper slightly. Demonstrating the performance of the proposed estimators in practice would further strengthen the claims.
* **Overly verbose introduction:** The introduction is a bit lengthy. Could be made more concise.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.1:** While the theoretical results are clear, a brief intuitive explanation of *why* the effective sample size decreases by a factor of α<sup>2</sup>/d would be helpful.
* **Section 4.2 & 4.3:** The discussion of the Laplace noise addition is a bit hand-wavy. Providing more details about how the choice of B (noise scale) affects the convergence rate would be helpful.
* **General:** The authors use the notation “≍” frequently, which is standard, but a brief reminder of its meaning in the text might be helpful for some readers.

**5. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical results with clear and rigorous proofs.
* Significant contribution to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Demonstrates optimality of existing techniques (randomized response).

**Against Acceptance:**

* Primarily theoretical; limited discussion of practical considerations.
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Reliance on assumptions that may not hold in all real-world scenarios.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The theoretical contributions are substantial, and the clarity of the presentation is commendable. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above (specifically, adding a discussion of computational complexity, acknowledging the sensitivity to assumptions, and considering a small-scale empirical evaluation) would further strengthen the paper. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field, and their work is likely to stimulate further research on privacy-preserving statistical inference.
