

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper studies minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, focusing on multinomial estimation and density estimation. The authors derive sharp lower and upper bounds showing that local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d for multinomial estimation, and changes the convergence rate for density estimation from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)). They demonstrate that Warner's randomized response mechanism achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation.

The paper builds on established work in differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006; Duchi et al., 2013) and minimax theory, applying standard techniques to a well-defined problem. The theoretical treatment is rigorous, with careful derivation of bounds and matching algorithms. The connection to Warner's 1965 randomized response procedure is historically interesting, though the optimality result for this simple method is somewhat expected given the theoretical framework.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with correct proofs and appropriate use of minimax theory. The bounds are derived carefully using established techniques from information theory and statistical decision theory. However, the paper lacks empirical validation - it's purely theoretical with no experiments to demonstrate the practical relevance of the derived rates. This is a significant limitation for a NIPS submission, where empirical evidence often complements theoretical results.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the proofs are presented logically. The connection between classical minimax theory and privacy constraints is well-explained, making the technical content accessible to experts in the field.

**Originality**: The main contribution is the derivation of sharp minimax rates under local privacy constraints. While the specific rates are novel, the approach is standard application of existing techniques rather than a methodological innovation. The paper doesn't introduce new privacy mechanisms or statistical approaches - it simply characterizes the performance of existing methods under privacy constraints. The connection to Warner's randomized response is interesting but doesn't represent a substantial methodological advance.

**Significance**: The theoretical results are valuable for understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how these results translate to practical applications. The authors provide no discussion of computational complexity, implementation challenges, or comparisons with alternative privacy-preserving methods in real-world scenarios. Without this practical context, the significance of the results is limited for a machine learning conference like NIPS.

The paper's main weakness is its lack of novelty relative to the state of the art. While the specific rates derived are new, the approach is a straightforward application of established minimax theory to a well-studied problem (local differential privacy). Recent work by Duchi et al. (2013), Chaudhuri and Hsu (2012), and Smith (2011) has already established the general framework for such analyses. The paper doesn't significantly extend or challenge this framework.

The strengths of the paper include its rigorous theoretical treatment, clear exposition, and interesting historical connection to Warner's work. However, the weaknesses are substantial: the results are largely expected given prior work, there's no empirical validation, and the practical significance is underdeveloped.

The paper would be appropriate for a statistics or privacy-focused venue, but it doesn't meet NIPS's standard for novelty and significance. The theoretical contribution is incremental rather than transformative. While the bounds are sharp and the proofs are correct, the paper doesn't advance the field in a way that would be meaningful for the broader machine learning community.

I recommend rejection. The paper is technically sound but lacks the novelty and practical significance expected at NIPS. The theoretical results are a natural extension of existing work rather than a substantial new contribution that would advance the field. Without empirical validation or clear demonstration of how these results translate to practical applications, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence of its importance to the machine learning community.
