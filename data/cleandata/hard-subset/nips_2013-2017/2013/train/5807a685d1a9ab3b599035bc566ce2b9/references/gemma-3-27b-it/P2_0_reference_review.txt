## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation. Building upon the framework of differential privacy (specifically, local privacy) and minimax decision theory, the authors derive sharp lower and upper bounds on the convergence rates for estimating multinomial probabilities and nonparametric densities. A key contribution is demonstrating how local privacy introduces a penalty on the effective sample size for multinomial estimation and alters the convergence rate for density estimation compared to classical non-private settings.  They also showcase that Warner’s randomized response procedure is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation and propose efficient estimators for density estimation that achieve the established lower bounds.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper nicely bridges several lines of research: Warner’s early work on privacy in surveys [23], the more recent developments in differential privacy [1, 11], and the classical theory of minimax estimation [25, 26].  It builds on the authors’ previous work [9] by applying the divergence bounds established there to specific statistical problems. The work clearly positions itself within the broader context of privacy-preserving data analysis and demonstrates a rigorous theoretical understanding of the limitations imposed by privacy constraints.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is built upon a solid theoretical framework, leveraging differential privacy and minimax theory effectively. The derivation of the minimax rates is rigorous and well-justified.
* **Sharp Results:** The derived bounds are demonstrably sharp, with the authors providing matching upper and lower bounds for both the multinomial and density estimation problems. This is a significant achievement.
* **Practical Relevance:** The recovery of optimality for Warner's randomized response is a particularly impactful result, demonstrating that a decades-old solution remains optimal even within a rigorous privacy framework. The proposed estimators are efficiently implementable.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is standard, and the key ideas are explained clearly. The introduction provides a good motivation and context.
* **Addresses Important Problem:** The problem of quantifying the trade-off between privacy and utility is fundamental and increasingly important in many applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density Estimation Complexity:** While the analysis for density estimation is theoretically sound, the proposed estimators might be less practical than simpler alternatives in certain scenarios. The reliance on orthogonal series and the complexities of the associated sampling scheme could limit its widespread adoption. A more thorough discussion of the computational costs associated with the proposed density estimation method would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** The focus on smoothness classes defined by bounded derivatives (Sobolev spaces) might be restrictive. Discussing the implications for other density classes or exploring different notions of smoothness would broaden the impact of the work.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The notation can be dense at times, particularly in Section 2. While technically correct, it might require careful reading for readers unfamiliar with the background material.  More intuitive explanations of Lemma 1 and Proposition 1 would be helpful.



**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The analysis is complete and the authors acknowledge limitations.
* **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally clear, but could benefit from simplified explanations of key concepts and more intuitive notation.
* **Originality (9/10):** The combination of differential privacy, minimax theory, and specific statistical estimation problems is novel and makes a significant contribution. The sharp bounds and recovery of Warner’s optimality are particularly original.
* **Significance (9/10):** The results are highly significant. They provide a fundamental understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and utility and will likely influence future research in this area. The practical implications, such as confirming the optimality of randomized response, are substantial.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Rigorous theoretical analysis with sharp results.
* Addresses a fundamentally important and timely problem.
* Demonstrates the optimality of a well-known practical solution (randomized response).
* Clear presentation and well-motivated work.

**Against:**

* Density estimation approach might be computationally complex and potentially less practical than alternatives.
* Scope of density estimation analysis could be broadened.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and significant paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The rigorous theoretical analysis, sharp results, and practical implications outweigh the minor weaknesses.  I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.



**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially expand discussion on the practicality of the density estimation method).
