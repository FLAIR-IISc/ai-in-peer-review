This paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy, bridging concepts from Warner’s early work on privacy in surveys, the formalization of privacy via differential privacy, and statistical decision theory. The core contribution lies in establishing minimax convergence rates for multinomial and density estimation when data is subject to local privacy constraints. The results are sharp, with matching upper and lower bounds, and demonstrate a clear trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction motivates the problem effectively, situating it within a historical context and highlighting the novelty of combining these theoretical frameworks. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations appear sound (though a full verification is beyond the scope of this review). The authors demonstrate a strong grasp of both the privacy and statistical literature, appropriately citing relevant work. The inclusion of proofs in a supplementary material (referenced as [9]) is appropriate for a NIPS submission.

The *originality* of this work is its major strength. While differential privacy and statistical estimation have both been extensively studied, bringing them together within a minimax framework, and specifically analyzing the impact of *local* differential privacy on convergence rates, is a novel and significant contribution. The identification of a different convergence rate for density estimation – a departure from the classical result – is particularly noteworthy. The result regarding Warner’s randomized response being optimal is also interesting, highlighting a connection between a practical, early privacy technique and modern theoretical guarantees.

The *significance* of this work is high. The results provide fundamental limits on the accuracy of statistical estimation when privacy is a concern. This has direct implications for a wide range of applications, including surveys, medical data analysis, and machine learning with privacy constraints. Establishing these theoretical trade-offs is crucial for designing practical privacy-preserving algorithms and for understanding the limitations of such algorithms. The clear quantification of the “privacy penalty” – a reduction in effective sample size or a change in convergence rate – provides valuable guidance for practitioners.

However, a minor weakness is the limited discussion of the practical implications of the derived rates. While the paper demonstrates optimality of randomized response, a more in-depth exploration of efficient algorithms achieving the bounds for density estimation beyond the briefly mentioned perturbation approach would be valuable. Similarly, while Proposition 3 details an optimal estimator, a discussion of the computational cost associated with k = (nα2)1/(2β+2) bins would enhance the practical relevance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** A truly novel combination of theoretical frameworks with significant results.
* **Sharpness:** The minimax rates are established with matching upper and lower bounds.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis is mathematically sound and well-supported.
* **Significance:** The results have broad implications for privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Practical Discussion:** The discussion of practical algorithms achieving the bounds could be more detailed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The novelty, theoretical rigor, and significance of the work outweigh the minor weakness regarding practical discussion. I strongly recommend acceptance. While the results are primarily theoretical, the establishment of fundamental limits and the quantification of trade-offs are invaluable for guiding future research and development in this important area. The paper's bold approach to linking disparate fields and deriving sharp theoretical guarantees is highly commendable, aligning perfectly with the spirit of NIPS.
