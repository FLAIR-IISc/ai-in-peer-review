

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the minimax rates of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, connecting Warner's classical randomized response procedure with modern differential privacy theory. The authors derive sharp lower and upper bounds for multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation under α-local privacy, showing that local privacy introduces a significant penalty in the effective sample size. The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging classical privacy concepts with modern statistical decision theory.

## Major Technical Flaws

The paper contains several critical technical errors that undermine its main claims:

1. **Fundamental Definition Error**: Equation (1) defines α-local differential privacy as "sup { Q(S | Xi = x) Q(S | Xi = x′) | S ∈ σ(Z), and x, x′ ∈ X } ≤ exp(α)." This is incorrect; it should be a ratio (Q(S | Xi = x)/Q(S | Xi = x′)) rather than a product. This is a basic error in the definition of differential privacy that invalidates the entire theoretical framework. The authors cite Evfimievski et al. [13] for this definition, but the correct definition is well-established in the differential privacy literature.

2. **Critical Typo in Randomized Response**: In equation (10), the probability is written as "11+exp(α/2)" instead of "1+exp(α/2)". This typo makes the randomized response mechanism mathematically incoherent, as it would produce probabilities greater than 1. This is a severe error that affects the entire section on optimal mechanisms.

3. **Inconsistent Theorem 1**: The lower bound in Theorem 1 states "cℓmin{1, √(nα²), d/(nα²)}" but should be "cℓmin{1, √(d/(nα²))}" to match the upper bound. The current lower bound is inconsistent with the upper bound and does not match the claimed rates. The term "√(nα²)" is dimensionally inconsistent with the rest of the expression.

4. **Inconsistent Rate in Proposition 2**: For β=1, Theorem 2 states the minimax rate is (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) = (nα²)^(-1/2), but Proposition 2 states the bound as "5(α²n)^(-1/2) + √α n^(-3/4)". The second term should be (α²n)^(-3/4) to match the theoretical rate, not √α n^(-3/4). This inconsistency suggests a miscalculation in the proof.

## Other Significant Issues

5. **Incorrect Reference for Projection**: The paper claims "the projection of θ̂part onto the probability simplex can be done in time linear in the dimension d of the problem [3]" but reference [3] is "An O(n) algorithm for quadratic knapsack problems" by Brucker, which is unrelated to simplex projection. This is a clear reference error that undermines the claim of computational efficiency.

6. **Incomplete Proof of Optimality**: The paper claims Warner's randomized response "enjoys optimal sample complexity" but provides only a sketch of the proof rather than a complete demonstration that the rate achieved matches the lower bound in Theorem 1. The proof is incomplete and lacks necessary details.

7. **Omission of Key Related Work**: The paper fails to cite Smith's "Privacy-preserving statistical estimation with optimal convergence rates" (NIPS 2011), which directly addresses the same problem. The authors should explicitly discuss how their work differs from Smith's.

8. **Notation Inconsistency**: The paper uses "an . bn" to denote "an = O(bn)" in the notation section, which is non-standard. The standard notation is "an = O(bn)".

## Strengths

Despite these critical flaws, the paper has notable strengths:
- It provides a clear connection between classical privacy concepts (Warner [23]) and modern differential privacy.
- The minimax framework for privacy-constrained estimation is well-motivated and theoretically sound (except for the critical definition error).
- The results for density estimation are interesting and show a different rate behavior than classical results.

## Overall Assessment

The paper's main contribution is significant in theory, but the critical technical errors (particularly in the definition of local privacy and the randomized response mechanism) invalidate the main results. These are not minor typographical errors but fundamental mistakes that undermine the entire theoretical framework. The paper also contains other significant issues with notation, references, and incomplete proofs.

While the paper addresses an important problem at the intersection of privacy and statistics, the presence of these critical technical flaws prevents me from recommending acceptance. The errors are severe enough that the main claims of the paper cannot be trusted.

## Recommendation

**Reject** - The paper contains critical technical errors that invalidate the main results, and the errors are substantial enough to overshadow the paper's theoretical contribution. The definition error in Equation (1) is particularly severe, as it fundamentally misrepresents the privacy constraint being studied. The paper would require significant revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
