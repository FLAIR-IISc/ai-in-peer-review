## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation. Building on the framework of differential privacy, the authors provide sharp minimax lower and upper bounds for estimating multinomial probabilities and nonparametric densities under local privacy constraints.  They demonstrate that local privacy introduces a penalty on the effective sample size for multinomial estimation (scaling with nα²/d) and alters the convergence rate for density estimation (scaling with (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2)).  Importantly, they show that Warner's randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation and provide efficiently implementable algorithms achieving the derived bounds for both problems.  The work rigorously connects classical statistical decision theory with the modern demands of privacy-preserving data analysis.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper nicely bridges several established fields. It connects Warner’s (1965) work on randomized response with the more recent developments in differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006) and minimax theory (e.g., Tsybakov, 2009). The authors build upon their prior work (Duchi et al., 2013) establishing divergence bounds for differentially private estimation, and appropriately apply those bounds to these specific estimation problems. The paper appropriately places itself in relation to existing literature on differential privacy and statistical inference, referencing crucial papers in each area. 

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:**  The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, deriving sharp minimax bounds.  The use of Lemma 1 (Le Cam’s method and Fano’s inequality) and Propositions 1 & 2 (from Duchi et al., 2013) is well-justified and effectively utilized.
* **Clear Connections to Practical Problems:** The focus on probability estimation is highly relevant, stemming from the original motivations for privacy in surveys and extending to modern applications.
* **Optimality Results:** Demonstrating the optimality of Warner’s randomized response procedure is a significant and historically insightful contribution.
* **Constructive Algorithms:**  The authors don't just provide lower bounds; they furnish algorithms achieving matching upper bounds, validating the sharpness of their results. This is crucial for practical applicability.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clear and well-structured.  The notation is standard, and the mathematical derivations appear sound.
* **Important Contribution:**  This work provides a fundamental understanding of the costs associated with privacy and offers a benchmark for evaluating the performance of privacy-preserving statistical methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** The density estimation results are presented for a specific class of functions (smooth densities) and with a particular metric (L2 norm). While this is a reasonable starting point, exploring other density classes or metrics (e.g., Hellinger distance, Kullback-Leibler divergence) could broaden the impact.
* **Computational Cost of the Histogram Estimator:**  While theoretically optimal, the histogram estimator, as described in Section 4.2, might become computationally expensive for large dimensions or data volumes, and the discussion of this aspect is limited.
* **Complexity of Orthogonal Series Estimator:** The implementation of the orthogonal series estimator presented in Section 4.3, particularly the sampling scheme (19), appears complex and may not be easily implemented in practice.  A more detailed discussion of the practical considerations would be beneficial.
* **Discussion on Real-World Applicability:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper could benefit from a more extensive discussion on the limitations and challenges of applying these results in real-world scenarios. For instance, the assumption of α-local differential privacy might not always be realistic.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a critical problem at the intersection of statistics and privacy.
* The theoretical results are rigorous, novel, and impactful.
* The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature.
* The paper provides both lower and upper bounds, establishing sharpness.
* The algorithms presented are efficiently implementable and achieve optimal rates.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scope of the density estimation results could be broadened.
* Some aspects of the proposed algorithms may be complex to implement in practice.
* A more detailed discussion of real-world limitations would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a few minor weaknesses, this is a strong and significant contribution to the field. The paper provides a fundamental understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy in probability estimation, and the results are both theoretically sound and practically relevant. The novelty of the work and its clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. 

Specifically, I suggest the authors:

*  Briefly discuss the potential for extending the density estimation results to other function classes and metrics.
*  Address the computational complexity of the proposed estimators.
*  Expand the discussion on the practical implications and limitations of the presented framework.



**Confidence:** 8/10.
