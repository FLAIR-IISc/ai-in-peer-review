This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building on the framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner’s original problem of estimating population proportions with privacy constraints and extend it to nonparametric density estimation. The core contribution lies in establishing sharp minimax rates for both problems under local differential privacy, demonstrating a predictable loss in sample efficiency for multinomial estimation and a more nuanced change in convergence rate for density estimation. The paper is well-motivated, connecting historical work on privacy with modern theoretical tools.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents rigorous mathematical analysis, carefully deriving lower and upper bounds on minimax rates. The use of KL-divergence and mutual information bounds is appropriate and well-executed. The proofs (detailed in the accompanying work [9]) are likely sound, given the authors’ expertise.
* **Novelty:** The combination of differential privacy with minimax theory to characterize the privacy-utility trade-off in probability estimation is a novel contribution. The identification of a different convergence rate for density estimation under local privacy is particularly interesting.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides a good overview of the motivation and contributions. The notation is standard and well-defined. The connection to Warner’s original work is explicitly highlighted.
* **Significance:**  The results provide a fundamental understanding of the costs associated with privacy-preserving statistical inference. The identified rates serve as benchmarks for evaluating the performance of practical privacy mechanisms. The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response procedure is a noteworthy practical implication.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Achievability Results:** While the paper demonstrates achievability for the multinomial estimation problem using randomized response, the density estimation results rely on a more complex orthogonal series estimator. The practical challenges of implementing this estimator in high dimensions are not fully discussed. While the authors note that Laplace perturbation is sub-optimal, a more detailed comparison of different practical estimators (beyond histograms) and their performance would be valuable.
* **Dependence on Prior Work [9]:** A significant portion of the technical details and proofs are relegated to a previous work [9]. While this is common practice, it makes it difficult to fully assess the completeness of the current paper in isolation.  Readers need to consult [9] to fully understand the mathematical details.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical rates. A discussion of the sensitivity of these rates to realistic data distributions, sample sizes, and parameter settings would strengthen the paper.  How do these theoretical penalties manifest in real-world applications?
* **Incremental Nature:**  While the combination of tools is novel, the results largely confirm intuition - privacy comes at a cost.  The paper doesn't present a surprising or paradigm-shifting result.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in privacy and statistical inference with a rigorous mathematical framework.
* The derived minimax rates provide valuable benchmarks for evaluating privacy-preserving algorithms.
* The work is technically sound, well-written, and clearly presented.
* The novelty lies in the rigorous combination of established techniques to provide sharp bounds.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper’s results are largely consistent with intuition and may not represent a significant breakthrough.
* The limited discussion of practical considerations reduces the immediate impact of the work.
* The heavy reliance on prior work [9] necessitates consulting another resource for a complete understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.  The paper rigorously characterizes the trade-offs between privacy and efficiency in probability estimation and provides sharp minimax rates. While the results are largely intuitive, the mathematical rigor and clarity of presentation are commendable.  I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors might benefit from expanding the discussion of practical considerations and providing more detail on the limitations of the proposed estimators.  A more self-contained presentation (perhaps incorporating key results from [9] into the appendix) would also improve the paper's accessibility.
