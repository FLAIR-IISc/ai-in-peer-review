This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building upon the framework of differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors revisit Warner’s original problem of estimating population fractions while protecting individual responses and extend it to nonparametric density estimation. The core contribution lies in deriving sharp minimax rates for both problems under local differential privacy, revealing a penalty in statistical efficiency due to the privacy constraint. The paper is well-motivated, connecting a classic problem in statistical disclosure control with modern privacy concepts.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper rigorously applies minimax theory and differential privacy, providing a solid theoretical basis for the results. The use of lower and upper bounds is a hallmark of good theoretical work.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The setup is clearly defined, and the authors accurately situate their work within the existing literature on privacy and statistical inference.
* **Sharp Results:** The derived minimax rates are sharp, demonstrating that the proposed estimators achieve optimal performance under the given privacy constraints. This is evidenced by the achievability results, like showing randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation.
* **Interesting Departure from Classical Results:** The observation that the convergence rate for density estimation changes in a non-trivial way under local privacy is a significant finding.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors skillfully connect their work to Warner’s original randomized response procedure, highlighting its optimality even in the light of recent advances in privacy-preserving techniques.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the arguments. The notation is mostly standard and clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Heavy reliance on existing results:** While appropriately citing related work, a significant portion of the technical machinery (Lemma 1, Proposition 1) is relegated to a previous paper ([9]). This makes it harder for readers unfamiliar with [9] to fully appreciate the technical details of the proofs without referencing it. A more self-contained presentation, perhaps including key proofs or lemmas directly in the appendix, would be beneficial.
* **Limited discussion of practical implications:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical guarantees. While the optimality of randomized response is noted, a more in-depth discussion of the practical challenges of implementing these estimators (e.g., parameter selection, computational cost) would be valuable.
* **Density estimation practical aspects could be expanded:** While the paper provides a theoretical rate for density estimation, the practical considerations of the orthogonal series estimator are less detailed. Discussing the choice of basis functions and the impact of the projection operator on performance would enhance the practical relevance.
* **Minor Notation Issues:** The notation could be slightly more consistent. For instance, the use of both "an . bn" and "an ≍ bn" for asymptotic equivalence is slightly awkward. Choosing one convention would improve readability.
* **Limited exploration of alternative privacy mechanisms:** The paper largely focuses on local differential privacy. A brief discussion of how the results might differ under other privacy models (e.g., central differential privacy) could broaden the scope of the work.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem at the intersection of statistics and privacy.
* The results are technically sound and provide sharp bounds on the performance of estimators under privacy constraints.
* The connection to Warner’s work and the identification of optimal mechanisms are significant contributions.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The heavy reliance on a previous paper ([9]) makes the paper less self-contained and potentially difficult to fully grasp without consulting it.
* The limited discussion of practical implications and implementation challenges reduces the paper's immediate impact.
* While the theoretical results are strong, the lack of more extensive exploration of alternative privacy mechanisms limits the scope of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While the reliance on a previous paper and the limited discussion of practical issues are weaknesses, the rigor, clarity, and sharpness of the results outweigh these concerns. I lean towards **acceptance**, but with the strong recommendation that the authors consider making the paper more self-contained by including key technical details in the appendix and expanding the discussion of practical implications.  Addressing the minor notation inconsistencies would also be beneficial.
