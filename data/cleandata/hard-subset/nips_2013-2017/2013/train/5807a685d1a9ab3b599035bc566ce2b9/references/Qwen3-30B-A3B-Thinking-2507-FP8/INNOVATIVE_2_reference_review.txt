

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by bridging the classical privacy work of Warner [23] with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, revealing fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The paper's main contributions are threefold: (1) It establishes minimax convergence rates for multinomial estimation under local privacy, showing that the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d; (2) It demonstrates that Warner's randomized response procedure is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation, a surprising and valuable result given that this method has been known for nearly 50 years; and (3) It provides sharp minimax rates for nonparametric density estimation under local privacy, revealing a fundamental difference in convergence rates compared to classical results (from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2)).

The theoretical framework is rigorous, with carefully constructed lower and upper bounds that match in all cases. The authors effectively use information-theoretic tools to derive their results, demonstrating how to combine the minimax framework with differential privacy constraints. The paper is well-organized, with clear notation and logical progression from the theoretical foundation to specific applications.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While the connection between differential privacy and statistical inference has been explored in prior work (e.g., [11, 9, 24]), this paper provides the first sharp minimax analysis for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The finding that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly striking, as it reveals that an approach developed half a century ago remains state-of-the-art. The density estimation results also offer a new perspective on how privacy constraints fundamentally alter convergence rates, moving beyond the "multiplicative loss" intuition to reveal a different polynomial rate.

The paper's significance is high. It establishes fundamental theoretical trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, providing a rigorous framework for understanding privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results have direct implications for survey design, data collection, and privacy-preserving machine learning. The authors successfully show how classical privacy concepts can be brought into contact with modern statistical theory to yield new insights.

The paper is technically sound with no apparent methodological flaws. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the authors carefully address both the strengths and limitations of their approach. The paper's theoretical nature is appropriate for the scope of NIPS, as it provides fundamental insights rather than just incremental improvements.

The main potential limitation is the purely theoretical nature of the work, with no empirical validation. However, for a paper that establishes sharp theoretical rates, this is expected and appropriate. The authors could briefly discuss practical implementation challenges in future work, but this doesn't detract from the paper's theoretical contribution.

In terms of originality, this paper stands out from previous NIPS work on privacy and statistics. While there have been many papers on differential privacy and statistical inference (e.g., [9, 11, 24]), this is the first to establish sharp minimax rates under local privacy constraints for both finite-dimensional and nonparametric estimation problems. The connection between Warner's 1965 work and modern differential privacy is particularly novel.

The paper's most striking contribution is the revelation that the optimal privacy-preserving strategy for multinomial estimation has been known since the 1960s. This historical insight, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis, makes the paper both conceptually rich and technically sound.

Given the reviewer's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. The theoretical contributions are significant, the results are sharp and well-supported, and the paper establishes a new direction for understanding privacy-statistics trade-offs. The paper's novelty and theoretical depth outweigh any minor limitations in scope or lack of empirical validation.

Strengths: Novel connection between classical privacy and modern minimax theory, sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation, surprising optimality result for Warner's randomized response, clear theoretical framework.

Weaknesses: Purely theoretical (no empirical validation), narrow focus on specific estimation problems (though this is appropriate for the theoretical contribution).

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results establish fundamental limits and optimal procedures for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, providing a new theoretical foundation for future work in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
