

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary

This paper establishes a theoretical framework for understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors bridge Warner's classical randomized response approach (1965) with modern differential privacy and minimax statistical theory. They provide sharp minimax rates for two canonical problems: multinomial estimation (where privacy reduces effective sample size from n to nα²/d) and nonparametric density estimation (where the convergence rate changes from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to (nα²)^{-2β/(2β+2)} for densities with β continuous derivatives). The paper demonstrates that Warner's randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation, and provides efficient estimators that achieve the established lower bounds for both problems.

This work builds upon Duchi et al.'s "Local Privacy and Statistical Minimax Rates" (arXiv:1302.3203) and connects with prior work on differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006) and minimax theory (Tsybakov, 2009), while offering novel sharp rates for specific privacy-constrained estimation problems.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds. The authors carefully establish the minimax rates for two important statistical problems under local privacy constraints, with precise constants and clear conditions. They properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., that direct perturbation of samples isn't optimal for density estimation) and provide comprehensive theoretical justification for their claims.

**Originality (Strong):** The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates for local privacy-constrained estimation. The demonstration that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response is minimax optimal is particularly insightful. The density estimation results, showing a different convergence rate exponent under privacy constraints, represent a novel theoretical insight that departs from classical minimax results.

**Significance (High):** This work provides a fundamental understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The theoretical framework can guide the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The paper's connection between classical privacy concepts and modern differential privacy creates a valuable bridge between historical and current research in the field.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The main results are stated precisely with appropriate technical details. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of Empirical Validation (Moderate):** While the paper is theoretically strong, it lacks empirical validation of the proposed estimators. Given that the authors claim the estimators are "efficiently computable," a brief empirical demonstration would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

**Scope Limitation (Minor):** The paper focuses on two specific problems (multinomial and density estimation), which are important but somewhat narrow. While the theoretical framework is generalizable, the paper doesn't discuss how their approach might extend to other statistical models (e.g., regression, classification).

**Related Work (Minor):** While the paper cites relevant work, it could better distinguish itself from recent work on differentially private statistical estimation (e.g., Chaudhuri & Hsu, 2012; Smith, 2011). A more detailed comparison with these works would strengthen the paper's position in the literature.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results are rigorous, the connections to prior work are well-established, and the insights about the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility are important. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

The main limitations (lack of empirical validation and narrow scope) are relatively minor for a theoretical paper, especially given the paper's clear focus on establishing sharp theoretical bounds. The paper advances the state of the art in understanding the theoretical limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Provides sharp minimax rates for two important statistical problems under local privacy
- Demonstrates that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal (a surprising and significant insight)
- Establishes a theoretical framework connecting classical privacy concepts with modern differential privacy
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with both lower and upper bounds
- Clear and well-organized presentation of technical results

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Lacks empirical validation (though this is less critical for a theoretical paper)
- Focuses on specific problems rather than broader applications
- Could better distinguish itself from recent work on differentially private statistical estimation

## Overall

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The minor limitations do not detract from the paper's strong theoretical contribution. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

The paper's insights about the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation will be valuable to researchers working at the intersection of statistics, machine learning, and privacy. The demonstration that a method developed nearly 50 years ago is minimax optimal is particularly noteworthy and should be of interest to the broader NIPS community.
