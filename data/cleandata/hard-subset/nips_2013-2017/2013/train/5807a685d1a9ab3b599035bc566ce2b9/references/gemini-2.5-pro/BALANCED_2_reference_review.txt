### Summary

This paper establishes a rigorous connection between the modern framework of α-local differential privacy and classical minimax decision theory. The authors aim to characterize the fundamental trade-off between privacy guarantees and statistical efficiency by deriving sharp minimax rates for two canonical probability estimation problems.

The first main contribution concerns the estimation of a d-dimensional multinomial probability vector. The authors prove that under α-local privacy, the minimax risk (in both squared L2 and L1 norms) scales as if the effective sample size were reduced from `n` to approximately `n * α^2 / d`. This provides a precise, quantitative penalty for privacy. Crucially, they show that this lower bound is achievable, and that a mechanism equivalent to Warner's classic 1965 randomized response is, in fact, minimax optimal.

The second, and perhaps more striking, contribution addresses nonparametric density estimation for functions in a Sobolev smoothness class `F_β`. While the classical non-private minimax rate for this problem is `n^(-2β / (2β+1))`, the authors demonstrate that local privacy fundamentally alters the problem's difficulty. They establish a new, slower minimax rate of `(n * α^2)^(-2β / (2β+2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent represents a more severe penalty than the effective sample size reduction seen in the multinomial case. The paper provides constructive and computationally efficient estimators—a private histogram for Lipschitz densities (`β=1`) and a novel orthogonal series estimator for general `β > 1`—that achieve this new optimal rate. The analysis also highlights that naive privacy mechanisms, such as adding Laplace noise to the data points, are provably suboptimal.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are significant and appear to be well-supported by the theoretical analysis. The authors correctly frame their problem of estimating a population parameter `θ(P)` as being distinct from, and more challenging than, the common DP goal of estimating a sample quantity `θ(x1:n)`, making their lower bounds stronger and more fundamental. The use of information-theoretic tools (Le Cam's and Fano's methods combined with custom divergence bounds from their companion paper [9]) is appropriate and powerful. The work is a complete and self-contained intellectual contribution, presenting both lower bounds (characterizing the fundamental limits) and matching upper bounds (providing constructive, achievable schemes).

One limitation is the heavy reliance on a companion arXiv preprint [9] for the proofs of the main information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) and the density estimation lower bound (Theorem 2). While this is a common practice, it prevents a full and independent verification of the paper's technical backbone from the submitted manuscript alone. The reviewer must take the correctness of these foundational lemmas on faith.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent motivation, clearly situates the work within the broader context of privacy and statistics, and precisely states the main contributions. The background section on the minimax framework is concise and helpful for readers who may not be experts in decision theory. The progression from the finite-dimensional multinomial problem to the infinite-dimensional density estimation problem is logical and effective. The notation is standard and clearly defined. The authors do a good job of providing intuition for their results, such as explaining why simple noise addition is suboptimal for density estimation by referencing deconvolution literature.

A small point for improvement would be to provide more intuition or implementation details for the orthogonal series sampling strategy in Section 4.3. While the description is formal, the mechanism for sampling uniformly from the half-space of a hypercube (Eq. 19) is non-trivial, and a brief comment on how this can be done efficiently (e.g., via rejection sampling as mentioned) would enhance the paper's practical value and reproducibility.

### Originality

The paper is highly original and makes a foundational contribution. While minimax theory and differential privacy are established fields, this work is one of the first to rigorously synthesize them to derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds for estimation under *local* privacy constraints.

The key novelties are:
1.  The precise characterization of the minimax rate for multinomial estimation, and the elegant connection showing that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response technique is optimal under the modern definition of differential privacy.
2.  The discovery that for nonparametric density estimation, local privacy changes the exponent in the rate of convergence. This is a deep and non-obvious result that fundamentally reshapes our understanding of the cost of privacy in infinite-dimensional statistical problems.
3.  The design of a novel and non-trivial orthogonal series-based mechanism that is provably optimal for density estimation, demonstrating that sophisticated mechanisms are required to achieve fundamental limits.

The paper is well-referenced and clearly delineates its contributions from prior work in both privacy (e.g., [24, 19]) and classical statistics (e.g., [22]).

### Significance

The significance of this work is very high. It provides a formal, decision-theoretic language for reasoning about the price of privacy, moving the discussion from ad-hoc algorithm design to identifying fundamental limits. Researchers and practitioners can now use these results to understand the inherent statistical cost for a given level of local privacy (`α`). The results are likely to be influential and built upon in several ways:
*   They provide a benchmark against which new privacy-preserving algorithms can be measured.
*   The techniques developed can likely be extended to other statistical problems, such as regression, classification, and structured prediction.
*   The finding that optimal mechanisms can be non-obvious (and that simple perturbation is suboptimal) will encourage more principled design of privacy-preserving machine learning algorithms.

The re-validation of randomized response is also a significant practical result, giving strong theoretical backing to a simple, interpretable, and widely-used method. This paper is a landmark contribution to the theoretical foundations of private data analysis and is likely to become a key reference in the field.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pros)
*   **Fundamental Contribution:** Establishes the first sharp minimax rates for canonical estimation problems under local differential privacy.
*   **High Impact:** The results provide a precise, quantitative understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off, which is of broad interest to both the machine learning and statistics communities.
*   **Novel Insights:** The discovery of a change in the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a deep and surprising finding.
*   **Constructive and Complete:** Provides both tight lower bounds and efficient, optimal algorithms to match them.
*   **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, motivated, and organized.

### Arguments for Rejection (Cons)
*   **Proof Deferral:** The reliance on an external manuscript for key technical proofs hinders a fully self-contained review of its correctness.

### Suggestions for Improvement
*   If space permits, consider including a brief proof sketch of Proposition 1 in an appendix to make the paper more self-contained.
*   A brief discussion on the behavior of the rates for `α > 1` would be welcome, as the current analysis is focused on `α ∈ [0, 1]`. The dependence on `α` seems to be through terms like `(e^α - 1)^2`, which grows much faster than `α^2` for larger `α`. Clarifying this would be valuable.
*   Could you comment on the magnitude of the constants hidden by the `≍` notation? While asymptotic rates are the main goal, understanding if the constants are small or large is important for practical application, especially in the density estimation case.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is an outstanding paper that makes a fundamental and elegant contribution to an important problem. The strengths are numerous and significant. Despite the minor issue of deferring proofs, the quality of the ideas, the clarity of the presentation, and the importance of the results make this a clear accept.
