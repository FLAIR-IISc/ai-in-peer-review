

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper addresses the theoretical trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation problems under local differential privacy constraints. The authors connect classical minimax decision theory with differential privacy to establish sharp convergence rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. The paper builds on Warner's randomized response framework [23] and provides a formal minimax analysis under local privacy constraints, demonstrating how privacy requirements affect statistical efficiency.

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) For multinomial estimation, it shows that the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy, with Warner's randomized response achieving this optimal rate. (2) For density estimation with β continuous derivatives, it establishes a new convergence rate of (α²n)^(-2β/(2β+2)), differing from the classical n^(-2β/(2β+1)) rate. The authors provide matching upper bounds with computationally efficient algorithms for both problems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Rigor and Novelty:** The paper provides sharp minimax bounds that are both theoretically interesting and practically relevant. The connection between statistical decision theory and differential privacy is novel and well-executed. The result showing Warner's randomized response procedure is optimal is particularly elegant and insightful.

2. **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper thoroughly covers both finite-dimensional (multinomial) and nonparametric (density estimation) settings, providing a unified theoretical framework. The use of Le Cam's two-point method and Fano's inequality for lower bounds is appropriate and well-executed.

3. **Practical Relevance:** The authors provide efficient algorithms that achieve the theoretical bounds, including a simple randomized response mechanism for multinomial estimation. The connection to classical statistical problems makes the results accessible to a broad audience.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, logical flow, and sufficient technical detail. The notation is consistent throughout, and the exposition effectively bridges concepts from statistics and privacy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Practical Discussion:** While the paper provides theoretical results, it lacks detailed discussion of how these bounds translate to practical applications. For instance, it doesn't address how to choose α for a given utility requirement, or how the results scale with dimensionality in real-world settings. The paper would benefit from a more concrete discussion of the privacy-utility trade-off in applications.

2. **Empirical Validation Missing:** The paper focuses on theoretical results without providing empirical validation. It would strengthen the paper to include numerical experiments showing how the proposed estimators perform in practice compared to existing methods.

3. **Computational Complexity Details:** While the paper mentions that the estimators are "efficiently computable," it doesn't provide detailed computational complexity analysis. For instance, the projection step in the multinomial case requires O(d) time, but the paper doesn't discuss how this scales with dimensionality in practice.

4. **Comparison with Prior Work:** The paper could better contextualize its results against recent work on privacy-preserving statistical estimation. For example, how do the results compare to those in Chaudhuri & Hsu (2012) or Smith (2011), which also study privacy-utility trade-offs?

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes significant contributions to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. The results are sharp, well-motivated, and provide important theoretical foundations for privacy-preserving statistical inference. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

While the paper excels in theoretical rigor, it would be strengthened by more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations and empirical validation. The theoretical results are compelling, but the practical implications could be made more tangible for the broader NeurIPS community.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference, providing sharp minimax bounds that clarify fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The results are significant, well-executed, and would be of interest to the NeurIPS community. The paper would be even stronger with a brief empirical validation and more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations, but these are not required for acceptance at NeurIPS.

**Suggested Improvements:**
1. Add a short section discussing practical implementation considerations (e.g., how to choose α for a given utility requirement).
2. Include a brief empirical comparison of the proposed estimators with existing methods (even if just synthetic experiments).
3. Expand the discussion of computational complexity for the proposed algorithms.
4. Provide a clearer comparison with recent work on privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation.
