This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem: understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The authors bring together differential privacy, minimax decision theory, and classical statistical estimation, a combination I find quite compelling and novel. The results are mathematically rigorous and demonstrate a clear, quantifiable penalty for imposing local privacy, both in multinomial estimation (where they interestingly show optimality of Warner's randomized response) and density estimation. The departure from standard minimax rates in the density estimation setting is a particularly noteworthy and intriguing finding.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of differential privacy and minimax theory is a strong contribution. The specific results regarding the convergence rate degradation in density estimation and the optimality of randomized response are particularly novel.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is grounded in solid theoretical foundations, with clear definitions and rigorous proofs (detailed in the referenced [9]).
*   **Significance:** Understanding these trade-offs is crucial for deploying privacy-preserving machine learning and statistical analysis in real-world applications. The results provide a principled way to assess the cost of privacy.
*   **Clarity:** While dense, the paper is reasonably well-written. The introduction does a good job of motivating the problem and situating the work within the existing literature. The notation is standard, and the derivations are presented in a logical manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is heavily theoretical. While the authors mention the existence of efficient algorithms, the empirical validation is relatively weak. Demonstrating the practical impact of these rate degradations with simulations, even simple ones, would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Scope of Density Estimation:** The density estimation section feels somewhat less developed. The choice of the trigonometric basis, while mathematically convenient, may not be representative of all real-world density estimation problems. Exploring other basis functions or kernel density estimation approaches could broaden the applicability of the results.
*   **Discussion of Practical Implications:** The discussion could be expanded to address more explicitly the practical implications of the derived rates. For example, what sample sizes are needed to achieve a certain level of accuracy with a given privacy parameter in realistic scenarios?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous and provide valuable insights into the fundamental trade-offs involved.
*   The demonstration of optimality for Warner's randomized response is a surprisingly elegant result.
*   The authors clearly identify limitations and suggest directions for future work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the findings.
*   The density estimation section could benefit from further development and exploration of different settings.
*   While clearly written, the paper’s density may deter broader adoption.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly lean towards acceptance. The paper's novelty and theoretical depth are impressive. While the lack of extensive empirical validation is a concern, I believe the significance of the theoretical results outweighs this weakness. The paper’s contribution to understanding the theoretical limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation is substantial, and the results provide a solid foundation for future work in this area. I encourage the authors to consider adding some simulations to illustrate the practical implications of their findings in a revised version. I view this paper as a significant step forward in our understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off and expect it to have a lasting impact on the field.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
