### Summary

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and privacy in two fundamental probability estimation problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The authors operate within the framework of local differential privacy, where each data point is privatized individually before being analyzed. Using tools from minimax theory, they derive sharp lower bounds on the estimation error for any locally private procedure. They complement these lower bounds with matching, computationally efficient upper bounds, thereby precisely characterizing the statistical cost of privacy.

The main results are twofold:
1.  For estimating a d-dimensional multinomial probability vector, the authors show that `α`-local privacy incurs a penalty that reduces the effective sample size from `n` to `nα^2/d`. They demonstrate elegantly that a variant of Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal in this modern privacy framework.
2.  For nonparametric estimation of a density with `β` degrees of smoothness, they establish a more dramatic result: local privacy fundamentally alters the rate of convergence. The classical minimax risk, which scales as `n^(-2β/(2β+1))`, worsens to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent represents a significant and permanent loss of statistical information. The authors propose novel and non-trivial estimators—a privatized histogram for Lipschitz densities (`β=1`) and an orthogonal series estimator for general `β>1`—that achieve this new optimal rate.

This work bridges the gap between classical statistical privacy (Warner, 1965), modern computer science notions of privacy (differential privacy), and classical statistical decision theory (minimax rates).

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Impact:** This is a foundational paper that addresses a crucial question at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy. By quantifying the exact statistical price of local privacy, the work provides a clear and interpretable guideline for researchers and practitioners. The discovery that privacy can change the fundamental polynomial convergence rate for nonparametric problems is a deep and important insight that will likely influence future research in private statistical inference.

*   **Technical Quality and Completeness:** The paper is technically outstanding. It provides a complete minimax analysis, establishing both lower bounds (impossibility results) and matching upper bounds (achievability results via constructive algorithms). This "sharp" characterization is the gold standard for theoretical work and offers a definitive answer to the question of optimal rates. The authors are careful to distinguish their stronger "population error" bounds from the more common "sample error" bounds in prior privacy literature.

*   **Originality:** The synthesis of local differential privacy and minimax theory to derive sharp rates for these canonical statistical problems is highly original. While related ideas have appeared in different communities, this paper brings them together in a powerful and coherent framework. The design of the orthogonal series estimator for density estimation is particularly novel, as it correctly identifies that simple noise-addition mechanisms are suboptimal and devises a more sophisticated privacy mechanism to achieve the minimax rate. The connection drawn to Warner's randomized response is also an elegant and insightful contribution.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a concise yet thorough introduction to local privacy and the minimax framework. The main results are stated clearly, and the implications (e.g., reduction in effective sample size, change in convergence rate) are explained intuitively. The contrast with classical non-private rates makes the impact of the privacy constraint immediately apparent.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Lack of Self-Contained Proofs:** The paper relies heavily on an external arXiv preprint [9] by the same authors for its main technical proofs, including the key information-theoretic bounds in Proposition 1 and the lower bound for density estimation in Theorem 2. While this is understandable given conference page limits, it makes the paper difficult to fully verify on its own. It would strengthen the submission if the authors could include a proof sketch for at least one of their main lower bounds in an appendix to give the reader more confidence in the technical machinery.

*   **Practicality of the Orthogonal Series Estimator:** The sampling strategy (19) for the optimal density estimator is quite sophisticated. The paper asserts that it is "efficiently computable (for example by rejection sampling)" but provides no further details. A brief discussion of the computational complexity, particularly the acceptance rate of a potential rejection sampler and its dependence on the dimension `k`, would be very valuable for assessing the practical viability of the proposed method.

*   **Dependence on Small `α`:** The derived rates often scale with `α^2`, which typically comes from an approximation like `(e^α - 1)^2 ≈ α^2`. This is most accurate for small `α` (e.g., `α ≤ 1`), a regime the authors explicitly mention. It would be helpful to state more formally the range of `α` for which the stated rates are sharp and perhaps comment on the rate dependence for large `α`. While the small-`α` regime is arguably the most meaningful for strong privacy guarantees, clarifying this point would make the results even more precise.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  The paper solves a fundamental theoretical problem with significant practical implications.
2.  The results are sharp (matching upper and lower bounds), representing a very strong and complete theoretical contribution.
3.  The finding that privacy can degrade the polynomial convergence rate is a major and novel insight.
4.  The work is exceptionally clear, well-written, and connects several important fields in a novel way.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  The lack of self-contained proofs for key results makes full technical verification challenging during the review process.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper of high quality, originality, and significance. It establishes a fundamental benchmark for the performance of statistical estimators under the constraint of local differential privacy. The theoretical results are deep and the exposition is crystal clear. Despite the minor weakness regarding deferred proofs, the contribution is substantial and impactful. This paper will be of great interest to the NIPS community and is a clear candidate for acceptance, potentially as an oral presentation.
