

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary and Context
This paper bridges minimax decision theory with differential privacy to establish sharp convergence rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors revisit Warner's randomized response for multinomial estimation, proving it achieves optimal rates, and derive new minimax rates for density estimation under local differential privacy. The work connects classical privacy concepts with modern differential privacy frameworks, providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs. This aligns with recent NIPS work on privacy and statistics (e.g., [9, 24]), but extends it by establishing sharp minimax rates rather than just feasibility results.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor and Novelty**: The paper establishes sharp minimax lower and upper bounds for both multinomial and density estimation under local differential privacy. The key insight that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation is both theoretically elegant and practically significant, resolving a long-standing question about optimal privacy-preserving mechanisms.

2. **Clear Separation of Privacy and Statistical Trade-offs**: The paper successfully separates the privacy constraint from the statistical estimation problem, demonstrating how privacy constraints fundamentally alter convergence rates. The density estimation result (Theorem 2) is particularly noteworthy, showing a different exponent (2β/(2β+2) vs. 2β/(2β+1)) compared to classical results, which is not immediately obvious from prior work.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The paper connects historical privacy methods (Warner's randomized response) with modern theoretical frameworks, demonstrating that some classical approaches are actually optimal. This has important implications for practitioners designing privacy-preserving surveys.

4. **Comprehensive Treatment**: The authors provide both theoretical lower bounds and efficiently computable estimators that achieve these bounds, which is rare in privacy literature. The histogram and orthogonal series estimators (Propositions 2 and 3) are practical and well-analyzed.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity**: While the paper mentions that projection onto the simplex is linear in d, it doesn't address how this scales in high-dimensional settings (e.g., d = 10^6). For many modern applications, this computational cost could be prohibitive, and the paper doesn't discuss alternatives.

2. **Narrow Range of α Considered**: The analysis focuses on α ∈ [0,1], which is reasonable for small privacy budgets, but doesn't address the behavior for large α (e.g., α > 1), which could be practically relevant for applications requiring minimal privacy.

3. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical, which is appropriate for NIPS, but the absence of any empirical validation (even synthetic experiments) makes it difficult to assess the practical magnitude of the privacy-utility trade-off.

4. **Ambiguity in Density Estimation Framework**: The density estimation results assume a specific trigonometric basis (Theorem 2), but the paper doesn't clarify whether the rate is basis-dependent or if the bound holds for other common bases (e.g., wavelets).

## Originality and Significance

The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates under local privacy, moving beyond previous work that established feasibility or suboptimal rates. The connection between classical randomized response and modern differential privacy is particularly original and valuable. The density estimation result is especially novel, as it reveals a different convergence rate structure than classical minimax theory.

The significance is high: the results provide a fundamental understanding of the price of privacy in statistical estimation, which is crucial for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The finding that Warner's method is optimal is particularly significant, as it suggests that optimal privacy-preserving survey mechanisms have been known for nearly 50 years.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. The results are rigorous, well-motivated, and connect historical work with modern theoretical frameworks. While the lack of computational complexity analysis and empirical validation are notable limitations, the theoretical contribution is substantial and aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical advances in machine learning.

The paper is of high quality and significance, with clear novelty that builds on but extends prior work. The theoretical results provide a foundation for future work in privacy-preserving statistics, making them valuable to both theorists and practitioners.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Briefly discuss computational complexity of the proposed estimators in high dimensions (e.g., d >> n).
2. Consider extending the analysis to α > 1 to understand the full privacy-utility trade-off.
3. Include a sentence discussing whether the density estimation lower bound (Theorem 2) holds for other orthonormal bases.
4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how these theoretical results might inform practical choices of α in real-world applications.

Overall, this is an excellent theoretical contribution that will be of interest to the NIPS community working at the intersection of privacy and statistics. The results are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the connection to historical work adds valuable perspective.
