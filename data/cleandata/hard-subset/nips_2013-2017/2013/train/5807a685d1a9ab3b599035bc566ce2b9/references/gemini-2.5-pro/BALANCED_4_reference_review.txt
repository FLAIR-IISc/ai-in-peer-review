**Summary of the Paper**

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical accuracy and privacy under the constraint of local differential privacy (LDP). The authors use the minimax framework to establish sharp (i.e., matching upper and lower bounds) convergence rates for two fundamental statistical estimation problems: estimating multinomial probabilities and nonparametric density estimation.

For the d-dimensional multinomial estimation problem, the authors show that the minimax squared error rate is on the order of d/(nα²), where n is the sample size and α is the local privacy parameter. This reveals that LDP induces an effective sample size reduction from n to approximately nα²/d. A key insight is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal in this modern LDP setting.

For nonparametric density estimation of functions in a Sobolev class with smoothness β, the paper demonstrates a more severe penalty for privacy. While the classical minimax rate is n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹), the authors prove that under α-LDP, the optimal rate degrades to (nα²)⁻²β/(²β⁺²). This is not merely a constant-factor loss but a fundamental change in the polynomial convergence rate. They present practical, computationally efficient estimators (a private histogram for β=1 and a private orthogonal series estimator for general β > 1) that achieve this new optimal rate. The work situates itself by bridging classical privacy concepts (Warner), modern cryptographic definitions (LDP), and foundational statistical theory (minimax rates), building upon prior work in differential privacy and statistics.

**Strengths**

1.  **Fundamental Contribution and Significance**: This is a landmark theoretical paper that provides the first sharp minimax characterization for fundamental estimation problems under local differential privacy. By quantifying the precise statistical cost of LDP, the paper provides a foundational understanding that is crucial for both theorists and practitioners in privacy-preserving machine learning. The results allow one to reason about the feasibility of statistical tasks given certain privacy and data constraints.

2.  **Technical Quality and Completeness**: The paper is technically very strong. The authors skillfully apply powerful tools from information theory and statistical decision theory (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) to the LDP setting. The derivation of matching upper and lower bounds ("sharp rates") represents a complete and satisfying theoretical result, leaving no ambiguity about the optimal achievable performance.

3.  **Novel and Insightful Results**: The findings are both novel and insightful. The demonstration that randomized response is minimax optimal is an elegant result that connects a 50-year-old technique to the cutting edge of privacy research. Even more striking is the result for density estimation, which shows that LDP changes the exponent of the convergence rate. This implies that for complex, nonparametric problems, the "price of privacy" is qualitatively higher than for simpler parametric ones. The authors' point that simple data perturbation schemes (like adding Laplace noise to the raw data) are suboptimal is a critical insight for designing effective private mechanisms.

4.  **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, clearly defining the LDP and minimax frameworks, and structuring the presentation of their results. The direct comparisons between the classical rates and the new private rates effectively highlight the paper's core contributions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, a few points could be clarified or strengthened to improve it further.

1.  **Dependence on External Proofs**: The main technical tools for the lower bounds are presented in Proposition 1, with the proofs deferred entirely to an external arXiv paper [9]. While common practice, the paper would be more self-contained and its claims more easily verifiable if a brief proof sketch of these key information-theoretic inequalities was included in an appendix. This would help the reader build intuition for how the privacy constraint (the likelihood ratio bound) translates into a constraint on KL-divergence and mutual information.

2.  **Practicality of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism**: The sampling strategy proposed in Section 4.3 for the orthogonal series estimator is creative but complex. It requires sampling uniformly from one of two high-dimensional half-spaces, which the authors suggest can be done via rejection sampling. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion of the computational complexity of this procedure. Is the expected number of rejection samples polynomial in the number of basis functions, k? A more detailed comment on its practicality would strengthen the "achievability" claim.

3.  **Choice of Hyperparameters**: The proposed optimal estimators for density estimation depend on a hyperparameter `k` (the number of bins or basis functions) that in turn depends on the sample size `n`, the privacy level `α`, and the unknown smoothness `β` of the underlying density. In practice, `β` is never known. It would be valuable to add a short discussion on whether standard data-driven methods for choosing such hyperparameters (e.g., Lepski's method or private cross-validation) could be adapted to this setting to make the proposed estimators fully practical.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pro):**
*   Provides a foundational, sharp characterization of the minimax risk for canonical statistical problems under local differential privacy.
*   The results are highly significant, revealing both constant-factor penalties (multinomial) and rate-changing penalties (density estimation) for privacy.
*   Connects classical and modern privacy literature in a novel and elegant way (re: randomized response).
*   Technically sound, well-written, and provides important insights for designing future private algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance (Con):**
*   The core technical lemmas for the lower bounds are not proven within the paper, relying on an external manuscript.
*   The practical implementation and computational cost of the proposed optimal mechanism for density estimation could be further elaborated.

**Decision**

This is an outstanding piece of theoretical work that addresses a fundamental question at the intersection of statistics and privacy. The strengths—significance, technical quality, and clarity—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper establishes a benchmark for understanding the inherent statistical costs of local privacy and will undoubtedly influence future research in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
