**Summary**

This paper provides a minimax analysis of two fundamental statistical estimation problems—multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation—under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors use the tools of statistical decision theory (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) to establish sharp lower bounds on the convergence rates for any estimator that satisfies α-local privacy. They then provide explicit, computationally efficient estimation procedures that achieve these lower bounds, thus characterizing the optimal trade-off between privacy and statistical utility for these problems. A key result is that for multinomial estimation, local privacy effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `nα²/d`. For density estimation over Sobolev classes `F_β`, the minimax rate degrades from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, a change in the polynomial exponent. The paper also demonstrates that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal for multinomial estimation.

**Critique**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that rigorously connects the classical framework of minimax decision theory with the modern concept of local differential privacy. The methodological approach is sound, leveraging established and powerful tools to derive its results.

**Quality and Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation. The use of minimax analysis provides a powerful, worst-case framework for understanding the fundamental costs of privacy. The application of information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) to control the divergence terms in the minimax lower bounds (Lemma 1) is elegant and technically sound.
2.  **Sharpness of Results:** A major strength is the derivation of sharp, matching upper and lower bounds. This provides a complete characterization of the minimax rates and leaves no ambiguity about the price of local privacy in these settings.
3.  **Validation of an Established Method:** The finding that Warner's randomized response [23] is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation is a significant and reassuring result. It grounds the modern, abstract framework of differential privacy in a well-understood, practical mechanism that has existed for decades. This provides strong validation for both the framework and the classical method.
4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem formulation, theoretical tools, and main results are presented clearly and logically. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work within both the classical statistics and modern privacy literature.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the asymptotic results are sharp and compelling, there are no experiments to validate the performance of the proposed estimators in a finite-sample regime. For a NIPS paper, some empirical illustration, even on synthetic data, would significantly strengthen the claims. How large do the constants in the rates tend to be? How does the performance degrade as `α` changes? Answering these questions empirically would make the work more accessible and impactful.
2.  **Practicality of the Proposed Density Estimator:** While the privatized histogram is a standard approach, the proposed orthogonal series estimator for achieving the optimal rate for `β > 1` (Section 4.3) is novel and somewhat intricate. The sampling strategy described in (19) is a non-standard mechanism specifically engineered to achieve the theoretical bound. The claim that it is "efficiently computable" is asserted but not demonstrated. This is precisely the kind of unproven approach that warrants skepticism. Without a more thorough discussion of its computational properties or a comparison to simpler (even if suboptimal) alternatives, its practical utility remains questionable. This is a key weakness; the paper proves a bound is achievable but does so with a mechanism that has not been tested or validated in practice.
3.  **Scope of Significance:** The theoretical significance is high, as the paper formalizes a fundamental trade-off. However, the practical implications are less clear. The paper correctly notes that simple Laplace perturbation is suboptimal for density estimation, which is an important negative result. But the proposed alternative is complex. A practitioner seeking to implement a private density estimator might be hesitant to adopt the mechanism in Section 4.3 without more evidence of its robustness and ease of implementation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a fundamental, rigorous, and complete minimax characterization of two core statistical problems under local privacy.
*   The connection establishing the optimality of Warner's randomized response is an elegant and important contribution that bridges classical and modern literature.
*   The technical quality is very high, and the results are likely to be foundational for future theoretical work on private statistical inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The work is purely theoretical, lacking any experiments to demonstrate how the asymptotic rates manifest in practice.
*   The novel mechanism proposed to achieve the optimal density estimation rate is complex and its practical viability is unproven. The paper endorses a new approach without sufficient evidence of its utility beyond achieving a theoretical bound.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important problem at the intersection of statistics and privacy. The methodological soundness and the sharpness of the results are commendable. The validation of Warner's randomized response is a particularly compelling finding. My primary reservations stem from the lack of empirical support and the introduction of a new, complex estimation mechanism for the density estimation problem without sufficient practical justification. While I value established methods, I am cautious about endorsing novel mechanisms that have not been rigorously tested.

Despite these reservations, the paper's theoretical merits are substantial enough to warrant publication at NIPS. The results are fundamental and advance our understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off. I would strongly encourage the authors to include an experimental section in the final version to address the concerns about finite-sample performance and the practicality of their proposed estimators.
