

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by bridging minimax decision theory with local differential privacy to establish sharp convergence rates for probability estimation under privacy constraints. The authors revisit Warner's classic randomized response framework [23] within the modern differential privacy paradigm, characterizing the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency for both multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation.

## Strengths

The paper's primary strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis that establishes sharp minimax rates for two important statistical problems under local privacy constraints. The authors demonstrate that for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, and that Warner's randomized response mechanism is optimal for this problem - a remarkable historical connection. For density estimation, they show a fundamental departure from classical rates, with the convergence rate changing from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2), which they prove is tight through matching lower and upper bounds.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with careful attention to theoretical details. The authors successfully connect classical survey methodology with modern privacy frameworks, providing valuable historical context. The proof techniques are sound, and the combination of minimax theory with privacy constraints is well-executed. The paper also makes a useful distinction between population estimation (which they study) and sample estimation (which previous work focused on), establishing stronger lower bounds.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper could better contextualize its results within the broader literature on differentially private statistical inference. The authors cite recent work on differential privacy (e.g., [9, 16, 21]), but a more detailed comparison with the most relevant recent NIPS papers on differentially private estimation (e.g., Chaudhuri and Hsu [5], Smith [23]) would strengthen the significance claim. Specifically, it would be valuable to explicitly discuss how their results improve upon or differ from previous rate analyses.

The paper's focus on specific statistical models (multinomial, density estimation) limits its immediate applicability to more general settings. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of how their framework could be extended to other statistical problems would enhance the paper's significance. The connection to practical implementation could also be strengthened - while the paper mentions that their mechanisms are efficiently computable, a brief discussion of computational complexity for high-dimensional settings would be helpful.

The paper's claim that "no estimator based on Laplace perturbation of the samples Xi themselves can attain the rate of convergence (14)" could be more thoroughly supported. The authors cite Carroll and Hall [4] for the deconvolution result, but a more direct argument showing why direct perturbation cannot achieve the rate in the local privacy setting would strengthen this important claim.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the related work section to more clearly position the paper relative to recent NIPS publications on differentially private estimation, particularly highlighting how the authors' minimax framework differs from and improves upon previous approaches.

2. Add a brief discussion of how the results might extend to more general statistical models beyond the specific cases studied (multinomial, density estimation), perhaps through a conjecture about the general form of the privacy penalty.

3. Provide a more detailed explanation for why direct perturbation methods fail to achieve the optimal rate, going beyond the deconvolution argument to show directly why Laplace perturbation cannot match the lower bound.

4. Include a brief discussion of computational considerations for high-dimensional settings, particularly for the orthogonal series estimator in Section 4.3, which may be prohibitive for large k.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a clear and important advancement in understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in privacy-preserving statistics. The results are novel, and the connection between classical survey methodology and modern privacy theory is insightful.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in contextualizing the work within the broader literature and discussing practical implications, these are easily addressable through minor revisions. The theoretical results are significant and will likely be of interest to both the privacy and statistics communities. The paper meets the high standards of NIPS in terms of theoretical contribution and technical quality.

The main concern is whether the significance claim is fully supported, as the paper focuses on specific estimation problems rather than a broad framework. However, the sharp rates they establish for these two fundamental problems provide a valuable foundation for future work in differentially private statistics. The paper would be strengthened by more explicit connections to the most recent NIPS work in this area.

**Decision**: Accept. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation, and the results are of sufficient importance to warrant publication at NIPS. The minor weaknesses identified can be addressed in revision.
