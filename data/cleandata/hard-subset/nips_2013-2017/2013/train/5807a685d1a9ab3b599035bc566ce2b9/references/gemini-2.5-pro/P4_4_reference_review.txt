**Summary of the Paper**

This paper establishes a formal connection between the framework of local differential privacy and classical minimax decision theory. The central goal is to characterize the fundamental statistical limits of estimation problems where each data point is privatized individually before being analyzed. The authors derive minimax convergence rates, providing matching lower and upper bounds on the estimation error for any procedure that guarantees `ﾎｱ`-local differential privacy.

The paper focuses on two canonical statistical problems:
1.  **Multinomial Estimation:** The authors analyze the problem of estimating a `d`-dimensional probability vector. They prove that the minimax mean-squared error scales as `d/(nﾎｱ^2)`, which can be interpreted as a reduction of the effective sample size from `n` to `nﾎｱ^2/d` due to the privacy constraint. They demonstrate that a variant of Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal, achieving this rate. This result provides a modern theoretical justification for a classical privacy technique.

2.  **Nonparametric Density Estimation:** The paper investigates the estimation of a probability density function belonging to a Sobolev class `F_ﾎｲ` (characterized by `ﾎｲ` degrees of smoothness). While the classical non-private minimax rate for this problem is `n^(-2ﾎｲ/(2ﾎｲ+1))`, the authors show that under `ﾎｱ`-local privacy, the optimal rate degrades to `(nﾎｱ^2)^(-2ﾎｲ/(2ﾎｲ+2))`. This is a significant finding, as it demonstrates that local privacy does not merely introduce a constant factor penalty but fundamentally alters the polynomial rate of convergence. To prove this rate is achievable, the authors propose and analyze efficient, optimal estimators: a private histogram for Lipschitz densities (`ﾎｲ=1`) and a more sophisticated orthogonal series estimator for general `ﾎｲ > 1`. They also show that naive mechanisms, like adding Laplace noise to the data points, are suboptimal for this problem.

The work builds upon the growing literature on differentially private statistical analysis but distinguishes itself by focusing on the local privacy model and providing sharp, population-level minimax bounds, which are stronger than bounds on sample quantities.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is of high technical quality. The main claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs that combine classical minimax tools (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) with modern information-theoretic bounds for private channels. The authors provide a complete analysis by deriving sharp, matching lower and upper bounds for the convergence rates, which is the gold standard for this type of theoretical work. The distinction between population parameter estimation and sample quantity estimation is correctly identified and adds to the strength of the lower bounds. A minor weakness is the paper's reliance on Proposition 1, which contains the key information-theoretic bounds and is cited from an arXiv preprint by the same authors. While common practice, this makes the paper not entirely self-contained, as the core technical engine for the lower bounds is stated without proof.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and clearly outlines the contributions. The problem formulation and necessary background on both minimax theory and local privacy are presented concisely. The results for the two main problems are structured logically, with each section first presenting the lower bound and then a constructive, rate-optimal estimator. The authors effectively convey the intuition behind their results, such as the interpretation of the rate change in multinomial estimation as an "effective sample size" reduction and highlighting the more severe polynomial rate degradation in the nonparametric case. The clarity of the exposition makes the sophisticated technical material accessible.

**Originality:**
The paper makes several novel and significant contributions.
1.  The primary contribution is the comprehensive application of the minimax decision-theoretic framework to the local differential privacy model. While lower bounds have been studied in privacy, this work provides a unified and rigorous characterization of the statistical cost of local privacy for fundamental estimation tasks.
2.  The result for nonparametric density estimation is particularly original and insightful. The discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial exponent in the convergence rate is a new finding that deepens our understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off.
3.  The design of the optimal mechanism for the orthogonal series estimator is a novel contribution, demonstrating that achieving optimal rates under local privacy can require non-trivial, problem-specific privatization schemes beyond simple noise addition.
4.  The formal proof that Warner's classical randomized response is minimax optimal within the modern differential privacy framework is an elegant and noteworthy result that connects historical and contemporary work on privacy.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is high for both the machine learning and statistics communities.
1.  It provides a fundamental theoretical foundation for understanding the limits of statistical inference under local privacy, a model of increasing practical importance due to its adoption in industry (e.g., by Google and Apple).

3.  The specific results offer concrete guidance for practitioners. The optimality of randomized response for survey-like questions is a clear and actionable finding. The sub-optimality of direct data perturbation for density estimation warns against naive approaches and highlights the need for more carefully designed privacy mechanisms. The paper is likely to be highly influential and to stimulate further research at the intersection of privacy, statistics, and information theory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a foundational and highly significant contribution by rigorously characterizing the minimax rates of estimation under local differential privacy.
*   The results are technically strong, providing sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for two fundamental statistical problems.
*   The findings are novel and insightful, particularly the discovery that local privacy alters the polynomial rate of convergence for nonparametric estimation.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex theoretical concepts clear and accessible.
*   The work addresses a problem of high theoretical and practical relevance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper is not fully self-contained, as the proofs for its core information-theoretic lemmas (Proposition 1) are deferred to a separate arXiv preprint. However, this is a minor issue given the overall strength and clarity of the paper's main contributions.
