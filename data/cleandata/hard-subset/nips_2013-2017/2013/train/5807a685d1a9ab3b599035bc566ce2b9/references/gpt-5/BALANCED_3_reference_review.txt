Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each data holder privatizes their own datum via an α-LDP channel before any aggregation. The authors place LDP within a minimax decision-theoretic framework, defining the α-private minimax risk and developing lower bounds by reducing estimation to testing under privatized observations. Central technical tools are information bounds that relate KL/mutual information after privatization to (squared) total variation between the underlying distributions, enabling Le Cam/Fano arguments in the LDP setting. The paper then applies this framework to two canonical problems:

- Multinomial (distribution) estimation: For estimating θ ∈ Δd from privatized categorical samples, the minimax mean-squared error scales as min{1, d/(nα^2)} (up to constants), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα^2/d when α is small. The authors show that a suitable randomized response mechanism achieves the rate, implying (a refinement of) Warner’s randomized response (1965) is optimal.

- Nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1]: Classical (non-private) rates are n^{-2β/(2β+1)}. Under LDP the minimax rate becomes (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, a genuine change in the exponent. The paper gives matching, efficiently implementable procedures: (i) privatized histograms attaining the optimal rate for β=1, and (ii) a bounded, unbiased orthogonal-series mechanism that attains the optimal rate for general β≥1. It is also shown that common Laplace-type perturbations of raw samples are suboptimal due to deconvolution limits.

The work connects to foundational DP and learning papers (Dwork et al., Hardt & Talwar, Kasiviswanathan et al., Wasserman & Zhou) and to classical minimax theory (Yang & Barron, Tsybakov). The novelty is a sharp, unified minimax analysis for LDP with matching mechanisms.

Strengths:
- Clear, compelling theoretical question: quantify the privacy-utility tradeoff under local privacy in canonical problems.
- Technically strong: the information bounds (KL and mutual information under LDP channels) are well-motivated, reusable, and lead to sharp rates.
- Sharp results with matching lower and upper bounds in both finite-dimensional (multinomial) and infinite-dimensional (Sobolev) settings.
- Mechanism design insight: simple randomized response is optimal for multinomials; for densities, the paper identifies why naive Laplace perturbation of raw data is suboptimal and proposes an alternative unbiased, bounded privatization for orthogonal series.
- Efficient procedures with attention to implementability (projection onto simplex; sampling strategies with bounded outputs).

Weaknesses and points for clarification:
- Minor inconsistencies/possible typos:
  - Theorem 1 (ℓ2-risk) lower bound includes a term sqrt(nα^2) inside a min; for a mean-squared error this term increases with n and does not fit dimensionally with the other terms. I suspect the intended form is c·min{1, d/(nα^2)} (matching the upper bound), or that the sqrt term belongs to the ℓ1 risk. Please clarify.
  - In Section 3.2, the randomized response mechanism states Z ∈ {0,1}^d but defines [Z]_j = −x_j with some probability; since x_j∈{0,1}, this yields entries −1, contradicting {0,1}^d. Please reconcile the support and notation.
- Self-containment: Several key proofs are deferred to [9]. While acceptable for space, including brief proof sketches (beyond references) for Proposition 1 and the main lower bounds would help readers assess correctness.
- Practicalities of the orthogonal-series privatization:
  - The sampling strategy (uniform over a halfspace of {−B,B}^k with a Bernoulli flip) is elegant but somewhat opaque. A brief pseudocode or complexity discussion (e.g., rejection sampling acceptance rates) would aid implementers.
  - The estimator requires knowledge of β (to set k). A comment on adaptivity (e.g., Lepski’s method or cross-validation under LDP) would strengthen practical relevance.
- Scope: The paper focuses on non-interactive LDP. It would be useful to comment explicitly on whether interactivity could improve rates in these problems, or why not, and whether the lower bounds extend to interactive channels.
- Assumptions for density estimation: The use of periodic boundary conditions via the trigonometric basis is standard but somewhat restrictive; a brief note on boundary correction or other bases with bounded sup-norm would improve clarity.

Quality:
- The results appear technically sound and are supported by clear constructions achieving the lower bounds. The information inequalities underpinning the lower bounds are plausible and valuable.

Clarity:
- Overall well-written and well-organized. Addressing the notational inconsistencies and adding short proof sketches for key steps would further improve clarity.

Originality:
- High. The paper provides the first sharp minimax characterization for classical estimation tasks under local DP, including the change in nonparametric rates, and identifies optimal privatization mechanisms.

Significance:
- High. These results set baselines for what is possible under LDP, likely to influence both theory and practice (survey design, federated analytics). The methodology (linking LDP to information-theoretic bounds for minimax analysis) is broadly applicable.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Sharp, novel, and significant theoretical contributions with matching algorithms.
- Clean conceptual message about sample complexity penalties under LDP.
- Reusable analytical framework and mechanisms.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Minor clarity/typo issues in statements and mechanism description.
- Limited discussion of adaptivity and interactivity; some proofs deferred.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Correct/clarify Theorem 1 and the randomized response mechanism’s support.
- Add brief proof sketches for Proposition 1’s bounds and at least one main lower bound.
- Provide implementation details (or a short appendix) for the orthogonal-series privatization, including expected runtime.
- Comment on adaptive selection of k and on whether interactive LDP could change rates.
- Note boundary handling alternatives beyond the periodic trigonometric basis.

Recommendation: Accept

Confidence: High (familiar with differential privacy and minimax theory; some details deferred but the core contributions are clear and compelling).
