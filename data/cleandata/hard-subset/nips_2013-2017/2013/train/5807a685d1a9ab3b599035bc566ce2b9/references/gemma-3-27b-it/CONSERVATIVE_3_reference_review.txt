This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy (via local differential privacy) and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, revisiting Warner's original motivation for privacy-preserving surveys. The authors establish minimax lower and upper bounds for multinomial and density estimation, demonstrating a predictable penalty on sample complexity due to privacy constraints. While the results are mathematically sound and build upon a solid foundation of differential privacy and minimax theory, the contributions feel incremental and lack a substantial departure from existing knowledge, particularly given the established literature on privacy and statistical estimation.

The paper is technically rigorous. The formalization of local privacy and its integration with minimax decision theory are presented clearly, and the proofs (referenced to a companion paper) are likely to be sound given the authors’ expertise. The bounds derived for multinomial estimation are particularly interesting, showing optimality of Warner’s randomized response. The density estimation results, however, are less surprising; the shift in the polynomial convergence rate is a natural consequence of applying differential privacy, and the link to existing bounds in non-private density estimation is well-articulated.  The authors correctly highlight the importance of the channel distribution *Q* and its impact on achievable rates.

However, the clarity suffers somewhat from the extensive notation and reliance on background material. While necessary, the large number of definitions (KL-divergence, total variation distance, mutual information) and references to prior work (particularly [9]) creates a steep learning curve for readers not already deeply familiar with the field. The presentation could be streamlined by providing more intuitive explanations alongside the formal definitions. While the authors acknowledge this challenge with re-stating theorems from their earlier work, a more holistic approach to making the paper self-contained would have been beneficial.

The originality of the work is moderate.  The core idea of applying minimax theory to analyze privacy-preserving estimation is not entirely new (see, for example, Duchi et al. [9], which the authors themselves cite). This paper extends those efforts, providing sharper bounds and specific results for multinomial and density estimation, but doesn’t present a fundamentally novel approach. The optimality result for Warner’s procedure is a welcome confirmation, but not a groundbreaking discovery; it's more of a theoretical justification for a well-established practice. The exploration of different noise mechanisms in density estimation is a positive step, but the conclusions about the necessity of specific strategies are somewhat limited by the focus on Laplace noise.

The significance of the work is questionable. While the results are mathematically correct, they largely confirm existing intuition about the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy. The practical implications are limited; the convergence rates are primarily of theoretical interest, and the proposed estimators (particularly the orthogonal series estimator) are likely to be computationally expensive in high dimensions. The paper doesn't offer concrete guidance for practitioners beyond confirming that privacy comes at a cost. The demonstrated optimality of randomized response is a positive result but is unlikely to dramatically alter practice.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and mathematically rigorous.
*   Clear formalization of privacy constraints within a minimax framework.
*   Confirmation of optimality for Warner’s randomized response.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution; largely confirms existing intuition.
*   Limited practical implications.
*   High level of technicality and notation may limit accessibility.
*   The connection to existing literature, while acknowledged, doesn’t feel thoroughly explored.  There's limited discussion of how these results build on or challenge prior findings in privacy-preserving machine learning.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the work is technically solid and the authors are clearly experts in the field, the contribution is not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The results are largely expected, and the paper lacks a compelling novelty or practical impact.  The paper would be more appropriate for a more specialized conference or journal focused on theoretical statistics or privacy. The authors should consider significantly expanding the scope of the work to address more challenging problems or explore more innovative privacy mechanisms before resubmitting.
