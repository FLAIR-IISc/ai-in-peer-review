### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy within the framework of minimax decision theory and local differential privacy. The authors aim to characterize the optimal convergence rates for statistical estimation when data is subjected to α-local differential privacy constraints. The work focuses on two canonical probability estimation problems: (1) estimating the parameters of a d-dimensional multinomial distribution, and (2) nonparametric density estimation for functions within a Sobolev smoothness class.

For multinomial estimation, the paper establishes that achieving α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα²/d`. This result is formalized through sharp minimax lower bounds on the squared `L2` and `L1` error. The authors complement this lower bound by showing that a modified version of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism [23] is computationally efficient and achieves this optimal rate, thereby demonstrating that this nearly 50-year-old method is optimal under the modern definition of local differential privacy.

For nonparametric density estimation of functions with β derivatives (in a Sobolev sense), the paper reveals a more severe penalty for privacy. The classical minimax rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` is shown to be unattainable. Instead, the authors derive a new, slower minimax rate of `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This result indicates that local privacy changes the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate, not just the constant factor or effective sample size. To demonstrate that this lower bound is tight, the authors propose two computationally efficient estimators that achieve this rate: a private histogram estimator for Lipschitz densities (β=1) and a more sophisticated private orthogonal series estimator for general β > 1. The paper also highlights that naive perturbation schemes, such as adding Laplace noise to the data samples, are suboptimal for this problem.

The paper's methodology connects information-theoretic tools from statistics (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) with the constraints of local differential privacy to derive the lower bounds. This work builds upon the growing literature at the intersection of differential privacy and statistical learning [e.g., 24, 6, 18] but distinguishes itself by focusing on minimax rates for estimating population parameters under the local privacy model, which provides stronger per-user guarantees than the more common central privacy model.

### Strengths

1.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The paper is technically very strong. It provides a complete decision-theoretic analysis by deriving matching lower and upper bounds on the minimax risk for the problems considered. This demonstrates a thorough and rigorous understanding of both statistical theory and privacy constraints. The use of classical information-theoretic inequalities to establish the fundamental limits of estimation under privacy is elegant and powerful.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The primary contribution is the sharp characterization of minimax rates under local differential privacy. The results are highly novel and, in the case of density estimation, non-intuitive. The discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate itself (from `2β/(2β+1)` to `2β/(2β+2)`) is a fundamental insight into the cost of privacy. Furthermore, the formal proof of the optimality of Warner's randomized response [23] within the modern differential privacy framework is an original and insightful connection between the historical origins and contemporary theory of privacy.

3.  **Significance and Impact:** The results are of high significance to both the machine learning and privacy communities. The paper provides a precise, quantitative answer to the question: "what is the statistical cost of local privacy?" for two fundamental problems. This formalizes the privacy-utility trade-off and offers clear guidance for practitioners. The finding that simple mechanisms (like direct data perturbation) can be suboptimal for certain problems is an important lesson for future mechanism design. These foundational results are likely to be widely cited and built upon in future research on private machine learning and statistics.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the relevant literature, and states its main contributions and their implications in a lucid manner. The background on the minimax framework and local privacy is concise and sufficient for understanding the main results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on External Proofs:** The paper states that proofs of all results are provided in an external arXiv paper [9]. While common for conference submissions, this prevents a self-contained verification of the technical claims. The review of the paper's technical soundness relies on trusting the correctness of the proofs in the supplementary material/external paper, which is a notable limitation.

2.  **Complexity of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The mechanism proposed in Section 4.3 to achieve the optimal rate for density estimation with smoothness β > 1 is fairly complex. While it serves its theoretical purpose of proving the attainability of the lower bound, its description is brief, and its practical implementation details (e.g., computation of the constant `ck`, efficiency of the sampling) are not fully elaborated. This makes it appear more as a theoretical construct than a simple, practical algorithm.

3.  **Focus on Small `α` Regime:** The interpretation of the results, particularly the `nα²/d` scaling for multinomials and the `(nα²)` term for densities, is most natural in the small `α` (high privacy) regime. While the formal bounds are correct for any `α` due to the `min{...}` terms, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the behavior and interpretation of the rates in the large `α` (low privacy) regime, where the privacy cost should vanish.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Presents a foundational, rigorous analysis connecting minimax theory and local differential privacy.
*   Provides sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for two fundamental estimation problems.
*   The results are novel and significant, particularly the discovery of a change in convergence rate for density estimation.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   The work advances the state of the art in understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility.

**Con:**
*   The lack of self-contained proofs is a methodological weakness, making full verification difficult.
*   The proposed mechanism for achieving the optimal rate in high-smoothness density estimation is complex and its practical aspects are not fully detailed.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a high-quality, technically deep, and significant contribution to an important and active area of research. The results are fundamental and will likely have a lasting impact on the field.
