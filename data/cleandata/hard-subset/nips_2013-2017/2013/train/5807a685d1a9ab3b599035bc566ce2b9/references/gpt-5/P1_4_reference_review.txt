Summary
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), bringing together minimax decision theory with the LDP model originally motivated by Warner’s randomized response. The authors define a private minimax risk that optimizes over estimators and α-LDP channels, and develop information-theoretic tools (KL/mutual information bounds under LDP) to derive sharp lower bounds. Two problems are analyzed:

- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd observed through α-LDP channels, the minimax MSE scales as min{1, d/(nα2)} (and analogous ℓ1 bounds), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d for small α. They give matching upper bounds via a simple mechanism, showing that (a slight variant of) Warner’s randomized response attains the optimal rate, as do Laplace-perturbed count estimators.

- Density estimation on [0,1] over Sobolev classes Fβ: Non-private rates n−2β/(2β+1) degrade under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), i.e., the exponent changes. Lower bounds follow from packing/Fano arguments combined with new information bounds under LDP. Achievability is shown by (i) privatized histograms for Lipschitz (β=1), and (ii) a locally private orthogonal series estimator using a carefully designed unbiased privatization mechanism, both achieving the lower bound. They also argue that naively adding Laplace noise to raw samples yields suboptimal rates due to deconvolution limitations.

Relation to prior work: The work connects classical minimax theory (Yang & Barron, Tsybakov) with LDP notions from databases/crypto (Dwork et al., Evfimievski et al.) and earlier statistical privacy frameworks (Wasserman & Zhou; Smith). It positions Warner’s randomized response within an optimality framework and extends private minimax analysis to nonparametric estimation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements are precise and the techniques are appropriate. The core lower bounds rely on information contraction under LDP and standard minimax reductions; upper bounds are constructive with computationally efficient estimators. The multinomial and Sobolev-density rates are sharp with matching bounds.

- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to a longer version [9]. While acceptable for space-constrained venues, brief proof sketches for the main theorems would improve self-containment.

- Correctness of mechanisms: The multinomial privatization and projection-based estimator are sound; the orthogonal series mechanism is thoughtfully designed to ensure unbiasedness and α-LDP with optimal variance scaling. The critique of Laplace perturbation of raw samples is well supported via deconvolution theory.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well organized, with clear statements of the model, risks, and results. The definitions of local privacy, private minimax risk, and the information bounds are helpful.

- Some notation could be clarified:
  - In (10), “− xj” appears to mean “1 − xj”; please rewrite explicitly to avoid confusion.
  - The variance bound for Z in Sec. 3.2 includes “+1”; since each coordinate is Bernoulli, Var sum ≤ d/4 should suffice—check constants.
  - The regimes in Theorem 1’s lower bound (e.g., min{1, sqrt(nα2), d/(nα2)}) deserve a short discussion to help readers interpret transitions across n,d,α.
  - In Sec. 4.2, explicitly justify α-LDP for Z = e_k(X) + Lap(α/2) by referencing ℓ1 sensitivity of 2.

- Reference formatting is inconsistent/incomplete; please standardize (years, venues, full titles).

Originality
- The multinomial result formalizes and sharpens long-known intuition (randomized response) by giving exact private minimax rates and optimality. The density estimation result is more novel: it shows a qualitative shift in the convergence exponent under LDP, and provides mechanisms attaining the new rates. The information inequalities tailored to LDP and the orthogonal-series privatization are technically interesting.

- The work is distinguished from global DP analyses and from earlier LDP learning results by providing sharp nonparametric rates and matching mechanisms. Related work is acknowledged, though a clearer contrast with Smith (STOC’11) and Kasiviswanathan et al. on local learning/sample complexity would strengthen positioning.

Significance
- The results are important for both theory and practice: they quantify the sample complexity penalty of local privacy and show which mechanisms are optimal. The identification that LDP changes the nonparametric rate exponent is a noteworthy insight likely to influence future work.

- The focus on non-interactive (memoryless) channels is practical, but a brief discussion of whether interactivity can change rates would be valuable.

Strengths
- Sharp, matching private minimax rates for central problems (multinomial and Sobolev density estimation).
- Clean, implementable mechanisms with clear intuition (randomized response; privatized histograms; orthogonal series).
- General information bounds under LDP that may be reusable.
- Clear articulation of the privacy–utility trade-off.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Proofs are omitted; add concise sketches for Theorems 1–2 and Propositions 2–3.
- Scope limitations: non-interactive LDP only; densities on [0,1] (1D). A discussion of extensions to higher-dimensional densities and potential dimension dependence under LDP would broaden impact.
- No empirical demonstrations. Simple simulations illustrating the rates and comparing mechanisms (randomized response vs alternatives; histogram vs Laplace-perturbed samples) would aid readers.
- Tighten and correct minor notational issues (noted above) and unify references.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contribution with sharp, novel rates and optimal mechanisms.
- Relevance to ML/privacy community; unifies foundational threads.
- Clear practical takeaways (e.g., optimality of randomized response; avoid naive sample perturbation for density estimation).

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on external long version for proofs.
- Limited discussion of interactivity and higher-dimensional extensions.
- Lack of experiments.

Recommendation: Accept (or strong weak accept). This is a high-quality theoretical paper with clear contributions and significance to NIPS.

Minor comments
- Clarify constants and α ranges in theorems; specify when rates saturate at trivial bounds.
- In Prop. 2 bound, the second term’s dependence “√(α n−3/4)” looks unusual dimensionally; please double-check constants/rates and dominance conditions.
- Briefly mention computational cost of the orthogonal-series privatization (e.g., expected number of trials in rejection sampling).
