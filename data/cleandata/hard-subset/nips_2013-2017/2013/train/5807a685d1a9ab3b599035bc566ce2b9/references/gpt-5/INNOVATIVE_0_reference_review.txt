Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), bringing classical minimax decision theory together with modern privacy constraints. The authors formalize the private minimax risk in a non-interactive (memoryless channel) model and derive sharp lower and upper bounds for two fundamental problems:
- Multinomial probability estimation over the simplex: With α-LDP, the effective sample size drops from n to nα2/d (for small α), yielding minimax risk scaling as min{1, d/(nα2)} in ℓ2 and min{1, d/√(nα2)} in ℓ1.
- Nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ: The private minimax rate changes polynomially from n−2β/(2β+1) to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), a qualitatively different regime.

The lower bounds follow from private versions of Le Cam/Fano arguments using information bounds (Proposition 1) that relate mutual information under LDP channels to total variation distances; these are taken from the authors’ prior work [9] and specialized here. Matching upper bounds are achieved by:
- A simple modification of Warner’s randomized response, shown optimal for multinomial estimation (and equivalently, the “add Laplace then project” method gives the same rate).
- For densities, private histogram estimators (β=1) and a clever locally private orthogonal-series mechanism (β≥1) that attains the (nα2)−2β/(2β+2) rate. The authors also explain why naïve additive Laplace perturbation of samples is provably suboptimal via deconvolution limits.

The work connects classic randomized response [Warner], private inference [Wasserman–Zhou; Smith], and geometric/information-theoretic views of DP [Hardt–Talwar], while providing new, sharp rates specific to the LDP setting.

Quality
The technical development is solid: the private minimax framework is clearly specified; the lower bounds are supported by appropriate information inequalities tailored to LDP; and the upper bounds are constructive and computationally plausible. The density estimation section is the most novel technically: the change in exponent and the construction of an LDP mechanism for orthogonal series that is both private and rate-optimal are important. The negative result on additive noise for density estimation is persuasive, tying to classical deconvolution impossibility. Proofs of some technical lemmas are deferred to [9]; given space constraints this is acceptable, but a self-contained supplement would strengthen reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The problem setup and minimax definitions are precise, and the statements of the main theorems are clean. A few places could be clearer:
- The orthogonal-series mechanism relies on a constant ck and a sampling scheme over {−B, B}k; practical computation and calibration of B and ck should be spelled out (even if by giving simple bounds) and accompanied by pseudocode or a short algorithmic description.
- The choice of k as a function of n, α, β is explicit; discussion of adaptivity to unknown β would help practitioners.
- Some references are incomplete in the bibliography.

Originality
High. While local privacy and randomized response are classical, the paper provides the first sharp private minimax rates for these problems in the LDP setting, including:
- A precise characterization of the “price of privacy” for multinomial estimation, and the clean optimality of randomized response.
- A qualitatively new minimax rate for Sobolev density estimation under LDP, with matching mechanisms.
- Information bounds specialized to LDP that enable these results.
This is more than an incremental extension of non-private minimax theory; it reshapes core rates under a modern constraint that matters in practice.

Significance
High. These results set baselines for what is statistically possible under LDP and will likely guide both theoreticians and practitioners designing private protocols. The demonstration that optimality may require nontrivial privatization (beyond additive noise) is impactful, and the clean rate exponents will be widely cited. The multinomial results connect directly to many modern tasks (frequency estimation, sketches), and the density results open a path to understanding nonparametric inference under LDP more broadly.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Reliance on [9] for key inequalities and some proofs: include a self-contained appendix with the information bounds or at least a proof sketch tailored to the settings here.
- Practicality of the orthogonal-series mechanism: provide algorithmic details, complexity, and guidance on setting B and estimating/approximating ck without knowing it exactly. A small synthetic experiment would go a long way to illustrate constants and stability.
- Non-interactive restriction: discuss whether interaction could improve rates (even if only to note known lower bounds or open questions).
- Parameter knowledge and adaptivity: results assume α and β are known to set k; discuss data-driven selection (e.g., private CV or Lepski-type schemes) and expected rate trade-offs.
- Scope of α: many statements assume α ∈ [0,1]; clarify behavior for larger α and whether bounds extend smoothly.
- Citations: clean up and complete references.

Reasons to accept
- Establishes sharp, conceptually clean private minimax rates under LDP for two fundamental problems.
- Reveals a qualitatively new exponent for nonparametric density estimation, with matching private estimators.
- Shows the optimality of randomized response for multinomial estimation in a rigorous minimax sense.
- Advances methodology by designing privatization mechanisms that are both locally private and statistically optimal.
- Foundational contribution likely to influence subsequent LDP theory and practice.

Reasons to reject
- Some proofs deferred to prior work; the submission is not fully self-contained.
- Limited discussion of practicality and no empirical illustrations.
- Focused on non-interactive channels; does not explore interaction or broader models.
- Parts of the lower-bound machinery are reused from [9]; novelty might be seen as concentrated in the density results.

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. This is a bold, foundational paper that quantifies the statistical price of local privacy with sharp rates and matching constructions. Even with minor omissions (proof details, experiments), the conceptual and technical advances are significant and will shape the field.
