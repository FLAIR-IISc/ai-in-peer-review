This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical accuracy and privacy. It bridges the fields of minimax statistical theory and local differential privacy to establish sharp, quantitative bounds for two fundamental probability estimation problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The main idea is to derive minimax lower bounds on the estimation error for any procedure satisfying α-local differential privacy, and then to construct explicit, computationally efficient estimators whose error rates match these lower bounds. This provides a complete characterization of the optimal convergence rates.

The work relates to a long line of research, starting with Warner's [23] work on randomized response for surveys, which this paper recasts within the modern framework of differential privacy. It builds upon the theoretical computer science literature that formalized differential privacy [11] and the statistical literature on minimax theory [26, 22]. A key distinction from much of the prior work on optimality in differential privacy is the focus on estimating underlying population parameters rather than finite-sample quantities, which yields stronger and more fundamental statistical guarantees.

The paper's two main contributions are as follows. First, for estimating a d-dimensional multinomial, it is shown that the minimax squared error scales as `d/(nα^2)`, which corresponds to an effective sample size reduction from `n` to `nα^2/d`. The paper elegantly demonstrates that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal. Second, for nonparametric estimation of a density with β derivatives, the paper reveals a more dramatic effect of privacy. While the classical minimax rate is `n^(-2β/(2β+1))`, under α-local privacy, the optimal rate degrades to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent of `n` is a significant finding, indicating a fundamental structural change in the difficulty of the problem, not just a penalty in the effective sample size. The authors provide privatized histogram and orthogonal series estimators that achieve these new optimal rates.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The approach of combining information-theoretic lower bounds (via Le Cam's and Fano's methods) with specific privacy constraints is sound and powerful. The work is very complete, providing both lower bounds that apply to *any* private algorithm and matching upper bounds achieved by concrete, constructive algorithms. This "sharp" characterization represents a strong theoretical contribution. The authors are careful to distinguish their population-based risk from the sample-based risk considered in some prior works, correctly highlighting that their lower bounds are stronger.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation and a clear summary of the contributions. The problem setting and technical background are established concisely but with sufficient detail. The main results for multinomial and density estimation are presented in separate, well-structured sections, making the paper easy to follow. The notation is standard and used consistently.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. While minimax theory and local privacy are not new concepts, their synthesis to derive sharp rates for fundamental statistical problems is a novel and important contribution. The key result—that local privacy changes the exponent in the minimax rate for density estimation—is a striking and non-obvious finding. Furthermore, the development of a non-trivial privacy mechanism for the orthogonal series estimator (Section 4.3) to achieve the optimal rate is a creative and valuable contribution, especially given the authors' argument that simpler mechanisms like direct Laplace perturbation are suboptimal. The connection drawn to Warner's 50-year-old work, showing its optimality in a modern context, is both insightful and elegant.

**Significance:** The results in this paper are of fundamental importance to the fields of private data analysis and theoretical statistics. It provides one of the first and most complete characterizations of the precise statistical cost of implementing local differential privacy. The established rates serve as a crucial benchmark for any future development of private estimation algorithms. The finding that optimal mechanisms can be more complex than simple noise addition provides critical guidance for practitioners and algorithm designers. The theoretical framework presented is likely to be influential and adaptable to analyzing the privacy-utility trade-off in a wide range of other statistical models.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of the paper is that it is not fully self-contained. The proofs of the main lower bounds rely on two key information-theoretic results (Proposition 1) that are stated without proof and are attributed to a separate, co-authored manuscript [9]. The proofs of the main theorems are also deferred to that manuscript. While page limits often necessitate such omissions, this makes it impossible for a reviewer to fully verify the technical correctness of the central claims from this submission alone. A self-contained appendix, even if brief, would have strengthened the submission.

A minor point is that the description of the sampling strategy for the orthogonal series estimator in Section 4.3 is quite dense. While technically precise, a few sentences of intuition explaining *why* this particular multi-step sampling process (involving random projections and Bernoulli trials) is necessary to achieve both privacy and optimal statistical rates would improve readability and aid the reader's understanding.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Presents a foundational and novel connection between minimax decision theory and local differential privacy.
*   Derives sharp, optimal convergence rates for two canonical statistical problems, providing a definitive answer to the statistical cost of local privacy in these settings.
*   The result for density estimation, which shows a change in the polynomial rate, is a highly significant and surprising theoretical discovery.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and positions its contributions effectively within the literature.
*   It provides new, non-trivial, and optimal private algorithms, demonstrating that the theoretical bounds are achievable.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The submission is not self-contained, as key propositions and all proofs are deferred to an external manuscript, preventing a full technical verification.
