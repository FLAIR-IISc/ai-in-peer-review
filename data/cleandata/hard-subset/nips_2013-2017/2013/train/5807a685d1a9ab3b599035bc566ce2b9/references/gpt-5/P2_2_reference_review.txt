Summary
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It formalizes the private minimax risk for population quantities and derives sharp rates for two canonical problems: (i) multinomial probability estimation over the d-simplex, and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ. Using information-theoretic lower bounds adapted to LDP channels and constructing matching algorithms, the authors show:
- For multinomial estimation under α-LDP (non-interactive), the optimal mean-squared error scales as d/(n α^2) up to constants (with matching ℓ1 rates), implying an effective sample size reduction n → n α^2 / d. A simple variant of Warner’s randomized response attains the rate.
- For density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ, the classical non-private rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} degrades under LDP to (n α^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, with matching upper bounds via privatized histograms (β=1) and privatized orthogonal series (β≥1). The paper also explains why naïve Laplace perturbation of raw samples is suboptimal for this problem (via deconvolution limits).

Relation to prior work is solid: the paper leverages differential privacy as disclosure risk (Dwork et al.) in a decision-theoretic minimax framework, extends the authors’ prior technical information bounds for LDP, connects to Warner’s randomized response, and contrasts with deconvolution lower bounds (Carroll–Hall) and private estimation baselines.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds follow from Le Cam/Fano-type reductions combined with crisp LDP-constrained information bounds; the upper bounds are constructive and computationally efficient. The matching rates for both problems strongly support the claims. The observation that direct additive noise on samples cannot reach the optimal density rates is convincing and appropriately cited.
- Completeness: The core results are presented clearly with algorithms and rate calculations. However, several proofs are deferred to a cited longer version [9]; for NeurIPS, it would strengthen self-containment to include key proofs or full details in the supplement.
- Assumptions: Focus is on non-interactive (memoryless) LDP channels and α in [0,1]. The scope is clearly stated. It would help to comment on whether interactivity can change rates for these tasks (likely not for multinomial distribution estimation, but the authors should clarify).
- Minor technical issues to fix:
  - Equation (8) includes a term proportional to sqrt(n α^2), which increases with n and seems dimensionally inconsistent for a risk lower bound; likely a typo. Please correct.
  - In Section 3.2, the randomized response mapping defines Z in {0,1}^d but uses “− x_j” in the sampling rule; this likely should be “1 − x_j.”
  - The histogram LDP guarantee (Sec. 4.2) would benefit from an explicit calibration argument for the Laplace noise choice to ensure α-LDP in the local (per-sample) sense.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, well organized, and precise about definitions, problem setup, and results. The narrative is accessible: it motivates LDP, defines the private minimax criterion, presents lower bounds, and then gives constructive mechanisms that attain them. Figures are not needed given the theoretical nature. The constants and tuning (e.g., choice of k for histograms/series) are stated explicitly. A few notational slips (above) should be corrected. It would also help to summarize in one place the exact dependence on (n, d, α, β) for all four combinations (ℓ2/ℓ1 in multinomial; β=1 and β>1 in density) for quick reference.

Originality
The contribution is original and substantial:
- It provides the first sharp private minimax rates under local DP for multinomial estimation and Sobolev density estimation, with matching achievability.
- It connects classical randomized response to optimality in modern LDP terms.
- It demonstrates a qualitative change in the exponent for nonparametric rates under LDP and designs tailored mechanisms (beyond naïve sample perturbation) to achieve them.

While the information bounds used were developed by the authors in prior work, their application here to concrete problems with tight lower/upper bounds and optimal mechanisms is new and valuable.

Significance
The results are important for privacy-preserving data analysis:
- They quantify the exact statistical price of LDP for fundamental tasks, offering clear guidance on sample complexity and mechanism design.
- They identify when simple mechanisms (randomized response, privatized histograms) are optimal and when more careful strategies are needed (orthogonal series with carefully calibrated privatization).
- The techniques and insights will likely influence subsequent work on LDP minimax theory and practical mechanism design in learning and statistics.

Pros
- Sharp, matching lower and upper bounds with clear, implementable mechanisms.
- Conceptually clean unification of minimax theory and LDP.
- Illuminating negative result about naïve additive noise for density estimation.
- Strong and appropriate connections to classical statistics and privacy literature.

Cons
- Some proofs deferred to an external longer paper; better self-containment would help.
- Minor typos/notation bugs and a potentially confusing term in the multinomial bound.
- Limited discussion of interactivity and adaptivity (unknown β) under LDP.
- Some constants (e.g., ck in Sec. 4.3) are referenced abstractly; a brief derivation or bound would aid reproducibility.

Questions/requests for clarification
- Can the authors comment on whether interactivity can improve rates for multinomial or Sobolev density estimation under LDP?
- Is there a private, adaptive selection of k (bins/series truncation) that achieves the optimal rate without knowing β?
- Please correct the issues in Eq. (8) and the randomized response sampling rule.
- Include a short proof sketch or supplement pointers for the key information bounds and for the α-LDP calibration in Sec. 4.2.

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. The paper is technically solid, original, and significant, providing definitive rates and mechanisms for foundational estimation problems under local differential privacy. Addressing the minor clarity and self-containment issues will further strengthen the contribution.
