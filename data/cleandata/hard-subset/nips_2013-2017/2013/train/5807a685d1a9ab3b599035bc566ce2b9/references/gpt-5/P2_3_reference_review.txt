Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It formalizes the private minimax risk for population estimation and derives sharp rates for two canonical problems:
- Multinomial probability estimation in d dimensions: the private minimax risk scales as d/(n α^2) (up to constants), implying an effective sample size reduction n → n α^2/d for α in a constant range. A variant of Warner’s randomized response achieves the upper bound, showing it is minimax optimal.
- Nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ: the classical n−2β/(2β+1) rate deteriorates under LDP to (n α^2)−2β/(2β+2). The paper gives two constructive upper bounds: a privatized histogram achieving the sharp rate for β=1, and a privatized orthogonal series estimator achieving the sharp rate for all β≥1 via a tailored LDP mechanism.

Lower bounds follow from a packing reduction and information-theoretic inequalities (Le Cam/Fano), together with privacy-constrained divergence/mutual information bounds from the authors’ prior work. The upper bounds are constructive and computationally efficient in the non-interactive LDP model.

This work builds on and connects: Warner’s randomized response; differential privacy foundations (Dwork et al.), local model learning (Kasiviswanathan et al.), minimax theory (Yang–Barron, Tsybakov), and recent information bounds for DP (Hardt–Talwar; the authors’ arXiv [9]). It advances beyond prior DP optimality work that targets sample quantities rather than population risk, and beyond deconvolution-based noisy releases, which cannot attain the sharp nonparametric rates under LDP.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: Theorems state clear rates with matching upper/lower bounds. Lower bounds are grounded in standard minimax testing reductions combined with LDP-specific information bounds (Proposition 1). The upper bounds are explicit mechanisms with variance/control calculations. The orthogonal-series mechanism is carefully designed to be unbiased (up to a known scaling) and α-LDP, and the choice of truncation k yields the target bias–variance tradeoff.
- Completeness: Some proofs are deferred to the longer arXiv version [9]. The main arguments (rate exponents, privacy verification, estimator construction) are sketched clearly, but a reader relying solely on this submission cannot reproduce all proofs. This is a minor weakness for a theory paper.
- Assumptions: Non-interactive, i.i.d. sampling, α in a bounded range (often α≤1), bounded basis functions for series estimators. These are reasonable and clearly stated. The paper is careful to note when constants depend on β or basis bounds.

Clarity
- The presentation is generally clear and well-structured: background on minimax and LDP, then multinomial and density sections with theorem statements and intuition.
- Notation is heavy but standard for the audience. A few places could benefit from extra clarification (e.g., the role and magnitude of constants cℓ, cu; explicit conditions under which the “effective sample size” statement holds; more detail on the sampling constant ck and efficiency of the rejection sampler).
- Citations are broad and relevant; a couple of references could be updated/adjusted (see suggestions).

Originality
- The core contributions—sharp private minimax rates for population estimation in the local model, with matching nontrivial mechanisms—are novel and nontrivial. Showing a qualitative change in the nonparametric exponent under LDP is particularly insightful.
- The result that (a suitable) randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation under LDP is conceptually satisfying and new in this precise minimax sense.
- The orthogonal-series privatization mechanism differs from Laplace perturbation and is tailored to achieve optimal rates; this is original and technically interesting.

Significance
- Establishes fundamental limits and optimal procedures in the LDP model for two canonical problems; likely to influence subsequent theoretical and algorithmic work (indeed, later literature builds on these tradeoffs).
- Clarifies the privacy–utility frontier quantitatively: multiplicative sample-size penalty in finite-dimensional problems vs. exponent degradation in nonparametrics.
- Practical relevance: supports the use of randomized response for categorical data and provides guidance for nonparametric density estimation under LDP. While the paper is theory-heavy, the mechanisms are implementable.

Strengths
- Sharp, matching lower and upper bounds with clear dependence on n, d, α, β.
- Conceptually clean unification of minimax theory and LDP via information bounds.
- Elegant demonstration that Warner’s randomized response is optimal.
- Nontrivial, rate-optimal mechanism for density estimation beyond naïve Laplace perturbation.
- Clear exposition and useful context linking classical and modern privacy notions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Proof completeness: Key technical proofs are deferred to [9]. Including at least outlines or appendices would strengthen the submission’s self-containment.
- Non-interactive focus: The paper restricts to non-interactive LDP. It would be valuable to discuss whether interaction could change rates (even by constants) for these problems, or cite known results on interaction gaps.
- Practicalities of the orthogonal-series mechanism: The efficiency and constants of the sampling scheme (dependence on k via B and the acceptance rate) are only briefly discussed; more detail on computational cost and numerical stability would help.
- Tuning under privacy: The choice of k requires knowledge of β; discussion of adaptivity and its privacy cost (e.g., private model selection) would improve practicality.
- Minor citation issue: The projection onto the simplex cites Brucker (quadratic knapsack). More standard references for simplex projection (e.g., Michelot 1986; Condat 2016; or Duchi et al. 2008 for l1-ball) might be more appropriate.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a brief appendix sketching the lower bound construction and the key steps using Proposition 1, to make the paper more self-contained.
- Discuss interaction in the local model and whether it can improve rates for these tasks.
- Elaborate on the computational aspects of the orthogonal-series mechanism (expected number of trials for rejection sampling, constant ck behavior) and provide guidance on implementation.
- Comment on adaptivity to unknown β (e.g., Lepski’s method) and how to accomplish selection privately.
- Replace or augment the simplex projection citation with a standard projection reference; also clarify constants in Theorem 1 and the α regimes.
- If space permits, a small synthetic experiment illustrating the scaling trends would broaden appeal.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, rigorous theoretical contributions with matching upper and lower bounds.
- Clear conceptual advance: quantifies the cost of LDP in two fundamental settings, including a change in nonparametric rates.
- Practical impact: validates classical randomized response as optimal; provides implementable mechanisms.
- Well written and well situated in the literature.

Cons for acceptance
- Some reliance on a separate arXiv paper for proofs reduces self-containment.
- Limited discussion of interaction, adaptivity, and empirical illustration.
- A few implementation details of the privatization mechanism could be clarified.

Overall recommendation
This is a high-quality theory paper with novel, significant results and solid methodology. Despite minor concerns about self-containment and practical details, I recommend acceptance. The work will be of broad interest to the NeurIPS theory and privacy communities and sets a clear foundation for future developments in learning under local privacy.
