Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It formalizes the private minimax risk by restricting estimators to those whose data access is through an α-LDP channel and derives sharp rates for two canonical problems: (i) multinomial (discrete distribution) estimation and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. The lower bounds are proved via information-contraction inequalities that relate divergences after privatization to total variation separation before privatization, combined with Le Cam/Fano techniques. Matching upper bounds are provided by computationally efficient mechanisms: a modified randomized response for multinomials and histogram/orthogonal-series estimators with carefully designed LDP randomization for densities.

The work builds on and connects: classical minimax theory (Le Cam, Fano; Tsybakov, Yang & Barron), survey privacy/randomized response (Warner), general/central DP (Dwork et al.), LDP/learning theory (Kasiviswanathan et al.), and an information-theoretic analysis of DP channels (the authors’ prior work). It goes beyond most DP optimality work focused on sample accuracy by targeting population risk and deriving sharp rates, and it pinpoints the cost of local privacy in both finite- and infinite-dimensional models.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Lower bounds: The KL and mutual information contraction bounds under α-LDP are plausible and appropriately used to translate packings in parameter space into testing hardness after privatization. The dependence ∝(e^α−1)^2 is consistent with small-α behavior yielding α^2. The packings for multinomial and Sobolev density classes are standard. The switch from exponent 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2) under LDP is technically nontrivial and appears correct.
- Upper bounds: The multinomial mechanism and projection estimator attain ℓ2 risk O(d/(nα^2)) and ℓ1 risk O(d/√(nα^2)), matching the lower bounds. For density estimation, the histogram scheme for β=1 and the orthogonal-series scheme for β≥1 are well designed: the series mechanism ensures unbiasedness with a bounded-output privatizer whose variance scales as B^2∝k/α^2, giving the right bias–variance tradeoff with k≈(nα^2)^{1/(2β+2)}. The argument that simple additive Laplace noise on raw data provably cannot attain the optimal rate is supported by classical deconvolution limits (Carroll–Hall).
- Completeness: Proof sketches are convincing; full proofs are deferred to the authors’ longer manuscript. The algorithms are implementable (projection onto simplex; histogram; sampling from the sign-restricted hypercube via rejection or constructive procedures).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well organized, and situates the contribution within minimax decision theory and DP. A few clarity/typo issues should be addressed:
- Eq. (10): “− xj with probability …” appears to be a typo; should be “1 − xj.”
- Theorem 1 (ℓ2 risk lower bound): the term involving √(nα^2) in the lower bound for E||θ̂−θ||_2^2 seems dimensionally inconsistent and likely a transcription error; please check the statement.
- The sampling description for the orthogonal-series mechanism refers to a constant ck=O(1) “bounded away from 0”; giving an explicit bound or citing a lemma would help reproducibility.
- For the histogram mechanism, explicitly justify α-LDP calibration (choice of Laplace(α/2) per coordinate matches ℓ1 sensitivity 2 of a one-hot vector).

Originality
- Conceptual novelty: Formalizes private minimax risk under LDP and provides sharp rates for two cornerstone problems, including the striking change in nonparametric rates under LDP.
- Methodological novelty: The mutual information and KL contraction inequalities under LDP (restated from the authors’ prior work) are powerful tools; their tailored LDP series mechanism (uniform over half-hypercube with randomized sign) is a notable design that achieves optimal rates and improves over naïve additive noise.

Significance
- Establishes definitive sample-complexity penalties for LDP: for multinomials, effective sample size degrades to nα^2/d; for Sobolev densities, the smoothness exponent loses one in the denominator. These results will inform both theory and practice of LDP.
- Shows that classical randomized response is optimal for discrete distributions, lending strong guidance for practitioners.
- The density results make clear that mechanism design matters: simple Laplace perturbation of raw data is suboptimal, while suitable privatization of feature maps achieves optimality.

Weaknesses and questions
- Non-interactivity: All results are for non-interactive (memoryless) channels. While this is the dominant architecture in practice, it would be helpful to clarify whether interactivity could improve rates or whether the lower bounds already apply more broadly. Even a brief discussion or citation would strengthen the scope claims.
- Constants and implementability: For the orthogonal mechanism, provide quantitative bounds on ck and the variance to assure the constants are reasonable in finite samples. A constructive sampler for the “uniform over {±B}^k with 〈z,τ̃〉>0” without rejection would be useful (e.g., generate sign vector with specified Hamming correlation).
- Comparisons to other LDP mechanisms for multinomials: Relate your mechanism to k-ary randomized response/Optimized Unary Encoding/Local Hashing (even if later work) only if contemporaneous; otherwise, briefly argue optimality within the class of locally private one-shot mechanisms and note whether your bounds imply optimality of generalized randomized response.
- Experimental illustration: While not necessary for acceptance, a small simulation demonstrating the change in rates and the suboptimality of additive Laplace would make the results more tangible.

Pros
- Sharp, principled minimax characterization of LDP penalties in both finite- and infinite-dimensional settings.
- Matching, efficiently implementable mechanisms; clear guidance that randomized response is optimal for multinomials.
- Elegant and broadly applicable information-contraction tools under LDP.

Cons
- A few presentation/typo issues in key theorems/mechanisms.
- Restriction to non-interactive channels is not discussed in depth.
- Full proofs are deferred to an external manuscript; include key lemmas in the supplement.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper makes substantial and rigorous contributions to the theory of local privacy in statistical estimation, with clear implications and well-designed mechanisms achieving the derived limits. Addressing the minor clarity issues and adding a brief discussion on interactivity would further improve an already strong submission.
