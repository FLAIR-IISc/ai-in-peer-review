This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the statistical cost of enforcing local differential privacy. Using the framework of minimax decision theory, the authors derive sharp convergence rates for two fundamental statistical estimation problems: estimating the parameters of a multinomial distribution and nonparametric density estimation. The main contributions are the characterization of how the minimax risk scales with the sample size n, the number of dimensions d (for the multinomial case), the smoothness of the density β, and the privacy parameter α.

For multinomial estimation, the authors show that α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to approximately nα²/d, and notably, that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is rate-optimal. For nonparametric estimation of a β-smooth density, the paper reveals a more severe penalty: the optimal convergence rate changes from the classical n⁻²β/(²β+¹) to (nα²)⁻²β/(²β+²). This change in the exponent itself is a key finding. The authors establish these results by deriving minimax lower bounds using Fano's and Le Cam's methods, complemented by information-theoretic bounds from their prior work. They demonstrate the sharpness of these bounds by constructing and analyzing explicit estimators that achieve them.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The paper is built on a solid and well-established theoretical foundation. The use of minimax theory provides a principled and powerful way to quantify the fundamental trade-offs between statistical utility and privacy. The arguments are clear and follow from classical statistical theory, which lends significant credibility to the results.

2.  **Sharpness of Results:** A major strength is that the paper provides not just lower bounds, but a complete picture with matching upper bounds. This demonstrates that the derived rates are not loose and represent the best possible performance under local privacy constraints. This level of completeness is commendable.

3.  **Significant Theoretical Insights:** The findings are of substantial theoretical interest. The result that Warner's randomized response is optimal provides an elegant link between the historical origins of privacy in statistics and the modern framework of differential privacy. More importantly, the discovery that local privacy alters the polynomial rate of convergence for density estimation is a fundamental insight. It shows that the cost of privacy in nonparametric settings can be much more than a simple reduction in effective sample size; it changes the nature of the statistical problem.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It clearly lays out the problem setting, the necessary theoretical background, and its main results. The distinction between estimating population parameters (the focus here) versus sample quantities (the focus of some prior work) is well-articulated and correctly highlights the strength of the obtained lower bounds.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality of Technical Machinery:** The core technical lemmas for deriving the lower bounds (Proposition 1) are directly imported from the authors' companion paper [9]. This suggests that the primary technical innovation in bounding information-theoretic quantities under privacy constraints was developed in that prior work. While this paper's contribution lies in the successful application of these tools to important problems, it somewhat lessens the novelty of the technical methodology presented here.

2.  **Practicality of Proposed Mechanisms:** While the estimators proposed to achieve the upper bounds are shown to be rate-optimal and computationally efficient, their practical viability is not established. The orthogonal series estimator for density estimation, in particular, involves a somewhat complex sampling scheme (Section 4.3) that seems tailored to the proof rather than for practical implementation. A discussion or comparison with simpler, more established (though theoretically suboptimal) mechanisms, such as adding Laplace noise directly to the data, would have provided valuable context. The paper convincingly argues against the optimality of direct perturbation but does not evaluate the magnitude of its sub-optimality in a practical sense.

3.  **Limited Scope:** The analysis is confined to two specific, albeit canonical, problems. The insights are powerful for these settings, but their generalizability to other statistical models of interest in machine learning, such as high-dimensional regression or structured prediction, remains an open question. The focus on the small-α regime (α ∈ [0, 1]) is also a limitation, as larger values of α are often considered in applied settings.

**Summary of evaluation:**

The paper is a high-quality, technically sound contribution to the theoretical foundations of private statistical inference. Its primary strength lies in its rigorous and sharp characterization of the minimax rates for fundamental estimation problems under local privacy constraints. The finding that privacy can fundamentally alter convergence exponents in nonparametric estimation is a significant and important result.

While the core proof techniques rely on the authors' prior work and the practical utility of the proposed optimal estimators is not fully demonstrated, these points do not detract significantly from the paper's theoretical importance. It successfully bridges the gap between classical decision theory and modern privacy research, providing clear, quantitative answers to fundamental questions about the cost of privacy. This work is likely to be influential and to stimulate further research into understanding these trade-offs in a broader range of statistical problems.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
