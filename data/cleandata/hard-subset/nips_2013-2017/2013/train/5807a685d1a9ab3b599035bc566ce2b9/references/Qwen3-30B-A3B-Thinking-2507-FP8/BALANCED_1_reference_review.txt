

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper studies the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors bridge minimax decision theory with differential privacy to characterize optimal estimation rates, focusing on two key problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The work is well-motivated, connecting Warner's classic randomized response survey technique to modern privacy theory.

## Strengths

The paper's main strengths are its theoretical rigor and clear contributions to the intersection of statistical decision theory and privacy. The authors successfully establish tight minimax lower and upper bounds for two important statistical problems under local differential privacy. For multinomial estimation, they show the effective sample size reduction from n to nα²/d, and importantly demonstrate that Warner's randomized response procedure achieves this optimal rate. This historical connection is insightful and well-justified. For density estimation, the paper reveals a fundamental difference in convergence rates under local privacy (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) compared to classical results (n^(-2β/(2β+1))), which is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper provides efficiently computable estimators that achieve these optimal rates, including histogram and orthogonal series approaches for density estimation.

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient mathematical detail for experts to follow the arguments. The authors properly contextualize their work within both the statistical decision theory and differential privacy literatures, citing relevant prior work. The connection between classical minimax theory and differential privacy is particularly compelling, and the paper makes a strong case for viewing privacy as a fundamental constraint in statistical estimation.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical contributions are strong, there are several areas for improvement. First, the paper focuses exclusively on local differential privacy (LDP), which is a specific privacy model. The authors could better articulate how their results compare to those for central differential privacy, which is more commonly used in practice. The discussion of LDP's limitations as a privacy model (e.g., the inefficiency of LDP compared to central DP for many tasks) would strengthen the paper's relevance.

Second, while the paper demonstrates that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation, it doesn't provide concrete guidance for practitioners on when to use this method versus other privacy-preserving techniques. A brief discussion of practical implementation challenges or examples would make the results more accessible to applied researchers.

Third, the paper assumes α is a constant, but doesn't explore how the rates behave as α approaches zero (which is often the practical case where high privacy is desired). A more nuanced discussion of the scaling as α varies would provide deeper insight into the privacy-utility trade-off.

Fourth, the proof of the density estimation lower bound (Theorem 2) is referenced to a longer paper, which makes it difficult for readers to verify the completeness of the argument. While referencing a longer paper is acceptable, the authors should include at least a high-level sketch of the proof technique to demonstrate its validity.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief discussion comparing the results for local privacy to those for central differential privacy, highlighting key differences in the privacy-utility trade-off.

2. Include a short practical example or case study showing how the theoretical results would translate to real-world survey design, perhaps comparing classical survey methods with privacy-preserving alternatives.

3. Extend the analysis to consider the behavior of the rates as α → 0, which is often the practical regime for privacy applications.

4. Provide a more detailed explanation of the density estimation lower bound proof or include the key steps in the main paper.

5. Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed estimators for density estimation, particularly for large β, to better inform practitioners.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a clear and significant advance in understanding the fundamental limits of statistical estimation under privacy constraints. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem at the intersection of statistics and privacy. The results on both multinomial and density estimation are novel and well-supported.

The paper meets all the criteria for NeurIPS: it is of high quality with rigorous theoretical analysis, it is clearly written, it is highly original in its approach to bridging minimax theory and differential privacy, and it makes a significant contribution to the field with clear implications for both theory and practice.

I recommend acceptance with the suggested improvements to strengthen the paper's practical relevance and completeness. The work provides a valuable framework for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation and will likely be influential in future research on privacy-preserving statistical methods.
