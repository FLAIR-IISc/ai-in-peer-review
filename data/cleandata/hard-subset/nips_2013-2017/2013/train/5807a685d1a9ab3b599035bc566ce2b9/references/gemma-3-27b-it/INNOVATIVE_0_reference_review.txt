This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem: understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in probability estimation. The authors bring together differential privacy, minimax theory, and classical statistical estimation, providing a formal framework for analyzing these trade-offs. The connection to Warner’s randomized response, demonstrating its optimality, is a particularly nice historical touch and highlights the enduring relevance of early work in this area. The theoretical results—particularly the differing convergence rates for multinomial estimation vs. density estimation under local privacy—are novel and significant.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of differential privacy with minimax analysis in this context is genuinely novel. The differing convergence rates observed for multinomial vs. density estimation are a surprising and important result.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is theoretically sound, building on established frameworks and providing clear bounds. The use of Proposition 1 (from their prior work) is efficient.
*   **Significance:** Understanding the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation is crucial as privacy concerns grow. This work provides concrete answers about the costs of privacy, which is valuable for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Clarity:** While the mathematical notation is dense (as expected for this topic), the paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and connects it to real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density Estimation - Limited Scope:** The density estimation results are presented with a focus on smoothness (β) and L2 error. It's unclear how readily these results translate to practical density estimation scenarios involving, for example, high-dimensional data or non-smooth densities. The impact of the chosen basis functions (trigonometric) isn’t fully discussed.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The practical implications of achieving the optimal rates (e.g., the cost of the sampling schemes) aren’t fully explored. Specifically, the computational cost associated with the projection onto the simplex (in the multinomial case) or sampling from the proposed distribution (density estimation) could be substantial and are not discussed.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper is heavily theoretical. While rigorous theoretical results are valued, some limited empirical validation – even on simple examples – would strengthen the claims and provide intuition for the practical impact of the findings. A small simulation study would go a long way.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
*   The identified trade-offs are fundamental and have broad implications.
*   The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response is a strong and surprising finding.
*   The authors clearly articulate the problem and position their work within the broader literature.
*   The flaws are not critical and do not invalidate the core theoretical contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The limited scope of the density estimation results and lack of empirical validation make the practical impact somewhat unclear.
*   The computational complexities of the proposed schemes are not adequately addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to an important area. While the lack of empirical validation and the limited discussion of computational complexity are weaknesses, they are outweighed by the novelty, theoretical rigor, and significance of the results. The paper introduces a valuable framework for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs and provides concrete theoretical bounds that will be useful for future research. I believe the paper’s strength lies in the novelty of the approach and theoretical depth, which are hallmarks of a good NIPS submission. 

**Recommendation:** Accept. While a more thorough discussion of computational costs and some limited empirical validation would be ideal, the paper’s core contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance. I encourage the authors to address these points in future work.
