Summary
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP) constraints using a minimax decision-theoretic framework. The setting is non-interactive (memoryless channels), where each user privatizes Xi to Zi via an α-LDP mechanism. The authors develop lower bounds on private minimax risk by coupling Le Cam/Fano reductions with information-contraction inequalities specific to LDP (KL and mutual information bounds), and they give matching upper bounds via computationally simple privatization schemes.

Two main estimation problems are treated:

- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the minimax mean-squared error under LDP scales as min{1, d/(nα2)} (and ℓ1-risk as min{1, d/√(nα2)}), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d for small α. A slight variant of Warner’s randomized response attains the optimal rate; Laplace-perturbed one-hot encodings also suffice.

- Density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1]: The classical non-private rate n−2β/(2β+1) (in L2) degrades under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2). For β=1, a privatized histogram with Laplace noise per bin and projection achieves the optimal (nα2)−1/2 rate. For β≥1, an orthogonal-series estimator with a carefully designed α-LDP mechanism that privately releases bounded basis coefficients attains the (nα2)−2β/(2β+2) rate. Notably, direct Laplace noise added to raw Xi cannot reach this rate due to deconvolution limits.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on the differential privacy literature (Dwork et al.; Hardt & Talwar; Smith; Wasserman & Zhou; Kasiviswanathan et al.) and classical minimax techniques (Tsybakov; Yang & Barron; Yu). It connects LDP to decision-theoretic lower bounds via information contraction specific to LDP (presented as Proposition 1 and originating from the authors’ longer technical report). It revisits randomized response (Warner) in a modern LDP/minimax lens, and extends prior private estimation work by providing sharp population-level risk trade-offs and matching mechanisms in both finite-dimensional (multinomial) and nonparametric (Sobolev) settings.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds use standard reduction (packing + Fano/Le Cam) combined with tight LDP-specific KL/MI contractions; the upper bounds provide explicit α-LDP mechanisms and projections with variance control. The change in nonparametric polynomial exponent is well-justified and aligns with intuition about information loss under LDP. The impossibility of attaining optimal rates via direct Laplace perturbation of Xi is supported via deconvolution theory (Carroll & Hall).
- Completeness: Many proofs are deferred to the long version; this is acceptable for NIPS if all key lemmas are stated clearly and the main steps are reproducible. The proposed mechanisms are implementable (randomized response; histogram with Laplace noise; series-based privatization with rejection sampling).
- Correctness caveats: The series mechanism includes a constant ck and requires bounded basis (B0) and selection of B=Θ(√k/α). The privacy guarantee is asserted “by inspection”; a short formal proof sketch verifying the LDP likelihood ratio bound for (19) would strengthen the paper. For the multinomial mechanism, the α/2 parameterization in coordinate-wise randomized response is plausible and looks correct.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, well-structured, and self-contained in its statements (though full proofs are in [9]). The exposition of the minimax framework and information bounds is crisp, making the lower bound technique transparent.
- Suggestions:
  - Provide a brief proof or lemma showing (19) is α-LDP and computing E[Z|X] to justify (20) in the main text.
  - Clarify the role and bounds on ck and its dependence on k.
  - Explicitly state computational complexity for the series mechanism (rejection sampling is expected O(1) since acceptance prob ≈1/2; make this explicit).
  - In Section 3.2, tidy the variance bound for Z and detail the projection step (mention Brucker’s linear-time projection algorithm more precisely).

Originality
- The combination of sharp LDP information contraction with minimax lower bounds, and the identification of exact rate changes (not just constants) under LDP for both multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, is original and impactful. The optimality of (a variant of) randomized response for multinomial estimation under population risk is a clean and useful message. The series-based private mechanism tailored to achieve optimal rates (and the negative result for naive Laplace mechanisms) is novel and insightful.

Significance
- The results provide a clear, quantitative privacy-utility trade-off under LDP that researchers can use to design private algorithms with near-optimal accuracy. The shift in nonparametric exponents under LDP is a fundamental insight likely to influence subsequent work in locally private learning and estimation. The mechanisms are simple enough to be deployable.

Pros
- Sharp, matching minimax lower/upper bounds under local DP for two canonical problems.
- Conceptually clean message: LDP reduces effective sample size (multinomial) and changes the nonparametric exponent (Sobolev).
- Practical, simple mechanisms attaining the bounds.
- Solid connections to information theory and classical statistics.

Cons / weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on the long version for proofs; some mechanisms (series privatization) would benefit from a fuller privacy proof in the main text.
- Restriction to non-interactive mechanisms; discussion of whether interaction could improve rates (or not) for these tasks would add value.
- No empirical illustrations; while not necessary, small simulations could illuminate constants and finite-sample behavior.
- Adaptivity to unknown smoothness β is mentioned but not addressed under privacy (model selection under LDP may require care).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you comment on whether interaction helps for multinomial or Sobolev density estimation under LDP in terms of rates? If known to be unnecessary, please cite.
- For the orthogonal series mechanism, please provide a concise, explicit proof that (19) is α-LDP and quantify ck. Is ck uniformly bounded away from 0 for all k?
- Could your techniques extend to other loss metrics (e.g., L1 or Hellinger for density), and do exponents change similarly?
- For multinomial estimation, can you discuss communication-efficient variants (e.g., one-bit or k-ary reports) that still attain optimal rates?
- Briefly discuss adaptivity to unknown β with privacy-preserving model selection or Lepski-type methods.

Recommendation
Strong accept. This is a high-quality theoretical contribution with precise, sharp results, clear practical mechanisms, and strong relevance to the NIPS community working on privacy, learning theory, and statistics.
