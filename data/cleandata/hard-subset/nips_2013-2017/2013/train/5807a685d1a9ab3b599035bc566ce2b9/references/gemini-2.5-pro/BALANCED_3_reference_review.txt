### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical accuracy and privacy under the constraint of α-local differential privacy. It connects the modern framework of differential privacy with classical minimax statistical theory to derive sharp convergence rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation in `d` dimensions, the authors show that local privacy induces a penalty that effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `nα²/d`. They demonstrate that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal in this setting. For nonparametric density estimation of functions in a Sobolev class with smoothness `β`, the paper reveals a more severe penalty: the optimal convergence rate degrades from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, a change in the polynomial exponent of `n`. The authors complement these lower bounds with computationally efficient, achievable upper bounds using private histogram and orthogonal series estimators, demonstrating the sharpness of their results.

This work builds upon a rich history. It modernizes Warner's original motivation for randomized response by placing it within the formal framework of local differential privacy, a concept more recently explored in the database and theory communities (e.g., Evfimievski et al.). It distinguishes itself from much of the differential privacy literature (e.g., Dwork et al., Chaudhuri et al.) by focusing on the stringent model of *local* privacy and by analyzing the minimax risk for estimating *population* parameters rather than finite-sample quantities, a more classical and often harder statistical problem. The technical approach leverages standard tools from minimax theory (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) but adapts them by using novel information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1, drawn from the authors' companion work [9]) that precisely quantify the information loss due to the local privacy constraint.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental question at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy: what is the inherent statistical cost of local privacy? By providing sharp, quantitative answers for canonical problems, the work provides a foundational understanding of this trade-off. The results are likely to be influential for both researchers developing new private algorithms and practitioners who need to understand the costs of deploying them.

2.  **Novelty and Insight:** The connection between minimax theory and local differential privacy is powerful and yields deep insights. The result for multinomial estimation, which formally proves the optimality of a mechanism closely related to Warner's 50-year-old randomized response, is elegant. Even more striking is the finding for density estimation, where privacy doesn't just reduce the effective sample size but fundamentally alters the difficulty of the problem by changing the convergence rate's exponent. This is a novel and important discovery. The analysis also insightfully shows that naive privacy mechanisms, like adding Laplace noise directly to data points, are suboptimal for density estimation, highlighting the need for carefully designed protocols.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The authors establish both minimax lower bounds (what is impossible) and matching upper bounds via constructive, efficient algorithms (what is possible). This "sharp" analysis is the gold standard in statistical theory and leaves little room for ambiguity about the optimal rates. The theoretical framework is sound, and the claims are well-supported.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides clear background on both local privacy and the minimax framework, making the work accessible to a broad NIPS audience. The main theorems are stated precisely, and the implications are discussed lucidly.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Heavy Reliance on an External Preprint:** The key technical lemmas used for the lower bounds (Proposition 1) are stated without proof and cited from the authors' own arXiv preprint [9]. Similarly, the proofs for the main theorems are also deferred to [9]. While this is understandable for a conference submission, it makes the paper less self-contained and renders a full verification of the technical core difficult. Including proof sketches for Proposition 1 in an appendix would significantly strengthen the paper's completeness and allow reviewers to better assess the foundational technical contributions.

2.  **Practicality of the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The proposed sampling strategy (19) for the optimal orthogonal series estimator is clever but complex. The description involves uniform sampling from one of two halves of a `k`-dimensional hypercube. The paper suggests this is "efficiently computable (for example by rejection sampling)," but provides no further details. Rejection sampling could be very inefficient as the dimension `k` grows (which it does with `n`). A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of this sampling step, or a description of a more direct sampling algorithm, would be valuable for assessing the "practicality" part of the claim that the upper bound is achieved by a practical scheme.

3.  **Limited Discussion of the Privacy Parameter `α`:** The analysis and rate expressions primarily focus on the regime where `α` is a small constant (e.g., `α ∈ [0, 1]`). While this high-privacy regime is important, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the behavior for large `α`. How do the rates smoothly transition to the classical non-private rates as `α → ∞`? This would provide a more complete picture of the privacy-utility trade-off across the full spectrum of `α`.

### Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance

*   **Pro:**
    *   Addresses a fundamental and timely problem.
    *   Provides novel, insightful, and sharp results that significantly advance our understanding of local privacy.
    *   Technically strong and well-executed.
    *   Clearly written and accessible.
    *   Bridges the fields of privacy, theoretical computer science, and statistical decision theory in a meaningful way.

*   **Con:**
    *   The core technical proofs for the lower bounds are located in an external manuscript, making the paper not fully self-contained.
    *   The practical implementation and complexity of the proposed optimal mechanism for density estimation could be elaborated upon more thoroughly.

### Final Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and foundational contribution. It provides a rigorous and insightful characterization of the statistical limits of learning under local differential privacy. The discovery that privacy can alter the polynomial convergence rate for nonparametric problems is a particularly important finding. While the reliance on an external paper for key proofs is a minor drawback, the clarity of the results, the strength of the claims, and the overall importance of the topic make this a clear accept. The work is of high quality and is certain to be of great interest to the NIPS community.

**Decision:** Accept
