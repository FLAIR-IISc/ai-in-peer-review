Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It formulates a decision-theoretic, minimax framework for private estimation: the statistician observes privatized samples Zi generated by an α-LDP channel Q(·|Xi), and aims to estimate a population parameter θ(P). Building on information-contraction bounds developed by the authors (restated here), the paper derives sharp minimax lower bounds and matching upper bounds for two canonical problems:

- Multinomial (discrete probability) estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the minimax mean-squared error scales as min{1, d/(nα2)} (up to constants), and the ℓ1 error as min{1, d/√(nα2)}. Thus, for small constant α, LDP reduces the effective sample size from n to nα2/d. The classical randomized response mechanism (Warner, 1965), with a modern tuning, achieves these rates; simple Laplace perturbation on one-hot encodings also attains the rate.

- Density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1]: The nonprivate minimax rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2). The paper shows Laplace perturbation of raw samples is provably suboptimal (via deconvolution limits). It then gives two optimal, efficiently implementable mechanisms: (i) a privatized histogram achieving the β=1 rate, and (ii) a locally private orthogonal series estimator using a carefully designed unbiased, α-LDP vector privatization that achieves the lower bound for all β≥1.

This work connects the privacy literature (differential privacy, especially local privacy as in Evfimievski et al., Kasiviswanathan et al.) with minimax lower bound techniques (Le Cam/Fano) and information inequalities. It differs from global DP utility analyses (e.g., Dwork et al.; Hardt & Talwar; Smith; Wasserman & Zhou) by focusing on local privacy constraints and on population, not sample, estimation error, and it provides sharp rates with matching mechanisms.

Strengths
- Sharp theory with matching upper and lower bounds. The multinomial and Sobolev density results are clean and interpretable, with optimal dependence on n, α, d, β.
- Conceptual contribution: quantifies the price of LDP in classical estimation problems, revealing a new polynomial rate for nonparametric density estimation.
- Mechanism design: identifies that naive Laplace perturbation of samples is suboptimal for density estimation; proposes tailored, unbiased, α-LDP randomization schemes that are computationally efficient and rate-optimal.
- Clear positioning relative to prior work: distinguishes population vs sample estimation, local vs global DP; connects to deconvolution limits to explain suboptimality of direct perturbation.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Proof availability: Many proofs (especially for the density lower bound and the orthogonal-series mechanism) are deferred to the long version [9]. While typical for NeurIPS theory, including more detailed proof sketches (key packing construction, how the variational mutual information bound is optimized, and the LDP verification for the vector mechanism) would improve self-containment.
- Practical guidance: The histogram and orthogonal-series procedures depend on B0 and tuning k≈(nα2)1/(2β+2). Discussion of data-driven selection of k under LDP (e.g., cross-validation with privatized scores) and robustness to unknown smoothness β would broaden applicability.
- Mechanism details: The sampling strategy (19) uses rejection or constrained sampling over {−B,B}k with a sign constraint. Clarify its computational complexity and constants (ck), and provide a simple sampling algorithm description and expected running time.
- Interactivity: The paper restricts to non-interactive (memoryless) channels. A brief discussion on whether interaction could improve rates under LDP for these tasks would be valuable (even negative results or intuition).
- Constants and regimes: Bounds are given up to universal constants for α∈[0,1]. It would help to clarify regimes where min terms in (8) are active and to comment on behavior for larger α (e.g., α→∞ recovering nonprivate rates).
- Related work completeness: The paper cites Kasiviswanathan et al. on local privacy in learning. A more explicit comparison of sample complexity phenomena there vs here (estimation vs learning) would help readers.

Quality
- Technically sound and careful. The information-contraction bounds are appropriate for LDP; lower bounds use standard tools adapted to private channels. Upper bounds are constructive, efficient, and match the lower bounds.
- Claims are well-supported, though important proofs are deferred. The mechanisms are specified clearly enough to implement.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Definitions, problem setup, and results are stated precisely. Minor notation issues: in Definition 1, the constraint should be ∑j j2β θ2j ≤ C2 (looks like a typo using ϕ instead of θ). In Section 3.2, the variance bound of Z could be tightened/explained (Bernoulli variance ≤1/4 per coordinate suggests ≤d/4).
- Adding a high-level intuition for the orthogonal-series privatization (why the hypercube mechanism preserves coefficients under LDP) would aid readability.

Originality
- High. The identification of sharp LDP minimax rates for multinomial and Sobolev density estimation with matching mechanisms is, to my knowledge, new. The local orthogonal-series mechanism is a novel and insightful design.

Significance
- High. The results provide a principled, quantitative understanding of the privacy-utility tradeoff under LDP in foundational estimation problems, with implications for survey design and private analytics. The mechanisms are likely to influence subsequent work in locally private statistics.

Arguments for acceptance
- Sharp, novel theoretical contributions with matching constructions.
- Clear advance over prior DP analyses by focusing on local privacy and population minimax risk.
- Elegant unification of information-theoretic lower bounds with privacy constraints; actionable mechanisms.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on the long version for proofs; limited empirical or practical evaluation.
- Some implementation details (sampling complexity, parameter selection) are under-explained.
- Scope constrained to non-interactive LDP; broader discussion of interaction could strengthen impact.

Minor comments
- Typos: In Def. 1, replace ϕ by θ in the coefficient constraint. Check equation formatting around (10) probabilities (“− xj with probability ...” likely means “1−xj”).
- Cite deconvolution rate result more precisely (assumptions on noise CF tails).
- Suggest adding a short appendix with proof sketches for Theorems 1–2.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper offers significant, technically strong, and well-presented advances in the theory of locally private estimation with clear implications and well-chosen example problems.
