## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a strong theoretical contribution bridging differential privacy, minimax estimation, and classical statistical inference. It provides sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy, demonstrating a clear trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency. The results are well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly presented. While the focus is quite theoretical, the implications are significant, particularly for applications where privacy is paramount (e.g., surveys, medical data analysis). I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper revisits Warner’s original problem of probability estimation with privacy considerations, but within a rigorous framework leveraging differential privacy and minimax theory. It establishes minimax lower and upper bounds for both multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation under α-local differential privacy. A key contribution is quantifying the penalty (in terms of sample size or convergence rate) imposed by privacy guarantees.  The work builds on the differential privacy framework of Dwork et al. [11] and the statistical decision theory tools of Tsybakov [26] and others. It importantly connects these fields, moving beyond simply achieving privacy to *quantifying* the cost of privacy in a statistically optimal way. The authors correctly position their work relative to prior studies on differential privacy focusing on sample quantity estimation, emphasizing that their lower bounds on population estimation are stronger. They also correctly highlight the optimality of Warner’s randomized response procedure, a surprisingly enduring result.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical analysis is meticulous and the proofs (referenced to [9], which I assume is of comparable quality) appear solid. The use of minimax theory provides a strong foundation for establishing optimality results.
* **Sharp Bounds:**  The derived minimax rates are sharp, meaning the upper and lower bounds match (or are very close).  This is a significant achievement, demonstrating a deep understanding of the underlying trade-offs.
* **Clear Motivation and Connection to Classical Work:** The paper effectively connects modern differential privacy to the historical motivations of Warner’s work, providing valuable context.
* **Practical Implications:** While theoretical, the results have direct implications for designing privacy-preserving statistical estimators.  The analysis of Warner’s randomized response and the proposed histogram estimator provide concrete guidance.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and logically structured. The notation is consistent, and the introduction provides a good overview of the problem and contributions.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper is heavily theoretical. While the achievability results are presented,  a more detailed empirical evaluation of the proposed estimators (particularly for density estimation) could strengthen the paper. Simulating the performance of the estimators under different privacy levels (α) and comparing them to non-private benchmarks would be valuable.
* **Complexity of Density Estimation:** The density estimation section, while theoretically sound, feels a bit less polished than the multinomial estimation section. The discussion around the choice of basis functions and the role of the parameter *B* feels somewhat dense and could be clarified with a more intuitive explanation.
* **Limited Discussion of Real-World Constraints:** The analysis assumes a relatively idealized setting. Discussing the practical challenges of implementing these techniques in real-world datasets (e.g., high dimensionality, non-i.i.d. data) would be beneficial.
* **Dependence on [9]:**  The core technical results rely on a separate paper ([9]). While this is not unusual, it makes a complete assessment of the work difficult without access to that paper. It would be helpful to include more detailed summaries of key lemmas and proofs directly within the main manuscript.



**4. Specific Comments and Suggestions:**

* **Page 3, Line 2:** “likelihood ratio also controls the error rate in tests…” - Could expand on this slightly, providing a brief example or citation.
* **Page 5, Theorem 1:** Clarify the meaning of the “min” function. Is it a minimum over α, or over d and n?
* **Page 8:** While mentioning the perturbation approach of Dwork et al., a short discussion of why it’s *not* optimal in this setting would be helpful.
* **Page 10 onwards (Density Estimation):**  Provide more intuition about why the classical rate changes to the new rate under privacy constraints. What specific aspects of the privacy mechanism are responsible for the different scaling?
* **Proposition 3:** Specify which orthonormal basis is being used when referring to the parameter *B*.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.
* The results are sharp and rigorously proven.
* The work is well-motivated and connects to classical problems in statistical inference.
* The paper addresses a timely and important topic.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of extensive empirical evaluation.
* The density estimation section is slightly less polished than the multinomial estimation section.
*  The reliance on a separate paper ([9]) for core technical details.


**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I encourage the authors to:

*  Consider adding some simulation results to validate the performance of their proposed estimators.
*  Provide more intuitive explanations for the density estimation results, particularly regarding the impact of privacy on the convergence rate.
*  Expand on key proofs and lemmas from [9] within the main manuscript to improve self-containedness.



This is a valuable contribution to the field, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
