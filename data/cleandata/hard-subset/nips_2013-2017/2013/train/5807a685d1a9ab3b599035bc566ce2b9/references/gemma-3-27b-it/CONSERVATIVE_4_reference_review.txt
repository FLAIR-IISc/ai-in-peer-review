This paper addresses the fundamental problem of probability estimation under local differential privacy, revisiting Warner’s original motivation and providing formal theoretical guarantees. The authors connect concepts from statistical decision theory, specifically minimax rates, with the privacy framework of differential privacy. They derive minimax rates for multinomial estimation and density estimation, demonstrating a quantifiable trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The work is technically sound and builds upon established results in minimax theory and differential privacy (citing key papers in both fields, such as Dwork et al., Chaudhuri et al., and Tsybakov). The proofs appear rigorous, relying on well-known techniques like Le Cam’s method and Fano’s inequality. The derivations of the minimax rates are clear and logically presented. The analysis of the randomized response mechanism for multinomial estimation is particularly strong, showing its optimality even under privacy constraints, a noteworthy connection to the early work of Warner.

However, while the technical aspects are solid, the overall contribution feels incremental. The core idea – applying minimax theory to locally private estimation – is natural and, while valuable, doesn’t represent a paradigm shift. The results, while mathematically precise, largely quantify what one might intuitively expect: adding privacy constraints degrades performance. The impact of the differing polynomial rate for density estimation (from n<sup>-2β/(2β+1)</sup> to (nα<sup>2</sup>)<sup>-2β/(2β+2)</sup>) is more significant, but even this feels like a refinement of existing knowledge rather than a breakthrough. 

A key weakness is the limited exploration of the practical implications of the derived rates. The authors mention implementing efficiently implementable algorithms (histogram and orthogonal series estimation), but the discussion remains high-level. More detailed analysis of the constant factors, computational complexity, and robustness to real-world data variations would strengthen the paper. The discussion of prior work on differentially private estimation (e.g., [11, 16, 8]) feels somewhat cursory; a more thorough comparison with existing approaches is needed.  Furthermore, the paper relies heavily on established methods. While rigor is appreciated, the lack of truly novel algorithmic ideas beyond the application of known techniques is a drawback.  The authors rightly point out that existing perturbation methods are sub-optimal for density estimation, but do not explore alternative non-perturbation methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and mathematically rigorous.
*   Connects important areas of research (statistical decision theory and differential privacy).
*   Provides sharp bounds on the convergence rates of estimation procedures under privacy constraints.
*   Demonstrates optimality of the classical randomized response mechanism.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution; doesn't present a fundamentally new approach.
*   Limited exploration of practical implications and algorithmic details.
*   Relatively narrow scope, focused primarily on theoretical analysis.
*   Lack of significant novelty beyond applying known tools to a well-defined problem.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. It is a solid and technically correct work, but lacks the originality and impact to stand out as a top-tier NIPS paper. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of practical considerations and a clearer articulation of its significance in the broader context of privacy-preserving data analysis. The connection to the randomized response mechanism is a strength, but the overall presentation feels somewhat conservative. I believe the paper's strengths are sufficient to justify acceptance, but it is not a particularly strong contribution.
