Summary
This paper develops a principled minimax framework for statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It brings together information-theoretic lower-bounding techniques (Le Cam/Fano) with LDP channels to quantify fundamental privacy–utility trade-offs. The core technical contributions are:
- General information bounds: KL/mutual information contractions under α-LDP channels, enabling lower bounds via Le Cam/Fano in the private setting.
- Multinomial estimation: Sharp minimax rates under α-LDP in ℓ2 and ℓ1, showing an effective sample size reduction of n → nα2/d for small α, with matching upper bounds. A minor variant of Warner’s randomized response is shown to be minimax optimal and computationally simple (projection onto the simplex).
- Density estimation over Sobolev classes: A striking change in statistical difficulty under LDP. The non-private rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), i.e., a new polynomial exponent. Matching upper bounds are achieved: for β=1 by privatized histograms, and for general β via a locally private orthogonal series estimator that requires a non-trivial privatization mechanism (not simple additive Laplace noise), with k ≍ (nα2)1/(2β+2).

The work situates itself relative to classical density estimation (Tsybakov, Efromovich), deconvolution limits (Carroll–Hall), and the DP literature (Dwork et al., Kasiviswanathan et al., Wasserman–Zhou, Smith). It also leverages and restates information bounds from the authors’ prior work [9], using them to derive concrete sharp rates for two canonical estimation problems.

Quality
- The theory appears technically sound and clean. The lower bounds follow from carefully controlled information contractions under LDP; the upper bounds are constructive, efficient, and match the lower bounds.
- The multinomial section is especially crisp, including a clear mechanism and finite-sample risk calculation with projection.
- The density section is compelling: it explains why naive additive-noise mechanisms cannot be optimal (deconvolution barriers) and introduces a carefully designed local privatization scheme that preserves the right moments.
- However, many proofs are deferred to [9]. For a NeurIPS paper, the main ideas of the proofs (beyond statements) should be sketched more fully here, especially for the density achievability mechanism and constants in the unbiasedness calibration. The paper would benefit from at least high-level proof sketches for the key steps.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized with clear motivation and problem setup. The minimax objective under LDP is precisely defined.
- Some notation/typos need fixing:
  - In the Sobolev class definition, the constraint should be sum_j j^{2β} θ_j^2 ≤ C^2, not involving ϕj.
  - In randomized response, [Z]_j equals x_j with some probability and “1−x_j” otherwise; the paper currently writes “−x_j”, which is inconsistent with Z ∈ {0,1}^d.
  - Specify the dependence and bounds of ck in equation (20).
- A brief intuitive explanation for why the orthogonal-series privatization (19) achieves both α-LDP and the right bias/variance trade-off would improve accessibility. A small figure or pseudocode would help.
- References are incomplete in places (missing years/pages).

Originality
- Strong. The central conceptual contribution is identifying and proving sharp minimax rates under local privacy with matching mechanisms, including the novel exponent change for nonparametric density estimation and the demonstration that naive perturbation is suboptimal. The orthogonal-series privatization mechanism is nontrivial and tailored to LDP constraints.
- The paper builds on the authors’ prior information bounds [9], but the concrete application to multinomial and Sobolev density estimation with tight upper/lower bounds and explicit mechanisms is substantial and, to my knowledge, new in the LDP setting (as opposed to global DP). The connection to classical deconvolution limitations underscores the novelty.

Significance
- High. The results provide a foundational understanding of how local privacy changes the statistical difficulty of core problems—precisely the kind of guidance both theoreticians and practitioners need. The identification of randomized response as minimax-optimal for multinomials is impactful given its practical simplicity. The nonparametric results (new exponent) are likely to influence future work on private estimation, mechanism design, and optimality theory under LDP.

Pros
- Conceptually bold synthesis of minimax theory and LDP.
- Sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for two canonical problems.
- Practical, efficient mechanisms; clear message about randomized response optimality.
- Clear separation between what can/cannot be achieved with simple additive noise.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on the arXiv technical report [9] for proofs; limited proof sketches in the submission.
- Minor clarity/typo issues (noted above).
- No empirical illustrations; while not essential, small simulations could enhance intuition.
- Scope restricted to non-interactive LDP and α ∈ [0,1]; some discussion of interactive channels and larger α would be valuable.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide brief proof sketches for the density achievability mechanism and clarify constants (ck, B) and their dependence on k and α.
- Fix typos in the Sobolev definition and randomized response.
- Discuss whether interaction could improve rates in these problems, or argue why not.
- Add a short comparison to Smith (STOC’11) and Wasserman–Zhou in terms of local vs global privacy and population vs sample estimation goals.

Recommendation: Accept
This is a significant and novel contribution that advances our theoretical understanding of learning under local privacy, with sharp results and practically meaningful mechanisms. The paper is strong enough to merit acceptance; the minor clarity issues and reliance on the companion report can be addressed in revision.

Confidence: High
