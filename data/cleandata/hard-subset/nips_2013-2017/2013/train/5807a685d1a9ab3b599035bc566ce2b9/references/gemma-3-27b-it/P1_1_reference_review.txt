## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in probability estimation. Building on the framework of differential privacy, specifically local privacy, the authors derive minimax lower and upper bounds for estimating multinomial probabilities and nonparametric densities. Their key contribution lies in rigorously quantifying the penalty incurred due to privacy constraints. They demonstrate that for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size is reduced by a factor related to the privacy parameter α, and for density estimation, the convergence rate changes in a non-trivial way compared to the non-private setting. Crucially, they provide computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these optimal bounds, connecting theoretical results to practical applicability. The work firmly grounds classical statistical decision theory within a modern privacy context, revisiting Warner’s early work on randomized response and showing its optimality.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper bridges several research areas. It connects Warner's (1965) pioneering work on randomized response with the more recent advancements in differential privacy from cryptography and theoretical computer science (Dwork et al., 2006). It builds upon the authors’ prior work (Duchi et al., 2013) on statistical inference under differential privacy, refining and extending those results to provide sharper bounds. The work is also closely related to classical minimax theory (e.g., Tsybakov, 2009) and nonparametric density estimation (e.g., Efromovich, 1996), showcasing how privacy constraints alter fundamental limits. The paper also acknowledges and builds on recent work on differentially private estimation (e.g., Chaudhuri et al., 2012).

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a strong theoretical analysis based on minimax optimality and differential privacy, deriving tight bounds on estimation rates.
* **Novel Contribution:** The work provides a formal treatment of the privacy-utility tradeoff in probability estimation, a crucial aspect often overlooked in privacy-preserving data analysis.
* **Sharp Results:** The identified scaling factors (e.g., nα²/d for multinomial estimation) are precise and provide valuable insights into the cost of privacy.
* **Practical Algorithms:** The authors not only derive theoretical bounds but also demonstrate how to achieve them with computationally feasible algorithms (randomized response and orthogonal series estimation).
* **Connections to Classical Work:** The revisiting and confirming of Warner’s randomized response optimality is a significant and insightful observation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical framework and key results. The notation is standard and well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** While the density estimation results are theoretically strong, the focus is somewhat limited to smoothness classes defined by bounded derivatives. Exploring other density classes or alternative smoothness measures could broaden the applicability.
* **Computational Complexity:**  While the authors claim efficiency, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms (especially the orthogonal series estimator) would be beneficial.  The k-selection process is mentioned but not fully detailed.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of a known sample space *X* and channel distributions *Qi* may be restrictive in some applications. Discussing potential extensions to unknown or more complex scenarios would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** Although theoretical results are central, a small-scale empirical demonstration illustrating the performance of the proposed algorithms and the impact of the privacy parameter *α* could further strengthen the paper.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental and timely problem in privacy-preserving data analysis.
* The theoretical results are rigorous, novel, and provide valuable insights into the privacy-utility trade-off.
* The practical algorithms demonstrate the achievability of the theoretical bounds.
* The connection to Warner’s work and the reaffirmation of its optimality are compelling.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* The scope of the density estimation results could be broader.
* A more detailed computational analysis and some empirical results would increase confidence in the practical relevance of the algorithms.
* The assumptions, while reasonable, could be acknowledged as limitations.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theory of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The theoretical results are rigorous and insightful, and the practical algorithms demonstrate the achievability of the optimal rates. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is well-suited for NIPS and recommend **acceptance**.



**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)



**Confidence:** 9/10
