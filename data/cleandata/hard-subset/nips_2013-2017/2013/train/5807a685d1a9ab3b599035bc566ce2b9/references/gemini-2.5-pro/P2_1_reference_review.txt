### Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

**Summary**

This paper establishes a rigorous connection between the information-theoretic framework of local differential privacy (LDP) and the statistical framework of minimax decision theory. The authors aim to characterize the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical utility by deriving sharp minimax rates for two canonical probability estimation problems: d-dimensional multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation for β-smooth functions.

For multinomial estimation, the paper demonstrates that imposing α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `n * α^2 / d`. A key finding is that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal, achieving this lower bound.

For nonparametric density estimation, the paper reveals a more severe penalty. The classical minimax rate of `n^(-2β / (2β+1))` is degraded to `(n * α^2)^(-2β / (2β+2))`. This is not just a change in the constant or the effective sample size, but a change in the polynomial exponent of convergence. The authors show that this optimal rate is achievable via a privatized histogram estimator (for β=1) and a more sophisticated privatized orthogonal series estimator for general β > 1. They importantly note that simpler, more intuitive privacy mechanisms, such as adding Laplace noise directly to the data points, are provably suboptimal.

The core technical machinery relies on information-theoretic bounds (KL-divergence and mutual information) under LDP, which the authors developed in a previous work and apply here to derive the minimax lower bounds using standard techniques like Fano's and Le Cam's methods.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance:** This is a highly significant and foundational paper. It provides one of the first and most complete characterizations of the statistical cost of *local* differential privacy. By using the minimax framework, it moves beyond the analysis of specific algorithms to establish fundamental limits on *any* possible algorithm, which is a powerful contribution. The results provide a clear, quantifiable answer to the question: "What is the price of local privacy?" This is of great interest to both the machine learning theory community and practitioners designing private data collection systems.

2.  **Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is very high. The authors provide sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for the convergence rates in both problems considered. This "sharpness" is the gold standard in statistical theory and demonstrates a complete understanding of the problem. The analysis is rigorous and builds upon established theoretical tools in a novel context.

3.  **Originality and Insight:** The paper is highly original in its synthesis of modern privacy concepts (LDP) with classical statistical decision theory. While others have studied privacy and utility, this work's focus on minimax optimality under the local model is a key contribution. The results yield important insights:
    *   The rediscovery of Warner's randomized response as an optimal mechanism is an elegant and impactful finding, connecting the very origins of the field to its modern formulation.
    *   The demonstration that the convergence rate *exponent* changes for density estimation is a crucial and perhaps surprising result. It shows that the cost of privacy can be more fundamental than a mere reduction in sample size.
    *   The analysis revealing the sub-optimality of direct data perturbation (e.g., adding Laplace noise) for density estimation is an important practical lesson, guiding the design of better privacy mechanisms.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized for a dense theoretical work. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates it within the literature. The contributions are stated upfront. The progression from the general framework to the specific applications (multinomial and density estimation) is logical and easy to follow. The comparison to non-private rates at each step effectively highlights the impact of the privacy constraints.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

1.  **Reliance on Prior Work for Core Tools:** The key technical lemmas used to bound the information-theoretic quantities (Proposition 1) are directly imported from the authors' own prior work [9]. While this is perfectly acceptable, it does mean that the primary technical innovation in *this* paper is the application of those tools, rather than the development of new foundational proof techniques. The program committee should see this work as a significant and novel application paper that builds on the authors' established theoretical framework.

2.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The paper defers the proofs of its main theorems to the longer version [9]. While standard for conference submissions due to page limits, it means a reviewer cannot fully verify the technical correctness of the lower bounds from this manuscript alone.

3.  **Theoretical Focus:** The work is entirely theoretical. While the implications are practical, the paper does not include any experiments to validate the constants in the bounds or to explore the performance of the proposed estimators in finite-sample regimes. The proposed mechanism for the orthogonal series estimator is quite complex, and a practical exploration of its implementation would have strengthened the paper, though this is not strictly necessary for a strong theory paper at NIPS.

**Overall Evaluation**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that addresses a fundamental and important problem at the intersection of privacy and machine learning. It provides sharp, insightful, and significant results on the ultimate limits of statistical estimation under local privacy constraints. The strengths—in particular, the significance of the problem, the high technical quality of the results, and the clarity of the exposition—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper makes a lasting contribution to our understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off and is a perfect fit for NIPS. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a theoretical foundation upon which much future work can be built. I strongly recommend acceptance.
