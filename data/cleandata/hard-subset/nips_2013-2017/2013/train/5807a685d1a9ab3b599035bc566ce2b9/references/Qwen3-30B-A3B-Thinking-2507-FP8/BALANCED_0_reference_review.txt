

# Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper makes a significant contribution by bridging minimax decision theory with local differential privacy to establish sharp convergence rates for probability estimation under privacy constraints. The authors analyze two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, they show that the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy, proving Warner's randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For density estimation, they demonstrate a fundamental shift in convergence rates from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2), and provide efficient estimators (histogram and orthogonal series) that match these lower bounds.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Quality:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with both sharp lower and upper bounds. The proofs are well-structured, building on established minimax techniques while making novel contributions to the privacy-statistics interface. The authors carefully handle the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, addressing both theoretical and practical implications.

2. **Originality:** The paper makes a clear and important contribution by formally connecting Warner's classical survey methodology with modern differential privacy through minimax analysis. The density estimation result revealing a different polynomial exponent (2β/(2β+2) vs 2β/(2β+1)) represents a novel theoretical insight that hasn't appeared in previous NIPS literature.

3. **Significance:** The results provide fundamental understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility, with direct practical implications. The demonstration that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response technique is optimal for multinomial estimation under local privacy is both surprising and practically valuable for survey design.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear notation and logical flow. The connection between theoretical results and practical implications (e.g., explaining why Warner's mechanism works) is particularly effective.

**Weaknesses:**
1. While the paper establishes sharp rates, it could provide more intuition about *why* the density estimation rate changes from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2) under local privacy. A more detailed discussion of this conceptual shift would strengthen the paper.

2. The paper focuses exclusively on α-local differential privacy but doesn't discuss how the results might extend to other privacy definitions (e.g., ε-differential privacy for the entire dataset) or how the bounds scale with varying privacy budgets. This limits the broader applicability of the findings.

3. The computational complexity of the orthogonal series estimator (Proposition 3) could be more thoroughly discussed. While the authors note it's "efficiently computable," the exact complexity relative to non-private estimators would be valuable for practitioners.

4. The paper could better position itself against recent NIPS work on private statistical estimation (e.g., Chaudhuri & Hsu, NIPS 2013; Smith, NIPS 2011). While they cite relevant work, a more detailed comparison would clarify the paper's unique contribution.

**Constructive Suggestions:**
1. Expand the discussion in Section 4.1 to provide more intuition about why the privacy constraint changes the exponent in the density estimation rate. A brief comparison to the standard deconvolution problem could help.

2. Add a short discussion (1-2 paragraphs) on how the results might extend to other privacy definitions or how the bounds scale with varying α (e.g., for small α vs large α).

3. Include a brief computational complexity analysis for the orthogonal series estimator, particularly regarding the cost of the sampling strategy (19) compared to standard non-private estimators.

4. Add a sentence or two in the introduction comparing this work to recent NIPS papers on private estimation, highlighting the specific novelty of the minimax approach.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. The results are technically sound, the analysis is rigorous, and the findings have clear significance for both theory and practice. While there are minor opportunities for improvement in the discussion of the density estimation rate change and computational aspects, these do not undermine the paper's core contributions. The work represents a substantial advance in the theoretical understanding of privacy-preserving statistical estimation, making it highly suitable for publication at NIPS. The paper is well-positioned to become a foundational reference in the growing area of privacy-preserving statistical inference.

**Recommendation:** Accept
