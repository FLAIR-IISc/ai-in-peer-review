### Summary

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and privacy under the constraint of α-local differential privacy. It connects the classical statistical framework of minimax estimation with the modern computer science notion of differential privacy. The authors focus on two fundamental probability estimation problems: d-dimensional multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes.

For multinomial estimation, the paper establishes sharp minimax rates, demonstrating that local privacy effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `n * α² / d`. A key finding is that a variant of Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal, a striking result that connects modern privacy theory back to its earliest roots.

For nonparametric density estimation of a function with β derivatives, the paper reveals a more severe penalty. The classical minimax rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` is shown to degrade to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent is a significant finding, indicating that privacy fundamentally alters the difficulty of the problem, rather than just imposing a penalty on the effective sample size. The authors provide matching upper bounds by constructing explicit, computationally efficient estimators (a privatized histogram for β=1 and a novel privatized orthogonal series estimator for general β) that achieve these optimal rates. This latter construction is particularly notable, as the authors show that simpler privacy mechanisms, like adding Laplace noise to data points, are provably suboptimal.

The technical approach relies on information-theoretic lower bounding techniques (Fano's inequality, Le Cam's method) coupled with novel bounds on the KL divergence and mutual information between distributions under local privacy constraints, which are leveraged from the authors' companion work.

### Quality

The paper is of very high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The work is complete, providing not only lower bounds on the minimax risk but also constructing explicit, efficient estimators that achieve these bounds, making the results "sharp." This completeness is a major strength. The authors are careful to delineate their contributions and place them within the context of both classical statistics and recent work on differential privacy. They correctly point out that their lower bounds on *population* parameter estimation are stronger than much of the prior computer science literature, which often focuses on estimating *sample* quantities. The argument that simple noise-addition mechanisms fail for density estimation is insightful and well-argued. The reliance on a companion paper [9] for the full proofs of the main information-theoretic lemmas is reasonable for a conference submission, though it makes the paper slightly less self-contained.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly stating the problem setting, the definition of local differential privacy, and the main contributions.
*   The background section on the minimax framework is concise and provides the necessary context for readers less familiar with statistical decision theory.
*   The results are presented logically, tackling multinomial estimation first, followed by the more complex density estimation problem. Within each section, the structure of presenting the lower bound first, followed by an analysis of an optimal mechanism, is highly effective.
*   The notation is standard and clearly defined. The high-level implications of the mathematical results are explained in clear prose, making the paper accessible to a broad audience at NIPS.

### Originality

The work is highly original and represents a significant conceptual advance. While others have studied differential privacy from a statistical perspective (e.g., Wasserman & Zhou [24], Smith [21]), this paper is a pioneering work in applying the full machinery of minimax decision theory to the *local* privacy model.

Key points of originality include:
1.  **Bridging Fields:** The formal bridge between minimax theory and local differential privacy is a major contribution in itself.
2.  **Sharp, Novel Rates:** The derivation of the precise minimax rates is new. The discovery that the convergence rate *exponent* changes for density estimation is a fundamental and surprising insight.
3.  **Optimal Mechanisms:** The paper goes beyond just stating limits by designing novel mechanisms (especially the orthogonal series estimator) that are provably optimal. Showing that this mechanism is necessary because simpler ones fail is a strong and original result.
4.  **Historical Connection:** The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal nearly 50 years after its invention is a beautiful and insightful connection.

The work is clearly distinguished from prior work, which either focused on different (non-local) privacy models or did not establish sharp minimax optimality. All relevant work appears to be adequately cited.

### Significance

The significance of this paper is substantial and multifaceted.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** It provides a foundational understanding of the ultimate statistical limits imposed by local differential privacy. These results are fundamental and likely to be cited for years to come as the benchmark against which new private algorithms are measured.
*   **Guidance for Algorithm Design:** The paper offers concrete guidance for practitioners. For surveys, it provides strong theoretical backing for using randomized response. For more complex problems like density estimation, it warns against naive approaches (like direct data perturbation) and provides a template for a more sophisticated, optimal mechanism.
*   **Broad Impact:** The ideas and analytical tools presented are generalizable and will likely inspire similar minimax analyses for a wide range of other statistical problems under privacy constraints (e.g., regression, classification, etc.). It lays the groundwork for a much deeper, more principled study of private machine learning.

The results are important because they quantify a fundamental trade-off that is becoming increasingly critical as data collection becomes more pervasive. Researchers and practitioners in both machine learning and privacy will build upon these ideas.

### Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Establishes a fundamental and principled connection between statistical decision theory and local differential privacy.
*   Provides sharp, non-asymptotic minimax rates for two core statistical problems, quantifying the price of privacy.
*   The discovery of a change in the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a major theoretical insight.
*   The work is technically deep, yet the paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   It provides constructive, computationally efficient algorithms that are proven to be optimal, making the results highly impactful.
*   The work is highly original and significant, with the potential to influence a large body of future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The work is purely theoretical, without experimental validation. However, for a paper of this nature establishing fundamental limits, this is not a significant weakness.
*   The full proofs of key lemmas are deferred to a companion paper, making the submission not entirely self-contained. This is a minor point and common practice.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that makes a foundational contribution. Its combination of technical depth, clarity, originality, and significance makes it an ideal candidate for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
