## NIPS Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a strong theoretical contribution linking differential privacy with minimax estimation theory. The results are technically sound, the writing is generally clear, and the work is original and significant. The paper rigorously analyzes the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in both multinomial and density estimation problems, providing sharp bounds and demonstrating optimality of existing techniques like Warner's randomized response. While the mathematical formalism is dense, it's necessary for the rigor of the results. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the fundamental problem of statistical estimation under privacy constraints, specifically utilizing the framework of *local* differential privacy. Building on Warner’s early work on randomized response, the authors provide a formal, minimax-optimal characterization of the privacy-utility trade-off. They bridge the gap between classical statistical decision theory and the recent surge in differential privacy research (Dwork et al., 2006; Chaudhuri et al., 2009). The authors correctly position their work relative to existing privacy literature, noting that previous work often focuses on disclosure risk rather than formal decision-theoretic trade-offs. The connection to minimax theory is well-established (Tsybakov, 2009), and the paper leverages this framework effectively. The authors rightly point out the optimality of Warner's approach – a surprising result given the recent attention on privacy.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The core strength lies in the rigorous mathematical treatment. The derivation of minimax bounds, both lower and upper, is well-justified and uses established techniques from minimax theory and differential privacy.
* **Sharp Bounds:** The authors obtain *sharp* convergence rates, meaning the upper and lower bounds match (up to constant factors). This is a significant accomplishment, demonstrating a deep understanding of the inherent limits of privacy-preserving estimation.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The analysis is not purely theoretical. The paper demonstrates the optimality of the randomized response mechanism and provides insights into practical considerations for implementing privacy-preserving statistical procedures.  The density estimation results also offer guidance on efficient estimation schemes in the presence of privacy constraints.
* **Novel Contribution:**  The combination of local differential privacy, minimax theory, and the characterization of the trade-offs in multinomial and density estimation is a novel and significant contribution.
* **Clear Structure and Notation:** Despite the complexity of the material, the paper is logically organized and the notation is generally consistent, making it relatively easy to follow the arguments.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Density:** The paper is heavily laden with mathematical formalism. While necessary for the rigor, it could make the paper inaccessible to a broader audience, potentially limiting its impact. A more intuitive explanation of the key results could be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Challenges:** While the authors establish theoretical optimality, they don't delve deeply into the practical challenges of implementing these techniques in real-world scenarios.  For example, choosing the appropriate level of privacy (α) in practice is a complex problem that warrants further discussion.
* **Density Estimation Section Feels Rushed:**  Compared to the detailed analysis of multinomial estimation, the density estimation section feels somewhat less fleshed out.  The connection between the derived rate and practical implications for bandwidth selection in kernel density estimation (or other smoothing techniques) could be explored further.
* **Minor Notation Quibbles:** The use of both "≍" and "O" notation can be slightly confusing.  Consistent use of one or the other would improve clarity.

**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Page 2, Line 1:** The motivation presented – respondents' reluctance to disclose information – feels somewhat dated in the context of modern privacy concerns.  Acknowledging more contemporary motivations (e.g., data breaches, surveillance) could strengthen the introduction.
* **Page 3, Equation (1):**  The definition of α-local differential privacy is standard, but a brief intuitive explanation of its implications would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the concept.
* **Page 6, Theorem 1:**  The statement that the effective sample size decreases to nα²/d is a key result.  It would be helpful to provide a more intuitive explanation of why this is the case.
* **Page 8, Randomized Response Discussion:**  It’s great that the authors highlight the optimality of Warner’s approach. Expanding on its limitations (e.g., potential for reduced accuracy if p is not sufficiently large) could provide a more balanced assessment.
* **Page 9-12, Density Estimation:** The discussion of the trade-off between privacy and convergence rate in density estimation is important. Expanding on the implications of this rate for specific density estimation methods (e.g., kernel density estimation) would be valuable.

**5. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous analysis.
* Sharp minimax bounds demonstrating optimal performance.
* Novel and significant contribution to the field.
* Clear structure and well-defined notation.
* Demonstrates the optimality of existing privacy-preserving techniques.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Mathematical density might limit accessibility.
* Limited discussion of practical challenges.
* Density estimation section could be more thoroughly developed.

**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

*   Provide more intuitive explanations of the key results, particularly regarding the impact of α on convergence rates.
*   Expand the discussion of practical challenges in implementing these techniques.
*   Further elaborate on the implications of the density estimation results for common estimation methods.
*   Ensure consistent use of notation (e.g., using only "≍" or "O").

Addressing these minor points will further enhance the clarity and impact of this already excellent paper.
