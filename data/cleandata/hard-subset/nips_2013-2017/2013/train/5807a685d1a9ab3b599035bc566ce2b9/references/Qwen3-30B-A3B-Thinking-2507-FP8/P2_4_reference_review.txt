

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

## Summary

This paper bridges minimax decision theory with differential privacy to provide sharp rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors revisit Warner's randomized response technique (1965) within a modern privacy framework, establishing fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. They provide minimax lower bounds and matching upper bounds for two key problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, they show the effective sample size degrades from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy. For density estimation of β-smooth densities, they establish a new rate of (α²n)^(-2β/(2β+2)) compared to the classical n^(-2β/(2β+1)) rate. The paper demonstrates that Warner's randomized response achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation and provides efficient algorithms for both problems.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates exceptional technical quality. The authors establish rigorous minimax lower bounds using information-theoretic tools (KL divergence, mutual information) and complement them with matching upper bounds using computationally efficient algorithms. The proofs are well-structured and leverage appropriate theoretical machinery from both statistical decision theory and privacy literature. The paper properly distinguishes between private and non-private minimax rates, with careful attention to the nuances of local privacy versus central privacy. The demonstration that Warner's 50-year-old technique is optimal provides a satisfying theoretical resolution to a long-standing problem.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting two important areas—minimax estimation and differential privacy—that have largely been studied separately. The paper establishes novel minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, with the density estimation result representing a fundamental departure from classical rates. The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal is particularly original and insightful, revealing that optimal privacy-preserving strategies for basic survey problems have been known for decades.

**Significance:** The results provide a precise quantitative understanding of the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical utility, which is highly relevant to the growing field of privacy-preserving machine learning. The rates established provide concrete guidelines for how much sample size must increase to compensate for privacy constraints. The paper establishes a framework that can be applied to other statistical problems beyond those considered, potentially influencing future work on privacy-preserving statistical inference.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent context, clearly articulating the connection between Warner's original work and modern differential privacy. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The main results are stated precisely with appropriate mathematical formalism, and the paper is structured to build logically from foundational concepts to specific results. The proofs are appropriately referenced to a longer paper, which is standard for conference submissions.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong theoretically, there are a few areas for improvement. First, the paper is purely theoretical without experimental validation. While this is acceptable for a theoretical contribution, a brief simulation demonstrating the practical performance of the proposed estimators would strengthen the paper's impact. Second, the paper focuses exclusively on local differential privacy without discussing how the results might extend to other privacy models (e.g., central differential privacy or other variants), which would provide broader context for the results. Third, while the paper references Duchi et al. [9] for some technical results, a brief sketch of the key proof ideas for the main theorems would make the paper more self-contained and accessible to readers who may not have access to the longer paper.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistical estimation. The results are novel, well-supported by rigorous mathematics, and address a fundamental question in privacy-preserving statistics. The connection between Warner's randomized response and modern minimax theory is particularly insightful. While the absence of experimental results is a minor limitation for a conference paper, the theoretical depth and significance of the work more than compensate for this.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria well: it is technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), makes a significant theoretical contribution (originality), and provides fundamental insights into the privacy-utility trade-off (significance). The results advance the state of the art in privacy-preserving statistical inference and provide a framework that can be applied to other problems.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper is a significant contribution to the field and would be of interest to a wide audience at NIPS, including researchers working on privacy, statistical theory, and machine learning. The theoretical results are elegant, the proofs are rigorous, and the practical implications are clear. The paper successfully demonstrates that fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility can be precisely characterized using minimax decision theory, which is a major contribution to the field.
