

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper studies minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, providing sharp lower and upper bounds for multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors successfully bridge Warner's randomized response framework with modern differential privacy theory, offering quantitative trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper makes a theoretically significant contribution by characterizing the "effective sample size" reduction under privacy constraints, with the interesting finding that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response procedure is optimal for multinomial estimation.

## Technical Strengths
The paper's main strength lies in its rigorous minimax analysis of privacy-constrained estimation. The authors establish novel lower bounds that capture the fundamental trade-offs between privacy level α and estimation accuracy, which are tight with their proposed estimators. The density estimation results are particularly noteworthy, showing a different convergence rate (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) compared to the classical n^(-2β/(2β+1)) rate, demonstrating that privacy constraints fundamentally alter statistical behavior.

## Technical Weaknesses
Despite the significant theoretical contribution, the paper contains several critical technical errors that undermine its validity:

1. **Dimensional inconsistency in Theorem 1**: The lower bound for ℓ₂ error contains "√(nα²)" which would grow with n, whereas error rates must decrease with sample size. The correct expression should be "d/(nα²)" (as seen in the upper bound), not "√(nα²)".

2. **Incorrect randomized response probabilities**: In equation (10), the probability of "−xj" should be "exp(α/2)/(1+exp(α/2))" to sum to 1 with the truth probability, but the paper incorrectly writes it as "1/(1+exp(α/2))".

3. **Misstated variance bound**: The claim "E[‖Z - E[Z]‖²₂] < d/4+1 < d" is incorrect. For Bernoulli variables, the variance of each coordinate is p(1-p) ≤ 1/4, so the expected squared norm should be ≤ d/4, not d/4+1.

4. **Ambiguous estimator formulation**: In Proposition 3, the estimator "f̂ := (1/n)∑_{i=1}^n ∑_{j=1}^k Zi,jϕj" is ambiguous. It should be "f̂ := ∑_{j=1}^k (1/n)∑_{i=1}^n Zi,j ϕj" to clearly separate the summation order.

5. **Incomplete reference**: The Warner [23] reference is missing the publication year in the bibliography.

## Presentation and Clarity Issues
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, but contains several notational inconsistencies:

1. The notation Φ ◦ ρ in equation (2) is unconventional; it should be Φ(ρ(·)).

2. Equation (5b) lacks parentheses around "I(...) + log 2", which could lead to misinterpretation.

3. The paper claims "privacy reduces the exponent from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2)" but 2β/(2β+2) = β/(β+1), which is mathematically correct but could be simplified for clarity.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a theoretically important contribution to the intersection of differential privacy and statistical estimation. However, the technical errors in the core results (particularly in Theorem 1 and the randomized response mechanism) are substantial and cannot be easily corrected. These errors affect the paper's main claims about minimax rates and the optimality of the proposed mechanisms. The paper's technical soundness is compromised by these issues, which are not minor typographical errors but fundamental mistakes in the mathematical development.

While the paper's main ideas are significant and the theoretical framework is sound, the presence of these technical errors prevents the results from being fully trusted. The reviewers must be able to verify the mathematical arguments, and these errors make that verification impossible without significant additional work.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

The paper's main contribution is compelling, but the technical errors in the core results are too severe to allow acceptance. The errors affect the fundamental claims about minimax rates and the optimality of the proposed mechanisms. The authors should address these technical issues before the paper can be considered for publication.
