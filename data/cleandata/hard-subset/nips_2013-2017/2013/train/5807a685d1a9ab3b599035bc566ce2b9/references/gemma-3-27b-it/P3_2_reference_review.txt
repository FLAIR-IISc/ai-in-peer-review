## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy (via local differential privacy) and statistical efficiency in probability estimation. Building on the work of Warner and the recent surge in differential privacy research, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical framework leveraging minimax decision theory to characterize optimal rates of convergence for estimating multinomial distributions and density functions under local privacy constraints. They demonstrate that local privacy induces a penalty on sample size (for multinomial estimation) or a change in the convergence rate (for density estimation), and crucially, show that these bounds are achievable with efficiently implementable algorithms. A particularly noteworthy result is the optimality of Warner's randomized response mechanism, even after decades of research in privacy-preserving data analysis.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper effectively bridges several areas. It connects the classical problem of privacy in surveys (Warner, 1965) with the modern formalization of privacy using differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006).  It further integrates this with the well-established minimax theory from statistical decision theory (e.g., Tsybakov, 2009).  The authors rightly position their work as stronger than existing differential privacy literature by focusing on population estimation rates rather than sample estimation rates.  The references are comprehensive and well-chosen, acknowledging key contributions from cryptography, statistics, and machine learning. The work builds on the authors' prior work (Duchi et al., 2013) establishing divergence bounds under differential privacy.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a solid theoretical framework, providing both lower and upper bounds on minimax rates. The proofs, while deferred to a longer paper, seem well-motivated based on the described methodology.
* **Sharpness of Results:** Establishing matching upper and lower bounds is a significant achievement, demonstrating the optimality of the proposed mechanisms.  The finding that Warner’s randomized response is optimal is particularly compelling.
* **Novelty & Significance:** The combination of local differential privacy with minimax estimation is a novel contribution.  The identified trade-offs are fundamental and have implications for a wide range of privacy-preserving statistical applications. The differing convergence rates for density estimation compared to the classical rate is a key finding.
* **Practical Relevance:** The proposed algorithms are computationally efficient, making the results directly applicable to real-world problems. The use of histograms and orthogonal series estimators are practical and readily implementable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, presenting the concepts in a logical flow.  The notation is standard and the introduction effectively motivates the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density Estimation Specificity:** The density estimation section focuses on smoothness measured by Sobolev spaces. While mathematically convenient, this might limit the immediate applicability of the results to other density estimation problems or other smoothness measures. Discussing the implications for other smoothness classes would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Complexity of Optimal Mechanisms:** While the algorithms are stated to be efficiently implementable, a more detailed analysis of their computational complexity – particularly for the orthogonal series estimator – would be helpful.  How does the complexity scale with *n* and *α*?
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is highly theoretical.  While strong theoretical results are valuable, some limited empirical validation (even with simulated data) to demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed mechanisms and confirm the theoretical convergence rates would be beneficial.
* **Minor Notation Issue:** The notation "an . bn" for "an = O(bn)" is unconventional and could be replaced with standard asymptotic notation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* The results are sharp, establishing fundamental trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.
* The work is well-motivated, connects to a broad range of literature, and has practical implications.
* The identified optimality of Warner’s method is a particularly strong result.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation somewhat limits the immediate impact of the theoretical results.
* The focus on Sobolev spaces in the density estimation section might narrow the applicability of the findings.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to NIPS and should be accepted.  The theoretical results are rigorous, the bounds are sharp, and the implications are significant. While the lack of empirical validation is a minor drawback, the strength of the theoretical framework outweighs this concern.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above (clarifying computational complexity and discussing implications for other smoothness classes).



**Score:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
