This paper provides a detailed study of the estimation of probability distributions under local privacy constraints, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. The authors bring together the formal treatment of disclosure risk provided by differential privacy with the tools of minimax decision theory to provide a theoretical treatment of probability estimation under privacy constraints.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the study of local privacy and its connection to the original work of Warner on randomized response. The authors also provide a thorough review of the relevant literature, including the work on differential privacy and statistical decision theory.

The technical contributions of the paper are significant, particularly in the derivation of sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in locally private settings. The authors provide lower bounds on the convergence rates of any estimator, which are complemented by matching upper bounds achievable using computationally efficient algorithms. The results are applied to the problems of multinomial estimation and density estimation, providing new insights into the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to characterize the limitations of learning from data perturbed to guarantee privacy. The authors provide a clear exposition of the local privacy model and its connection to differential privacy, which is largely contained in their previous work [10]. The new bounds for density estimation are a significant contribution, and the authors provide a thorough comparison to related work, including the M-estimation density estimator of Lei.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. The commentary in some places could be more precise, and the connection to existing differential privacy results and literature could be more clearly explained. The restriction to local privacy makes the model different from differential privacy, and the paper sometimes overstates or blurs that distinction. For example, the statement at the bottom of page 4 could be clarified to avoid confusion.

Additionally, the relevance of the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is unclear, particularly in experimental regimes where alpha is approximately 1 under differential privacy. The authors could provide more commentary on the choices of rho (utility measures) to show how different utilities fit the analysis.

In terms of related work, the authors could provide more comparison to other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods to show how they perform relative to randomized response. A comparison to related work, such as Lei's M-estimation density estimator, would also be useful for context.

Finally, the authors could provide a clearer explanation of the distinction between population and sample statistics, particularly in the context of local privacy. This clarification would be helpful for readers who may not be familiar with the technical details of the paper.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of statistical estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors provide a clear and thorough exposition of the technical results, and the paper is well-written and well-organized. With some minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, the paper would be even stronger.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a significant contribution to the field of statistical estimation under local privacy constraints.
* The authors provide a clear and thorough exposition of the technical results.
* The paper is well-written and well-organized.
* The results have important implications for the field of statistical estimation and machine learning.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from more precise commentary in some places.
* The connection to existing differential privacy results and literature could be more clearly explained.
* The restriction to local privacy makes the model different from differential privacy, and the paper sometimes overstates or blurs that distinction.
* The relevance of the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is unclear.
* The paper could benefit from more comparison to other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods and related work.

Recommendation:

I recommend acceptance of the paper, pending minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, and the authors provide a clear and thorough exposition of the technical results. With some revisions to address the weaknesses, the paper would be even stronger.
