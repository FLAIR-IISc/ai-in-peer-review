This paper provides a detailed theoretical study of probability estimation under local privacy constraints, deriving sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems. The authors leverage tools from differential privacy and minimax decision theory to characterize the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. A notable consequence of their results is the confirmation that Warner’s classical randomized response mechanism is optimal for survey sampling with privacy guarantees.

The paper is well-motivated, beginning with the historical context of privacy in surveys (Warner, 1965) and connecting it to the modern framework of differential privacy. The authors clearly articulate the setting, defining local privacy and its relationship to disclosure risk. They successfully bridge classical statistical decision theory with privacy, providing a rigorous analysis of the resulting trade-offs. The work builds upon their prior work (Duchi et al., 2013) by extending the results to density estimation. 

A key strength of the paper is the sharpness of the derived minimax rates. The authors not only provide lower bounds but also matching upper bounds, demonstrating the achievability of these rates. This is particularly impressive for the density estimation problem, where they identify a departure from standard minimax rates due to the privacy constraints. The demonstration of optimality for Warner’s randomized response is also a strong result, highlighting the enduring relevance of this classic technique. 

However, there are areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the authors acknowledge the connection to differential privacy, the paper often blurs the distinction between local and global differential privacy. The restriction to *local* privacy is a significant modeling choice, and the implications of this choice (and its relevance to scenarios where alpha ≈ 1 under global differential privacy) could be discussed more thoroughly. The paper occasionally suffers from imprecise language which could be clarified to benefit a broader audience. For example, some statements (like the one cited on p.4) could benefit from more careful phrasing.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work. While the authors cite relevant literature on differential privacy, the connections to other estimation techniques under privacy constraints are not fully explored. In particular, Lei (2017) proposes differentially private M-estimators, and a comparison to this work, especially regarding density estimation, would be valuable. A discussion of how existing histogram or discrete-data estimation methods perform relative to randomized response in the locally private setting would also strengthen the analysis. Further, exploring different choices of the utility function ρ and how those impact the analysis would be illuminating, demonstrating the flexibility of the framework. 

The authors acknowledge that a central conceptual point – the distinction between population and sample statistics – requires greater clarity. While the mathematical formalism is sound, this distinction is crucial for understanding the implications of the results and should be highlighted earlier in the paper. After rebuttal, clarifying this and incorporating a comparison to Lei’s work would significantly improve the contextualization of the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a fundamental problem: statistical estimation under privacy constraints.
*   The derived minimax rates are sharp, offering valuable insights into the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.
*   The confirmation of optimality for Warner’s randomized response is a significant and practical result.
*   The work bridges concepts from differential privacy and minimax decision theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper sometimes blurs the distinction between local and global differential privacy.
*   The discussion of related work is somewhat limited.
*   Some statements lack precision and clarity.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The theoretical results are rigorous and insightful, and the connection to practical techniques like randomized response is compelling. While there are areas for improvement, particularly in terms of clarity, contextualization, and discussion of related work, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. The contribution is twofold: a clear exposition of the local privacy model and new bounds for density estimation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
