- Provides theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy, giving sharp minimax rates for discrete and continuous problems.
- Builds on Warner’s randomized response, proving its optimality in privacy-preserving surveys.
- Connects classical randomized response to differential privacy and statistical decision theory.
- Uses minimax and information-theoretic tools to quantify privacy–efficiency trade-offs.
- Well-motivated work that situates itself within privacy-preserving data analysis literature.
- Clear introduction outlining problem, contributions, and relation to Warner’s formulation.
- Strong technical exposition overall, applying established minimax concepts.
- Studies both multinomial and density estimation, offering breadth in scope.
- Characterizes limitations of local privacy and quantifies the convergence-rate penalty due to privacy.
- Shows a different polynomial rate for density estimation under local privacy versus classical settings.
- Occasionally blurs the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy, overstating implications.
- The necessity of the constraint on α in Theorem 1 is not clearly justified or discussed in practical contexts.
- Choice and impact of utility measures (ρ) are not thoroughly explored or motivated.
- Omits comparisons to relevant differentially private estimation work (notably Lei 2015) and other histogram/discrete methods.
- Some technical statements lack precision and could confuse non-expert readers; explanations and assumptions need clarification.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, sharp minimax rates, optimality of randomized response, and links between classical and modern privacy concepts.
- Arguments against acceptance: conflation of privacy definitions, missing literature comparisons, limited discussion of utility measures, and occasional imprecision.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify privacy distinctions, compare to prior work (including Lei 2015), expand on utility measures, and improve exposition.
