This paper provides a detailed study of minimax rates for probability estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors analyze two canonical problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation. By applying tools from minimax decision theory to the local privacy setting, they derive sharp lower bounds on the estimation error and provide computationally efficient estimators that achieve these bounds. For d-dimensional multinomial estimation, a key finding is that α-local privacy effectively reduces the sample size by a factor proportional to α²/d. This result formally establishes that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For estimating β-smooth densities, the paper demonstrates a more severe penalty for privacy: the optimal non-private convergence rate of n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹) is unattainable, and the new minimax rate under local privacy is (nα²)⁻²β/(²β⁺²).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Quality and Novelty:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a rigorous analysis of statistical estimation under local privacy constraints. The proofs appear correct. The derivation of the minimax lower bound for density estimation under local privacy is a novel and important contribution, highlighting a fundamental change in the convergence rate compared to the non-private setting.

2.  **Significance of Results:** The results are highly significant. They provide a precise, quantitative characterization of the trade-off between privacy and statistical accuracy for fundamental estimation tasks. The finding that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal elegantly connects a classic idea from the survey literature with the modern, formal framework of differential privacy. This is a valuable insight for both the privacy and statistics communities.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The problem setting is clearly defined, and the main theorems are stated precisely. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their results in relation to classical minimax theory.

**Weaknesses:**

The main weakness of this work is the lack of engagement with and citation of highly relevant prior work on noise-complexity lower bounds in the (central) differential privacy literature.

1.  **Missing Comparison for Multinomial Estimation:** The authors should discuss the lower bounds established by Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012). These papers provide worst-case lower bounds on the L2 error for answering a set of linear queries under central differential privacy. Since multinomial estimation (i.e., estimating the proportions for each of the d categories) is a special case of answering d linear queries, these bounds are directly applicable. A central privacy guarantee is weaker than a local one, so a lower bound for central DP is also a lower bound for local DP. It is therefore crucial for the authors to clarify whether their new local-DP bounds for multinomial estimation improve upon these known central-DP bounds. Without this comparison, the novelty and strength of the multinomial lower bound are difficult to assess. While the authors' rebuttal partially addressed this, the paper would be much stronger with an explicit discussion of these connections.

2.  **Scope of the Density Estimation Lower Bound:** The lower bound for density estimation is derived for a Sobolev class defined via a specific trigonometric basis (Eq. 12). However, the corresponding non-private lower bound cited in Eq. (13) is often stated for more general Sobolev spaces. For the comparison between the private and non-private rates to be valid and meaningful, both bounds must be established over the same class of functions. The authors should clarify whether the non-private rate of n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹) holds specifically for this trigonometric class or if their private lower bound can be generalized.

3.  **Additional Missing Citations:** In addition to the works mentioned above, the authors should also cite and discuss Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which provides strong results on the mean-squared error for privately releasing query answers and is thus highly relevant to the estimation problems studied here.

**Summary:**

This is an interesting, well-written paper presenting important and novel results on the fundamental limits of statistical estimation under local differential privacy. The technical contributions, particularly the sharp minimax rate for density estimation, advance our understanding of the price of privacy. The primary shortcoming is the paper's positioning relative to existing literature on lower bounds in differential privacy. Addressing the points above, especially by explicitly comparing the derived bounds to known results from the central DP model, would substantially strengthen the paper and clarify the precise nature of its contribution. Despite this criticism, the paper's core results are valuable and of clear interest to the NIPS community.
