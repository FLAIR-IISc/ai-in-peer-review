

This paper studies minimax estimation rates under local differential privacy constraints, establishing sharp bounds for both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors leverage Fano's and Le Cam's inequalities to derive lower bounds that characterize the fundamental trade-off between privacy (measured by α) and statistical efficiency. The work connects Warner's classical randomized response mechanism to modern differential privacy, showing it achieves optimal sample complexity for multinomial estimation. For density estimation, they establish a novel rate of (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2) compared to the classical n⁻²β/(2β+1), demonstrating that privacy constraints alter the fundamental convergence rates in nonparametric estimation.

The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing the statistical cost of local privacy through minimax analysis, which is lacking in most differential privacy literature. The results provide a quantitative framework for understanding how privacy requirements impact estimation quality, which is valuable for practitioners designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The technical development is sound, with rigorous lower bounds complemented by efficient upper bound algorithms for both multinomial and density estimation. The connection to Warner's 1965 work is particularly insightful, revealing that an optimal privacy-preserving survey method has been known for nearly 50 years.

The paper's main strength lies in its clear characterization of the statistical limitations imposed by local privacy constraints. The minimax approach provides a principled way to measure the cost of privacy, which is more satisfying than ad hoc analyses. The authors successfully demonstrate that privacy constraints fundamentally change the convergence rates in nonparametric settings, which is a novel and important finding.

However, several weaknesses limit the paper's impact. The distinction between local privacy (which the paper studies) and standard differential privacy (which is often conflated with it) is not consistently maintained throughout the paper. For instance, the statement on page 4 that "α-local differential privacy is a form of differential privacy" is misleading—the paper explicitly studies local privacy (where each data point is perturbed individually), which is a stricter constraint than standard differential privacy (where the entire dataset is perturbed). This conflation weakens the paper's technical precision.

The paper also lacks crucial connections to recent literature. The authors should compare their density estimation results with Lei's work on differentially private M-estimators [19], which addresses a similar problem but with different privacy guarantees. The absence of this comparison makes it difficult to assess the novelty of the authors' approach. Additionally, the paper does not adequately discuss how different utility measures (ρ in the minimax formulation) affect the results, which would help readers understand the generality of the findings.

The restriction to α ∈ [0,1] in Theorem 1 is problematic. In practical differential privacy applications, α is often set to 1 or higher (e.g., α=1 for ε=1 in standard DP terminology). The authors should clarify why the restriction to small α is necessary for their bounds and whether the results extend to larger values of α, which would increase the paper's practical relevance.

The paper would benefit from clearer exposition of the population vs. sample estimation distinction. The authors state that "lower bounds on population estimation imply those on sample estimation," but this relationship is not sufficiently explained early in the paper, potentially confusing readers unfamiliar with this technical nuance.

While the paper's main contribution is the minimax bounds themselves, the authors' claim that they provide "a clear exposition of the local privacy model" is overstated—this model is well-established in Duchi et al. [9] (cited as [10] in the paper), which the authors themselves cite. The novelty primarily lies in the specific minimax bounds for probability estimation.

The paper's significance is moderate. While the results are technically sound and provide important theoretical insights about the cost of privacy, the practical implications for real-world applications are not sufficiently explored. The authors could strengthen their case by discussing how their bounds inform the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures in practice.

Strengths: Rigorous minimax analysis, clear demonstration of privacy-statistical efficiency trade-offs, optimal analysis of randomized response, and efficient algorithms for both multinomial and density estimation.

Weaknesses: Confusion between local privacy and standard differential privacy, insufficient comparison to related work (particularly Lei [19]), unclear treatment of α range, insufficient discussion of utility measures, and overstatement of the novelty of the local privacy model exposition.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding the statistical cost of local privacy constraints, particularly in the density estimation setting where the convergence rates differ fundamentally from non-private estimation. However, the paper would be significantly improved by addressing the technical clarifications and literature connections noted above. The results are important for the field, but the current presentation limits their impact. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns about privacy model distinctions and literature connections in the final version.
