- Studies trade-offs between utility and privacy in the local differential privacy model via minimax decision-theoretic analysis for probability and density estimation.  
- Establishes sharp minimax expected-error rates for estimating discrete distributions over d categories measured in l2 distance.  
- Proves Warner’s randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal, achieving the derived lower bound.  
- Extends results to nonparametric density estimation, showing local privacy alters the polynomial rate of convergence.  
- Proposes a simple, per-user perturbation scheme that attains the optimal rate in the nonparametric setting.  
- Builds on and connects classic privacy (Warner 1965; Dwork et al. 2006) and minimax statistical theory (Tsybakov 2009).  
- Strength: provides a rigorous, significant connection between statistical decision theory and local differential privacy.  
- Strength: proving optimality of a simple, classic mechanism (randomized response) is elegant and impactful.  
- Strength: results are technically sound and advance understanding of privacy-preserving estimation.  
- Weakness: exposition and derivations are overly complex and could be streamlined for readability.  
- Weakness: notation is cumbersome; bounds could be presented more directly given the l2 focus.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion comparing results to l1 analyses and non-local differential privacy lower bounds.  
- Suggestion: explicitly state that per-user independent randomization is optimal even when more complex, conditioned schemes are allowed.  
- Concern: novelty is diminished by overlap with a recently published FOCS 2013 paper.  
- Recommendation: accept, but less strongly due to clarity issues and reduced originality from the overlap.
