This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between statistical efficiency and privacy under the stringent model of local differential privacy. The authors connect the modern cryptographic notion of differential privacy with classical statistical minimax theory to derive sharp bounds on estimation error. The work focuses on two core statistical problems: estimating the parameters of a discrete (multinomial) distribution and nonparametric density estimation.

The main contributions are twofold. First, for estimating a `d`-dimensional multinomial probability vector, the paper establishes that the minimax squared `l2` error scales as `d / (nα²)`, a significant degradation from the non-private `1/n` rate. This result implies an effective sample size reduction from `n` to `nα²/d`. Crucially, the authors show that this lower bound is achieved by Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965, thereby proving its minimax optimality nearly 50 years after its invention. Second, for nonparametric density estimation of `β`-smooth functions (in the Sobolev sense), the paper demonstrates a more severe penalty for local privacy. The optimal non-private rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` is shown to degrade to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, a change in the polynomial exponent of the rate. The authors complement this lower bound with two constructive, efficient, and optimal procedures: a privatized histogram estimator for Lipschitz densities (`β=1`) and an orthogonal series estimator for general `β > 1`.

This work builds on a rich history of privacy in statistics, starting with Warner [23], and connects it to the modern framework of differential privacy [11]. It distinguishes itself from much of the recent literature on differential privacy by focusing on minimax bounds for estimating underlying *population* parameters rather than sample-based quantities, which generally leads to stronger lower bounds.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and rigorous analysis. The claims are well-supported by matching lower and upper bounds, which is the gold standard in minimax theory. The authors are careful to provide constructive and computationally efficient mechanisms that achieve their optimal rates, making the results practical.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the presentation could be improved in a few areas. The general minimax framework presented in Section 2.1 is overly abstract and could be simplified. The use of the generic loss `Φ ◦ ρ` is cumbersome, especially since all the main theorems and results are presented in terms of the squared `l2` norm (and `l1` norm for the multinomial case). The paper would be more direct and easier to follow if the minimax rates `Mn(...)` were defined and stated directly in terms of the specific norms being analyzed.

**Originality:** The connection between local differential privacy and minimax decision theory to derive sharp rates is a novel and important contribution. The result establishing the optimality of randomized response is particularly elegant and insightful. The discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is also a key original finding.

However, the novelty of the results in Section 3 on multinomial estimation is somewhat diminished by their similarity to results in a very recent FOCS 2013 paper by Kasiviswanathan et al. While the approach and context may differ, the core results appear to overlap, which the authors should acknowledge and discuss. Despite this, the density estimation results appear to be entirely novel and significant on their own.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are highly significant. It provides a foundational, quantitative understanding of the "price of privacy" for fundamental statistical tasks. The results are likely to be of great interest to researchers and practitioners in machine learning, statistics, and privacy. The confirmation that a simple, intuitive, and long-standing mechanism like randomized response is optimal is a powerful and impactful result. This work provides a strong theoretical basis for choosing privacy mechanisms and is likely to inspire further research into optimal private estimation for other statistical models.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

1.  **Simplifying the Framework:** I strongly suggest the authors simplify the exposition in Section 2. Stating the minimax risk directly for the expected squared `l2`-error would make the paper more accessible without losing rigor, as this is the primary metric used.

2.  **Comparison to `l1` Norm:** The paper provides results for both `l1` and `l2` loss for multinomial estimation. A brief discussion on the relationship between these bounds and why one might be preferred over the other in certain contexts would be beneficial.

3.  **Contextualizing Lower Bounds:** It would be helpful to include a discussion that compares the derived lower bounds under *local* privacy to existing lower bounds for *central* differential privacy (e.g., [8, 18]). This would help readers understand the additional statistical cost incurred by the stronger, non-interactive local privacy guarantee.

4.  **Optimality of Non-Interactive Schemes:** A major implication of the achievability results is that for these problems, simple, non-interactive schemes where each user randomizes their own data independently are minimax optimal. This is a powerful conclusion, as it suggests that more complex interactive protocols that might condition a user's response on others' (privatized) data are not necessary to achieve optimal rates. This point is worth highlighting explicitly in the discussion.

5.  **Future Directions:** While the non-interactive (memoryless channel) setting is well-motivated, a brief comment on whether these minimax techniques could be extended to analyze more general local privacy mechanisms would be a welcome addition to the discussion.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem at the intersection of machine learning and privacy.
*   It provides sharp, minimax optimal rates of convergence for two canonical estimation problems.
*   The results are technically strong, featuring matching lower and upper bounds.
*   It provides a landmark result by proving the optimality of Warner's randomized response, a classic and practical mechanism.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The presentation of the theoretical background is unnecessarily complex and could be improved for clarity.
*   The originality of the discrete estimation results is weakened by significant overlap with a recent paper in another top-tier conference.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the theory of private statistical analysis. While the novelty of one of its two main results is somewhat reduced by recent concurrent work, the second main result on density estimation is novel and important, as is the overall framework. The strengths substantially outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper will be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, though my enthusiasm is slightly tempered by the originality concern.
