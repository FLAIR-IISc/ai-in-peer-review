This paper studies estimation under local differential privacy, modeling each datum as passed through an α-private channel and using Fano/Le Cam–style information inequalities to derive minimax lower bounds. For multinomial estimation, the authors show an “effective sample size” reduction n → nα2/d; for nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes, local privacy fundamentally changes the rate from n−2β/(2β+1) to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2). They complement the bounds with mechanisms attaining the rates and show that Warner’s randomized response is optimal in a precise sense. The work contributes a clear exposition of the local privacy model and new sharp bounds for density estimation. I did not examine the supplementary material.

Quality: The technical core appears solid: the reductions to testing, divergence control under privacy, and tightness via matching constructions are convincing. That said, Theorem 1’s restriction on α deserves discussion: is it necessary for the lower bound, and how does it affect relevance when many differentially private deployments operate at α≈1? The paper also needs a more explicit and earlier explanation of whether the targets are population parameters θ(P) versus sample statistics θ(x1:n), as this distinction is crucial for interpreting the bounds.

Clarity: Generally well written, but some imprecise commentary could confuse readers unfamiliar with privacy models. The paper sometimes blurs the distinction between local and (central) differential privacy (e.g., statement at the bottom of p.4); please state clearly throughout which model is assumed and how results translate (or not) across models. Provide guidance on choices of utility/semi-metrics ρ with concrete examples to show how different utilities fit the analysis.

Originality: The adaptation of classical information-theoretic lower bounds to local privacy is well executed, and the density estimation rates appear novel and important. The exposition of local privacy largely builds on prior work (e.g., Duchi–Jordan–Wainwright), but the new bounds are a meaningful advance.

Significance: The paper crisply characterizes the limitations of learning from privatized data and gives optimal mechanisms, including a principled justification of randomized response.

Related work: The paper should situate itself more fully within the NIPS privacy literature, including differential privacy for estimation/ERM and lower bounds, and directly compare to Lei’s private M-estimation density estimator, as well as other histogram and discrete-data methods to contextualize the optimality of randomized response.

Pros for acceptance:
- Sharp, tight minimax bounds and matching mechanisms
- Clear articulation of privacy–statistical efficiency trade-offs
- Conceptually important result that randomized response is optimal

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing comparisons (Lei and other DP estimation methods)
- Blurred local vs. central DP distinctions; imprecise commentary
- Unclear necessity of α restriction; limited guidance on ρ choices
- Population vs. sample targets not explained early enough
