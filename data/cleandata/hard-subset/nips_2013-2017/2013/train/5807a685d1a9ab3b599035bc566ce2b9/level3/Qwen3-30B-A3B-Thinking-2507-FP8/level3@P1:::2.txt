

This paper provides a comprehensive minimax analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, bridging classical statistical decision theory with modern privacy frameworks. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation, revealing novel trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. For multinomial estimation over d dimensions, they show that local privacy at level α reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d, making Warner's randomized response mechanism (1965) minimax optimal. For density estimation of β-smooth densities, they prove a new convergence rate of (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2), which differs from the classical non-private rate of n⁻²β/(2β+1). The paper demonstrates that these bounds are tight by providing efficiently computable estimators that match the lower bounds.

The paper's theoretical contributions are significant. The authors successfully connect Warner's classical survey sampling work with modern differential privacy through minimax analysis, demonstrating that the optimal privacy-preserving strategy for population probability estimation has been known for nearly 50 years. The technical development is sound, with careful construction of minimax lower bounds using information-theoretic tools and matching upper bounds via novel privacy-preserving estimators. The proofs appear rigorous and the results represent a substantial advancement over prior work that has primarily focused on sample-level estimation rather than population-level estimation under privacy constraints.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured arguments that progressively build from the theoretical framework to specific applications. The notation is consistently applied, and the relationship between the classical survey sampling literature and modern privacy frameworks is clearly articulated. The authors effectively demonstrate the practical implications of their theoretical results through the optimality of randomized response for multinomial estimation.

However, the paper would benefit from more thorough engagement with related work in differential privacy. The authors should explicitly address the connection to prior noise-complexity lower bounds for linear queries with L2 error (Hardt & Talwar, 2010; De, 2012), noting that multinomial estimation constitutes a special case of linear queries. The authors should clarify whether their local-DP bounds improve upon or are consistent with these prior differential-privacy bounds. Additionally, the density-estimation lower bound is derived for trigonometric-basis expansions; it would be valuable to establish whether this result holds for general Sobolev spaces or is specific to this basis. The authors should cite and discuss Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013), which addresses mean-squared error in differential privacy, to better position their contribution within the broader literature.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides the first sharp characterization of minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, revealing fundamental trade-offs that have practical implications for survey design and privacy-preserving statistics. The authors' demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly noteworthy, as it shows that optimal privacy-preserving strategies for certain problems have been known for decades. This work provides a theoretical foundation for understanding how privacy constraints affect statistical efficiency, which is increasingly important as privacy-preserving data collection becomes more widespread.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The minimax bounds are sharp and the estimators are efficiently computable. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical development follows a logical progression, and the relationship between the classical survey sampling literature and modern privacy frameworks is clearly articulated. The proofs are presented with sufficient detail for verification.

Originality: This paper represents a significant advance in the intersection of privacy and statistics. While prior work has studied privacy-preserving estimation, this paper provides the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, revealing novel trade-offs that distinguish it from previous work. The connection to Warner's classical work is particularly novel.

Significance: The results have clear implications for the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The characterization of the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off provides a theoretical foundation for practitioners to make informed decisions about privacy parameters. The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is both theoretically elegant and practically relevant.

Pro: Sharp minimax rates that reveal fundamental privacy-statistical efficiency trade-offs; demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal; novel connection between classical survey sampling and modern privacy frameworks; rigorous theoretical development with matching upper and lower bounds.

Con: Insufficient engagement with related work on noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy; unclear if density-estimation results hold for general Sobolev spaces; failure to cite and discuss relevant recent work (Nikolov et al., 2013).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistics. While the authors should address the missing engagement with prior work on noise-complexity bounds, the core theoretical contributions are sound, novel, and significant. The paper advances our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency and provides a valuable theoretical foundation for privacy-preserving statistical inference. I recommend acceptance.
