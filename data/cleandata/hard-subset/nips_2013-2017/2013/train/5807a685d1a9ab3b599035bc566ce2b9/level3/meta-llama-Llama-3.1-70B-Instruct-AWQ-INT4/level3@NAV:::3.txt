This paper studies local differential privacy with minimax expected-error rates for estimating probabilities and densities. The authors provide lower bounds on minimax rates for l2 distance with discrete distributions over d types and show that randomized response (each user perturbs only their own type) meets the lower bound, proving optimality. They also extend results to density estimation with a simple per-user perturbation scheme achieving the lower bound.

The paper is well-written and provides a thorough analysis of the problem. The authors provide a detailed study of the estimation of probability distributions and densities in a stringent setting where data is kept private even from the statistician. They give sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate.

The paper's main contributions are the provision of lower bounds on minimax rates for l2 distance with discrete distributions over d types and the demonstration that randomized response meets the lower bound, proving optimality. The authors also extend results to density estimation with a simple per-user perturbation scheme achieving the lower bound.

The paper's strengths include its importance, as it shows that classic procedures are optimal and is of interest to the community. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in locally private settings.

However, the paper's exposition of minimax bounds is overly complex and could be simplified. The notation is also cumbersome, and the bounds could be stated directly in l2 norm since all results use it. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a discussion or comparison to l1 norm results and other differential-privacy lower bounds (mostly non-local settings). The authors could also consider broader settings beyond the tailored local-privacy model and provide minimax lower bounds for them.

In terms of originality, the paper's results overlap or extend a recently published FOCS 2013 paper (Section 3), weakening novelty. However, the paper still makes significant contributions to the field and is recommended for acceptance.

Overall, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10 (the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis)
* Clarity: 8/10 (the paper is clearly written, but the exposition of minimax bounds is overly complex)
* Originality: 7/10 (the paper's results overlap or extend a recently published FOCS 2013 paper)
* Significance: 9/10 (the paper is important, shows classic procedures are optimal, and is of interest to the community)

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should simplify the exposition of minimax bounds and consider broader settings beyond the tailored local-privacy model.
