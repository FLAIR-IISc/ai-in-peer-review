## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of statistical estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors derive minimax lower bounds for estimating probability distributions (both discrete and continuous) when the data holder does not trust the statistician, establishing trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. A notable consequence of their work is the optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism for survey sampling under local privacy. The paper builds upon recent work connecting differential privacy with minimax decision theory, aiming to provide a more rigorous theoretical foundation for privacy-preserving statistical inference.

The strength of this work lies in its characterization of the limitations imposed by local privacy on statistical estimation. The derived bounds are sharp and provide valuable insights into the inherent costs of protecting individual data privacy. The demonstration of the optimality of randomized response is a significant result, connecting a classical solution to a modern privacy concern. The exposition of the local privacy model, drawing heavily from Duchi et al. [9], is clear and well-motivated. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The commentary is occasionally imprecise, potentially confusing readers unfamiliar with the nuances of the field. For example, the discussion around the relationship between local privacy and differential privacy could be more carefully worded to avoid blurring the distinction, particularly the statement on page 4. A significant omission is the lack of engagement with existing differential privacy literature beyond citing its existence. The restriction to local privacy, while a valid modeling choice, is not sufficiently contrasted with the broader differential privacy framework. 

Furthermore, the necessity of the restriction on α in Theorem 1 is unclear. Given that α ≈ 1 is often encountered in differential privacy applications, justifying this constraint would strengthen the result. The authors also need to elaborate on their choice of utility measures (ρ) and demonstrate how different choices impact the analysis. A comparison to Lei’s M-estimation density estimator [19] is crucial, as it represents a related approach to private density estimation. Finally, a discussion of how their results compare to existing methods for histogram/discrete data estimation would help contextualize the performance of randomized response.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of a fundamental problem in privacy-preserving statistics, deriving sharp minimax bounds and establishing the optimality of a classical technique.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The paper lacks sufficient connection to the broader differential privacy literature, contains imprecise commentary in places, and omits important comparisons to related work.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a solid contribution with a twofold impact: a clear exposition of the local privacy model and new, sharp bounds for density estimation. However, addressing the weaknesses regarding context, clarity, and comparison to related work would significantly enhance the paper's value. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors *must* clarify the population vs. sample statistics distinction earlier in the paper and provide a more thorough comparison to related work, including Lei’s estimator.
