Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each datum is privatized before reaching the statistician via a non-interactive channel Q that satisfies α-local DP. The authors develop a minimax decision-theoretic framework for private population estimation, derive information-theoretic lower bounds (via Le Cam/Fano arguments with new KL/MI contraction bounds under LDP), and match them with constructive upper bounds.

Two main results: (i) Multinomial estimation over the d-simplex under LDP has mean-squared error scaling like d/(nα2) (up to constants and truncation effects), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d when α ≤ 1. The paper shows that a randomized response mechanism achieves the rate, establishing the optimality (in a minimax sense) of Warner’s randomized response for this task. (ii) For Sobolev-smooth density estimation on [0,1], the non-private minimax rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2) under LDP. The authors give practical mechanisms achieving the lower bound: a privatized histogram for β=1 and an orthogonal series estimator with a carefully designed locally private sampling strategy for general β≥1. The analysis connects decision-theoretic lower bounds with variational information upper bounds for locally private channels, building on the authors’ earlier technical report [9], and relates to foundational DP work in databases/learning [11,16,17,21,24] and classical minimax theory [22,25,26].

Strengths
- Clear, principled minimax treatment of population estimation under LDP; lower bounds and matching upper bounds for two canonical problems.
- Sharp identification of privacy–accuracy tradeoffs, including the change of exponent for nonparametric density estimation.
- Conceptually valuable: formalizes and proves the optimality of randomized response for multinomials; provides constructive and efficient mechanisms for achievability.
- Technically solid use of information-contraction bounds under LDP combined with Le Cam/Fano methods.
- Practical insight: shows that naive per-sample Laplace perturbation is suboptimal for density estimation and designs a better mechanism.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope/terminology: The paper sometimes blurs the distinction between local DP (this work) and the standard curator/central model of DP. Please consistently say “local differential privacy” and state explicitly in the Introduction that all results are in the non-interactive LDP model. Some statements (e.g., at the end of the discussion of DP guarantees on p.4) could mislead readers into conflating models with different power and guarantees.
- Relation to prior DP work: The paper should provide a clearer positioning relative to existing results in central DP estimation (e.g., Wasserman & Zhou 2010; Smith 2011; Hardt & Talwar 2010; Chaudhuri et al. 2011; Chaudhuri & Hsu 2012; De 2012) and to prior LDP/interactive local learning work (Kasiviswanathan et al. 2011). The current related work is present but not sufficiently contrasted to highlight what is new specifically under LDP and how the minimax rates differ from central DP.
- Population vs. sample estimation: The distinction that lower bounds target population parameters θ(P) (rather than sample-based statistics θ(x1:n)) is crucial and should be explained clearly and early (e.g., end of Section 1). This will help avoid confusion with prior optimality results that bound sample error under central DP.
- α regime in Theorem 1: The theorem is stated for α∈[0,1]. Is this restriction essential? Do the rates extend to α>1 (e.g., by replacing α with min{α,1} in constants) or do constants/exponents change? Because many DP deployments use α≈1 (or larger), please discuss the relevance and any smooth transition to the non-private regime as α grows.
- Utility metrics: The framework allows general loss via Φ∘ρ. The paper analyzes ℓ2 and ℓ1 for multinomials and L2(squared) for densities. A brief discussion on how other utilities (e.g., TV, Hellinger, sup-norm) fit the approach would strengthen the exposition and help practitioners map their goals to your results.
- Comparisons for discrete/multinomial mechanisms: Beyond stating optimality of randomized response, provide a more concrete comparison to other simple mechanisms (e.g., adding Laplace noise to one-hot vectors, count sketching of categories) with explicit constants and perhaps small empirical illustrations to back the claims about suboptimality or constant factors.
- Relation to Lei (2012) and other estimators: The paper cites Lei [19] but the comparison is cursory. Please expand the discussion: (i) clarify that Lei studies central DP M-estimators (not local), (ii) compare rates and smoothness assumptions, and (iii) explain why the two-stage histogram/M-estimation approach is suboptimal under LDP. Also situate your histogram choice w.r.t. classical data-based histogram selection (Scott 1979) and whether such adaptivity is compatible with LDP.
- Clarity/editorial: Some passages contain imprecise commentary that may confuse casual readers; tightening language around privacy definitions and model assumptions would help. A figure summarizing the main rates versus α, n, d, β would be valuable. State computing costs explicitly for the orthogonal-series mechanism (dependence on k, α, and the basis bound B0).

Quality
The results appear technically sound, with sharp lower bounds and matching upper bounds using efficient mechanisms. Some proofs rely on the longer version [9]; I did not verify those derivations, but the overall methodology is standard and credible. The achievability constructions for density estimation are nontrivial and insightful.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Improvements needed: earlier and consistent emphasis on LDP (non-interactive); clearer population vs. sample distinction; expanded related-work comparisons; and brief guidance on utility metrics.

Originality
High in the context of LDP: the density estimation rates and mechanisms under LDP appear new, and the tight optimality characterization for multinomials under LDP adds value even if randomized response is classical.

Significance
Significant and timely. The results quantify fundamental limits under strong, deployment-relevant privacy constraints (local privacy), and the constructive mechanisms are practical. Likely to influence subsequent work on private learning and statistics.

Pros for acceptance
- Sharp, matched minimax bounds for two fundamental estimation problems under LDP.
- Clean connection between information contraction under LDP and statistical lower bounds.
- Practical, efficient mechanisms with clear guidance on tuning (k vs. n, α).
- Clarifies the optimality of randomized response for multinomials.

Cons for acceptance
- Needs clearer and earlier model framing (LDP vs. central DP) and population vs. sample focus.
- Related work comparison (especially Lei and central-DP estimation) should be strengthened.
- Limited discussion of broader utility metrics and mechanism comparisons for discrete data.

Additional minor comments
- State whether constants in Theorem 1 depend on d and whether the bounds are dimension-free up to the explicit d factors.
- For the orthogonal-series mechanism, discuss the impact of basis sup-norm B0 and practicality of rejection sampling as k grows.
- Small typos: ensure consistent notation for Φ∘ρ, and define Πk/ΠΔd upon first use.

Overall recommendation
A strong paper with novel and important contributions to private estimation under local privacy. With the clarifications and expanded related-work discussion suggested above, it would make a valuable addition to NIPS.
