## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in the context of local differential privacy, specifically focusing on minimax estimation rates for probabilities and densities. The authors provide tight lower bounds for multinomial estimation and density estimation under this privacy constraint, demonstrating that the classic randomized response mechanism is, in fact, optimal for the former. They extend these results to density estimation, offering a per-user perturbation scheme that achieves the derived lower bound. 

The work builds upon the growing body of research on differential privacy, going beyond merely ensuring disclosure risk reduction to formally characterize the impact of privacy on statistical efficiency using minimax theory. This is a valuable contribution, connecting Warner’s early work on randomized response with modern privacy frameworks and decision-theoretic tools. The results are clearly important, showing how local privacy impacts the achievable convergence rates, and the optimality proof for randomized response is a strong finding. The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem with increasing relevance.

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical rigor and the clear demonstration of optimality for a practical mechanism. However, the exposition of the minimax bounds, particularly the lemmas and proofs utilizing KL-divergence and mutual information, is overly complex and could benefit from simplification. The heavy reliance on specialized notation further obscures the core ideas.  Specifically, consistently stating bounds directly in the l2 norm, given that all results ultimately utilize it, would improve readability. 

Several avenues for further exploration could strengthen the work. A discussion comparing the results to those in the l1 norm setting, and a broader comparison with existing differential privacy lower bounds (many of which focus on non-local privacy) would be beneficial.  Considering more general privacy models beyond the tailored local-privacy model investigated here, and providing corresponding minimax lower bounds, would expand the scope and impact of the results.  Finally, explicitly stating that per-user independent randomization is optimal despite the existence of schemes that condition on other users' data would emphasize a key aspect of the local privacy model.

A concerning reservation is the overlap with the FOCS 2013 paper referenced in Section 3. This significantly weakens the novelty of some aspects of the work, though the results are still valuable and well-presented. Despite this, I recommend acceptance, albeit with slightly less enthusiasm than I would have otherwise. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and will be of interest to the NIPS community.
