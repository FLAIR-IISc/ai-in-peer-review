

This paper provides a comprehensive study of minimax rates for probability and density estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors establish tight lower bounds for l2 estimation of discrete distributions (multinomial) and continuous densities, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response mechanism achieves optimal rates for the former, while a histogram-based approach achieves the optimal rate for the latter. The work effectively bridges classical survey sampling techniques with modern differential privacy theory, showing that fundamental privacy-utility trade-offs can be precisely characterized.

The paper's main strengths lie in its technical depth and the clear demonstration that classical methods like randomized response are indeed optimal in this setting, a finding with significant practical implications. The minimax approach provides rigorous theoretical foundations for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs, making the work highly relevant to the NIPS community. The authors also provide efficiently computable estimators that match the lower bounds, strengthening the practical relevance.

However, the exposition suffers from unnecessary complexity in the minimax bound derivations, which could be simplified without losing rigor. The notation is cumbersome, and the authors could have directly stated all results in the l2 norm since all analysis uses this metric. I recommend explicitly stating that per-user independent randomization is optimal despite the existence of more complex schemes that condition on other users' data. The paper could also benefit from comparing results to l1 norm settings and discussing how these findings relate to non-local differential privacy lower bounds.

While the core contributions are important, I note some overlap with Duchi et al.'s FOCS 2013 paper (Section 3), which slightly weakens the novelty claim. Despite this, the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the statistical limits of local privacy.

Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with complete proofs.
Clarity: Moderate - could be improved with simplified exposition.
Originality: Good, though some overlap with prior work.
Significance: High - addresses a fundamental question with practical implications.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, providing a rigorous theoretical framework for privacy-preserving estimation. I recommend acceptance, though the novelty is somewhat diminished by the FOCS 2013 overlap. The authors should address the clarity and notation issues in the camera-ready version.
