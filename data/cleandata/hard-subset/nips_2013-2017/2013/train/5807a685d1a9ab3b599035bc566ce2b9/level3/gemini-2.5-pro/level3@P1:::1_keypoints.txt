- Paper provides a minimax analysis of probability estimation under α-local differential privacy using Le Cam’s two-point method and Fano’s inequality.  
- Focuses on multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation, deriving sharp lower bounds for any α-locally private estimator.  
- For d-dimensional multinomial estimation, local privacy reduces effective sample size roughly from n to nα²/d.  
- Proves Warner’s randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation.  
- For β-smooth density estimation, the optimal rate worsens from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to (nα²)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, changing the polynomial exponent.  
- Authors propose computationally efficient, rate-optimal estimators (privatized histogram and orthogonal series estimator) matching the lower bounds.  

Strengths:  
- Fundamental contribution: quantifies the trade-off between statistical utility and local privacy with sharp minimax rates.  
- Novel and significant: shows local privacy can qualitatively change convergence exponents in nonparametric problems.  
- Constructive and complete: provides efficient estimators achieving the derived minimax rates.  
- Strong theoretical framework: lower bounds apply to population-parameter estimation, a stronger guarantee than many prior sample-based results.  

Weaknesses / suggestions:  
- Clarity: paper sometimes conflates "differential privacy" with "local differential privacy"; needs an explicit intro paragraph distinguishing local vs central models and motivating local choice.  
- Positioning to prior work: comparisons are incomplete — e.g., expand discussion of Lei [19] and compare rates/techniques; compare randomized response optimality against a broader class of discrete-data mechanisms.  
- Scope/generalization: Theorem 1 is stated for α ∈ [0,1]—clarify if this is technical or fundamental and comment on α>1; discuss extending analysis beyond squared ℓ2 and ℓ1 losses to other utility measures.  
- Emphasis: highlight the novelty of population-level lower bounds more prominently in the introduction.  

- Overall: technically strong, novel, and impactful work that would benefit from clearer model distinctions and broader contextualization with related literature.
