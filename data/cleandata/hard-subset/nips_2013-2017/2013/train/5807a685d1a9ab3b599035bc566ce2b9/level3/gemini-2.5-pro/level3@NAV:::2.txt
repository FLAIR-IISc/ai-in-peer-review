This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between statistical efficiency and privacy by deriving sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. This work connects the classical privacy framework of Warner's randomized response with modern differential privacy and statistical decision theory. The main contributions are twofold: for multinomial estimation, the authors show that local privacy effectively reduces the sample size by a factor proportional to the privacy parameter squared (α²), demonstrating that for small α, the rate is comparable to the non-private setting. For nonparametric density estimation, they prove a more significant result: local privacy fundamentally degrades the optimal convergence rate, changing the exponent in the minimax risk.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The technical results are significant, particularly the novel minimax lower bound for density estimation under local privacy, which establishes a fundamental limitation that was not previously quantified. The proofs appear sound, and the paper is well-written and clear. By providing sharp, matching upper and lower bounds, the authors offer a complete and compelling theoretical picture of the problems studied. The finding that a modified version of Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation is an elegant and insightful connection between classical and modern privacy research.

The primary weakness of the paper is its insufficient engagement with related literature on noise-complexity lower bounds in the standard (centralized) differential privacy model. The authors should cite and discuss the foundational work of Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (2012), which provide worst-case lower bounds for releasing linear queries with L2 error. Since multinomial estimation is a special case of linear queries, it is crucial for the authors to clarify whether their local-DP bounds are novel improvements over these existing differential-DP bounds or if they represent a different trade-off specific to the local model. Additionally, the density estimation lower bound is derived for a trigonometric basis; the authors should clarify if the non-private comparison rate (eq. 13) holds for the same specific function class or for general Sobolev spaces to ensure a fair comparison. Finally, the discussion of prior work on mean-squared error should also include Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013).

In summary, this is an interesting and high-quality paper with important theoretical contributions to the understanding of privacy and statistical inference. The core results are strong, but the paper would be significantly improved by situating its contributions more thoroughly within the context of existing lower bounds in differential privacy.
