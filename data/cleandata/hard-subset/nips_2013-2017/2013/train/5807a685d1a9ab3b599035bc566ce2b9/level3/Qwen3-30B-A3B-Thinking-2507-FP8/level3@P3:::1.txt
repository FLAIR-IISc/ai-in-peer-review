

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation under local privacy constraints, using minimax decision theory. The authors derive sharp lower bounds for estimation in locally private settings using Fano/LeCam-style arguments, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response is optimal for certain problems. They establish that providing α-local differential privacy reduces the effective sample size in multinomial estimation from n to nα²/d, and for density estimation, the convergence rate degrades from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2). The paper complements these lower bounds with matching upper bounds using efficiently implementable algorithms.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a significant contribution by formally characterizing the limitations of learning from data perturbed for privacy, providing the first sharp minimax rates for locally private probability estimation. The connection between Warner's classical work and modern privacy frameworks is insightful, and the authors successfully bridge statistical decision theory with differential privacy. The technical results are rigorous, and the paper demonstrates that randomized response is optimal for survey sampling, a result that has remained largely overlooked in the modern privacy literature.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from several critical issues that diminish its clarity and impact. First, it fails to properly distinguish between local privacy and differential privacy, often blurring the distinction (e.g., the statement at the bottom of p.4). The restriction to local privacy makes the model fundamentally different from standard differential privacy, and the paper does not adequately address this distinction. Second, the paper contains imprecise commentary that could confuse casual readers (e.g., unclear treatment of the α restriction in Theorem 1, which appears unnecessary and irrelevant given that α ≈ 1 in typical differential privacy applications). Third, the paper misses crucial connections to existing literature: it fails to cite or compare with Lei's M-estimation density estimator (NIPS 2011), and does not discuss how their results relate to other histogram or discrete-data estimation methods. The paper also lacks commentary on the choice of utility measures ρ, which would help readers understand how different utility functions fit the analysis.

**Quality:** The technical content is sound, with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. However, the paper's quality is undermined by its failure to properly contextualize its contributions within the broader privacy literature. The lower bounds are technically correct but lack the necessary comparison to existing work, making it difficult to assess their novelty.

**Clarity:** The paper would benefit from significantly improved clarity, particularly regarding the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy. The authors should clarify earlier in the paper that they are studying a different privacy model than standard differential privacy (where the data holder trusts the statistician), and explicitly state the consequences of this restriction. The distinction between population and sample statistics (raised in the rebuttal) needs to be explained more clearly and earlier in the paper.

**Originality:** The paper's main original contribution is the derivation of sharp minimax rates for locally private estimation, but much of the theoretical framework (the local privacy model) is already contained in [10] (Duchi et al.). The novelty lies in the application of these tools to probability estimation, but the paper fails to adequately distinguish its contribution from prior work on privacy and statistical estimation.

**Significance:** The results have important implications for privacy-preserving data collection, particularly for survey sampling. However, the significance is diminished by the lack of connection to existing privacy literature and the failure to compare with alternative approaches. The paper would be more significant if it demonstrated how their results improve upon existing methods or provided practical guidance for implementation.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Provides the first sharp minimax rates for locally private probability estimation, demonstrates the optimality of randomized response, and offers a clear theoretical framework for understanding privacy-statistical efficiency trade-offs.
*Con:* Fails to properly distinguish local privacy from differential privacy, lacks comparison to key related work (Lei, Duchi et al. 2013), contains imprecise commentary, and fails to address practical considerations like utility measure choices.

**Final Assessment:** The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, but its presentation and contextualization are insufficient to justify acceptance at NIPS. The core technical results are sound and interesting, but the paper's failure to properly connect with existing literature and clarify the relationship between local privacy and differential privacy significantly weakens its contribution. The authors should substantially revise the paper to address these issues, particularly by adding a detailed comparison with Lei's work and clarifying the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy. With these revisions, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's contribution is largely contained in the theoretical framework rather than the specific results, which are not sufficiently novel or contextualized.
