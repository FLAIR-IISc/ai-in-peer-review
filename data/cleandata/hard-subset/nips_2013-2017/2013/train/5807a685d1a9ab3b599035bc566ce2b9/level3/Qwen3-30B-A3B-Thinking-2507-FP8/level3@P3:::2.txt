

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation" (NIPS 2013)

This paper provides a detailed study of minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors establish sharp minimax bounds for two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The paper bridges Warner's classical randomized response framework with modern differential privacy theory, using minimax decision theory to quantify the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The main contributions are significant. For multinomial estimation, the paper proves that local privacy reduces the effective sample size quadratically in the privacy parameter ﾎｱ, with the effective sample size scaling as nﾎｱﾂｲ/d. This shows that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal, which is an important historical connection. For density estimation, the paper demonstrates that local privacy fundamentally alters the convergence rates, degrading the classical rate from n竅ｻﾂｲﾎｲ/(2ﾎｲ+1) to (nﾎｱﾂｲ)竅ｻﾂｲﾎｲ/(2ﾎｲ+2). The authors provide matching upper bounds using histogram and orthogonal series estimators, establishing sharp rates for both problems.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The proofs appear correct, and the minimax bound for density estimation under privacy is novel. The connection between classical survey sampling and modern privacy theory is particularly insightful, and the authors effectively demonstrate how to move along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum.

However, the paper has significant shortcomings in its literature review and contextualization. The authors fail to discuss related work on noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy, specifically Hardt & Talwar (2010) and De (2012), which provide worst-case lower bounds for linear queries with L2 error. Since multinomial estimation is a special case of linear queries, the authors should clarify whether their local-DP bounds improve on differential-DP bounds. This omission is substantial because it leaves the reader unable to assess the novelty of their bounds relative to prior work on differential privacy.

Additionally, the density-estimation lower bound (Theorem 2) is derived for trigonometric-basis expansions, but the non-private lower bound (eq.13) appears to be derived for a specific basis. The authors should clarify whether the non-private rate n竅ｻﾂｲﾎｲ/(2ﾎｲ+1) holds for general Sobolev spaces or only for the trigonometric basis, as the comparison must be on the same function class. This is particularly important since the authors claim their result shows that the classical rate is no longer attainable under privacy constraints.

The rebuttal partially addressed these issues, but the authors should also cite Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013), which addresses mean-squared error in differential privacy, as this is directly relevant to their setting.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with correct proofs and novel results. However, the lack of discussion of relevant prior work weakens the evaluation of the novelty and significance of the results.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear definitions and theorems. The connection between classical survey sampling and modern privacy theory is particularly well-explained.

**Originality**: The minimax bounds under local privacy for these problems are novel, though the paper fails to properly contextualize them within the broader differential privacy literature. The connection to Warner's work is an interesting historical contribution.

**Significance**: The results are important for understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper establishes sharp rates that are crucial for practitioners designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the failure to properly situate the results within existing differential privacy literature.

**Arguments for acceptance**: 
- Provides sharp minimax rates for two fundamental statistical problems under local privacy
- Demonstrates the optimality of Warner's randomized response
- Establishes fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency
- Well-written and technically sound

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Fails to cite and discuss relevant prior work on noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy
- Does not clarify whether the density estimation lower bound holds for general Sobolev spaces
- Omits key references (Hardt & Talwar 2010; De 2012; Nikolov et al. 2013) that directly relate to the results

Overall, this is an interesting and well-written paper with important results. The technical contributions are valuable for the field, but the lack of proper contextualization within differential privacy literature significantly weakens the paper's impact. The authors should address the missing literature citations and clarify the scope of their density-estimation results before the paper can be considered for publication. The results would be substantially stronger with a more thorough discussion of how they relate to existing work on differential privacy lower bounds.
