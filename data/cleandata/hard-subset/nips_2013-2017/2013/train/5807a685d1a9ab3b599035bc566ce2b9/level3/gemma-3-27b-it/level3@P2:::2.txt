This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in probability estimation, specifically in settings where data is kept private even from the statistician via local differential privacy. The authors derive sharp minimax rates of convergence for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy constraints, demonstrating how privacy impacts the achievable convergence rates. A key finding is that for multinomial estimation, local privacy effectively reduces the sample size by a factor related to the privacy parameter α, while for density estimation, it alters the polynomial rate of convergence compared to the non-private case. The paper also provides efficiently implementable algorithms that achieve these rates, and notably recovers Warner’s randomized response as an optimal strategy for survey sampling.

The work builds on a rich history of research in privacy-preserving statistical inference, citing foundational contributions from Warner (1965) and more recent advances in differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006). The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape, bridging classical statistical decision theory with the formal guarantees of differential privacy. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the practical relevance of the setting. The formalization of local privacy using the likelihood ratio bound (Evfimievski et al., 2003) is standard and well-presented.

A significant strength of the paper is the rigor of the analysis. The authors leverage minimax theory to provide lower bounds on the convergence rates, complementing these with matching upper bounds achievable through practical algorithms. The proofs appear sound, and the careful consideration of both the strengths and weaknesses of their results demonstrates a commendable level of scientific integrity. The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response is a particularly noteworthy contribution, showing that a decades-old technique remains state-of-the-art in this context.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. A primary concern is the limited discussion of related work in differential privacy, specifically concerning noise-complexity lower bounds. While the authors acknowledge the broader literature, they fail to adequately engage with prior results such as those by Hardt and Talwar (2010) and De (2012). These works establish lower bounds on the amount of noise required to achieve differential privacy for a broad class of linear queries, and multinomial estimation can be viewed as a special case. The authors should clarify whether their local-DP bounds improve upon existing differential-DP bounds for this specific problem. The authors did address this in a rebuttal, but a more thorough discussion in the original submission would be beneficial. Relatedly, the work by Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (2013) which addresses MSE bounds in differential privacy, should be cited and discussed.

Another point of clarification relates to the density estimation lower bound. The analysis focuses on density estimation using trigonometric-basis expansions. While this is a well-motivated choice, the authors do not fully address the connection to the standard non-private minimax rate (Eq. 13), which is often stated for more general Sobolev spaces. A clearer comparison of the rates under different function class assumptions is needed to fully appreciate the impact of local privacy. Does the non-private lower bound (Eq. 13) universally hold for all Sobolev spaces, or only for those amenable to trigonometric basis expansions?

Despite these weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The results are novel, the analysis is rigorous, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors successfully demonstrate the inherent trade-offs between privacy and accuracy in probability estimation and provide a solid theoretical foundation for future research in this area.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper derives sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy, which is a novel contribution.
*   **Rigor:** The analysis is mathematically sound, with both lower and upper bounds provided.
*   **Significance:** The results have implications for a wide range of applications where privacy is a concern, such as survey sampling and data analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited discussion of related work:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of existing lower bounds in differential privacy.
*   **Function class assumptions:** The density estimation lower bound is specific to trigonometric-basis expansions, and the connection to more general Sobolev spaces could be clarified.

**Overall assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. While the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, the core contributions are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the writing is clear. The results represent an important step forward in our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
