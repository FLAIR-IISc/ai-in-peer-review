

# Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

This paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of minimax estimation under local differential privacy constraints, establishing sharp rates for both discrete probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The authors bridge classical statistical decision theory with modern differential privacy, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response procedure is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation under local privacy, a result that is both elegant and practically significant.

The paper makes several key contributions. For discrete distributions over d types, the authors establish minimax lower bounds for l2 estimation error under α-local differential privacy, showing that the effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d. They prove these bounds are tight by demonstrating that Warner's randomized response (with a minor modification) achieves the optimal rate. For density estimation under the same privacy constraints, they establish that the minimax rate for β-smooth densities degrades from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2), and provide simple, efficiently computable estimators (histogram and orthogonal series) that attain these rates.

The paper is of significant importance to the machine learning and statistics communities working on privacy-preserving data analysis. It resolves a fundamental question about the optimality of classical privacy-preserving techniques, demonstrating that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response method is indeed optimal for its intended purpose. The results provide a clear theoretical foundation for understanding the trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency, which is highly relevant to real-world applications like survey sampling and data collection.

The paper's strengths are numerous: it provides rigorous theoretical guarantees, demonstrates practical algorithms that achieve the optimal rates, and connects historical privacy techniques with modern theoretical frameworks. The results are well-supported by both lower and upper bounds, and the authors carefully distinguish between population estimation (their focus) and sample estimation (the focus of prior work), making their lower bounds stronger than previous results.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. The exposition of the minimax bounds is overly complex, particularly in Sections 2-3, where the authors employ a convoluted notation that obscures the main insights. The paper consistently uses l2 norm for all results, yet the notation is cumbersome and could be significantly simplified by directly stating bounds in l2 norm throughout. The authors also miss an opportunity to discuss how their results might extend to l1 norm, which would be particularly relevant for probability estimation problems where l1 error is often more meaningful than l2.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how their results relate to existing privacy literature. While the authors reference differential privacy and Warner's work, they could provide a clearer comparison to other differential privacy lower bounds (most of which focus on non-local settings) and discuss the implications of their results for broader privacy models beyond the tailored local-privacy framework they consider.

A significant new reservation arises from the observation that the results in Section 3 (on multinomial estimation) overlap substantially with a FOCS 2013 paper by Duchi et al. ("Local Privacy and Statistical Minimax Rates"), which was published just a few months before this NIPS submission. While the current paper extends the results to density estimation, the core minimax bounds for multinomial estimation appear in the earlier work. This overlap weakens the novelty of the paper, though the extension to density estimation and the clear exposition of the minimax framework still make it valuable.

The paper makes a strong case for the optimality of per-user independent randomization (like randomized response) by showing that it achieves the lower bound, but it could more explicitly address why conditioning on other users' data doesn't lead to better rates, which is an important point for the privacy community.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The clarity could be improved by simplifying the notation and exposition. The originality is somewhat diminished by the overlap with the FOCS 2013 paper, but the extension to density estimation and the comprehensive theoretical framework still provide novel contributions. The significance is high, as the results establish fundamental limits for privacy-preserving estimation that will inform both theoretical and practical work in the field.

In summary, this is an important paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of privacy-preserving statistics. While the novelty is somewhat reduced by the overlap with prior work, the paper's clarity on the optimality of randomized response and its extension to density estimation make it a strong contribution. The paper is recommended for acceptance, though the novelty is somewhat weaker than might be expected for a NIPS paper.
