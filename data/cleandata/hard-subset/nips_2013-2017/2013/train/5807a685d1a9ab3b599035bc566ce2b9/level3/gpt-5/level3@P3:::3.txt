Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It develops sharp minimax expected-error rates for two core problems: (i) estimating a discrete distribution over d categories, and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. The authors first provide information-theoretic lower bounds on minimax risk in the local model via new contraction inequalities that control divergences and mutual information after privatization. For discrete distributions, they give lower bounds for ℓ2 risk (and also discuss ℓ1), showing the effective sample size degrades from n to roughly nα^2/d when α is small. They then show that a simple per-user mechanism—Warner’s randomized response with a minor calibration—matches the lower bound, establishing optimality. For density estimation over Sobolev smoothness β, they prove that LDP changes the nonprivate rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, and they provide two simple, non-interactive, per-user mechanisms that achieve this rate: a Laplace-perturbed histogram for β=1 and an orthogonal series mechanism with coordinate-wise privatization for β≥1. The results quantify privacy–accuracy trade-offs cleanly and demonstrate that classic procedures (randomized response, histograms/series) are statistically optimal under LDP when properly tuned.

The work connects classical minimax theory (Le Cam, Fano, Assouad) and nonparametric estimation (e.g., Tsybakov) with differential privacy. Prior DP lower bounds mostly target the central model and sample/empirical accuracy; here the focus is population minimax risk in the local model. The paper complements Wasserman–Zhou (statistical framework for DP), Hardt–Talwar (geometry), and Smith (private estimation rates), and relates to deconvolution limits (Carroll–Hall) to argue why naive additive noise on X cannot attain optimal density rates. The core technical tools and several results overlap with the authors’ FOCS 2013 paper on local privacy and minimax rates; this submission expands achievability and applications to probabilities/densities.

Quality
- Technically strong: the lower bounds are derived via careful information-contraction under LDP and classical testing reductions. Upper bounds are constructive, per-user, non-interactive, and computationally simple.
- The discrete-case optimality of randomized response is crisply established; the density schemes are tailored and sharp, with clear comparisons showing why direct Laplace noise on X is suboptimal.
- Assumptions and constants/regimes (small α) are discussed; proofs appear sound and complete for the claims made.

Clarity
- The exposition of the general minimax framework is longer and heavier than needed for readers whose only interest is ℓ2 risk; the notation (ρ, Φ, packing, etc.) obscures the simplicity of the final rates, all of which are in ℓ2 (with one ℓ1 corollary).
- The paper is otherwise well-organized; the mechanisms and estimators are described concretely and are reproducible.

Originality
- The contraction bounds for LDP and the sharp rates in the local model are important. However, portions of the lower-bound framework and some rates overlap with the authors’ FOCS 2013 paper (notably Section 3). The current paper extends and simplifies the achievability story, especially for density estimation, but the novelty is somewhat reduced.

Significance
- High. Establishing exact local-privacy rates and the optimality of classical, simple procedures will influence both theory and practice, and clarifies the statistical price of local privacy versus central DP.

Strengths
- Sharp minimax lower bounds in the local model for discrete and density estimation.
- Simple, per-user, non-interactive mechanisms that match the lower bounds.
- Clear quantification of privacy–accuracy trade-offs; effective sample-size reductions are easy to interpret.
- Demonstrates optimality of randomized response, connecting modern LDP to Warner’s classic idea.

Weaknesses
- Exposition of the general minimax machinery and notation is more complex than necessary for the results presented.
- Limited discussion of ℓ1 risk beyond the multinomial case and of how the results relate to central-DP lower bounds.
- Results confined to a particular non-interactive local model; the paper could clarify the role (or limits) of interaction/shared randomness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Simplify the presentation of the lower bounds: state the main results directly in ℓ2 (and ℓ1 where applicable) with the high-level proof idea, moving general ρ–Φ machinery and variational bounds to the appendix.
- Streamline notation; since all main results use ℓ2, present bounds directly in ‖·‖2 and reserve generality for supplementary material.
- Add a short discussion comparing ℓ2 and ℓ1 risks. For multinomials you provide both; for densities, explain whether analogous ℓ1 or Hellinger rates are attainable or provably different under LDP.
- Situate the results relative to other DP lower bounds (mostly central model): explicitly contrast the dependence on α, d, and n, and clarify when local vs central models diverge.
- Broaden the scope discussion: consider interactive local protocols, shared randomness, pan-private/communication-constrained models, or heterogeneous per-user privacy (αi), and state what is known or conjectured about minimax rates there.
- Explicitly state the optimality of per-user independent randomization in your setting. While interactive or dependent schemes exist in the local model, clarify whether your lower bounds cover them and whether interaction could improve rates for these problems.
- Acknowledge and delineate the overlap with the FOCS 2013 paper (especially Section 3), and emphasize the genuinely new components here (e.g., the orthogonal series mechanism, tight constants/regimes, or new achievability proofs).

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is important and timely: it settles sharp minimax rates under local privacy for two canonical problems and shows that simple, classic mechanisms are optimal. The overlap with the authors’ FOCS 2013 paper reduces the novelty somewhat, so my enthusiasm is moderated, but the clarity on achievability and the density-estimation results make this a valuable NIPS contribution.

Confidence
High.
