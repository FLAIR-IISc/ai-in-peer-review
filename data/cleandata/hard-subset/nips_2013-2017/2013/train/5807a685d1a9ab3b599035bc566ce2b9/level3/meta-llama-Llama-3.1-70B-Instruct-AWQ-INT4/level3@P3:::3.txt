This paper studies local differential privacy with minimax expected-error rates for estimating probabilities and densities. The authors provide lower bounds on minimax rates for l2 distance with discrete distributions over d types and show that randomized response (each user perturbs only their own type) meets the lower bound, proving optimality. They also extend their results to density estimation with a simple per-user perturbation scheme achieving the lower bound.

The paper is well-written and provides a detailed study of the estimation of probability distributions in a stringent setting where data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors give sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. One of the consequences of their results is that Warner's classical work on randomized response is an optimal way to perform survey sampling while maintaining privacy of the respondents.

The paper's main contributions are the development of minimax lower bounds for probability estimation and density estimation under local differential privacy. The authors show that providing local privacy can sometimes be quite detrimental to the quality of statistical estimators. They also demonstrate that Warner's classical randomized response mechanism achieves the optimal convergence rate for multinomial estimation.

The paper's strengths include its technical soundness, clarity, and originality. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and derive sharp lower bounds on the convergence rates of any estimator. They also provide matching upper bounds that are achievable using computationally efficient algorithms. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their results.

One criticism of the paper is that the exposition of minimax bounds is overly complex and could be simplified. Additionally, the notation is cumbersome, and the bounds could be stated directly in l2 norm since all results use it. However, these are minor issues, and the paper's contributions outweigh its weaknesses.

In terms of significance, the paper's results are important and have implications for the field of statistics and machine learning. The authors show that local differential privacy can be achieved without sacrificing too much in terms of statistical efficiency. Their results provide a foundation for further research in this area and have the potential to impact the development of privacy-preserving statistical methods.

One suggestion for future work is to include a discussion or comparison to l1 norm results and other differential-privacy lower bounds (mostly non-local settings). Additionally, the authors could consider broader settings beyond the tailored local-privacy model and provide minimax lower bounds for them.

In conclusion, this paper is a significant contribution to the field of statistics and machine learning. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem of local differential privacy and derive sharp lower bounds on the convergence rates of any estimator. Their results have implications for the development of privacy-preserving statistical methods and provide a foundation for further research in this area. I recommend acceptance of this paper.
