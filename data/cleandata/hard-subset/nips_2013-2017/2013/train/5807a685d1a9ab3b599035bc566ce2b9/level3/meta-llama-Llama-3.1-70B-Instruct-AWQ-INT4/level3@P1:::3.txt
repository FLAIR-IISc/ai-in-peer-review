**Paper Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation**

The paper "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation" presents a comprehensive study of the estimation of probability distributions under local differential privacy constraints. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. This review summarizes the main ideas, discusses the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, and provides a list of arguments for and against acceptance.

**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper revisits Warner's classical problem of estimating the fractions of the population belonging to certain strata while maintaining respondent privacy. The authors extend this problem to a theoretical framework that allows for the characterization of optimal estimation under constraints on privacy. They study minimax convergence rates when the data provided satisfies the local privacy guarantee, which is a formal treatment of disclosure risk known as "differential privacy."

The main results of the paper quantify the penalty that must be paid when local privacy at a level α is provided in multinomial estimation and density estimation problems. The authors show that the effective sample size of any statistical estimation procedure decreases from n to nα^2/d whenever α is a sufficiently small constant. They also demonstrate that Warner's randomized response procedure enjoys optimal sample complexity.

**Strengths**

1. **Important contribution**: The paper makes an important contribution to the field of differential privacy, providing a theoretical framework for understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.
2. **Sharp rates**: The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation under local differential privacy constraints, which is a significant achievement.
3. **Optimality results**: The paper shows that classic procedures, such as randomized response, are optimal in certain settings, which is a valuable insight for practitioners.
4. **Broader implications**: The results have implications for other statistical problems, such as density estimation, and demonstrate the importance of considering privacy constraints in statistical inference.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Complex exposition**: The exposition of minimax bounds is overly complex and could be simplified to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
2. **Cumbersome notation**: The notation is cumbersome, and the bounds could be stated directly in l2 norm since all results use it.
3. **Limited discussion**: The paper could benefit from a more extensive discussion of the results, including comparisons to l1 norm results and other differential-privacy lower bounds.
4. **Overlap with existing work**: The results overlap or extend a recently published FOCS 2013 paper, which weakens the novelty of the paper.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**For Acceptance**

1. The paper makes an important contribution to the field of differential privacy.
2. The results are significant and have broader implications for statistical inference.
3. The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive study of the estimation of probability distributions under local differential privacy constraints.

**Against Acceptance**

1. The exposition is overly complex and could be simplified.
2. The notation is cumbersome and could be improved.
3. The paper could benefit from a more extensive discussion of the results.
4. The overlap with existing work weakens the novelty of the paper.

**Recommendation**

Despite the weaknesses, I recommend acceptance of the paper. The contribution is significant, and the results have important implications for statistical inference. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a comprehensive study of the estimation of probability distributions under local differential privacy constraints. However, I suggest that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above to improve the paper's clarity and impact.
