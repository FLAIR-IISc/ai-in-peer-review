This paper addresses the fundamental problem of statistical estimation under local differential privacy, providing minimax rates for both multinomial estimation and density estimation. The authors leverage Fano/Le Cam-style arguments to derive these bounds, demonstrating a clear trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency. A notable consequence is the optimality of Warner’s randomized response in a specific survey sampling context. The work is well-motivated, building on a long line of research in privacy and statistical inference, and attempts to bridge differential privacy with classical minimax theory.

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and clear characterization of the limitations imposed by local privacy. The derivation of minimax rates, particularly for density estimation, is a significant contribution. Establishing the optimality of randomized response is also a valuable result, reinforcing the enduring relevance of this classical technique. The exposition of the local privacy model, while building on prior work ([10]), is presented in a sufficiently self-contained manner to be accessible.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The commentary is occasionally imprecise, which could hinder understanding for readers less familiar with the technical details. For example, certain statements could benefit from more careful phrasing to avoid ambiguity. A major omission is the lack of a thorough connection to existing differential privacy literature. While differential privacy is mentioned, the paper doesn't adequately situate its “local privacy” framework within the broader landscape of privacy definitions and mechanisms. The distinction between local privacy and standard (global) differential privacy is crucial, and the paper sometimes blurs this line – the statement on p.4 is a prime example. The restriction to local privacy fundamentally alters the problem setting, and this difference needs to be consistently emphasized.

The necessity of the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is unclear. The authors state that alpha must be sufficiently small, but given experimental regimes where alpha ≈ 1 under differential privacy, the relevance of this restriction is questionable and deserves further discussion. A key request is for more detailed commentary on the choices of the rho (utility) measures used in the analysis. Illustrating how different utility functions impact the resulting bounds would strengthen the paper’s practical implications. 

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a comparison to Lei’s M-estimation density estimator [19]. This work is directly relevant and should be discussed, particularly concerning the trade-offs between different privacy-preserving estimation strategies. A more comprehensive comparison to other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods is also needed to demonstrate how randomized response and the proposed estimators perform relative to existing techniques.

Following the rebuttal, the authors acknowledged the need for clarity regarding population versus sample statistics and committed to addressing this in a revised version. This is a critical improvement, as the ambiguity in this regard hampered understanding. The inclusion of a comparison to Lei’s work and other relevant literature, also promised in the rebuttal, is vital for providing appropriate context for the contributions.

In summary, the core contribution of this paper is twofold: a clear exposition of the local privacy model (largely based on [10]) and the derivation of new minimax bounds for density estimation under this model. While the paper exhibits some weaknesses in clarity and contextualization, the theoretical results are significant and represent a valuable addition to the literature on privacy-preserving statistical inference.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and derivation of minimax rates.
*   Establishment of the optimality of randomized response.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem at the intersection of privacy and statistics.
*   Potential to inform the development of privacy-preserving statistical methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Imprecise commentary in places.
*   Insufficient connection to existing differential privacy literature.
*   Unclear relevance of the restriction on alpha.
*   Missing comparisons to related work (Lei’s estimator, other histogram methods).
