

This paper provides a rigorous minimax analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy, bridging classical survey methodology (Warner's randomized response) with modern differential privacy frameworks. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation over $d$ categories and nonparametric density estimation on $[0,1]$. For multinomial estimation, they prove that the minimax rate for $\ell_2$-error scales as $\min\{1, d/(n\alpha^2)\}$, demonstrating that local privacy reduces the effective sample size from $n$ to $n\alpha^2/d$. Crucially, they show Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism achieves this optimal rate, resolving a longstanding question about the optimality of privacy-preserving survey techniques. For density estimation with $\beta$-smooth densities, the minimax rate degrades from $n^{-2\beta/(2\beta+1)}$ (classical) to $(n\alpha^2)^{-2\beta/(2\beta+2)}$, with achievable rates via histogram and orthogonal series estimators matching the lower bounds.

**Strengths**  
The paper's primary strength lies in its technical precision and theoretical completeness. The minimax lower bounds (Theorems 1–2) are tight and derived using elegant information-theoretic tools (Proposition 1), avoiding the common pitfall of merely providing upper bounds. The optimality of Warner's mechanism is a significant historical and practical insight, demonstrating that a 50-year-old survey technique is statistically optimal under local privacy. The density estimation results (Propositions 2–3) are particularly compelling, showing that standard nonparametric methods can be adapted to local privacy with minimal rate degradation. The work directly addresses a critical gap in the literature: prior privacy literature focused on disclosure risk reduction without quantifying statistical efficiency trade-offs, while statistical decision theory lacked formal privacy constraints. The paper thus successfully integrates these domains, providing a quantitative framework for privacy-utility trade-offs.

**Weaknesses**  
The exposition suffers from excessive notational complexity, particularly in Sections 2–3. The minimax risk definition (Equation 2) and testing reductions (Lemma 1) are unnecessarily convoluted; the core results could be presented more accessibly by focusing directly on $\ell_2$-error from the outset, as all key bounds use this metric. The paper also fails to explicitly contrast its results with non-local differential privacy settings (e.g., [11, 16]), where privacy-utility trade-offs differ fundamentally due to central processing. While the authors reference related work, the connection to the recent FOCS 2013 paper by Duchi et al. [9] (which studies the same local privacy model) is not sufficiently distinguished. The FOCS paper establishes foundational divergence bounds (Proposition 1) and solves the multinomial case, making the current paper's novelty contingent on extending these to density estimation and providing matching upper bounds. This overlap weakens the originality claim, though the density extension remains valuable.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Provides the first sharp minimax rates for local privacy in probability estimation, resolving optimality of Warner's method.  
- Demonstrates practical applicability (survey sampling) and theoretical depth (density estimation rates).  
- Rigorous lower/upper bound matching, addressing a clear gap in privacy-statistics integration.  
- Highly relevant to NIPS' focus on statistical theory and privacy.  

*Con*:  
- Overly technical exposition obscures key insights; notation lacks clarity.  
- Novelty partially overlaps with Duchi et al. (FOCS 2013), though density results are new.  
- Fails to discuss $\ell_1$-error (used in survey contexts) or compare with non-local privacy rates.  
- Does not address broader settings (e.g., adaptive privacy mechanisms).  

**Significance and Recommendation**  
This work is significant for establishing a formal minimax framework for local privacy, moving beyond ad hoc privacy guarantees to quantify statistical costs. The optimality of randomized response alone makes it a landmark contribution to survey methodology. While the FOCS overlap slightly diminishes novelty, the density estimation extension and tight rate analysis provide substantial new value. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: technically sound (lower/upper bounds), clear in core results (despite notation issues), and significant for both theory and practice. The clarity shortcomings can be addressed in revision, but the core contributions warrant acceptance. The authors should streamline notation, explicitly compare with non-local privacy (e.g., [11]), and clarify the FOCS 2013 relationship to strengthen novelty. As a foundational piece linking privacy and minimax theory, it advances the field demonstrably and deserves publication. **Recommendation: Accept.**
