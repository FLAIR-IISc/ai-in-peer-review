## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates minimax estimation rates under the constraint of local differential privacy, a strong privacy model where data is perturbed *before* being shared with the statistician. The authors derive lower bounds for density and multinomial estimation, demonstrating inherent trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. A notable result is the confirmation of the optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism in certain survey sampling scenarios.

The work builds upon recent advances in differential privacy and connects them to classical minimax theory, a valuable contribution. It’s particularly significant to see these fundamental limits formally characterized in the challenging local privacy setting. The authors correctly identify the difference between local privacy and the more commonly studied differential privacy, and highlight how the restrictions imposed by local privacy lead to different convergence rates compared to the standard non-private case. The exploration of both multinomial and density estimation problems provides breadth and demonstrates the generality of their findings.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. While the core ideas are sound, the commentary is occasionally imprecise, potentially confusing for readers less familiar with the technical details. The connection to existing differential privacy literature feels somewhat underdeveloped. Although the authors acknowledge the broader field, a more explicit discussion of how their work relates to, and differs from, established results (e.g., in terms of privacy guarantees and achievable rates) would be beneficial. The authors’ assertion that their lower bounds are “stronger” than prior work feels unsupported without a more direct comparison. 

The restriction to local privacy necessitates a clear justification for why this model was chosen, and how it differs in practical applications compared to differential privacy. The necessity of the α restriction in Theorem 1 also remains unclear – a discussion of scenarios where α ≈ 1 under differential privacy would strengthen the argument. I also agree with previous reviewers that a comparison to Lei’s M-estimation density estimator and other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods would provide crucial context for the proposed methods. Finally, the population vs. sample statistics distinction needs more upfront clarification, as the current presentation obscures the implications of the bounds.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem: characterizing the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation.
*   Presents a clear exposition of the local privacy model (building on prior work [10]).
*   Provides novel and sharp minimax bounds for density estimation.
*   Confirms the optimality of a classical privacy-preserving mechanism (randomized response).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Imprecise wording in places hinders clarity.
*   Missing connection to broader differential privacy literature.
*   Insufficient justification for the choice of the local privacy model. 
*   Lack of empirical validation or comparison to alternative estimators.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity and contextualization issues outlined above. The contribution is valuable, providing a foundational theoretical understanding of estimation under strong privacy constraints. Addressing the weaknesses will significantly enhance the paper's impact and accessibility.
