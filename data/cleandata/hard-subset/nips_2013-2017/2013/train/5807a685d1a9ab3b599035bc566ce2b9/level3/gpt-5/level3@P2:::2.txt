Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies probability estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). It formalizes a private minimax risk—infimizing over both estimators and channels satisfying α-local DP—and derives sharp convergence rates for two canonical problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. The authors provide both lower bounds (via reductions to testing and new information-theoretic inequalities specialized to LDP) and matching upper bounds achieved by computationally efficient mechanisms.

For multinomial estimation over the d-simplex, the minimax mean-squared error scales as Θ(min{1, d/(nα2)}), with a corresponding ℓ1 rate Θ(min{1, d/√(nα2)}). Thus, relative to the nonprivate 1/n rate, LDP imposes an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d. The paper further shows that (a slightly modified) Warner randomized response is optimal under LDP for this task, establishing a direct bridge to classical survey methodology.

For density estimation over Sobolev balls Fβ on [0,1], the classical nonprivate minimax rate n−2β/(2β+1) is unattainable under LDP; instead the optimal rate becomes (nα2)−2β/(2β+2). Matching upper bounds are achieved by two constructive mechanisms: (i) privatized histograms (β=1), and (ii) a locally private orthogonal series scheme that uses a carefully designed randomized sign-and-threshold mechanism rather than simple Laplace perturbation. The paper also explains why direct (Laplace) perturbation of the raw samples cannot achieve the optimal rate by appealing to deconvolution limits.

This work relates to a line of DP lower bounds for linear queries and geometric noise-complexity trade-offs (e.g., Hardt & Talwar 2010; De 2012; Smith 2011). It differs in that it studies population (not sample) risk and focuses on the stronger local privacy model. The results complement earlier DP optimality work by giving sharp, task-specific population risks and constructive optimal mechanisms under LDP. A clearer comparison to prior linear-query lower bounds would further contextualize the multinomial results.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous formulation of LDP minimax risk with broadly useful information bounds.
- Sharp lower and upper bounds for two central problems; the density result (rate change in the exponent) is novel and insightful.
- Constructive, efficient mechanisms that attain the lower bounds; especially the optimality of randomized response for multinomials is an appealing and practical message.
- Good connection between privacy mechanism design and classical statistical decision theory, with tools likely to generalize.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Relation to known DP lower bounds for linear queries/noise complexity: Please expand the discussion to situate the multinomial rates vis-à-vis Hardt & Talwar (2010), De (2012), and related STOC’13 results (e.g., Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang) on mean-squared error for linear queries. Clarify in what sense the LDP bounds strictly strengthen or are incomparable to those in the central DP model, and whether any of those techniques could imply parts of your lower bounds.
- Basis/Sobolev class specification: The density lower bound is proven for Sobolev classes defined via the trigonometric basis. While you cite standard equivalences, it would help to (i) make explicit that the comparison to the nonprivate rate (Eq. 13) is on the same function class, and (ii) state whether your lower bound extends to general (equivalent) definitions of Sobolev balls and boundary conditions.
- Small-α regime: Since rates scale in α2, a brief discussion of constants and practical regimes (e.g., how small α must be before the asymptotics become visible) would aid practitioners.
- Noninteractive vs interactive LDP: You focus on noninteractive (memoryless) channels. Please comment on whether interaction could improve rates for these problems, or cite known separations.
- Orthogonal-series mechanism details: The sampling scheme involves constants (ck, B) and rejection sampling. Providing an explicit bound on expected runtime and a short recipe for choosing k (possibly data-driven) would improve reproducibility.
- Minor: The statement “Laplace perturbation can’t attain the optimal density-estimation rate” is convincing via deconvolution tails; adding a short, self-contained argument sketch would be helpful to readers unfamiliar with [4].

Quality
Technically sound, with careful use of minimax testing reductions and new LDP-tailored information bounds. Claims are supported by matching constructions and by negative results explaining why naive noise addition fails. Some proofs are deferred, but the outline is clear.

Clarity
Well written and well organized. Definitions and assumptions are explicit. Two places could be clarified: (i) the basis dependence of Fβ and its equivalence to the standard Sobolev ball; (ii) a succinct comparison table contrasting nonprivate vs LDP rates and mechanisms.

Originality
High. The sharp minimax characterization under LDP, especially the change in polynomial rate for density estimation and the optimality of specific local mechanisms, appears new and important.

Significance
High. The results should influence both the theory of private statistical estimation and the design of practical LDP data collection mechanisms. The tools (information bounds under LDP) seem broadly applicable.

Arguments for acceptance
- Sharp, conceptually clean results answering fundamental questions under LDP.
- Matching lower/upper bounds with simple, efficient mechanisms.
- Novel insights for nonparametric estimation under privacy.
- Clear writing and potential for impact.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete discussion of related DP noise-complexity lower bounds and how the results compare/improve.
- Basis-specific presentation for density lower bounds could be broadened/clarified.

Overall recommendation
Accept. Strong theoretical contribution with practical implications; a fuller treatment of related DP lower bounds and an explicit statement about basis/general Sobolev classes would further strengthen the final version.

Reviewer confidence: High.
