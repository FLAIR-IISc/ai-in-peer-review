

**Review for "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors provide sharp minimax bounds for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems, demonstrating how privacy constraints degrade statistical performance. They show that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation, and they provide efficient algorithms achieving the lower bounds for both problems. By connecting classical privacy work with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory, the paper establishes quantitative trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds. The authors provide matching upper and lower bounds for minimax rates under local privacy constraints, establishing sharp convergence rates. The analysis is comprehensive, with careful attention to the trade-offs between privacy level and statistical efficiency.

*Significance:* The results are highly significant as they provide concrete quantification of the privacy-utility trade-off, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response remains optimal for survey sampling despite recent focus on privacy. This insight is valuable for both practitioners designing privacy-preserving statistical methods and researchers studying theoretical foundations of differential privacy. The extension to density estimation with sharp minimax rates represents a meaningful contribution beyond previous work.

*Originality:* The paper connects classical privacy work (Warner [23]) with modern differential privacy in a novel way, providing new theoretical insights. The density estimation results extend the scope beyond previous work, offering a more complete picture of the privacy-utility trade-off across different statistical problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*Clarity:* The exposition of minimax bounds is overly complex with cumbersome notation. The paper uses multiple representations of similar concepts (e.g., different notations for the minimax rate), making it difficult to follow. The authors could simplify by stating all results directly in terms of the L2 norm, which is the primary metric used throughout the paper.

*Novelty:* There is significant overlap with Duchi et al.'s FOCS 2013 paper "Local Privacy and Statistical Minimax Rates," particularly in Section 3 on multinomial estimation. While the current paper extends to density estimation, this overlap weakens the novelty claim. The authors should more explicitly address this relationship with prior work and clarify the distinct contributions of their paper.

*Scope:* The paper focuses on a specific model of local privacy where each user's perturbation is independent of others. It would be valuable to explicitly address why this model is optimal despite the availability of more complex schemes that condition on other users' data.

**Specific Suggestions:**

1. Simplify the exposition by using a more direct notation for the L2 norm throughout the paper.

2. Explicitly address the relationship with the FOCS 2013 paper and clarify the novel contributions of this work.

3. Include a brief discussion comparing L1 and L2 results, as well as how these results relate to non-local differential privacy lower bounds.

4. Explicitly state why per-user independent randomization is optimal, despite the availability of more complex schemes that condition on other users' data.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Provides sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, filling a significant gap in the literature.
- Demonstrates that Warner's randomized response is optimal, a valuable theoretical insight with practical implications.
- Results are important for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation.
- Technically sound with rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds.

*Con:*
- Overlap with FOCS 2013 paper significantly weakens novelty claims.
- Exposition is overly complex with cumbersome notation that could be simplified.
- Does not address why independent user perturbation is optimal compared to more complex schemes.
- Could better contextualize results within the broader literature on differential privacy.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that provides important insights into the privacy-utility trade-off in statistical estimation. While the novelty is somewhat weakened by overlap with prior work, the paper makes valuable contributions to our understanding of local privacy, particularly with its extension to density estimation. The results are significant for both theory and practice, as they demonstrate that classical privacy techniques remain optimal in modern settings. With revisions to clarify the novelty and simplify the exposition, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, though the novelty is somewhat reduced by the FOCS 2013 overlap. The paper makes an important contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation.
