Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies estimation under local (non-interactive) differential privacy (LDP). The setting assumes each user privatizes her datum Xi via a memoryless channel Q that satisfies α-local DP and the analyst only observes Zi ~ Q(·|Xi). The authors develop a minimax decision-theoretic framework to quantify the statistical price of local privacy and derive sharp (matching) lower and upper bounds for two core problems:

- Multinomial (discrete) probability estimation over d categories: The α-private minimax mean-squared error scales as min{1, d/(nα^2)}. Equivalently, privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to roughly nα^2/d for small α. They show that Warner’s classical randomized response is rate-optimal (with a simple projection step), as is an appropriately calibrated Laplace-perturbation scheme.

- Nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1], measured in L2 risk: The optimal rate degrades from the classical n^{-2β/(2β+1)} (no privacy) to (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under LDP. They prove lower bounds via Le Cam/Fano arguments combined with new information-contraction inequalities under local privacy and give matching upper bounds using (i) a privatized histogram for β=1 and (ii) a locally private orthogonal series estimator for β≥1. They also show that naive Laplace-perturbation of raw data cannot attain the optimal rate (via deconvolution limits).

The work connects classical survey methodology (Warner 1965) with modern DP (Dwork et al.) and nonparametric minimax theory (Tsybakov; Yang and Barron), and relates to prior LDP/learning results (Kasiviswanathan et al.), the geometry of DP (Hardt and Talwar), statistical DP frameworks (Wasserman and Zhou), and lower bounds (Smith; De). The information bounds used to control KL/mutual information under LDP build on the authors’ earlier work.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds are derived via carefully tailored packing constructions and information-contraction under α-LDP; the upper bounds match with simple, implementable mechanisms. The arguments appear correct and complete for the stated model (non-interactive local channels).
- Completeness: Both parametric (multinomial) and nonparametric (Sobolev) regimes are covered, with tight rates and constructive procedures. The negative deconvolution argument for sample perturbation is insightful and strengthens the case for the proposed mechanisms.
- Empirical aspects are not central here; this is a theory paper and the results are self-contained and reproducible from the descriptions.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written but dense. The notation and some derivations (e.g., the variational information bound and its use inside Fano) could be streamlined. Stating the main rates early (both L2 and L1 for multinomial) and using a running summary table of regimes (d, n, α, β) would improve readability. The orthogonal-series mechanism would benefit from a concise algorithmic summary.

Originality
The synthesis of minimax theory with local DP to produce sharp rates and optimal mechanisms is impactful. That said, parts of Section 3 (discrete estimation under LDP) overlap with recent FOCS’13 work on minimax rates under local privacy; the novelty there is incremental. The density estimation results and the identification of mechanism-dependent optimality (beyond direct Laplace perturbation) are, to my knowledge, new and significant.

Significance
The paper cleanly quantifies the privacy–utility trade-off under LDP and identifies simple optimal mechanisms (notably, randomized response). The results will influence both theory (benchmarks for LDP) and practice (guidance on mechanism choice). The density estimation rates and mechanisms broaden the scope of LDP beyond discrete problems and are likely to be built upon.

Pros
- Sharp, matching minimax rates under LDP for two canonical problems.
- Elegant information-contraction tools under local privacy; clear use of Le Cam/Fano.
- Simple, implementable optimal mechanisms (randomized response; privatized histograms; private orthogonal series).
- Clarifies when naive perturb-then-estimate is suboptimal.

Cons
- Exposition is heavy; notation could be simplified and results highlighted earlier.
- Overlap with recent FOCS’13 results for discrete estimation reduces novelty in that part.
- Scope is restricted to non-interactive, per-user independent channels; optimality relative to interactive or correlated local mechanisms is not discussed in depth.

Suggestions for improvement
- Simplify notation and presentation: emphasize the main rate statements upfront; confine technical lemmas to an appendix; include a short algorithmic summary of the sampling schemes.
- Expand discussion of L1 metrics: you already give L1 bounds for multinomials—briefly discuss L1 risk for densities or explain why L2 is the focus.
- Situate results against broader DP lower bounds beyond local models (e.g., database DP lower bounds) and highlight why the local setting is qualitatively different.
- Clarify scope and optimality claims: explicitly state that the upper bounds are attained with per-user independent, non-interactive randomization, and discuss whether interaction or dependence across users could help in your settings (or cite results showing no/minor gains).
- Add a concise comparison to contemporaneous FOCS’13 work on LDP minimax rates, clearly delineating what is new here (especially for density estimation and mechanism design).

Minor comments
- Throughout, remind the reader that e^α−1 ≈ α for small α to interpret effective sample size reductions.
- Provide constants’ dependence where feasible, or summarize them qualitatively.
- A small figure/table summarizing rates across problems/metrics would aid comprehension.

Overall recommendation
Accept. Despite some overlap in the discrete case, the paper provides a clear, rigorous, and broadly relevant account of minimax rates under local privacy, identifies optimal mechanisms (notably validating randomized response), and extends the theory to nonparametric density estimation with sharp rates and constructive procedures. The contributions are significant and of interest to the NIPS community.
