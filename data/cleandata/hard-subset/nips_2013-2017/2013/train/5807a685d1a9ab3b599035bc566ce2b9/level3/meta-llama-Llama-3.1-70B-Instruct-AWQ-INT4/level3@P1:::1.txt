**Paper Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation**

**Summary**

The paper presents a study on the estimation of probability distributions under local privacy constraints. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. They also propose tools to allow movement along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum. The paper focuses on two main results: the first characterizes the minimax rate of estimation of a multinomial probability mass function, and the second exhibits an interesting departure from standard minimax estimation results in the context of density estimation.

**Strengths**

1. **Characterization of limitations of learning from data perturbed to guarantee privacy**: The paper provides a clear exposition of the local privacy model and derives lower bounds for estimation when the algorithm is untrusted by the data holder. This is a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
2. **New bounds for density estimation**: The paper presents new bounds for density estimation under local privacy constraints, which is an important problem in nonparametric statistics.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Imprecise commentary in places**: The paper's commentary on the connection to existing differential privacy results and literature is sometimes imprecise, which could confuse casual readers.
2. **Restriction to local privacy**: The paper's focus on local privacy makes the model different from differential privacy, and the authors sometimes overstate or blur this distinction.
3. **Unclear relevance of restriction on alpha**: The paper's restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is unclear, and its relevance is not well-explained, particularly in experimental regimes where alpha ≈ 1 under differential privacy.
4. **Lack of comparison to other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods**: The paper does not provide a comparison to other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods to show how they perform relative to randomized response.
5. **Missing citation/comparison to Lei's M-estimation density estimator**: The paper does not discuss Lei's work on M-estimation density estimators, which is an important related work.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

1. **Clarify the connection to existing differential privacy results and literature**: The authors should provide a more precise commentary on the connection to existing differential privacy results and literature to avoid confusing casual readers.
2. **Explain the relevance of the restriction on alpha**: The authors should provide a clear explanation of the relevance of the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1, particularly in experimental regimes where alpha ≈ 1 under differential privacy.
3. **Provide a comparison to other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods**: The authors should provide a comparison to other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods to show how they perform relative to randomized response.
4. **Discuss Lei's work on M-estimation density estimators**: The authors should discuss Lei's work on M-estimation density estimators and compare their results to Lei's work.
5. **Explain population vs. sample statistics more clearly and earlier in the paper**: The authors should explain the distinction between population and sample statistics more clearly and earlier in the paper to avoid confusion.

**Originality**

The paper presents a new and original contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The authors' use of Fano/LeCam-style arguments to derive lower bounds for estimation when the algorithm is untrusted by the data holder is a novel approach. The paper's focus on local privacy and its application to density estimation is also original.

**Significance**

The paper's contribution is significant, as it provides a clear exposition of the local privacy model and derives new bounds for density estimation under local privacy constraints. The paper's results have important implications for the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference and estimation procedures.

**Rating**

I would rate this paper 8/10. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference, but it has some weaknesses that need to be addressed. With some revisions to address the weaknesses, the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field.
