Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies statistical estimation when each user applies a local, non-interactive α-differentially private channel to their data before release. Using Fano/Le Cam-style information-theoretic arguments specialized to locally private channels, the authors derive minimax lower bounds and matching upper bounds for two canonical problems: discrete multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation. A notable corollary is that Warner’s randomized response is optimal, in a precise minimax sense, for multinomial estimation. For density estimation over Sobolev classes, the paper shows a qualitative shift in the rate: the classical n−2β/(2β+1) becomes (nα2)−2β/(2β+2) under local privacy, and provides histogram (β=1) and orthogonal-series (β≥1) procedures achieving the lower bounds.

The work connects classical decision-theoretic tools (Le Cam, Fano) with privacy via new “information contraction” inequalities that bound KL/mutual information after privatization, providing a clean route to lower bounds. It is positioned against prior DP work on accuracy of released sample statistics (e.g., Dwork et al., Smith, Hardt–Talwar, Wasserman–Zhou) and the local model from the theoretical CS literature (e.g., Kasiviswanathan et al.; Beimel et al.). Randomized response emerges as an optimal mechanism for discrete data, and the paper highlights that naive Laplace perturbation of raw samples is suboptimal for density estimation due to deconvolution barriers.

I did not examine the supplementary material.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds are carefully constructed via packings and mutual-information bounds tailored to α-local channels; the upper bounds are constructive and computationally simple. The multinomial rates are tight in ℓ2 and ℓ1, and the density results include mechanisms that match the lower bounds. The arguments appear sound and useful beyond the specific problems treated.
- Assumptions and regimes: Theorem 1 is stated for α∈[0,1] with “sufficiently small constant” caveats in the discussion. Please clarify whether the α≤1 restriction is essential to the n→nα2/d effective-sample-size phenomenon, and whether constants change or rates break for α≈1–3, which are common in DP practice. A brief discussion of how the bounds scale outside the small-α regime would greatly aid applicability.
- Utility/loss choices: The framework allows general Φ∘ρ, but the paper focuses on ℓ2/ℓ1 for multinomials and integrated L2 for densities. It would help to add guidance on choosing ρ: e.g., do TV or Hellinger risks for discrete distributions yield the same rates and mechanisms? For densities, what changes under L1 or Hellinger loss? A short subsection mapping common utilities to your analysis would increase practical value.
- Comparisons: For discrete data, please provide a direct comparison (theoretical bounds and, if possible, a small simulation) between randomized response and alternative locally private mechanisms (e.g., adding Laplace noise to one-hot encodings or to aggregate counts) under ℓ1 and ℓ2 risk, to solidify the optimality narrative beyond order-of-magnitude rates.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, with helpful background on minimax testing and the local privacy model. However:
  - The distinction between population versus sample (data-dependent) targets is crucial and should be explained very early (Section 1 or 2), including the consequences for interpreting lower bounds. The current discussion (bottom of p.4) can be misread as overstating strength relative to prior DP lower bounds; please temper and clarify this point.
  - The paper sometimes blurs local privacy with the (more common) central DP model. Because the restriction to local privacy is substantial and leads to different rates and mechanisms, please make this distinction explicit throughout and avoid phrasing that might imply transferability of your bounds to central DP. The statement at the bottom of p.4 is one place where extra care would help.
  - A brief, consolidated discussion of ρ, Φ, and how different utilities affect both lower and upper bounds would improve readability.

Originality
- The contribution is twofold: (i) a clear and accessible exposition of the local privacy model and how to analyze it via minimax testing (material largely contained in [10]/arXiv), and (ii) genuinely new and sharp bounds for density estimation under local privacy, including mechanisms that achieve the new rates. The discrete results (optimality of randomized response) are conceptually satisfying, while the nonparametric results are novel and important.

Significance
- The results are significant: they precisely characterize what is and is not possible under local privacy, quantifying the privacy–utility trade-off, and will influence both mechanism design and expectations for private learning. The finding that local privacy changes the minimax exponent for densities is especially consequential.

Missing connections and related work
- Please strengthen connections to: Kasiviswanathan et al. (local model and SQ), Wasserman–Zhou (statistical DP framework), Smith (optimal rates for private estimation), Hardt–Talwar (geometry of DP), and De (lower bounds). The paper mentions some of these but could better position its population-risk results relative to central DP lower bounds.
- Lei’s differentially private M-estimators and density estimation: the current brief mention would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how Lei’s approach compares in assumptions (central vs local), rates, and practicality, and why it is suboptimal or inapplicable in the local model.

Pros
- Clean, general technique (Fano/Le Cam with privacy-contraction).
- Sharp, interpretable rates for multinomial and density estimation.
- Optimality and simplicity of randomized response for discrete data.
- Constructive mechanisms with matching upper bounds.

Cons
- Occasional imprecision that may confuse readers about local vs central DP and population vs sample targets.
- Limited discussion of how alternative utilities fit the framework.
- α-regime and constants insufficiently discussed for typical DP settings.
- Related work section needs deeper engagement (Lei and broader DP literature).

Questions for the authors
- Is the α≤1 restriction in Theorem 1 essential? How do your rates/constants behave for α≈1–3?
- For multinomial estimation, do TV or Hellinger losses change the rate or the optimality of randomized response?
- For densities, what are the rates under L1/Hellinger risk in the local model?
- Can you include a direct comparison between randomized response and Laplace-to-counts mechanisms for histograms?

After rebuttal
- Please clarify population vs sample targets earlier and more explicitly.
- Expand the related-work discussion (Lei and others cited above) to better contextualize your contributions.

Recommendation
Strong paper with high technical quality and significant results. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the modeling distinctions, discussing α-regimes and utilities, and strengthening related-work comparisons.
