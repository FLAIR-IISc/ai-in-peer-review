- Paper studies minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, establishing sharp rates for both multinomial and density estimation.  
- Key result: local privacy reduces effective sample size in multinomial estimation by a factor ~α².  
- Density estimation rate changes under local privacy, giving a different polynomial convergence than the non-private case.  
- Connects Warner’s randomized response to modern differential privacy and minimax theory, showing randomized response achieves optimal complexity.  
- Well-motivated and historically grounded, building on Warner (1965) and subsequent privacy/statistics literature.  
- Technical approach is sound, using minimax decision theory and a formal likelihood-ratio-based definition of local privacy.  
- Propositions 2 and 3 provide concrete algorithms that achieve the stated bounds.  
- Major weakness: insufficient discussion of related work on noise complexity and differential privacy lower bounds (e.g., Hardt & Talwar 2010; De 2012).  
- Authors should clarify whether their local-DP bounds improve upon or relate to known differential-DP lower bounds for linear queries.  
- Density estimation comparison lacks nuance: lower bound is derived for a trigonometric basis, so the non-private rate comparison requires explicit function-class assumptions (e.g., Sobolev).  
- Initial omission of Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (2013) was noted; it should be cited and discussed.  
- Strengths: rigorous mathematics, novel and important results, clear writing, and meaningful connection between classical and modern privacy methods.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical contributions, sharp rates for both problems, rigorous analysis, and demonstration of randomized-response optimality.  
- Arguments against acceptance: inadequate engagement with DP noise-complexity literature, potential ambiguity in density-rate comparisons, and missing related work.  
- Overall recommendation: accept conditioned on addressing criticisms—explicitly discuss relevant differential privacy literature and clarify function-class assumptions for density comparisons.
