This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the statistical cost of local differential privacy. The authors study the minimax rates of convergence for two fundamental statistical problems: estimating the parameters of a multinomial distribution and nonparametric density estimation. The main contribution is a set of sharp upper and lower bounds that precisely characterize the trade-off between the level of privacy (α) and statistical efficiency. For multinomial estimation, the paper shows that local privacy reduces the effective sample size by a factor of approximately α²/d, and demonstrates that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev spaces, the paper reveals a more severe penalty: local privacy changes the polynomial rate of convergence, degrading the exponent from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2). The work connects the original motivation for privacy in surveys (Warner, 1965) with the modern framework of differential privacy and the tools of classical minimax decision theory.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs, which build upon the authors' previous information-theoretic bounds relating privacy to statistical divergence, appear correct and lead to sharp, matching upper and lower bounds. The work is complete, providing not just lower bounds on what is possible, but also constructive and computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these bounds. The analysis of both a parametric (multinomial) and a nonparametric (density estimation) problem demonstrates the broad applicability of the framework.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem setting is introduced clearly, and the main results are stated precisely. The authors do a good job of providing the necessary background on the minimax framework and local differential privacy, making the paper accessible. The distinction between their work on population parameter estimation and prior work on sample quantity estimation is also helpfully noted. One point that could be clarified is in the density estimation section. The lower bound is derived for a Sobolev class defined via a specific trigonometric basis. To make the comparison fully transparent, the authors should explicitly state whether the classical non-private rate cited in Eq. (13) applies to the exact same function class or to a more general one. The comparison is only meaningful if the underlying classes are identical.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While there has been significant research on differential privacy, much of it has focused on the central model or on computational aspects. This paper is one of the first to provide a sharp, information-theoretic characterization of the minimax statistical risk under the stringent constraint of *local* privacy. The result that local privacy fundamentally alters the convergence rate for density estimation is novel and surprising.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its positioning with respect to related work on lower bounds in differential privacy from the theoretical computer science community. The authors do not cite or discuss key papers on noise-complexity lower bounds, such as those by Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012). These papers establish lower bounds on the error for answering linear queries under (central) differential privacy. Since multinomial estimation can be viewed as estimating the means of indicator variables (a special case of linear queries), the relationship between the bounds in this paper and these prior results needs to be explicitly discussed. A clarification of how the local-privacy setting differs from and strengthens these existing bounds is essential. Furthermore, the recent work of Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013), which provides tight bounds for mean-squared error, is also highly relevant and should be cited.

**Significance:** The results are of high significance to both the machine learning and statistics communities. The paper provides a fundamental and quantitative understanding of the price of achieving local privacy. This is increasingly important as data collection becomes more distributed. The finding that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is optimal is a beautiful and impactful result. The novel rate for density estimation provides a crucial benchmark for future algorithm design in private nonparametric inference. Researchers and practitioners looking to implement private statistical procedures will find the sharp trade-offs identified in this paper invaluable for understanding the fundamental limits they face.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

*   **Strengths:**
    *   Provides the first sharp minimax analysis for canonical statistical problems under local differential privacy.
    *   The results are fundamental, revealing that privacy can sometimes just reduce the effective sample size (multinomial) and other times fundamentally change the convergence rate (density estimation).
    *   Technically strong, with matching upper and lower bounds. The upper bounds are constructive and efficient.
    *   Clearly written and well-motivated.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   Fails to cite and discuss a highly relevant line of work on noise-complexity lower bounds for differential privacy from the theoretical computer science literature (e.g., Hardt & Talwar 2010; De 2012; Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang 2013).
    *   As a result of the missing citations, the novelty and relationship of the multinomial estimation bound to existing results for linear queries under central DP is unclear.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper presenting important, novel, and technically sound results on a fundamental problem. The main drawback is the inadequate discussion of related prior work on differential privacy lower bounds. Addressing this point by adding the relevant citations and a discussion clarifying the relationship between the local-privacy bounds presented here and the existing central-privacy bounds would significantly strengthen the paper.
