### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic study of statistical estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors focus on two fundamental problems: estimating discrete probability distributions (multinomial estimation) and continuous probability densities. The main contribution is the derivation of sharp minimax lower bounds for the expected squared l2-error in these settings. The paper shows that these bounds quantify a fundamental trade-off between the level of privacy (α) and statistical accuracy.

For estimating a discrete distribution over `d` categories from `n` samples, the paper establishes that the minimax rate scales as `d / (n * α^2)`, effectively reducing the sample size by a factor of `α^2 / d`. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that this lower bound is achieved by Warner's classic randomized response mechanism, proving its minimax optimality nearly 50 years after its introduction.

For density estimation of functions with `β` degrees of smoothness, the paper shows that local privacy changes the exponent of the convergence rate. The classical non-private rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` degrades to `(n * α^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This bound is also shown to be sharp, with the paper providing an achievable upper bound using a privatized histogram estimator (for β=1) and an orthogonal series estimator for general `β`.

The work connects the classical statistical tool of minimax analysis with the modern computer science notion of differential privacy, placing a foundational problem from the privacy literature on firm statistical ground.

### Strengths

1.  **Importance and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem: understanding the ultimate statistical limits of what can be learned under local privacy constraints. Establishing sharp minimax rates is a significant contribution that provides a precise, quantitative understanding of the price of privacy for basic estimation tasks. These results are likely to be widely cited and built upon by researchers in privacy, statistics, and machine learning.

2.  **Proving Optimality of Simple Mechanisms:** A major strength of this work is that it doesn't just provide abstract lower bounds. It proves that simple, practical, and well-known mechanisms are actually optimal. The result that randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly elegant and impactful. This provides strong theoretical justification for using these simple methods in practice.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs leverage established tools from information theory and minimax theory (e.g., Le Cam's and Fano's methods) and adapt them effectively to the local privacy setting. The construction of estimators that match the lower bounds completes the analysis, making this a well-rounded and complete piece of theoretical work.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Exposition:** While technically correct, the exposition of the minimax framework and the derivation of the lower bounds in Section 2 is overly complex and dense. The general formulation involving `Φ o ρ` is notationally heavy, especially since all subsequent results focus specifically on the squared l2 norm (`||.||_2^2`). The key results could be stated more directly in terms of the l2 norm from the outset, which would make the paper more accessible without sacrificing rigor. Simplifying the presentation of the information-theoretic bounds would significantly improve readability.

2.  **Limited Scope and Contextualization:**
    *   The paper would be strengthened by a discussion comparing the l2 rates with rates for other natural distances, such as the l1 norm (total variation distance), which is often used for probability distributions.
    *   The authors should provide more context by comparing their *local* privacy lower bounds to existing bounds in the *central* differential privacy model (e.g., from Wasserman & Zhou 2010 or Hardt & Talwar 2010). This would help readers understand the specific cost incurred by the local model, where no trusted curator exists.
    *   The analysis is tailored to a specific non-interactive, memoryless channel model of local privacy. It would be valuable to discuss whether these techniques or results could extend to broader settings, or to provide minimax lower bounds for them.

3.  **Understated Implication:** A key implication of the results is that mechanisms using independent randomization for each user are minimax optimal. This is a powerful and non-obvious conclusion, as one might hypothesize that more complex schemes where the randomization for user `i` depends on the (private) data from users `1, ..., i-1` could improve accuracy. The authors should explicitly state and emphasize this optimality result, as it is a strong justification for simple, decentralized privacy mechanisms.

### Originality and Novelty

The approach of using minimax decision theory to analyze local privacy is powerful and provides novel insights. However, there is a significant concern regarding the novelty of the results presented in Section 3. The work on multinomial estimation appears to be a direct extension or concurrent publication of the authors' own work that recently appeared at FOCS 2013. While the density estimation results in Section 4 appear to be new and are a substantial contribution in their own right, the overlap with a very recent top-tier publication weakens the novelty of the paper as a whole for a NIPS audience. The authors should clarify the specific contributions of this manuscript relative to their FOCS paper.

### Recommendation

This is a strong, technically sound paper that addresses an important problem at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy. The results establishing sharp minimax rates and proving the optimality of classical methods are significant and of high interest to the NIPS community.

However, the weaknesses in exposition and, more importantly, the significant overlap of the multinomial results with a recent FOCS 2013 paper temper my enthusiasm. The contribution remains valuable, particularly the novel results on density estimation, but the overall novelty is less than it would otherwise appear.

Despite these reservations, the paper's strengths and the importance of its contributions lead me to recommend acceptance.

**Final assessment: Accept (with reservations about novelty).**
