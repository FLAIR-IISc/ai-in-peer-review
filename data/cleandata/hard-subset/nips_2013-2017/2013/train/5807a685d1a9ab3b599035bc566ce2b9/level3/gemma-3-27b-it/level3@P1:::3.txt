This paper provides a detailed study of probability estimation—both discrete and continuous—under the stringent constraint of local differential privacy. The authors establish sharp minimax rates of convergence, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency and offering tools to navigate this continuum. A key result is the confirmation that Warner’s randomized response method is, in fact, optimal for survey sampling while preserving respondent privacy.

The work builds upon a large body of research on privacy and statistical inference, referencing key contributions from Warner [23], Dwork et al. [11], and others. It distinguishes itself by formally integrating the disclosure risk treatment of differential privacy with minimax decision theory, a previously unexplored connection. The introduction effectively contextualizes the research, linking historical motivations for privacy (Warner’s original work on survey sampling) with modern cryptographic concerns.  The formulation of local privacy, building on Evfimievski et al. [13], and the subsequent analysis using differential privacy’s formal guarantees, is a central contribution.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. First, the theoretical rigor is commendable. The derivation of minimax rates and the accompanying lower and upper bounds are clearly presented (although, as discussed below, somewhat complex in their exposition). The optimality result for randomized response is particularly compelling, demonstrating that a decades-old technique remains state-of-the-art even within a rigorous theoretical framework. Second, the extension of these results to nonparametric density estimation is significant, revealing a different polynomial rate of convergence compared to classical results. Third, the efficient algorithms provided alongside the lower bounds suggest practical applicability of the findings. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach, honestly evaluating both strengths and weaknesses. Finally, the work has high potential significance, offering a foundational understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs with relevance to practitioners and researchers alike.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. The exposition of the minimax framework and the associated Lemma 1 and Proposition 1 (Section 2) can be quite dense. While technically correct, simplifying this presentation would substantially improve readability and accessibility for a broader audience. The extensive use of notation, particularly in Section 2, is also cumbersome. While understandable given the technical nature of the work, directly stating bounds in terms of the l2 norm (since all results ultimately rely on it) could streamline the presentation. 

The authors address related work effectively, but a more detailed discussion comparing their results to other differential privacy lower bounds – particularly those developed in non-local privacy settings – would be beneficial.  Similarly, exploring the implications for other distance metrics (e.g., the l1 norm) would broaden the scope and impact. Expanding the scope to consider minimax lower bounds for broader settings beyond the tailored local-privacy model would also enhance the paper’s generality. 

I request clarification regarding the optimality of per-user independent randomization. Specifically, it would be valuable to explicitly state that this strategy is optimal despite the potential existence of schemes that might condition on data from other users, thus reinforcing the practical appeal of randomized response.

Furthermore, the authors acknowledge a potential overlap with a recently published paper at FOCS 2013 (Section 3), indicating a weakening of the novelty. While the overlap is acknowledged and doesn’t invalidate the contributions entirely, it does necessitate caution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundations and rigorous analysis.
*   Important result demonstrating the optimality of Warner’s randomized response.
*   Extension of results to density estimation and identification of new trade-offs.
*   High potential significance and relevance to the research community.
*   Clear articulation of limitations and avenues for future work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Complex exposition of minimax framework.
*   Cumbersome notation.
*   Potential overlap with recent FOCS 2013 publication.
*   Limited discussion of connections to other differential privacy work.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses concerning exposition and the noted overlap with recent work, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contributions are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the results are likely to have a lasting impact on the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While the novelty may be slightly diminished by the recent FOCS publication, the paper remains a valuable and important contribution to NIPS. I believe the strengths of the paper substantially outweigh its weaknesses.
