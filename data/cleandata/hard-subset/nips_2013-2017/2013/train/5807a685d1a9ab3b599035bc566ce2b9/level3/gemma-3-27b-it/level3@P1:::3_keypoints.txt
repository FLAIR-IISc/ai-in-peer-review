- Studies discrete and continuous probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints.  
- Establishes sharp minimax rates of convergence, characterizing privacy–utility trade-offs.  
- Proves Warner’s randomized response is optimal for private survey sampling.  
- Integrates differential privacy disclosure risk with minimax decision theory, a novel connection.  
- Situates work historically, linking Warner’s survey motivations to modern cryptographic concerns.  
- Formulates local privacy (building on Evfimievski et al.) and analyzes it with formal DP guarantees.  
- High theoretical rigor: clear derivation of lower and upper minimax bounds (though technically dense).  
- Extends results to nonparametric density estimation, showing a different polynomial convergence rate.  
- Provides efficient algorithms matching lower bounds, indicating practical applicability.  
- Authors transparently acknowledge limitations and discuss future directions.  
- Exposition of the minimax framework (Lemma 1, Proposition 1) is dense and hampers readability.  
- Notation is cumbersome; presenting bounds directly in l2 norm could streamline presentation.  
- Needs more comparison to other differential privacy lower bounds, especially non-local settings.  
- Broader exploration of other distance metrics (e.g., l1) and wider minimax settings would strengthen impact.  
- Requests explicit clarification that per-user independent randomization is optimal versus conditioned schemes.  
- Notes potential overlap with a recent FOCS 2013 paper, which may weaken novelty.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, optimality of randomized response, density-extension, practical relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: complex exposition, heavy notation, overlap with FOCS, limited connections to other DP work.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — significant, rigorous contributions that outweigh minor weaknesses.
