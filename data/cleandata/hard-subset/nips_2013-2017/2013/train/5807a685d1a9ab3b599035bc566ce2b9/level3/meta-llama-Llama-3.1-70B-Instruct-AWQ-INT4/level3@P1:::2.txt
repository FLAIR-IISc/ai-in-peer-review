This paper presents a comprehensive study of local privacy and minimax bounds for probability estimation and density estimation. The authors provide sharp minimax rates for these problems under local differential privacy constraints. Here, we summarize the main ideas of the paper and relate them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere.

The authors revisit Warner's classical work on randomized response and provide a theoretical framework for characterizing optimal estimation under local privacy constraints. They apply their theoretical tools to multinomial estimation and density estimation problems, obtaining sharp minimax rates for these problems. The results show that local privacy can significantly impact the quality of statistical estimators, with the effective sample size decreasing quadratically in the privacy parameter α for multinomial estimation. For density estimation, local privacy prevents attaining the non-private optimal rate.

The authors' approach combines minimax decision theory with differential privacy, bringing these two fields into close contact. The paper provides a novel minimax bound for density estimation under local differential privacy, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the paper lacks a discussion of related noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy, such as those presented in Hardt and Talwar (2010) and De (2012). These prior works provide worst-case lower bounds for linear queries with L2 error, and multinomial estimation is a special case. The authors should clarify whether their local-DP bounds improve on differential-DP bounds.

Additionally, the density-estimation lower bound is derived for trigonometric-basis expansions, and it is unclear whether the non-private lower bound (eq. 13) holds for general Sobolev spaces or only for that basis. The comparison must be on the same function class.

The rebuttal partially clarifies the relations to prior work, but the authors should also cite Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which addresses mean-squared error.

Overall, the paper presents interesting and well-written results, but it is missing a discussion of known noise-complexity/differential-privacy lower bounds.
