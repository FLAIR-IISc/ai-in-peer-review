This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), providing sharp minimax rates for probability estimation in both discrete multinomial models and nonparametric density estimation. The authors combine information-theoretic lower bounds tailored to LDP channels with matching upper bounds via computationally efficient mechanisms, thereby charting the privacy/statistical-efficiency trade-off.

Summary and relation to prior work
- Problem and model: Each individual privatizes their datum Xi through a non-interactive α-LDP channel Zi ∼ Q(·|Xi). The paper defines the α-private minimax risk over all estimators and all α-LDP channels, then uses Le Cam/Fano reductions with new divergence bounds specific to local privacy to derive fundamental limits.
- Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the minimax MSE scales as min{1, d/(nα2)}; equivalently, for small α, the effective sample size shrinks from n to nα2/d. The authors also provide ℓ1 risk rates min{1, d/√(nα2)}. They show that a slight variant of Warner’s randomized response (RR) achieves these rates, proving RR is optimal for locally private survey sampling. This connects classical work on evasive-answer bias [Warner ’65] to modern DP.
- Density estimation: For Sobolev smoothness β, the nonprivate minimax L2 rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), i.e., the polynomial exponent worsens by one in the denominator. The paper proves lower bounds and then matches them with:
  - A privatized histogram when β=1 (Lipschitz), and
  - A locally private orthogonal series estimator for general β≥1 using a carefully designed per-user randomized mapping that preserves unbiasedness of basis coefficients while satisfying α-LDP.
  The paper also explains why simple Laplace noise on raw samples is provably suboptimal via classical deconvolution limits [Carroll & Hall ’88].
- Relation to prior work: The paper bridges DP (Dwork et al.) with statistical decision theory, extending earlier local learning results [Kasiviswanathan et al.] and geometric lower bounds [Hardt & Talwar; Smith] by giving population-level minimax rates under local constraints with matching algorithms. The orthogonal-series mechanism improves on global-DP estimators (e.g., Wasserman & Zhou) and on Laplace-perturb-then-estimate approaches.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds follow a clear reduction to testing, with new KL/mutual information control under LDP; the upper bounds are constructive and efficient. The multinomial rates and the β-smooth density rates match, indicating sharpness.
- Completeness and honesty: The paper contrasts with nonprivate baselines and explains the failure of naive noise addition for densities.

Clarity
- The core ideas are solid but the exposition is dense. The minimax framework, channel notation, and measure-theoretic setup (σ-fields) could be streamlined. Early presentation of the main rates with brief intuition, followed by technical development, would improve readability.
- Notation is heavy; since the primary losses are L2 (and ℓ1 once for multinomials), focusing notation and results on these metrics and pushing general Φ∘ρ treatments to the supplement would help.

Originality
- The combination of LDP-specific information bounds, sharp population minimax rates, and matching mechanisms in both discrete and nonparametric settings is novel and valuable. That said, parts of Section 3 overlap with the authors’ FOCS 2013 results; the NIPS contribution appears to extend, unify, and tailor them to statistical estimation (population risk) and to provide new achievability constructions for density estimation. The paper should better delineate novelty relative to FOCS and the arXiv version.

Significance
- The results quantify how much accuracy one must lose to guarantee local privacy and identify optimal mechanisms (notably, RR) along the privacy-utility continuum. These findings are likely to influence both theoretical work on LDP and practice in privacy-preserving data collection.

Strengths
- Sharp, matching minimax lower/upper bounds under LDP for fundamental problems.
- Clear operational guidance: RR is optimal for multinomials; specialized series estimators are optimal for densities.
- Conceptual contribution linking DP and minimax decision theory with reusable information bounds.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Exposition:
  - Simplify the minimax setup and notation; move general Φ∘ρ formulations and σ-field details to the appendix.
  - Present a compact summary (e.g., a table) of rates for multinomial ℓ2/ℓ1 and Sobolev β classes, nonprivate vs α-LDP.
  - Provide intuition for the α2 penalty and for why the exponent degrades in density estimation.
- Scope:
  - Clarify the role of non-interactivity. The paper focuses on memoryless channels; please state explicitly whether the lower bounds extend to interactive local protocols and whether any interaction can improve rates. If not, emphasize that per-user independent randomization is optimal within the local model.
  - Discuss how results relate to global DP lower bounds and whether any techniques transfer.
- Comparisons:
  - Expand discussion of ℓ1 risk beyond multinomials (e.g., density estimation in L1 or Hellinger/TV) or at least comment on expected rates and whether the techniques extend.
  - Compare more explicitly with other DP lower bounds (e.g., Smith; Hardt & Talwar) and with recent FOCS 2013 contributions; delineate the precise novelties here (e.g., population risk, density achievability, constants).
- Practicality:
  - Give more detail on the constants and computational cost of the orthogonal-series mechanism (including how to implement the sign-restricted sampling efficiently and how k, B, and α interact).
  - Comment on adaptivity to unknown β (e.g., locally private model selection or cross-validation) and on robustness to α>1.

Questions for the authors
- Do any of your lower bounds change under interactive local protocols? Can interaction recover the nonprivate exponent for density estimation?
- Can your information bounds yield minimax rates under other losses (TV, Hellinger) for densities?
- Is there an LDP-adaptive procedure achieving near-optimal rates without knowing β?
- For multinomials, do your results extend to structured simplices (sparsity or monotonicity) under LDP?

Pros for acceptance
- Sharp theoretical results with matching constructions.
- Clarifies optimality of classic randomized response.
- Broad relevance to privacy-preserving statistics and learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition is heavy; some notation and proofs could be streamlined.
- Overlap with recent FOCS work raises novelty concerns; clearer positioning needed.
- Limited discussion of interactive LDP or broader privacy models.

Recommendation: Accept. Despite some overlap and presentation issues, the paper makes a clear, rigorous, and impactful contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates under local privacy and by identifying optimal mechanisms for both discrete and nonparametric settings. The results will be of strong interest to the NIPS community.
