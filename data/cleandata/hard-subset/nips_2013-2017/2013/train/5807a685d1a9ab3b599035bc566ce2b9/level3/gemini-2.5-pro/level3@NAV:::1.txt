This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between statistical efficiency and privacy in the *local privacy* model, where data is privatized before being sent to an untrusted analyst. The authors use minimax decision theory, specifically Fano's and LeCam's methods, to derive sharp lower bounds for two core statistical tasks: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. A key finding is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation. For density estimation, the work demonstrates that local privacy constraints fundamentally alter the rate of convergence, slowing it from the classical n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹) to (α²n)⁻²β/(²β⁺²).

The paper's primary strength is its rigorous characterization of the limitations of learning under local privacy constraints. The derivation of sharp minimax bounds for both discrete and continuous estimation problems is a significant theoretical contribution that quantifies the inherent statistical cost of this strong privacy guarantee. The result establishing the optimality of randomized response provides a firm theoretical foundation for a long-standing and practical technique.

Despite its technical quality, the paper could be improved in several areas. The main weakness is a lack of sufficient context, which may confuse readers less familiar with the nuances of privacy models. The distinction between local privacy and the more common central model of differential privacy is crucial but sometimes blurred. For example, the important point about estimating population versus sample statistics should be explained more clearly and introduced much earlier to properly frame the results.

Furthermore, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison to related work. The authors should cite and discuss Lei's work on private M-estimators for density estimation to situate their results. For the discrete setting, a comparison to other private histogram or discrete estimation methods would better contextualize the performance of randomized response.

A few technical points also warrant clarification. The authors should comment on whether the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is necessary, as its relevance is unclear in practical regimes where alpha may be larger. Providing more discussion on the choice of the utility measure, rho, and how different utility functions fit into the analysis would also be beneficial.

In summary, the paper's originality and significance are high. Its contribution is twofold: a clear exposition of the local privacy framework and the derivation of new, sharp bounds for estimation within it. The work advances our understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off and is likely to be influential. I recommend acceptance, provided the authors address the requested clarifications and comparisons to related literature to maximize the paper's impact.
