This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between statistical accuracy and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors use minimax decision theory (specifically, Le Cam's and Fano's methods) to derive sharp lower bounds on the estimation error for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation. In the local privacy model, each individual's data is perturbed before being sent to an untrusted curator, providing a very strong privacy guarantee.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous and clear characterization of the statistical cost of local privacy. By establishing sharp minimax rates, it quantifies the inherent limitations of learning in this strong privacy model, which is a significant contribution to both the privacy and statistical learning communities.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The application of classical minimax theory to the local privacy setting is elegant and technically solid. The information-theoretic bounds from the authors' companion work are leveraged effectively to derive tight results.

3.  **Important Connections:** The work builds a powerful bridge between S. Warner's foundational 1965 work on randomized response and the modern framework of differential privacy. Demonstrating that this half-century-old technique is, in fact, optimal is a beautiful and important result.

4.  **Novel Insights for Nonparametrics:** The result for density estimation is particularly insightful. Showing that local privacy does not just affect the constant factors or logarithmic terms but fundamentally alters the polynomial convergence rate is a novel finding that highlights the increased challenge of privacy in complex, high-dimensional estimation problems.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Local vs. Central Privacy Models:** The paper studies the *local* privacy model, which is distinct from and stronger than the more commonly studied *centralized* differential privacy model. While the definition is stated correctly, the paper's exposition could be improved by more consistently and explicitly highlighting this distinction. Certain statements, such as the one at the end of section 2.1 claiming the lower bounds are "stronger than most of those in prior work," could be confusing to readers not deeply familiar with the literature. This claim hinges on the local model and the distinction between population and sample parameter estimation, which should be explained more clearly and earlier in the paper to provide better context for the results.

2.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper would be strengthened by more detailed comparisons to related work.
