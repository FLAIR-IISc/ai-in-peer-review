This paper develops sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). The setting is non-interactive local privacy: each observation Xi is privatized independently via a channel Q satisfying α-local DP, and only Zi are available to the statistician. The key technical tool is a pair of information-theoretic inequalities that upper bound KL divergences and mutual information after privatization (via Le Cam/Fano plus variational bounds), yielding lower bounds on private minimax risk. The paper applies this framework to two problems: (i) d-dimensional multinomial estimation, and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes.

For multinomial estimation, the authors show that privacy induces an effective sample-size reduction from n to roughly n α^2 / d (for small α), with matching upper and lower bounds. Specifically, the minimax ℓ2 error scales as min{1, d/(n α^2)}, and the ℓ1 error as min{1, d/√(n α^2)}, both sharp up to constants over α ∈ [0,1]. They further show that a minor variant of Warner’s randomized response achieves these rates, establishing its optimality for locally private multinomial estimation. This connects classical randomized response to modern DP and validates its near 50-year-old intuition in a rigorous minimax sense.

For density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ (with trigonometric basis), the results are more striking: local privacy changes the polynomial rate. While the non-private minimax L2 risk is n^{-2β/(2β+1)}, under α-LDP it becomes (n α^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)}. Thus, privacy effectively adds one to the denominator in the classical bias–variance trade-off. The paper provides two matching constructions: privatized histograms that are optimal for β=1, and a locally private orthogonal series estimator achieving the lower bound for all β≥1 via a tailored privatization mechanism that is provably superior to naïve Laplace/exponential noise addition (which yields strictly worse rates due to deconvolution limits).

Relation to prior work: The work builds on differential privacy foundations [11,16,17] and the statistical decision-theoretic tradition (Le Cam, Fano, Assouad). In contrast to prior DP optimality results that target sample quantities (e.g., answering linear queries with L2 error under central DP) [16,20], the focus here is population estimation under local privacy with minimax guarantees. The local setting is strictly more stringent than central DP; the information bounds used here (also in the authors’ arXiv [9]) are tailored to LDP and yield population-level lower bounds. The density estimation lower bound under LDP appears novel. It is also informative that direct noise addition to samples cannot attain the optimal rate due to deconvolution constraints [4], motivating their bespoke mechanisms.

Quality: The results are technically sound and carefully argued. The minimax lower bounds combine clean divergences after privatization with standard testing reductions. Upper bounds are constructive and computationally efficient (simple randomized response for multinomial; histogram and series estimators with explicit privatization schemes). The claims about optimality and the impossibility of Laplace-perturbed estimators are well supported. Some proofs are deferred to [9], but the main arguments are clear.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and easy to follow. The distinction between local and central DP, the non-interactive assumption, and the implications of the likelihood ratio bound are clearly stated. A few points would benefit from clarification (see suggestions below), especially around the precise function classes in the density setting and the relation to prior “noise-complexity” lower bounds.

Originality: High. The density estimation rate change under LDP and the sharpness results are new and insightful. The sharp multinomial rates with a direct optimality proof for randomized response in the LDP minimax sense are also valuable.

Significance: High. The work provides crisp baselines for what is statistically possible under LDP, a model of growing practical relevance. The identification of optimal mechanisms and the explicit privacy–accuracy trade-offs will likely inform both theory and practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Relation to central-DP lower bounds: The paper should discuss how its LDP population-estimation lower bounds relate to the well-known “noise complexity” lower bounds for answering linear queries under central DP, e.g., Hardt and Talwar (2010) [16], De (2012) [8], and the closely related mean-squared error results of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013). Multinomial estimation can be cast as estimating means of a standard basis (a special linear query class). Clarify whether your LDP rates strictly strengthen or are incomparable to those central-DP bounds, and emphasize that you treat population risk under LDP rather than empirical queries under central DP.
- Density function class: The lower bound (eq. 14) is proved for Fβ defined via the trigonometric basis with periodic boundary conditions. The non-private rate (eq. 13) is standard for Sobolev balls, but its exact form depends on boundary conditions and basis. Please state explicitly that both lower and upper bounds are established for the same periodic Sobolev class, and discuss whether the rate extends (up to constants) to non-periodic Sobolev spaces and other bases (e.g., compactly supported wavelets) and what modifications would be required (boundary corrections).
- Mechanism details and constants: In the orthogonal-series mechanism (Section 4.3), the constant ck and acceptance probabilities are asserted to be O(1); it would help to quantify these constants, the computational overhead (e.g., rejection rates), and any stability issues as k grows. Similarly, for the histogram mechanism, provide guidance on the choice of k in finite samples and how to adapt when β is unknown.
- Scope of non-interactivity: Since the paper restricts to non-interactive channels, comment on whether interaction could improve rates in either problem, or provide a reference/argument that non-interactivity is without loss for these tasks (if known).

Pros for acceptance:
- Sharp and conceptually clean minimax theory for LDP with matching constructions.
- Novel and important insight that LDP changes the nonparametric rate, with optimal, practical mechanisms.
- Clear connection of classical randomized response to optimality under LDP.
- Strong potential impact on the theory and practice of private statistical estimation.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of connections and separations with central-DP “noise complexity” lower bounds; missing citation to Nikolov–Talwar–Zhang (2013).
- Density results are framed for periodic Sobolev spaces; basis/boundary assumptions should be made explicit and broader applicability clarified.
- Some constants and practical aspects of the privatization mechanisms could be better quantified.

Overall evaluation: An interesting, well-written, and technically strong paper that advances our understanding of statistical efficiency under local privacy. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarification and citation suggestions above.
