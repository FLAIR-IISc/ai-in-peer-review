This paper provides a decision-theoretic analysis of statistical estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy. The authors use minimax theory, employing Fano/LeCam-style arguments, to derive lower bounds on the estimation error for two fundamental problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The core idea is to characterize the fundamental trade-off between the level of local privacy (parameterized by α) and the achievable statistical accuracy. The derived lower bounds are shown to be sharp by constructing explicit, computationally efficient procedures that achieve matching upper bounds.

Key results include showing that for d-dimensional multinomial estimation, local privacy effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `n * α^2 / d`. A significant consequence is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal in this setting. For density estimation of functions in a Sobolev class with smoothness β, the authors show that local privacy changes the exponent of the convergence rate, from the classical `n^(-2β / (2β+1))` to `(n * α^2)^(-2β / (2β+2))`, a more severe penalty than in the parametric case.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength is its rigorous characterization of the fundamental limitations of learning from data perturbed to guarantee local privacy. By framing the problem within the minimax framework, the authors provide sharp, quantitative bounds on the "price of privacy." This provides a solid theoretical foundation for understanding what is and is not possible in this stringent privacy model. The connection to Warner's 50-year-old work on randomized response is particularly elegant, demonstrating its optimality within a modern, formal privacy framework. The results are significant and provide a clear benchmark against which future algorithms can be measured.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
While the technical contributions are solid, the paper could be significantly strengthened by improving its clarity, precision, and connection to the broader literature.

1.  **Distinction from Central Differential Privacy:** The paper studies the "local" privacy model, where each user perturbs their own data before sending it to an analyst. This is a crucial distinction from the more common "central" model of differential privacy, where a trusted curator holds the raw data. The paper sometimes blurs this distinction or overstates its claims relative to the central DP literature. For instance, the statement at the end of Section 2.1, "our lower bounds are stronger than most of those in prior work," needs careful qualification. While lower bounds on population parameter estimation are indeed strong, much of the prior DP work has focused on the different (and practically important) problem of accurately estimating sample quantities in the central model. The distinction between population vs. sample statistics, and local vs. central models, should be explained more clearly and much earlier in the paper to properly contextualize the contributions.

2.  **Missing Connections to Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to related literature.
    *   The discussion of Lei [19] is too brief. The paper dismisses it by stating it is "not locally private" and has "sub-optimal convergence rates." A more substantive discussion is warranted. Lei's work on private M-estimators is highly relevant, and a deeper comparison of the models, assumptions, and results would provide valuable context for readers and highlight the specific contributions of this paper more effectively.
    *   For the multinomial estimation problem, a comparison to other potential methods for discrete data estimation beyond randomized response would be useful to further justify the optimality claims in a practical sense.

3.  **Clarity and Precision:**
    *   Some commentary is imprecise and could confuse a non-expert reader. For example, the final sentence of Section 4 states, "It appears that appropriately chosen noise mechanisms are crucial for obtaining optimal results." While true, this is a very general statement. The paper's contribution is in showing *which* specific mechanisms (e.g., the orthogonal series projection) are optimal and *why* others (e.g., direct Laplace perturbation of samples) fail for this problem. The commentary should be sharpened to reflect these specific insights.
    *   In Theorem 1, the restriction `α ∈ [0, 1]` is not fully justified. Is this a technical convenience for the proof, or is it a fundamental limitation of the analysis? Since regimes where `α ≈ 1` or slightly larger are common in the DP literature, clarifying the necessity of this constraint is important.

**Questions for the Authors:**
*   Could you comment on how the choice of the utility measure `ρ` affects the analysis? The paper focuses on L1 and L2 norms, but it would be helpful to understand how other utility functions might fit within your framework.
*   Could you elaborate on the necessity of the `α ≤ 1` constraint in Theorem 1?

**Final Assessment:**
The paper makes a twofold contribution: it provides a clear exposition of the local privacy model and its statistical implications (building on concepts largely from [10]), and it derives novel and sharp minimax bounds for probability and density estimation in this model. The technical results are sound and significant. The primary weaknesses lie in the paper's positioning relative to the broader differential privacy literature and a lack of detailed comparison to closely related work. Addressing the points above, particularly regarding the local vs. central DP distinction and the comparison with work like Lei [19], would substantially improve the paper's clarity and impact. The work is a valuable contribution to the intersection of privacy and machine learning.
