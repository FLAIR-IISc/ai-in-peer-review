Paper studies minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy for multinomial and nonparametric density estimation.  
Shows local privacy reduces effective sample size to nα²/d for multinomial and proves Warner’s randomized response is minimax optimal.  
Shows density-estimation rates degrade from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to (nα²)^{-2β/(2β+2)} and provides matching upper bounds (histogram, orthogonal series).  
Paper is well-written and technically sound; proofs appear correct and the density-estimation lower bound is novel.  
Provides an insightful historical connection between Warner’s randomized response and modern differential privacy.  
Major shortcoming: literature review omits noise-complexity lower bounds in DP (Hardt & Talwar 2010; De 2012).  
Authors must clarify how their local-DP bounds relate to existing (central) differential-privacy lower bounds for linear queries.  
Density-estimation lower bound is derived for trigonometric-basis expansions; unclear if the non-private rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} holds for general Sobolev spaces or only that basis.  
Rebuttal partially addressed concerns but authors should also cite Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013) on MSE in DP.  
Quality assessment: technically strong but lack of contextualization weakens novelty and significance claims.  
Clarity assessment: paper is clear, well-organized, with precise definitions and theorems.  
Originality assessment: minimax bounds under local privacy are novel, but placement within broader DP literature is insufficient.  
Significance assessment: results are important for understanding privacy–efficiency trade-offs but impact is diminished by missing citations and context.  
Arguments for acceptance: sharp minimax rates, optimality of randomized response, clear trade-off characterization, and technical correctness.  
Arguments against acceptance: fails to cite key prior work and does not clarify scope of the density-estimation lower bound.  
Recommendation: add and discuss missing DP literature and clarify the function-class assumptions (Sobolev vs trigonometric basis) to strengthen the paper.
