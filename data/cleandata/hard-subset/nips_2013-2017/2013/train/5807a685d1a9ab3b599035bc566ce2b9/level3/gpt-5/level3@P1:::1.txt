This paper studies probability estimation under α-local differential privacy (LDP), giving sharp minimax lower and upper bounds in both discrete (multinomial) and nonparametric (density) settings. The authors use Le Cam/Fano-style reductions together with information-contraction bounds tailored to LDP channels to quantify the privacy–utility trade-off. In the multinomial case, the minimax mean-squared error scales as min{1, d/(nα2)}, implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d when α is a small constant. A corollary is that Warner’s randomized response (RR) is minimax optimal (with an explicit private mechanism and estimator). In density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ, the private minimax rate changes its exponent: from the classical n−2β/(2β+1) to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2). The paper also provides matching constructive upper bounds: a privatized histogram that is optimal for β = 1, and an orthogonal-series estimator with a carefully designed binary-valued privatization mechanism that is optimal for all β ≥ 1. The work relates to foundational privacy models (Warner; Evfimievski et al.), differential privacy (Dwork et al.; Hardt–Talwar; De), private learning (Kasiviswanathan et al.), and nonprivate minimax theory (Yang–Barron; Tsybakov), while extending recent LDP-minimax analyses (Duchi–Jordan–Wainwright arXiv:1302.3203).

Strengths
- Clear, principled characterization of how LDP degrades estimation accuracy, with tight matching lower and upper bounds.
- Conceptual contribution: a precise, decision-theoretic privacy–efficiency trade-off, distinct from central DP. The change in exponent for nonparametric density estimation is especially striking and important.
- Algorithmic side is constructive and computationally simple (RR/projections; histogram; orthogonal series with an implementable privatization scheme).
- The identification (and proof) of RR optimality for multinomials is valuable and broadly informative.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope and positioning: The paper sometimes blurs LDP and (central) DP, e.g., the discussion around why the lower bounds are “stronger” than prior DP optimality results at the bottom of p.4. Please clearly distinguish local vs. central models early and consistently, including implications for utility and adversary models.
- Missing or light connections to existing DP literature. In addition to the cited works, please compare more directly to:
  - Lei (NIPS 2012): differentially private M-estimators via histograms. You briefly mention Lei in Sec. 4.3, but a fuller comparison of assumptions (local vs. central), privacy budgets, and rates would help readers situate your contribution.
  - Smith (STOC 2011) and Chaudhuri–Hsu (ICML 2012) on convergence rates under central DP, to emphasize what changes in the local model.
- Choice of loss ρ and Φ: The paper analyzes ℓ2 and ℓ1 for multinomials and L2 for densities, but it would help to discuss how the framework extends to other utilities (e.g., Hellinger, TV, KL) and whether the contraction bounds yield the same rates. Even brief guidance would broaden applicability.
- The restriction α ∈ [0,1] in Theorem 1: Is this technical or essential? Given typical regimes with α ≈ 1 in privacy practice, clarify how rates behave for α > 1 and whether constants cℓ, cu degrade gracefully. A figure/table discussing risk as a function of α would be very useful.
- Discrete-data mechanisms: Beyond RR and Laplace-perturbed counts, please compare with other simple local mechanisms for categorical data (e.g., one-hot perturbation vs. k-ary RR), at least theoretically. This will help practitioners choose mechanisms.
- Clarity: Some commentary is imprecise and could confuse readers not steeped in DP. Examples:
  - Be explicit that your lower bounds are for population parameters, not sample statistics, and move this explanation earlier (Sec. 2.1 or Intro), as it frames the comparison to prior DP optimality results.
  - When asserting optimality of RR, state the metric and regime (e.g., ℓ2 or ℓ1 risk, α-small constants, dimension dependence).
- I did not examine the supplementary material; please ensure key proof ideas (especially the information-contraction bounds used with Fano/Le Cam) are summarized in the main text.

Quality
- Technically sound, with careful minimax constructions and privacy-aware information bounds. The constructive mechanisms match the lower bounds and are implementable. Some proofs rely on prior arXiv [9]; ensure completeness and self-contained statements here.

Clarity
- Well-organized overall, but could benefit from: an early, explicit definition and discussion of local vs. central DP; earlier emphasis on population vs. sample estimation; a short subsection explaining the choice of losses and how to extend them; and a more precise statement of parameter regimes (n, d, α).

Originality
- The density-estimation exponent change under LDP and the optimal orthogonal-series mechanism are new and interesting. The multinomial results and RR optimality are valuable, though partly anticipated by folklore; your sharp constants/rates strengthen the case. Some exposition overlaps with [9].

Significance
- High. The results should influence how practitioners design local data-collection mechanisms and how theorists approach private learning under LDP. The RR optimality message is especially impactful for surveys and telemetry.

Questions for authors
- Is the α ≤ 1 restriction necessary in Theorem 1? What happens for α > 1?
- Can your information bounds handle other distances (e.g., Hellinger) without loss in rates?
- For density estimation, can you provide guidance on constants and practical choice of k beyond asymptotics?

Arguments for acceptance
- Sharp, conceptually clean minimax characterization of LDP costs in both parametric and nonparametric settings.
- Matching, simple mechanisms with clear guidance for practice.
- Novel and striking exponent change in nonparametrics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Blurred positioning between local and central DP in places; related work comparison (e.g., Lei) is too light.
- Some technical details are deferred to [9]/supplement; clarity could be improved.

Overall assessment
A strong and timely contribution. With clearer positioning (local vs. central DP), expanded discussion of loss functions, and a more thorough related-work comparison (particularly to Lei and central-DP rate results), this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
