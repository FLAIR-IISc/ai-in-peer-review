This paper presents a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of statistical estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors study the minimax rates of convergence for two fundamental problems: estimating the parameters of a discrete multinomial distribution and non-parametric density estimation. For both problems, the paper derives sharp lower bounds on the squared L2 error achievable by any estimator operating on locally privatized data. It then provides explicit, computationally efficient estimation procedures that achieve these bounds, thereby establishing the minimax-optimal rates. A key finding is that for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size is reduced from n to approximately nα²/d, and that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal. For density estimation over Sobolev smoothness classes, local privacy is shown to be more costly, changing the exponent in the rate of convergence from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2).

This work builds upon a rich history of minimax statistical theory (e.g., Le Cam, Fano, Tsybakov) and connects it to the modern computer science framework of differential privacy. While much of the recent literature on privacy has focused on the "central" model (where a trusted curator holds the data) and sample-level accuracy, this paper tackles the more stringent "local" model and provides stronger, population-level guarantees. The work's most direct predecessor is Warner's work on randomized response, which this paper places on a firm decision-theoretic footing.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a central question at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy: what is the fundamental statistical cost of ensuring privacy? By providing sharp, quantitative trade-offs between the privacy parameter α and the minimax estimation error, the work provides a clear and interpretable answer. These results are of high importance to both researchers designing new private algorithms and practitioners who need to understand the costs of deploying them.

2.  **Originality and Impact:** The connection established between Warner's randomized response and minimax optimality under local differential privacy is a beautiful and impactful result. It demonstrates that a simple, intuitive, and 50-year-old technique is fundamentally the best possible for this problem, lending it significant new weight. The analysis of density estimation is also novel and reveals a deeper cost of privacy, where the convergence rate's polynomial exponent is altered, a more severe penalty than a mere reduction in effective sample size.

3.  **Quality:** The technical analysis appears to be sound. The authors skillfully apply information-theoretic lower-bounding techniques (via Fano's and Le Cam's methods) to the setting of local privacy. The fact that the derived lower bounds are matched by achievable upper bounds from constructive and efficient algorithms demonstrates the sharpness and completeness of the results.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity:** The paper's main weakness is its clarity of exposition. The formalism, particularly in Section 2, is dense and could be simplified to improve accessibility. For instance, the general minimax risk is defined using a generic function `Φ ◦ ρ`, but all subsequent theorems and analysis specialize to squared `l2` or `l1` norms. Stating the problem and bounds directly in terms of these specific, standard norms would reduce notational overhead and make the core results easier to parse.

2.  **Originality Concern:** The novelty of the results in Section 3 on multinomial estimation is a potential concern. These results appear to be very similar to or an extension of work presented by the authors in a recent FOCS 2013 paper. The authors should explicitly clarify the novel contributions of this manuscript relative to their other recent publications to allow reviewers to accurately assess its originality. While the density estimation results are a significant and new contribution, the overlap on the discrete case tempers the overall novelty of the paper.

3.  **Scope of Discussion:** The paper could be strengthened by placing its results in a broader context.
    *   A comparison with lower bounds from the "central" model of differential privacy would be highly valuable. This would help readers understand the additional price paid for local privacy, where no trusted curator is assumed.
    *   While the focus is on `l2` loss, the `l1` bound for the multinomial case is also given. A more detailed comparison, and perhaps a discussion of the potential for `l1` bounds in the density estimation case, would be welcome.

4.  **Explicit Statement of a Key Result:** The results imply that for these estimation problems, non-interactive privacy mechanisms (where each user randomizes their data independently) are sufficient to achieve the optimal rate. This is a powerful conclusion, as it suggests that more complex interactive protocols provide no asymptotic benefit. This is a major takeaway and should be stated explicitly and highlighted as a key contribution.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a fundamental and important problem.
    *   Provides sharp, minimax-optimal rates for both discrete and continuous probability estimation under local privacy.
    *   The proof of optimality for Warner's randomized response is an elegant and significant result.
    *   The non-parametric density estimation results are novel and reveal an interesting shift in convergence behavior.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The exposition is overly complex and could be significantly clarified.
    *   There appears to be a significant overlap of the multinomial results (Section 3) with a prior/concurrent publication, which reduces the novelty.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong technical paper with significant results. The framework for analyzing the minimax rates under local privacy is valuable, and the findings for both multinomial and density estimation advance our understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off. While my enthusiasm is tempered by the apparent overlap with prior work and the lack of clarity in the presentation, the non-parametric results and the overall theoretical contribution are of high quality and will be of great interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to revise the paper to improve its clarity and to explicitly delineate its novel contributions from their previous work.
