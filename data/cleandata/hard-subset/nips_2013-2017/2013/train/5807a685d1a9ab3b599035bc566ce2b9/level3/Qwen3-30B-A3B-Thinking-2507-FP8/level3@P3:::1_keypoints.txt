- Derives sharp minimax lower bounds for probability estimation under local privacy and provides matching, efficiently implementable upper bounds.  
- Demonstrates Warner's randomized response is optimal for certain locally private estimation tasks.  
- Shows α-local differential privacy reduces effective multinomial sample size from n to nα²/d.  
- Shows density estimation convergence degrades from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to (nα²)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under local privacy.  
- Technical results and proofs are rigorous and sound.  
- Fails to clearly distinguish local privacy from standard (central) differential privacy, causing conceptual confusion.  
- Contains imprecise commentary (e.g., unclear necessity of the α restriction in Theorem 1) that may mislead readers.  
- Omits important related-work comparisons, notably Lei (NIPS 2011), and insufficiently connects to Duchi et al. (2013) and histogram/discrete-data methods.  
- Lacks discussion of the choice and implications of utility measures ρ.  
- Main novelty is applying existing local-privacy frameworks to probability estimation rather than introducing new privacy models.  
- Significance is weakened by inadequate contextualization and missing comparisons to existing methods.  
- Clarity suffers from insufficient early explanation of model assumptions and the population vs. sample distinction.  
- Recommends substantial revision: clarify local vs. central privacy, add detailed literature comparisons (Lei, Duchi et al.), and discuss utility choices.  
- Pro for acceptance: first sharp minimax rates for locally private probability estimation and clear theoretical privacy–efficiency trade-offs.  
- Con for acceptance: poor distinction between privacy models, missing related work, imprecise arguments, and limited practical guidance.  
- Final assessment: valuable theoretical contribution but presentation and contextualization flaws currently justify revision rather than acceptance.
