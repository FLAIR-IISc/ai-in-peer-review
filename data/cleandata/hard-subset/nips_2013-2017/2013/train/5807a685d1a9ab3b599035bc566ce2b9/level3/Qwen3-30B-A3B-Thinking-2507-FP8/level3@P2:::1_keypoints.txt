- Studies probability estimation under local privacy, deriving sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation.  
- Connects Warner’s randomized response to modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory.  
- Proves Warner’s randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation.  
- Shows local privacy alters density estimation rate from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to n^{-2β/(2β+2)}.  
- Provides efficient algorithms that achieve the derived lower bounds.  
- Theoretical contributions are clear, precise, and quantify privacy–statistical-efficiency trade-offs.  
- Confuses local privacy with centralized differential privacy in places (misleading statement on p.4).  
- Insufficient comparison to related work, notably Lei (2011) and other private discrete estimation methods.  
- Theorem 1’s restriction to small α (α ∈ [0,1]) is not well-justified or discussed for larger, practical α.  
- Fails to clearly explain the important distinction between population parameters θ(P) and sample statistics θ(x1:n) early enough.  
- Quality: solid theoretical results but limited discussion of practical implications due to local-privacy focus.  
- Clarity: generally well-written but marred by imprecise privacy-model statements and population/sample ambiguity.  
- Originality: novel minimax analysis for local privacy in density estimation; connection to Warner is interesting though not wholly new.  
- Significance: important theoretical insights on privacy–utility trade-offs, but limited immediate applicability because centralized DP is more common.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify privacy distinctions, add related-work comparisons, and address the α restriction.
