

This paper provides a rigorous study of minimax estimation under local privacy constraints, establishing sharp rates for multinomial and density estimation. The authors demonstrate that local privacy fundamentally alters statistical efficiency: for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size decreases quadratically in the privacy parameter α (n → nα²/d), while for density estimation with β smoothness, the convergence rate degrades from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2). The proofs appear technically sound, and the density estimation lower bound represents a novel contribution to the literature.

The paper's strengths include its clear exposition of the connection between Warner's classical randomized response and optimal privacy-preserving estimation, as well as the development of efficiently implementable algorithms that match the lower bounds. The theoretical framework successfully bridges statistical decision theory with differential privacy, providing a principled approach to understanding the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off.

However, the paper would benefit from addressing several gaps in the literature discussion. The authors should explicitly compare their results to noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy (Hardt & Talwar 2010; De 2012), which establish worst-case lower bounds for linear queries with L₂ error—multinomial estimation being a special case. Clarifying whether their local-DP bounds improve upon differential-DP bounds would strengthen the contribution. Additionally, the density-estimation lower bound is derived for trigonometric-basis expansions, but it's unclear whether the non-private lower bound (eq. 13) holds for general Sobolev spaces. The authors should specify the function class under consideration for proper comparison.

The rebuttal partially addressed prior work relations, but the authors should also cite Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013), which provides relevant mean-squared error results. While the paper makes important contributions to understanding privacy-constrained estimation, this missing discussion of established noise-complexity results represents a significant gap in contextualizing the novelty of the results.

Overall, this is an interesting, well-written paper with important theoretical results. With minor revisions to address the literature gaps, it would make a strong contribution to the field. The quality is high, the significance is clear for privacy-preserving statistics, and the originality is demonstrated through the novel minimax bounds. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria but requires improved contextualization of its results relative to existing work in differential privacy lower bounds.
