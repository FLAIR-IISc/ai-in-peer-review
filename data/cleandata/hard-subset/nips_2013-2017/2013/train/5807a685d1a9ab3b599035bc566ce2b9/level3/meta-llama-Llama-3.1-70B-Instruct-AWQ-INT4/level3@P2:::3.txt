This paper studies the problem of local differential privacy with minimax expected-error rates for estimating probabilities and densities. The authors provide lower bounds on minimax rates for l2 distance with discrete distributions over d types and show that randomized response (each user perturbs only their own type) meets the lower bound, proving optimality. They extend their results to density estimation with a simple per-user perturbation scheme achieving the lower bound.

The paper is well-motivated, and the results are important and of interest to the community. The authors bring together the formal treatment of disclosure risk provided by differential privacy with the tools of minimax decision theory to provide a theoretical treatment of probability estimation under privacy constraints. They provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate.

The authors' use of the minimax framework is sound, and their lower bounds are derived using Le Cam's two-point method and Fano's inequality. They provide matching upper bounds that are achievable using computationally efficient algorithms. The paper also provides a detailed study of the estimation of probability distributions—discrete and continuous—in a stringent setting in which data is kept private even from the statistician.

One potential criticism of the paper is that the exposition of minimax bounds is overly complex and could be simplified. The notation is also cumbersome, and the bounds could be stated directly in l2 norm since all results use it. Additionally, the authors could include a discussion or comparison to l1 norm results and other differential-privacy lower bounds (mostly non-local settings). It would also be beneficial to consider broader settings beyond the tailored local-privacy model and provide minimax lower bounds for them.

A new reservation is that the results overlap or extend a recently published FOCS 2013 paper (Section 3), weakening novelty. However, the paper still makes a significant contribution to the field and is recommended for acceptance.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear, although the notation could be improved.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but the notation and exposition of minimax bounds could be simplified. The paper adequately informs the reader, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.

In terms of originality, the paper provides new results on local differential privacy with minimax expected-error rates for estimating probabilities and densities. The authors bring together the formal treatment of disclosure risk provided by differential privacy with the tools of minimax decision theory to provide a theoretical treatment of probability estimation under privacy constraints. The paper is novel and makes a significant contribution to the field.

In terms of significance, the results are important and of interest to the community. The paper provides sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. The authors' results have implications for the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures and provide a way to adjust statistical procedures to meet the privacy or utility needs of the statistician and the population being sampled.
