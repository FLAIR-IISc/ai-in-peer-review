

This paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, establishing sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors connect Warner's classical randomized response framework [23] with modern differential privacy, demonstrating that Warner's approach is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation. For density estimation, they show a fundamental shift in the convergence rate under local privacy, changing from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2) for β-smooth densities.

The paper makes several important contributions. For multinomial estimation, the authors prove that local privacy at level α reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d, showing that Warner's randomized response achieves this optimal rate. For density estimation, they establish a novel lower bound that differs from classical minimax rates, demonstrating that direct perturbation methods (like adding Laplace noise) cannot achieve the optimal rate, and instead propose efficient histogram and orthogonal series estimators that match the lower bound.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs, and the results are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors provide clear connections to prior work on differential privacy [11, 1, 23] and statistical decision theory, though they could better address the relationship with existing noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy literature.

Strengths: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in privacy-preserving statistics with important theoretical and practical implications. The minimax bounds are sharp and match achievable rates, providing a complete characterization of the trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides efficient algorithms that attain the theoretical bounds. The connection between Warner's classical work and modern differential privacy is particularly insightful.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient discussion of related work in differential privacy lower bounds. Specifically, it should address prior work on noise-complexity trade-offs (Hardt & Talwar 2010, De 2012) which provide worst-case lower bounds for linear queries with L2 error. Since multinomial estimation is a special case of linear queries, the authors should clarify how their local-DP bounds relate to these differential-DP bounds. Additionally, the density estimation lower bound is derived specifically for trigonometric-basis expansions; the paper should clarify whether this bound holds for general Sobolev spaces or only for the specific basis used. The authors should also cite Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013) which addresses mean-squared error in privacy-preserving estimation.

Quality: The theoretical results are sound, with correct proofs and appropriate use of minimax framework. The paper carefully establishes both lower and upper bounds that match, providing a complete characterization. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The connection between classical privacy work and modern differential privacy is particularly well-explained.

Originality: The work represents a significant advance by providing sharp minimax rates under local privacy, a setting that has not been fully characterized before. The connection between Warner's work and modern differential privacy is novel, and the density estimation results demonstrate a fundamental shift from classical minimax rates.

Significance: The results have important implications for privacy-preserving statistical estimation. By characterizing the trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency, the paper provides a theoretical foundation for designing optimal privacy mechanisms. The practical relevance is demonstrated by showing that Warner's classical randomized response is optimal, which has been known for decades but not formally proven under the modern privacy framework.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Sharp minimax bounds for fundamental problems under local privacy; important theoretical contribution connecting classical and modern privacy work; practical implications for privacy-preserving statistics; well-written and technically sound.

Con-acceptance arguments: Lacks sufficient discussion of related work on noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy; unclear whether density estimation bounds hold for general Sobolev spaces; should cite additional relevant work (Nikolov et al., STOC 2013).

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistics. The results are novel, technically sound, and have significant implications for practitioners designing privacy mechanisms. While the paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of related work on differential privacy lower bounds, the core contributions are substantial and well-executed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the literature gaps.
