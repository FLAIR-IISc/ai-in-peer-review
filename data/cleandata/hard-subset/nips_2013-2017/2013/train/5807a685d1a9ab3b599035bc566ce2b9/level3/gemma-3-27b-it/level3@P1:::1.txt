This paper provides a theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints, establishing sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems. The work builds on Warner’s classical randomized response technique, demonstrating its optimality in a privacy-preserving survey setting, and connects it to the more recent framework of differential privacy and statistical decision theory. The authors leverage tools from minimax theory to quantify the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical intersection of statistics and privacy. It appropriately positions the work within the broader context of privacy-preserving data analysis, referencing key literature on differential privacy and disclosure risk. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and the contribution of the work, highlighting the connection to Warner's original formulation. The technical exposition is generally strong, utilizing established concepts from minimax theory and information theory.  The exploration of both multinomial and density estimation problems provides breadth to the investigation.

A significant strength of the paper lies in characterizing the limitations imposed by local privacy on statistical estimation. The results demonstrate a quantifiable penalty in convergence rate when data is perturbed to ensure privacy. The demonstration of optimality for Warner’s randomized response, even after decades of research in privacy, is a valuable and interesting result. The exploration of the specific trade-offs in density estimation, revealing a different polynomial rate of convergence compared to classical settings, is a noteworthy contribution.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The distinction between local privacy and differential privacy is occasionally blurred, and the paper sometimes overstates the relationship. For example, the statement on page 4 about the implications for previous work needs clarification. While the authors acknowledge the difference, further emphasis on this distinction would be beneficial for clarity. The necessity of the constraint on α in Theorem 1 is not clearly justified. Given that alpha often approaches 1 in practical applications of differential privacy, the relevance of this restriction needs to be discussed.

The choice of utility measures (ρ) is not thoroughly discussed. Expanding on how different choices of ρ would impact the analysis would strengthen the paper.  A critical omission is a comparison to existing work on differentially private estimation, particularly Lei’s (2015) work on M-estimation for density estimation. Discussing this work and positioning the results within the broader literature is crucial. Furthermore, a comparison to other histogram or discrete-data estimation methods would help contextualize the performance of randomized response.

The paper would benefit from more precise commentary in certain sections. Several statements, while likely correct, could confuse a casual reader. More explicit explanations of technical terms and assumptions would improve accessibility. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound analysis of privacy-preserving statistical estimation.
*   Sharp minimax rates of convergence for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy.
*   Demonstration of optimality for Warner’s randomized response.
*   Connection between classical privacy concepts and modern differential privacy.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Occasional blurring of the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy.
*   Omission of relevant comparisons to existing literature (e.g., Lei, 2015).
*   Lack of detailed discussion on the impact of different utility measures.
*   Some imprecision in the technical exposition that could hinder understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The theoretical results are significant, and the characterization of the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy is insightful. The authors have provided a solid foundation for future research in this area. While improvements in clarity and completeness are needed, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. The contribution is twofold: a clear exposition of the local privacy model (building on previous work like Duchi et al. [10]) and new bounds for density estimation.

**Recommendation:**  Accept with minor revisions.  The authors should address the points raised above, specifically clarifying the relationship between local privacy and differential privacy, comparing their results to existing literature (including Lei, 2015), and providing a more detailed discussion of the utility measures used in their analysis. Furthermore, they should refine the technical exposition to enhance clarity and accessibility.
