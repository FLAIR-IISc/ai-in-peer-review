## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of estimating probabilities and densities under local differential privacy, a stringent privacy model. The authors derive sharp minimax rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation, demonstrating a clear trade-off between privacy and statistical accuracy. A key result is the optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism for multinomial estimation, showing it achieves the derived lower bound. The extension to density estimation, with a simple per-user perturbation scheme also achieving the lower bound, is a significant contribution.

The work is highly relevant to the NIPS community, building on recent interest in privacy-preserving machine learning and statistical inference. It connects classical statistical decision theory with the modern framework of differential privacy, providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding the costs of privacy. The results are important as they quantify the unavoidable loss in accuracy when strong privacy guarantees are required, and demonstrate that established techniques like randomized response are, in fact, optimal in this setting. The paper builds upon and extends prior work on differential privacy [Dwork et al., Chaudhuri et al.] by focusing on *local* privacy and deriving tight minimax rates, going beyond previous bounds on sample estimation.

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical rigor and clear demonstration of optimality. The derivation of the minimax rates is a substantial achievement, and the connection to Warner’s work is particularly insightful. However, the exposition of the minimax bounds, particularly the use of Lemma 1 and associated notation, is overly complex and could benefit from simplification. The notation is also cumbersome; given that all results ultimately relate to the l2 norm, stating bounds directly in this norm would improve readability. 

Several avenues for future work are suggested. A comparison to results in the l1 norm, and a discussion of other differential privacy lower bounds (mostly focused on non-local settings), would be valuable. Exploring broader settings beyond the tailored local-privacy model and providing minimax lower bounds for those settings would also strengthen the work. The authors should explicitly state that per-user independent randomization is optimal, even considering schemes that condition on other users’ data. Finally, the authors should address the overlap with the FOCS 2013 paper (Section 3) more thoroughly, clarifying the novel contributions despite this overlap. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Important theoretical results, demonstrates optimality of classic procedures, high interest to the community.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Overly complex exposition, cumbersome notation, some overlap with existing work.

Despite the noted weaknesses, the paper’s significant theoretical contributions and relevance to the field lead me to recommend acceptance, albeit with a slightly lower confidence level due to the overlap with the FOCS 2013 work. Addressing the suggestions for clarification and comparison would further strengthen the paper.
