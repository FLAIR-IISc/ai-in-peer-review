This paper studies the estimation of probability distributions under local privacy constraints, providing sharp minimax rates of convergence for both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors bring together the formal treatment of disclosure risk provided by differential privacy with the tools of minimax decision theory to provide a theoretical treatment of probability estimation under privacy constraints.

The authors begin by introducing the concept of local privacy, which is a stringent setting in which data is kept private even from the statistician. They define the α-private minimax rate for the family θ(P) and provide a minimax bound using Le Cam's two-point method and Fano's inequality. They then review two results from their previous work that obtain information bounds as a function of the amount of privacy provided.

The authors then turn to the problem of multinomial estimation under local privacy. They show that providing local privacy can sometimes be quite detrimental to the quality of statistical estimators. Indeed, they prove that the minimax rate for multinomial estimation satisfies certain bounds, which imply that the effective sample size of any statistical estimation procedure decreases from n to nα2/d whenever α is a sufficiently small constant. They also demonstrate that Warner's classical randomized response procedure enjoys optimal sample complexity.

Next, the authors consider the problem of density estimation under local privacy. They show that imposing local differential privacy leads to a different convergence rate than the classical rate. They prove a lower bound on the minimax rate of estimation of densities when data is kept differentially private, and demonstrate that no estimator based on Laplace (or exponential) perturbation of the samples Xi themselves can attain this rate. They then show that a privatized histogram estimator is minimax-optimal for Lipschitz densities, and that an orthogonal-series estimator can achieve the optimal rate for all orders of smoothness β ≥ 1.

The authors' results provide a fundamental continuum along which privacy may be traded for utility in the form of accurate statistical estimates. They have identified a way to adjust statistical procedures to meet the privacy or utility needs of the statistician and the population being sampled. However, the paper lacks citation and discussion of related noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy, which is a significant omission. Additionally, the density-estimation lower bound is derived for trigonometric-basis expansions, and it is unclear if the non-private lower bound holds for general Sobolev spaces or only that basis.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides important results, but it would benefit from a more thorough discussion of prior work in the field. The authors should clarify whether their local-DP bounds improve on differential-DP bounds, and they should also cite Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013), which addresses mean-squared error.
