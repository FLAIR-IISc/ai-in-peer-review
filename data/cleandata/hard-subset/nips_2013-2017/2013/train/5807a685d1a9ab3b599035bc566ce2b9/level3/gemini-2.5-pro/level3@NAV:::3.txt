This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between utility and privacy in the local differential privacy model, providing a minimax decision-theoretic analysis for probability and density estimation. The authors establish sharp minimax expected-error rates for estimating discrete distributions over `d` categories, measured by the `l2` distance. A key result is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal, achieving the derived lower bound. This analysis is then extended to nonparametric density estimation, where the authors show that local privacy constraints alter the polynomial rate of convergence compared to the non-private setting, and they present a simple, optimal per-user perturbation scheme to achieve this new rate. This work builds upon a rich history of both privacy (Warner, 1965; Dwork et al., 2006) and minimax statistical theory (Tsybakov, 2009).

The paper's primary strength lies in its quality and significance. It provides a rigorous and important connection between statistical decision theory and local differential privacy, formalizing the intuition that privacy has a quantifiable cost in terms of statistical efficiency. Proving the optimality of a classic, simple mechanism like randomized response is an elegant and impactful result that will be of great interest to the community. The results are technically sound and advance the state-of-the-art in understanding privacy-preserving estimation.

However, the paper has weaknesses in clarity and originality. The exposition, particularly the derivation of the minimax bounds, is overly complex and could be streamlined for better readability. The notation is cumbersome; given that all primary results are for the `l2` norm, the bounds could be stated more directly to improve clarity. The paper would also benefit from a discussion comparing these results to `l1` norm analyses and other differential privacy lower bounds from the (mostly non-local) literature. A minor but useful addition would be to explicitly state that per-user independent randomization is optimal, even when more complex schemes that condition on other users' data are available.

A more significant concern is that the novelty of the results in Section 3 appears to be diminished by their overlap with a recently published FOCS 2013 paper. While the work is still valuable, this overlap weakens the originality of the submission.

**Recommendation:** Despite the concerns regarding clarity and reduced novelty, the paper's core contributions are strong and foundational. I recommend acceptance, though less strongly than I would without the noted overlap. The work is an important step in building a formal theory for local privacy.
