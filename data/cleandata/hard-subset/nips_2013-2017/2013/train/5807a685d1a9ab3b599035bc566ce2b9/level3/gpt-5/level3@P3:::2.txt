Summary
This paper studies minimax risk under local differential privacy (LDP) for two canonical estimation problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The authors develop a clean decision-theoretic framework that combines classical minimax lower-bound techniques (Le Cam/Fano) with new information-contraction inequalities specialized to LDP channels. They prove sharp lower bounds and give matching, efficiently implementable estimators.

For multinomial estimation over the d-simplex, they show that LDP induces an “effective sample size” reduction from n to roughly nα^2/d (for α ≤ 1), yielding minimax mean-squared error of order min{1, d/(nα^2)} and ℓ1 error of order min{1, d/√(nα^2)}. Thus, privacy degrades performance quadratically in α. At the same time, the estimator’s dependence on n remains 1/n up to constants, so for fixed α and d the same order in n as the non-private setting is preserved. They further show that a slight modification of Warner’s classical randomized response achieves these rates, establishing its optimality for locally private multinomial estimation.

For density estimation over Sobolev-type classes (defined via a trigonometric orthonormal basis), the situation is qualitatively different: LDP changes the minimax rate’s exponent from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} (non-private) to (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)}. They provide matching upper bounds via two constructive mechanisms: privatized histograms (β=1) and a novel locally private orthogonal-series estimator (β ≥ 1). The analysis also explains why simple additive Laplace noise on the raw data cannot attain the optimal rate (linking to deconvolution lower bounds).

Overall, the paper situates LDP squarely within statistical minimax theory, quantifying privacy–efficiency trade-offs and identifying optimal mechanisms.

Quality
- The results are technically solid. The proofs appear correct, and the arguments bridge information-theoretic contractions for LDP with standard minimax testing reductions cleanly.
- The multinomial rates match tight upper bounds achievable by randomized response; the density lower bound under LDP is, to my knowledge, novel, and the matching orthogonal-series construction is carefully designed to meet both privacy and optimality.
- Assumptions are explicit (non-interactive LDP, α ∈ [0,1], Sobolev classes defined via a basis), and algorithms are implementable.

Clarity
- The paper is well-written and well-organized. The minimax setup, the role of the channel Q, and the two core information bounds are clearly presented.
- A few points merit clarification:
  - Effective sample size: The text states a reduction to nα^2/d. It would help to contrast “order in n” (which remains 1/n for fixed α, d) versus constants (scaling with d/α^2), and to avoid phrasing that could suggest that small α preserves non-private sample complexity without qualification.
  - Function classes: The density lower bound (Theorem 2) is proved for Sobolev classes defined using the trigonometric basis. Equation (13) cites the classical non-private minimax rate. Please clarify whether (13) is established for the identical function class used in the private lower bound, and whether the private lower bound extends to more general Sobolev/Besov classes or to other orthonormal bases.

Originality
- Establishing sharp minimax rates for LDP in both multinomial and density estimation is original and impactful. The density-estimation lower bound under LDP (rate exponent change) and the matching locally private orthogonal-series mechanism are particularly novel.
- The paper also provides a satisfying optimality statement for randomized response in the multinomial case.

Significance
- The results are important for both the privacy and statistics communities. They quantify the intrinsic cost of LDP and identify optimal mechanisms, which will influence practice (e.g., validating randomized response for categorical data) and guide future work on locally private nonparametrics.
- The framework and techniques (information contraction under LDP, reduction to testing) should be reusable for other statistical functionals.

Weaknesses and missing related work
- Relation to known noise-complexity lower bounds in (central) differential privacy:
  - The paper would benefit from a dedicated discussion comparing its LDP minimax lower bounds to central-DP noise lower bounds for answering linear queries with small L2 error, e.g., Hardt & Talwar (STOC’10) and De (TCC’12). Multinomial estimation can be viewed as estimating answers to a collection of linear queries; clarifying whether and how the LDP lower bounds (population-risk minimax bounds) strengthen or differ from these central-DP worst-case per-query bounds would help readers position the contribution.
  - The rebuttal partially clarified the relation; please also cite and discuss Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC’13), which provides mean-squared-error lower bounds for answering linear queries and helps triangulate the landscape of lower bounds across central DP and LDP.
- Density-estimation function class: The private lower bound is stated for trigonometric-basis Sobolev classes. It is crucial that the non-private benchmark (Eq. 13) be for the identical class, and that the paper explicitly state whether the private lower bound (and matching upper bounds) extend beyond that basis (e.g., to standard Sobolev spaces on [0,1] with non-periodic boundary conditions). Otherwise, the comparison risks mixing function classes.

Pros
- Sharp, matching minimax bounds under local privacy for two fundamental problems.
- Novel and conceptually important finding that LDP changes the density-estimation rate exponent.
- Clear identification of optimal mechanisms: randomized response for multinomials; specialized locally private orthogonal-series estimators for densities.
- Technically sound, elegant use of information-contraction inequalities tailored to LDP.

Cons
- Missing discussion and citations connecting to central-DP noise lower bounds for linear queries (Hardt & Talwar 2010; De 2012; and also Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang 2013).
- Some ambiguity around the function-class alignment in the density results and around the “effective sample size” narrative.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a subsection contrasting LDP minimax bounds with central-DP noise lower bounds for linear queries, clarifying implications for multinomial estimation and whether LDP bounds improve upon or are orthogonal to those results.
- Explicitly align function classes in Eq. (13) and Theorem 2 and state the scope of generality (trigonometric basis vs. general Sobolev/Besov).
- Tighten the discussion of effective sample size to distinguish n-dependence from α- and d-dependent constants.

Overall assessment
An interesting, well-written, and technically strong paper that advances our theoretical understanding of local privacy in statistics. The main results are important and executed carefully. I recommend acceptance after the authors incorporate the missing related-work discussion and clarify the function-class and effective-sample-size points.
