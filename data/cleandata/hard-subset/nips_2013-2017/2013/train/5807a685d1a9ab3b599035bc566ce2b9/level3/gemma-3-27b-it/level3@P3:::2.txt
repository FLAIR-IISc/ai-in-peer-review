This paper studies minimax bounds for multinomial and density estimation under local privacy, establishing sharp rates of convergence and characterizing the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The work builds upon the formal treatment of disclosure risk provided by differential privacy and integrates it with minimax decision theory, revisiting Warner’s classical randomized response method in this context. It is a well-written and technically sound contribution that addresses an important problem in privacy-preserving statistical inference.

The authors correctly identify a gap in the existing literature – the lack of sharp minimax bounds under local privacy constraints – and fill it with rigorous analysis. The results are significant: the quadratic reduction in effective sample size for multinomial estimation with small α, and the demonstration that local privacy prevents achieving the non-private optimal rate for density estimation, are important findings. The proofs appear correct, and the novelty of the minimax bound for density estimation under privacy is a strong point. The efficient algorithms provided to achieve the upper bounds complement the theoretical lower bounds effectively.

However, the paper suffers from a notable omission: a detailed discussion of related noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy, specifically those by Hardt & Talwar (2010) and De (2012). These prior works establish worst-case lower bounds for linear queries with L2 error, and multinomial estimation can be framed as a special case. The authors should clarify whether their local-DP bounds improve upon or complement these existing differential-DP bounds. While the rebuttal addresses this somewhat, a more comprehensive comparison is needed within the main body of the paper. 

Furthermore, the density estimation lower bound is derived for trigonometric-basis expansions. It’s unclear whether the non-private lower bound (eq.13) holds generally for all Sobolev spaces or is specific to the trigonometric basis. A clear statement and justification of this assumption are necessary. A comparison with known lower bounds for general Sobolev spaces would strengthen the argument. The authors need to explicitly state the function class they are working with and compare their results accordingly.

The rebuttal mentions Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013), which addresses mean-squared error, and acknowledges the need to include it. This is a crucial addition, as their work provides relevant results for the MSE metric used in the paper. Incorporating this citation and discussing its relevance would further contextualize the contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous and well-supported.
*   **Novelty:** The minimax bounds for density estimation under local privacy are a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:** The results are important for understanding the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.
*   **Completeness:** The paper provides matching upper bounds for the derived lower bounds.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Missing Related Work:** Lack of thorough discussion of noise-complexity lower bounds in differential privacy (Hardt & Talwar, De).
*   **Basis Specificity:** The density estimation lower bound’s reliance on the trigonometric basis needs clarification and potentially generalization.
*   **Citation Omission:** Failure to cite Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang in the initial submission.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and open problem in privacy-preserving statistical inference.
*   The technical results are strong and appear correct.
*   The work provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The omission of relevant related work weakens the paper's contribution and positioning within the field.
*   The basis-specific nature of the density estimation lower bound limits its generality.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with important results. While the omission of related work is a significant weakness, the novelty and technical soundness of the contribution outweigh this flaw. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding related work and clarifying the scope of the density estimation lower bound, and including the citation of Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang. Addressing these points would elevate the paper from a strong contribution to an excellent one.
