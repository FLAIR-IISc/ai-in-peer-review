This paper provides a thorough investigation of minimax rates for probability estimation under the constraint of local privacy, a challenging setting where data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. A key finding is that local privacy incurs a penalty, reducing the effective sample size in multinomial estimation by a factor proportional to α², and altering the convergence rate in density estimation. Importantly, the work connects classical privacy notions (Warner’s randomized response) with modern differential privacy and minimax theory.

The paper is well-motivated, building on Warner’s (1965) original work on randomized response and the growing body of research on privacy and statistical inference (Duncan & Lambert, 1981; Dwork et al., 2006). The technical approach, utilizing tools from minimax decision theory and differential privacy, is sound. The analysis clearly defines the setting, establishing a formal treatment of local privacy through the likelihood ratio bound (Evfimievski et al., 2003). The results concerning the reduction in effective sample size for multinomial estimation (Theorem 1) are particularly insightful, and the demonstration that Warner's randomized response achieves optimal complexity is a noteworthy historical connection.  The density estimation results (Theorem 2), revealing a different polynomial rate of convergence under privacy, represent a significant contribution to the field.  The accompanying algorithms, presented in Propositions 2 and 3, provide concrete methods for achieving these bounds.

However, a critical weakness lies in the lack of discussion of related work on noise complexity and differential privacy. While the paper meticulously cites work within statistical minimax theory, it largely overlooks the extensive literature on lower bounds in differential privacy, specifically regarding the amount of noise required to achieve a given privacy level. Works such as Hardt & Talwar (2010) and De (2012) provide worst-case lower bounds for linear queries with L2 error, and multinomial estimation can be viewed as a special case of linear query estimation.  The authors should explicitly clarify whether their locally-DP bounds improve upon those obtained using differential-DP, and if not, acknowledge this relationship.  

Furthermore, the discussion of density estimation needs more nuance. The lower bound (Theorem 2) is derived for densities expressed in terms of a trigonometric basis. While this is a valid approach, the comparison to the non-private rate (equation 13) is less meaningful if the non-private rate also holds only for densities in the same function class (i.e., those with a trigonometric representation). The authors should clarify if the non-private rate holds for a broader class of densities (e.g., within a general Sobolev space) and explicitly state the function class under consideration when comparing the private and non-private rates. The authors’ rebuttal addresses this point somewhat, but a clearer statement in the paper itself would strengthen the argument.  Additionally, the rebuttal acknowledging Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (2013) is a positive step; citing this work directly in the original submission would have been beneficial.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper's strengths are considerable. The mathematical treatment is rigorous, the results are novel and important, and the writing is clear and concise. The connection between classical privacy methods and modern theoretical frameworks is particularly valuable.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant theoretical results on minimax rates under local privacy.
*   Sharp convergence rates established for both multinomial and density estimation.
*   Clear and rigorous mathematical treatment.
*   Demonstration of optimality of Warner's randomized response.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of discussion of related work on noise complexity and differential privacy.
*   Potential ambiguity in comparison between private and non-private rates for density estimation, stemming from differing function class assumptions.
*   Initial omission of key related work (Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang, 2013).

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While the lack of engagement with related work on differential privacy is a significant concern, the novelty and importance of the results, combined with the clarity of the presentation, outweigh this weakness. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the cited criticisms in a final revision by explicitly acknowledging and discussing the relevant differential privacy literature, and clarifying the assumptions regarding function classes when comparing private and non-private rates.
