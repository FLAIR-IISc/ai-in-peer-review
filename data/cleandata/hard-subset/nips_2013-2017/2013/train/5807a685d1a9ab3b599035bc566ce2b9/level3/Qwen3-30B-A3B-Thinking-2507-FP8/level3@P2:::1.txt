

# Review for "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper studies probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, providing sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors connect Warner's classical randomized response method to modern differential privacy frameworks and establish fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
The paper addresses a fundamental question: how does privacy affect statistical estimation? The authors focus on the local privacy model, where data is perturbed before reaching the statistician, rather than the more common centralized differential privacy where a trusted curator applies noise. This work builds on Warner's 1965 randomized response technique and connects it to modern differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006) and minimax decision theory. While the minimax approach to privacy was partially explored in Duchi et al. (2013), this paper provides sharper rates for specific estimation problems, particularly highlighting that Warner's method is optimal for multinomial estimation. The paper also establishes a surprising result for density estimation, where local privacy changes the convergence rate from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to n^(-2β/(2β+2)), which differs from standard non-private results.

## Strengths
The paper's primary strength is its clear theoretical contribution, providing sharp minimax rates for estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors successfully bridge classical privacy work with modern statistical decision theory, offering precise quantitative trade-offs. The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly insightful, showing that a 50-year-old technique remains optimal in modern privacy settings. The paper also provides efficient algorithms that achieve the lower bounds, making the theoretical results practically relevant. The connection between local privacy and the statistical efficiency continuum is well-articulated and provides valuable insights for practitioners.

## Weaknesses
The paper suffers from several issues that weaken its impact. First, it often blurs the distinction between local privacy (which is what the paper studies) and differential privacy (which is a different model). The statement at the bottom of page 4 ("differential privacy has strong semantic privacy guarantees that make it a good candidate for declaring a statistical procedure private") incorrectly implies that local privacy is a form of differential privacy, which it is not. This confusion could mislead readers unfamiliar with the distinction.

Second, the paper lacks sufficient comparison to related work. The authors should have engaged with Lei's (2011) work on differentially private M-estimators, which addresses similar density estimation problems. The paper also fails to compare its histogram and orthogonal series estimators with other privacy-preserving discrete estimation methods, making it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed approaches.

Third, the restriction to small α in Theorem 1 (α ∈ [0,1]) is not well-justified. In practical applications of differential privacy, α often takes values around 1, so the relevance of the restriction to small α is unclear. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how the results scale when α is larger.

Finally, the distinction between population and sample statistics (a key point raised in the rebuttal) is not sufficiently clarified. The paper's focus on population estimation (θ(P)) rather than sample estimation (θ(x1:n)) is important but not adequately explained early in the paper.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance
**Quality**: The theoretical results are sound and well-supported, though the paper could better acknowledge the limitations of the local privacy model. The lower and upper bounds are sharp, but the connection to practical implementation could be strengthened.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written, but the imprecise statements about privacy models and the lack of clear distinction between population vs. sample statistics detract from clarity. A more careful exposition of the local privacy model early in the paper would help readers.

**Originality**: The application of minimax analysis to local privacy for density estimation is novel, though the core approach builds on existing work. The connection to Warner's classical work is interesting but not entirely new.

**Significance**: The paper provides important theoretical insights about the privacy-utility trade-off, which is crucial for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. However, the restriction to local privacy limits the immediate applicability, as most practical privacy implementations use centralized differential privacy.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution by providing sharp minimax rates for estimation under local privacy constraints and demonstrating that Warner's randomized response is optimal. The theoretical insights are sound and well-motivated. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by clarifying the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy, adding comparisons to related work (particularly Lei's M-estimators), and better explaining the practical relevance of the α restriction. 

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these weaknesses. The paper's theoretical contribution is strong, and with the suggested clarifications, it would make a significant contribution to the NIPS community. The connection between classical privacy work and modern theoretical frameworks is particularly valuable for the field.
