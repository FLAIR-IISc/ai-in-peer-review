This paper studies the fundamental limits of local differential privacy for probability and density estimation, providing sharp minimax rates of convergence. It builds upon recent work connecting differential privacy with minimax decision theory, offering a theoretical treatment of privacy-utility tradeoffs. The authors demonstrate that randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation and extend this analysis to density estimation, showcasing a different convergence rate compared to the non-private setting.

The paper is a significant contribution to the field. Establishing the optimality of a classical procedure like randomized response is valuable and demonstrates a deep understanding of the interplay between privacy and statistical efficiency. The results are likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community, given the growing importance of privacy-preserving machine learning. The connection to established statistical theory, via minimax rates, provides a rigorous foundation for understanding the limitations imposed by privacy constraints.

However, the paper does suffer from some weaknesses. The exposition of the minimax bounds, particularly in Section 2, is overly complex and could benefit from simplification. The extensive use of notation, while mathematically precise, creates a barrier to entry for readers less familiar with the specific tools of minimax theory.  Stating the bounds directly in the l2 norm, since all results ultimately rely on it, would improve readability. 

Several avenues for future work are suggested. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion comparing the results to those in the l1 norm, and a more thorough comparison with other differential privacy lower bounds, many of which focus on non-local settings. Broadening the scope beyond the tailored local-privacy model defined in the paper and deriving minimax lower bounds for more general settings would be valuable. A crucial point, explicitly stating that per-user independent randomization is optimal *despite* the existence of schemes that condition on other users’ data, would clarify the optimality claim and preempt potential confusion.

A recent reservation regarding overlap with the FOCS 2013 paper (Section 3) is noted. This does somewhat diminish the novelty of the work, although the paper still presents a valuable and comprehensive analysis. The authors appropriately acknowledge this overlap.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by established results in minimax theory and differential privacy.
* **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in privacy-preserving statistical inference and provides important insights into the tradeoffs involved.
* **Originality:**  While acknowledging overlap with recent work, the paper offers a novel and comprehensive treatment of local differential privacy with sharp minimax rates, extending existing results.
* **Clarity (with caveats):** The paper is generally well-organized, but suffers from notation complexity which detracts from clarity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Complexity:** The exposition of minimax bounds is unnecessarily complex.
* **Notation:** Cumbersome notation hinders readability.
* **Novelty (reduced):** The overlap with the FOCS 2013 paper slightly reduces the originality of the work.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the noted weaknesses, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, provides valuable insights, and is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. Addressing the suggestions regarding clarity and comparison with related work would further strengthen the paper. The reduced novelty due to the FOCS paper is a concern, but the overall quality and importance of the work still warrant acceptance, though with slightly less enthusiasm than if the novelty were entirely unique.
