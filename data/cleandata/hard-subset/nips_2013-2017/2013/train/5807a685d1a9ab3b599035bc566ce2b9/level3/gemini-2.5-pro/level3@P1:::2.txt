This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between statistical efficiency and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors use minimax decision theory to derive sharp convergence rates for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation over `d` categories, the paper demonstrates that imposing α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα^2/d` for small `α`. A notable consequence is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is shown to be minimax optimal. For nonparametric density estimation of functions in a Sobolev class with smoothness `β`, the authors show a more severe penalty: local privacy changes the polynomial rate of convergence for the integrated squared error from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. The paper complements these lower bounds with computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these rates.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper makes a significant and original contribution by formally connecting the rigorous framework of minimax decision theory with local differential privacy. This provides a principled way to quantify the cost of privacy in terms of statistical convergence rates.

*   **Quality and Significance:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proofs appear sound, and the results are important. Quantifying the precise statistical cost of local privacy is a fundamental problem, and this work provides sharp answers for two widely studied estimation tasks. The result that local privacy fundamentally alters the convergence rate exponent for density estimation is particularly insightful and significant. It demonstrates that the privacy-utility trade-off can be more complex than a simple reduction in effective sample size. These results are likely to be built upon by researchers working at the intersection of privacy, statistics, and machine learning.

*   **Originality:** The core idea of applying minimax analysis to local privacy is novel and powerful. While minimax theory is classical and differential privacy has been studied for several years, their synthesis to derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds in the *local* model is a new and valuable contribution. The derivation of the new rate for density estimation is a key original finding.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem setting is introduced effectively, and the main theorems are stated precisely, making the contributions easy to understand. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their results with respect to classical (non-private) statistical rates.

Despite these strengths, there are a few weaknesses that should be addressed:

1.  **Missing Discussion of Related Work:** The primary weakness is the lack of engagement with a significant body of literature on lower bounds in the (central) differential privacy model. Multinomial estimation is equivalent to answering a set of `d` counting queries, a canonical example of linear queries. There are well-known lower bounds on the noise complexity required to answer such queries under central differential privacy, notably in the work of Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012). These papers establish lower bounds on the L1 or L2 error for answering linear queries, which directly apply to multinomial estimation. The authors should explicitly discuss how their lower bounds for the *local* model compare to these existing bounds for the *central* model. It is expected that local privacy incurs a higher cost, but a direct comparison and discussion would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and place it in proper context. Furthermore, the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013) on the geometry of differential privacy also provides relevant mean-squared error bounds and should be discussed.

2.  **Clarity of Comparison in Density Estimation:** The comparison between the private and non-private minimax rates for density estimation needs a minor clarification. The authors derive their lower bound (Eq. 14) for the Sobolev class `Fβ` defined using a specific trigonometric basis. They compare this to the classical non-private rate `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` (Eq. 13). For the comparison to be completely rigorous, it should be made clear that this classical rate holds for the *exact same* function class (i.e., the Sobolev ellipsoid defined by the trigonometric basis). While this is likely true, explicitly stating it would remove any ambiguity about whether the change in rate is an artifact of comparing slightly different function classes.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents novel, technically strong, and significant results on a fundamental problem: quantifying the statistical cost of local privacy.
*   **Pro:** It elegantly unifies two major theoretical frameworks—minimax decision theory and differential privacy.
*   **Pro:** The finding that local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a deep and important insight.
*   **Pro:** The paper is well-written and provides achievable, efficient algorithms that match the derived lower bounds.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The paper has a major omission in its discussion of related work, failing to compare its results for multinomial estimation to established lower bounds for linear queries under central differential privacy (e.g., Hardt & Talwar 2010, De 2012, Nikolov et al. 2013). This makes it difficult to assess the precise contribution relative to the state of the art in privacy research.
*   **Con:** The comparison of private vs. non-private rates in density estimation could be made more precise by clarifying that the function classes are identical.

**Conclusion:**

This is a very interesting and well-executed paper with important theoretical contributions. The main drawback is the failure to adequately situate the results within the broader context of differential privacy lower bounds. However, this is a correctable flaw. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors thoroughly addressing the relationship of their work to the existing literature on noise-complexity lower bounds in their final version. This would substantially strengthen an already impressive paper.
