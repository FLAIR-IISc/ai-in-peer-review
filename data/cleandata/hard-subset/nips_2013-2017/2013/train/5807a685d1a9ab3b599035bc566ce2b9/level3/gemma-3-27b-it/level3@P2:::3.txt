This paper provides a detailed study of probability estimation—both discrete and continuous—under the constraints of local differential privacy. The authors establish sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these settings, rigorously characterizing the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. A particularly compelling result is the demonstration that Warner’s classical randomized response mechanism is, in fact, optimal for survey sampling while maintaining privacy.

The work builds upon a strong foundation in statistical decision theory, differential privacy, and information theory. The authors effectively integrate these concepts to provide a formal treatment of disclosure risk, allowing for the use of decision-theoretic tools to analyze privacy-utility trade-offs. This contribution is significant as it moves beyond simply *achieving* privacy to *quantifying* its cost in terms of statistical accuracy. The connection made between classical privacy notions (Warner's work) and modern differential privacy is well-articulated and insightful.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The technical analysis is rigorous, and the results are presented with a clear understanding of their implications. The optimality result for randomized response is especially noteworthy, showing that a decades-old technique continues to hold up under modern theoretical scrutiny. The extension to density estimation and the associated bounds are also valuable contributions. The clear demonstration of the penalty paid for privacy in terms of effective sample size and convergence rates is a significant step forward in understanding the fundamental limits of private statistical inference. The paper addresses a difficult problem with a demonstrable advancement of the state of the art and provides unique conclusions on existing data.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the thoroughness of the minimax analysis is commendable, the exposition is at times overly complex. The presentation of Lemma 1, for example, could benefit from simplification, potentially by focusing on the core intuition rather than presenting all the general formalism upfront. The notation throughout the paper is also somewhat cumbersome. Since all results ultimately concern the l2 norm, consistently framing bounds and discussions directly in terms of this norm would improve clarity and readability. 

It would be valuable to include a more extensive discussion of related work, particularly concerning l1 norm results and existing differential privacy lower bounds, many of which operate in non-local settings. A comparison of the results obtained here with these prior works would provide further context and highlight the specific contributions of this paper.  Furthermore, the authors could consider discussing broader settings beyond the tailored local-privacy model considered in the paper, and provide minimax lower bounds for such scenarios.

The authors should also explicitly state the optimality of per-user independent randomization. While it is implied, stating clearly that no scheme conditioning on other users’ data can improve upon the proposed mechanisms would strengthen the argument and clarify the implications of their results.

I note a recent reservation regarding potential overlap with a paper presented at FOCS 2013 (Section 3). While this does somewhat diminish the novelty of the work, the results presented here are still significant and represent a valuable contribution to the field. The careful analysis and clear articulation of the privacy-utility trade-off remain important, even if some individual results have been independently discovered.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and timely problem at the intersection of privacy and statistical inference.
*   The technical analysis is rigorous and the results are significant.
*   The optimality result for randomized response is particularly compelling.
*   The extension to density estimation provides further valuable insights.
*   The work is well-motivated and clearly written (despite some areas for improvement).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The exposition of the minimax analysis can be overly complex.
*   The notation is somewhat cumbersome.
*   Potential overlap with recent work at FOCS 2013.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses and the noted overlap with recent work, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The contributions are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the results are of broad interest to the NIPS community. The work offers a valuable theoretical foundation for understanding and designing privacy-preserving statistical methods.  I would rate it as a strong paper with the potential to have a lasting impact on the field.
