This paper presents a detailed minimax analysis of probability estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy. The authors use tools from classical statistical decision theory, such as Le Cam's two-point method and Fano's inequality, to derive sharp lower bounds on the convergence rates for any estimator that satisfies α-local privacy. The work focuses on two fundamental problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For the d-dimensional multinomial problem, the authors show that local privacy induces a penalty that reduces the effective sample size from n to approximately nα²/d. A significant consequence of this result is a formal proof that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal. For nonparametric density estimation of β-smooth functions, the paper reveals a more severe penalty: the optimal convergence rate worsens from the classical n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹) to (nα²)⁻²β/(²β⁺²), a change in the polynomial exponent itself. The authors complement these lower bounds by proposing computationally efficient and rate-optimal estimators, including a privatized histogram and an orthogonal series estimator.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous and much-needed characterization of the fundamental trade-off between statistical utility and privacy in the stringent *local* privacy model. By establishing sharp minimax rates, it quantifies the inevitable price of privacy for fundamental estimation tasks, moving beyond the analysis of specific algorithms to bound the performance of *any* possible procedure.

2.  **Novel and Significant Results:** The results for density estimation are particularly striking and important. The discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate is a deep insight that distinguishes this work from much of the existing literature, where privacy often introduces logarithmic factors or affects constants and effective sample sizes. This finding highlights that the penalty for local privacy can be qualitatively different and more severe for complex, infinite-dimensional problems.

3.  **Constructive and Complete:** The work is not limited to lower bounds. For both problems studied, the authors present computationally efficient algorithms that achieve the established minimax rates. This demonstrates that the bounds are not merely theoretical artifacts but are attainable in practice, making the contribution whole and impactful. The demonstration that the 50-year-old randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation is an elegant and satisfying result.

4.  **Strong Theoretical Framework:** The application of minimax decision theory to the local privacy setting is powerful. A key strength of the theoretical approach is that the lower bounds apply to the estimation of underlying *population* parameters, which is a stronger and more general result than bounds on estimating finite-sample statistics, a common focus in prior differential privacy literature.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Local vs. Central Privacy:** The paper's primary weakness is its imprecise distinction between the "local privacy" model it studies and the more widely known "central" differential privacy model. In local privacy, each user perturbs their own data before sending it to an analyst, trusting no one. In central privacy, a trusted curator collects raw data and adds noise to aggregate query results. These models have vastly different trust assumptions, guarantees, and performance trade-offs. The paper sometimes uses the general term "differential privacy" when it specifically means "local differential privacy" (e.g., in the discussion following Theorem 1 on page 4), which could confuse readers unfamiliar with this distinction. A dedicated paragraph in the introduction clarifying these two models and motivating the choice of the local model would significantly improve the paper's clarity and properly frame its contributions.

2.  **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** The comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.
    *   The discussion of Lei's work [19] on private M-estimators is quite brief. While the authors correctly note that Lei's estimator is not locally private, a more detailed comparison of the rates and techniques would help readers better understand the landscape and the specific contributions of this paper.
    *   For multinomial estimation, the paper establishes the optimality of randomized response. This claim would be stronger if compared against a broader class of plausible private mechanisms for discrete data beyond the standard Laplace mechanism, further cementing its privileged status.

3.  **Scope and Generalization:**
    *   Theorem 1 on multinomial estimation is restricted to α ∈ [0, 1]. It would be helpful if the authors could comment on whether this is a technical convenience for the proof or a fundamental limitation. Since α values greater than 1 are sometimes considered in the literature, clarifying the behavior for larger α would be valuable.
    *   The analysis is performed for the squared ℓ2 and ℓ1 norms. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on the role of the loss function (ρ) and the potential to extend the analysis to other measures of utility, which would speak to the generality of the framework.

4.  **Emphasis of Contributions:** The paper's lower bounds on *population* estimation are a key strength over prior work focused on *sample* estimation. This point is mentioned in Section 2.1 but deserves to be highlighted more prominently in the introduction, as it is a crucial element of the paper's originality and significance.

**Summary:**

This paper makes a significant and technically sound contribution to the intersection of statistical learning theory and privacy. Its main results—establishing sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation under local privacy—are novel, interesting, and important. The finding that local privacy can alter the polynomial rate of convergence for nonparametric problems is a particularly valuable insight. While the paper would benefit from clearer positioning regarding the local vs. central privacy models and a more detailed comparison with related work, its core contributions are strong. The work provides a clear theoretical foundation for understanding the limits of what can be learned privately and is likely to be influential for researchers in both machine learning and privacy.
