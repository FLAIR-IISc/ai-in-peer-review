

This paper investigates minimax estimation under local differential privacy constraints, using Fano/LeCam arguments to derive sharp rates for probability estimation. The authors establish that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation, and derive new minimax rates for density estimation that differ from classical results due to privacy constraints. The work connects Warner's early privacy work with modern differential privacy frameworks.

Strengths include clear theoretical characterization of the privacy-utility tradeoff, particularly the novel finding that local privacy degrades density estimation rates from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to n^(-2β/(2β+2)). The paper provides computationally efficient algorithms that match the lower bounds, demonstrating practical relevance.

However, several weaknesses concern the presentation and context. The paper fails to properly distinguish local privacy from differential privacy (e.g., the statement at the bottom of p.4 blurs this critical distinction), and the restriction to local privacy is under-justified despite being central to the analysis. The treatment of parameter α in Theorem 1 lacks clarity regarding its necessity, especially since α≈1 is common in practical DP applications. The authors omit crucial comparisons to Lei's M-estimation work and other histogram-based methods, making it difficult to assess the novelty and practical value of their approach.

The paper's clarity is hampered by imprecise commentary that could confuse casual readers, and the distinction between population and sample statistics remains unclear until later sections. While the theoretical contribution is sound (with the minimax bounds being technically rigorous), the presentation would benefit from earlier and clearer explanation of the local privacy model's relationship to standard differential privacy.

The significance is limited by the narrow focus on local privacy, which differs substantially from the broader DP literature. The connection to existing DP results is underdeveloped, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how their results advance the state of the art beyond the already established local privacy model in [10]. The contribution appears more as a careful exposition of local privacy bounds rather than a significant advancement.

Overall, the paper makes a technically sound contribution to a niche area of privacy-preserving statistics, but the lack of context with the broader DP literature and imprecise distinctions significantly diminish its impact. The work would benefit from substantial revision to clarify the relationship to differential privacy and properly contextualize the results within the existing literature.
