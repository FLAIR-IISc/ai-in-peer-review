The paper "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation" presents a comprehensive study of the estimation of probability distributions in a setting where data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. The paper's main contribution is the derivation of lower bounds on the convergence rates of any estimator under local differential privacy constraints, which are then complemented by matching upper bounds achievable using computationally efficient algorithms.

The paper's strengths include its clear exposition of the local privacy model, which is largely contained in previous work, and the derivation of new bounds for density estimation. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The commentary in some places is imprecise and could confuse casual readers. For example, the statement at the bottom of page 4 overstates the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more explicit connection to existing differential privacy results and literature.

The restriction to local privacy makes the model different from differential privacy, and the paper sometimes blurs this distinction. The question of whether the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is necessary is also unclear, given experimental regimes where alpha is approximately 1 under differential privacy.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the choices of rho (utility measures) and how different utilities fit the analysis. The authors should also provide a comparison to other histogram/discrete-data estimation methods to show how they perform relative to randomized response.

In terms of related work, the paper could benefit from a more explicit comparison to Lei's M-estimation density estimator and a discussion of how the authors' work differs from previous contributions.
