This paper tackles the important problem of probability estimation under local differential privacy, providing sharp minimax rates and demonstrating the optimality of Warner’s randomized response. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and builds upon a solid foundation of existing work in differential privacy and minimax theory. The authors successfully bridge concepts from disparate fields, offering a rigorous theoretical treatment of a practically relevant problem.

The paper’s strengths lie in its technical rigor and clear presentation. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the authors provide sufficient detail to allow for verification. The derivation of the minimax rates for both multinomial estimation and density estimation is a significant contribution. Establishing the optimality of Warner's randomized response, a result with historical significance, is another strong point. The careful consideration of efficiently implementable algorithms alongside the theoretical bounds demonstrates a pragmatic approach. The connection to existing literature, including references to Warner’s original work and more recent advances in differential privacy, is thorough. The notation is generally clear, and the organization of the paper is logical.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the paper provides a comprehensive treatment of the theoretical aspects, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical considerations. For instance, the choice of the parameter 'k' in the histogram estimator, beyond simply stating k = (nα2)1/4, could be explored in more depth.  A discussion of the computational cost of the proposed estimators, particularly for higher-dimensional problems, would be valuable.  The paper assumes familiarity with a considerable body of literature; while the references are comprehensive, briefly outlining some key concepts (e.g., Sobolev spaces) within the text would improve accessibility for a broader audience. The leap between Proposition 2 and 3, while technically sound, feels a bit abrupt. More detailed intuition on how the orthogonal series estimator achieves the optimal rate, particularly regarding the impact of the noise introduced via the differentially private mechanism, would be helpful. The discussion of why perturbation of the samples directly leads to suboptimal rates is a little vague. Expanding on this point with a more detailed explanation would strengthen the argument. The comparison to other potentially relevant works in the broader area of private density estimation beyond those explicitly cited (e.g., some work from the machine learning community) could provide further context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical framework and provides sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy.
*   **Originality:** The work bridges the gap between differential privacy and minimax theory, providing novel results and insights.
*   **Significance:** The results have implications for various applications where privacy is a concern, such as survey sampling and statistical inference.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the complex concepts accessible.
*   **Optimal Mechanisms:** Demonstrating the optimality of Warner’s randomized response is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Discussion:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges and computational costs.
*   **Accessibility:** Assumes a high level of prior knowledge, potentially limiting its reach.
*   **Abrupt Transitions:** Some transitions between theoretical results and explanations could be smoother.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The technical rigor, clarity, and originality of the work are commendable. The authors have successfully established a strong theoretical foundation for understanding the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy in probability estimation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically expanding on the practical considerations and providing more intuitive explanations for some of the technical results.
