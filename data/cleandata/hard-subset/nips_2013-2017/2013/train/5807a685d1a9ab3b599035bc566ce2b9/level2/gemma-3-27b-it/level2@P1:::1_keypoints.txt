- Thorough investigation of probability estimation under local differential privacy with sharp minimax rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) settings.  
- Connects classical minimax decision theory with differential privacy, characterizing privacy–utility trade-offs.  
- Shows Warner’s randomized response is optimal for privacy-preserving survey sampling.  
- Builds on Warner and DP literature and provides matching lower and upper bounds with efficiently implementable algorithms.  
- Focuses on population-level estimation, yielding stronger lower bounds than prior sample-focused work.  
- Identifies and addresses limitations of Laplace perturbation for density estimation, proposing an alternative approach.  
- Technical soundness: rigorous derivations and correct analyses of lower and upper bounds.  
- Clarity: generally well-written, organized, and properly notated.  
- Originality: novel synthesis of minimax theory and local DP, with sharp rates and optimality results.  
- Significance: practical implications for privacy-preserving data analysis and survey sampling; quantifies cost of privacy.  
- Comprehensive: treats both discrete and continuous estimation problems.  
- Strong literature connection: situates contributions well within prior work.  
- Weakness — density implementation: choice of basis functions and parameter B need clearer guidance and practical discussion.  
- Weakness — computational complexity: more detailed discussion of algorithmic complexity is needed.  
- Weakness — limited baselines: broader comparison with other privacy-preserving density estimation methods is lacking.  
- Weakness — assumptions: relies on bounded derivatives and suitable orthonormal bases without robustness analysis to violations.  
- Weakness — notation: repetitive use of symbols (e.g., Mn) can hinder readability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem, technically strong, impactful, and well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: requires more implementation detail for density methods and broader methodological comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to expand practical considerations and comparisons.
