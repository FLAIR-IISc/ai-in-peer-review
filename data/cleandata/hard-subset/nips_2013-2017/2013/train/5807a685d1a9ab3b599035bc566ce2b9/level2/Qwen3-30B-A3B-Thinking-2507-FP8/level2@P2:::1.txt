

This paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of statistical decision theory and differential privacy by establishing sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical framework that connects Warner's classical randomized response method with modern differential privacy, demonstrating that it achieves optimal statistical efficiency for multinomial estimation. The paper's main strength lies in its comprehensive treatment of two fundamental statistical problems (multinomial estimation and density estimation) under α-local differential privacy, with tight lower and upper bounds that precisely characterize the trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The authors carefully establish the theoretical foundations, building upon minimax decision theory and differential privacy concepts. The proofs are technically sound, with the key results (Theorems 1 and 2) supported by matching lower and upper bounds. The paper makes a clear distinction between their work and prior studies on differential privacy by focusing on population estimation (θ(P)) rather than sample estimation (θ(x₁:n)), which leads to stronger lower bounds. The authors also provide efficient algorithms that achieve the theoretical rates, demonstrating practical relevance.

The paper's originality is substantial. It brings together two important research strands—minimax theory and differential privacy—in a novel way, providing the first sharp characterization of minimax rates under local privacy constraints. The insight that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation (a technique developed nearly 50 years ago) is particularly compelling. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from prior work by providing precise rates that account for the privacy parameter α, rather than just qualitative statements about privacy-utility trade-offs.

The significance of the results is high. The paper establishes fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, showing that local privacy imposes a penalty that depends on both the privacy level α and the dimensionality of the problem. For multinomial estimation, the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, while for density estimation with β smoothness, the convergence rate changes from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2). These precise characterizations provide practitioners with concrete guidance for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to results to applications. The notation is consistent, and the key results are well-explained with appropriate examples. The authors make a strong effort to place their work in context, citing relevant prior work while clearly articulating their contributions.

A few minor points for improvement: The paper could better contextualize its results relative to other recent works on private statistical estimation (e.g., Chaudhuri & Hsu, 2013; Smith, 2011), particularly regarding the computational aspects of the proposed algorithms. While the authors note that their orthogonal series estimator is "efficiently computable," a more detailed discussion of computational complexity would strengthen the practical relevance. Additionally, the paper could briefly discuss how the choice of α affects the practical utility-privacy trade-off, beyond the theoretical rates.

One technical point to clarify: The paper uses "α-local differential privacy" (equation 1) while many other works use ε-differential privacy. The authors should explicitly address the relationship between these two parameters and why they chose this specific formulation for their analysis.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical foundations of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results are technically sound, the theoretical framework is well-justified, and the practical implications are clear. The paper successfully bridges classical statistical theory with modern privacy concerns, providing sharp characterizations of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The results have the potential to influence the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures across a wide range of applications, from survey sampling to machine learning.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS and represents a high-quality contribution to the field. The main strengths are the sharp minimax rates, the connection to Warner's classical work, and the practical algorithms that achieve these rates. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's overall significance and technical quality. I recommend acceptance.
