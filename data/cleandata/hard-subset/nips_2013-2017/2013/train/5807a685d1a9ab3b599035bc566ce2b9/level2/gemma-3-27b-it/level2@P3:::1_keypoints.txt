- Paper studies privacy vs. statistical efficiency trade-offs in probability estimation under differential privacy, giving sharp minimax rates for discrete and continuous problems.  
- Demonstrates Warner’s randomized response is optimal for locally private survey sampling.  
- Connects classical privacy notions with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory.  
- The problem is well-motivated by historical and current privacy concerns.  
- Theoretical framework is rigorous, combining differential privacy with minimax optimality.  
- Results are significant, quantifying the price of privacy and proving optimality of solutions.  
- Technical analysis appears sound with clear derivations and literature connections.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and accessible despite complex ideas.  
- Inclusion of both discrete and continuous estimation broadens applicability.  
- Achievability results show the bounds are attainable with concrete algorithms.  
- Needs more detailed comparison with existing privacy-preserving inference work beyond high-level remarks.  
- Should clarify differences between the local privacy model used and central privacy in rates and implementation.  
- Computational complexity claims need quantification (e.g., dependence on problem dimension).  
- Section 4.3’s noise mechanism and scaling require a more intuitive explanation of optimality.  
- Practical implications (e.g., sample sizes for given accuracy/privacy budgets) should be discussed to enhance impact.  
- Notation issues: clarify symbols like "ck" and explicitly state smoothness parameter β requirements (e.g., integer).  
- Arguments for acceptance: fundamental problem, rigorous and significant theoretical contributions, compelling optimality results, historical connection to Warner.  
- Arguments against acceptance: highly theoretical, may lack accessibility and practical/computational exploration; related-work comparison insufficient.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper deserving acceptance pending revisions to expand practical discussion and related-work comparison; optional revisions include computational complexity analysis and clearer intuition for the noise mechanism.
