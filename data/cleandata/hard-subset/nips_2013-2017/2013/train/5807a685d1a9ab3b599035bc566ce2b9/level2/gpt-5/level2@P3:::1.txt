Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each datum is privatized before release via a memoryless channel satisfying α-local privacy. Within a minimax decision-theoretic framework, the work derives sharp rates for two canonical problems: (a) multinomial probability estimation and (b) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. For multinomial estimation in d dimensions, the minimax mean-squared and L1 risks scale as Θ(min{1, d/(nα2)}), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d for small α. This establishes that Warner’s randomized response is rate-optimal up to constants. For density estimation over an order-β Sobolev class on [0,1], the nonprivate rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), changing the exponent. Matching upper bounds are achieved via appropriately designed mechanisms: privatized histograms for β=1 and a bounded, unbiased orthogonal series mechanism for general β≥1. The analysis builds on Le Cam/Fano methods coupled with information-contraction inequalities specialized to LDP, yielding KL/mutual information bounds that drive the lower rates.

The work connects to and advances a line of research on statistical inference with privacy constraints: local privacy (Evfimievski et al., 2003), differential privacy mechanisms (Dwork et al., 2006; Hardt & Talwar, 2010), statistical frameworks for DP (Wasserman & Zhou, 2010; Smith, 2011), and private learning under local models (Kasiviswanathan et al.). It clarifies the population-risk viewpoint and gives optimal rates in the stringent LDP setting. It also relates to deconvolution lower bounds (Carroll & Hall, 1988) to show why direct additive noise is suboptimal for density estimation. The result that randomized response is minimax-optimal provides a principled endorsement of a classic technique.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound. The lower bounds carefully control information leakage under LDP via divergence bounds and then apply standard minimax testing reductions. The upper bounds are constructive and computationally reasonable (simple projection for multinomial; histogram and orthogonal-series estimators with bounded mechanisms). The discussion of why Laplace perturbation of raw samples cannot achieve optimal nonparametric rates is well-supported by deconvolution theory. Assumptions are stated clearly (non-interactive channels, α∈[0,1], bounded bases), and dependence on n, d, α, β is explicit.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, with a coherent progression from framework to results to mechanisms. The distinction between population and sample estimation is helpful. Some sections rely on results deferred to a longer version; the main ideas and statements required to understand the contributions are present, but a brief proof sketch for the key information bounds would further aid readability. Notation is consistent, though a summarized table of variables/channels could help readers less familiar with LDP.

Originality:
The paper provides, to the best of this reviewer’s knowledge, the first sharp minimax rates for these estimation problems under local privacy, including an unexpected change in the nonparametric exponent and a proof of optimality for randomized response in the multinomial setting. The orthogonal-series mechanism tailored to LDP to achieve the optimal rate is novel and insightful.

Significance:
Results are of high importance. Local privacy is widely deployed in practice, and understanding the precise privacy–accuracy trade-offs is central to both theory and applications. The identification of optimal mechanisms and provable rates will likely influence subsequent work on private estimation, learning under LDP, and mechanism design.

Pros for acceptance:
- Sharp, principled minimax rates under LDP for both discrete and continuous settings, with matching upper and lower bounds.
- Clear characterization of privacy–statistical efficiency trade-offs, including exponent change in nonparametrics.
- Optimality of randomized response is established, resolving a long-standing question rigorously.
- Mechanisms are constructive and computationally simple.
- Strong connections to and advancement over prior DP and statistical decision theory.

Cons and limitations:
- Focuses on non-interactive (memoryless) mechanisms; potential gains from interaction are not explored.
- Practical guidance on tuning (e.g., choice of k and constants) and finite-sample behavior is limited; no empirical illustration.
- Dependence on bounded bases and knowledge of smoothness β is assumed; adaptivity to unknown β is not treated.
- Some proofs and technical details are deferred to a longer version.

Suggestions for improvement:
Essential:
- Clarify the novelty relative to the cited extended version [arXiv:1302.3203], specifying what is new in this submission (e.g., tightened constants, new mechanisms, additional problem classes).
- Provide a brief proof sketch for the key LDP information-contraction inequalities to make the paper more self-contained.

Nice-to-have (optional):
- Add a short empirical section or simulations corroborating the predicted rates and constants for multinomial and Lipschitz density cases.
- Discuss whether interactive LDP protocols could improve rates in either problem, or provide a brief argument suggesting optimality of the non-interactive setting here.
- Comment on adaptivity to unknown β (e.g., Lepski-type selection) under LDP and whether the optimal rate is achievable adaptively.
- Include guidance on choosing k and constants in practice, and complexity notes for the orthogonal-series sampling scheme.
- Expand on robustness to model misspecification (e.g., densities outside the Sobolev class) and to larger α regimes.

Overall assessment:
A strong theoretical contribution establishing foundational limits and optimal mechanisms for estimation under local differential privacy. The results are technically solid, novel, and significant for the NeurIPS community. Pending minor clarifications, the paper merits acceptance.
