Summary
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each user privatizes their data before releasing it to the statistician. It develops a minimax framework to quantify the fundamental tradeoffs between privacy and statistical efficiency and provides sharp (matching) rates for two canonical problems:

- Multinomial probability estimation in d dimensions: the minimax mean-squared error scales as d/(nα^2) (up to constants), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα^2/d in the small-α regime. The paper shows that a variant of Warner’s randomized response is minimax-optimal under LDP. 
- Nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1]: privacy changes the polynomial rate from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} (non-private) to (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under LDP. The authors provide matching, efficiently implementable upper bounds using privatized histograms (β=1) and a privatized orthogonal series estimator (general β≥1) with a bespoke local randomization mechanism.

Technically, the lower bounds rely on contraction inequalities that relate KL/mutual information after an LDP channel to pre-channel total variation quantities, combined with Le Cam/Fano arguments. Upper bounds are achieved by explicit private mechanisms and estimators whose tuning matches the lower-bound rate.

Relation to prior work
The work connects classical randomized response (Warner ’65) and minimax decision theory with modern differential privacy. It complements prior central-DP results (e.g., Wasserman & Zhou; Smith; Hardt & Talwar) and local-DP learning results (Kasiviswanathan et al.) by giving sharp, problem-specific minimax rates in the local model. The deconvolution lower bound (Carroll & Hall) is used to argue the suboptimality of direct additive noise on raw samples for density estimation, motivating the tailored mechanisms.

Quality
- Correctness: The lower-bound technique is sound and well-placed in the information-theoretic toolkit; the upper bounds are constructive and plausible. The claim that randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is well-supported. The density estimation achievability via privatized series estimators is technically interesting and appears to meet the lower bound.
- Rigor and completeness: Several key inequalities are stated (Proposition 1) with proofs deferred to the authors’ longer work [9]. While acceptable for space, it would help to ensure the conference version contains proof sketches highlighting the main ideas and any delicate steps (e.g., the variational bound in (7) and its optimization over sets S).
- Scope: The work focuses on non-interactive channels. This is clearly stated. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether interaction can improve rates in these problems, or to argue that it cannot.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well-organized, with careful definitions and motivation. The construction and analysis of the multinomial mechanism and the histogram estimator are easy to follow.
- Minor issues:
  - Equation (8) appears to contain a term sqrt(nα^2) inside a “min” for an MSE risk; this is dimensionally inconsistent and presumably a typo. Please correct/clarify the precise lower-bound expression.
  - The orthogonal-series mechanism introduces a constant ck and uses rejection sampling; the acceptance rate/complexity and the range of ck (bounded away from 0) should be made explicit to support the “efficiently computable” claim.
  - The Sobolev class is defined on [0,1] with periodic boundary conditions via the trigonometric basis. A brief comment on extending to compact intervals without periodicity or to R would aid readers.

Originality
- High. The paper provides sharp minimax rates under local privacy for classical estimation problems, identifies the precise change in exponent for nonparametric densities, and isolates randomized response as optimal for multinomial estimation within LDP. The general information-contraction bounds under LDP and their deployment in minimax lower bounds are valuable and appear broadly applicable.

Significance
- Significant. The results quantify the privacy–utility tradeoff in concrete problems, offering guidance for practitioners (e.g., effective sample size under LDP) and setting benchmarks for future private estimators. The constructive mechanisms (histogram/series) show that optimality is attainable with simple estimators, not just lower bounds. These ideas are likely to influence further work on private nonparametrics and on designing optimal LDP mechanisms.

Pros
- Sharp, matching minimax rates under LDP for multinomial and density estimation.
- Clean, interpretable privacy-induced penalty (effective sample size scaling; change in nonparametric exponent).
- Conceptually clarifies the optimality of randomized response in LDP.
- Technically solid information-theoretic lower bounds and constructive, efficient mechanisms.

Cons
- No empirical validation or simulations to illustrate constants, finite-sample behavior, and robustness to tuning; even small synthetic experiments would be informative.
- Reliance on a companion technical report for key proofs may limit accessibility.
- Non-interactive focus; limited discussion on whether interaction could improve rates.
- Minor clarity issues (possible typo in (8); details of the orthogonal-series mechanism’s constants and computational overhead).

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the apparent typo in Theorem 1’s lower bound and verify all units/rates across norms.
- Include brief proof sketches for the central information-contraction results to make the NIPS version self-contained.
- Provide a short empirical study (synthetic) comparing: (i) naive Laplace-on-samples, (ii) randomized response, (iii) the proposed histogram/series estimators, highlighting rate behavior and constants.
- Discuss interaction: can interactive LDP protocols improve rates in multinomial/density estimation? If known, state impossibility or point to open problems.
- Clarify the constants and efficiency of the orthogonal-series mechanism (bounds on ck, acceptance rate, and dependence on k, α).
- Comment on adaptivity: can k be chosen data-privately (e.g., via private cross-validation) without losing rates?

Recommendation
Accept. This is a strong theoretical paper with clear, original, and significant contributions, sharp results, and constructive mechanisms. Addressing minor clarity and completeness issues would further strengthen the submission.

Confidence
High.
