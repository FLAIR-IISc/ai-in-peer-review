

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, making significant contributions to the intersection of privacy and statistical inference. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for both multinomial estimation and density estimation under α-local differential privacy, demonstrating that privacy imposes fundamental trade-offs between statistical efficiency and privacy guarantees.

The main contributions include: (1) showing that for multinomial estimation, the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d, with Warner's randomized response mechanism being optimal; (2) demonstrating that for density estimation with β continuous derivatives, the minimax rate degrades from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)). The paper provides matching lower and upper bounds, with carefully constructed algorithms that achieve these rates, including a privatized histogram estimator for Lipschitz densities and an orthogonal series estimator for higher smoothness.

The paper is technically sound, with well-justified theoretical results supported by comprehensive proofs. The writing is clear and well-organized, with appropriate notation and careful discussion of connections to prior work. The novelty lies in the precise characterization of the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off using minimax decision theory, which bridges classical statistical theory with modern privacy frameworks. The identification that Warner's classical randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly noteworthy, offering theoretical justification for a method that has been used for nearly 50 years.

The significance of the results is substantial, as they provide fundamental insights into the costs of privacy in statistical estimation. The paper's theoretical framework establishes a principled approach for understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility, which is valuable for both practitioners and researchers in privacy-preserving statistics.

One minor limitation is that the paper focuses solely on theoretical analysis without empirical validation, which is understandable given its theoretical nature. The authors could consider discussing potential extensions to more complex statistical models or practical implementation challenges of their proposed mechanisms.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical estimation. The paper meets all criteria for a strong NIPS submission, with significant novelty, technical quality, and potential impact on the field. I recommend acceptance.
