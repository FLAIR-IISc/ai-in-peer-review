Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies distribution estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). The setting assumes each sample Xi is privatized independently via a channel Q satisfying α-local DP, and only the privatized Zi are observed. The authors develop sharp minimax rates for two canonical problems:

- Multinomial estimation on the d-simplex: they prove matching upper and lower bounds for ℓ2- and ℓ1-risk. In particular, E[‖θ̂−θ‖2 2] scales as min{1, d/(nα2)}, and E[‖θ̂−θ‖1] as min{1, d/√(nα2)}. This shows an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d when α is small. They also show that a variant of Warner’s randomized response (RR) attains the optimal rate, as does a simple Laplace-perturbation scheme.

- Nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1]: they prove a lower bound Mn ≳ (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), strictly worse in exponent than the classical non-private rate n−2β/(2β+1). They provide two constructive schemes that match this lower bound: (i) a privatized histogram achieving the optimal rate for Lipschitz densities (β=1), and (ii) a locally private orthonormal series estimator with a carefully designed privatization mechanism that is not simple additive noise; it achieves the optimal rate for all β≥1. They argue that direct additive noise on Xi (e.g., Laplace) cannot attain the optimal rate due to deconvolution limits.

Technically, the lower bounds combine Assouad/Fano/Le Cam methods with information-contraction inequalities under LDP. The paper restates bounds on KL and mutual information that depend on α and TV distances between hypotheses and uses them to control testing risk under privatization. This connects classic minimax theory with modern privacy notions. Relative to prior work (Warner randomized response; Dwork et al. differential privacy; Wasserman–Zhou statistical DP; Hardt–Talwar geometry; Smith on optimal DP rates), this paper focuses on population minimax risk under LDP and delivers sharp rates plus optimal mechanisms, which appears novel and significant.

Quality:
- The results are technically sound as presented. The rates and matching constructions are consistent and carefully stated (including regimes and constants). The argument that RR is optimal in the multinomial case is compelling and useful.
- For density estimation, the change in minimax exponent under LDP is a striking and well-justified finding. The orthogonal-series mechanism is thoughtfully designed to ensure both LDP and unbiasedness, and the choice of B scaling shows the α dependence needed to achieve the target risk.
- Some key proofs are deferred to a companion source; including at least core proof sketches in the supplementary would strengthen self-containment. The efficiency claims for the series mechanism would benefit from a brief complexity analysis and sampling pseudocode.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well organized, with helpful background on minimax testing reductions and notation. The multinomial mechanism and estimator are explicit and easy to implement. The density section clearly lays out the class, metrics, and estimators. A few constants (e.g., ck) are referenced abstractly; giving guidance on how to set them in practice would aid reproducibility.

Originality:
- High. The sharp minimax characterization under LDP for both discrete and nonparametric settings, the identification of the exponent degradation for densities, and the optimality of RR in a modern LDP framework are novel contributions. The information-contraction bounds tailored to LDP and their use in Fano/Le Cam reductions consolidate a useful methodology.

Significance:
- High. The work offers a principled privacy–utility trade-off for fundamental estimation tasks, with concrete, implementable mechanisms. Establishing RR as optimal under LDP is practically impactful. The nonparametric results clarify the intrinsic statistical cost of local privacy and will likely guide future algorithm design.

Pros:
- Sharp, matching lower and upper bounds in both problems.
- Clear identification of effective sample size under LDP for multinomials.
- New, rate-optimal locally private mechanisms for density estimation.
- Methodological contribution linking LDP to minimax information bounds.
- Practical relevance (randomized response; simple histogram variant).

Cons / questions for authors:
- Non-interactive restriction: Could interactivity improve rates in these problems, or are the rates tight even for interactive LDP? A brief discussion or citation would help.
- Adaptivity to unknown smoothness β: Can the orthogonal-series approach be made adaptive (e.g., Lepski, cross-validation) while preserving LDP and rates up to logs?
- Computational aspects of the series mechanism: Please detail a practical sampler for Z in high dimension k, and its time/communication complexity. Is there a low-variance alternative with the same privacy and mean?
- Constants and calibration: The mechanism requires B and an implicit ck. How should practitioners set these without over- or under-noising?
- Communication efficiency in the multinomial mechanism: The RR variant uses a d-dimensional 0/1 vector. Is a k-ary RR with O(log d) bits per response equally rate-optimal under the same α?
- Robustness: How sensitive are the rates to model misspecification (e.g., densities outside Sobolev, boundedness assumptions on ϕj)? Any extension to multivariate densities?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses core NIPS topics (statistical learning, privacy) with rigorous theory and constructive algorithms.
- Delivers clean, sharp, and interpretable results with practical implications (optimality of RR, histogram guidance).
- Advances understanding of information–privacy contraction in minimax analysis.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Reliance on external proofs reduces self-containment; some mechanisms’ computational aspects could be elaborated.
- Scope limited to local privacy; a comparison with central DP rates would broaden context.

Suggestion:
- Include proof sketches for the main theorems in the supplementary.
- Add a short empirical illustration to display constants and practical performance of the proposed mechanisms.

Overall recommendation:
A strong paper with clear, sharp, and significant contributions at the interface of minimax statistics and local differential privacy. I recommend acceptance, contingent on improving self-containment and clarifying implementation details.
