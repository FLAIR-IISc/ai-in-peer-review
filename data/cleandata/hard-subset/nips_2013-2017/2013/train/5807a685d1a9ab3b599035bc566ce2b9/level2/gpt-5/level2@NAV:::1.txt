Summary: The paper studies probability estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), providing sharp minimax rates for both discrete (multinomial) and nonparametric density estimation. The main claims are: (i) for d-dimensional multinomials, LDP with parameter α reduces the effective sample size from n to nα^2/d, with matching lower and upper bounds; randomized response (Warner) is shown to be optimal up to constants. (ii) For density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ, privacy changes the polynomial rate from n−2β/(2β+1) to (α^2n)−2β/(2β+2), and the paper gives efficient mechanisms (private histograms for β=1 and orthogonal series for β≥1) attaining these rates. The lower bounds build on Le Cam/Fano combined with new information bounds for LDP channels; the upper bounds use simple, implementable mechanisms and show that additive Laplace noise on the raw samples is provably suboptimal. This connects classical randomized response [Warner] with modern DP [Dwork et al., Hardt & Talwar, Wasserman & Zhou] and statistical decision theory (Assouad/Fano).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound, with matching rates and explicit mechanisms. The information-theoretic lemmas are plausible and appropriately leveraged. Some proofs are deferred to a longer version; providing concise proof sketches would strengthen completeness. No empirical evaluation is presented, though not required.

Clarity: Generally well organized, but notation is dense. More intuition for the key inequalities, and a figure or table summarizing rates would help. The sampling scheme for orthogonal series could use pseudocode and complexity discussion.

Originality: High. Sharp LDP minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation, the optimality of randomized response in a general minimax sense, and suboptimality of naïve Laplace perturbation for densities are novel and important.

Significance/usefulness: High. Results quantify the privacy–utility trade-off and provide practical mechanisms. This advances understanding of LDP beyond previous global-DP risk analyses.

Limitations and suggestions: 
- Scope restricted to non-interactive LDP; discuss whether interactivity can improve rates. 
- Adaptivity to unknown smoothness β and data-driven bin/series selection under privacy would enhance practicality. 
- Provide finite-sample constants or small simulations to illustrate the n→nα^2/d effect. 
- Clarify assumptions (bounded basis, α∈[0,1]) and robustness to misspecification.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Sharp, matching bounds; clear theoretical contribution.
- Practical, efficient mechanisms; clarifies optimality of randomized response.
- Broad relevance to privacy-preserving statistics.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on external longer version for proofs.
- Limited discussion of interactivity/adaptivity; no empirical illustrations.

Overall: Strong accept.
