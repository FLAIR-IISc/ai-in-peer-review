- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy, establishing sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation.  
- Bridges Warner’s randomized response with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory, quantifying privacy–utility trade-offs.  
- Multinomial result: local privacy at level α reduces effective sample size from n to nα²/d; Warner’s randomized response is minimax optimal.  
- Density result: non-private rate n⁻²β/(2β+1) degrades to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2) under local privacy (Theorem 2).  
- Lower bounds are matched by constructive upper bounds using histogram and orthogonal series estimators (Propositions 2–3), showing tightness.  
- Technical approach is rigorous, using information-theoretic tools (Lemma 1, Proposition 1) and a clearly defined minimax framework.  
- Provides practical, constructive algorithms (randomized response estimator, histogram, orthogonal series) making theory applicable.  
- Writing is clear, well-organized, with careful notation and logical exposition.  
- Minor issue: explanation of why Laplace perturbation fails could be strengthened by detailing characteristic-function tail effects leading to the suboptimal rate.  
- Minor issue: computational complexity of the orthogonal series estimator could be clarified beyond “efficiently computable.”  
- Could add a brief discussion of practical implications for survey design or data collection.  
- Originality and significance are high: first to establish sharp minimax rates under local privacy, with direct relevance to survey methodology.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution, rigorous and elegant; recommendation for acceptance.
