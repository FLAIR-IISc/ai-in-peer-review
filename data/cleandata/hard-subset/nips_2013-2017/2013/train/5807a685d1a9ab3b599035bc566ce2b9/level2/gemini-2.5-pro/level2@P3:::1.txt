This paper provides a foundational study of the trade-off between statistical efficiency and privacy guarantees in the setting of local differential privacy. It connects the statistical framework of minimax decision theory with the computer science notion of differential privacy to derive fundamental limits on estimation accuracy. The main contributions are sharp minimax rates of convergence for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, the work shows that α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `n * α^2 / d`, and remarkably, that Warner's 1965 randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. For nonparametric estimation of a density with β derivatives, the paper reveals a more severe penalty: the convergence rate is altered from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(n * α^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, a change in the polynomial exponent. The analysis is completed by providing computationally efficient procedures that achieve these optimal rates.

This work builds upon a rich history of minimax theory in statistics and the growing literature on differential privacy. It makes a significant conceptual advance by shifting the focus from the privacy of a specific dataset to analyzing the estimation error of population parameters, which provides stronger, more fundamental lower bounds. The paper rigorously formalizes the intuition that privacy must come at a statistical cost and provides the tools to precisely quantify this cost for important estimation problems.

**Quality and Significance:**

The paper is of very high quality, both in its technical execution and in the importance of its results.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The technical contributions are substantial. By deriving matching upper and lower bounds ("sharp rates"), the paper provides a complete and definitive characterization of the minimax risk for the problems studied. This is a hallmark of excellent theoretical work.
    *   The results are of fundamental significance. The framework developed offers a principled and powerful way to understand the inherent trade-offs between privacy and utility. This moves the field beyond analyzing specific algorithms to understanding the fundamental limits of any possible private algorithm.
    *   The insights generated are profound. The discovery that local privacy can change the polynomial rate of convergence for density estimation is a novel and striking result. Furthermore, demonstrating that a nearly 50-year-old method (randomized response) is optimal within the modern framework of differential privacy is a beautiful and elegant finding that connects historical and contemporary research.
    *   The work is likely to have high impact, laying the groundwork for a new line of inquiry into minimax optimal private statistical inference. Researchers in machine learning, statistics, and theoretical computer science will be able to build on these ideas to analyze a wide range of other problems.

*   **Weaknesses/Areas for Improvement:**
    *   The technical arguments rely on results from a companion paper [9], particularly for the key information-theoretic bounds in Proposition 1. While common for conference submissions, this makes the paper not entirely self-contained and complicates a full verification of the claims from the manuscript alone.
    *   The motivation for the sophisticated sampling strategy in Section 4.3 for the orthogonal series estimator could be strengthened. The paper correctly notes that simpler additive noise mechanisms are suboptimal, but a brief, intuitive explanation in the main text for *why* they fail and why a more complex mechanism is necessary would enhance the reader's understanding.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The logical flow from the general framework to specific applications is effective. The introduction successfully situates the work and articulates its contributions. The main theorems are stated precisely, and their implications are explained well. However, the presentation is extremely dense due to the technical depth of the material. While likely unavoidable, this may limit its accessibility to readers not already deeply familiar with both minimax theory and differential privacy.

**Originality:**

The originality of this work is exceptionally high.

*   The central idea of applying the full power of minimax decision theory to the local differential privacy setting is novel and represents a significant conceptual leap.
*   The results themselves are new and non-obvious. Prior to this work, the precise minimax rates for these fundamental problems under local privacy were unknown.
*   The achievability schemes, particularly the orthogonal series estimator for density estimation, are non-trivial constructions tailored to meet the privacy constraints while matching the lower bounds.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** Establishes a foundational and rigorous link between statistical minimax theory and local differential privacy.
    2.  **Highly Significant Results:** Provides sharp, definitive rates for fundamental estimation problems, revealing deep insights into the cost of privacy.
    3.  **Technical Excellence:** The work is technically deep, sound, and complete, presenting both lower bounds and matching, constructive upper bounds.
    4.  **High Impact Potential:** The framework and results are poised to inspire a significant amount of follow-up research.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    1.  The primary weakness is the reliance on a companion paper for key proofs, which slightly undermines the self-contained nature of the submission. However, this is a minor point in light of the contribution's strength.

In summary, this is a landmark paper that makes a fundamental, technically deep, and highly significant contribution to the understanding of private data analysis. It provides a definitive answer to the question of the optimal statistical accuracy achievable for two core estimation problems under local privacy constraints. The strengths of the paper are exceptional and far outweigh any minor weaknesses.
