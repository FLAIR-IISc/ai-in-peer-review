This paper provides a comprehensive study of the estimation of probability distributions in a locally private setting. The authors develop a theoretical framework that combines differential privacy with minimax decision theory to characterize optimal estimation under constraints on privacy. The paper focuses on two main problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation.

The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for these problems, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. They show that providing local privacy can be detrimental to the quality of statistical estimators and that the effective sample size decreases with the level of privacy.

The paper also presents efficiently implementable algorithms that attain sharp upper bounds, including a privatized histogram estimator and an orthogonal series estimator. These results demonstrate that the minimax lower bounds are sharp and provide a private analog of classical results in non-private settings.

The authors' use of differential privacy and minimax decision theory provides a rigorous framework for analyzing the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper's results have implications for the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures and highlight the importance of considering the privacy-utility trade-off in statistical inference.
