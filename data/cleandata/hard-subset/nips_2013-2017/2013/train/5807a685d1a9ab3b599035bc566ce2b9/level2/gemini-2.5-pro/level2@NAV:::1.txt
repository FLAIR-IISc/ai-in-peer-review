This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of statistical estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors apply minimax theory to derive sharp convergence rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The key claims are that for d-dimensional multinomial estimation, α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to approximately nα²/d. For estimating a density with β derivatives, privacy fundamentally changes the optimal rate from n⁻²β/(²β⁺¹) to (nα²)⁻²β/(²β⁺²). The paper connects this modern privacy framework to classical work by showing that Warner's randomized response is, in fact, a minimax optimal mechanism. For both problems, the authors provide computationally efficient estimators that achieve these optimal rates, demonstrating that the lower bounds are tight.

The paper's main strength is its foundational contribution to understanding the inherent trade-off between privacy and statistical accuracy. The technical quality is excellent, with tight, matching lower and upper bounds that provide a complete theoretical picture. The originality is high; it is among the first to apply the full power of minimax analysis to the local privacy setting and to characterize how privacy constraints alter fundamental statistical limits. The result establishing the optimality of Warner’s 50-year-old method is particularly elegant and significant. The paper is very well-written, clearly motivating the problem and situating its results within the broader literature on both privacy and statistical estimation.

The paper has very few weaknesses. One minor suggestion for improvement would be to provide more intuition for the complex orthogonal series sampling mechanism in Section 4.3, as the current description is quite dense. Additionally, while deferring full proofs to a longer version is standard, including a brief proof sketch for the main lower bounds in an appendix would have made the submission more self-contained and impactful.

Overall, this is an outstanding paper that makes a significant and original contribution. It expertly bridges statistical decision theory and modern privacy concepts, delivering sharp, important results that will likely be influential for future work in this area. The paper strongly satisfies the criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
