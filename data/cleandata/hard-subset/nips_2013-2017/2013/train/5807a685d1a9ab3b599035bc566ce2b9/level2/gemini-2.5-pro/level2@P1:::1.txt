### Review

**Summary of the paper:**

This paper establishes a rigorous connection between the concept of local differential privacy and the tools of minimax decision theory. The authors aim to characterize the fundamental trade-offs between the level of privacy guaranteed and the achievable statistical accuracy for classical estimation problems. The work focuses on two specific settings: estimating the parameters of a d-dimensional multinomial distribution and nonparametric estimation of a probability density function belonging to a Sobolev smoothness class.

For multinomial estimation, the paper demonstrates that imposing α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα^2/d`. A key and elegant finding is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal in this setting. For density estimation of functions with `β` derivatives, the authors show a more severe penalty: local privacy changes the polynomial exponent of the minimax convergence rate from `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. The paper provides matching upper bounds by constructing explicit, efficiently implementable estimators (a private histogram for `β=1` and a private orthogonal series estimator for general `β`) that achieve these optimal rates.

**Quality:**

The paper is of very high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors derive sharp minimax rates by providing both lower bounds (using information-theoretic tools like Fano's inequality) and matching upper bounds (by constructing explicit estimators). This represents a complete and compelling piece of theoretical work. The authors are careful to distinguish their work from prior art, correctly pointing out that their lower bounds on estimating population parameters are stronger than those on estimating sample quantities. The discussion on the sub-optimality of naive privacy mechanisms, such as adding Laplace noise directly to the samples, is insightful and underscores the importance of carefully designing privacy-preserving mechanisms.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and clear. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the concepts of local differential privacy and the minimax framework before diving into their main results. The problem statements are precise, and the main theorems are stated clearly, with their implications explained in an intuitive way. The connection to Warner's classic work is highlighted effectively, making the results accessible to a broad audience. The structure, moving from the finite-dimensional multinomial problem to the infinite-dimensional density estimation problem, is logical and effective.

**Originality:**

The paper is highly original. While there has been growing interest in the intersection of differential privacy and statistics, this work is one of the first to apply the full power of minimax decision theory to the problem of *local* privacy. Deriving fundamental limits that hold for *any* possible algorithm is a significant conceptual advance over analyzing specific proposed algorithms. The key contributions—quantifying the exact price of privacy for these canonical problems and proving the optimality of a 50-year-old mechanism within this modern framework—are novel and insightful. The discovery that privacy can alter the polynomial convergence rate for nonparametric problems is a particularly important and non-obvious finding.

**Significance:**

The results presented in this paper are of high significance to both the machine learning and statistics communities. The local privacy model is becoming increasingly important as it provides strong guarantees to individuals without relying on a trusted data curator. This paper provides the first sharp, quantitative characterization of the statistical costs of implementing such a model. These results provide a foundational benchmark against which future privacy-preserving algorithms can be measured. Researchers and practitioners can use these findings to understand the inherent limits of what can be learned under local privacy constraints and to make informed decisions about the trade-off between user privacy and model accuracy. The ideas and techniques are likely to be built upon to analyze a wider range of statistical problems under privacy constraints.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a fundamental and rigorous characterization of the trade-off between local privacy and statistical accuracy.
*   Presents sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for two canonical statistical problems, which is the gold standard for this type of theoretical work.
*   The result on the optimality of Warner's randomized response is an elegant and impactful connection between classical and modern literature.
*   The finding that privacy changes the convergence rate exponent for nonparametric estimation is a deep and important insight.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and will be of broad interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The proofs of the main results are deferred to a companion technical report. While this is common practice for conference submissions due to space constraints, including sketches of the key proof ideas (particularly for Proposition 1) in an appendix would have made the paper more self-contained.
*   The optimal mechanism for density estimation in Section 4.3 is quite complex. A more detailed discussion of its practical implementation and computational cost would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Overall assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to our understanding of privacy-preserving machine learning and statistics. The technical results are strong, the insights are novel and significant, and the presentation is exceptionally clear. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall impact of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
