## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in probability estimation, specifically within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems under this privacy constraint, building upon recent work in differential privacy and classical minimax theory. The work is motivated by the historical context of privacy in surveys (Warner, 1965) and aims to provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding these trade-offs.

The core contribution lies in establishing how local privacy impacts the achievable convergence rates. For multinomial estimation, the authors demonstrate an effective sample size reduction of n to nα²/d, highlighting the cost of privacy. More strikingly, for density estimation, they show a deviation from the classical rate, achieving (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2) compared to the standard n⁻²β/(2β+1). They also provide efficiently implementable algorithms (randomized response for multinomial, and a carefully constructed orthogonal series estimator for density estimation) that achieve these bounds, demonstrating attainability.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The authors clearly define the problem, establish necessary background, and present their results with appropriate mathematical rigor. The connection to Warner’s work is well-placed, and the use of differential privacy as a formalization of privacy concerns is justified. The proofs, while not included in the submission, are referenced to a companion paper, which is a reasonable approach for a conference submission. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the identified trade-offs. While the theoretical results are strong, a more intuitive explanation of how these rates translate to real-world scenarios would be valuable. Additionally, the discussion of alternative privacy mechanisms for density estimation (beyond the Laplace perturbation) could be expanded. While they mention it's suboptimal, a brief comparison would strengthen the argument for their proposed approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** Provides sharp minimax rates for a fundamental problem under a relevant privacy constraint.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized.
*   **Practical Algorithms:** Achievable bounds are demonstrated with concrete algorithms.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** Bridges classical statistical theory with modern privacy concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Discussion:** Could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of real-world implications.
*   **Brief Comparison of Alternatives:** A more thorough comparison of alternative density estimation methods would be helpful.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. Despite minor areas for improvement, the work is of high quality and relevant to the NIPS audience. I recommend acceptance.
