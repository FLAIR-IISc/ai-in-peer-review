- Provides a rigorous minimax analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy, establishing sharp rates for multinomial and density estimation problems.
- Bridges classical statistical decision theory with differential privacy, showing privacy fundamentally alters statistical convergence rates.
- Extends Warner’s 1965 randomized response within a minimax framework and proves its minimax optimality for multinomial estimation.
- Shows lower bounds that match efficiently computable upper bounds, demonstrating tight trade-offs between privacy level α and statistical efficiency.
- Theorem 1 (multinomial): effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d, with Warner’s method achieving this bound.
- Theorem 2 (density): privacy changes the Sobolev-smooth density rate exponent from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2).
- Propositions 2 and 3 give constructive, computationally feasible estimators (histogram and orthogonal series) that achieve the lower bounds.
- Technical proofs are rigorous, using minimax reductions to hypothesis testing and information-theoretic arguments (Proposition 1); notation and writing are clear.
- Novelty is substantial—first paper to establish sharp minimax rates for local privacy in both discrete and nonparametric settings and to resolve Warner’s optimality.
- Significance is high, providing foundational insights into the statistical cost of privacy with implications for survey design, data sharing, and private ML.
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical implementation and computational complexity for high-dimensional settings when k becomes large.
- Weakness: analysis restricted to univariate density estimation; multivariate extensions are not addressed.
- Weakness: underdeveloped connection to contemporary privacy-preserving applications and practical system design guidance.
- Meets NIPS criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) and is recommended for acceptance with minor revisions (add discussion of computational scalability and multivariate extensions).
