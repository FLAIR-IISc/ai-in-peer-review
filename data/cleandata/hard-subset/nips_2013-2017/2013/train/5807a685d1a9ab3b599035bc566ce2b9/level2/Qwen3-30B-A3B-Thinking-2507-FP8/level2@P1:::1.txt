

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, establishing sharp minimax convergence rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors bridge classical privacy work (Warner's randomized response) with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy guarantees and statistical efficiency.

The paper's main contributions are compelling and well-executed. For multinomial estimation, Theorem 1 establishes that local privacy at level α reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d, with Warner's randomized response mechanism shown to be minimax optimal (Theorem 1 and Section 3.2). For density estimation (Section 4), the authors prove a striking rate change: while non-private minimax rates scale as n⁻²β/(2β+1), locally private rates degrade to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2) (Theorem 2). The authors complement these lower bounds with efficient upper bounds via histogram and orthogonal series estimators (Propositions 2-3), demonstrating tightness of the rates. The connection to Warner's 50-year-old work being optimal is particularly elegant and well-motivated.

Strengths are numerous. The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs using information-theoretic tools (Lemma 1, Proposition 1), properly contextualizing the work within both classical privacy literature (Warner [23]) and modern differential privacy (Dwork et al. [11]). The minimax framework is clearly defined (Section 2.1), and the results are significant—they quantify the precise statistical cost of privacy in fundamental estimation problems. The authors also provide constructive algorithms (e.g., the randomized response estimator in Section 3.2, histogram estimator in Section 4.2), making the theoretical results immediately applicable. The writing is clear and well-organized, with careful notation and logical flow from background to results.

Minor weaknesses are present but not critical. In Section 4.1, the argument about why Laplace perturbation fails could be strengthened by briefly explaining how the characteristic function tail behavior (|φ_W(t)| = O(|t|⁻²)) leads to the suboptimal n⁻²β/(2β+5) rate, rather than simply citing Carroll and Hall [4]. While the orthogonal series estimator (21) is shown to be optimal, the computational complexity (beyond the note that sampling is "efficiently computable") could be clarified for readers concerned about implementation. The paper's focus on theoretical rates, while appropriate for NIPS, might benefit from a brief discussion of practical implications for survey design or data collection.

The paper excels in originality—this is the first work to establish sharp minimax rates for these problems under local privacy, resolving a key theoretical gap. The significance is high: the results provide a quantitative foundation for privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical practice, with the Warner optimality result having immediate relevance to survey methodology. The authors properly reference prior work (including NIPS papers like Duchi et al. [9]), and the results are clearly distinct from previous contributions (e.g., Chaudhuri et al. [6] on empirical risk minimization).

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, meeting all key criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), offers novel theoretical insights (Originality), and provides significant advances in understanding privacy-utility trade-offs (Significance). The results advance the field by transforming qualitative privacy discussions into precise statistical trade-offs.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical contribution that effectively bridges two important areas. The authors have made a substantial advance in understanding the statistical costs of privacy, with results that are both elegant and practically relevant. The paper is well-written, rigorous, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
