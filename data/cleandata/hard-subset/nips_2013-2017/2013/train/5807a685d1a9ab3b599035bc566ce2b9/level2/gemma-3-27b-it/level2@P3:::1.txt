This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, building on the framework of differential privacy. The work provides sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems under local privacy constraints. A key contribution is demonstrating that Warner’s randomized response mechanism is optimal for survey sampling with privacy. The paper successfully connects classical notions of privacy with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory.

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The problem setting is well-motivated, stemming from both historical concerns about data disclosure and contemporary demands for privacy-preserving data analysis. The theoretical framework, combining differential privacy with minimax optimality, is rigorous and well-developed. The results are significant, providing quantifiable bounds on the price of privacy and demonstrating the optimality of a long-standing solution (randomized response). The technical analysis appears sound, with clear derivations and connections to existing literature. The paper is well-written and organized, making the complex ideas relatively accessible. The inclusion of both discrete and continuous estimation problems broadens the applicability of the findings.  The demonstration of achievability results, showing that the derived bounds are attainable with concrete algorithms, adds substantial value.

However, several areas could benefit from further clarification or elaboration. While the paper references prior work on differential privacy, a more detailed comparison with existing approaches for privacy-preserving statistical inference (beyond simply mentioning limitations) would be helpful. Specifically, how does the local privacy model considered here compare to central privacy in terms of achievable rates and practical implementation?  The discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed estimators could be expanded. While the paper claims efficiency, quantifying the computational cost (e.g., dependence on the problem dimension) would be valuable.  In Section 4.3, the discussion of the noise mechanism used in conjunction with orthogonal series estimation feels somewhat abrupt. A more intuitive explanation of *why* this specific noise distribution and scaling are optimal would enhance understanding.  The connection between the derived rates and practical implications for real-world applications could be strengthened. For example, how many respondents would be needed in a survey to achieve a specific level of accuracy with a given privacy budget? While the focus is on theoretical bounds, a brief discussion of practical considerations would enhance the paper’s impact.  The notation could be improved. For example, clarifying what "ck" represents in Section 4.3 would be helpful. The statement about the smoothness constraint of f in Section 4.3 can be made clearer; for example, explicitly mentioning that the smoothness parameter β needs to be an integer.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses a fundamental and timely problem in statistical inference. The theoretical results are rigorous, significant, and provide sharp bounds. The optimality results are particularly compelling. The connection to Warner’s work is historically interesting and provides a concrete validation of the theory. The work contributes to a growing body of literature on privacy-preserving data analysis and provides valuable tools for practitioners. 

Arguments against acceptance: The paper is highly theoretical and may not be immediately accessible to a broad audience. While the authors provide achievability results, the practical implications and computational costs are not fully explored. A more detailed comparison with related work in differential privacy and privacy-preserving statistical inference would be beneficial. 

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed with revisions. The potential impact of the work justifies acceptance to NIPS, contingent upon addressing the reviewer's comments.  Essential revisions would include expanding the discussion of practical implications and providing a clearer comparison with related work. Optional revisions would include a more detailed analysis of computational complexity and a more intuitive explanation of the noise mechanism in Section 4.3.
