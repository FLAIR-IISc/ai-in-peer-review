

The paper provides a rigorous minimax analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, establishing sharp rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems. It bridges classical statistical decision theory with modern differential privacy, demonstrating how privacy requirements fundamentally alter statistical convergence rates. The work extends Warner's 1965 randomized response framework within a formal minimax setting, proving its optimality for multinomial estimation, and derives novel minimax rates for nonparametric density estimation under local privacy. The authors establish lower bounds that match efficiently computable upper bounds, demonstrating tight trade-offs between privacy level α and statistical efficiency.

The primary strengths include the paper's theoretical depth and precision. The minimax rates for multinomial estimation (Theorem 1) reveal a clear reduction in effective sample size from n to nα²/d, with the elegant proof that Warner's method achieves this bound. For density estimation, the paper identifies a fundamental departure from classical rates (Theorem 2), showing that privacy changes the exponent from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2) for Sobolev-smooth densities. The constructive achievability results (Propositions 2 and 3) provide practical algorithms—including histogram and orthogonal series estimators—that match the lower bounds, demonstrating computational feasibility. The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contributions against prior work on differential privacy (e.g., Dwork et al. [11]) and minimax estimation (e.g., Tsybakov [22]), properly distinguishing its focus on *population* estimation under local privacy from prior *sample* estimation problems.

Key weaknesses involve limited discussion of practical implementation challenges and scope limitations. While the density estimation algorithms are theoretically efficient, the paper does not address computational complexity for high-dimensional settings (e.g., when k = (nα²)^(1/(2β+2)) becomes large), which could impact real-world applicability. The analysis is restricted to univariate density estimation; extension to multivariate cases would strengthen the significance. Additionally, the connection to contemporary privacy-preserving applications (e.g., in machine learning pipelines) is underdeveloped, despite the paper's claim of enabling "adjustment of statistical procedures to meet privacy needs." The authors could better emphasize how their trade-off continuum (privacy vs. utility) informs practical system design.

The paper excels in technical quality: all claims are rigorously proven via minimax reduction to hypothesis testing, with tight bounds supported by information-theoretic arguments (Proposition 1). The notation is consistent, and the writing is clear, though the density estimation section (Section 4) becomes dense with technical details. The novelty is substantial—this is the first work to establish sharp minimax rates for local privacy in both discrete and nonparametric settings, resolving an open question about Warner's method's optimality. The significance is high: the results provide foundational tools for understanding the statistical cost of privacy, with implications for survey design, data sharing, and private machine learning.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria. It is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel synthesis of minimax theory and differential privacy (Originality), and establishes fundamental trade-offs with wide applicability (Significance). The main weakness—limited discussion of computational scalability and multivariate extensions—is a reasonable suggestion for revision rather than a critical flaw. The authors' focus on sharp rates rather than heuristic methods aligns with NIPS' emphasis on theoretical contributions.

The paper's contribution is substantial and timely. By formalizing the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off, it advances the theoretical foundation for privacy-preserving statistics. The finding that a 50-year-old survey technique (Warner's randomized response) is minimax-optimal under local privacy is particularly compelling. While the density estimation results for β > 1 require non-trivial sampling strategies (Section 4.3), the paper demonstrates their feasibility without undue complexity. The work sets a new standard for analyzing statistical utility under privacy constraints and will inform future research on private estimation.

Given the paper's technical rigor, novelty, and significance, it should be accepted. The suggested revisions—adding a brief discussion of computational complexity for the density estimators and extending the multivariate case—are minor and would strengthen the paper without requiring major rework. The current version already provides a definitive theoretical treatment of the problem, making it a strong contribution to NIPS.
