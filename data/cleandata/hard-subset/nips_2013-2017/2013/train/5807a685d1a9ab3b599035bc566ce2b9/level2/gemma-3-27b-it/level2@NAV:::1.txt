## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation, specifically within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems, building upon Warner’s work on randomized response and recent advances in differential privacy. The work is well-motivated, addressing a critical issue in modern data analysis where privacy concerns are paramount.

The core contribution lies in establishing precise lower and upper bounds on the achievable estimation accuracy under local privacy constraints. The result for multinomial estimation is particularly strong, demonstrating that randomized response is, in fact, optimal.  The analysis for density estimation reveals a novel polynomial rate of convergence differing from the classical non-private case, offering valuable insights into the impact of privacy on nonparametric estimation.  The paper leverages tools from minimax theory, information theory, and differential privacy in a sophisticated manner, and provides efficient algorithms achieving the derived upper bounds.

A significant strength is the clear presentation of the theoretical framework, carefully defining local privacy and relating it to existing concepts like differential privacy. The connection back to Warner’s original work is also nicely highlighted, providing historical context. The detailed proofs (available elsewhere, as noted) are crucial for establishing the rigor of the results. The inclusion of both lower and upper bounds strengthens the claim of sharpness.

However, the paper could benefit from a more extensive discussion of the practical implications of the derived rates. While the optimality of randomized response is stated, a deeper exploration of its limitations in real-world survey settings would be valuable. Furthermore, the discussion on density estimation, while theoretically sound, could be expanded to address the computational challenges associated with the proposed estimators and their applicability to high-dimensional data.  The exposition is dense and assumes a strong background in statistical decision theory and information theory, which may limit accessibility for some readers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant theoretical results on the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.
*   Sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete and continuous estimation problems.
*   Rigorous mathematical analysis and well-defined framework.
*   Connection to classical work on randomized response and recent advances in differential privacy.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Dense exposition that may be inaccessible to readers without a strong theoretical background.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications and challenges.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While the density of the presentation and lack of extensive discussion on practical considerations are minor weaknesses, the novelty and rigor of the results strongly support acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, potentially with a request for the authors to expand the discussion on practical implications in a revised version.
