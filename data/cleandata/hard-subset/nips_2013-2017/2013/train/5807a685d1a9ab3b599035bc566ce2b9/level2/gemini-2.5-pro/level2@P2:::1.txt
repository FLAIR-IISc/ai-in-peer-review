This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical utility and privacy, focusing on the strong guarantee of local differential privacy. The authors study two fundamental statistical problems: estimating the parameters of a multinomial distribution and nonparametric density estimation. Using minimax theory, they derive sharp lower bounds on the estimation error for any procedure satisfying α-local privacy. They complement these lower bounds with matching upper bounds achieved by computationally efficient and practical algorithms. This work builds upon the emerging literature connecting differential privacy with statistical inference, such as the work of Wasserman & Zhou (2010) and Smith (2011), but is one of the first to provide a complete and sharp minimax characterization in the challenging *local* privacy setting, where data is privatized at the source.

The main contributions are twofold. First, for estimating a d-dimensional multinomial, the authors show that the minimax squared error scales as d/(nα^2), which implies that local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to nα^2/d. Strikingly, they demonstrate that a modified version of Warner's classic 1965 randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal. This provides a beautiful connection between the historical origins of privacy in statistics and modern, formal definitions. Second, for nonparametric estimation of a density with β derivatives (in a Sobolev class), they show that the minimax integrated squared error scales as (nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2)). This is a particularly important result, as it shows that local privacy fundamentally changes the polynomial rate of convergence from the classical non-private rate of n^(-2β/(2β+1)). This degradation in the exponent represents a more severe statistical cost than the simple sample size reduction seen in the multinomial case. To achieve this optimal rate, the authors propose novel and non-trivial privacy mechanisms, including a privatized histogram for Lipschitz densities (β=1) and a carefully designed orthogonal series estimator for smoother densities (β>1).

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a fundamental question at the intersection of privacy and statistics: what is the ultimate statistical cost of strong privacy guarantees? The results provide a sharp, quantitative answer for two core estimation problems. The finding that the convergence rate itself can change is a novel and deep insight. By establishing a formal bridge between minimax decision theory and local differential privacy, this work lays a strong theoretical foundation for future research in this area.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically excellent. The application of information-theoretic tools (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) to the local privacy setting is rigorous and powerful. The work is very complete, providing both tight lower bounds on the error for *any* private procedure and constructive upper bounds via explicit, efficient algorithms that match these limits.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the problem setting, the privacy model, and the main results. The comparison between the derived private rates and the well-known classical rates is used effectively to highlight the statistical cost of privacy. The authors do an excellent job of conveying the high-level intuition behind their significant findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Self-Containedness:** The paper defers all proofs to a longer manuscript [9]. While this is understandable given page limits, including brief proof sketches for the main theorems in an appendix would significantly improve the paper's self-containedness and allow reviewers to better appreciate the technical arguments.
*   **Intuition for the Orthogonal Series Mechanism:** The sampling strategy proposed in Section 4.3 for the orthogonal series estimator is quite sophisticated. While the authors correctly point out that simpler methods like direct Laplace perturbation are suboptimal, a bit more intuition behind the design of their specific mechanism (Eq. 19) would be beneficial for the reader. Explaining why this particular construction is necessary to achieve the optimal rate would strengthen the presentation.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  As mentioned above, please consider adding an appendix with proof sketches of the main results (Theorems 1 and 2). This would greatly enhance the submission.
2.  In Section 3.1, the `min` terms in the rate expressions (8) and (9) correctly handle the different regimes of n, d, and α. A brief sentence of interpretation for the different parts of the minimum (e.g., the trivial bound of 1 for very small n) would add clarity.
3.  The paper focuses on α ∈ [0, 1]. It would be useful to briefly comment on whether the results extend to the large α regime and how they connect to the non-private rates as α → ∞.
4.  The result on the optimality of randomized response is elegant and important. It might be worth highlighting this connection to the classic 1965 paper even more prominently, perhaps in the abstract or the beginning of the introduction, as it provides a compelling narrative hook.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that makes a fundamental contribution to our understanding of private statistical inference. The results are significant, the technical work is of high quality, and the exposition is clear. The weaknesses are minor and easily addressable. The paper establishes a benchmark for analyzing the statistical limits of privacy and is likely to be highly influential.
