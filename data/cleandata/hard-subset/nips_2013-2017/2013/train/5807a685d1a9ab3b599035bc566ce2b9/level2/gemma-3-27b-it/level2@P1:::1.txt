This paper provides a thorough investigation of probability estimation under local differential privacy, establishing sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems. The work elegantly connects classical statistical decision theory with the modern framework of differential privacy, offering valuable insights into the inherent trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The authors demonstrate that Warner's randomized response procedure is, in fact, optimal for survey sampling with privacy constraints, a notable and satisfying result.

**Summary of Contributions & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Warner [23] on randomized response and extends it within a rigorous theoretical framework incorporating differential privacy [11, 1]. It utilizes minimax decision theory to characterize the optimal convergence rates achievable under privacy constraints, complementing lower bounds with matching upper bounds derived through efficiently implementable algorithms. This work differentiates itself from prior work [2, 16, 8] focused on estimating sample quantities by focusing on population-level estimation, leading to stronger lower bounds. The authors effectively bridge concepts from classical statistical estimation (e.g., minimax theory, density estimation) with privacy-preserving mechanisms, providing a comprehensive analysis of the privacy-utility trade-off.  They also correctly identify and address the limitations of directly applying Laplace perturbation in the density estimation context, proposing an alternative approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically rigorous and well-supported by theoretical analysis. The derivations of both lower and upper bounds appear correct and are presented with sufficient detail (though some details are relegated to a longer version [9]).
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The notation is generally standard and well-defined.
* **Originality:** The combination of minimax theory and differential privacy in this setting is a significant contribution. Establishing sharp minimax rates and demonstrating the optimality of Warner's method are novel and important results.
* **Significance:** The results have practical implications for privacy-preserving data analysis and survey sampling. The identified trade-offs offer a quantifiable understanding of the cost of privacy, enabling informed decisions about privacy levels. The findings are likely to influence future research in this area.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper addresses both discrete and continuous estimation problems, providing a broad perspective on the impact of local privacy on statistical inference.
* **Strong Connection to Prior Work:** The authors expertly situate their work within the existing literature, properly referencing and acknowledging relevant contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density Estimation Complexity:** While the results for density estimation are significant, the practical implementation of the proposed approach, especially the choice of the basis functions and the parameter B, could be further clarified. A more detailed discussion of computational complexity would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Baselines:** While the paper establishes optimality against certain perturbation-based methods, a broader comparison with other existing privacy-preserving density estimation techniques (beyond Laplace perturbation) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on assumptions such as bounded derivatives (for density estimation) and the availability of a suitable orthonormal basis. Discussing the robustness of the results to violations of these assumptions would enhance the paper.
* **Repetitive Notation:** The repeated use of "Mn" for the minimax rate and other quantities throughout the equations can make the paper harder to follow for the reader, without substantial payoff.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in privacy-preserving data analysis.
* The results are technically sound and supported by rigorous analysis.
* The paper establishes sharp minimax rates and demonstrates the optimality of Warner's method.
* The work has significant practical implications and is likely to influence future research.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The density estimation section could benefit from a more detailed discussion of implementation and computational complexity.
* A broader comparison with other privacy-preserving density estimation techniques would strengthen the analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The combination of rigorous theoretical analysis, insightful results, and clear presentation make it a worthy addition to NIPS. The minor weaknesses identified above do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with a minor revision request to elaborate on the practical considerations of the density estimation algorithm and to broaden the comparison with existing techniques.
