Summary
This paper studies probability estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each user independently randomizes their own data via an α-LDP channel before any analysis. The authors develop sharp minimax rates for two canonical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. They prove information-contraction inequalities tailored to the local model (building on their prior work), use Le Cam/Fano techniques to derive lower bounds, and give matching upper bounds via efficient mechanisms and estimators.

For d-ary multinomial estimation, they show that local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to nα2/d (for small α). They provide matching lower and upper bounds in both ℓ2 and ℓ1 risk. Notably, Warner’s randomized response is shown to be minimax optimal (up to constants), and simple perturbation of counts also achieves the rate.

For density estimation over Sobolev class Fβ on [0,1], the non-private minimax rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (α2n)−2β/(2β+2). They give private estimators that attain this new rate: a privatized histogram for β=1 and a privatized orthogonal series estimator for β≥1 using a carefully designed sampling scheme. They also show why directly adding Laplace noise to the raw samples is suboptimal for density estimation, via deconvolution lower bounds.

Relation to prior work
The work connects classical randomized response (Warner, 1965) to modern DP optimality, and leverages information-theoretic tools (Le Cam, Fano; Cover & Thomas). It complements differential privacy foundations (Dwork et al., Hardt & Talwar, Smith; Wasserman & Zhou) and local privacy formulations (Evfimievski et al.). The use of minimax decision theory under LDP and the sharp rates for multinomial and density estimation appear novel to NIPS, though there is overlap with the authors’ earlier arXiv/FOCS work, especially for the multinomial case.

Quality
The results are technically strong: lower and upper bounds match in rate, mechanisms are computationally simple, and the information bounds are carefully derived. The paper convincingly quantifies privacy–utility trade-offs in the local model and provides constructive procedures to navigate them.

Clarity
The exposition is generally sound but notationally heavy. The minimax setup, channels, and induced marginals could be introduced more intuitively before the full formalism. It would help to emphasize early that the focus is the non-interactive (memoryless) LDP setting—each user’s mechanism depends only on their own datum—and to state explicitly that no benefit is obtained from conditioning on others’ data in this model.

Originality
The characterization of LDP minimax rates and the optimality of randomized response for multinomial estimation are impactful. The shift in the density estimation rate and the matching private orthogonal series estimator are particularly interesting. However, parts of Section 3 overlap with results presented in FOCS 2013; the paper should clearly delineate what is new here (e.g., sharper constants, extended classes, new mechanisms, or improved proofs).

Significance
The results are important for both theory and practice. They provide clear guidance on sample complexity under LDP, identify optimal mechanisms, and show where standard noise-addition approaches fail. The insights are likely to influence private survey design and distributed analytics.

Strengths
- Sharp minimax lower bounds with matching, efficient estimators in both discrete and continuous settings.
- Clear, actionable message: randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation under LDP.
- Elegant information-contraction bounds enabling the analysis.
- Practical guidance: effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d; rate shift for density estimation.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Exposition and notation: simplify the presentation of the minimax framework; move some technical definitions to an appendix; provide more intuition for the contraction inequalities.
- Make the ℓ1 risk guarantees for multinomial estimation more prominent in the main text, since ℓ1 is often the natural metric in probability estimation.
- Briefly compare to global (central) DP rates where known, to contextualize the extra cost of local privacy.
- Clarify novelty versus the FOCS 2013 paper: specify what is new in the multinomial section and what is new in the density results/mechanisms.
- Provide practical guidance on tuning (e.g., α, number of bins k, series truncation) and, if space allows, add small simulations illustrating constants and estimator behavior.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, correct, and complete theory with matching constructions.
- Conceptually clean and impactful results linking classic randomized response to modern optimality.
- Advances understanding of local privacy’s fundamental costs.

Cons against acceptance
- Overlap with FOCS 2013 results; novelty should be better delineated.
- Dense notation and presentation may hinder accessibility.
- No empirical illustrations.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a clear and rigorous characterization of optimal rates under local differential privacy for two fundamental problems, with practical mechanisms. My enthusiasm is slightly tempered by overlap with prior conference publication; a revision clarifying the novelty and streamlining exposition would strengthen the contribution.
