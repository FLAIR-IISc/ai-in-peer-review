## Review - Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

This paper investigates local privacy, a setting where each user provides an α-differentially private signal based on their individual type and the signals of others, reporting this to a data curator. The authors employ a min-max expected error framework to analyze this scenario – an adversary selects an input distribution (user types), and a local differentially private (DP) scheme is chosen to minimize the distance between estimations derived from the original data and those from the reported signals (typically sums, averages, or aggregations). They establish a lower bound for the min-max rate concerning l₂ distance estimations with users drawn from a discrete distribution over *d* types, demonstrating that the randomized response technique achieves this bound and is, therefore, optimal. The analysis extends to density estimation, where a perturbation scheme perturbing only individual types again meets the established lower bound.

This is a strong and valuable contribution; demonstrating the optimality of classic procedures by matching them to fundamental lower bounds is a significant result. I believe the NIPS community would find this work of interest and recommend acceptance.

However, I have several reservations. Regarding presentation, the explanation of the min-max bounds could benefit from greater clarity. The paper also utilizes a somewhat cumbersome notation – given that all bounds are expressed for the l₂ norm, could they be consistently presented in terms of this norm? I would also appreciate discussion of the l₁ norm, which is more restrictive, including a comparison of the presented bounds to l₁ norms and relevant lower bounds from the broader differential privacy literature (acknowledging the differences in the non-local setting). Furthermore, the min-max bounds appear specifically tailored to this local privacy setting; exploring a wider range of settings and deriving corresponding min-max lower bounds would strengthen the work. I particularly appreciate the finding that, despite the allowance for users to randomize their reported type based on the signals of others, a simple scheme with individual-type-based randomization proves optimal, and I suggest the authors explicitly state this in the text.

I have a further reservation now that the FOCS 2013 acceptances have been published. The paper appears to be an extension of work presented there, particularly the results in Section 3. Consequently, while I still recommend acceptance, my support is somewhat diminished. 

In summary, the paper provides a compelling analysis of local privacy through the lens of min-max rate bounds, establishing lower bounds and demonstrating that randomized response achieves optimal performance. It is a worthwhile contribution, albeit with some areas for improvement.
