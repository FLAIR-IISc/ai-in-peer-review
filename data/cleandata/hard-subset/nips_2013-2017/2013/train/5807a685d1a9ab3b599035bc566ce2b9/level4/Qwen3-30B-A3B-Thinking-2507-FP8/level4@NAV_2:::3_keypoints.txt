- Main contribution: Establishes sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy and links Warner’s randomized response to modern minimax theory.  
- Multinomial result: Shows randomized response achieves optimal sample complexity for multinomial estimation.  
- Density result: Derives novel density-estimation rates under local privacy that differ fundamentally from classical (non-private) rates.  
- Quality: Technically rigorous with matching upper and lower bounds for both multinomial and density problems.  
- Methods: Uses appropriate minimax and differential-privacy tools; results are well-supported theoretically.  
- Clarity: Well-organized but uses cumbersome notation that could be simplified.  
- Norms: Focuses on ℓ2 norms; should explicitly discuss ℓ1 norms which are more common/restrictive for probability estimation.  
- Accessibility: Minimax-theory exposition could be made more accessible to non-experts.  
- Originality: Substantial and novel—especially the connection showing a 1965 technique is optimal under modern privacy constraints.  
- Significance: Theoretical and practical implications for privacy-preserving inference and survey design; advances understanding of privacy–efficiency trade-offs.  
- Strength: Establishes sharp minimax rates for both discrete and continuous settings under local privacy.  
- Strength: Demonstrates and formalizes optimality of Warner’s randomized response for multinomial estimation.  
- Strength: Develops efficient algorithms that attain the lower bounds for density estimation.  
- Strength: Bridges classical privacy literature with modern differential-privacy and decision-theory frameworks.  
- Weakness: Notation is cumbersome and centered on ℓ2, which may limit readability for some audiences.  
- Weakness: Lacks explicit treatment of ℓ1 error metrics relevant to probability estimation.  
- Weakness: Results are tailored to the local privacy setting without broader contextual discussion.  
- Weakness: Connection to Duchi et al. (FOCS 2013) is not explicitly addressed despite overlap.  
- Recommendation: Strong theoretical paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve exposition and address ℓ1/related-work issues.
