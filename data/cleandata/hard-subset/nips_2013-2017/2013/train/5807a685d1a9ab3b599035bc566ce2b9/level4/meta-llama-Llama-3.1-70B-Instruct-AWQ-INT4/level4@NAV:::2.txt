This paper examines minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint of preserving individual data privacy, focusing on a privacy definition known as local privacy. The authors investigate two probability estimation problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation, demonstrating sharp minimax rates of convergence in both cases. Notably, for discrete multinomial estimation, local privacy results in a quadratic reduction in effective sample size with respect to the privacy parameter alpha, which often remains a small constant, thereby maintaining the effective sample size comparable to the non-private scenario. Conversely, for the density estimation problem, the authors show that the optimal rate attainable in the non-private setting is no longer achievable when local privacy must be preserved.

The results presented in this paper are highly interesting, and upon review, the proofs appear to be correct. Furthermore, to the best of my knowledge, the minimax bound for density estimation under the privacy constraint has not been previously explored.

However, my primary concern is that the minimax bound for multinomial estimation bears a close relationship to previous works on noise complexity for differential privacy, which is not adequately acknowledged. Specifically, two papers, Hardt and Talwar's "On the geometry of differential privacy" (STOC, 2010) and De's "Lower bounds in differential privacy" (TCC, 2012), investigated highly relevant problems, studying worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under the constraint of differential privacy. The probability estimation problem examined in this paper is, in fact, a special case of the linear counting query explored in those papers, utilizing the same L2 metric for error measurement. The key distinction lies in the consideration of local differential privacy in this paper, as opposed to differential privacy in the aforementioned works. Given that local privacy imposes a stronger constraint than differential privacy, lower bounds for differential privacy are also applicable to local privacy. I would appreciate clarification on whether the bound for local privacy presented in this paper improves upon previous bounds for differential privacy.

Regarding the density estimation problem, the statement of the result is somewhat ambiguous. The authors assert that the lower bound in the local privacy setting exceeds that of the non-private setting. However, the minimax bound established in this paper pertains specifically to the case where the density can be expanded with a trigonometric basis. I would like to know whether the lower bound for the non-private setting, as expressed in equation (13), holds for the general Sobolev space defined in Definition 1 or only for the special case of a trigonometric basis. A fair comparison necessitates that the non-private lower bound applies to the trigonometric basis.

In response to the rebuttal, while the feedback partially clarifies the relationship to previous worst-case lower bounds, it is worth noting that the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, "The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases" (STOC 2013), also examines Mean Square Error. I recommend that the authors incorporate their explanations and missing references into the paper.

In conclusion, this paper establishes sharp minimax bounds for pmf and pdf estimation with a privacy guarantee, yielding interesting results presented in a well-written paper. Nevertheless, there is a notable omission regarding known results on noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy, which are highly relevant to this paper.
