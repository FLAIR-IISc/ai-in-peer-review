This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of work initially appearing on arXiv, employing a Fano/LeCam-style argument to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms applied to private data under the assumption of an untrusted algorithm. A notable result is the identification of randomized response as an optimal strategy under certain conditions. Please note this review does not cover the supplementary material.

The paper’s strengths lie in characterizing the fundamental limitations of learning from privacy-perturbed data. However, the commentary accompanying the results contains some imprecision that could potentially mislead a casual reader (see detailed comments below). Furthermore, the paper appears to lack discussion of connections to existing work in differential privacy.

Additional comments:

The focus on local privacy, crucial to the presented results, results in a privacy model distinct from the standard differential privacy model – a distinction that many readers may not immediately recognize. This potential for confusion is amplified by statements like the one on page 4 (“Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…”), which may be overly broad and potentially inaccurate given the specific context of the work. The authors should avoid such generalizations when interpreting their findings.

The requirement on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 warrants further scrutiny. Experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may represent an upper bound for certain learning problems under differential privacy; therefore, it’s unclear if the derived bound provides meaningful information in this relevant case. 

Including discussion of possible choices for $\rho$ would benefit the reader by illustrating how different utility measures can be incorporated into the analysis.

A comparison to the M-estimation procedure of Lei is missing, despite its relevance. The authors should be aware of and address this work.

While the paper demonstrates that randomized response achieves an optimal convergence rate, it would be valuable to understand how other histogram estimation algorithms for discrete data perform in comparison.

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:

* The distinction between population and sample statistics requires clearer and more explicit explanation, preferably at the outset of the paper (as also noted by Rev.9).
* A comprehensive comparison to related work, including Lei and other contributions highlighted by other reviewers, is essential for providing adequate context.
* Following the appearance of [10], I now view the paper’s contribution more narrowly. While the target venues and audiences differ, this paper’s contribution primarily consists of a careful exposition of the local privacy model and bounds for density estimation. The latter is novel, but the former is largely contained within [10].
