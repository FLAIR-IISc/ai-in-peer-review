## Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

This paper investigates the problem of local privacy, a setting where each user contributes an α-differentially private signal based on their individual type and the signals of others, reporting this to a data curator. The authors employ a min-max expected error framework – an adversarial approach where an adversary designs an input distribution and a differentially private mechanism is chosen to minimize the discrepancy between estimations derived from the original data and those derived from the reported, privatized signals (typically sums, averages, or other aggregations). 

The paper presents a lower bound for the min-max rate achievable when estimating with the l₂ distance, assuming users are drawn from a discrete distribution over *d* types. Significantly, the authors demonstrate that the simple randomized response technique achieves this lower bound, establishing its optimality within this setting. They extend this analysis to the problem of density estimation, again finding that a perturbation scheme where each user perturbs only their own type attains the derived lower bound. 

Overall, this is a strong and important contribution. The paper convincingly shows that well-known, practical procedures are, in fact, optimal given the established min-max bounds. I believe the NIPS community would find this work of interest and recommend acceptance.

However, I do have some reservations. First, the explanation of the min-max bound framework could benefit from increased clarity and simplification. The paper’s notation is also quite dense; given that all bounds are presented for the l₂ norm, it would improve readability if the bounds were consistently expressed in terms of this norm. 

Second, I would appreciate some discussion regarding the l₁ norm, which imposes a stronger constraint. Comparing the derived bounds to those achievable under the l₁ norm, or relating them to existing lower bounds in the broader field of differential privacy (even those developed for non-local settings), would strengthen the analysis. 

Third, the min-max bounds appear specifically tailored to this local privacy setup. Considering a wider range of settings and providing corresponding min-max lower bounds would broaden the scope and impact of the work. 

On a positive note, the authors highlight a compelling observation: despite the local setting allowing users to randomize their reported type based on the signals of others, the optimal scheme surprisingly relies on individual randomization based solely on one’s own type. Explicitly stating this key insight in the text would be valuable.

Finally, I recently noted the publication of accepted papers from FOCS 2013. This reveals that the current work is an extension of existing research, particularly the results presented in Section 3. While I still recommend acceptance, my confidence has diminished somewhat.

In summary, the paper successfully analyzes local privacy using min-max rate bounds, provides insightful lower bounds, and demonstrates the optimality of randomized response. Despite some minor concerns regarding clarity, notation, and scope, the results are valuable and warrant publication.
