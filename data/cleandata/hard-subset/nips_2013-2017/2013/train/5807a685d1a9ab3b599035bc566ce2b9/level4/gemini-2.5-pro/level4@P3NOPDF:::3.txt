### **Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations"**

#### **Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of statistical estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. In this model, each of `n` users possesses a private "type" from a discrete set of `d` possibilities. To preserve privacy, each user transmits a privatized signal to a central data curator, where the signal-generating mechanism for each user must be `α`-differentially private with respect to their own type.

The authors analyze this problem through the lens of minimax theory, aiming to establish sharp bounds on the expected error of estimators derived from the privatized signals. The core contributions are twofold:
1.  For the problem of estimating the empirical distribution of user types, the paper derives a minimax lower bound for the squared `l2`-error. It then demonstrates that the classic Randomized Response mechanism achieves this bound, thereby proving its optimality for this task.
2.  The analysis is extended to the problem of non-parametric density estimation. Here too, the authors establish a minimax lower bound and show that it is met by a simple local perturbation scheme, proving its optimality as well.

#### **Strengths**

The paper addresses a significant and increasingly relevant problem in privacy-preserving data analysis. Its primary strength lies in its definitive characterization of optimality for fundamental estimation tasks within the local privacy model. By rigorously matching the upper bounds of well-known mechanisms with newly derived lower bounds, the paper provides a complete and satisfying answer to the question of "what is the best possible accuracy?" for these problems. This type of tight, foundational result is of great value.

Particularly noteworthy is the finding that simple, non-interactive mechanisms—where each user perturbs their data independently—are optimal. This is an elegant and impactful result, especially since the local privacy model *a priori* allows for more complex protocols where a user's output could depend on the signals of others. The authors should consider stating this important implication more explicitly in the main text.

The results are of clear interest to the machine learning and privacy communities, and the paper makes a solid technical contribution.

#### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

Despite the paper's strengths, there are several areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The explanation of the minimax framework, while technically sound, could be simplified for broader accessibility. The paper also employs a significant amount of notation that can be cumbersome to follow. Given that the main results concern the `l2`-norm, the bounds could potentially be stated more directly in terms of this norm to enhance readability.

2.  **Scope and Context:** The paper's focus is sharply on `l2`-error. While this is a standard and well-justified choice, a discussion of how these results relate to other important error metrics, such as the `l1`-norm (which is often more restrictive), would significantly broaden the paper's impact. Additionally, a brief comparison to existing lower bounds in the traditional (non-local) central differential privacy model would help situate the contributions within the wider literature.

3.  **Generalizability of the Framework:** The minimax bounds discussed appear specifically tailored for the local-privacy setting. A discussion on whether this analytical approach could be extended to other privacy models or a broader class of statistical problems would strengthen the paper's conceptual contribution.

4.  **Novelty in Light of Recent Work:** A significant concern has arisen since this paper's submission: a paper with substantial overlap, particularly concerning the results in Section 3, has been accepted to FOCS 2013. This new development materially diminishes the novelty of the present submission. What would have been a groundbreaking contribution now reads more as an extension or a parallel discovery.

#### **Recommendation and Justification**

Initially, my recommendation for this paper was a strong accept. The work is technically solid, addresses a fundamental problem, and elegantly proves the optimality of classic, simple mechanisms.

However, the recent publication of a similar result at a top-tier conference (FOCS 2013) substantially reduces the novelty of the work presented here. While the paper remains a valuable and well-executed contribution, its originality and immediate impact are lessened by this external development.

Therefore, I am revising my recommendation to **Accept**, but with less enthusiasm than I would have initially. The paper is a good addition to the NIPS proceedings, but it no longer represents the breakthrough contribution it would have just a few months ago.
