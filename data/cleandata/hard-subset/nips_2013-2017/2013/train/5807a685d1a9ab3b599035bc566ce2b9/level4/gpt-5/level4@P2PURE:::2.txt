This paper analyzes minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint that individual data must remain private, using the notion of local privacy. The authors study two settings—multinomial estimation and density estimation—and establish sharp minimax convergence rates in both. For the discrete multinomial problem, they show that local privacy effectively reduces the sample size by a factor quadratic in the privacy parameter alpha. Since alpha is often treated as a small constant, the effective sample size remains of the same order as in the non-private setting. In contrast, for density estimation, the optimal non-private rate is no longer achievable when enforcing local privacy.

Overall, the results are very interesting. To the extent I could verify, the proofs are correct. To the best of my knowledge, the minimax bound for density estimation under privacy constraints has not been previously studied.

My main concern is that the minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely connected to earlier work on noise complexity for differential privacy, which is insufficiently discussed. In particular, Hardt & Talwar, On the Geometry of Differential Privacy (STOC 2010), and De, Lower Bounds in Differential Privacy (TCC 2012), address highly relevant problems: worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem here is a special case of the linear counting queries considered in those papers, and the error is measured in the same L_2 metric. The key difference is that this paper studies local differential privacy, whereas Hardt & Talwar and De study (central) differential privacy. Because local privacy imposes a stronger constraint, lower bounds for differential privacy also apply to local privacy. It would be helpful to clarify whether the local privacy bounds in this paper improve upon those prior differential privacy lower bounds.

Regarding density estimation, the presentation of the results is somewhat confusing. The paper states that the local-privacy lower bound is higher than the non-private bound, but the minimax analysis here is restricted to densities that admit a trigonometric basis expansion. It is unclear whether the non-private lower bound in eq. (13) holds for the general Sobolev space in Definition 1 or only for the trigonometric-basis subclass. The comparison is fair only if the non-private lower bound also applies to the trigonometric-basis setting.

Additional comments on the rebuttal:
The response partially clarifies the connection to prior worst-case lower bounds. However, note that Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases (STOC 2013), also analyzes mean squared error. I recommend incorporating these explanations and the missing references into the paper.

In summary, the paper proves sharp minimax bounds for pmf and pdf estimation under privacy constraints. The results are interesting and the exposition is generally clear. Nonetheless, the paper should better acknowledge and relate to existing lower bounds on noise complexity in differential privacy, which are highly relevant to the contributions here.
