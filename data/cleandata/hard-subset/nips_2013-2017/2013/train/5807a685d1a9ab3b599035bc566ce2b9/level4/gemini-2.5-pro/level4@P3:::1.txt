This review provides a detailed analysis of the paper "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation." The paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical accuracy and privacy in the *local differential privacy* model, where each user's data is privatized before being sent to a central aggregator. Using tools from minimax decision theory, the authors derive sharp, matching upper and lower bounds on the convergence rates for two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation.

The core technical approach involves applying Fano's inequality and Le Cam's method to establish lower bounds on the minimax risk for any estimator that satisfies α-local differential privacy. The authors then construct explicit, computationally efficient estimators that achieve these lower bounds, thereby characterizing the optimal rates.

The main contributions are twofold:
1.  For the d-dimensional multinomial estimation problem, the paper demonstrates that local privacy reduces the effective sample size from *n* to approximately *nα² / d*. A significant and elegant consequence of this result is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal in this setting.
2.  For nonparametric density estimation of functions with β derivatives, the paper reveals a more severe penalty. The minimax rate degrades from the classical non-private rate of *n⁻²ᵝ/(²ᵝ⁺¹)* to *(nα²)⁻²ᵝ/(²ᵝ⁺²)*. This change in the polynomial exponent highlights a fundamental structural difference in what is learnable under local privacy. The authors provide private histogram and orthogonal series estimators that achieve this new optimal rate.

**Strengths:**

*   **Fundamental Contribution:** The paper addresses a crucial and timely problem: quantifying the statistical cost of strong privacy guarantees. By providing sharp minimax rates, it offers a definitive characterization of the limitations and possibilities of learning under local privacy.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The application of minimax theory to the local privacy setting is rigorous and well-executed. The derivation of matching upper and lower bounds is a strong technical achievement that provides a complete picture for the problems studied.
*   **Significant Results:** The finding that randomized response is optimal is a particularly insightful result, connecting a half-century-old technique to the modern differential privacy framework. The discovery of a different polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is also a novel and important finding for the field.
*   **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, effectively introducing the concepts of local privacy and the minimax framework before presenting the main results.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of the Privacy Model:** A major weakness is the potential for confusion between the *local privacy* model used in the paper and the more widely known *central differential privacy* model. In the local model, the aggregator is untrusted, and privacy is ensured at the individual level, which typically incurs a much higher statistical cost. The authors should explicitly define and contrast these two models early in the paper. Statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4 ("the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α…") are imprecise and could mislead readers into thinking these rates apply to the central model. Clarifying this distinction is critical for accurately framing the paper's contribution.

2.  **Positioning with Respect to Related Work:** The paper could be better situated within the existing literature.
    *   The work of Lei (2011) on differentially private M-estimators, which includes an analysis of density estimation, is a highly relevant piece of prior art that is not cited or discussed. A comparison of the rates and privacy models would strengthen the paper.
    *   Other methods for private histogram estimation exist. While the paper proves randomized response is optimal, a brief discussion of how alternative approaches (e.g., adding Laplace noise to bin counts) compare to the derived lower bound would provide valuable context.

3.  **Scope of Theoretical Results:**
    *   Theorem 1 restricts α to the range [0, 1]. This restriction is not well-motivated in the text. Given that practical applications of differential privacy often use α values in this range or slightly larger, it is important to understand if the bounds hold more generally or if the analysis is specific to small α.
    *   The framework is built on a general semi-metric ρ to measure error. While this provides generality, the paper would benefit from a short discussion on how different choices of ρ (e.g., other Lₚ norms) could capture different utility goals and how the analysis might change accordingly.

4.  **Novelty Relative to ArXiv Preprint:** The paper appears to be a version of the authors' existing ArXiv manuscript [9]. While this is standard practice, the unique contributions of this conference version should be made clear. Based on a comparison, it seems the primary new contribution for this venue is the density estimation analysis, as the framework and multinomial results are present in the preprint. Acknowledging this would help readers understand the specific advance being presented here.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes significant contributions to our understanding of the interplay between privacy and statistical estimation. The results are novel, important, and technically sound. The primary weaknesses relate to clarity and positioning, specifically the need for a clearer distinction between local and central privacy models and a more thorough comparison with related work. These are addressable issues. The paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
