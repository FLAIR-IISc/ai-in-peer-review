- The paper analyzes probability estimation under local privacy using minimax decision theory to characterize privacy–efficiency trade-offs.
- Main contribution: establishes sharp minimax convergence rates for multinomial and density estimation when data are locally private.
- Key consequence: Warner’s randomized response is shown to be optimal for private survey sampling.
- Problem setting and background are clearly presented, including a motivated formalization of local privacy via a likelihood-ratio bound.
- The authors derive matching lower and upper bounds on minimax risk, showing degraded performance and different convergence rates for density estimation under local privacy.
- Theoretical rigor: solid mathematical foundations combining minimax theory and differential-privacy concepts.
- Sharp bounds: provides a precise characterization of the trade-off between privacy and accuracy.
- Practical relevance: results have immediate implications for privacy-preserving algorithm design.
- Clear problem formulation: assumptions and setup are explicitly stated.
- Novelty: combines differential privacy, statistical decision theory, and minimax analysis in a new way.
- Commentary precision: some discussions lack precision and risk overgeneralizing links to differential privacy.
- α restriction: the necessity and practical implications of the α constraint in Theorem 1 are not fully explored (e.g., α ≈ 1).
- Metric choice: reliance on the semi-metric ρ needs justification and discussion of alternative utility measures.
- Related work: insufficient engagement with prior DP estimation literature, notably Lei [19].
- Histogram discussion: should compare with other histogram algorithms and their performance.
- Acceptance arguments: significant theoretical contribution, rigorous sharp bounds, and broad potential impact.
- Rejection concerns: occasional imprecise commentary, incomplete related-work coverage, and narrow focus on local privacy.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper; accept with minor revisions to clarify commentary, expand related work, and examine α’s implications.
