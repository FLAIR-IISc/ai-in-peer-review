SUMMARY:
This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical estimation accuracy and the constraints of local differential privacy. Using tools from minimax decision theory, such as Le Cam's and Fano's methods, the authors derive sharp lower bounds on the convergence rates for estimators that operate on locally privatized data. The analysis is applied to two core problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. Key findings include the demonstration that Warner's randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation, and that local privacy constraints alter the polynomial rate of convergence for density estimation compared to the non-private setting.

*As a caveat to this review, I did not read the supplementary material.*

**STRENGTHS:**
The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous characterization of the inherent costs of local privacy. By establishing sharp minimax rates, the work quantifies the fundamental limits of learning from privatized data, providing a clear and formal view of the privacy-utility trade-off. The results are significant, particularly the connection of a classical mechanism like randomized response to the modern framework of differential privacy, showing it to be optimal. This provides a valuable bridge between decades of work on privacy in surveys and contemporary privacy research.

**WEAKNESSES AND SUGGESTIONS FOR IMPROVEMENT:**
While the technical results are strong, the paper could be improved in terms of clarity, positioning, and engagement with related work.

1.  **Clarity on the Local Privacy Model:** A central point that needs to be communicated more clearly is the distinction between the *local* privacy model used here and the more common *central* differential privacy model. In the local model, data is privatized before being sent to an untrusted curator, which is a substantially different and stronger privacy setting. This distinction may not be apparent to all readers, and the potential for confusion is heightened by statements (e.g., bottom of page 4) that generalize the results to "differential privacy" without this crucial qualifier. The authors should be more precise in their language to avoid misleading readers.

2.  **Positioning and Originality:** The paper is presented as a NIPS-formatted version of a more comprehensive arXiv manuscript [9]. To help the NIPS audience, the unique contributions of this specific submission should be more explicitly delineated. Based on the technical report, it appears the primary novel contribution for this venue is the analysis of density estimation, whereas the general framework and multinomial results are established in the longer work. A clear statement to this effect would help readers correctly assess the paper's specific contribution.

3.  **Comparison with Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be strengthened. In particular, a comparison with the differentially private M-estimation procedure of Lei [19] is missing but seems highly relevant for the density estimation results. Additionally, for multinomial estimation, while the paper demonstrates the optimality of randomized response, a brief discussion of how other private histogram estimation methods compare to the derived minimax bounds would provide valuable context.

4.  **Framing of the Contribution:** The paper's focus on estimating *population* parameters, rather than sample statistics, is an important and subtle aspect of its contribution that distinguishes it from some prior work. This point is mentioned in Section 2.1 but should be highlighted more prominently at the beginning of the paper to help readers properly situate the results and their significance.

**QUESTIONS FOR THE AUTHORS:**
*   **Restriction on α:** Theorem 1 requires α ∈ [0, 1]. Is this restriction essential for the proof, or can the bounds be extended? Given that many practical applications of differential privacy consider α values around 1 or slightly larger, understanding the behavior of the bounds in this regime is important.
*   **Choice of Metric ρ:** The analysis is framed in terms of a general semi-metric ρ. The paper would be more accessible if it included a brief discussion on the implications of choosing different metrics (e.g., L1 vs. L2 norm for the multinomial case), and how this choice reflects different utility goals for an analyst.
