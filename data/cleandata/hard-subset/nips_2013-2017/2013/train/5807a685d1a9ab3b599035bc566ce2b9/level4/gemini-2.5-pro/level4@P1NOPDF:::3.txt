**Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations**
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This paper studies the problem of probability estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy. In this setting, each of the *n* users holds a private data type. To preserve privacy, each user sends a randomized signal to a central curator, where the signal is an α-differentially private function of their own type and, in the general model, the types of the other *n-1* users.

The authors analyze this problem through the lens of minimax estimation error. The goal is to find a local privacy protocol that minimizes the maximum expected error for a given estimation task, where the maximum is taken over all possible prior distributions on the users' data. The paper focuses on estimating the underlying data distribution, measuring error using the squared l2 distance.

The main contributions are twofold. First, for discrete distributions over *d* types, the authors establish a sharp minimax lower bound on the estimation error. They then show that the classic randomized response mechanism achieves this bound, proving its optimality in this context. Second, they extend this analysis to the problem of density estimation for continuous distributions, again providing a lower bound that is met by a simple, non-interactive perturbation scheme where each user randomizes their report based solely on their own data.

This is a nice and important paper. By establishing fundamental lower bounds, it formally demonstrates that a classic and simple procedure like randomized response is not just a heuristic but is, in fact, the best possible solution. The results will be of significant interest to the NIPS community, and I recommend acceptance.

I do have a few suggestions and reservations that could strengthen the paper:

1.  **Presentation and Clarity:** In terms of presentation, the explanation of the minimax framework could be simplified for greater accessibility. The paper also uses notation that is at times cumbersome. For example, since the primary bounds are developed for the l2 norm, could the results be stated more directly in terms of this norm to improve readability?

2.  **Scope and Connections:** The analysis is focused on the l2 norm. I would have appreciated a discussion of the l1 norm, which is a more restrictive metric. A comment relating these l2 bounds to the l1 case, or a comparison to other lower bounds in the (admittedly, non-local) differential privacy literature, would help place the contributions in a broader context.

3.  **Generality of the Bounds:** The minimax bounds appear to be specifically tailored to the local privacy setting. It would be valuable if the authors discussed whether their proof techniques could be applied to a broader range of settings or used to derive minimax lower bounds for other privacy models.

4.  **Optimality of Non-Interactive Schemes:** A key and elegant finding is that while the general local privacy model allows a user's report to depend on the types of all other *n-1* users, the optimal scheme only requires each user to randomize their report based on their own type. This is a powerful result, demonstrating that interaction among users is unnecessary for this task. I encourage the authors to state this important implication more explicitly in the text.

**Update:**
Since this review was initiated, the list of FOCS 2013 accepted papers has been published. It appears there is significant overlap with one of those papers, and this submission may be an extension, particularly concerning the results in Section 3. While I still believe the work is valuable and recommend acceptance, this context of prior publication slightly tempers my initial enthusiasm.

In summary, the paper provides a rigorous minimax analysis for estimation under local privacy, deriving sharp lower bounds that are met by the classic randomized response mechanism. This is a valuable contribution to our understanding of the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving data analysis.
