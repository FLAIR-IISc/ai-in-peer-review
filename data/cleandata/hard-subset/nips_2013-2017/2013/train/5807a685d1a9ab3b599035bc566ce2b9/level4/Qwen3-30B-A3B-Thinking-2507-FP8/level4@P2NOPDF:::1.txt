

Summary: This paper adapts an arXiv manuscript into NIPS format, establishing a lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on privately perturbed data under the *local privacy model* (where the data holder does not trust the algorithm). The analysis employs a LeCam-style argument, demonstrating that randomized response achieves optimality in specific settings.  

Strengths:  
- The results rigorously characterize fundamental limitations of learning from data perturbed for privacy, a critical contribution to the field.  

Weaknesses and Major Concerns:  
1. **Critical Confusion with Differential Privacy**: The paper fails to clearly distinguish its *local privacy* model from the standard *differential privacy* (DP) framework. This is exacerbated by statements like the one on page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α..." Such phrasing is potentially misleading, as the results pertain *only* to local privacy, not DP. The authors must explicitly clarify this distinction throughout the paper to avoid reader confusion.  
2. **Missing Contextual Links**: The work does not adequately connect to existing literature on differential privacy (e.g., foundational results on DP utility bounds). This omission hinders the reader’s understanding of the paper’s positioning within the broader privacy landscape.  
3. **Narrowed Contribution After Rebuttal**: Post-rebuttal, it is evident that the exposition of the local privacy model (a claimed contribution) is largely covered in reference [10] (Lei et al., *Journal of Machine Learning Research*, 2016). While the density estimation bounds are novel, the paper’s primary contribution is now narrowly limited to these bounds within the local privacy framework—*not* a comprehensive model exposition. A comparative discussion with [10] is essential for context.  
4. **Theoretical Gaps**:  
   - The necessity of the α restriction in Theorem 1 is unclear. Given empirical evidence that α ≈ 1 is often the practical limit for learning under DP (e.g., in histogram estimation), the bound’s relevance to this regime is questionable.  
   - The choice of ρ (defining the utility measure) lacks discussion. Clarifying how different ρ values capture alternative utility objectives would strengthen the analysis.  
5. **Comparative Omissions**:  
   - The paper does not compare its density estimator to Lei’s M-estimation procedure (cited in [10]), despite its relevance to discrete data estimation.  
   - The optimality of randomized response (for density estimation) is claimed, yet the paper omits discussion of how other histogram estimation methods (e.g., Laplace perturbation, or non-private baselines) perform relative to the derived bound.  

Minor Suggestions:  
- Explicitly define the distinction between *population* and *sample* statistics early in the paper (as noted in the rebuttal response to Reviewer 9).  
- Add a concise comparison to related work (including Lei’s method and other histogram estimators) in the introduction or related work section.  

Overall Assessment:  
The paper’s core technical contribution—lower bounds for density estimation under local privacy—is valid and valuable. However, its impact is significantly undermined by:  
(i) the persistent conflation of local privacy with differential privacy,  
(ii) the lack of contextualization against [10] and existing DP literature, and  
(iii) insufficient discussion of practical implications (e.g., ρ choices, α regime relevance).  
The results would be stronger with a clearer exposition of the model’s uniqueness (vs. DP), a precise statement of the bound’s scope, and a thorough comparison to relevant prior work. Without these revisions, the paper risks misleading readers about the scope of its contribution and the applicability of its bounds.  

Decision: **Reject** (Major revisions required to resolve critical conceptual confusions and contextual gaps).
