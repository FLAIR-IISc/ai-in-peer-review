**Summary**

This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiv manuscript that uses a LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on private data. The analysis is situated in the local privacy model, where the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. As a corollary, the authors show that randomized response is an optimal strategy in a specific sense.

*Note: This review was conducted without examining the supplementary material.*

**Review**

The primary strength of this paper is that its results characterize the fundamental limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy. However, the paper could be improved in several areas. The commentary on the results lacks precision in places, which could confuse readers. Furthermore, the paper does not sufficiently connect its findings to existing results in the differential privacy literature.

**Detailed Comments**

*   A key aspect of this work is its focus on the local privacy model, which is fundamentally different from the more common central differential privacy model. The authors should emphasize this distinction more clearly and explicitly, as many readers may not appreciate its significance. This potential for confusion is exacerbated by statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…" To avoid misinterpretation, the authors should refrain from making such broad statements, particularly when their results apply specifically to the local privacy setting.

*   Is the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 necessary? Experimental results in the differential privacy literature suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may be a practical upper limit for some learning problems. It is therefore unclear what the bound in Theorem 1 implies for these relevant scenarios. The authors should clarify if this restriction is necessary and what its implications are.

*   The paper would be strengthened by including some discussion on the possible choices for the loss function $\rho$. This would help readers understand how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.

*   The paper does not cite or compare its density estimator to the M-estimation procedure of Lei. This appears to be a relevant piece of prior work that should be discussed.

*   While the paper shows that randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence for histogram estimation, a discussion of how other approaches for discrete data compare would provide valuable context for the result.

**Addendum After Rebuttal**

*   The distinction between population and sample statistics should be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (as noted in the response to Reviewer 9).

*   A comparison to related work, including the work of Lei and others mentioned during the review process, is important for contextualizing the paper's contributions.

*   A closer inspection of reference [10], which has recently appeared, suggests that the novel contributions of this paper are narrower than initially perceived. While the venues (and audiences) for this work and [10] are different, the primary contributions of this paper appear to be a careful exposition of the local privacy model and new bounds for density estimation. The latter contribution is new, but the former is essentially contained in [10].
