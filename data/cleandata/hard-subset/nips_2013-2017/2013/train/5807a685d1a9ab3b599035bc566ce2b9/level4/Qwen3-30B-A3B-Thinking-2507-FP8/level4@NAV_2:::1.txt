

SUMMARY: This paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints, using minimax decision theory to characterize optimal rates of convergence. It demonstrates that Warner's randomized response is optimal for certain estimation problems, establishing fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The work builds on classical minimax theory while incorporating local differential privacy guarantees, with applications to both multinomial and density estimation problems.

PROS:
- The paper provides rigorous minimax bounds that precisely quantify the statistical cost of local privacy constraints
- The results establish that randomized response achieves optimal sample complexity for survey sampling, resolving an important question in privacy-preserving statistics
- The authors provide efficiently computable algorithms that match the theoretical lower bounds

CONS:
- The paper contains imprecise statements that could confuse readers about the relationship between local privacy and differential privacy, such as claiming "differential privacy" in contexts where only local privacy is considered
- The connection to existing work on differential privacy is insufficiently developed, particularly regarding the distinction between local and central privacy models
- The analysis does not adequately address the practical implications of the alpha restriction in Theorem 1, which may limit applicability to real-world privacy settings

ADDITIONAL COMMENTS:
- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy at the outset, as this is a critical conceptual difference that many readers may overlook
- The restriction to α ∈ [0,1] in Theorem 1 warrants further discussion, particularly given empirical evidence suggesting α ≈ 1 is the practical limit for many learning problems
- A brief exploration of how different utility metrics (ρ) affect the results would help readers understand the scope of the analysis
- The paper should directly compare its density estimation approach with Lei's M-estimation procedure, as this represents a significant related work that should be acknowledged
- A discussion of how other histogram-based privacy-preserving estimation methods compare to the proposed approach would strengthen the practical relevance of the results

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:
* The paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between population and sample statistics at the beginning, as this is a fundamental conceptual issue that impacts interpretation
* A more comprehensive comparison with related work, including Lei's M-estimation and the recent paper by [10], is essential for establishing the paper's contribution
* While the local privacy model is carefully developed, the primary novelty appears to be the density estimation bounds, as the foundational aspects of local privacy are largely covered in [10] (which has since appeared in a different venue)
