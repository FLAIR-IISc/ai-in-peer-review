This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between statistical efficiency and privacy by deriving minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors investigate two canonical problems: discrete probability estimation (multinomial model) and non-parametric density estimation. For both problems, they establish sharp minimax rates of convergence, providing both lower bounds on the error of any privacy-preserving estimator and matching upper bounds achieved by specific, efficient algorithms.

For the multinomial estimation problem, a key finding is that imposing α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα²/d`, where `d` is the number of categories. This implies that for a small privacy parameter α, the statistical cost is manageable. An interesting consequence highlighted is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism is, in fact, minimax optimal. For the non-parametric density estimation problem, the authors demonstrate a more severe penalty for privacy. The minimax convergence rate degrades from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`, where `β` measures the smoothness of the density. This change in the polynomial exponent represents a fundamental shift in statistical difficulty, not just a constant-factor loss in sample size.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a rigorous, well-executed analysis. The application of minimax decision theory to the formal framework of local differential privacy provides a principled way to quantify the cost of privacy. The proofs, from what I could verify, appear correct.
*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contributions are novel and important. To my knowledge, the derivation of sharp minimax rates for density estimation under a local privacy constraint is a new result. The finding that local privacy changes the polynomial rate of convergence is particularly insightful and significant. It deepens our understanding of the fundamental limits of what can be learned privately. Furthermore, formally proving the optimality of Warner's nearly 50-year-old randomized response mechanism within this modern framework is an elegant and valuable contribution. The presented tools and results are likely to be built upon by other researchers studying the intersection of statistics and privacy.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

My primary concerns relate to the positioning of the results with respect to existing literature on differential privacy lower bounds.

1.  **Relation to Lower Bounds for Central Differential Privacy:** The minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely related to prior work on the error/noise complexity of linear queries under (central) differential privacy, which is not sufficiently discussed. Specifically, the works of Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010), De (TCC 2012), and Nikolov, Talwar, & Zhang (STOC 2013) establish worst-case lower bounds for the error of releasing answers to linear counting queries, which includes multinomial probability estimation as a special case. Many of these results also consider Mean Squared Error. Since local differential privacy is a stronger constraint than central differential privacy, any lower bound for the central model is automatically a lower bound for the local model. It would significantly strengthen the paper if the authors could clarify how their new bound for local privacy compares to these existing bounds. Does it represent an improvement, and if so, by how much? A detailed discussion of this relationship is essential for contextualizing the contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Comparison for Density Estimation:** The comparison between the private and non-private rates for density estimation could be made more precise. The paper establishes its new lower bound for densities within a Sobolev space defined specifically via a trigonometric basis. To ensure a fair comparison, it should be explicitly clarified whether the cited classical minimax rate (Eq. 13) also applies to this specific function class, or if it holds for a more general Sobolev space. This clarification is needed to definitively attribute the change in the convergence rate exponent to the privacy constraint rather than a potential specialization of the function class.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   The paper addresses a fundamental and timely problem at the intersection of statistics and privacy.
    *   It provides novel, sharp minimax rates for two important estimation problems under local privacy.
    *   The result for density estimation, demonstrating a degradation in the polynomial convergence rate, is a significant and non-obvious finding.
    *   The work is technically solid, well-written, and connects classical ideas (randomized response) with a modern privacy framework.

*   **Arguments Against Acceptance:**
    *   The paper fails to adequately discuss and compare its multinomial estimation results with highly relevant and well-known lower bounds from the differential privacy literature. This omission weakens the context and assessment of the contribution's novelty.

**Recommendation:**

This paper proves interesting and sharp minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy. The results are technically strong, and the paper is well-written. The primary weakness is the lack of discussion and comparison with closely related prior work on lower bounds for central differential privacy. I recommend that the authors add a thorough discussion of the relationship between their multinomial estimation bound and the results in the aforementioned literature to the final version of the paper. Addressing this point would substantially strengthen the manuscript.
