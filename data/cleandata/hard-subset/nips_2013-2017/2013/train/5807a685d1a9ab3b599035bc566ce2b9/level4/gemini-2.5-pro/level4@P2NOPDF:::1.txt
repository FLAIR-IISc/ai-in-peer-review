### Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates minimax lower bounds for statistical estimation under the local model of privacy, where data holders perturb their own data before sharing it with an untrusted aggregator. Using a Fano's inequality and LeCam-style argument, the authors derive lower bounds for density estimation. A key corollary of this analysis is that the classic randomized response mechanism is, in a specific sense, a minimax-optimal strategy for this problem.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this work is its characterization of the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility in the local privacy setting. By establishing formal lower bounds, the paper delineates the inherent statistical cost of privacy and quantifies the limitations of any algorithm operating under this constraint. This provides a valuable theoretical benchmark for the field.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Clarity on the Privacy Model and its Scope:** A major concern is the potential for confusion regarding the privacy model. The paper's results hinge on the *local privacy* model, which is substantially different from the more common *central model* of differential privacy. This critical distinction is not made sufficiently clear early in the paper. This lack of clarity could be exacerbated by overly broad statements, such as the one at the bottom of page 4 ("Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…"). Such statements risk misleading readers into incorrectly generalizing these bounds to the central privacy setting. The authors should be more precise in their language and explicitly contrast the local and central models to properly frame their contributions.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper's connection to existing literature is underdeveloped. For a reader to appreciate the context and contribution, a comparison to related work is essential.
    *   The M-estimation procedure for private density estimation by Lei is a highly relevant work that is not cited or compared against.
    *   While randomized response is shown to be optimal, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of how other known algorithms for private histogram estimation compare to the derived lower bound.

3.  **Novelty and Contribution in Light of [10]:** The novelty of the paper's contribution should be more carefully situated, particularly with respect to reference [10]. It appears that the exposition of the local privacy framework is largely contained within [10]. This narrows the primary new contribution of this manuscript to the derivation of minimax bounds for density estimation. While this is a valuable contribution, the authors should clearly acknowledge the prior work on the model itself to provide readers with an accurate understanding of this paper's specific advance.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Distinction Between Population and Sample Statistics:** The paper would benefit from a clearer and more explicit explanation of the distinction between population and sample statistics, introduced early on to orient the reader.

*   **On the Restriction of $\alpha$ in Theorem 1:** Is the restriction on the privacy parameter $\alpha$ necessary for the result in Theorem 1? In many practical applications of differential privacy, particularly for complex learning tasks, $\alpha \approx 1$ is a common and meaningful regime. It is currently unclear how the derived bounds apply to this important case, and clarification would be beneficial.

*   **Interpretation of the Loss Function $\rho$:** To help readers better grasp the framework's versatility, it would be helpful to include some commentary on the possible choices for the loss function $\rho$. Providing concrete examples of how different utility measures (e.g., for different estimation tasks) can be captured by the analysis would broaden the paper's impact.
