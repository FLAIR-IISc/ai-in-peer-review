This paper studies minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint of preserving individual data privacy, utilizing the definition of local privacy. The authors investigate two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation, and establish sharp minimax rates of convergence for both. They demonstrate that, for discrete multinomial estimation, local privacy induces a reduction in effective sample size that is quadratic in the privacy parameter α. Given that α is often a small constant, this reduction results in an effective sample size comparable to the non-private case. For density estimation, the authors show that the optimal convergence rate achievable in the non-private setting becomes unattainable when local privacy is enforced.

Overall, the results are highly interesting, and the proofs appear correct upon review. To the best of my knowledge, the minimax bound for density estimation under a privacy constraint has not been previously considered. 

My primary concern is that the minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely related to existing work on noise complexity for differential privacy, yet this connection is not explicitly acknowledged. Specifically, the papers by Hardt & Talwar (On the geometry of differential privacy, STOC 2010) and De (Lower bounds in differential privacy, TCC 2012) address highly relevant problems: worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem considered here is, in fact, a special case of the linear counting query studied in those works, and the error metric used (L_2) is identical. The key distinction lies in this paper’s focus on local differential privacy, which imposes a stronger constraint than standard differential privacy. Consequently, lower bounds for differential privacy also apply to local privacy. It would be valuable to know if the bound presented for local privacy improves upon existing bounds for differential privacy.

Regarding the density estimation problem, the statement of the result is somewhat unclear. The authors claim the lower bound in the local privacy setting is higher than in the non-private setting; however, the presented minimax bound applies specifically to the case where the density can be expanded using a trigonometric basis. I question whether the non-private lower bound (eq. 13) holds generally for the Sobolev space defined in Definition 1, or only for the special case of trigonometric basis functions. A fair comparison necessitates that the non-private lower bound also applies to the trigonometric basis.

**Additional comments to the rebuttal:**

The feedback partially clarifies the relationship to previous worst-case lower bounds. However, the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases, STOC 2013), which also considers Mean Square Error, should also be discussed. I recommend the authors incorporate their explanations and the aforementioned missing references into the paper. 

In summary, this paper proves sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function and probability density function estimation with privacy guarantees. The results are interesting and the paper is well-written. However, a more thorough discussion of known results regarding noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy—which are highly relevant—would strengthen the contribution.
