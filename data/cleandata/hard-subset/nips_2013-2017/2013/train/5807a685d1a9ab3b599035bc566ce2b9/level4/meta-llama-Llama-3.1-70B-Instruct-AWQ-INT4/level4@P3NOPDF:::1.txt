This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript, employing a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on estimation algorithms that operate on private data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. Notably, randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy in certain contexts.

The paper has both strengths and weaknesses. On the positive side, the results provide valuable insights into the limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy. However, there are some areas that require improvement. Specifically, the commentary on the results could be more precise, as it may lead to confusion among casual readers. Furthermore, the connections to existing results on differential privacy are not explicitly established, which might be beneficial for readers familiar with this field.

It is essential to acknowledge that the restriction to local privacy, crucial for the results, makes the privacy model distinct from the differential privacy model. This distinction may not be immediately apparent to many readers, and statements such as those at the bottom of page 4 ("Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…") might be perceived as overly broad or even incorrect. To avoid confusion, the authors should refrain from making such statements and instead focus on accurately describing their results.

Additionally, it is unclear whether the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is necessary. Experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may be the most one can expect for certain learning problems under differential privacy, which raises questions about the implications of the bound in this case. Providing some commentary on the possible choices of $\rho$ would also be beneficial, as it would allow readers to understand how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.

Furthermore, a comparison with the M-estimation procedure of Lei would be valuable, as this paper is not cited, despite its relevance to the topic. Moreover, there are numerous other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data, and it would be interesting to see how these algorithms compare to the proposed density estimator, particularly in terms of their convergence rates.

In response to the rebuttal, I would like to reiterate the importance of clearly explaining the distinction between population and sample statistics at the beginning of the paper. A comparison with related work, including Lei and other relevant studies, would also provide essential context. Upon closer inspection of [10], it appears that the additional contribution of this paper is twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model and bounds for density estimation. While the latter are new, the former is largely contained in [10]. As the venues and audiences for these papers differ, it is crucial to acknowledge the overlap and clearly articulate the unique contributions of this work.

Overall, the paper provides valuable insights into the limitations of learning from private data, but could benefit from more precise commentary, connections to existing results, and a clearer explanation of the contributions.
