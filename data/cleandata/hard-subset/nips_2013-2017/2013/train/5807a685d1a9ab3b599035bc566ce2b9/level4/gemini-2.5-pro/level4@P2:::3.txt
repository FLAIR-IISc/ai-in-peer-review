This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between statistical accuracy and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors apply minimax decision theory to quantify the inherent cost of privacy for two core statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. In the local privacy model, each individual's data is privatized before being sent to an aggregator, offering a very strong privacy guarantee.

The paper's main contributions are the derivation of sharp minimax convergence rates for these estimation problems. For estimating a d-dimensional multinomial distribution, the authors show that achieving α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `n * α^2 / d`. A significant consequence of this result is that Warner's classic randomized response technique from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal. For the more complex problem of estimating a probability density with β degrees of smoothness, the paper reveals a more severe penalty: local privacy changes the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate, from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(n * α^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. The authors complement these information-theoretic lower bounds with computationally efficient algorithms that achieve matching upper bounds, demonstrating that the derived rates are tight.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance:** The paper establishes a rigorous and important connection between modern concepts of differential privacy and classical statistical decision theory. By providing sharp minimax rates, it offers a formal, quantitative understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off, moving beyond ad-hoc analyses of specific algorithms. This foundational work is likely to be influential for future research in private statistical inference.

2.  **Quality of Results:** The technical contributions are excellent. The derivation of matching upper and lower bounds is the gold standard for this line of theoretical work. The results are not just asymptotic but provide clear dependencies on the privacy parameter `α` and problem dimension `d`.

3.  **Important Insights:** The paper provides two key insights. First, it proves that a simple, 50-year-old mechanism (randomized response) is optimal for private multinomial estimation, which is both an elegant and practically relevant finding. Second, the discovery that local privacy alters the convergence rate's exponent for density estimation is a novel and striking result, highlighting that the cost of privacy can be fundamentally different for finite-dimensional and nonparametric problems. The fact that simple, non-interactive mechanisms are sufficient to achieve optimality is also a powerful conclusion.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The presentation, particularly in the background section on the minimax framework, is quite dense and assumes significant prior knowledge. While technically precise, a more intuitive high-level explanation of the concepts could make the paper accessible to a broader segment of the NIPS audience.

2.  **Discussion of L1 Norm:** While Theorem 1 commendably provides bounds for both L2 and L1 norms in the multinomial case, the subsequent discussion focuses primarily on L2 error. Given that the L1 (total variation) distance is arguably a more natural metric for probability distributions, expanding the discussion on the L1 results and their implications would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Originality Concern:** A point of concern is the significant overlap with a paper by the same authors accepted at FOCS 2013 (referenced in the paper as the arXiv preprint [9]). The NIPS community values novelty, and the submission should clearly articulate its contributions beyond the FOCS version to warrant a separate top-tier publication. The program committee should carefully consider the extent of new material presented here.

4.  **Emphasis on Key Findings:** The authors correctly note that their lower bounds on *population* parameter estimation are stronger than prior work focusing on *sample* quantity estimation. This is a crucial distinction that could be stated more explicitly and prominently in the introduction to better frame the paper's contribution relative to existing literature. Similarly, the insight that simple, non-interactive privacy mechanisms are optimal (i.e., users do not need to coordinate) is a powerful conclusion that deserves to be explicitly stated and emphasized.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality, technically deep paper that makes a significant contribution to the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy. The results are strong, insightful, and foundational. The main weaknesses are related to the clarity of the presentation for a general audience and a potential lack of novelty given the concurrent FOCS publication.

Despite the concern about originality, the work is of high caliber and of definite interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, but I urge the program committee to evaluate the submission's novel contributions relative to the authors' FOCS 2013 paper.
