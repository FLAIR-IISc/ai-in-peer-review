

SUMMARY: This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript that employs a LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on privately held data when the algorithm is untrusted by the data holder. As a corollary, randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy under specific conditions.  
As a caveat, I did not review the supplementary material.  

PROS AND CONS:  
- The results provide a characterization of the limitations of learning from data perturbed to ensure privacy.  
- The commentary on the results contains imprecisions that could confuse casual readers (see additional comments).  
- Connections to existing differential privacy literature appear to be missing.  

ADDITIONAL COMMENTS:  
- The restriction to local privacy, which is essential for the results, substantially differentiates the privacy model from standard differential privacy. Many readers may overlook this distinction, which is exacerbated by statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…" Such broad (and potentially incorrect) descriptions should be avoided.  
- Is the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 necessary? Experimental evidence suggests $\alpha \approx 1$ represents the practical limit for certain learning problems under differential privacy; the bound's relevance to this case is unclear.  
- A discussion of possible $\rho$ choices would clarify how different utility measures are captured by the analysis.  
- The density estimator should be compared to Lei's M-estimation procedure (not cited; authors should address this work).  
- While randomized response achieves the optimal convergence rate for histogram estimation, the paper omits comparison with other discrete-data histogram estimation approaches.  

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:  
* The distinction between population and sample statistics requires clearer and more explicit explanation at the paper's outset (cf. response to Rev. 9).  
* A comparative analysis with related work (including Lei and other references raised by reviewers) is essential for context.  
* A closer examination of [10], now published, indicates the paper's contribution is narrower than initially suggested. While venues differ, the local privacy model exposition is largely contained in [10]; the paper's novelty is limited to a careful exposition of this model and new density estimation bounds.
