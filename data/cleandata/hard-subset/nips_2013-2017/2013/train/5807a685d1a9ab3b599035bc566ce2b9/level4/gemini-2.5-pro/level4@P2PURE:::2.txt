This paper derives sharp minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under the constraint of local privacy. The authors address two fundamental problems: discrete multinomial estimation and non-parametric density estimation. For multinomial estimation, they demonstrate that local privacy introduces a factor of the privacy parameter, alpha, into the rate, though the convergence rate's dependence on the sample size `n` remains the same as in the non-private setting. For density estimation, they establish that the optimal non-private rate is fundamentally unattainable under the local privacy constraint.

Overall, the paper's results are novel and significant. The proofs appear to be correct, and the minimax analysis for density estimation under local privacy constraints is, to my knowledge, the first of its kind.

My main concern is the lack of discussion regarding previous, highly relevant work on lower bounds for differential privacy. The minimax bound for multinomial estimation, in particular, is closely related to the noise complexity results from this literature. Specifically, the work of Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012) established worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under central differential privacy. The multinomial probability estimation problem is a special case of the linear counting queries they study, and the L2 error metric is identical.

While this paper focuses on local differential privacy, which imposes a stronger constraint than the central differential privacy model, any lower bound for central DP also applies to local DP. It is therefore crucial for the authors to clarify whether their new bound for local privacy improves upon these existing bounds. Furthermore, the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013) also considers the Mean Squared Error for similar problems. The authors should incorporate a thorough discussion of this related work and add these missing references to the manuscript.

For the density estimation problem, the comparison between the private and non-private rates is potentially misleading. The paper claims that the lower bound under local privacy is strictly worse than the non-private rate. However, the derived minimax bound is specific to a class of densities that can be expanded with a trigonometric basis. For this comparison to be valid, the non-private lower bound cited in Eq. (13) must also apply specifically to this trigonometric basis class, rather than the more general Sobolev space from Definition 1. The authors should clarify whether both the private and non-private bounds are being compared over the same function class.

In summary, this paper presents interesting and well-supported results, proving sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function and probability density function estimation under local privacy. The paper is well written, but its primary weakness is the insufficient discussion of highly relevant prior work on lower bounds in differential privacy. Addressing the relationship to this literature is essential for contextualizing the paper's contribution.
