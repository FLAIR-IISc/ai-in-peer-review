

**Summary:**  
This paper adapts an arXiv manuscript into NIPS format, employing a LeCam-style argument to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on data perturbed under *local privacy*—where the data holder does not trust the algorithm. The key result characterizes randomized response as optimal in a specific sense.  

**Strengths:**  
The work clearly delineates the fundamental limitations of learning from locally privatized data, offering a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding privacy-utility trade-offs in untrusted settings.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Critical Mischaracterization of Differential Privacy (DP):**  
   The paper repeatedly conflates *local privacy* with *differential privacy*, creating significant confusion. For instance, the statement on page 4 ("for suitably large sample sizes \(n\), the effect of providing differential privacy at level \(\alpha\)...") is misleading and potentially incorrect. Local privacy (e.g., randomized response) and DP are distinct models with different guarantees and sample complexities. The paper must explicitly clarify this distinction, as readers unfamiliar with the nuances may misinterpret the results as applying broadly to DP, which they do not.  

2. **Incomplete Contextualization with Existing Work:**  
   The connection to foundational DP literature is superficial. The paper fails to discuss how its lower bounds relate to established results (e.g., in the DP literature for global privacy), leaving readers without a clear sense of novelty or positioning. Crucially, the recent work [10] (now published) provides a comprehensive treatment of local privacy and density estimation, including the same lower bounds derived here. While the venues differ, [10] subsumes the core theoretical contribution of this paper (the local privacy model and its implications for density estimation), reducing the novelty claim. The authors must explicitly compare and contrast with [10] to justify their contribution.  

3. **Ambiguity in Theorem 1’s Conditions:**  
   The restriction on \(\alpha\) (e.g., \(\alpha < 1/2\)) in Theorem 1 appears arbitrary. Given that \(\alpha \approx 1\) is often the practical limit for learning under DP (e.g., in high-dimensional settings), the bound’s relevance for realistic scenarios is unclear. The paper should justify this restriction or discuss whether the bound holds for larger \(\alpha\).  

**Minor Concerns:**  
- **Parameter \(\rho\):** A brief discussion of how different choices of \(\rho\) (e.g., KL divergence, Hellinger distance) affect the utility-privacy trade-off would help readers contextualize the analysis.  
- **Comparison to Alternative Methods:** The paper claims randomized response achieves optimal rates but omits comparison to other histogram estimators (e.g., Lei’s M-estimation procedure [cited in the draft] or other local-privacy techniques). A short discussion of how these methods stack up would strengthen the argument for optimality.  
- **Population vs. Sample Statistics:** As noted in the rebuttal, the distinction between population-level and sample-level quantities (e.g., in the definition of density estimation error) requires explicit clarification early in the paper to avoid confusion.  

**Additional Clarifications Needed:**  
- The phrase "differential privacy" should be replaced with "local privacy" throughout to avoid misalignment with the model.  
- The claim that randomized response is "optimal" should specify *under what conditions* (e.g., for a given \(\alpha\) and utility measure \(\rho\)). Without this precision, the conclusion risks overgeneralization.  
- The supplementary material (unreviewed) may contain critical details on \(\rho\) or comparisons; the authors should ensure these are reflected in the main text.  

**Rebuttal Considerations:**  
The authors’ rebuttal addressed the population/sample statistic confusion and promised a comparison to [10] and related work (e.g., Lei). These are essential additions. However, the addendum reveals that [10] already covers the local privacy framework, making the paper’s contribution primarily a *rederivation* of bounds for density estimation. This significantly narrows the novelty. The paper must explicitly state:  
> *"While [10] establishes the local privacy model, our work provides a tight lower bound for density estimation under this model, complementing their framework."*  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper addresses a meaningful problem but suffers from critical misrepresentations of privacy models and insufficient engagement with the literature. The core technical contribution (the lower bound) is valid but not novel in light of [10]. The confusion between local privacy and DP undermines the paper’s credibility, and the omission of key comparisons (Lei, [10]) weakens its positioning.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject.** The paper’s contribution is marginal relative to [10], and the persistent mischaracterization of DP risks misleading readers. To merit acceptance, the authors would need to:  
(i) Rewrite the paper to *explicitly* distinguish local privacy from DP,  
(ii) Provide a rigorous comparison to [10] and Lei’s work,  
(iii) Justify the \(\alpha\) restriction in Theorem 1, and  
(iv) Clarify the scope of "optimality" for randomized response.  
Without these, the paper does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS.
