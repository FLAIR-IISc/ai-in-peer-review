This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript, and uses a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on estimation algorithms that operate on private data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. As a corollary, randomized response turns out to be an optimal strategy in some sense. 

The paper provides a detailed study of the estimation of probability distributions—discrete and continuous—in a stringent setting in which data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors give sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate, as well as providing tools to allow movement along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum. 

The main contributions of the paper are the characterization of the limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy and the demonstration that randomized response is an optimal strategy in some sense. 

However, there are some imprecisions in the commentary on the results which could lead a casual reader to become confused. For example, the restriction to local privacy, which is important for the results, makes the privacy model quite different than the differential privacy model, a fact which many readers may not appreciate. This confusion may be exacerbated by statements such as those at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…" The authors should avoid making such overly broad (and perhaps incorrect) statements when describing their results.

Additionally, the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 may not be necessary. In particular, experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may be the most one can expect for certain learning problems (under differential privacy), so it is unclear what the bound tells us about this case. 

Some commentary on the possible choices of $\rho$ may be nice, so that readers can see how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis. 

Furthermore, a comparison to related work, such as the M-estimation procedure of Lei, would be beneficial for context. 

The density estimator proposed in the paper compares favorably to the M-estimation procedure of Lei, and the authors should be aware of this work. 

There are many other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. While randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, how do these other algorithms stack up?

In conclusion, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of private data analysis, but could benefit from some clarifications and additional comparisons to related work.

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL: 
* I think the distinction between the population vs. sample statistics needs to be explained more clearly and more explicitly at the beginning of the paper. 
* A comparison to related work (Lei and those brought up by another reviewer) is important for context. 
* A closer inspection of [10], which has now appeared, makes me construe the additional contribution of this paper more narrowly. While the venues (and hence audiences) for this and [10] are different, the contribution of this paper is twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model, and bounds for density estimation. The latter are new but the former is essentially contained in [10].
