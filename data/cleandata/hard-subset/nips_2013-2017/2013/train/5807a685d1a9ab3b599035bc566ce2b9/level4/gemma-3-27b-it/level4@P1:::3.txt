Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ 
This paper provides a detailed study of probability estimation – both discrete and continuous – in a stringent setting where data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors establish sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate, and identifying the points along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum. A key result is that Warner’s classical randomized response mechanism is optimal for survey sampling while maintaining respondent privacy.

The paper is a valuable contribution and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community. The analysis is rigorous, and the connection between classical privacy techniques and modern statistical decision theory is insightful. I recommend acceptance.

However, there are areas where the presentation could be improved. While the technical content is strong, the explanation of the minimax framework could be more accessible, particularly for readers less familiar with the area. The paper also uses a considerable amount of notation, which sometimes makes following the arguments cumbersome. Specifically, given that many of the bounds are expressed in terms of the l2 norm, it would enhance clarity to consistently frame the results with respect to this norm.

I would also appreciate a discussion of the implications for the l1 norm, which represents a more restrictive error metric, or a comparison of the obtained bounds with existing lower bounds in (centralized) differential privacy. Furthermore, the minimax bounds presented appear specifically tailored to the local privacy setting. Exploring broader settings and providing corresponding minimax lower bounds would strengthen the generality of the results.

A particularly interesting finding is that, despite the possibility of users randomizing their reported types based on the signals of other users, the simple randomized response scheme – where each person randomizes their report solely based on their own type – is optimal. Explicitly stating this observation in the text would be beneficial.

A recent development is the publication of the accepted papers from FOCS 2013. This reveals that the work presented builds upon and extends results from a related paper, especially those in Section 3. While I still recommend acceptance, my recommendation is somewhat less enthusiastic given this overlap. 

In summary, the paper analyzes the local privacy framework using minimax rate bounds, establishing lower bounds on the rate and demonstrating that randomized response achieves the upper bound. This is a significant and well-executed result.
