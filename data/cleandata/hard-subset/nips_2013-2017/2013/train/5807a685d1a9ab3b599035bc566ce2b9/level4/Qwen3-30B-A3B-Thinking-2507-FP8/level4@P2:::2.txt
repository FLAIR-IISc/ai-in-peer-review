

This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, providing sharp convergence rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors formalize a stringent privacy model where data remains private even from the statistician, and establish fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. Their key contributions include showing that for multinomial estimation, local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d (where α is the privacy parameter), and for density estimation with β smooth derivatives, the convergence rate changes from n⁻²β/(2β+1) in the non-private setting to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2) under local privacy. The authors demonstrate that Warner's randomized response mechanism is optimal for multinomial estimation, a finding that has remained largely overlooked despite its 50-year history.

The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging classical privacy techniques with modern differential privacy frameworks. It builds on Warner's work [23] while connecting to contemporary theoretical treatments of privacy [1, 11, 13, 24]. The authors' minimax analysis provides a rigorous foundation for understanding the statistical costs of privacy, which aligns well with NIPS's interest in privacy-preserving machine learning.

Quality: The technical content is strong, with rigorous proofs for both lower and upper bounds. The paper provides matching upper bounds using computationally efficient algorithms, a notable strength. The proofs appear sound, though the connection to prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy requires clarification.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results. The discussion of Warner's randomized response and its optimality is particularly well-explained.

Originality: This paper addresses a significant gap by establishing sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, which has not been previously established. While the authors acknowledge Warner's work, they make a novel contribution by formalizing the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-offs. However, the paper would be stronger with a more thorough comparison to prior work on noise complexity in differential privacy.

Significance: The results have important implications for privacy-preserving statistical analysis, particularly in survey sampling where Warner's technique is commonly used. The identification of the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum provides a practical framework for balancing privacy and utility needs. These findings are likely to be valuable to researchers working on privacy-preserving machine learning.

Strengths:
- Provides sharp minimax bounds for both discrete and continuous probability estimation under local privacy
- Demonstrates the optimality of Warner's randomized response for multinomial estimation
- Offers computationally efficient algorithms that achieve theoretical bounds
- Connects historical privacy techniques to modern theoretical frameworks

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not sufficiently acknowledge and contrast its results with prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy, particularly Hardt & Talwar [16] and De [8], which study worst-case lower bounds for linear queries under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem considered here is a special case of linear counting queries, and the L₂ metric used is identical. While the paper focuses on local privacy (a stronger constraint than differential privacy), it should explicitly discuss how its bounds improve upon or differ from those in the differential privacy literature. The recent work by Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang [21] on Mean Square Error under differential privacy should also be cited.
- The density estimation result's comparison to the non-private setting is somewhat ambiguous. The lower bound (14) is derived for densities in the Sobolev space defined using the trigonometric basis (Definition 1), but the comparison to the classical rate (13) assumes the same basis. The paper should clarify whether the non-private lower bound (13) holds for the trigonometric basis or for general Sobolev spaces. If the latter, the comparison is invalid as the non-private bound might be tighter for specific bases.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Adding a more thorough discussion of the relationship to prior work on noise complexity in differential privacy, with explicit comparisons to Hardt & Talwar [16], De [8], and Nikolov et al. [21].
2. Clarifying the relationship between the non-private lower bound (13) and the specific basis used in the density estimation analysis.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to privacy-preserving statistical estimation. The results are novel, technically sound, and well-motivated. With minor revisions to address the concerns about related work and clarity in the density estimation analysis, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy
- Demonstrates the optimality of Warner's randomized response
- Connects historical privacy techniques to modern theoretical frameworks
- Offers practical algorithms that achieve theoretical bounds
- Has significant theoretical implications for privacy-preserving statistical analysis

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient discussion of related work on noise complexity for differential privacy
- Ambiguity in the density estimation comparison to non-private results

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about related work and clarity in the density estimation analysis.
