This paper studies minimax lower bounds for probability estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors analyze two fundamental statistical problems: discrete multinomial estimation and non-parametric density estimation. For each problem, they derive sharp minimax rates of convergence, providing both lower bounds on the error for any locally private mechanism and matching upper bounds achieved by specific, efficient algorithms.

For the discrete multinomial estimation problem, the authors demonstrate that imposing 帢-local privacy results in a reduction of the effective sample size from `n` to `n帢^2/d`, where `d` is the number of categories. For the continuous density estimation problem over a Sobolev class of smoothness `帣`, they show that local privacy changes the fundamental convergence rate, slowing it from the classical non-private rate of `n^(-2帣/(2帣+1))` to `(n帢^2)^(-2帣/(2帣+2))`.

The results presented in this paper are significant and interesting. To the best of my knowledge, the problem of deriving minimax bounds for density estimation under a formal privacy constraint has not been previously addressed. The technical arguments appear to be sound, and the paper makes a valuable contribution by connecting the tools of minimax decision theory with the formal guarantees of differential privacy.

Despite the strengths of the paper, there are a few points that could be improved, primarily concerning the positioning of the results with respect to prior work and the clarity of a key comparison.

**1. Relation to Lower Bounds in Central Differential Privacy:**
My main concern is that the analysis of multinomial estimation is closely related to a line of work on lower bounds for (central) differential privacy, which is not adequately discussed. The problem of estimating multinomial probabilities is a special case of answering a set of linear counting queries. The error of such queries under differential privacy has been studied extensively, for instance, in:
- Hardt & Talwar, "On the geometry of differential privacy," STOC, 2010.
- De, "Lower bounds in differential privacy," TCC, 2012.

These papers establish worst-case lower bounds on the error for linear queries, measured using the same L2 metric (or Mean Square Error) as in this work. While this paper focuses on local privacy and the prior work focuses on central privacy, the problem settings are deeply connected. The authors should explicitly discuss this literature and clarify how their lower bounds for the stricter local privacy model compare to the existing bounds for the central model. This discussion is crucial for contextualizing the paper's contribution. Furthermore, more recent work, such as Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, "The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases" (STOC 2013), also analyzes Mean Square Error and should be referenced. I strongly suggest that the authors add a detailed discussion of these connections and include the missing references.

**2. Clarity of Comparison for Density Estimation:**
For the density estimation problem, the presentation of the main result could be slightly confusing. The authors claim that the private lower bound they derive is fundamentally different from the classical non-private rate. However, the private lower bound in Theorem 2 is specifically derived for the class of densities that can be expanded using a trigonometric basis. For the comparison to be fair and transparent, it is essential to clarify whether the cited non-private minimax rate in Eq. (13) also holds for this specific subclass of the Sobolev space. The paper would be strengthened by explicitly stating that the `n^(-2帣/(2帣+1))` baseline applies to the same trigonometric function class for which the new private bound is established.

**Summary:**
This is a well-written paper that proves sharp and interesting minimax bounds for probability distribution estimation under local privacy constraints. The results are a valuable contribution to the field. The primary weakness is the insufficient discussion of highly relevant prior work on accuracy lower bounds in the central differential privacy model. Addressing this by adding the missing context and references, and clarifying the basis for comparison in the density estimation section, would significantly improve the paper.
