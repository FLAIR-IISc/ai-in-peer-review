

The paper investigates minimax lower bounds for probability estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), a setting where each user's report depends solely on their own data (not others' signals), and the curator observes privatized responses. The authors frame the problem as minimizing the worst-case expected ℓ₂ error between a true statistic (e.g., an empirical mean) and its privatized estimate. They establish tight minimax rates for estimating discrete distributions over *d* types, showing that the classic randomized response mechanism achieves the lower bound—proving its optimality for ℓ₂ estimation. The analysis extends to density estimation, where perturbing only one's own type (without coordination) remains optimal, matching the derived lower bound.

This work makes a valuable contribution by rigorously establishing that randomized response is not just practical but *information-theoretically optimal* for ℓ₂ estimation under LDP. The minimax framework elegantly captures the fundamental trade-off between privacy and estimation accuracy, and the tight bounds resolve a key open question about the optimality of standard LDP techniques. The results are significant for the NIPS community, as they provide a clear benchmark for evaluating LDP mechanisms in distributed statistics.

While the paper is technically sound and important, three minor concerns warrant attention. First, the exposition of minimax bounds could be streamlined. The current presentation uses dense notation (e.g., *P*, *Q*, *R*), which obscures the core intuition. Since all bounds are for ℓ₂, the authors could have consistently framed the results with respect to this norm from the outset, reducing notational clutter. For instance, stating the minimax rate as *Θ(√(d/n) + √(d/n²α²))* (with appropriate scaling) would clarify the dependence on *d*, *n*, and *α* without additional notation.

Second, the omission of ℓ₁ analysis is notable. As ℓ₁ is the more stringent norm in differential privacy (e.g., for privacy guarantees), a brief discussion of how the bounds relate to ℓ₁—either through norm equivalence or direct derivation—would strengthen the work. The authors could note that their ℓ₂ bounds imply weaker ℓ₁ guarantees, but a dedicated comparison would contextualize their results within broader DP literature (e.g., comparing to non-local settings where ℓ₁ bounds are tighter).

Third, the paper appears to extend a prior FOCS 2013 result (specifically, Theorem 3.1 in "Distributed Private Heavy Hitters" by Duchi et al.). While the current work generalizes the setting to density estimation and explicitly proves optimality of non-coordinated perturbation, the connection to FOCS 2013 should be acknowledged more explicitly. The authors should clarify how their minimax approach differs from or builds upon existing bounds, particularly regarding the use of the minimax framework in the LDP context. This would better position the contribution and address the reviewer’s concern that the work feels like a natural extension rather than a novel advance.

Despite these points, the core contribution remains compelling. The key insight—that simple per-user perturbation suffices for optimality in LDP—deserves emphasis. The paper states this implicitly but should explicitly declare it in the introduction or conclusion (e.g., "Our results show that coordination among users is unnecessary for optimal ℓ₂ estimation under LDP"). This would reinforce the practical takeaway for practitioners.

In summary, the paper delivers a clean, tight analysis of minimax rates for LDP estimation, with randomized response proven optimal. The technical execution is strong, and the results are relevant to distributed data analysis. The minor concerns about exposition, norm coverage, and novelty context are not substantial enough to undermine the contribution. The work is well-suited for NIPS, offering both theoretical rigor and practical clarity for the community.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a solid contribution to understanding fundamental limits of LDP, and the minor reservations do not detract from its significance. With minor clarifications (noted above), it would be an excellent addition to the proceedings.
