Summary: This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript and uses a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on the estimation algorithm when the algorithm data is not reliable by the data holder. As a corollary, random response proves to be an optimal strategy in some senses.

As a caveat for this review, I have not reviewed the supporting material.

Pros and Cons:

- The results illustrate the limitations of learning from perturbed data to guarantee privacy.
- There is some ambiguity in the comments on the results that may confuse a casual reader (see below).
- The connection to the existing results on differential privacy seems missing.

Additional Notes:

- The restriction on local privacy, which is crucial for the results, makes the privacy model significantly different from the differential privacy model, a fact that many readers may not understand. This confusion can be exacerbated by statements like the ones at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for a sufficiently large sample size n, the effect of providing differential privacy at level $\alpha...$. The authors should avoid making such overly broad (and perhaps incorrect) statements when describing their results."
- Is a restriction on alpha necessary in Theorem 1? Specifically, experimental results suggest that for some learning problems (under differential privacy), $\alpha \approx 1$ is the most likely value one can expect, so it is not clear that the limit tells us about this case.
- Some comment on possible alternatives to $\rho$ would be good, so readers can see how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.
- How does this density estimator compare to the M-estimation process of Lei? This paper is not cited at all, but I think the authors should be aware of it.
- There are many other approaches to histogram estimation for perturbed data. While random response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, how do these other algorithms stack up?

Appendix after rebuttal:

* I think the difference between population versus sample statistics should be more explicit and clearly explained at the beginning of the paper (in reference to Rev.9's response).
* Comparison with related work (brought up by Lei and another reviewer) is important for context.
* A closer look at [10], which is now visible, leads me to understand the additional contribution of this paper more narrowly. While the location (and therefore the audience) for this and [10] are different, the contribution of this paper is twofold: a careful description of the local privacy model, and limits for density estimation. The latter are new but the former are essentially contained in [10].
