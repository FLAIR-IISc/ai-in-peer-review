This paper presents a theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints, employing minimax decision-theoretic tools to characterize fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The core contribution lies in establishing sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems when data is kept private even from the statistician. A notable consequence of the results is the optimality of Warner’s randomized response method for survey sampling while preserving respondent privacy.

The paper begins by clearly outlining the problem setting and relevant background, including a discussion of differential privacy and its relationship to prior work on privacy and statistical inference. The formalization of local privacy via a likelihood ratio bound is well-motivated. The authors then derive lower and upper bounds on the minimax risk for multinomial and density estimation, demonstrating the performance degradation incurred by enforcing local privacy. Notably, the analysis reveals a different rate of convergence for density estimation under local privacy compared to the classical nonparametric estimation setting. The paper is well-structured and presents a rigorous mathematical treatment of the subject.

Strengths:
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper offers a solid theoretical foundation for understanding the limitations of statistical estimation with local privacy, building upon classical minimax theory and differential privacy concepts.
* **Sharp Bounds:** The derived minimax rates are sharp, providing a precise characterization of the trade-off between privacy and accuracy. 
* **Practical Relevance:** The demonstration of the optimality of Warner’s randomized response procedure highlights the practical implications of the theoretical results.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem setting is clearly defined and the assumptions are explicitly stated.
* **Novel Combination:** The work provides a novel combination of differential privacy, statistical decision theory, and minimax analysis to address a significant problem.

Weaknesses:
* **Precision in Commentary:** The discussion surrounding the results sometimes lacks precision, potentially leading to misinterpretations. For example, statements linking the results to differential privacy more broadly (e.g., on page 4) should be carefully worded to avoid overgeneralization.
* **Limited Discussion of α:** The necessity of the restriction on α in Theorem 1 is not fully explored. The implications of the results for practical values of α (e.g., α ≈ 1) could be further investigated.
* **Choice of Metric:** While the use of the semi-metric ρ is justified, a discussion of how different utility measures can be incorporated into the analysis would be valuable.
* **Missing Related Work:** The paper does not adequately address related work on differentially private estimation, particularly Lei [19], which proposes an alternative approach to private estimation.
* **Alternative Histogram Methods:** The discussion of histogram estimation could benefit from a comparison with other existing algorithms and their performance characteristics.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper provides a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of privacy-preserving statistical estimation.
* The results are mathematically rigorous and offer sharp bounds on performance.
* The work has the potential to impact the design of privacy-preserving algorithms in various applications.
* The identified trade-offs between privacy and accuracy are fundamental and relevant to the broader research community.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The commentary surrounding the results occasionally lacks precision and may be misleading.
* The paper does not fully engage with related work in differential privacy and private estimation.
* The analysis is limited to local privacy, which may not be applicable to all scenarios.

Overall, this is a strong paper with important theoretical contributions. Addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly by clarifying the commentary, expanding the discussion of related work, and exploring the implications of the results for different values of α, would further strengthen the manuscript and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
