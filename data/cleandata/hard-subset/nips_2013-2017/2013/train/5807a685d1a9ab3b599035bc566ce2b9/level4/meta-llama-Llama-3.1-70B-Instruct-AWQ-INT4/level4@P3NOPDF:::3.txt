Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

The paper explores the concept of local privacy, where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of the other n-1 users, and reports this type to the data curator. The authors analyze this setting using the framework of min-max expected error, where the adversary selects a distribution over inputs (types for the n users), and we choose an α-DP local scheme to minimize the distance between an estimation derived from the original data and a similar estimation derived from the reported signals.

The authors provide a lower bound for the min-max rate for l2 distance estimations and users drawn from a discrete distribution over d types. They demonstrate that the simple technique of randomized response achieves this bound, making it optimal. Furthermore, the authors analyze the problem of density estimations, where the lower bound on the min-max rate is met by a perturbation scheme in which each person perturbs only their own type.

The paper is well-written and important, as it shows that classic procedures can be optimal in achieving certain lower bounds. The NIPS community will likely find it interesting, and I recommend acceptance.

However, I have a few reservations. Firstly, the explanation of min-max bounds could be simplified for better clarity. Additionally, the paper's notation is cumbersome, particularly since all bounds apply to the l2 norm. It would be beneficial to phrase the bounds with respect to this norm. I would also appreciate a comment on the l1 norm, which is more restrictive, or a comparison of the given bounds to l1 norms and other lower bounds in differential privacy, even if they apply to a classical, non-local setting.

Moreover, the min-max bounds discussed seem tailored to the local-privacy setting. I wish the authors had considered a broader set of settings and provided min-max lower bounds for them as well. Nevertheless, I appreciate that the local-setting allows users to randomize the type they report based on the remaining n-1 users, and it turns out that the simple scheme in which each person randomizes the report solely based on their type is optimal. I suggest the authors explicitly state this in the text.

A recent development has led me to reevaluate my recommendation. With the publication of the FOCS 2013 accepted papers list, I now feel that the paper is an extension of an existing paper, particularly the results in Section 3. While I still recommend acceptance, my endorsement is not as strong as it was initially.

In summary, the paper provides a thorough analysis of the local privacy framework using min-max rate bounds, gives lower bounds on the rate, and demonstrates that the upper bound is met by randomized response. This is a nice result, and with some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, it has the potential to be even more impactful.
