Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

The paper explores the concept of local privacy, where each user outputs an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and reports it to the data curator. The authors analyze this setting using the framework of min-max expected error, where the adversary picks a distribution over inputs and the goal is to minimize the distance between an estimation derived from the original data and a similar estimation derived from the reported signals.

The authors provide a thorough summary of the main ideas and relate them to previous work on local privacy and minimax bounds. They clearly explain the concept of local privacy and its connection to differential privacy, as well as the min-max framework used to analyze the problem. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the technical details.

The authors' main contribution is the derivation of sharp lower bounds for the min-max rate for l2 distance estimations and users drawn from a discreet distribution over d types. They show that the simple technique of randomized response achieves these bounds, making it an optimal solution. The authors also analyze the problem of density estimations and provide a lower bound on the min-max rate, which is met by a perturbation scheme in which each person perturbs only their own type.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the technical details. However, some of the notation could be simplified, and the authors could have provided more intuition behind the proofs. Additionally, it would have been helpful to see a comparison of the results with other lower bounds in differential privacy, as well as a discussion on the implications of the results for other settings.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of local privacy and minimax bounds. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and derive sharp lower bounds for the min-max rate. The results have important implications for the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures and provide a foundation for future research in this area.
