## Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

This paper investigates the fundamental limits of local privacy, a setting where each user contributes a differentially private signal based on their own data and the signals of others, reporting to a central curator. The authors employ a min-max expected error framework – where an adversary designs an input distribution and a mechanism designer selects a differentially private scheme to minimize the distance between estimations derived from the original and reported data – to analyze this problem. They derive a lower bound for the min-max rate of l₂ distance estimation when users are drawn from a discrete distribution over *d* types, demonstrating that the simple randomized response mechanism achieves this bound, and is therefore optimal. The paper extends this analysis to the problem of density estimation, again finding that a perturbation scheme where each user perturbs only their own type achieves the derived lower bound.

This is a valuable and interesting contribution. Establishing the optimality of well-known procedures like randomized response provides strong justification for their continued use and offers a solid theoretical foundation for understanding the limits of local privacy. I believe the NIPS community would find this work compelling and I recommend acceptance.

However, I have several reservations. The exposition surrounding the min-max bounds could be clarified. While technically sound, the explanation currently lacks intuitive accessibility. Furthermore, the paper utilizes a considerable amount of cumbersome notation. Given that all presented bounds apply specifically to the l₂ norm, I suggest phrasing them directly with respect to this norm to improve readability. 

I would also appreciate a discussion regarding the l₁ norm, which is a more restrictive measure of distance, or at least a comparison of the derived bounds to known l₁ lower bounds. Similarly, contextualizing these results within the broader landscape of differential privacy, particularly in relation to bounds developed for the classical (non-local) setting, would strengthen the paper. 

My further concern is that the min-max bounds appear highly specialized to this local privacy setting. Exploring a broader range of scenarios and deriving corresponding min-max lower bounds would enhance the generality of the results. Conversely, a stronger statement explicitly highlighting the surprising fact that, despite the ability of users to condition their randomization on the reports of others, a simple scheme based solely on individual data randomization proves optimal would be beneficial. This key insight deserves greater emphasis in the text.

Finally, since the publication of the FOCS 2013 acceptance list, I now recognize that this work significantly extends results from a paper presented at FOCS. Specifically, the findings in Section 3 overlap with existing work. While this does not invalidate the contribution, it does diminish its novelty and therefore weakens my recommendation for acceptance, though I still believe the paper is worthy of inclusion in the NIPS proceedings.

In summary, the paper provides a significant analysis of local privacy using min-max rate bounds, establishing fundamental limits and demonstrating the optimality of randomized response. While the presentation could be refined and the connection to related work better articulated, the core results are compelling.
