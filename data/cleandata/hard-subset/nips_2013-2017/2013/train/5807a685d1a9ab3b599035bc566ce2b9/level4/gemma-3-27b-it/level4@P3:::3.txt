## Review of “Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation”

This paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of probability estimation under the constraint of local privacy, a strong privacy notion where each data contributor randomizes their report independently. The authors establish sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial estimation) and continuous (density estimation) settings, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. A key result is the optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism for survey sampling under local privacy.

The paper is a valuable contribution to the growing body of work on privacy-preserving statistical inference. It rigorously connects differential privacy with classical minimax theory, providing a formal understanding of the price of privacy in terms of convergence rates. The results are well-motivated, clearly presented, and demonstrate a strong technical understanding of the field. The NIPS community would benefit from this work, and I recommend acceptance.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper’s analysis is grounded in solid mathematical principles, utilizing minimax theory and differential privacy in a coherent framework.
*   **Sharp Results:** Establishing *sharp* minimax rates is a significant achievement, providing tight bounds on achievable performance.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Demonstrating the optimality of Warner’s randomized response is a particularly impactful result, linking theoretical work to a widely-used technique.
*   **Clear Presentation (Overall):** The core ideas and results are clearly articulated, although some sections could benefit from simplification (see weaknesses below).
*   **Well-Defined Problem:** The paper clearly focuses on the challenging setting of *local* privacy, which is a crucial distinction from global privacy models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Burden:** The paper employs a considerable amount of notation, which can make it challenging to follow the technical details. While much of the notation is standard, streamlining it where possible (e.g., consistently using the ℓ₂ norm throughout) would improve readability.
*   **Complexity of Minimax Introduction:** The initial explanation of the minimax framework could be made more accessible to a broader audience. Expanding on the intuition behind this approach, and potentially providing a simplified example, would be helpful.
*   **Limited Discussion of Alternative Norms:** While the focus on ℓ₂ distance is justifiable, a brief discussion of the implications for other norms, such as ℓ₁ distance (which is often more relevant in practical applications), would strengthen the analysis.  Relating the results to existing lower bounds in the classical (non-local) differential privacy setting would also be beneficial.
*   **Scope of Minimax Bounds:** The paper focuses on minimax bounds tailored to the local privacy setting. Briefly acknowledging the broader landscape of minimax bounds and discussing the limitations of applying these specific bounds to other scenarios would provide valuable context.
*   **Explicit Statement of Optimality:** The authors correctly demonstrate that the simple randomized response scheme is optimal. However, this key insight is not explicitly highlighted in the main text.  Stating this more directly would emphasize the significance of the result.
*   **Related Work – FOCS 2013 Paper:** As noted by the authors, related work was published at FOCS 2013, extending prior work. While the paper still represents a significant contribution (particularly in its broader analysis and density estimation results), this connection should be more explicitly discussed in the introduction and related work sections.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in privacy-preserving statistics: quantifying the trade-off between privacy and accuracy.
*   The results are technically sound and rigorously proven, establishing sharp minimax rates.
*   The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response is a significant practical contribution.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a clear exposition of a complex topic.
*   The work pushes the boundaries of our understanding of statistical inference under privacy constraints.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The notational complexity and initial introduction to minimax theory could hinder accessibility for some readers.
*   The limited discussion of alternative norms and broader minimax bounds represents a minor shortcoming.
*   The connection to the FOCS 2013 paper weakens the novelty somewhat, but does not negate the overall value of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses noted above, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The rigorous analysis, sharp results, and practical implications make it a worthy addition to NIPS 2013. The benefits of publishing this work significantly outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend acceptance.
