

This paper establishes minimax bounds for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, addressing two key problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. The authors derive sharp minimax rates, revealing that for multinomial estimation, local privacy reduces the effective sample size quadratically in the privacy parameter α. Since α is typically small, this reduction does not significantly degrade performance compared to the non-private case. For density estimation, the authors demonstrate that the optimal non-private rate becomes unattainable under local privacy, with the minimax rate exhibiting a higher order of convergence.

The results are significant and well-motivated. The proofs appear technically sound, and to the best of my knowledge, the density estimation problem under local privacy has not been previously analyzed in this minimax framework. The paper is clearly written and makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistical estimation.

However, a critical omission concerns the relationship to prior work on differential privacy. The multinomial estimation problem studied here is a special case of linear counting queries, for which Hardt and Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012) established worst-case lower bounds under standard differential privacy. The error metric (L₂) and query structure align precisely with those works. Since local differential privacy imposes a stronger constraint than standard differential privacy, their lower bounds also apply to the local setting. The authors should clarify whether their bound for local privacy improves upon the existing differential privacy lower bounds or merely matches them. This connection is substantial and requires explicit discussion.

Regarding the density estimation result, the comparison between private and non-private rates requires clarification. The authors state that the local privacy lower bound exceeds the non-private bound (Eq. 13), but Eq. (13) is derived specifically for densities expandable in a trigonometric basis. The non-private lower bound must be established for the *same* trigonometric basis framework to enable a valid comparison. The paper should explicitly confirm whether the non-private bound in Eq. (13) applies to this basis or holds generally for Sobolev spaces (as defined in Definition 1). If the former, the comparison is valid; if the latter, the private bound should be compared against the non-private bound for the trigonometric basis case.

Additionally, the rebuttal mentions Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also studies mean square error under differential privacy. This work is highly relevant and should be cited in the main text to contextualize the noise complexity results. The authors must integrate this reference and explicitly address its relationship to their findings.

In summary, the paper presents novel and important results for minimax estimation under local privacy. The core contributions are strong, but the work would be significantly strengthened by: (1) a thorough discussion of the connection to Hardt & Talwar (2010), De (2012), and Nikolov et al. (2013), clarifying whether the bounds improve upon prior differential privacy results; and (2) explicit verification that the non-private lower bound (Eq. 13) applies to the trigonometric basis used in the private analysis. Addressing these points will solidify the paper's position in the literature and enhance its impact. The results are otherwise compelling and well-executed.
