This paper studies minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint that the estimation must preserve privacy of the individual data. The authors consider a privacy definition called local privacy. They study two probability estimation problems, multinomial estimation and density estimation. In both problems, the authors show sharp minimax rates of convergence. They demonstrate that, for the discrete multinomial estimation problem, local privacy causes a reduction in the effective sample size quadratic in the privacy parameter alpha. Since alpha can often be seen as a small constant, the effective sample size is of the same order as the non-private case. For the density estimation problem, the authors demonstrate that the optimal rate for the non-private setting is no longer attainable if local privacy must be preserved.

The results are very interesting and contribute to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The paper is well-written, and the proofs appear to be correct. To the best of my knowledge, the minimax bound for density estimation under a privacy constraint has not been considered before.

However, I do have some comments on the related work. The minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely related to previous works on the noise complexity for differential privacy. In particular, two papers consider highly relevant problems: Hardt and Talwar, "On the geometry of differential privacy," STOC, 2010; and De, "Lower bounds in differential privacy," TCC, 2012. These two papers study worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under the constraint of differential privacy. The probability estimation problem considered in this paper is actually a special case of the linear counting query studied in those two papers. Also, the measures used for the error are the same L2 metric. The only difference is that this paper considers local differential privacy, while Hardt and Talwar and De consider differential privacy. Local privacy posts stronger constraints than differential privacy, and therefore lower bounds for differential privacy are also lower bounds for local privacy. I am wondering if the bound for local privacy given in this paper improves over previous bounds for differential privacy.

Additionally, for the density estimation problem, the statement of the result is a bit confusing. The authors state that the lower bound in the local privacy setting is higher than the non-private setting. However, the minimax bound in this paper is for the special case that the density can be expanded with a trigonometric basis. I would like to know if the lower bound for the non-private setting (equation 13) holds for the general Sobolev space as given in Definition 1 or for the special case of the trigonometric basis. It is a fair comparison only if the non-private lower bound holds for the trigonometric basis.

In the rebuttal, the authors partially clarify the relation to previous worst-case lower bounds. However, I would like to suggest that the authors add their explanations and missing references to the paper. In particular, the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, "The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases" (STOC 2013), also considers Mean Square Error.

Overall, I believe that this paper provides interesting results and contributes to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. However, I think that the authors should clarify the relation to previous works on differential privacy and provide more context for the results.
