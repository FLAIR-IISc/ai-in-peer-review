

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints, establishing sharp minimax convergence rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density estimation) settings. The authors bridge classical statistical decision theory with modern privacy frameworks, demonstrating how differential privacy constraints fundamentally alter the statistical efficiency of estimation procedures. The work builds upon Warner's randomized response framework [23], showing it achieves optimal sample complexity for multinomial estimation, and extends these insights to nonparametric density estimation under local privacy.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it characterizes the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency, demonstrating that for d-dimensional multinomial estimation, the effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d when providing α-local differential privacy. This resolves a long-standing question about the optimality of Warner's approach, showing it remains optimal even after nearly 50 years. Second, for density estimation with β-smooth densities, the paper establishes a novel convergence rate of (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2), which differs from the classical n⁻²β/(2β+1) rate. The authors complement their lower bounds with matching upper bounds using efficiently implementable histogram and orthogonal series estimators.

The paper excels in technical quality. The minimax framework is carefully developed, with precise definitions of local privacy and clear connections to differential privacy. The proofs of the main theorems are rigorous, and the authors provide concrete, computationally efficient estimators that achieve the derived bounds. The paper effectively addresses the quality criterion by thoroughly evaluating both strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

The clarity of the presentation is generally excellent. The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and logical progression from problem formulation to results. The theoretical development is accessible to readers familiar with statistical learning theory, and the technical arguments are well-motivated. The authors successfully balance mathematical rigor with readability, making the paper suitable for the NIPS audience.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by precisely quantifying the statistical cost of local privacy. While the concept of local privacy was introduced by Evfimievski et al. [13], this paper provides the first sharp minimax analysis of statistical estimation under this constraint, filling an important gap in the literature. The connection between classical survey sampling (Warner [23]) and modern privacy frameworks represents a particularly insightful contribution.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a fundamental understanding of the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off, which is critical for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The results offer practical guidance for practitioners, showing that randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation and providing explicit rates for density estimation under privacy constraints. The paper advances the state of the art by establishing sharp rates that were previously unknown.

The paper has several minor weaknesses that could be improved. First, the relationship between local privacy and differential privacy needs more precise clarification. The paper occasionally conflates these concepts, as seen in the statement on page 4: "the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α." The authors should consistently use "local privacy" when referring to their model, as it differs from standard differential privacy (which typically refers to the centralized model). This distinction is crucial for readers unfamiliar with the local privacy framework.

Second, while the paper references relevant work, the connection to Lei's [19] private M-estimators could be strengthened. The authors should explicitly discuss how their orthogonal series estimator differs from Lei's approach, particularly regarding the privacy guarantees and convergence rates. The claim that density estimators based on Laplace perturbation are suboptimal (page 16) could be better substantiated with a direct comparison to Lei's work.

Third, the restriction to α ∈ [0,1] in Theorem 1 is not fully justified. Given that in practice α is often constrained to small values (e.g., α ≤ 1) for meaningful privacy guarantees, this restriction is reasonable, but the authors should clarify why the bound doesn't hold for larger α and whether this limitation affects practical applications.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the choice of loss functions (ρ). While the authors consider ℓ₁ and ℓ₂ errors for multinomial estimation, a brief exploration of how different utility metrics (e.g., KL divergence) might affect the results would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes significant progress in understanding the statistical implications of local privacy. The paper's technical quality is excellent, the results are novel and important, and the significance extends to both theoretical and applied statistics. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's core contributions. The authors have made a valuable addition to the literature on privacy-preserving statistical inference that should be of interest to both the privacy and machine learning communities. I recommend acceptance.
