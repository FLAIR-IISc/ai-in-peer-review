Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

This paper explores the concept of local privacy, where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of other users, and then reports this type to the data curator. The authors employ the min-max expected error framework to analyze this setting, where the adversary selects a distribution over inputs and the goal is to minimize the distance between an estimation derived from the original data and a similar estimation derived from the reported signals. 

The authors establish a lower bound for the min-max rate for l_2 distance estimations when users are drawn from a discrete distribution over d types. They demonstrate that the simple technique of randomized response achieves this bound, making it optimal. Furthermore, they investigate the problem of density estimations and show that the lower bound on the min-max rate is met by a perturbation scheme where each individual perturbs only their own type.

The paper is well-written and significant, as it proves that certain classic procedures are optimal since they meet specific lower bounds. The NIPS community will likely find it interesting, and I recommend acceptance.

However, I have some reservations. Firstly, the explanation of min-max bounds could be simplified. Additionally, the paper contains cumbersome notations, particularly since all bounds apply to the l_2 norm. It would be beneficial to phrase the bounds with respect to this norm. I would also appreciate a discussion on the l_1 norm, which is more restrictive, or a comparison of the given bounds to l_1 norms and other lower bounds in differential privacy, albeit in a classical, non-local setting. 

Moreover, the min-max bounds discussed seem tailored to this local-privacy setting. I wish the authors had considered a broader range of settings and provided min-max lower bounds for them as well. Nevertheless, I appreciate that the local setting allows users to randomize the type they report based on the remaining n-1 users, and it turns out that the simple scheme where each person randomizes the report solely based on their type is optimal. I suggest the authors explicitly state this in the text.

A recent development has led me to reassess my recommendation. Given that the list of FOCS 2013 accepted papers has been published, I now perceive this paper as an extension of an existing paper, particularly the results in Section 3. Although I still recommend acceptance, my endorsement is not as strong as it was initially.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the local privacy framework using min-max rate bounds, establishes lower bounds on the rate, and demonstrates that the upper bound is met by randomized response, yielding a nice result.
