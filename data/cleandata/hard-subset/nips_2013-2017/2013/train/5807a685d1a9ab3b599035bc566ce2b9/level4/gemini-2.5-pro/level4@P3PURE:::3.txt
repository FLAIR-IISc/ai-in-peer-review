**Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations"**

This paper studies statistical estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy. In this model, each of `n` users provides an `alpha`-differentially private signal to a data curator, where this signal can, in general, be a function of the user's own private type as well as the types of the other `n-1` users. The authors analyze this problem through the lens of minimax risk. In this framework, an adversary chooses a distribution over user types, and the goal is to design a local `alpha`-differentially private mechanism that minimizes the worst-case expected error between an estimate computed from the private signals and the true parameter of the underlying data.

The paper's main contribution is a lower bound on the minimax rate for estimating discrete probability distributions over `d` types under `l_2` loss. The authors demonstrate that this bound is tight by showing that it is matched by the well-known randomized response mechanism, thereby proving its optimality in this setting. A similar result is presented for the problem of density estimation, where a local perturbation scheme is again shown to be optimal.

This is a nice and important paper that provides a valuable theoretical contribution. By establishing sharp lower bounds, it formally proves that a classic and simple procedure like randomized response is optimal, which is the best result one can hope for. The NIPS community should find these results interesting, and I recommend the paper for acceptance.

I have a few reservations and suggestions for improvement:

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The explanation of the minimax framework could be simplified for broader accessibility. Furthermore, the notation is often cumbersome. Since all bounds presented apply to the `l_2` norm, the authors could consider phrasing the main results and exposition specifically with respect to this norm to improve clarity.

2.  **Scope and Relation to Prior Work:** The analysis is focused entirely on the `l_2` norm. The paper could be strengthened by including a discussion of the more restrictive `l_1` norm, or by relating the derived `l_2` bounds to potential `l_1` bounds. Additionally, a discussion connecting these results to other lower bounds in the differential privacy literature, even those in the traditional (non-local) setting, would help contextualize the contribution.

3.  **Generality of Minimax Bounds:** The minimax bounds appear to be specifically "tailored" for the local-privacy setting. It would be beneficial if the authors discussed whether their techniques could be extended to derive minimax lower bounds for a broader class of privacy settings.

4.  **Highlighting a Key Insight:** A powerful and perhaps surprising finding is that even though the local privacy model allows each user's report to depend on the data of all `n-1` other users, the optimal scheme involves each user randomizing their report based solely on their own private type. This is a significant result that simplifies the design of optimal mechanisms, and the authors should state it more explicitly in the text.

Finally, it should be noted that a related paper has been accepted to FOCS 2013. This context makes the current submission, particularly the results in Section 3, appear more as an extension of existing work. While this tempers my enthusiasm slightly by reducing the perceived novelty, the paper still stands as a valuable contribution on its own merits.

In summary, the paper successfully analyzes local privacy using minimax bounds, derives lower bounds on the error rate, and shows these bounds are met by randomized response. This is a nice result worthy of publication.
