SUMMARY: This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript. It uses a Fano/Le Cam–style argument to derive lower bounds on estimation algorithms that operate on privatized data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. As a corollary, randomized response is shown to be optimal in a certain sense.

As a caveat, I did not read the supplementary material.

Pros and cons:
- The results characterize fundamental limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy.
- Some of the commentary around the results is imprecise and may confuse readers (see below).
- Connections to existing results on differential privacy appear to be missing.

Additional comments:
- The restriction to local privacy is central to the results but makes the privacy model quite different from the standard (central) differential privacy model—a distinction many readers may not appreciate. This confusion is compounded by statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4: “Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α …” The authors should avoid such broad (and possibly incorrect) statements when describing their results, or else explicitly qualify them as applying to the local privacy setting only.
- Is the restriction on α in Theorem 1 necessary? Empirically, α ≈ 1 is often the most one can hope for in certain learning problems under differential privacy, so it is unclear what the bound implies in this practically relevant regime.
- Some discussion of possible choices of ρ would be helpful, so readers can see how different utility measures are captured by the analysis.
- How does the proposed density estimator compare to Lei’s M-estimation procedure? This related work is not cited, but seems directly relevant; a comparison (both conceptual and, if possible, empirical) would strengthen the paper.
- There are many approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. While randomized response attains the optimal rate of convergence, how do other algorithms perform in practice and theory relative to this benchmark?

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:
- The distinction between population quantities and sample-based statistics should be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (cf. the response to Reviewer 9).
- A more comprehensive comparison to related work, including Lei and the additional papers raised by another reviewer, is important for context.
- After a closer look at [10], which has now appeared, I view the contribution of this paper more narrowly. Although the venues and audiences differ, the contributions here are twofold: (i) a careful exposition of the local privacy model and (ii) new bounds for density estimation. The latter appear to be new, whereas the former is essentially contained in [10].
