Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

The paper explores local privacy, a setting where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of the other n-1 users, and reports this type to the data curator. The authors analyze this setting using the framework of min-max expected error, where the adversary selects a distribution over inputs (types for the n users) and the goal is to design an α-DP local scheme that minimizes the distance between an estimation derived from the original data and a similar estimation derived from the reported signals. Typically, these estimations involve sums, aggregations, or averages.

The authors derive a lower bound for the min-max rate for l2 distance estimations and users drawn from a discrete distribution over d types. They demonstrate that the simple technique of randomized response achieves this bound, establishing its optimality. Furthermore, the authors investigate the problem of density estimations, where the lower bound on the min-max rate is met by a perturbation scheme in which each person perturbs only their own type.

The paper is well-written and important, as it shows that a classic procedure is optimal since it meets certain lower bounds. The NIPS community is likely to find it interesting, and I therefore recommend acceptance.

However, I have a few reservations. Firstly, the explanation of min-max bounds could be simplified. Additionally, the paper employs numerous cumbersome notations; in particular, since all bounds apply to the l2 norm, it would be more intuitive to phrase the bounds with respect to this norm. Secondly, I would appreciate a comment on the l1 norm, which is more restrictive, or a comparison of the given bounds to l1 norms, as well as other lower bounds in differential privacy (although these typically apply to classical, non-local settings). Lastly, the min-max bounds discussed seem tailored to this local-privacy setting. I wish the authors had considered a broader range of settings and provided min-max lower bounds for those as well. On a positive note, I appreciate that the local setting allows users to randomize the type they report based on the remaining n-1 users, yet it turns out that the simple scheme where each person randomizes the report solely based on their type is optimal. I suggest the authors explicitly state this in the text.

A new reservation has arisen since the list of FOCS 2013 accepted papers has been published. Given this, I now perceive the paper as an extension of an existing paper, particularly the results in Section 3. Although I still recommend acceptance, my endorsement is not as strong as it was initially.

The paper provides a valuable analysis of the local privacy framework using min-max rate bounds, offering lower bounds on the rate and demonstrating that the upper bound is met by randomized response. This is a nice result.
