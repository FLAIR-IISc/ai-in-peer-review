Summary: This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript, and it sets a lower bound through a Fano/LeCam-style argument. This bound applies to algorithms that operate on private data when the data holder does not trust the algorithm. Consequently, in some cases, randomized response is proven to be an optimal strategy.

A limitation of this review is that I did not see the additional materials.

Pros and Cons:
- The results highlight the limitations of learning from scattered data to ensure confidentiality.
- There is some ambiguity in the interpretation of the results that may confuse a layperson (see below).
- There seems to be a lack of connection with existing results related to differential privacy.

Additional Comments:
- The local privacy constraint, which is important for the results, makes the privacy model quite different from the differential privacy model, which many readers may not realize. This confusion may be further exacerbated by statements given below page 4: "So, for a sufficiently large sample size for n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha\..." The authors should avoid making such overly broad (and possibly incorrect) statements when describing their results.
- Is the alpha constraint in Theorem 1 necessary? In particular, experimental results show that $\alpha \approx 1$ is most likely to be expected for some learning problems (under differential privacy), so it is not clear what this bound tells us in this case.
- Some comments about the possible choice of ρ would be good, so that readers can see how different utility measures can be captured by analysis.
- How does this density estimator compare to Leigh's M-estimation method? This paper is not cited, but I think the authors should be aware of it.
- There are many other histogram estimation methods for scattered data. How do these other algorithms fare where randomized response achieves the best rate of convergence?

Post-refutation additions:
* I think the difference between population versus sample statistics needs to be explained more clearly at the beginning of the paper (see the response to Rev.9).
* Comparison with relevant work (raised by Leigh and another reviewer) is important.
* A closer inspection of [10], which has now been published, forces me to explain the additional contribution of this paper more narrowly. Although the location (and therefore the audience) of this and [10] are different, the contribution of this paper is twofold: a careful explanation of the local privacy model and the setting of the density estimation bound. The latter is new, but the former is essentially included in [10].
