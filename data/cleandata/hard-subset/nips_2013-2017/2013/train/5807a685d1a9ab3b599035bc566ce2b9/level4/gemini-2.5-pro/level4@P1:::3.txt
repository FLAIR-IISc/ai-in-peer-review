This paper provides a rigorous analysis of statistical estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy. The authors connect the formalisms of minimax decision theory and differential privacy to characterize the fundamental trade-offs between the privacy level (α) and statistical convergence rates. The analysis focuses on two core problems: estimating discrete (multinomial) probability distributions and estimating continuous probability densities.

For multinomial estimation, the paper establishes sharp minimax lower bounds on the L1 and L2 estimation error. A key finding is that providing α-local privacy effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to approximately `nα²/d`, where `d` is the number of categories. Importantly, the authors show that this lower bound is achieved by Warner's classic randomized response mechanism, proving that this simple, 50-year-old technique is, in fact, minimax optimal.

For the more complex problem of nonparametric density estimation, the paper demonstrates that local privacy has a more profound impact. Instead of merely reducing the effective sample size, it changes the polynomial exponent in the rate of convergence. For densities with β derivatives, the classical minimax rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` is slowed to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. The authors present practical estimators (based on histograms and orthogonal series) that achieve these new, slower rates, thereby proving the sharpness of their lower bounds.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Quality:** This is a high-quality and important paper that builds a solid theoretical bridge between statistical decision theory and the modern literature on differential privacy. By providing sharp, matching upper and lower bounds, it rigorously quantifies the "price of privacy" for fundamental estimation tasks.
*   **Novelty and Impact:** The result that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal is a beautiful and satisfying finding. It provides a strong theoretical justification for a classic and widely understood mechanism. The analysis of density estimation is also novel and reveals a deeper consequence of local privacy, showing that it can fundamentally alter the convergence rate of a nonparametric problem. These results are likely to be influential and built upon by researchers in both privacy and statistics.
*   **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper clearly articulates its goals and contributions, and the technical results appear sound and well-supported.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Presentation:** While the technical content is strong, the exposition could be improved. The introduction to the minimax framework in Section 2 is quite dense and relies on specialized notation (e.g., `Φ ◦ ρ`). The presentation might be made more accessible to a broader NIPS audience by simplifying the notation where possible or providing more intuition.

2.  **Explicit Statement of a Key Result:** A powerful and non-obvious conclusion from this work is that simple, non-interactive mechanisms (where each user randomizes their data independently) are optimal. The general definition of local privacy does not preclude more complex, interactive schemes. The authors should consider highlighting this important implication more explicitly in the text, as it is a significant practical and theoretical insight.

3.  **Contextualization and Scope:**
    *   A more detailed discussion connecting these lower bounds to existing bounds in the standard (non-local) differential privacy literature would be valuable for contextualizing the results.
    *   The theoretical framework is developed specifically for local privacy. It would be interesting for the authors to comment on the potential for extending this minimax analysis to other privacy models, such as the central (curator) model.

4.  **Originality Concern:** It has come to my attention that a related paper by the same authors has been accepted at FOCS 2013, which appears to have significant overlap with the results presented here for multinomial estimation (Section 3). While the analysis of density estimation is a substantial and distinct contribution, this overlap somewhat diminishes the novelty of the first half of the paper. It would be helpful if the authors could clarify the precise relationship and unique contributions of this manuscript relative to their FOCS publication.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this paper makes a strong and valuable contribution by providing a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of local privacy. The results for density estimation are particularly novel and significant. I recommend acceptance. My enthusiasm is slightly tempered by the apparent overlap with the authors' recent FOCS paper, which reduces the novelty of a portion of the work presented. Nevertheless, the paper as a whole is an excellent fit for the NIPS community.
