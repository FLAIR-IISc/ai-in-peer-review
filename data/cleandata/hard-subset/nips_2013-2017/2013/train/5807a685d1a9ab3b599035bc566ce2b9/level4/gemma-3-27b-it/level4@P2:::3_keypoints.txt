- Studies estimation of discrete and continuous probability distributions under local privacy constraints, keeping data private even from the statistician.
- Provides sharp minimax rates of convergence for locally private estimation, characterizing privacy–accuracy trade-offs.
- Identifies optimal points along the privacy–statistical efficiency continuum.
- Proves Warner’s randomized response is optimal for private survey sampling.
- Integrates differential privacy, minimax theory, and classical privacy concepts, yielding a novel combination of techniques.
- Technically sound and practically relevant; recommended for acceptance by the reviewer.
- Presentation is dense and could be simplified, especially the initial minimax explanations.
- Excessive and sometimes cumbersome notation reduces readability.
- Many bounds are given for the l2 norm; consistent l2 framing would improve clarity.
- Paper should discuss l1 results or compare to existing centralized differential privacy lower bounds.
- Should briefly acknowledge broader local privacy settings and potential minimax bounds for them.
- The independence-optimality result (individuals randomizing independently is optimal) should be explicitly highlighted.
- Novelty concern: Section 3 appears related to recently published FOCS 2013 results, slightly reducing impact.
- Summary of pros: strong theory, practical optimality result, novel interdisciplinary connections, clear trade-off analysis.
- Summary of cons: complex presentation, limited scope without broader context, and somewhat incremental novelty due to related FOCS work.
