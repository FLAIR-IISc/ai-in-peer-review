## Review - Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

This paper investigates local privacy, a scenario where each user provides an α-differentially private signal based on their own data and the signals of others, reporting this to a data curator. The authors employ a min-max expected error framework – an adversarial approach where a distribution over inputs is chosen to maximize error, and a local privacy mechanism is selected to minimize it – to analyze the performance of estimators derived from the reported signals compared to those from the original data (specifically, sums, averages, and aggregations). They establish a lower bound on the min-max rate for l₂ distance estimation with users drawn from a discrete distribution over *d* types, demonstrating that the randomized response technique achieves this bound, and is therefore optimal. The analysis extends to density estimation, where a perturbation scheme based on individual type perturbation also meets the established lower bound.

This is a valuable and significant contribution; the paper demonstrates the optimality of established techniques by showing their alignment with fundamental lower bounds. I believe the NIPS community would find this work of interest and I recommend acceptance.

However, I do have several points for consideration. Regarding presentation, the explanation of min-max bounds could benefit from greater clarity and simplification. The paper also utilizes a considerable amount of complex notation; given that all bounds are presented for the l₂ norm, expressing them directly in terms of this norm would improve readability. I would also appreciate a discussion of the l₁ norm – which represents a more stringent privacy constraint – and a comparison of the derived bounds to those established in the broader differential privacy literature (acknowledging the differences between local and central privacy settings). Furthermore, the min-max bounds appear specifically tailored to this local privacy setting. Exploring a wider range of settings and providing corresponding min-max lower bounds would strengthen the generality of the results. I particularly appreciate the finding that, despite the ability of users to condition their randomization on the reports of others, a simple scheme based solely on individual type randomization proves optimal; this observation should be explicitly highlighted in the text.

A further concern has arisen with the recent publication of accepted papers from FOCS 2013. The work presented here now appears as an extension of existing results, particularly those in Section 3. While I still recommend acceptance, my support is somewhat diminished in light of this overlap. 

In summary, the paper provides a compelling analysis of local privacy through the lens of min-max rate bounds, establishing lower bounds and demonstrating that randomized response achieves optimal performance. It is a worthwhile contribution to the field.
