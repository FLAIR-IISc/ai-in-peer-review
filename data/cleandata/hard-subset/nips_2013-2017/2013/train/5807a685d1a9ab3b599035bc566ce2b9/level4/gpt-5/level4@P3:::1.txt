Summary
This paper studies probability estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each datum is privatized by the data holder before any analysis. The authors combine classical minimax decision theory with information-theoretic tools to derive sharp lower bounds on estimation risk under LDP, and they match these with achievable algorithms.

For multinomial estimation, the paper shows that when α-LDP is enforced, the effective sample size degrades from n to roughly nα^2/d, yielding minimax risk of order min{1, d/(nα^2)} in squared error and min{1, d/√(nα^2)} in ℓ1 error (up to constants). A notable corollary is that Warner’s randomized response achieves the optimal rate under LDP.

For nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ, the classical nonprivate minimax rate n−2β/(2β+1) is shown to deteriorate under LDP to (nα^2)−2β/(2β+2), i.e., the privacy constraint changes the polynomial exponent. The authors provide matching upper bounds with computationally simple estimators: a privatized histogram is minimax-optimal for β=1 (Lipschitz densities), and a privatized orthogonal series estimator attains the optimal rate for general β≥1 via a carefully designed locally private mechanism.

These results relate to prior work on privacy and inference (Warner; Wasserman & Zhou; Hardt & Talwar; Smith; De; Chaudhuri et al.) but differ in two key respects: (i) the focus on the local (not central) privacy model, and (ii) lower bounds on population estimation risk, not the accuracy of released sample statistics.

Strengths
- Quality: The technical development is solid. The lower bounds build on Fano/Le Cam arguments together with information-contraction bounds tailored to LDP, and the paper supplies matching, efficient estimators. The density estimation results carefully separate approximation and privatization noise and identify optimal tuning (e.g., number of bins/basis terms).
- Originality: The sharp minimax characterization under LDP for multinomials and especially for nonparametric density estimation appears novel. The identification of randomized response as optimal within this formal framework is both conceptually and practically valuable.
- Significance: LDP is increasingly relevant in crowdsourced data collection and distributed analytics. Establishing tight rates and simple optimal mechanisms provides clear benchmarks for practitioners and theorists.
- Clarity (partial): The problem formulation and core results are clearly stated, and the constructions are simple and implementable.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope and terminology: Several statements conflate “differential privacy” with the local model. Early and explicit clarification that all minimax results hold for LDP (not central DP) would avoid confusion. Phrases like “the effect of providing differential privacy” should be qualified as “local differential privacy.”
- Population vs. sample targets: The paper’s bounds concern population parameters θ(P), whereas much DP work studies accuracy of released sample statistics. This distinction should be explained prominently up front, with a brief discussion of implications for practice and how population-level bounds relate to sample-level guarantees.
- Role and range of α: Theorem 1 assumes α∈[0,1]. Is this restriction essential for the constants/rates, or do the conclusions extend (possibly with different constants) to α>1? Since α≈1 is common in applications, more explicit guidance on this regime and a brief discussion of the small- vs. moderate-α behavior would help.
- Metric choices (ρ): The framework allows general losses Φ∘ρ. While the paper presents ℓ2 and ℓ1 for multinomials and L2 risk for densities, a short discussion of other natural metrics (e.g., total variation, Hellinger) and whether rates change under LDP would broaden applicability.
- Related work coverage: Please position the results more carefully with respect to:
  - Lei (Differentially private M-estimators), which operates in the central model and is not locally private, but offers an alternative pathway for density-related inference; contrast rates and mechanisms explicitly.
  - Wasserman & Zhou and Smith/De on private minimax bounds and testing; clarify how your population-risk lower bounds under LDP strengthen or differ from central-DP results.
  - Prior mechanisms for categorical data beyond randomized response (e.g., Laplace perturbation of counts, unary encoding, k-ary randomized response variants). While you note Laplace can be rate-optimal in the multinomial case, a concise comparison (theoretical or empirical) would be useful.
- Exposition: Some commentary could be tightened to avoid overgeneralization. For example, claims about “effective sample size reduction” should be conditioned on the model (multinomial) and on LDP. A brief “what is new vs. prior arXiv version” statement would also help readers understand incremental contributions (especially the density estimation results).

Quality
Technically sound with clear proofs and constructions. Lower and upper bounds match in order, and mechanisms are implementable. It would be helpful to explicitly state whether constants in the rates depend on d or β and to clarify the necessity of α-bounded assumptions.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable for a theoretical audience, but could benefit from:
- An explicit early section contrasting LDP vs. central DP and population vs. sample targets.
- A short subsection outlining admissible ρ and Φ and mapping them to standard notions of utility.

Originality
Strong in the LDP setting, particularly for nonparametric density estimation and the optimal privatization mechanisms. The multinomial result and optimality of randomized response are valuable formalizations of a classical idea, even if anticipated informally.

Significance
High for the LDP literature: the paper establishes definitive benchmarks and simple optimal procedures. Impact on central DP is indirect but still informative through methodological contrasts.

Pros for acceptance
- Sharp, matching minimax upper and lower bounds under LDP for both multinomial and nonparametric density estimation.
- Simple, efficient mechanisms (randomized response; privatized histogram/orthogonal series) achieving the bounds.
- Clear articulation of the privacy–statistical efficiency trade-off.

Cons for acceptance
- Potential confusion between local and central DP in the exposition; needs clearer positioning and terminology.
- Limited discussion of α beyond the small-α regime and of metric choices ρ.
- Related work comparison (e.g., Lei; central-DP estimators; other categorical mechanisms) could be expanded.

Recommendation
A strong theoretical contribution to the LDP literature. With clarifications on the privacy model, target of estimation, the role of α, and a more thorough related work comparison, this paper would make an excellent NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
