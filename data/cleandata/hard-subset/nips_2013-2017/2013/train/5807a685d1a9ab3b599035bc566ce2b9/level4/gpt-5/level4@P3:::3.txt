Paper summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each user privatizes her own datum via a non-interactive channel Q that satisfies α-local DP. The authors develop a minimax decision-theoretic framework for population-level estimation under LDP, derive sharp lower bounds on minimax rates, and provide matching algorithms.

Two problems are treated in depth:
- Multinomial probability estimation: For θ ∈ Δd and n i.i.d. draws, the paper shows that under α-LDP, the effective sample size deteriorates from n to roughly nα2/d. Theorem 1 provides sharp rates for both ℓ2 and ℓ1 risks. A simple variant of Warner’s randomized response attains the optimal rate, establishing its minimax optimality.
- Density estimation on [0,1] over Sobolev classes Fβ: The classical non-private L2 minimax rate n−2β/(2β+1) degrades under LDP to (α2n)−2β/(2β+2). The authors construct mechanisms and estimators—private histograms (β=1) and private orthogonal series (β≥1)—that achieve these rates. They also explain why direct additive noise on samples (e.g., Laplace) cannot attain the optimal rate, connecting to deconvolution lower bounds (Carroll & Hall, 1988).

The work situates itself clearly within the literatures on DP (e.g., Dwork et al., Hardt & Talwar), randomized response (Warner), and nonparametric minimax theory (Yang & Barron, Tsybakov). A key technical ingredient is an information-contraction bound under LDP that upper-bounds mutual information and KL between privatized distributions; the paper restates these bounds (from the authors’ prior work) and uses them with standard testing reductions (Le Cam/Fano) to derive sharp minimax lower bounds. The results emphasize that, unlike much of the DP literature focused on accurate release of sample statistics, the bounds here are on population risk, which is strictly stronger.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds follow from well-established minimax testing techniques combined with privacy-induced information bounds; the upper bounds are achieved by computationally simple mechanisms. The match between upper and lower bounds (including explicit constants in places) is convincing. Assumptions are appropriately stated (non-interactive channels; α in a moderate range; Sobolev smoothness).
- Completeness: For multinomial estimation, both ℓ2 and ℓ1 risks are treated with matching upper bounds (Theorem 1). For density estimation, the mechanisms are tailored to meet LDP and achieve the new rates; the negative result for direct noise addition is well-motivated by the deconvolution literature.
- Care in evaluation: Proof sketches are given with references to a longer version for details. The paper is primarily theoretical, which is appropriate for the contribution.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear but notation-heavy. The core ideas (privacy induces contraction of information; minimax testing reduction; design of private mechanisms to match lower bounds) could be highlighted earlier with more intuition before the full technical development. The background section is thorough but could be streamlined.

Originality
The integration of LDP with population minimax analysis and the derivation of sharp rates for both multinomial and Sobolev density estimation are significant. The identification and proof of randomized response optimality for multinomial estimation under LDP is particularly elegant. The private orthogonal-series mechanism achieving the optimal nonparametric rate under LDP appears novel. That said, parts of Section 3 (multinomial results and information bounds) overlap with the authors’ prior work (FOCS 2013 / arXiv), and the submission should clearly delineate what is new here (e.g., sharper constants, new proofs, the density estimation results and mechanisms, extensions to ℓ1).

Significance
The results are important for both theory and practice:
- They quantify the privacy–utility trade-off under LDP with sharp rates.
- They establish the optimality of a simple, widely used mechanism (randomized response).
- They show that in nonparametric settings privacy changes the convergence exponent, which is a qualitative departure from the parametric case and guides mechanism design.
These insights are likely to influence subsequent work on private learning and estimation in the local model.

Pros
- Sharp minimax lower bounds with matching, efficient mechanisms.
- Clear identification of the privacy–statistical efficiency trade-off; simple interpretation as effective sample size reduction in the multinomial case.
- Novel and practically relevant density estimation mechanisms that are rate-optimal under LDP; informative contrast with naive additive-noise schemes.
- Rigorous population-risk focus and solid connections to information theory and nonparametric statistics.

Cons and suggestions
- Overlap with prior work: Please clarify precisely what is new relative to the FOCS 2013 paper and arXiv version, especially for Section 3. If Section 4 (density estimation rates and mechanisms) is the primary new contribution, state this prominently.
- Scope: The analysis is restricted to non-interactive (memoryless) local mechanisms. A brief discussion of whether interaction can improve rates (and if not, why) would strengthen the positioning.
- Exposition: Reduce notation where possible and provide more intuition for the information-contraction bounds and their implications. A short, high-level overview of the proof strategy at the start of each main section would help readability.
- Practical guidance: A brief discussion of constants and practical ranges of α, or small simulations to illustrate finite-sample behavior, would make the work more accessible to practitioners (optional for this theory-focused submission).

Recommendation
Accept. The paper provides clear, technically strong, and timely advances in understanding statistical estimation under local differential privacy, with sharp rates and optimal mechanisms. Clarifying novelty relative to the FOCS 2013 version and slightly improving exposition would further strengthen the submission.
