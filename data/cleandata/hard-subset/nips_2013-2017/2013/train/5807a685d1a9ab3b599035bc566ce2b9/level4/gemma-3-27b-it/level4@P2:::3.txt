This paper investigates the problem of estimating probability distributions – both discrete and continuous – under the constraint of local privacy, where data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and accuracy, and identifying optimal points along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum. A key result establishes that Warner’s randomized response mechanism is optimal for survey sampling while preserving respondent privacy.

The paper is a valuable contribution, bringing together differential privacy, minimax theory, and classical privacy concepts. The NIPS community would likely find the results and connections between different areas of research to be of interest, and I recommend acceptance. The work is technically sound and addresses an important problem. 

However, the presentation could be improved for clarity and impact. The initial explanation of minimax bounds is somewhat dense and could benefit from simplification. The paper employs a substantial amount of notation, some of which feels unnecessarily cumbersome. Given that many of the bounds are presented specifically for the l2 norm, framing the results consistently with respect to this norm would enhance readability. It would also strengthen the paper to include a discussion of l1 norm results, or at least a comparison to existing lower bounds in (centralized) differential privacy.

The paper focuses on minimax bounds tailored to this specific local privacy setting. While this is a valid approach, briefly discussing broader settings and potential minimax bounds for those scenarios could add further context. I particularly appreciated the observation that, despite the ability of users to randomize their reports based on the data of others, the simple scheme where each individual randomizes independently based on their own type is optimal; this finding should be explicitly highlighted in the text. 

A recent discovery – the publication of accepted papers from FOCS 2013 – raises a concern regarding novelty. The results in Section 3 appear to be an extension of existing work presented there. While this doesn’t invalidate the contribution, it does slightly reduce its impact. Nevertheless, I still recommend acceptance, though with somewhat reduced enthusiasm.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis based on minimax theory and differential privacy.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response is a significant and practically relevant finding.
*   **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The work effectively integrates concepts from several different fields.
*   **Clear Trade-off Analysis:** The paper clearly articulates the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

**Arguments for Rejection (or Requesting Revision):**

*   **Complexity of Presentation:** Some sections are difficult to follow due to dense explanations and excessive notation.
*   **Limited Scope:** While the focus on local privacy is valuable, briefly acknowledging broader settings would be beneficial.
*   **Incremental Novelty:** The connection to recent FOCS work slightly diminishes the originality of the results in Section 3.
