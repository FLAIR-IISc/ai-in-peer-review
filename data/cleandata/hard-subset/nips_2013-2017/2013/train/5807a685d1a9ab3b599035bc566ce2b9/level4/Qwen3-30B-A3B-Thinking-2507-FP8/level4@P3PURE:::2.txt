

This paper establishes minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, addressing both multinomial estimation and density estimation. For multinomial estimation, the authors derive sharp minimax rates, showing that local privacy reduces the effective sample size quadratically in the privacy parameter α. Since α is typically small, the effective sample size remains comparable to the non-private case. For density estimation, the authors demonstrate that the optimal non-private rate is unattainable under local privacy. The results are significant, the proofs appear correct, and the minimax bound for density estimation under privacy constraints represents a novel contribution to the literature.

The primary concern pertains to the multinomial estimation bound. This result is closely related to prior work on noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy, specifically Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012), which establish worst-case lower bounds for linear queries under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem studied here constitutes a special case of the linear counting queries analyzed in those works (using the same L₂ error metric). While the current paper considers local differential privacy (a stronger constraint than standard differential privacy), the lower bounds for standard differential privacy also apply to local privacy. The authors should clarify whether their bound for local privacy improves upon existing differential privacy bounds or merely inherits them.

Regarding density estimation, the presentation requires clarification. The stated lower bound in the local privacy setting exceeds the non-private bound (equation 13), but it is unclear whether the non-private lower bound (equation 13) holds for the general Sobolev space defined in Definition 1 or only for the specific trigonometric basis case. A fair comparison necessitates that the non-private lower bound applies to the same trigonometric basis framework used in the privacy analysis.

The rebuttal partially addresses the connection to Hardt & Talwar and De, but overlooks Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also studies mean square error under differential privacy. The authors should explicitly discuss this work and its relevance to their results in the final version.

Overall, the paper presents interesting results on privacy-preserving estimation, but the omission of key prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy—particularly the direct relevance of Hardt & Talwar and De to the multinomial problem—weakens the novelty claim. The density estimation comparison also requires refinement to ensure the basis-specific setting is properly aligned. The paper is well-written, but these references and clarifications are essential for a complete contribution.
