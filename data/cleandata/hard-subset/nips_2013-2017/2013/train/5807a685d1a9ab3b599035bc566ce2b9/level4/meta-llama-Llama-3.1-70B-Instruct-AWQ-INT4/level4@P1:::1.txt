This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript, and uses a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on estimation algorithms that operate on private data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. As a corollary, randomized response turns out to be an optimal strategy in some sense.

The paper provides a detailed study of the estimation of probability distributions - discrete and continuous - in a stringent setting in which data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors give sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate, as well as providing tools to allow movement along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum. One of the consequences of their results is that Warner's classical work on randomized response is an optimal way to perform survey sampling while maintaining privacy of the respondents.

The authors bring together the formal treatment of disclosure risk provided by differential privacy with the tools of minimax decision theory to provide a theoretical treatment of probability estimation under privacy constraints. They are able to provide lower bounds on the convergence rates of any estimator, in their case under a restriction to estimators that guarantee privacy. They complement these results with matching upper bounds that are achievable using computationally efficient algorithms.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem of local privacy and its relation to differential privacy. They also provide a clear explanation of the minimax framework used throughout the paper. The technical results are well-presented, and the authors provide a good discussion of the implications of their results.

However, there are some imprecisions in the commentary on the results which could lead a casual reader to become confused. For example, the authors state that "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α..." This statement is overly broad and may be incorrect. The authors should avoid making such statements when describing their results.

Additionally, the restriction to local privacy makes the privacy model quite different from the differential privacy model, a fact which many readers may not appreciate. The authors should provide more commentary on the connections to existing results on differential privacy.

Some other minor points to consider:

* The authors should provide more commentary on the possible choices of ρ, so that readers can see how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.
* The authors should compare their density estimator to the M-estimation procedure of Lei, which is not cited in the paper.
* The authors should discuss how their results compare to other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data.
* The authors should provide more explanation of the distinction between population vs. sample statistics, and how this distinction affects their results.

Overall, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The results are well-presented, and the authors provide a good discussion of the implications of their results. However, there are some minor points that need to be addressed to improve the clarity and accuracy of the paper.
