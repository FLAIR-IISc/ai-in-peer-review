This paper establishes minimax lower bounds for probability estimation under the constraint of local privacy. The authors address two fundamental problems: multinomial parameter estimation and non-parametric density estimation. For both problems, they derive sharp minimax rates of convergence. For the discrete multinomial estimation problem, they demonstrate that local privacy introduces a penalty equivalent to reducing the effective sample size by a factor quadratic in the privacy parameter α. As the authors note, for small α, this results in an effective sample size of the same order as in the non-private setting. For density estimation, they prove that the optimal rate for the non-private setting is no longer attainable under the local privacy constraint.

Overall, the results are novel and significant. The proofs appear to be correct, and to the best of my knowledge, the minimax analysis of density estimation under privacy constraints is a new contribution. The paper is well-written, but its primary weakness is the lack of discussion regarding highly relevant prior work.

My main concern relates to the minimax bound for multinomial estimation, which is closely connected to previous work on the noise complexity of differential privacy. Specifically, the work of Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010), De (TCC 2012), and Nikolov, Talwar, & Zhang (STOC 2013) is highly relevant but not mentioned. These papers establish worst-case lower bounds on the Mean Squared Error for linear queries under the constraint of (central) differential privacy. The multinomial estimation problem analyzed here can be viewed as a special case of the linear counting queries studied in that literature, and the L2 error metric is the same.

The main distinction is this paper's focus on local differential privacy, which is a stronger constraint than central differential privacy. Consequently, lower bounds for central differential privacy apply directly to the local setting. A key question, which the paper should address, is whether the lower bound derived here for local privacy constitutes a quantitative improvement over the existing bounds for the central model. The authors should add the missing references and a thorough discussion of this relationship to the paper.

Regarding the density estimation problem, the comparison between the private and non-private lower bounds requires clarification. The authors state that their lower bound in the local privacy setting is strictly worse than the optimal rate in the non-private setting. However, the minimax bound in this paper is derived for the specific class of densities that can be expanded with a trigonometric basis. For the comparison to be sound, the non-private lower bound cited in eq. (13) must also apply to this same specific class, rather than the more general Sobolev space introduced in Definition 1. The authors should clarify that both bounds are derived for the same function class to ensure a fair comparison.

In summary, this paper presents interesting and well-supported results, providing sharp minimax bounds for probability and density estimation under local privacy. However, its main weakness is the omission of a discussion of highly relevant prior work on lower bounds for differential privacy. Addressing the points raised above, particularly by situating the results within the context of existing literature, would substantially strengthen the paper.
