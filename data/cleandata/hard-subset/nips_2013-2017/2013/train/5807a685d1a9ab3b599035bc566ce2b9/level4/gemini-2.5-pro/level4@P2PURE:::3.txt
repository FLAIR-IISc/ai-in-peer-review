### Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations"

This paper studies the problem of statistical estimation under local differential privacy. In this model, each of the *n* users perturbs their individual data point (or 'type') before sending it to a central curator, ensuring their raw data is never revealed. The model allows for interactive protocols where a user's randomized report can depend on the reports of other users.

The authors analyze this problem through the lens of minimax risk, where an adversary chooses the underlying data distribution, and the statistician designs a locally private mechanism to minimize the worst-case expected error (e.g., in estimating sums, aggregations, or averages). The paper establishes a minimax lower bound on the l₂ error for estimating the probability distribution of *d* discrete types. Crucially, it demonstrates that this bound is tight, as it is achieved by the well-known randomized response mechanism, proving its optimality for this task. This analysis is extended to the problem of non-parametric density estimation, where a similar result is shown: a lower bound is established and is met by a simple, non-interactive perturbation scheme.

This is a strong and important paper. Proving the optimality of a classic, simple mechanism like randomized response by establishing a tight minimax lower bound is a significant contribution. The results will be of great interest to the NIPS community, and I recommend acceptance.

However, I have a few reservations and suggestions for improvement:

1.  **Clarity and Notation:** From a presentation standpoint, the introduction to minimax bounds could be simplified for broader accessibility. The notation is often cumbersome. For instance, since the primary results concern the l₂ norm, it might be clearer to state the bounds directly in terms of this norm rather than in a more general form.

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** A discussion on the l₁ norm would strengthen the paper. A comparison of the l₂ bounds to potential l₁ bounds, or an explanation of the challenges in the l₁ case, would be valuable. Similarly, situating these results relative to other lower bounds in the (non-local) differential privacy literature would provide useful context.

3.  **Generalizability:** The minimax framework presented seems specifically tailored to the local privacy model. The paper's impact could be expanded by discussing how these techniques might apply to or be extended for deriving bounds in other privacy settings.

4.  **Highlighting a Key Insight:** A particularly noteworthy finding is that while the local privacy model permits interactive protocols, the optimal mechanism is non-interactive (i.e., each user's report depends only on their own data). This is a powerful conclusion that should be stated more explicitly and prominently in the paper.

5.  **Relation to Concurrent Work:** A recent development has slightly moderated my assessment. The publication of the FOCS 2013 accepted papers reveals concurrent work that overlaps with this submission, particularly concerning the results in Section 3. This positions the current paper more as an extension of existing work than I had initially perceived. Consequently, while I still recommend acceptance, my endorsement is less strong than before.

In summary, the paper provides a rigorous minimax analysis of local privacy, establishes sharp lower bounds for probability estimation, and demonstrates the optimality of randomized response. These are strong and valuable results.
