## Review of NIPS_2013-2017/2013

This paper presents a Fano/LeCam-style lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on data subject to local privacy constraints, specifically when the data holder does not fully trust the algorithm. A notable outcome of this analysis is the identification of randomized response as an optimal strategy under certain conditions. While the results offer valuable insights into the limitations of learning from privacy-perturbed data, some aspects of the presentation and contextualization require clarification. I have not reviewed the supplementary material.

**Strengths:**

* The paper provides a fundamental characterization of the limits on learning achievable when data is perturbed to guarantee privacy. This is a significant contribution to understanding the trade-offs inherent in privacy-preserving machine learning.
* The theoretical framework offers a rigorous foundation for analyzing the performance of estimation algorithms under local privacy.

**Weaknesses:**

* The paper occasionally suffers from imprecision in its discussion of the results, which could potentially mislead a reader unfamiliar with the nuances of the model.
* The connection to existing work on differential privacy, a closely related and widely studied privacy paradigm, appears underdeveloped. This omission limits the paper’s broader impact and places it somewhat in isolation.
* The restriction to the local privacy model, while crucial to the theoretical development, isn’t sufficiently emphasized. Many readers may assume a connection to standard differential privacy, and the paper’s statements sometimes inadvertently encourage this misinterpretation. For example, the claim on page 4 – “Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…” – should be carefully qualified or rephrased to accurately reflect the specific local privacy setting.

**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Parameter Restrictions:** Is the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 truly necessary? Existing empirical results, particularly in the context of differential privacy, suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ might represent an upper bound on achievable performance for certain learning tasks. If this is the case, the practical relevance of the bound in scenarios where $\alpha$ is close to 1 remains unclear and should be discussed.
* **Utility Measure ($\rho$):** The choice of the utility measure $\rho$ is important. Providing some discussion of how different choices for $\rho$ impact the analysis and allow the framework to capture different notions of utility would be beneficial to the reader.
* **Related Work (Lei):** The paper would benefit from a comparison to the M-estimation procedure presented by Lei. This work, which is not cited, appears highly relevant, and the authors should demonstrate their awareness of it and explain how their contribution differs.
* **Alternative Histogram Estimation Techniques:** While randomized response is shown to achieve an optimal convergence rate, the paper lacks a comparative analysis with other histogram estimation approaches for discrete data. A brief discussion of how these alternative algorithms perform within the established framework would strengthen the work. 
* **Population vs. Sample Statistics:** The distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be clarified earlier in the paper. A more explicit explanation, as suggested in the authors’ response to Reviewer 9, would greatly improve the clarity of the exposition.

**Addendum Following Rebuttal:**

My post-rebuttal assessment reinforces the need for greater clarity regarding the relationship between the population and sample statistics. This distinction should be explicitly addressed early in the paper. Furthermore, a more comprehensive comparison to related work, including Lei’s work and contributions from other reviewers, is vital for providing adequate context.

My review of [10] (now published) has led me to refine my understanding of this paper’s contribution. While both papers address related issues, their primary contributions differ. This paper offers a careful exposition of the local privacy model and establishes bounds for density estimation. While the density estimation bounds are novel, the core analysis of the local privacy model is largely contained within [10]. Therefore, the primary contribution of this work lies in its clear articulation of the local privacy framework and its application to a specific estimation problem.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and interesting investigation into the limitations of learning from privately perturbed data. While the technical results are strong, the presentation could be improved by addressing the points outlined above, particularly clarifying the relationship to differential privacy and related work. I believe with revisions addressing these concerns, this paper would represent a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference.
