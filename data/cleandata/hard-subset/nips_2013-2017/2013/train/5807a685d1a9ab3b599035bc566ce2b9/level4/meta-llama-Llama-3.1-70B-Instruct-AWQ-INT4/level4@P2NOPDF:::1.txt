This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript that derives a lower bound on estimation algorithms operating on private data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder, using a Fano/LeCam-style argument. Notably, the results imply that randomized response is an optimal strategy in certain scenarios.

I did not review the supplementary material.

The paper has several strengths and weaknesses. On the one hand, the results provide valuable insights into the limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy. On the other hand, there are some imprecisions in the commentary on the results that may confuse casual readers. Furthermore, the connections to existing results on differential privacy seem to be missing.

Some additional comments are in order. The restriction to local privacy, crucial for the results, makes the privacy model distinct from the differential privacy model. This distinction may not be immediately apparent to many readers, and statements such as those at the bottom of page 4 ("Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…") may exacerbate this confusion. The authors should avoid making overly broad statements when describing their results.

It is also unclear whether the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is necessary. Experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may be the most one can expect for certain learning problems under differential privacy, so it is unclear what the bound tells us about this case.

Providing some commentary on the possible choices of $\rho$ would be beneficial, allowing readers to see how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis. Additionally, a comparison with the M-estimation procedure of Lei would be useful, as this paper is not cited but is likely relevant.

There are many other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. While randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, it would be helpful to know how these other algorithms compare.

After the rebuttal, I would like to add a few additional points. The distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper. A comparison to related work, including Lei and other relevant papers, is essential for context.

A closer inspection of [10], which has now appeared, suggests that the additional contribution of this paper is more narrow than initially thought. While the venues and audiences for this and [10] are different, the contribution of this paper is twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model and bounds for density estimation. The latter are new, but the former is essentially contained in [10].
