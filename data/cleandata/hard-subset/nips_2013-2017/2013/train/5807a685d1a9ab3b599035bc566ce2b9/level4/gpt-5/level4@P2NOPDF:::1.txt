Summary:
This paper is a NeurIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript. It uses Fano/Le Cam-style arguments to derive lower bounds for estimation procedures under local privacy, where the data holder does not trust the learner and privatizes data before release. A corollary is that randomized response is, in a precise sense, an optimal strategy for certain problems. I did not review the supplementary material.

Pros:
- The results clearly characterize the limitations of learning from data that are perturbed to guarantee privacy.
- The lower bounds and the optimality of randomized response provide useful guidance for algorithm design under local privacy.

Cons:
- Some of the commentary is imprecise and could mislead readers about the scope of the results (see below).
- Connections to the broader differential privacy literature are underdeveloped.

Comments and suggestions:
- Clarify the privacy model. The restriction to local privacy is central to the results and makes the setting quite different from the standard (central) differential privacy model. Many readers may not appreciate this distinction. Statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4—“Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α …”—risk overgeneralizing beyond the local model. Please avoid language that conflates local privacy with central differential privacy or suggests conclusions that may not hold outside the local setting.
- Theorem 1: Is the restriction on α necessary? Empirical evidence in the central DP literature often suggests that α ≈ 1 is the practical regime for certain learning problems. It is unclear what the bound implies in this regime. Please discuss whether the restriction is technical or fundamental, and, if possible, how the results extend (or fail to extend) to α around 1.
- Utility measures: It would help to include guidance on choosing ρ and to provide examples illustrating how different loss/utility measures are handled within the analysis.
- Density estimation: Please compare your density estimator to Lei’s M-estimation approach, which is not currently cited. A direct comparison—assumptions, rates, and practical implications—would help situate the contribution.
- Discrete histograms: There are many approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. While randomized response achieves the optimal convergence rate under local privacy, it would be informative to discuss how alternative algorithms perform (theoretically and empirically) relative to your bounds.

Addendum after rebuttal:
- Population vs. sample quantities: The distinction between population parameters and sample statistics should be made clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (see also your response to Reviewer 9). Early clarification will help prevent confusion about what is being privatized, estimated, and bounded.
- Related work: A more comprehensive comparison to related work, including Lei and the additional references raised by other reviewers, is important for context. Please discuss similarities, differences in assumptions, techniques, and results.
- Relation to [10]: Now that [10] has appeared, the incremental contribution of this paper is narrower than originally construed. While the venues (and audiences) differ, the contribution appears twofold: (i) a careful exposition of the local privacy model, and (ii) new bounds for density estimation. The latter seem genuinely new; the former largely overlaps with material in [10]. Please position the paper accordingly and make explicit what is novel relative to [10].
