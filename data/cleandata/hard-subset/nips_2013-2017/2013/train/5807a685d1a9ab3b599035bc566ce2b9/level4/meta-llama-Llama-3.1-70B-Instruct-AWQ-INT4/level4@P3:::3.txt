Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

The paper "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations" by the authors deals with the concept of local privacy, where each user outputs an α-differentially private signal based on their own data, and reports this signal to a data curator. The authors analyze this setting using the framework of minimax expected error, where the adversary picks a distribution over inputs and the goal is to minimize the distance between an estimation derived from the original data and a similar estimation derived from the reported signals.

The authors provide a thorough background on the minimax framework, local privacy, and differential privacy, and then delve into the specific problems of multinomial estimation and density estimation under local privacy constraints. They derive lower bounds for the minimax rate for l2 distance estimations and users drawn from a discrete distribution over d types, and show that the simple technique of randomized response achieves these bounds, making it optimal.

The paper then proceeds to analyze the problem of density estimations, where again the lower bound on the minimax rate is met by a perturbation scheme in which each person perturbs only their own data. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the minimax rates for density estimation under local privacy constraints and show that the optimal rate is achieved by a privatized histogram estimator for Lipschitz densities and by an orthogonal series estimator for higher degrees of smoothness.

Strengths of the paper include its thorough analysis of the minimax framework and its application to local privacy, as well as its derivation of sharp lower bounds for the minimax rate for multinomial estimation and density estimation. The paper also provides a nice result in showing that the simple technique of randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation.

Weaknesses of the paper include its cumbersome notation, which can make it difficult to follow at times. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other lower bounds in differential privacy, particularly those that apply to non-local settings. Furthermore, the paper's focus on local privacy may limit its applicability to other settings.

In terms of originality, the paper's contribution is significant, as it provides a thorough analysis of the minimax framework and its application to local privacy. The paper's results on the optimality of randomized response for multinomial estimation and the derivation of sharp lower bounds for density estimation under local privacy constraints are also novel and important.

In terms of significance, the paper's results have important implications for the field of differential privacy and statistical estimation.
