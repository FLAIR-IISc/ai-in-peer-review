This manuscript investigates minimax lower bounds for probability estimation tasks subject to individual data privacy constraints. The privacy model adopted is local differential privacy. The analysis covers two specific problems: multinomial parameter estimation and non-parametric density estimation. For both settings, the authors derive sharp minimax convergence rates. In the discrete multinomial case, it is shown that local privacy induces a reduction in the effective sample size that is quadratic in the privacy parameter, alpha. As alpha is typically a small constant, this implies an effective sample size of the same order as in the non-private setting. Conversely, for density estimation, the analysis reveals that preserving local privacy makes the optimal non-private rate unattainable.

Overall, the paper presents compelling and interesting results. The proofs appear to be sound and technically correct upon inspection. To the best of my knowledge, the derivation of a minimax bound for density estimation under a privacy constraint is a novel contribution.

My primary concern is the omission of a discussion on the close relationship between the minimax bound for multinomial estimation and prior work on noise complexity in differential privacy. Specifically, at least two highly relevant papers address similar problems: Hardt & Talwar, "On the geometry of differential privacy" (STOC, 2010) and De, "Lower bounds in differential privacy" (TCC, 2012). These works establish worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under (central) differential privacy. The probability estimation problem herein is a specific instance of the linear counting queries analyzed in those publications. Furthermore, the error metric is the same L2 distance. The key distinction is the present work's focus on local differential privacy, whereas the cited papers examine the (central) differential privacy model. Given that local privacy is a more stringent constraint, any lower bound for central differential privacy also applies to the local setting. It is therefore unclear whether the new bound derived for local privacy offers a quantitative improvement over the existing bounds for central differential privacy.

Regarding the density estimation problem, the presentation of the main result lacks clarity. The authors claim that the lower bound under local privacy is strictly larger than its non-private counterpart. However, the paper's minimax bound is established for the specific case where the density function can be represented by a trigonometric basis. It is ambiguous whether the cited non-private lower bound in Eq. (13) applies to the general Sobolev space (as per Definition 1) or is also restricted to this specific trigonometric basis. The comparison is only valid if both the private and non-private bounds are derived for the same function class, namely the one defined by the trigonometric basis.

Additional comments to the rebuttal:
The author feedback provides partial clarification on the connection to prior worst-case lower bounds. However, it should be noted that the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, "The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases" (STOC 2013) also employs the Mean Square Error metric. I strongly recommend that the authors integrate their clarification and the missing citations into the revised manuscript.

In summary, this paper derives sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function and probability density function estimation under local privacy guarantees. The findings are significant, and the manuscript is well-written. However, its primary weakness is the failure to acknowledge and discuss highly relevant prior results on noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy.
