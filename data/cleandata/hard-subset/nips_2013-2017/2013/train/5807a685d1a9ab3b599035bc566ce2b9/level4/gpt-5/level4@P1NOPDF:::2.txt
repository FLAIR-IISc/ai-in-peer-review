This paper studies minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint that individual data must remain private, using the framework of local privacy. The authors consider two problems: multinomial (pmf) estimation and density (pdf) estimation. In both settings, they establish sharp minimax rates. For the discrete multinomial problem, they show that local privacy induces a reduction in effective sample size that is quadratic in the privacy parameter α. Since α is often treated as a small constant, the effective sample size remains of the same order as in the non-private case. For density estimation, they show that the optimal non-private rate is no longer achievable under local privacy.

Overall, the results are very interesting. As far as I can tell, the proofs are correct. To the best of my knowledge, the minimax bounds for density estimation under privacy constraints have not previously been studied.

My main comment concerns the connection between the multinomial minimax bounds and prior work on noise complexity in differential privacy, which is not sufficiently discussed. In particular, two highly relevant papers are Hardt and Talwar, “On the Geometry of Differential Privacy” (STOC 2010), and De, “Lower Bounds in Differential Privacy” (TCC 2012). These works establish worst-case lower bounds on error for linear queries under differential privacy, using the same L2 error metric. The probability estimation problem considered here is a special case of the linear counting queries studied in those papers. The key difference is that this paper considers local differential privacy, whereas Hardt–Talwar and De consider the standard (central) differential privacy model. Because local privacy imposes a stronger constraint than central differential privacy, lower bounds proved for the central model are also valid lower bounds for the local model. It would be helpful if the authors clarified whether their lower bounds for local privacy strictly improve upon, or merely match, the best known lower bounds for central differential privacy in this setting, and to what extent the techniques are comparable.

Regarding the density estimation results, I found the statement and comparison somewhat confusing. The authors claim that the minimax lower bound under local privacy is higher than in the non-private setting. However, the minimax analysis here is carried out for a special subclass of densities that admit a trigonometric basis expansion. It is not clear whether the non-private lower bound cited in equation (13) is established for the general Sobolev class defined in Definition 1 or for this trigonometric subclass. The comparison is only fair if the non-private lower bound also holds for the trigonometric basis case used in the private analysis. Clarifying this point—either by stating that the non-private lower bound applies to the trigonometric subclass or by providing (or citing) the appropriate subclass-specific bound—would make the comparison precise.

Additional comments on the rebuttal:
- The feedback partially clarified the relationship to previous worst-case lower bounds. However, please also consider the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, “The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases” (STOC 2013), which analyzes mean squared error and appears directly relevant.
- I strongly recommend that the authors incorporate these missing references and expand the discussion contrasting their results with prior lower bounds for linear queries under differential privacy, carefully delineating what is new in the local model and whether the constants or regimes improve on central-model bounds.

In summary, this paper establishes sharp minimax rates for pmf and pdf estimation under local privacy. The results are interesting and the paper is clearly written. The main shortcoming is the lack of discussion of closely related lower bounds on noise complexity in differential privacy, which are highly pertinent to the multinomial estimation results presented here. Addressing these connections and clarifying the basis-dependent comparison in the density estimation section would significantly strengthen the paper.
