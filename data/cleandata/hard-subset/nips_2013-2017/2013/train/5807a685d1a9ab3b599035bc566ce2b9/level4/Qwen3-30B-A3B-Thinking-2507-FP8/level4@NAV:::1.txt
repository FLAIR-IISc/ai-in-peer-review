

Review Summary: This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript, employing a Fano/LeCam-style argument to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on privately perturbed data when the algorithm is untrusted by the data holder. A corollary demonstrates that randomized response achieves optimality in a specific sense.  
Note: The supplementary material was not reviewed.  

Pros and Cons:  
- The results characterize the fundamental limitations of learning from data perturbed for privacy.  
- The commentary on results contains imprecisions that may confuse casual readers (see detailed comments).  
- Connections to established differential privacy results are absent.  

Additional Comments:  
- The restriction to local privacy (critical for the results) significantly diverges from the differential privacy model, a distinction many readers may overlook. Statements like those on page 4 ("Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…") are overly broad and potentially misleading; such phrasing should be avoided.  
- The necessity of the $\alpha$ restriction in Theorem 1 is unclear. Experimental evidence suggests $\alpha \approx 1$ is often the practical limit for learning under differential privacy, making the bound’s relevance to this case ambiguous.  
- A discussion of plausible $\rho$ choices would clarify how the analysis accommodates different utility measures.  
- The density estimator’s relation to Lei’s M-estimation procedure (unreferenced) merits comparison, as the authors should be aware of this work.  
- While randomized response achieves optimal convergence rates, the performance of other histogram estimation approaches for discrete data is not addressed.  

Addendum After Rebuttal:  
* The distinction between population and sample statistics requires clearer, more explicit explanation at the paper’s outset (per response to Rev.9).  
* A comparison to related work (including Lei and other cited papers) is essential for contextualization.  
* A closer examination of [10] (now published) refines the paper’s contribution: it comprises (1) a careful exposition of the local privacy model (largely covered in [10]) and (2) novel bounds for density estimation. The primary novelty thus lies in the density estimation bounds, with the model exposition being largely derivative.
