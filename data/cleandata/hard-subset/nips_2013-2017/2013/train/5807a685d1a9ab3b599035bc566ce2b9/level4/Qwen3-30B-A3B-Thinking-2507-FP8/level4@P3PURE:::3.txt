

Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation  

This paper investigates local privacy, where each user independently outputs an α-differentially private signal based solely on their own type (not the signals of others), and reports it to a curator. The authors analyze this setting via minimax expected error: an adversary selects a distribution over user types, and the curator chooses an α-DP local scheme to minimize the l₂ distance between an estimator derived from the original data and one derived from the reported signals (e.g., sums or averages). The authors establish a tight minimax lower bound for l₂ estimation when users are drawn from a discrete distribution over d types, and prove that randomized response achieves this bound—thus demonstrating its optimality. They further extend the analysis to density estimation, showing that a perturbation scheme where each user perturbs only their own type meets the minimax lower bound.  

The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing that a classic technique (randomized response) is minimax-optimal in this setting. This result is theoretically important and would interest the NIPS community; we recommend acceptance.  

However, we note three reservations:  
1. The explanation of minimax bounds is unnecessarily complex, and the notation is cumbersome. Since all bounds are for l₂, phrasing them explicitly in terms of l₂ would improve clarity.  
2. The work lacks discussion of l₁ norms, which are more restrictive in differential privacy. A comparison to l₁ bounds or relevant literature on classical (non-local) settings would strengthen the analysis.  
3. The minimax bounds appear tailored specifically to the local privacy setting. The authors could broaden the scope by deriving minimax lower bounds for other settings (e.g., centralized DP) to contextualize their results.  

We particularly appreciate the insight that optimal local privacy schemes need not leverage correlations across users—only per-user perturbation is required. This should be explicitly stated in the text.  

A critical reservation: The recent publication of FOCS 2013 accepted papers reveals that Section 3’s results closely overlap with an existing FOCS 2013 paper (e.g., [citation if known]), making this work an extension rather than a novel contribution. We therefore recommend acceptance but with reduced enthusiasm compared to our initial assessment.  

Overall, the paper establishes tight minimax rates for local privacy via randomized response, a significant contribution. The core technical arguments are sound, and the results are well-motivated.
