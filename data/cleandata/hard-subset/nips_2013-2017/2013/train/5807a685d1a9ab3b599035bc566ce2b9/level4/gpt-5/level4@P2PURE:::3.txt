Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This paper studies local privacy, where each user produces an alpha-differentially private signal based on her own type and the signals of the other n−1 users, and reports this to the data curator. The analysis uses a minimax expected error framework: an adversary chooses a distribution over inputs (the users’ types), and we choose an alpha-DP local scheme to minimize the distance between an estimate computed on the original data and the corresponding estimate computed on the reported signals (typically sums, aggregates, or averages). The authors establish a lower bound on the minimax rate for l2 distance estimation when users are drawn from a discrete distribution over d types, and they show that simple randomized response achieves this bound, proving optimality. They then analyze density estimation and again show that the minimax lower bound is attained by a scheme in which each user perturbs only her own type.

The paper is clear and important: it demonstrates that a classic procedure is optimal by matching sharp lower bounds. I believe the NIPS community will find it interesting, and I recommend acceptance.

I do have a few reservations:
- Style and exposition: the explanation of minimax bounds could be simplified. The notation is often cumbersome; since all stated bounds are for the l2 norm, could the results be expressed directly with respect to this norm?
- Additional norms and context: I would have liked a discussion of the l1 norm—which is more restrictive—or at least a connection between the presented bounds and l1 guarantees, as well as a comparison to known lower bounds in differential privacy (which, admittedly, are more classical and non-local).
- Scope of the minimax setting: the minimax bounds seem tailored to the local-privacy model. It would strengthen the paper to consider a broader class of settings and provide corresponding minimax lower bounds.
- Positive note: while the local setting allows users to randomize their reports based on the remaining n−1 users, it turns out that the simple scheme where each person randomizes solely based on her own type is optimal. It would help to state this explicitly.

A new reservation: the FOCS 2013 accepted papers list has now been published, and the current submission appears to extend an existing paper, especially in Section 3. I therefore still recommend acceptance, but less strongly than before.

In summary, the paper analyzes local privacy via minimax rate bounds, provides lower bounds, and shows that randomized response attains the upper bound, yielding a clean optimality result.
