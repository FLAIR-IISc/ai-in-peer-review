

This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, examining two core problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. The authors establish sharp minimax convergence rates for both settings, demonstrating that local privacy induces a fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency. For multinomial estimation, they show that the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d when α is a small constant, implying that Warner's randomized response method achieves optimal sample complexity. For density estimation, they prove that the minimax rate degrades from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) in the non-private setting to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) under local privacy, with this rate being attainable through carefully designed estimators.

The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing the trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency using minimax decision theory, bridging classical privacy concepts with modern differential privacy frameworks. The results are technically sound, with correct proofs and appropriate connections to relevant literature. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail to allow reproduction of the results.

Regarding originality, the paper presents novel minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. While the authors acknowledge Warner's classical work, they do not sufficiently connect their multinomial estimation results to prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy. Specifically, Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012) established worst-case lower bounds for linear queries under differential privacy, which are directly relevant as probability estimation constitutes a special case of linear counting queries. The authors should clarify how their local privacy bounds improve upon or relate to these differential privacy bounds, noting that local privacy imposes stronger constraints than standard differential privacy. Additionally, the recent work by Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013) on mean square error under differential privacy should be cited.

The density estimation results are well-motivated but require clarification regarding the non-private lower bound (13). The authors claim this bound applies to the Sobolev class Fβ defined in Definition 1, but the bound is actually derived for the trigonometric basis case. To ensure a fair comparison, the authors should explicitly confirm whether the non-private lower bound holds for the general Sobolev space as defined in Definition 1 or is specific to the trigonometric basis.

The significance of these results is substantial, as they provide quantitative trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency that can guide practitioners in designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The paper successfully demonstrates how to move along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum, with potential applications in survey sampling and density estimation.

Overall, this is an important contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical estimation. The paper is well-written and technically sound, but would be strengthened by more comprehensive engagement with prior work on noise complexity under differential privacy.
