- Provides a thorough minimax-theoretic analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints.
- Shows fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency and derives optimal rates of convergence.
- Establishes that Warner's randomized response is optimal for certain survey-sampling estimation problems.
- Extends classical minimax theory to include local differential privacy, with applications to multinomial and density estimation.
- Delivers rigorous minimax bounds that quantify the statistical cost of local privacy.
- Proves randomized response achieves optimal sample complexity for private survey sampling.
- Presents efficient, computable algorithms that match the theoretical lower bounds.
- Contains imprecise statements conflating local privacy with (central) differential privacy that may confuse readers.
- Fails to sufficiently develop connections and distinctions between local and central differential privacy in related work.
- Does not adequately address practical implications of the α restriction in Theorem 1, limiting real-world applicability.
- Needs a clearer initial explanation distinguishing local privacy from differential privacy.
- Should discuss the α ∈ [0,1] restriction in Theorem 1 in light of empirical evidence that α ≈ 1 is often the practical limit.
- Would benefit from exploring how different utility metrics (ρ) impact the results.
- Should compare its density estimation approach with Lei's M-estimation and other histogram-based privacy-preserving methods.
- Addendum: clarify population vs. sample statistics distinction, expand related-work comparisons (including Lei and paper [10]), and note that the primary novelty may be the density estimation bounds given prior foundational coverage.
