The paper provides a detailed study of probability estimation—discrete and continuous—under the constraints of local privacy, a stringent setting where data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors establish sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate, and identifying procedures that achieve optimal performance. A notable result is the confirmation that Warner’s randomized response is an optimal strategy for survey sampling with privacy guarantees.

The paper is well-motivated, revisiting Warner’s original work and placing it within a modern theoretical framework combining differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The contributions are significant, providing a rigorous characterization of the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off. The technical analysis appears sound, with clear derivations of both upper and lower bounds on convergence rates. The use of established techniques from statistical decision theory (minimax estimation) alongside recent advances in privacy (differential privacy) is commendable. The authors correctly identify and highlight the connection between local privacy and the effective reduction in sample size. The results on both multinomial estimation and density estimation are valuable, and the achievability results demonstrate that the derived bounds are tight.

Strengths of the paper include the mathematical rigor, the clear presentation of the core concepts, and the relevance of the problem to a growing area of research. The explicit connection drawn between Warner's work and the modern framework of differential privacy is particularly insightful. The analysis and results are well-supported by theoretical arguments and, where applicable, existing literature is appropriately cited. The paper addresses all four NIPS evaluation criteria effectively. The technical soundness is high, the clarity is good (although see below), the originality lies in the combination of techniques and the specific results obtained, and the significance is substantial given the increasing importance of privacy-preserving data analysis.

Weaknesses are relatively minor. While the technical analysis is rigorous, the initial presentation of the minimax framework could be more accessible to readers less familiar with the area. The notation is somewhat dense, and streamlining it could improve readability. For example, consistently framing bounds in terms of a single norm (e.g., l2) throughout the paper might reduce cognitive load. Expanding the discussion to include considerations beyond the l2 norm (e.g., l1 norm) or relating the results to other differential privacy bounds would be beneficial. Furthermore, while the authors establish lower bounds for the described setting, a broader discussion of the applicability of these bounds to other privacy models or estimation problems would be valuable. Although mentioned in passing, explicitly stating that the optimal strategy relies on each user randomizing their report based *only* on their own type, rather than conditioning on the reports of others, would strengthen the conclusion. A recent publication at FOCS 2013 extends some of the work, which slightly diminishes the novelty, but does not invalidate the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a timely and important problem in privacy-preserving data analysis.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous and provide sharp bounds on the achievable performance.
*   The connection to Warner’s classical work is insightful and provides valuable context.
*   The analysis combines techniques from both statistical decision theory and differential privacy.
*   The paper meets all four NIPS evaluation criteria.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The initial presentation of the minimax framework could be more accessible.
*   The notation is somewhat dense and could be streamlined.
*   The connection to related work in differential privacy could be more thoroughly explored.
*   Related work presented at FOCS 2013 lessens the novelty.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the contribution is valuable to the NIPS community. Addressing the minor weaknesses suggested above would further enhance the quality of the work.
