- Provides a decision-theoretic, minimax analysis of probability estimation under α-local differential privacy, quantifying privacy–efficiency trade-offs.  
- For multinomial estimation, proves sharp minimax rates showing privacy effectively reduces sample size from n to nα²/d.  
- Establishes minimax optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism for the multinomial setting.  
- For Sobolev-class density estimation, shows local privacy changes the polynomial convergence rate from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2).  
- Constructs computationally efficient private estimators (private histograms and orthogonal series) that achieve the new optimal rates.  
- Strength: formally connects differential privacy with classical minimax theory, offering foundational insights into privacy costs.  
- Strength: technically strong—derives matching upper and lower bounds and yields elegant results like randomized response optimality.  
- Strength: original finding that local privacy can fundamentally alter polynomial convergence rates in nonparametric settings.  
- Weakness: dense notation and heavy presentation limit accessibility; needs a gentler high-level exposition and clearer proof walkthroughs.  
- Weakness: substantial overlap with the authors’ public preprint, particularly for multinomial results, raising novelty concerns.  
- Weakness: should better contextualize comparisons to central (non-local) privacy lower bounds to isolate local-privacy costs.  
- Recommendation pro: timely, important, rigorous work with significant potential influence across privacy and statistics.  
- Recommendation con: novelty overlap and dense presentation may reduce suitability for broad conference audiences.  
- Overall: strong, valuable contribution despite presentation and overlap concerns; likely a worthwhile conference paper.
