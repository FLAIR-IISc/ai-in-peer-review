

Summary: This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript that establishes a lower bound on estimation accuracy for algorithms operating on privately perturbed data under a local privacy model, using a Fano-LeCam-style argument. The results imply that randomized response achieves optimality in certain settings. Note that I did not review the supplementary material.

Strengths: The work clearly characterizes fundamental limitations of learning from data perturbed for privacy, providing a valuable theoretical foundation.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Confusing privacy model comparisons:** The paper frequently conflates local privacy with differential privacy (DP), creating significant confusion. For instance, the statement at the bottom of page 4—"Thus, for suitably large sample sizes *n*, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level *α*..."—is overly broad and potentially incorrect. The distinction between local privacy (where data is perturbed before reaching the algorithm) and DP (where a trusted curator applies perturbation) is critical, yet inadequately explained. Many readers will overlook this key difference, misinterpreting the results as applicable to standard DP.  
2. **Necessity of *α* restriction:** Theorem 1 imposes restrictions on *α*. Given that experimental work (e.g., in DP learning) suggests *α* ≈ 1 represents a practical limit for certain problems, it is unclear whether the bound remains meaningful in this regime. The authors should clarify if the restriction is essential or merely technical.  
3. **Missing contextualization:** The paper omits crucial connections to existing literature. Specifically:  
   - It fails to cite Lei’s M-estimation procedure for density estimation under privacy constraints, which is highly relevant.  
   - It does not compare its density estimator to other established methods for discrete data (e.g., histogram estimators beyond randomized response), despite noting randomized response achieves the optimal convergence rate.  
   - A brief discussion of how different utility measures (*ρ*) might influence the analysis would help readers interpret the results.  

Additional context: The paper’s contribution appears twofold: (a) a detailed exposition of the local privacy model, and (b) novel bounds for density estimation. However, a closer look at [10] (which has since appeared) suggests the former is largely covered there. While the venues differ, this paper’s novelty is thus narrower than claimed.  

Addendum after Rebuttal:  
- The paper must explicitly clarify the population vs. sample statistic distinction early on (as noted in response to Reviewer 9).  
- A thorough comparison to Lei’s work and other related approaches (as raised by another reviewer) is essential for proper context.  
- Given [10]’s coverage of the local privacy model, the paper’s primary contribution is now limited to its density estimation bounds.  

In summary, the technical results are sound but the presentation risks misleading readers due to imprecise privacy model descriptions and omitted contextual work. The paper would benefit significantly from a clearer exposition of the local privacy framework, explicit acknowledgment of Lei’s M-estimation approach, and a more precise discussion of the *α* restriction. The core theoretical contribution (the lower bound) remains valuable, but its significance is diminished without stronger positioning against existing literature.
