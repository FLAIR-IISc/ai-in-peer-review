Summary
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy, where each datum is privatized before reaching the analyst via an α-locally private channel. The authors combine minimax decision theory with information-theoretic tools (Le Cam/Fano arguments) to derive sharp lower bounds on the rates of convergence for two problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. They also provide matching upper bounds via computationally simple procedures.

For d-dimensional multinomial estimation, they show that under α-local privacy the effective sample size scales as nα2/d (for small α), yielding minimax rates of order d/(nα2) in squared error and d/√(nα2) in ℓ1 error. A notable consequence is that Warner’s randomized response mechanism is optimal up to constants for this task.

For density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1], the optimal nonprivate rate n−2β/(2β+1) is no longer attainable under local privacy. The minimax rate becomes (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), reflecting a change in the polynomial exponent. The authors construct private histogram estimators (optimal for β=1) and private orthogonal series estimators (optimal for all β≥1) that achieve these rates. They also show that simple additive-noise mechanisms (e.g., Laplace perturbation of raw samples) are suboptimal, connecting to classical deconvolution lower bounds (Carroll and Hall).

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on differential privacy foundations and on earlier analyses of learning under privacy (e.g., Warner, Wasserman & Zhou, Kasiviswanathan et al., Smith, Hardt & Talwar). The local privacy model aligns with “randomized response” and the “local” model of private learning, as opposed to the standard “centralized” model of differential privacy. Much of the minimax lower-bound technique follows standard information-theoretic methods adapted to the local privacy constraint. The manuscript is a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv submission by the same authors.

Strengths
- Clear and rigorous formulation of minimax estimation under local privacy, with tight lower and upper bounds.
- Conceptually clean message about the privacy–accuracy trade-off, including the optimality of randomized response for multinomial estimation.
- Novel and sharp results for density estimation under local privacy, including constructive, efficient mechanisms that match the lower bounds.
- Useful information-contraction inequalities that may be of independent interest.

Weaknesses and points to clarify
- The paper sometimes uses “differential privacy” without emphasizing that all results are in the local (non-interactive, memoryless channel) model, which differs substantially from the standard centralized model. This could mislead casual readers. Language such as “the effect of providing differential privacy at level α” (p. 4) should be qualified as “local differential privacy.”
- Theorem 1 is stated for α∈[0,1]. Is this restriction essential for the rates, constants, or proofs? Empirically, α≈1 is common in private learning; readers would benefit from a discussion of how the bounds behave and how mechanisms should be tuned when α is not very small.
- The paper emphasizes squared and ℓ1 losses for multinomial and L2 loss for densities. A brief discussion of how other choices of ρ (e.g., Hellinger, TV, KL) fit into the framework would help readers map the results to alternative utility metrics.
- Comparison to related estimators appears incomplete. For density estimation, how do the proposed private orthogonal series/histogram estimators compare to Lei’s differentially private M-estimators and to other recent private density estimation approaches? The current draft does not cite Lei, and a direct comparison would better position the contribution.
- For discrete data, beyond randomized response there are numerous privatization schemes (e.g., Laplace-perturbed counts, Hadamard/raptor transforms used in heavy-hitter protocols). While randomized response achieves the optimal rate asymptotically, a discussion of constants, sample-size regimes, and empirical trade-offs among these mechanisms would be informative.

Quality
The technical development is sound and careful, and the lower and upper bounds match (up to constants). The information-contraction bounds and the constructive mechanisms are convincing. I did not read the supplementary material in detail; completeness of proofs there is assumed.

Clarity
The presentation is generally clear and well organized. However, the paper should be more explicit up front about population vs. sample estimation targets and about the distinction between local and centralized differential privacy. Some statements risk overgeneralization if not qualified as “local.”

Originality
The density estimation rates under local privacy and the matching constructions are new and significant. The characterization of multinomial rates and the optimality of randomized response are valuable, though more incremental given the classical origins of randomized response and prior work on local private learning. Portions of the local privacy framework have appeared in earlier work; the paper’s additional contribution is strongest for nonparametric density estimation.

Significance
The results are important for understanding what can be learned under stringent privacy constraints. The sharp trade-offs and optimal mechanisms should influence both theory and practice in locally private data collection and analysis.

Pros
- Sharp, matching minimax rates under local privacy for multinomial and density estimation.
- Simple, implementable mechanisms achieving optimality (randomized response; private orthogonal series).
- Clear articulation of privacy–accuracy trade-offs.

Cons
- Potential confusion between local and centralized DP due to imprecise phrasing.
- Missing or limited comparisons to related estimators (e.g., Lei) and alternative mechanisms.
- Limited discussion of the α-regime and of alternative loss metrics.

Requests for revision
- Clarify at the outset: (i) population vs. sample estimation targets; (ii) that all results are in the non-interactive local DP model, contrasting with the centralized model.
- Discuss the necessity of α∈[0,1] in Theorem 1 and interpret the bounds for α≈1.
- Add a brief section on alternative choices of ρ and how the analysis adapts.
- Include a comparison to Lei’s M-estimators and other related density estimators; for discrete data, situate randomized response among other practical mechanisms.
- Where appropriate, temper language that could be read as claims about centralized DP.

Addendum after rebuttal
- Please clearly distinguish population vs. sample objectives at the beginning (cf. response to Reviewer 9).
- Add explicit comparisons to Lei and other related work noted by reviewers.
- After reviewing [10] (now appeared), I view the incremental contribution as: (a) a careful exposition of the local privacy model (much of which overlaps prior work), and (b) new bounds and optimal mechanisms for density estimation. Framing the paper accordingly will help set expectations.
