Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

Summary
The paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy, where each individual releases an alpha-locally differentially private randomized signal of her own data via a channel chosen before seeing others’ data (non-interactive, memoryless). The authors bring together differential privacy and classical minimax decision theory by defining a private minimax risk and deriving sharp lower bounds on the best achievable rates over all alpha-private mechanisms, together with matching, computationally efficient procedures.

Two estimation problems are analyzed. For multinomial probability estimation over d categories, the authors prove that local privacy imposes an effective reduction in sample size: compared to the non-private n-rate, the minimax mean-squared error scales like d/(n alpha^2) (for small alpha), implying an effective sample size n alpha^2/d. They also give an ℓ1 counterpart. Crucially, a simple randomized response mechanism achieves these rates, showing that Warner’s classical scheme is optimal in the local model.

For nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes F_beta, the authors show a more severe impact: the classical non-private minimax rate n^{-2 beta/(2 beta + 1)} degrades under local privacy to (alpha^2 n)^{-2 beta/(2 beta + 2)}, i.e., the polynomial exponent changes. They complement this with constructions that match the lower bounds: for beta = 1 (Lipschitz densities), a privatized histogram estimator achieves the optimal rate; for general beta ≥ 1, an orthogonal series estimator based on a carefully designed locally private mechanism (not simple additive Laplace noise on raw samples) is minimax optimal. They also explain why naive Laplace perturbation of the data leads to strictly suboptimal deconvolution rates, grounding this observation in classical results.

Relation to prior work is clear and appropriate: the paper connects Warner’s randomized response, differential privacy (Dwork et al.), lower bounds and geometry (Hardt and Talwar), information-theoretic tools (Le Cam, Fano), and private estimation frameworks (Wasserman and Zhou, Smith), as well as the authors’ own longer technical report.

Evaluation

Quality
- Theoretical development appears technically sound, with clear lower-bound techniques (information-contraction bounds under local privacy combined with Le Cam/Fano) and matching upper bounds via explicit private mechanisms. The arguments make careful use of mutual information and total variation contraction under local channels.
- The constructive mechanisms are simple and implementable (randomized response; privatized histograms; bounded orthogonal series with a tailored sampling scheme), and the paper is honest about the limits of additive-noise approaches.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized but could be easier to read. The exposition of the minimax framework and the mapping from testing to estimation could be streamlined for a broader NIPS audience. The notation is heavy; since the main results are stated in ℓ2 (and also ℓ1 for multinomials), it would help to standardize early on and avoid switching between multiple notational variants.
- It would be helpful to state explicitly, early in Section 3, that simple per-user randomization based only on one’s own data attains the optimal rate, even though one could in principle consider more general local protocols. Clarifying the scope (non-interactive) and whether the lower bounds extend to interactive local mechanisms would strengthen the message.

Originality
- The combination of local differential privacy with minimax decision theory, and the demonstration that randomized response is rate-optimal for multinomial estimation, are compelling. The identification of a new exponent in the nonparametric rates under local privacy is novel and insightful.
- That said, now that the FOCS 2013 accepted list is public, parts of Section 3 appear overlapping with prior/parallel work. This reduces the novelty of that section, though the density-estimation results and the sharp characterization across problems remain significant.

Significance
- The results provide clean, quantitative trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency and show that simple, classical mechanisms can be optimal in the stringent local model. This is valuable for both theory and practice.
- The work is likely to influence how the community designs private estimators, emphasizing the need for problem-specific mechanisms (beyond naive additive noise) to achieve optimal rates.

Pros
- Sharp lower and upper bounds for local privacy in both finite-dimensional and nonparametric settings.
- Clear identification of randomized response as optimal for multinomial estimation.
- Novel rate change for density estimation under local privacy and matching constructions.
- Conceptually unifying framework connecting differential privacy and minimax theory.

Cons
- Exposition could be simplified; notation is heavier than necessary.
- The paper focuses on the non-interactive local model; broader consideration of interactive local protocols or other privacy models would increase generality.
- The discussion of ℓ1 risk could be more explicit and prominent, especially for probability estimation where ℓ1 is natural.
- Overlap with FOCS 2013 (especially Section 3) tempers the originality claim.

Suggestions for improvement
- Streamline the minimax/testing exposition and reduce notational overhead; define the primary norms once and stick to them.
- Make explicit that simple per-user, non-interactive randomized response achieves the optimal multinomial rates, and clarify the extent to which the lower bounds apply to interactive local mechanisms.
- Provide a more explicit discussion of ℓ1 risk (for multinomials, already present; a brief comparison and guidance would help readers).
- Briefly situate the bounds relative to central-model differential privacy lower bounds to highlight the stronger privacy constraints in the local model.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a solid, well-supported contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates under local privacy and by identifying optimal, simple mechanisms. I note some overlap with recent FOCS work, which slightly reduces the novelty of Section 3, but the overall significance and clarity of the privacy–efficiency trade-offs justify acceptance.
