This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in probability estimation. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial estimation) and continuous (density estimation) settings under the constraint of local differential privacy. A key result demonstrates the optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism for survey sampling while maintaining privacy. The work leverages tools from minimax decision theory and differential privacy to formally characterize these trade-offs.

The paper builds upon the classical work of Warner [23] and recent advancements in differential privacy [11, 1, 9]. It addresses a critical gap in the literature by providing a formal treatment of disclosure risk that allows for the application of decision-theoretic tools to quantify the privacy-utility trade-off. The authors establish lower and upper bounds on convergence rates, demonstrating that local privacy introduces a penalty in terms of effective sample size or convergence speed.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper employs a solid theoretical framework based on minimax decision theory and differential privacy, providing rigorous guarantees on the performance of estimators under privacy constraints.
* **Sharp Bounds:** The derived minimax rates are sharp, indicating that the established trade-offs are fundamental and cannot be improved upon.
* **Practical Relevance:** The demonstration of optimality for Warner’s randomized response mechanism is practically significant, as this technique is widely used in survey research.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the problem setting, introduces the necessary concepts of local privacy, and carefully states the assumptions.
* **Novel Combination:** The combination of differential privacy with minimax theory represents a novel approach to analyzing privacy-preserving statistical estimation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Precision in Commentary:** Some statements regarding the applicability of the results, particularly regarding the connection to differential privacy in general, could be more precise to avoid potential misinterpretations. The claim on page 4 linking the results to "differential privacy" requires careful qualification, as the local privacy model differs significantly.
* **Limited Discussion of α:** The restriction on the privacy parameter α in Theorem 1 and its implications for practical scenarios (e.g., α ≈ 1) could be further discussed. Exploring the behavior of the bounds for larger values of α would be insightful.
* **Utility Metric Sensitivity:** While the paper focuses on specific utility measures (e.g., squared error), a brief discussion of how the results extend to other utility measures (captured through different choices of the semi-metric ρ) would broaden the paper’s impact.
* **Related Work Omission:** The paper omits discussion of relevant work by Lei [19] on differentially private M-estimators, and potentially other related approaches to histogram estimation. A more comprehensive review of existing literature would strengthen the paper.
* **Scope of Contributions:** While valuable, the contribution regarding the local privacy model itself may be somewhat incremental, given its substantial presentation in prior work like [10]. Clarifying the unique contributions (the density estimation bounds and the careful exposition of local privacy) would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of privacy-preserving statistical estimation. The theoretical results are rigorous and the analysis is insightful. The demonstrated optimality of randomized response is a particularly notable finding. While some minor clarifications and additions to the related work section would improve the paper, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference with increasing relevance in the age of data privacy.
* The theoretical results are sharp and provide a clear understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and utility.
* The work is well-written and technically sound.
* The optimality result for randomized response is practically significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The connection to broader differential privacy literature could be made more explicit and nuanced.
* The discussion of the privacy parameter α and its implications for real-world scenarios could be expanded.
* The related work section could benefit from a more comprehensive review of existing approaches.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the points raised regarding the precision of their commentary, the discussion of the privacy parameter α, the inclusion of related work, and the scope of their contributions. Addressing these points will further strengthen an already valuable and insightful paper.
