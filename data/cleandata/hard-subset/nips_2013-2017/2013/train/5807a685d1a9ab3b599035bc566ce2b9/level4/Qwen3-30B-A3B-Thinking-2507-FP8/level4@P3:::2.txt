

This paper provides a comprehensive study of minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, a setting where data remains private even from the statistician. The authors establish sharp minimax bounds for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density estimation) probability estimation problems. For multinomial estimation, they show the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal for this problem. For density estimation, they establish a new convergence rate of (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2), which differs from the classical non-private rate of n⁻²β/(2β+1). The paper's main contribution lies in providing fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, showing that privacy constraints impose quantifiable penalties on estimation accuracy.

The paper is well-structured, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistical decision theory. The authors effectively bridge classical minimax theory with differential privacy, providing both matching lower and upper bounds with efficiently implementable algorithms. The proofs appear rigorous, and the paper's technical contributions are clearly presented. The connection to Warner's work provides historical context while demonstrating that optimal privacy-preserving strategies for survey sampling have been known for nearly 50 years.

The paper's significance is substantial, as it provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the cost of privacy in statistical estimation. The results are directly applicable to practical privacy-preserving data analysis, particularly in survey sampling and density estimation. The identification of the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum offers a principled way to adjust statistical procedures based on privacy requirements.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of its relationship to prior work on differential privacy. The minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely related to previous work on noise complexity for differential privacy, particularly Hardt and Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012), which establish worst-case lower bounds for linear queries under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem considered in this paper is a special case of the linear counting query studied in those works, and the error metric (L₂) is identical. While the current paper considers local privacy (a stronger constraint than standard differential privacy), the lower bounds for differential privacy should also apply to local privacy. The authors should clarify whether their bounds improve upon or are equivalent to previous results for differential privacy.

For the density estimation results, the comparison between non-private and locally private settings requires clarification. The non-private lower bound in equation (13) appears to be derived for the specific trigonometric basis used in the paper, but it's unclear whether this bound holds for the general Sobolev space defined in Definition 1. The comparison between the non-private rate n⁻²β/(2β+1) and the locally private rate (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2) is only fair if the non-private bound applies to the same function class as the locally private analysis. The authors should explicitly clarify this relationship and confirm whether the non-private bound (13) holds for the trigonometric basis case.

The paper would also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship to Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also considers mean square error under differential privacy. While the authors acknowledge the relevance of Hardt & Talwar, they should explicitly address how their results relate to this more recent work.

The paper's strengths are its theoretical rigor, clear presentation of both discrete and continuous estimation problems, and the practical implications of their results. The authors provide efficient algorithms that achieve the minimax rates, making their theoretical results immediately applicable. The connection to Warner's work adds historical context and reinforces the practical relevance of their findings.

The weaknesses are primarily related to the literature review and clarification of comparisons with existing work. The authors should explicitly address the relationship to prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy, particularly Hardt & Talwar (2010), De (2012), and Nikolov et al. (2013), and clarify how their local privacy bounds relate to these prior results.

To summarize:
- **Strengths**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, provides sharp minimax bounds with matching algorithms, and connects historical work with modern privacy frameworks.
- **Weaknesses**: The paper lacks sufficient discussion of the relationship to prior work on differential privacy lower bounds, and the comparison between non-private and locally private density estimation rates requires clarification.

Given the paper's theoretical contribution, technical soundness, and practical significance, I recommend acceptance. The minor weaknesses identified can be addressed through minor revisions that strengthen the connection to prior work and clarify the comparison between non-private and locally private rates. This paper advances our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference and provides a valuable framework for future work in this area.
