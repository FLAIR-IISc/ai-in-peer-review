

This paper establishes sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, addressing two core problems: multinomial estimation (discrete) and density estimation (continuous). The authors derive fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, demonstrating that local privacy reduces the effective sample size for multinomial estimation by a factor of α²/d, while for density estimation with β smoothness, the convergence rate degrades from n⁻²ᵝ/(2ᵝ⁺¹) to n⁻²ᵝ/(2ᵝ⁺²). Notably, they show Warner’s randomized response achieves minimax optimality for multinomial estimation—a result with significant practical implications given its 50-year history. The proofs are technically sound, and the paper provides matching upper bounds via efficient algorithms, making it a strong theoretical contribution.

The paper’s strengths are clear: it resolves a key gap in understanding privacy-statistics trade-offs through rigorous minimax analysis, connects classical privacy (Warner) with modern differential privacy frameworks, and delivers novel sharp rates for both discrete and continuous settings. The writing is precise, and the results are well-motivated. The authors’ claim that Warner’s method is optimal for survey sampling is particularly compelling.

However, the paper lacks sufficient contextualization against prior work on differential privacy lower bounds. Specifically, the multinomial estimation result is a special case of linear counting queries (e.g., estimating population proportions), which have been studied under standard differential privacy (not local privacy). Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012) established worst-case lower bounds for linear queries under differential privacy using similar L₂ metrics. Since local privacy implies stronger constraints than standard differential privacy (as the adversary can observe all data), their lower bounds also apply to local privacy. The authors should clarify whether their bound improves upon these prior results for the local privacy setting. The omission is significant because the paper’s core contribution hinges on establishing *tight* bounds, and failing to contrast with existing differential privacy literature weakens this claim. I recommend adding citations to Hardt & Talwar (2010), De (2012), and Nikolov, Talwar, & Zhang (STOC 2013), which also addresses MSE for linear queries under differential privacy.

For the density estimation result, the comparison between private and non-private rates requires clarification. Equation (13) cites the non-private minimax rate as n⁻²ᵝ/(2ᵝ⁺¹), but it is unclear whether this rate holds for the trigonometric basis case defined in Definition 1 or the general Sobolev space. The lower bound (Theorem 2) assumes the trigonometric basis, so the comparison is only valid if Equation (13) also holds for this specific basis. The authors should explicitly confirm that the non-private lower bound (13) applies to the trigonometric basis (e.g., by citing standard results like Tsybakov’s work on Sobolev classes with trigonometric bases). Without this, the claim that the rate "degrades" from (13) to (14) is potentially misleading.

The paper is well-written and technically strong overall, but these omissions prevent it from fully realizing its potential as a definitive reference on privacy-constrained minimax rates. The connection to prior work on differential privacy lower bounds is critical for establishing novelty. The authors’ assertion that "the minimax bound for density estimation under privacy constraint has not been considered before" is questionable given the extensive literature on differential privacy and linear queries. While the local privacy setting is distinct, the paper should explicitly position its contribution relative to these works.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding citations to Hardt & Talwar (2010), De (2012), and Nikolov et al. (2013) in the introduction, clarifying how their results relate to the local privacy setting.
2. Explicitly verifying that the non-private rate (13) applies to the trigonometric basis used in Theorem 2 (e.g., by noting that the trigonometric basis satisfies the Sobolev smoothness conditions in Tsybakov 2009).
3. Refining the discussion of Warner’s optimality to emphasize why it *remains* optimal under local privacy despite decades of differential privacy research (e.g., by contrasting with non-local mechanisms that fail to achieve the same rate).

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of privacy-statistics trade-offs, but addressing these points would significantly enhance its impact and clarity. As written, it is a solid technical contribution with room for improved contextualization.
