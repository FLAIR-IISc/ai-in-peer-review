### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in the context of probability estimation. The authors adopt a minimax decision-theoretic framework to analyze estimation problems under the constraint of α-local differential privacy, a stringent model where each data point is privatized before being observed by the statistician.

The main contributions are twofold. First, for the problem of multinomial probability estimation, the paper derives sharp minimax rates of convergence for both L2 and L1 loss. The analysis shows that local privacy induces a penalty that effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `nα²/d`. A key consequence of this result is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, rate-optimal for this task. Second, the paper addresses the nonparametric problem of density estimation for functions within a Sobolev smoothness class. Here, the authors demonstrate that local privacy imposes a more severe penalty, changing the polynomial exponent in the convergence rate from `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` in the non-private case to `(nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. The paper complements these lower bounds by presenting computationally efficient algorithms—a private histogram estimator for Lipschitz densities and an orthogonal series estimator for smoother densities—that achieve these optimal rates.

The work aims to bridge the gap between classical privacy concepts, modern differential privacy theory, and statistical decision theory to provide a quantitative understanding of the costs of privacy.

**Strengths**

1.  **Fundamental and Significant Problem:** The paper addresses the critical problem of quantifying the statistical cost of privacy. Establishing a rigorous, theoretical understanding of the trade-off between data utility and privacy guarantees is of paramount importance to the machine learning community.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The use of minimax analysis (Le Cam's and Fano's methods) provides a powerful and principled approach. By deriving lower bounds, the authors characterize the fundamental limits of *any* estimation procedure operating under local privacy constraints, making the results strong and general.

3.  **Sharp and Insightful Results:** The derived rates are sharp and reveal important phenomena. The finding that randomized response is an optimal mechanism for multinomial estimation is an elegant and significant connection between classical and modern privacy literature. Furthermore, the discovery that local privacy alters the polynomial convergence rate for density estimation is a novel and non-obvious result that highlights the profound impact of the privacy constraint in nonparametric settings.

4.  **Constructive and Practical Algorithms:** The paper does not only provide lower bounds but also demonstrates their attainability with practical, computationally efficient algorithms. This makes the theoretical contributions more concrete and provides practitioners with optimal strategies for private data collection and analysis.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on the Privacy Model:** The paper focuses exclusively on the "local privacy" model. This is a critical distinction from the more commonly discussed "central" differential privacy model, and this distinction is not made sufficiently clear. The current phrasing can lead to confusion for readers not deeply familiar with the privacy literature. For example, the statement in Section 3.1, "...the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α…" is an overgeneralization, as the result is specific to the *local* model.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should explicitly define both local and central differential privacy in the introduction, clarify why the local model is chosen for this work, and maintain precise terminology throughout the paper. Additionally, clarifying the distinction between estimating population parameters (this work's focus) versus sample statistics (the focus of some prior work on differential privacy) at the beginning would better frame the paper's contribution.

2.  **Incomplete Discussion of Related Work:** The paper's connection to existing literature could be strengthened.
    *   **Suggestion:** The work of Lei on differentially private M-estimators [19] is highly relevant to the density estimation setting but is not cited or discussed. A comparison of the proposed density estimator with Lei's procedure would provide valuable context. While Lei's estimator is not locally private, discussing the differences in the privacy model and resulting convergence rates would be highly informative.
    *   **Suggestion:** For multinomial estimation, while randomized response is shown to be optimal, a brief discussion of how other private mechanisms for histogram estimation compare to this optimal rate would make the treatment more complete.

3.  **Scope of Theoretical Claims:**
    *   **Suggestion:** Theorem 1 restricts the analysis to α ∈ [0, 1]. It would be helpful if the authors commented on whether this is a necessary technical constraint or a choice for convenience. Since practical applications of differential privacy often consider α in this range (or slightly larger), understanding the bounds' behavior, particularly as α grows, is important.
    *   **Suggestion:** The framework is introduced with a general semi-metric ρ for measuring error. While the paper provides excellent results for L1 and L2 norms, a brief commentary on how other utility measures might be captured by the analysis could broaden the paper's perceived impact.

4.  **Originality in Context of a Pre-existing Manuscript:** The paper appears to be a condensed version of the authors' longer arXiv preprint [9]. A review of the preprint suggests that the core framework and the multinomial estimation results are already established there. This narrows the primary novel contribution of this NIPS submission to the sharp minimax bounds for density estimation.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should be more explicit about the relationship between this submission and their preprint to accurately represent the new contributions being made here.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a strong, theoretically grounded analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The results are significant, providing sharp minimax rates that elucidate the fundamental cost of privacy in both parametric and nonparametric settings. The weaknesses primarily relate to clarity, positioning with respect to related work, and contextualizing the novelty of the submission. These issues are addressable. The core technical contributions, particularly the new rates for density estimation, are of high quality and will likely be influential. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised above to improve clarity and properly situate their work within the field.

*(Disclaimer: This review is based on the main paper; the supplementary material was not examined in detail.)*
