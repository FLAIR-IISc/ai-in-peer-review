

**Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"**

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints, bridging minimax decision theory with differential privacy frameworks. The authors establish sharp minimax convergence rates for both multinomial and density estimation under α-local differential privacy, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The work builds on Warner's classical randomized response framework [23] and extends it to modern privacy models, while also connecting to recent advances in differential privacy [1, 9, 11, 13, 16, 24].

**Quality**  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution with well-supported theoretical results. The authors derive sharp minimax lower bounds for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy, complemented by matching upper bounds achieved through computationally efficient algorithms. The theoretical framework is carefully developed, with clear definitions of local privacy (equation 1) and minimax risk (equation 2). The connection between the authors' framework and Warner's work [23] is particularly compelling, demonstrating that randomized response achieves optimal sample complexity for multinomial estimation. The paper also properly distinguishes between population estimation (the focus here) and sample estimation (addressed in prior work), making a stronger theoretical contribution than previous differential privacy literature [2, 8, 16]. A minor concern is the restriction to α ∈ [0, 1] in Theorem 1; while the authors justify this as "sufficiently small," the practical relevance of this restriction is not thoroughly addressed, especially since α ≈ 1 is often the practical limit in differential privacy applications [11].

**Clarity**  
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical exposition. However, the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy (which the authors correctly use as "local differential privacy" in the body but sometimes conflate in the abstract and introduction) needs more careful explanation upfront. The statement on page 4 that "for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α..." is misleading—this is a local privacy result, not a differential privacy result, and the paper should avoid such conflation. The authors should clarify this distinction early in the paper, as it's critical for understanding the setting. The presentation of the density estimation results (Section 4) could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the rate differences between classical and private settings.

**Originality**  
The paper makes two significant contributions: (1) a careful theoretical exposition of the local privacy model and its connection to Warner's work, and (2) sharp minimax bounds for density estimation under local privacy. While the local privacy framework is related to [9, 13], the authors provide a more comprehensive theoretical treatment of minimax rates in this setting, which is novel for NIPS. The density estimation results (Theorem 2 and Propositions 2-3) are particularly original, showing a different convergence rate than classical results. However, the authors should more explicitly acknowledge the related work of Smith [24] and Lei [19] on differentially private density estimation, which addresses similar problems. The comparison to Lei's M-estimation procedure [19] would strengthen the paper's positioning.

**Significance**  
This work provides valuable theoretical insights into the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility. By characterizing the precise loss in statistical efficiency due to local privacy constraints, the paper offers concrete guidance for practitioners designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The finding that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation (a technique known for nearly 50 years) is particularly significant for practical survey design. The density estimation results also have important implications for nonparametric statistical inference under privacy constraints. The paper successfully demonstrates how to apply minimax theory to privacy-preserving statistics, advancing the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Strengths**  
- Sharp minimax bounds that bridge classical statistics with privacy
- Optimal rate analysis for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation
- Clear demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal
- Efficient algorithms achieving the theoretical bounds
- Careful distinction between population and sample estimation

**Weaknesses**  
- Conflation of "local privacy" and "differential privacy" in the abstract and introduction
- Insufficient discussion of the α ∈ [0, 1] restriction in Theorem 1
- Limited comparison to related work (Lei [19], Smith [24])
- Need for clearer explanation of the privacy-utility trade-off continuum
- Some technical details (e.g., the sampling strategy in Section 4.3) could be more accessible

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the intersection of statistics and privacy. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and significant for the field. While the paper could be strengthened by more careful exposition of the local privacy framework and better contextualization within the broader literature on differentially private statistics, these are relatively minor issues. The paper is suitable for acceptance at NIPS with the following revisions: (1) clarify the distinction between local privacy and differential privacy throughout the paper, (2) address the α restriction in Theorem 1, and (3) expand the discussion of related work to include Lei [19] and Smith [24]. With these revisions, this paper will be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
