- Paper derives sharp minimax bounds for probability estimation under local differential privacy for two problems: multinomial and non-parametric density estimation.  
- For multinomial estimation, α-local privacy effectively reduces sample size from n to about nα²/d, implying manageable statistical cost for small α.  
- Warner’s randomized response is shown to be minimax optimal for the multinomial problem.  
- For density estimation, privacy changes the minimax rate from n^(-2β/(2β+1)) to (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)), representing a fundamental change in the polynomial convergence exponent.  
- Theoretical contributions are technically sound, with rigorous proofs and correct application of minimax decision theory to local privacy.  
- Results are original and significant, notably the new sharp minimax rates for density estimation and the formal optimality proof for randomized response.  
- The paper deepens understanding of the limits of private learning and provides tools likely to be useful to future research.  
- Main weakness: insufficient discussion of how the multinomial lower bound relates to existing central differential privacy/linear query lower bounds (Hardt & Talwar; De; Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang).  
- Authors should clarify whether their local-privacy bounds improve upon, match, or are implied by known central-DP lower bounds and quantify any differences.  
- Clarify whether the classical non-private minimax rate cited applies to the same Sobolev function class (trigonometric basis) used for the private lower bound to ensure a fair comparison.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses a fundamental problem, provides novel sharp rates, significant density-estimation finding, and is technically solid and well-written.  
- Rejection/concern argument: omission of an adequate comparison with closely related central-DP lower-bound literature weakens contextualization of the contribution.  
- Recommendation: accept with revision — add a thorough comparison to central differential privacy lower bounds for multinomial estimation to strengthen the manuscript.
