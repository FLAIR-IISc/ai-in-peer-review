Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations 

The paper explores the concept of local privacy, where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of the other n-1 users, and reports this type to the data curator. The authors analyze this setting using the framework of min-max expected error, where the adversary selects a distribution over inputs (types for the n users) and we choose an α-DP local scheme to minimize the distance between an estimation derived from the original data and a similar estimation derived from the reported signals. Typically, these estimations involve sums, aggregations, or averages. The authors then establish a lower bound for the min-max rate for l_2 distance estimations and users drawn from a discrete distribution over d types. They demonstrate that the simple technique of randomized response achieves this bound, making it optimal. The authors further analyze the problem of density estimations, where the lower bound on the min-max rate is met by a perturbation scheme in which each person perturbs only their own type.

The paper is well-written and important, as it shows that classic procedures can be optimal, meeting certain lower bounds. I believe the NIPS community would find it interesting, and I therefore recommend acceptance.

However, I do have a few reservations. Firstly, the explanation of min-max bounds could be simplified. The paper also employs cumbersome notation, particularly since all bounds given apply to the l_2 norm; it would be more intuitive to phrase these bounds with respect to this norm. Secondly, I would appreciate a comment on the l_1 norm, which is more restrictive, or a comparison of the given bounds to l_1 norms, as well as other lower bounds in differential privacy (albeit in a classical, non-local setting). Lastly, the min-max bounds discussed seem tailored to this local-privacy setting. I wish the authors had considered a broader set of settings and provided min-max lower bounds for them as well. Nevertheless, I appreciate that the local setting allows users to randomize the type they report based on the remaining n-1 users, yet it turns out that the simple scheme in which each person randomizes the report solely based on their type is optimal. I suggest the authors explicitly state this in the text.

Upon further consideration, I have an additional reservation. Given that the list of FOCS 2013 accepted papers has been published, I now feel that the paper is an extension of an existing paper, particularly the results in Section 3. While I still recommend acceptance, my endorsement is not as strong as it was initially.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the local privacy framework using min-max rate bounds, establishing lower bounds on the rate and demonstrating that the upper bound is met by randomized response. This is a nice result.
