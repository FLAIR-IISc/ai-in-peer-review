- Paper studies estimation under non-interactive, memoryless local differential privacy, defines a private minimax risk, and derives sharp lower bounds with matching efficient procedures.
- For multinomial estimation over d categories, private MSE scales like d/(n α^2) for small α (effective sample size n α^2/d); an ℓ1 counterpart is given.
- Warner’s randomized response is shown to achieve the optimal multinomial rates (simple mechanism is minimax optimal).
- For Sobolev density estimation (smoothness β), the non-private rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} degrades to (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under local privacy (polynomial exponent changes).
- Matching constructions: privatized histogram is optimal for β=1; a bounded orthogonal-series estimator with a tailored local mechanism is optimal for general β ≥ 1.
- Naive Laplace perturbation of raw samples is strictly suboptimal for density estimation; authors explain why via classical deconvolution results.
- Techniques combine information-contraction bounds under local privacy with Le Cam/Fano testing tools, carefully using mutual information and total variation contraction.
- Constructive mechanisms are simple and implementable (randomized response, privatized histograms, tailored sampling for orthogonal series).
- Paper is generally well organized and technically sound, but the exposition is notation-heavy and could be streamlined for a broader audience.
- The authors should state earlier and more explicitly that simple per-user non-interactive randomization attains the optimal multinomial rate and clarify whether lower bounds extend to interactive local protocols.
- The combination of local differential privacy with minimax decision theory and the novel nonparametric exponent are original and insightful.
- Parts of Section 3 overlap with recent FOCS 2013 work, which reduces novelty for that section.
- Results are significant: they give clean quantitative privacy–efficiency trade-offs and emphasize problem-specific private mechanisms over naive additive noise.
- Pros: sharp lower/upper bounds in finite and nonparametric settings; randomized response optimality; novel density rate change; unifying theoretical framework.
- Cons: heavy notation and exposition; restricted to non-interactive local model; ℓ1 risk discussion could be more prominent; some overlap with FOCS 2013.
- Suggestions: streamline minimax/testing exposition and notation; explicitly highlight per-user randomized response optimality and clarify scope regarding interactive protocols; make ℓ1 comparisons clearer; contrast with central-model DP bounds.
- Recommendation: Accept — solid, well-supported contribution despite some overlap with FOCS work.
