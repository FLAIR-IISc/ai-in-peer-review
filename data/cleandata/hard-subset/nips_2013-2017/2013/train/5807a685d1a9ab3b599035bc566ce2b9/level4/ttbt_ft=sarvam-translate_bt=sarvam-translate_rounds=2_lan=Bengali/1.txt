Summary: This paper is a NIPS-format version of an ArXiV manuscript and sets a lower bound through a Fano/Lecam-style argument. This bound applies to algorithms that work on private data when the data holder does not trust the algorithm. Consequently, in some cases, randomized response is proven to be an optimal strategy.

A limitation of this review is that I did not see the additional materials.

Pros and Cons:
- The results highlight the limitations of learning from scattered data to ensure confidentiality.
- There is some ambiguity in the interpretation of the results that may confuse the average person (see below).
- There seems to be a lack of connection with existing results related to differential privacy.

Additional Comments:
- The local privacy constraint, which is important for the results, makes the privacy model quite different from the differential privacy model, which many readers may not realize. This confusion can be further enhanced by the statement given below on page 4: "So, for a sample size large enough for n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha\..."". The authors should avoid making such overly broad (and possibly incorrect) statements when describing their results.
- Is the alpha constraint necessary in Theorem 1? In particular, experimental results show that for some learning problems (under differential privacy), $\alpha \approx 1$ is most likely to be expected, so it is not clear what this limit tells us in this case.
- Some comments about the possible choice of ρ would be good, so that readers can see how different utility measures can be captured by analysis.
- How does this density assumption compare with Leigh's M-estimation method? This paper is not cited, but I think the authors should be aware of it.
- There are many histogram estimation methods for scattered data. How do these other algorithms fare where randomized response achieves the best convergence rate?

Post-Protest Addendum:
* I think the difference between population versus sample statistics needs to be explained more clearly at the beginning of the paper (see Rev.9's response).
* Comparison with relevant work (raised by Leigh and another reviewer) is important.
* A closer inspection of [10], which has now been published, forces me to explain the additional contribution of this paper more narrowly. Although the position of this and [10] (and therefore the audience) are different, the contribution of this paper is twofold: a careful explanation of the local privacy model and the setting of the density estimation boundary. The latter is new, but the former is essentially included in [10].
