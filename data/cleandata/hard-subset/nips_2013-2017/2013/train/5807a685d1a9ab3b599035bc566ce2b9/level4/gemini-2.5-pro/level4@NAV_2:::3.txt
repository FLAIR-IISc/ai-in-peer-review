This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of probability estimation under the stringent constraint of α-local differential privacy. The authors leverage the framework of minimax estimation to formally characterize the trade-off between statistical efficiency and the degree of privacy. The work centers on two primary contributions. First, for discrete probability estimation (the multinomial setting), the paper establishes sharp minimax rates, demonstrating that local privacy incurs a penalty that effectively reduces the sample size n to nα²/d. A significant consequence of this result is that it proves the minimax optimality of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism. Second, for the problem of nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes, the authors show that local privacy imposes a more severe cost, altering the polynomial rate of convergence from the standard n⁻²β/(²β+¹) to (nα²)⁻²β/(²β+²). These lower bounds are complemented by the construction of computationally efficient estimators, based on private histograms and orthogonal series, that achieve these new optimal rates.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Impact:** The paper makes a foundational contribution by formally connecting the modern framework of differential privacy with classical minimax statistical theory. This provides a principled and powerful way to quantify the fundamental costs of privacy, moving beyond algorithm-specific analyses to characterize the best possible performance for entire classes of problems.
*   **Technical Quality:** The technical work is impressive. The derivation of sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for both the multinomial and density estimation settings is a substantial technical achievement. The result establishing the optimality of randomized response is particularly elegant and insightful, connecting a 50-year-old technique to the cutting edge of privacy research.
*   **Originality:** The analysis for density estimation, which reveals not just a change in constants but a fundamental shift in the polynomial convergence rate, is a novel and important finding. It highlights that the impact of local privacy can be more profound in nonparametric settings. The privacy mechanisms proposed to attain the upper bounds are also novel and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The presentation of the minimax framework is quite dense and relies on heavy notation. While technically precise, this may limit the paper's accessibility to the broader NIPS audience. A more gentle introduction to the key concepts or a clearer high-level walkthrough of the proof techniques could significantly improve readability and impact.
*   **Originality Concerns:** There is a substantial overlap between this submission and the authors' publicly available work [9], especially for the multinomial estimation results in Section 3. While it is common for conference papers to have corresponding technical reports, the submission would be stronger if it more clearly articulated its specific contribution beyond what is available in the preprint.
*   **Contextualization:** The paper could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion connecting its findings to other lower bounds in the differential privacy literature, particularly those in the more common central (non-local) model. This comparison would help readers better isolate and understand the specific cost imposed by the *local* privacy constraint. Furthermore, a key insight is that simple, non-interactive mechanisms are optimal; this important takeaway deserves to be stated more explicitly in the text.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper addresses a timely and important problem with a rigorous and technically deep approach. The results are significant, establishing fundamental limits for private statistical estimation and providing optimal, practical mechanisms. The work is likely to be highly influential for future research at the intersection of privacy, statistics, and machine learning.
*   **Arguments against Acceptance:** The primary concern is the potential lack of novelty given the significant overlap with the authors' existing preprint. Additionally, the dense presentation may hinder its accessibility and impact at a broad conference like NIPS.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting important and well-executed research. The results provide fundamental insights into the statistical costs of local privacy. Despite some concerns regarding the presentation and novelty relative to the authors' concurrent work, the quality and significance of the contributions are high, and the paper would be a valuable addition to the conference.
