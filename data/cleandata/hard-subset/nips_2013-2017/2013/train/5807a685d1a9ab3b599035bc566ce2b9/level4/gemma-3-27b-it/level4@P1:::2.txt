This paper studies minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint that the estimation must preserve privacy of the individual data. The authors consider a privacy definition called local privacy. They study two probability estimation problems, multinomial estimation and density estimation. In both problems, the authors establish sharp minimax rates of convergence, demonstrating that local privacy leads to a reduction in the effective sample size—quadratic in the privacy parameter α for multinomial estimation—and a different convergence rate for density estimation compared to the non-private setting. Overall, the results are very interesting and, to the best of my understanding, technically sound. The minimax bound for density estimation under a privacy constraint appears to be a novel contribution.

My main comment concerns the connection to prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy. While the paper clearly positions itself within the context of local privacy, a more explicit discussion of related results from the broader differential privacy literature would be beneficial. Specifically, papers such as Hardt and Talwar (On the geometry of differential privacy, STOC 2010) and De (Lower bounds in differential privacy, TCC 2012) study worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under differential privacy. The multinomial estimation problem considered here can be viewed as a special case of the linear counting query analyzed in those works, using the same error metrics (L2). Given that local privacy imposes a stronger constraint than differential privacy, it is natural to ask whether the bounds presented in this paper improve upon existing bounds for differential privacy. Clarifying this relationship would strengthen the paper's contribution. Additionally, Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases, STOC 2013) also consider Mean Square Error, and mentioning this work would be beneficial.

Regarding the density estimation problem, the presentation of the result could be slightly more precise. The authors state that the lower bound in the local privacy setting is higher than the non-private setting; however, the minimax bound presented (Theorem 2) applies specifically to densities that can be expanded using a trigonometric basis. It would be helpful to clarify whether the non-private lower bound (equation 13) holds generally for the Sobolev space defined in Definition 1, or only when considering the trigonometric basis. A fair comparison necessitates specifying the conditions under which both lower bounds are valid.

In summary, this paper presents significant results regarding minimax bounds for private probability estimation. Addressing the connection to related work in differential privacy and clarifying the scope of the density estimation result would further enhance the paper’s impact and clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides sharp minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy, a challenging and important problem.
*   The results demonstrate a clear trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency.
*   The paper is technically sound and well-written.
*   The study of density estimation under privacy constraints appears to be novel.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper does not sufficiently acknowledge or discuss relevant results from the broader differential privacy literature.
*   The scope of the density estimation result could be more clearly defined.
