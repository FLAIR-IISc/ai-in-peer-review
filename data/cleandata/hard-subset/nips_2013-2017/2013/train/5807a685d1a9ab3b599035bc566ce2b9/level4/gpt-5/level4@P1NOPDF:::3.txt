Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

This paper studies local privacy, where each user produces an alpha-differentially private signal based on their own type (and potentially on the signals of the other n−1 users) and reports that signal to the data curator. The analysis uses a minimax expected error framework: an adversary chooses a distribution over inputs (the users’ types), and we choose an alpha-DP local mechanism to minimize the discrepancy between an estimator computed on the true data and the corresponding estimator computed on the privatized reports (typically sums, aggregates, or averages).

The authors present a lower bound on the minimax rate for l2 estimation when users are drawn from a discrete distribution over d types. They show that simple randomized response attains this bound and is therefore optimal. They then analyze density estimation and again establish a lower bound on the minimax rate that is matched by a mechanism in which each user perturbs only their own type.

Overall, this is a nice and important result: it shows that a classic procedure achieves the best possible rate by matching sharp lower bounds. I believe the NIPS community will find it interesting, and I recommend acceptance.

Reservations and suggestions:
- Exposition: The discussion of minimax bounds could be simplified. The notation is often cumbersome; since all bounds are stated for the l2 norm, it would be clearer to present them directly and consistently with respect to l2 throughout.
- Other norms and connections: I would have liked to see comments about the l1 norm, which is more stringent, or at least a discussion relating the provided bounds to analogous l1 guarantees. It would also help to connect these results to known lower bounds in differential privacy, even if those primarily concern the classical (non-local) setting.
- Generality: The minimax bounds developed here seem tailored to the local-privacy model considered. It would strengthen the paper to discuss broader problem classes or settings and, where possible, provide corresponding minimax lower bounds.
- Optimality of per-user randomization: Although the local model considered allows a user’s report to depend on the other n−1 users’ signals, it turns out that the simple mechanism in which each user randomizes their report solely based on their own type is optimal. This is an appealing and clarifying point; I recommend stating it explicitly in the paper.

New reservation: Since the list of FOCS 2013 accepted papers has now been published, it appears that this submission is in part an extension of existing work, particularly the results in Section 3. I therefore still recommend acceptance, but less strongly than before.

In summary, the paper analyzes local privacy through minimax rate bounds, establishes lower bounds, and shows that randomized response matches these rates, yielding optimal procedures. A solid and noteworthy contribution.
