

The paper investigates local differential privacy (LDP) in the context of minimax estimation, where each user independently generates an α-differentially private signal based solely on their own type (not others' signals), and the curator estimates population statistics from these reports. The authors establish minimax lower bounds for ℓ₂-error estimation of discrete distributions over *d* types and demonstrate that the classic randomized response mechanism achieves these bounds, proving its optimality for this setting. The analysis extends to density estimation, showing that user-specific perturbation (without dependence on other users' reports) also meets the minimax rate.  

This work makes a significant contribution by rigorously establishing sharp minimax rates for LDP, resolving the optimality of fundamental techniques. The results are theoretically clean and directly relevant to the NIPS community, as they clarify the fundamental limits of privacy-utility trade-offs in decentralized data collection. I recommend acceptance, though with noted reservations.  

First, the exposition of the minimax framework could be simplified. The paper’s heavy reliance on complex notation (e.g., for the adversary’s strategy and estimation error) obscures the core insights. Since all bounds apply to the ℓ₂ norm, the presentation should consistently emphasize this norm (e.g., replacing "l_2 distance estimations" with "ℓ₂-error estimation") to reduce clutter.  

Second, the omission of ℓ₁ analysis is notable. The ℓ₁ norm is more stringent in differential privacy contexts, and comparing the ℓ₂ bounds to known ℓ₁ lower bounds (e.g., from centralized DP) would strengthen the discussion. While the authors acknowledge the ℓ₂ focus, a brief comparative remark would contextualize their results within broader DP theory.  

Third, the minimax bounds are tightly tailored to LDP, but the paper does not explore whether these rates extend to related settings (e.g., centralized DP or hybrid models). A short discussion on the generality of the bounds would enhance the paper’s impact.  

A critical reservation emerged after reviewing the FOCS 2013 accepted papers: Section 3’s results on ℓ₂ minimax rates for discrete distributions appear to directly extend work in [FOCS 2013, Author et al.]. While the current paper refines the LDP context and provides new density estimation results, the core lower bound technique in Section 3 is not novel. This diminishes the paper’s incremental contribution. Nevertheless, the density estimation extension and explicit demonstration that user-independent perturbation suffices (a key insight the authors do not emphasize) remain valuable. The authors should explicitly state this optimality (i.e., "no coordination across users is needed for optimality") in the text.  

Overall, the paper delivers a precise analysis of LDP’s minimax limits with a clean optimality proof for randomized response. The technical contributions are sound, but the novelty is partially offset by FOCS 2013 overlap. I recommend acceptance, though with weaker enthusiasm than initially anticipated. The work is important for privacy theory, but the lack of ℓ₁ discussion and over-reliance on specialized notation slightly reduce its accessibility. A revised version addressing these points would strengthen the paper significantly.
