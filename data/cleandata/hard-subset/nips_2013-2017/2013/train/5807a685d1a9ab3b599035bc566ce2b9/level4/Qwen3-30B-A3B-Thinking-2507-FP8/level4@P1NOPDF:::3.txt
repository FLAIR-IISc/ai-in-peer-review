

Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation  
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
This paper investigates local differential privacy (LDP), where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based solely on their own type (not the signals of others) and reports it to a curator. The authors analyze the setting using minimax expected error: an adversary selects a distribution over user types, and the mechanism designer chooses an α-LDP scheme to minimize the L2 distance between an estimate derived from the original data and one from the reported signals (e.g., sums, averages). They establish a tight minimax lower bound for L2 estimation error when users are drawn from a discrete distribution over d types, demonstrating that the classic randomized response mechanism achieves this bound and is therefore optimal. The authors further extend this to density estimation, showing that a perturbation scheme where each user independently perturbs only their own type meets the lower bound.  

The work is significant and well-motivated: it establishes that randomized response is minimax-optimal for LDP in these settings, a result of clear interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.  

However, I have three concerns. First, the exposition of minimax bounds is overly technical; simplifying the presentation would improve accessibility. Second, the paper consistently uses L2 norms but employs cumbersome notation (e.g., generic "ℓ" without specifying the norm). Given all bounds apply to L2, phrasing them explicitly with respect to this norm would clarify the results. Third, the authors omit discussion of L1 norms, which are more restrictive in differential privacy and relevant to prior work. A brief comparison to L1 or other standard DP lower bounds (even if derived for non-local settings) would strengthen the context.  

A related point: the paper demonstrates that optimal LDP mechanisms need not leverage dependencies between users’ signals (unlike some non-local settings), but this key insight is not explicitly stated. Highlighting that "independent perturbation per user suffices for optimality" would enhance clarity.  

Finally, I note that the results in Section 3 appear to extend prior work in FOCS 2013 (now published), diminishing the novelty somewhat. While the paper remains valuable and the extension is non-trivial, this reduces my enthusiasm for acceptance compared to a novel contribution.  

In summary: the paper delivers a clean, important result showing randomized response’s optimality for LDP in L2 estimation and density estimation. The technical arguments are sound, and the contribution is relevant to NIPS. With minor revisions addressing notation clarity and the L1/L2 context, it would be a strong fit. I recommend acceptance, though with less enthusiasm than initially anticipated.
