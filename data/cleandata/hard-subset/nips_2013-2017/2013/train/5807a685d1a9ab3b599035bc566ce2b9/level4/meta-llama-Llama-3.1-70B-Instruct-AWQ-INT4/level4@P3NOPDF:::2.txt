This paper explores minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint of preserving individual data privacy, specifically using the local privacy definition. The authors examine two probability estimation problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. They establish sharp minimax rates of convergence for both problems, demonstrating that local privacy reduces the effective sample size quadratically with respect to the privacy parameter alpha in the discrete multinomial estimation problem. Since alpha is often a small constant, the effective sample size remains of the same order as the non-private case. In contrast, the authors show that the optimal rate for the non-private setting is no longer attainable when preserving local privacy in the density estimation problem.

The results presented in this paper are highly interesting, and upon reviewing the proofs, they appear to be correct. To the best of my knowledge, the minimax bound for density estimation under a privacy constraint has not been previously considered. 

One of my primary comments is that the minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely related to previous works on noise complexity for differential privacy. However, the authors do not adequately acknowledge this connection. In particular, two papers, "On the geometry of differential privacy" by Hardt and Talwar (STOC, 2010) and "Lower bounds in differential privacy" by De (TCC, 2012), consider highly relevant problems. These papers study worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under the constraint of differential privacy. The probability estimation problem examined in this paper is a special case of the linear counting query studied in those papers, and the error measures used are the same L2 metric. The primary difference lies in the fact that this paper focuses on local differential privacy, whereas Hardt, Talwar, and De consider differential privacy. Since local privacy imposes a stronger constraint than differential privacy, lower bounds for differential privacy are also applicable to local privacy. I would like to see a comparison of the bound for local privacy presented in this paper to previous bounds for differential privacy to determine if it offers any improvements.

Regarding the density estimation problem, the statement of the result is somewhat unclear. The authors claim that the lower bound in the local privacy setting is higher than in the non-private setting. However, the minimax bound established in this paper is specific to the case where the density can be expanded using a trigonometric basis. I would like to know whether the lower bound for the non-private setting, as given in equation (13), holds for the general Sobolev space defined in Definition 1 or only for the special case of a trigonometric basis. A fair comparison can only be made if the non-private lower bound is applicable to the trigonometric basis.

In response to the rebuttal, the authors partially clarify the relationship to previous worst-case lower bounds. Nevertheless, I would like to point out that the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, "The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases" (STOC 2013), also considers Mean Square Error. I suggest that the authors include their explanations and relevant references in the paper.

Overall, this paper proves sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function (pmf) and probability density function (pdf) estimation with a privacy guarantee. The results are interesting, and the paper is well-written. However, the authors should address the lack of mention regarding known results on noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy, which are highly relevant to this paper.
