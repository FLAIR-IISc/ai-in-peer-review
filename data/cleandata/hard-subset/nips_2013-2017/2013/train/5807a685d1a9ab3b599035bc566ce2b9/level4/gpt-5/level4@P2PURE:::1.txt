SUMMARY: This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript. It uses a Fano/Le Cam-style argument to derive lower bounds for estimation algorithms operating on privatized data in a setting where the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder (local privacy). As a corollary, randomized response is shown to be optimal in a certain sense. As a caveat, I did not read the supplementary material.

Pros and cons:
- The results characterize the limits of learning from data perturbed to guarantee privacy.
- Some of the commentary is imprecise and may confuse a casual reader (see below).
- Connections to existing results on differential privacy seem to be missing.

Additional comments:
- The restriction to local privacy—crucial for the results—makes the privacy model quite different from the standard (central) differential privacy model, a distinction many readers may not appreciate. This confusion is heightened by statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4: “Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level alpha …” The paper should avoid overly broad (and potentially incorrect) statements when describing its results.
- Is the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 necessary? Experimental evidence suggests that alpha approximately 1 may be the best achievable for certain learning problems under differential privacy, so it is unclear what the bound says about this regime.
- Some commentary on reasonable choices of rho would help readers see how different utility measures are captured by the analysis.
- How does the proposed density estimator compare to Lei’s M-estimation procedure? This related work is not cited but seems relevant.
- There are many other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. Although randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, how do these other algorithms compare?

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:
- The distinction between population quantities and sample statistics should be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (see the response to Reviewer 9).
- A comparison to related work (Lei and the papers noted by another reviewer) is important for context.
- A closer look at [10], which has now appeared, leads me to view the additional contribution of this paper more narrowly. While the venues (and audiences) differ, the paper’s contributions are twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model and bounds for density estimation. The latter appear to be new, but the former is essentially contained in [10].

In sum, this NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript employs a Le Cam-style argument to establish lower bounds for estimators on privatized data in the untrusted-algorithm setting, and it identifies randomized response as optimal in a certain sense.
