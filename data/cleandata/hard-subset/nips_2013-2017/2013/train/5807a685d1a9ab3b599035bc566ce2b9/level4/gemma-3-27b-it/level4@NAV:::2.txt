This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation when individual data privacy is a key constraint, specifically employing the framework of local privacy. The authors analyze two fundamental problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation, establishing precise minimax rates of convergence for both. Their work demonstrates that, in the multinomial estimation scenario, local privacy incurs a reduction in effective sample size proportional to the square of the privacy parameter, alpha. Given that alpha is often a small constant, this reduction is often manageable and comparable to the non-private case. However, for density estimation, the authors show that achieving the optimal convergence rate attainable in the non-private setting becomes impossible under the constraint of local privacy.

Overall, the results are compelling and the proofs appear sound. To the best of my knowledge, a minimax bound for density estimation under privacy constraints has not been previously established.

My primary concern lies with the multinomial estimation analysis, which bears a strong connection to existing research on noise complexity for differential privacy. The paper lacks acknowledgement of this relationship, particularly concerning the work of Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012). These papers derive worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem studied here can be viewed as a specific instance of the linear counting queries investigated in those works, utilizing the same L2 error metric.  Since local privacy imposes a more stringent condition than differential privacy, lower bounds for differential privacy also apply to local privacy. Therefore, it would be valuable to understand whether the local privacy bound presented here offers any improvement over existing differential privacy bounds. 

Regarding the density estimation results, the presentation of the lower bound is somewhat unclear. The authors claim a higher lower bound in the local privacy setting compared to the non-private setting, but the presented minimax bound applies specifically to densities expressible with a trigonometric basis.  It is crucial to clarify whether the non-private minimax bound (eq. 13) holds generally for the Sobolev space defined in Definition 1, or only for the restricted case of trigonometric bases, as a fair comparison necessitates the latter.

(Following Rebuttal) The rebuttal provides partial clarification regarding the connection to existing lower bound results. However, the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also considers Mean Square Error, should also be acknowledged. I recommend that the authors incorporate these explanations and the missing references into the revised manuscript.

In summary, this paper presents valuable and well-written results concerning sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function and probability density function estimation with privacy guarantees. However, the omission of relevant prior work on noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy is a significant oversight.
