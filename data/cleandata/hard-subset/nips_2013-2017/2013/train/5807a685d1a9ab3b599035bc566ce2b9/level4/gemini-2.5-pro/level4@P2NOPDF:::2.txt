This paper investigates the minimax rates for probability estimation under the stringent constraint of local privacy. The authors derive sharp minimax bounds for two fundamental problems: multinomial parameter estimation and non-parametric density estimation. The results are technically interesting and, to my knowledge, the analysis of density estimation under local privacy is novel. While the paper is well-written and the proofs appear correct, its primary weakness is an insufficient engagement with closely related prior work on lower bounds in differential privacy.

**Summary of Contributions**

The paper establishes sharp minimax rates of convergence for two distinct estimation tasks under local privacy.
1.  **Multinomial Estimation:** The authors demonstrate that enforcing local privacy results in a reduction of the effective sample size that is quadratic in the privacy parameter, α. This implies that for a constant privacy parameter, the convergence rate remains of the same order as in the non-private case.
2.  **Density Estimation:** For this problem, the authors show that the optimal rate achievable in the non-private setting is no longer attainable when local privacy must be preserved, highlighting a fundamental cost of privacy in this context.

**Major Comments**

My main concerns relate to the positioning of these results within the existing literature and the clarity of a key comparison.

1.  **Relation to Lower Bounds in Central Differential Privacy:**
    The analysis of the multinomial estimation problem is closely related to previous work on the noise complexity of differential privacy, yet the paper fails to cite or discuss these crucial connections. Specifically, the problem of estimating a probability distribution is a special case of the linear counting queries studied in the following papers:
    *   Hardt & Talwar, "On the geometry of differential privacy," STOC, 2010.
    *   De, "Lower bounds in differential privacy," TCC, 2012.
    *   Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, "The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases," STOC 2013.

    These works study worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under the constraint of (central) differential privacy, using the same L2 metric (or Mean Square Error) as this paper. The primary distinction is that this paper considers *local* differential privacy, whereas the cited works focus on *central* differential privacy. Since local privacy is a stronger constraint, any lower bound established for central differential privacy automatically applies to the local privacy setting.

    This raises a critical question: Does the minimax bound derived here for local privacy improve upon the existing lower bounds for central differential privacy? The authors should explicitly address this. A thorough discussion comparing their bounds to those in the aforementioned papers is necessary to properly contextualize their contribution. I strongly suggest adding these references and the corresponding analysis to the paper.

2.  **Clarity of Comparison in Density Estimation:**
    The presentation of the density estimation result is potentially confusing. The authors claim that the lower bound in the local privacy setting is strictly higher than the optimal rate in the non-private setting. However, the new minimax bound is derived for the specific case where the density function can be expanded with a trigonometric basis. For the comparison to be valid, the non-private lower bound cited in equation (13) must also be restricted to this same class of functions.

    It is unclear whether the non-private bound in eq. (13) holds for the general Sobolev space (as introduced in Definition 1) or specifically for the subclass of functions with a trigonometric basis. This is a crucial detail, as the comparison is only meaningful if both bounds apply to the same function class. The authors should clarify the scope of the non-private lower bound to ensure the comparison is on an "apples-to-apples" basis.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents valuable and interesting results on sharp minimax bounds for estimation problems under local privacy. However, its contribution is diminished by the lack of discussion of highly relevant prior work on lower bounds in central differential privacy. I recommend that the authors thoroughly address the relationship with the cited literature and clarify the basis of comparison in the density estimation section. Incorporating this discussion would significantly strengthen the paper and more clearly delineate its contributions to the field.
