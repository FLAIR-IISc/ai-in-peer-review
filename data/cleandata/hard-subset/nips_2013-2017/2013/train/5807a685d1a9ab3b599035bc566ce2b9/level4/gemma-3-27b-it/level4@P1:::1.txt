SUMMARY: This paper presents a detailed analysis of probability estimation—both discrete and continuous—under stringent privacy constraints. Utilizing a minimax framework and tools from differential privacy, the authors derive sharp rates of convergence for estimation, highlighting the inherent trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. A key result demonstrates the optimality of Warner’s randomized response method for survey sampling while maintaining respondent privacy.

As a caveat, this review does not cover the supplementary material.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

This work provides a valuable characterization of the limitations imposed by privacy when learning from perturbed data. The lower bounds established offer important insights into the fundamental costs of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The connection to and reaffirmation of the optimality of Warner’s randomized response is a noteworthy result, demonstrating that a decades-old method remains competitive even within a modern theoretical framework.

However, the paper suffers from some imprecision in its discussion of the results, which could lead to misinterpretations. Additionally, the relationship to existing work on differential privacy could be made more explicit.

**Additional Comments:**

The focus on *local* privacy, while crucial for the presented results, establishes a privacy model significantly different from the more commonly studied *differential privacy* model. This distinction may not be immediately apparent to all readers. The authors should exercise caution when making broader statements about "differential privacy" (e.g., bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α…"). Such statements risk being misleading.

It would be beneficial to clarify whether the restriction on α in Theorem 1 is necessary. Experimental results in other contexts suggest that α ≈ 1 may represent an upper limit for practical application of differential privacy techniques, and it is unclear whether the bound provides meaningful information in this regime.

Expanding on the possible choices for the metric ρ would be valuable, allowing readers to understand how different utility measures can be incorporated into the analysis.

The paper would benefit from a discussion of how the proposed density estimator compares to related work, such as the M-estimation procedure presented by Lei [19]. This relevant work is currently not cited. Additionally, a comparison to other histogram estimation approaches for discrete data would strengthen the analysis of randomized response. While randomized response achieves the optimal convergence rate, contrasting its performance with alternative algorithms would provide a more comprehensive understanding.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding the trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy.
*   Establishes optimality results for a classic privacy-preserving technique (randomized response).
*   Contributes to the growing body of work connecting differential privacy and statistical decision theory.
*   Well-written and clearly presents a complex set of results, albeit with some areas for improvement.

**Con:**

*   Potential for misinterpretation due to imprecise language and conflation of local and differential privacy.
*   Limited discussion of related work, particularly more recent contributions in the field.
*   The significance of the bounds in specific regimes (e.g., α ≈ 1) is unclear.
*   The reliance on technical notation and a somewhat specialized framework might limit broader accessibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While some clarifications and additions would enhance its impact, the rigorous analysis and valuable insights justify acceptance to NIPS, contingent upon addressing the minor concerns raised above. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature and provide a compelling argument for the importance of their work.
