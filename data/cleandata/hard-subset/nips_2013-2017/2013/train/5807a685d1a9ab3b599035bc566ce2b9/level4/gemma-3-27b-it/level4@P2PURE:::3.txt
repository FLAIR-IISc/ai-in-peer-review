Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This paper investigates local privacy, a scenario where each user provides an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of others, reporting this signal to a data curator. The authors analyze this setting through the lens of minimax expected error – an adversary chooses a distribution over user inputs (types), and a differentially private (DP) local privacy mechanism is selected to minimize the distance between estimations derived from the original data and those derived from the reported signals (typically sums, averages, or aggregations). They establish a lower bound on the minimax rate for l₂ distance estimation when user types are drawn from a discrete distribution over *d* types, demonstrating that the simple randomized response technique achieves this bound and is therefore optimal. The paper further analyzes density estimation, finding that a perturbation scheme where each user perturbs only their own type also meets the corresponding lower bound on the minimax rate.

This is a valuable and important contribution, showing that established procedures are, in certain cases, the best achievable due to their alignment with fundamental lower bounds. I believe the NIPS community would find this work interesting and recommend acceptance.

However, I do have several reservations. First, the explanation of minimax bounds could be more accessible; its current presentation is somewhat complex. The paper also employs a considerable amount of cumbersome notation. Given that all bounds are presented for the l₂ norm, could they not be consistently expressed with respect to this norm? Second, a discussion of the more restrictive l₁ norm, or a comparison of the derived bounds to those achievable under l₁ norms, would be beneficial. Similarly, relating these results to existing lower bounds in the broader field of differential privacy – even acknowledging the differences arising from the non-local, classical setting – would strengthen the paper. Finally, the minimax bounds appear specifically tailored to this local privacy setting. It would be valuable if the authors considered a wider range of settings and presented minimax lower bounds for those as well. On a positive note, the finding that, despite the local setting allowing users to randomize their reported type based on the signals of others, the simple scheme of individual randomization based solely on one’s own type is optimal is a significant result. I recommend the authors explicitly state this finding within the text. 

An additional concern has arisen since the publication of the FOCS 2013 accepted papers. The current work now appears to be an extension of existing results, particularly those presented in Section 3. Therefore, I still recommend acceptance, but with somewhat less enthusiasm than before.

In summary, the paper analyzes local privacy using minimax rate bounds, provides lower bounds on the rate, and demonstrates that randomized response achieves these bounds – a commendable accomplishment.
