This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of work previously available on ArXiv, employing a Fano/LeCam-style argument to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on private data under the assumption of an untrusted algorithm. A key finding is that randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy within this framework. 

Please note this review does not cover the supplementary material.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

* The results effectively characterize fundamental limitations inherent in learning from privacy-perturbed data.
* The presentation contains some imprecision in its interpretation of the results, potentially leading to misunderstanding among readers (details below).
* The paper lacks sufficient connection to, and discussion of, existing work in differential privacy.

**Additional Comments:**

* The focus on local privacy, crucial to the results, defines a privacy model distinct from the more common differential privacy model. This distinction may not be readily apparent to all readers. Statements like those on page 4 – “Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…” – risk overgeneralization and potential inaccuracy, and should be carefully revised.
* The constraint on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 warrants further investigation. Given that empirical results suggest $\alpha \approx 1$ may represent an upper bound for certain learning problems (under differential privacy), the relevance of the bound in this regime is unclear.
* Providing guidance on selecting appropriate values for $\rho$ would enhance the paper’s utility, allowing readers to understand how different utility measures can be accommodated within the analysis.
* A comparison to the M-estimation procedure proposed by Lei is necessary. While not currently cited, this work appears relevant and the authors should demonstrate awareness of it.
* The paper focuses on randomized response for histogram estimation. A discussion of how other algorithms for discrete data estimation perform relative to this optimal rate would be valuable.

**Addendum Following Rebuttal:**

* The distinction between population and sample statistics requires clearer and more explicit explanation early in the paper (as noted in the response to Rev.9).
* A more thorough comparison to related work, including that of Lei and other works raised by reviewers, is essential for providing appropriate context.
* Following the appearance of [10], I now view the paper’s contribution as more focused. While the target audiences differ, this work primarily offers a careful exposition of the local privacy model and provides bounds for density estimation. The latter is novel, but the former is largely contained within [10].
