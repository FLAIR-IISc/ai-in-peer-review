- Studies sharp minimax bounds for probability estimation under a stringent local privacy model where data remains private even from the statistician.  
- Shows local privacy creates a fundamental privacy–efficiency trade-off in estimation.  
- For multinomial estimation, local privacy reduces effective sample size from n to nα²/d.  
- For β-smooth density estimation, the convergence rate shifts from n⁻²β/(2β+1) (non-private) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2) under local privacy.  
- Proves Warner’s randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation, highlighting a 50-year-old method’s modern optimality.  
- Bridges classical privacy techniques (Warner) with modern differential privacy frameworks.  
- Provides rigorous minimax lower and matching upper bounds with computationally efficient algorithms.  
- Technical proofs appear sound and reproducible for expert readers; mathematical notation and exposition are clear.  
- Original contribution: first to establish sharp minimax rates for local-privacy probability estimation.  
- Significant implications for privacy-preserving statistical analysis and survey sampling practice.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion and explicit comparison to prior noise-complexity results in differential privacy (e.g., Hardt & Talwar [16], De [8], Nikolov et al. [21]).  
- Weakness: ambiguity in the density-estimation comparison — unclear whether the non-private lower bound assumes the same trigonometric basis used in the paper.  
- Suggested revision 1: add thorough comparisons to differential-privacy noise-complexity literature and clarify how bounds differ or improve.  
- Suggested revision 2: clarify the basis assumptions for the non-private density lower bound and ensure comparisons are valid.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper, recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing related-work and density-estimation clarity.
