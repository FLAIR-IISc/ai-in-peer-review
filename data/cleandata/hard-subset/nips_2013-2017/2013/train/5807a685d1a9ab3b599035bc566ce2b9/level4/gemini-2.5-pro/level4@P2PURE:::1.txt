**SUMMARY**

This paper presents a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive a minimax lower bound for estimation algorithms under the local privacy model, where data holders do not trust the algorithm collecting their data. As a corollary, the analysis demonstrates that randomized response is an optimal strategy in this setting, as it achieves the derived lower bound.

Note: This review is based on the main paper and does not cover the supplementary material.

**PROS AND CONS**

*   **Pro:** The results characterize the fundamental limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee local privacy.
*   **Con:** The commentary surrounding the results contains some imprecision that could confuse readers, particularly those less familiar with the nuances of different privacy models.
*   **Con:** The paper does not adequately discuss connections to existing results in the broader differential privacy literature.

**ADDITIONAL COMMENTS**

*   The local privacy model is a core assumption of this work and differs significantly from the more common central model of differential privacy. This distinction is crucial but may not be apparent to all readers. This potential for confusion is exacerbated by broad statements, such as the one at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…" The authors should be more precise in their language to avoid conflating local and central privacy models.
*   Is the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 necessary? Much of the empirical work in differential privacy uses $\alpha \approx 1$, and it is unclear what the theorem implies for these practical parameter regimes.
*   A discussion on the possible choices for the loss function $\rho$ would be beneficial. This would help readers understand how the analysis can be applied to different utility measures.
*   How does the proposed density estimator compare to the M-estimation procedure of Lei? This highly relevant work is not cited.
*   While randomized response is shown to achieve the optimal rate of convergence, a comparison with other histogram estimation methods for discrete data would provide valuable context and strengthen the paper.

**ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL**

*   The distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (as noted in the authors' response to Reviewer 9).
*   A comparison to related work, including the work of Lei and other methods raised during the review process, is important for properly contextualizing the paper's contributions.
*   A closer inspection of reference [10], which has now been published, narrows the perceived contribution of this manuscript. While this paper and [10] are for different venues and audiences, the contribution here is twofold: an exposition of the local privacy model and bounds for density estimation. The latter appears to be new, but the former is largely contained in [10]. This should be acknowledged.
