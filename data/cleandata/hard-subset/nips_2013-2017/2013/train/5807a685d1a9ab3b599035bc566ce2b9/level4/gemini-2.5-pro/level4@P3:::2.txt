This paper provides a detailed, decision-theoretic study of probability estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. The authors use a minimax framework to derive sharp convergence rates for two fundamental statistical problems: estimating a discrete probability mass function (multinomial estimation) and estimating a continuous probability density function.

For the d-dimensional multinomial estimation problem, the authors demonstrate that imposing α-local privacy reduces the effective sample size from `n` to `n * α^2 / d`. A key consequence of their analysis is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal. For the problem of nonparametric density estimation over a Sobolev smoothness class `F_β`, the paper shows a more significant degradation in performance. The optimal non-private convergence rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` is shown to be unattainable; instead, the sharp minimax rate under local privacy is `(n * α^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. The authors complement these lower bounds with computationally efficient estimation procedures that achieve the optimal rates.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper forges a strong connection between the rigorous framework of minimax statistical decision theory and the modern cryptographic notion of differential privacy. This provides a formal basis for quantifying the fundamental trade-off between statistical utility and privacy. To my knowledge, the derivation of sharp minimax rates for density estimation under local privacy is a novel contribution.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and appear to be technically sound. By providing both lower bounds and matching upper bounds achieved by explicit algorithms, the authors establish the "sharpness" of their rates, offering a complete picture of the estimation limits for these problems.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It effectively introduces the concepts of local privacy and the minimax framework before applying them to the specific estimation problems.
*   **Impact:** The finding that Warner's randomized response is optimal is an elegant and impactful result, linking a foundational technique in survey privacy to the state-of-the-art definition of differential privacy.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
The primary weakness of the paper is the insufficient discussion of its relationship to prior work on lower bounds for differential privacy, particularly from the theoretical computer science literature. This is most apparent in the analysis of the multinomial estimation problem.

1.  **Relation to Lower Bounds for Linear Queries:** The problem of estimating the probabilities in a d-dimensional multinomial is equivalent to answering a set of `d` linear counting queries on the data. There is a line of work that establishes lower bounds on the error for answering such queries under the constraint of (central) differential privacy. Key papers in this area include Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010), De (TCC 2012), and more recently Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which specifically considers Mean Squared Error.

    While the present work considers *local* differential privacy, which is a stronger constraint than central differential privacy, any lower bound for the central model also serves as a lower bound for the local one. It is therefore crucial for the authors to clarify how their lower bound for multinomial estimation compares to, and potentially improves upon, the bounds established in this prior work. Does the local privacy setting permit a provably stronger (i.e., higher) lower bound on the error, and if so, by what factor? A detailed comparison is needed to properly situate the paper's contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Comparison in Density Estimation:** For the density estimation results, the comparison to the classical non-private rate in Equation (13) would be clearer if the authors explicitly confirmed that this classical rate holds for the specific subclass of Sobolev spaces defined by the trigonometric basis used in Theorem 2. While this is likely the case, stating it explicitly would confirm that the comparison is being made on the exact same function class, ensuring a fair assessment of the privacy-induced penalty.

**Summary of Recommendation:**
This paper presents significant and technically strong results on the fundamental limits of statistical estimation under local privacy constraints. The analysis is rigorous, and the conclusions, particularly regarding the optimality of randomized response and the change in convergence rates for density estimation, are insightful.

However, the contribution is somewhat undermined by a lack of engagement with a highly relevant body of literature on lower bounds for differential privacy. Addressing the connection to prior work on linear query lower bounds is essential for understanding the precise novelty of the multinomial estimation result.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first sharp minimax analysis for nonparametric density estimation under local privacy.
*   The results are technically strong, with matching upper and lower bounds.
*   The paper is well-written and connects two important fields in a principled way.
*   The finding on the optimality of randomized response is an elegant and valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Fails to discuss and compare its results to a significant line of highly relevant prior work on lower bounds for differentially private query release, making it difficult to assess the novelty of the multinomial estimation bound.

Overall, I am positive about the paper but believe its value would be substantially increased if the authors address the missing references and clarify the relationship of their work to existing lower bounds.
