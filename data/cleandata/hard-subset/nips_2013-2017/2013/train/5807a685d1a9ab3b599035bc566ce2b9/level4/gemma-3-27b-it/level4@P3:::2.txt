This paper studies minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint of individual data privacy, employing the concept of local privacy. The authors investigate two key problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation, deriving sharp minimax rates of convergence for both. They demonstrate that, in multinomial estimation, local privacy leads to a reduction in effective sample size that is quadratic in the privacy parameter, α. Importantly, they show this reduction is manageable when α is a small constant. For density estimation, the authors establish that the optimal convergence rate achievable in the non-private setting is unattainable when local privacy is enforced.

Overall, this is a strong and interesting paper. The proofs appear technically sound, and to the best of my knowledge, the minimax bounds for density estimation under a privacy constraint represent a novel contribution. The paper successfully integrates concepts from differential privacy and minimax theory, providing a valuable theoretical understanding of the trade-offs involved in privacy-preserving statistical inference.

However, the connection to existing work on differential privacy and noise complexity, particularly for linear queries, could be strengthened. While the authors acknowledge related literature, the discussion lacks depth regarding relevant lower bounds, such as those presented in Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012). These papers establish worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under differential privacy. The multinomial estimation problem examined in this paper can be viewed as a specific instance of a linear counting query, and the error metrics (L2 distance) are consistent. While local privacy imposes a stricter constraint than standard differential privacy, it's crucial to clarify whether the bounds achieved here improve upon existing results for differential privacy, and to discuss this relationship explicitly. Additionally, the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013) considering Mean Square Error should also be acknowledged.

Regarding the density estimation result, the presentation could be clarified. The authors note a divergence from the classical rate, but the minimax bound provided (and the comparison made) appears to be specifically for densities expressible in a trigonometric basis. It would be beneficial to clarify whether the classical minimax rate quoted in equation (13) holds generally for the Sobolev space defined in Definition 1, or only for the trigonometric basis case. A fair comparison requires ensuring the non-private lower bound is applicable to the same function class.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The derivation of sharp minimax bounds for density estimation under local privacy is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proofs appear rigorous and well-structured.
*   **Theoretical Insight:** The paper provides a clear and formal understanding of the privacy-utility trade-off in probability estimation.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The connection to existing work on differential privacy and noise complexity, especially for linear queries, is underdeveloped.
*   **Clarification Needed for Density Estimation:** The applicability of the non-private lower bound (eq. 13) to the specific function class used in the analysis requires further clarification.
*   **Potential for Improved Bounds:** Exploring connections with related work may reveal opportunities to refine the bounds presented.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in statistical inference – privacy-preserving estimation.
*   The results are technically sound and represent a novel contribution to the field.
*   The paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of a thorough discussion of related work weakens the paper's overall impact.
*   The ambiguity surrounding the density estimation results raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison to non-private benchmarks.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should expand the discussion of related work, particularly regarding differential privacy and noise complexity for linear queries. They should also clarify the scope of the non-private lower bound used in the density estimation analysis. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field.
