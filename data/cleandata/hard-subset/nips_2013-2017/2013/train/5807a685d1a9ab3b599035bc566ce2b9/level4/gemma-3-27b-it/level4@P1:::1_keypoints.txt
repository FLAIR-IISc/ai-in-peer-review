- Paper analyzes discrete and continuous probability estimation under local privacy using a minimax framework and differential-privacy tools, deriving sharp convergence rates.
- Establishes lower bounds that quantify fundamental costs of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
- Proves optimality of Warner’s randomized response for private survey sampling.
- Reinforces that a decades-old method remains competitive in modern theoretical settings.
- Review excludes the supplementary material.
- Discussion contains imprecisions that could lead to misinterpretation, especially conflating local privacy with the more common differential-privacy model.
- Relationship to existing differential-privacy literature is not made sufficiently explicit.
- Authors should be cautious about broad statements regarding “differential privacy” that may not apply to local privacy (e.g., bottom of page 4).
- Unclear whether the restriction on α in Theorem 1 is necessary; practical regime α ≈ 1 may limit the bound’s relevance.
- Recommend expanding on possible choices for the metric ρ to show how different utility measures fit the analysis.
- Suggest comparing the proposed density estimator to related work (e.g., Lei [19]) and citing omitted relevant papers.
- Recommend empirical or theoretical comparison of randomized response to alternative histogram/estimation approaches for discrete data.
- Pros for acceptance: rigorous theoretical foundation, optimality results for randomized response, strong connection between privacy and statistical decision theory, generally well-written.
- Cons against acceptance: risk of misinterpretation from imprecise language and model conflation, limited related-work discussion, unclear significance in specific α regimes, accessibility limited by technical notation.
- Overall assessment: solid theoretical contribution deserving acceptance provided the authors address the noted clarifications and citations.
