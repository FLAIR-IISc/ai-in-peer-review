This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of work initially appearing on arXiv, leveraging a Fano/LeCam-style argument to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms applied to private data under the assumption of a distrustful algorithm designer. A notable consequence of this work is the identification of randomized response as an optimal strategy within this framework. 

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

The paper's primary strength lies in its characterization of the fundamental limitations inherent in learning from privacy-perturbed data. However, certain imprecisions in the discussion of results could potentially mislead readers (details below). Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough engagement with existing literature on differential privacy. 

**Detailed Comments:**

The focus on *local* privacy, crucial for the theoretical results, creates a privacy model substantially different from the more commonly known differential privacy model. Many readers may not fully appreciate this distinction. Statements like the one on page 4 (“Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…”) risk exacerbating this confusion and should be carefully rephrased to avoid potentially incorrect generalizations. The authors should clearly delineate the specific privacy guarantees offered by their local privacy model.

I question whether the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 is strictly necessary. Empirical results in the differential privacy literature suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ often represents the practical limit for certain learning problems. Consequently, it remains unclear if the derived bound provides meaningful insight into this important regime.

Expanding the discussion to include guidance on choosing appropriate values for $\rho$ would enhance the paper's utility. Illustrating how different utility measures can be represented within the current analysis would be beneficial for readers seeking to apply the framework.

The authors should address the relationship between their density estimator and the M-estimation procedure proposed by Lei. Despite its relevance, this work remains uncited, and the authors should demonstrate awareness of its contributions.

While the paper demonstrates the optimality of randomized response in terms of convergence rate, it would be valuable to contextualize this result by comparing it to other existing approaches for histogram estimation of discrete data. How do alternative algorithms perform relative to randomized response within this privacy-constrained setting?

**Post-Rebuttal Addendum:**

Several points raised during the rebuttal period warrant further attention. Firstly, the distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be made more explicit and upfront in the introduction. This clarification, as highlighted by Reviewer 9, is essential for a clear understanding of the analysis.

Secondly, a more comprehensive comparison to related work, including Lei’s work and contributions identified by other reviewers, is crucial for situating this paper within the broader literature. 

Finally, a closer examination of reference [10], which has now been published, leads me to refine my assessment of this paper's contribution. While the target venues (and consequently, audiences) differ, the primary novelty of this work appears to be twofold: a rigorous and detailed exposition of the local privacy model, and the derivation of bounds for density estimation. While the latter represents a new contribution, the foundational aspects of the local privacy model are largely contained within [10].
