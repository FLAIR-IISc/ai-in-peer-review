

**Review: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation**

This paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, where each individual's data is privatized before being sent to a statistician. The authors establish sharp minimax convergence rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) probability estimation problems under α-local differential privacy. Their work bridges classical privacy research (Warner's 1965 randomized response) with modern differential privacy frameworks, demonstrating that Warner's method is optimal for multinomial estimation and developing new minimax-optimal techniques for density estimation.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency: for multinomial estimation with d categories, the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy. The authors prove that Warner's randomized response achieves this optimal rate, resolving a longstanding question about the optimality of this classical method. Second, for density estimation of β-smooth densities, they show the convergence rate degrades from n⁻²β/(2β+1) (classical) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2), and develop practical estimators (histogram and orthogonal series) that achieve this rate. These results provide the first sharp minimax rates for nonparametric density estimation under local privacy.

The paper excels in quality and theoretical rigor. The proofs are technically sound and well-structured, with tight lower and upper bounds that match for both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly noting that direct Laplace perturbation of samples cannot achieve their optimal rates. The theoretical framework is clearly developed, with proper connections to minimax decision theory and differential privacy literature.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some technical details could be more accessible. The authors could have provided more intuition about why the density estimation rate differs from classical results, particularly regarding the exponent change from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2). While the paper focuses on ℓ₂ loss, a brief discussion of ℓ₁ loss would be valuable, as it's often more relevant for probability estimation. The notation is consistent but occasionally dense; for instance, the treatment of the orthogonal series estimator could benefit from a more intuitive presentation.

The originality of this work is strong. The authors successfully connect Warner's classical 1965 work on randomized response with modern differential privacy and minimax theory, demonstrating that this 50-year-old method is minimax optimal. This represents a significant conceptual advance beyond previous work on differential privacy, which has largely focused on centralized privacy rather than local privacy. The paper also makes the important contribution of showing that simple, individual-level privacy mechanisms (like Warner's method) are optimal, rather than requiring complex coordination between users.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides the first sharp minimax rates for statistical estimation under local privacy, which is increasingly relevant for applications like surveys, health data collection, and other settings where data must remain private even from the statistician. The results help practitioners understand the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical utility, and the authors' optimal mechanisms (like randomized response) can be directly implemented in real-world applications.

Strengths:
- Provides sharp minimax rates for both discrete and continuous probability estimation under local privacy
- Demonstrates that Warner's classical randomized response is minimax optimal
- Develops practical, computationally efficient algorithms that achieve the optimal rates
- Connects classical privacy work with modern differential privacy frameworks
- Offers clear theoretical trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency

Weaknesses:
- The paper could better motivate why the density estimation rate differs from classical results (particularly the exponent change)
- Limited discussion of ℓ₁ loss, which is often more appropriate for probability estimation problems
- Some technical details (e.g., orthogonal series estimator) could be presented more intuitively
- While the paper mentions that previous work on differential privacy typically assumes centralized privacy, it could more explicitly contrast these settings

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem at the intersection of statistics and privacy, with clear theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs. The results are both theoretically rigorous and practically relevant, making them valuable to both theoretical researchers and practitioners in the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the intuition behind the density estimation rate change and to briefly discuss ℓ₁ loss. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistics, and its results provide crucial theoretical foundations for future work in this increasingly important area. The authors' demonstration that a 50-year-old method is minimax optimal is particularly compelling, bridging historical and contemporary work in the field.
