SUMMARY: This submission is a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv paper and employs a Fano/Le Cam-style argument to establish lower bounds for estimation procedures that operate on privatized data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. As a consequence, randomized response is shown to be optimal in a certain sense. As a caveat, I did not read the supplementary material.

pros and cons:
- The results characterize the limits of learning from data that has been perturbed to ensure privacy.
- Some imprecision in the exposition could confuse casual readers (see below).
- Connections to existing results on differential privacy appear to be missing.

additional comments:
- The restriction to local privacy, which is crucial for the results, yields a privacy model that differs substantially from the standard (global) differential privacy model, and many readers may not appreciate this distinction. This confusion is amplified by statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level alpha…" The authors should avoid such sweeping (and possibly incorrect) claims when presenting their findings.
- Is the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 necessary? In particular, experiments suggest that alpha ≈ 1 may be the best achievable for certain learning problems under differential privacy, so it is unclear what the bound says about this regime.
- Some discussion of possible choices of rho would be helpful, so readers can see how different utility measures are incorporated into the analysis.
- How does this density estimator compare to Lei’s M-estimation procedure? That paper is not cited, but the authors are likely aware of it.
- There are many alternative approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. While randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, how do these other algorithms compare?

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:
* The distinction between population and sample statistics should be made more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (cf. response to Rev. 9).
* A comparison to related work (Lei and those raised by another reviewer) is important for context.
* A closer reading of [10], which has now appeared, leads me to view the incremental contribution of this paper more narrowly. Although the venues (and thus audiences) differ, the contributions here are twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model and bounds for density estimation. The latter are new, but the former is largely covered by [10].

This submission is a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript and uses a Le Cam-style argument to derive lower bounds for estimators operating on private data when the analyst is untrusted. As a corollary, randomized response emerges as optimal in an appropriate sense.
