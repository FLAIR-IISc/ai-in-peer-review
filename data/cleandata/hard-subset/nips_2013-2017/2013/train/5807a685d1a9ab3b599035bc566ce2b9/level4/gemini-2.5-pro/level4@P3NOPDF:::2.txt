This paper investigates the fundamental limits of probability estimation under the constraint of local privacy. The authors derive sharp minimax rates of convergence for two key problems: discrete multinomial estimation and continuous density estimation. For multinomial estimation, the analysis reveals that local privacy induces a reduction in the effective sample size that is quadratic in the privacy parameter, alpha. As alpha is often a small constant, this implies that the rate of convergence is of the same order as in the non-private setting. In contrast, for the density estimation problem, the authors demonstrate that the optimal non-private rate is no longer attainable when local privacy must be preserved, highlighting a fundamental statistical cost associated with privacy in this context.

Overall, the paper addresses an important and timely problem, and the results are of significant theoretical interest. The proofs appear to be correct, and to the best of my knowledge, the derivation of a minimax bound for density estimation under a local privacy constraint is a novel contribution.

My main concerns are related to the positioning of the results with respect to prior work and a point of clarification regarding one of the main claims.

**1. Relationship to Prior Work on Differential Privacy Lower Bounds**

The primary weakness of the paper is its insufficient discussion of its relationship to a significant body of prior work on lower bounds for differential privacy, particularly concerning the "noise complexity" of linear queries. The multinomial estimation problem studied here can be viewed as a special case of the linear counting queries analyzed in several highly relevant papers, including:

*   Hardt & Talwar, "On the geometry of differential privacy," STOC, 2010.
*   De, "Lower bounds in differential privacy," TCC, 2012.

These works study worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under differential privacy, using the same L2 (Mean Squared Error) metric employed in this paper. The key distinction is that this paper considers local differential privacy (LDP), whereas the cited works focus on the standard (central) differential privacy (CDP) model.

Since LDP is a stronger constraint than CDP, any lower bound for CDP automatically applies to LDP. Therefore, a crucial question is whether the lower bound derived here for LDP is strictly stronger than the existing bounds for CDP. Without this comparison, the novelty and impact of the multinomial estimation result are difficult to assess. The authors should explicitly discuss this connection and clarify how their bound improves upon, or differs from, what could be inferred from the existing CDP literature.

The authors' rebuttal partially clarified this point, but it is essential that a detailed explanation and the relevant citations are integrated into the final version of the paper. I would also suggest including a reference to Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, "The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases" (STOC 2013), which also considers Mean Squared Error and is directly relevant to this discussion.

**2. Clarity of Comparison in Density Estimation**

The statement of the result for the density estimation problem could be clarified to ensure the comparison between the private and non-private settings is sound. The authors state that the lower bound in the local privacy setting is higher than in the non-private setting. However, the minimax bound in this paper is derived for the specific case where the density function can be expanded with a trigonometric basis. The non-private lower bound cited in equation (13), on the other hand, appears to hold for the general Sobolev space described in Definition 1.

To make a compelling argument that privacy introduces a fundamental gap, the comparison must be apples-to-apples. The authors should clarify whether the non-private lower bound in (13) also holds when restricted to the same class of densities with a trigonometric basis. If it does, the comparison is valid and the conclusion is strong. If not, the claim that the optimal non-private rate is unattainable needs to be more carefully qualified.

In summary, this paper presents interesting and sharp minimax bounds for probability and density estimation under local privacy guarantees. The paper is well-written and the results are a valuable contribution. However, its impact could be significantly strengthened by thoroughly addressing the connections to the existing literature on differential privacy lower bounds and by clarifying the basis for comparison in the density estimation result.
