This paper studies minimax bounds for probability estimation under the requirement that individual data remain private from the statistician, using the local differential privacy model. The authors analyze two problems: (i) multinomial probability estimation and (ii) nonparametric density estimation. They derive sharp minimax rates under α-local privacy, giving matching lower and upper bounds and exhibiting the privacy–statistical efficiency trade-off. For the multinomial case, they show that local privacy effectively reduces the sample size from n to nα^2/d (for small α), and they provide simple mechanisms attaining these rates, including a variant of Warner’s randomized response. For density estimation over Sobolev classes (using a trigonometric basis), they prove that the non-private rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} is not attainable under local privacy; instead, the optimal rate is (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, and they give histogram and orthogonal series estimators achieving this bound. As far as I could check, the proofs appear correct. To my knowledge, the minimax bounds for density estimation under local privacy have not been established previously.

Strengths
- Clear and rigorous minimax analysis under local privacy, with tight lower and upper bounds.
- Conceptually clean information-theoretic framework (via Le Cam/Fano) adapted to local privacy, including useful divergence and mutual information bounds.
- Identification of optimal mechanisms: in particular, the optimality of randomized response for multinomial estimation is both interesting and practically relevant.
- For nonparametric density estimation, the paper pinpoints a genuine change in the minimax exponent induced by privacy constraints and supplies efficient procedures that match the lower bounds.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Relation to prior work on worst-case error and noise complexity for (global) differential privacy in answering linear/counting queries is underdeveloped. The multinomial estimation problem is a special case of answering linear counting queries in L2, and there are relevant lower bounds in:
  - Hardt and Talwar, On the Geometry of Differential Privacy (STOC 2010).
  - De, Lower Bounds in Differential Privacy (TCC 2012).
  - Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases (STOC 2013), which also analyzes mean squared error.
  Since local privacy is strictly stronger than (global) differential privacy, lower bounds for DP apply to the local setting as well. It would be helpful if the authors explicitly compared their multinomial lower bounds to these results and clarified whether the local-privacy bounds are strictly stronger or essentially recover these DP lower bounds in the multinomial/L2 setting.
- For density estimation, the presentation around the comparison to non-private rates is somewhat confusing. Equation (13) states the classical non-private rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)}; the paper’s lower bounds are proved using the trigonometric basis. Please clarify whether (13) is intended for the general Sobolev class as defined in Definition 1 (elliptical Sobolev with arbitrary bounded orthonormal basis) or specifically for the trigonometric basis. A fair comparison requires that the non-private lower bound be stated for the same function class used in the private lower bound.
- Minor interpretation point: in discussing the multinomial rate, the paper notes that for small constant α the effective sample size is reduced quadratically in α. It may be worth emphasizing the dependence on d explicitly, since the effective sample size is nα^2/d; the degradation can be substantial for large d, even when α is treated as a small constant.

Quality
- Technical soundness: High. The lower bounds derive from carefully controlled information-theoretic arguments tailored to local privacy, and the upper bounds are constructive and efficient.
- Experimental/theoretical support: Theoretical results are complete; no experiments are needed for this contribution.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The density estimation section would benefit from a clearer statement of the function class alignment (general Sobolev vs. trigonometric specialization) when comparing private and non-private rates, as noted above.
- Please expand the related work section to include and contrast with Hardt–Talwar (2010), De (2012), and Nikolov–Talwar–Zhang (2013), and to articulate precisely how local-privacy minimax bounds relate to known DP lower bounds for linear queries.

Originality
- The density estimation results under local privacy and the demonstration of a changed minimax exponent are, to the best of my knowledge, new.
- The multinomial analysis is closely related to prior DP results for linear queries; the novelty lies in the local privacy setting and in establishing sharp constants/rates within a minimax decision-theoretic framework.

Significance
- High. The paper provides a clear quantitative picture of the privacy–accuracy trade-off in both finite- and infinite-dimensional estimation problems, and identifies optimal mechanisms. These results are likely to influence both theory and practice in locally private data analysis.

Suggestions for the authors
- Add a dedicated subsection comparing your multinomial lower bounds and mechanisms to the linear-query error lower bounds and upper bounds in:
  - Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010),
  - De (TCC 2012),
  - Nikolov, Talwar & Zhang (STOC 2013).
  Specify whether your local-privacy bounds are strictly stronger, and clarify the exact gap, if any.
- In Section 4, state explicitly that the non-private rate (13) holds for the same Sobolev class induced by the trigonometric basis, or adjust the comparison accordingly.
- Briefly discuss the role of d in the multinomial rate to avoid possible misinterpretation.

Reasons to accept
- New and sharp minimax results under local privacy, including a demonstrable change in the nonparametric rate.
- Conceptual clarity and practical relevance (e.g., optimality of randomized response).
- Solid, carefully executed theory.

Reasons to reject
- Incomplete discussion of highly relevant prior work on DP lower bounds for linear queries and mean squared error, which is important for positioning the multinomial results.

Overall recommendation
- Accept, contingent on adding the missing related work and clarifying the function class alignment in the nonparametric comparison. The contributions are substantial and of broad interest to the NIPS community.
