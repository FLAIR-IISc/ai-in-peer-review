Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This paper investigates the local privacy model, where each participant releases an α-locally differentially private report based on her own type (and, in general, potentially on others’ reports) to a data curator. The analysis is framed via minimax expected error: nature selects a distribution over the n users’ types, and we choose an α-LDP local mechanism to minimize the gap between an estimator computed from the original data and the analogous estimator computed from the privatized signals (typically sums, aggregates, or averages). The authors derive lower bounds on the minimax rate for ℓ2 estimation when users’ types are drawn from a discrete distribution over d categories, and they show that a slight variant of randomized response matches this lower bound, proving optimality. They then turn to density estimation, where they again establish a lower bound on the minimax rate and provide per-user perturbation mechanisms that attain it.

Overall, the paper is solid and impactful: it demonstrates that a classical method achieves the best possible performance by meeting sharp lower bounds. I expect the NeurIPS community will find these results of significant interest, and I recommend acceptance.

I do have several reservations. First, stylistically, the presentation of the minimax framework could be streamlined. The notation is heavy; since the main results are ultimately in the ℓ2 metric, it would help to state the bounds directly in ℓ2 where possible rather than maintaining the most abstract Φ ∘ ρ formulation throughout. Second, I would appreciate discussion of ℓ1 error as well—both because it is often the stricter metric for probability estimation and to relate these findings to ℓ1-type lower bounds in differential privacy more broadly (recognizing those are typically in the central, not local, model). Third, the minimax bounds here feel tailored to the local-privacy setting. It would strengthen the contribution to discuss whether comparable lower bounds can be established for a wider range of privacy models. I also like that, although the local model can in principle allow dependence on others’ reports, the simple scheme where each user randomizes solely based on her own type is optimal; making this point explicit in the text would be valuable.

An additional concern: with the FOCS 2013 accepted papers now public, parts of this submission—especially the Section 3 results—read as an extension of existing work. I still lean toward acceptance, but with a somewhat weaker endorsement than before.

In sum: the paper analyzes local privacy via minimax rates, establishes lower bounds, and shows that randomized response achieves the matching upper bounds—a clean and compelling result.
