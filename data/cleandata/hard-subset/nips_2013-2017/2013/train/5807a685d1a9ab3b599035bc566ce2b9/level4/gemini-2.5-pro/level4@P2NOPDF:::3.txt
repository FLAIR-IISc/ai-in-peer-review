### Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations"

#### Summary
This paper investigates the fundamental limits of utility in the local model of differential privacy. In this setting, individual users apply a privacy mechanism to their own data before sending it to an untrusted data curator. The authors analyze this problem through the lens of minimax estimation error. The goal is to design an $\alpha$-differentially private local protocol that minimizes the maximum possible estimation error, where the maximum is taken over all possible underlying data distributions chosen by an adversary.

The paper's main contributions are twofold. First, for the problem of estimating a discrete probability distribution over $d$ types, the authors derive a sharp minimax lower bound on the expected squared $\ell_2$ error. They then demonstrate that this bound is achieved by the classic randomized response mechanism, thereby proving its optimality for this task. Second, they extend this analysis to the problem of non-parametric density estimation, again providing a minimax lower bound that is met by a simple, non-interactive perturbation scheme.

#### Strengths and Overall Assessment
This is a well-executed and important paper that provides a foundational result in the emerging area of local differential privacy. Its primary strength lies in formally establishing the optimality of a simple, classic privacy mechanism (randomized response). By deriving a tight minimax lower bound and showing that randomized response achieves it, the authors offer a definitive answer to a core question in the field. Such results are highly valuable to the machine learning community, as they establish clear theoretical benchmarks and provide concrete guidance on optimal mechanism design. The paper is technically solid and the findings are relevant to the NIPS audience. I recommend acceptance.

#### Suggestions for Improvement
While the core results are strong, the paper could be improved in several areas to maximize its clarity and impact.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation**:
    *   The exposition of the minimax framework is quite technical and could be simplified for broader accessibility. A more intuitive explanation at the beginning of the relevant sections would help readers grasp the core ideas before delving into the formal details.
    *   The notation is dense and at times cumbersome. Given that the primary results are for the squared $\ell_2$ norm, the authors might consider if the notation could be streamlined by specializing to this case earlier in the exposition, which would improve readability.

2.  **Context and Discussion**:
    *   The analysis is focused entirely on the $\ell_2$ norm. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of other relevant error metrics, particularly the $\ell_1$ norm. A brief discussion on the challenges of extending these bounds to $\ell_1$ or the relationship between the two would be valuable.
    *   It would be beneficial to connect the derived lower bounds to existing lower bounds in the standard (centralized) differential privacy literature. While the settings are different, situating these results within the broader context of privacy-utility trade-offs would highlight the specific cost of enforcing privacy at the local level.
    *   The minimax framework presented seems specifically tailored for the local privacy setting. Could the authors comment on the applicability of their analytical techniques to a broader range of settings or other privacy models?

3.  **Highlighting a Key Implication**:
    *   A crucial and perhaps understated finding is that while the general local privacy model allows for complex protocols where users might coordinate or base their randomization on others' public signals, the optimal mechanism turns out to be a simple, non-interactive one where each user randomizes their data independently. This is a powerful and practical conclusion that deserves to be stated more explicitly and prominently in the text.

#### Concern Regarding Novelty
A significant concern has arisen since the time of submission. The list of accepted papers for FOCS 2013 has been published, and there appears to be significant overlap with this work, particularly regarding the results presented in Section 3. This new information reduces the perceived novelty of the submission, casting it more as an extension or parallel discovery rather than a completely foundational contribution in isolation.

#### Conclusion
This paper presents a strong theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy, proving the minimax optimality of the randomized response mechanism. The contribution is significant and of clear interest to the NIPS community. My primary reservations relate to the presentation, which could be clarified, and a new concern regarding the work's novelty in light of a concurrent publication at FOCS 2013.

Despite the issue of novelty, the results are independently valuable and the work is of high quality. I maintain my recommendation for acceptance, though with less conviction than I would have had prior to the FOCS announcement.
