Title: Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

Summary:
This paper studies local differential privacy (LDP) through the lens of minimax risk for estimation tasks. In a model where each user emits an α-DP signal—potentially as a function of their own type and the other users’ signals—the authors derive sharp minimax lower bounds for l2 error in estimating discrete distributions over d types. They show that a simple randomized response (RR) mechanism achieves these bounds, establishing optimality. The paper further treats density estimation, again proving lower bounds that are matched by per-user perturbation schemes depending only on individual types.

Strengths:
- Clarifies the statistical limits of learning under local privacy, providing sharp minimax rates rather than order-level guarantees.
- Identifies randomized response as optimal for the core probability estimation problem, which is both conceptually clean and practically relevant.
- Extends the analysis to density estimation, showing that fully local, per-user perturbations suffice to achieve the lower bounds.
- The results are timely and of broad interest to the NIPS community, at the intersection of statistics, privacy, and learning theory.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Exposition: The discussion of minimax bounds is more technical than necessary for conveying the main ideas. A higher-level overview and a succinct summary of the risk definitions and the role of α would help.
- Notation: The paper uses heavy notation throughout. Since the primary results are for l2 risk, many statements could be simplified by committing to l2 earlier and avoiding overgeneral notation unless needed.
- Norms: The paper focuses on l2 error but does not discuss l1, which is often more natural for distribution learning and total variation risk. Even a brief comparison—e.g., how the l2-optimal rates translate to l1 via standard inequalities or whether analogous sharp l1 rates hold—would strengthen the contribution.
- Scope: The minimax framework appears tailored to the local-privacy model considered. It would be valuable to comment on how the techniques extend (or do not) to related settings (e.g., shuffle models, communication-limited protocols without privacy constraints, or central DP) and whether similar lower bounds might be expected there.
- Optimality statement: A notable and appealing takeaway is that, although the model allows users’ signals to depend on others, the optimal scheme in the studied tasks is one where each user randomizes based solely on their own type (e.g., RR). This deserves to be stated explicitly and prominently in the paper.
- Relation to prior work: Now that the FOCS 2013 accepted papers are public, Section 3 appears closely related to prior results. The paper should clearly delineate what is new: Is the novelty in sharper constants, broader function classes, refined techniques, or a unifying framework? A side-by-side comparison would help avoid confusion about overlap.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add an intuitive overview of the minimax formulation: what the adversary chooses, the role of α in the risk, and how the rate depends on n, d, and α. A short paragraph before diving into theorems would aid accessibility.
- Consolidate notation around l2 for the main theorems, moving generalizations to an appendix unless they are directly used.
- Include a short discussion of l1 risk: known reductions between l2 and l1 in this context, whether the RR mechanism remains rate-optimal for l1, and any gaps.
- Make explicit the independence-of-others optimality result: state that the optimal mechanisms for the considered tasks do not require dependence on other users’ signals.
- Expand the related work section: contrast with central DP lower bounds and with contemporaneous FOCS 2013 papers; clarify conceptual and technical novelties.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong paper that advances our understanding of what is statistically achievable under local differential privacy and pins down optimal rates with simple, implementable mechanisms. Despite concerns about exposition, notation, and overlap with FOCS 2013, the core contributions are solid and of interest to the NIPS audience. I recommend acceptance, albeit with a weaker endorsement than initially, contingent on clarifying novelty relative to the recent FOCS work and improving readability.

Recommendation: Accept (borderline-strong)
Confidence: Moderate
