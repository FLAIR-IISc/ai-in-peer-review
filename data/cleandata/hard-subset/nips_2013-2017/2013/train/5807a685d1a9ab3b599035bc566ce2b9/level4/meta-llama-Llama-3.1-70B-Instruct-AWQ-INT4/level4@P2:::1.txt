This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript, and uses a LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on estimation algorithms that operate on private data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. As a corollary, randomized response turns out to be an optimal strategy in some sense.

The paper provides a detailed study of the estimation of probability distributions - discrete and continuous - in a stringent setting in which data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors give sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate, as well as providing tools to allow movement along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum.

One of the consequences of the results is that Warner's classical work on randomized response is an optimal way to perform survey sampling while maintaining privacy of the respondents. The authors also apply their theoretical tools to a further probability estimation problem - that of nonparametric density estimation.

The paper brings together the formal treatment of disclosure risk provided by differential privacy with the tools of minimax decision theory to provide a theoretical treatment of probability estimation under privacy constraints. The authors are able to provide lower bounds on the convergence rates of any estimator, in their case under a restriction to estimators that guarantee privacy. They complement these results with matching upper bounds that are achievable using computationally efficient algorithms.

The authors' results show that providing local privacy can sometimes be quite detrimental to the quality of statistical estimators. They demonstrate that the classical rate in which there is no privacy is no longer attainable when local differential privacy is required. Instead, they show that the minimax rate of estimation of a multinomial in both mean-squared error and absolute error is reduced.

The paper also considers the problem of density estimation, where the effects of local differential privacy turn out to be somewhat more severe. The authors show that for the problem of density estimation, instead of just multiplicative loss in the effective sample size as in the previous section, imposing local differential privacy leads to a different convergence rate.

The authors achieve the optimal rate using histogram and orthogonal series estimators. They demonstrate that the minimax lower bound is sharp, as claimed. The paper concludes by discussing the implications of the results and the potential for future work in this area.

Pros and cons:

Pros:

* The results provide a characterization of the limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy.
* The paper brings together the formal treatment of disclosure risk provided by differential privacy with the tools of minimax decision theory to provide a theoretical treatment of probability estimation under privacy constraints.

Cons:

* There is some imprecision in the commentary on the results which could lead a casual reader to become confused.
* Connections to existing results on differential privacy seem to be missing.
* The restriction to local privacy makes the privacy model quite different than the differential privacy model, a fact which many readers may not appreciate.
* The paper could benefit from a more explicit explanation of the distinction between population vs. sample statistics.
* A comparison to related work (Lei and those brought up by another reviewer) is important for context.

Additional comments:

* Is the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 necessary? In particular, experimental results suggest that alpha approximately 1 may be the most one can expect for certain learning problems (under differential privacy), so it is unclear what the bound tells us about this case.
* Some commentary on the possible choices of rho may be nice, so that readers can see how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.
* How does this density estimator compare to the M-estimation procedure of Lei? This paper is not cited at all, but I imagine the authors should be aware of it.
* There are many other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. While randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, how do these other algorithms stack up?
