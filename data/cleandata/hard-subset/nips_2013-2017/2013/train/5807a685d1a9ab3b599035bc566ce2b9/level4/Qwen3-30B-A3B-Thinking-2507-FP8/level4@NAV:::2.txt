

This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. The authors address two estimation problems—multinomial estimation and density estimation—and establish sharp minimax rates of convergence for both. For multinomial estimation, they demonstrate that local privacy reduces the effective sample size quadratically in the privacy parameter α; since α is typically small, the effective sample size remains comparable to the non-private case. For density estimation, they show that the optimal non-private rate is unattainable under local privacy.  

The results are compelling and technically sound, with proofs appearing correct. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to derive minimax bounds for density estimation under privacy constraints.  

A primary concern is the omission of relevant prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy (DP). The multinomial estimation bound closely parallels results in Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012), who establish worst-case lower bounds for linear queries under DP using the same L₂ error metric. Probability estimation here is a special case of linear counting queries, and since local privacy is stronger than DP, DP lower bounds also apply to local privacy. The authors should clarify whether their local privacy bound improves upon existing DP lower bounds.  

Additionally, the density estimation result requires clarification. The paper states that the local privacy lower bound exceeds the non-private bound (eq. 13), but eq. (13) applies specifically to densities expandable in trigonometric bases (per Definition 1). The comparison is only valid if the non-private lower bound (eq. 13) holds for this same trigonometric basis case, not for general Sobolev spaces.  

*Rebuttal note:* The authors’ response partially addresses the DP literature connection but omits Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also analyzes mean square error under DP. The authors should incorporate these references and explicitly discuss the relationship to prior noise complexity bounds.  

Overall, the paper presents interesting, sharp minimax results for privacy-preserving estimation, but requires critical expansion of the related work section to acknowledge foundational DP lower bounds. The writing is clear, yet the omission of key references undermines the novelty claim for the multinomial result.
