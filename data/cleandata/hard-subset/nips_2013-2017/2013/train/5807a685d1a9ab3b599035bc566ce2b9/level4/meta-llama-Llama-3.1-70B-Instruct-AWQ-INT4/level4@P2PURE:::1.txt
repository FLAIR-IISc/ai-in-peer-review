This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript, employing a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on estimation algorithms operating on private data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. As a corollary, randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy in some sense.

As a caveat, I did not review the supplementary material.

Pros and Cons:
- The results provide a characterization of the limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy.
- However, there is some imprecision in the commentary on the results, which could lead to confusion among casual readers (see below).
- Connections to existing results on differential privacy appear to be missing.

Additional Comments:
- The restriction to local privacy, crucial for the results, renders the privacy model distinct from the differential privacy model, a fact that many readers may not appreciate. This confusion may be exacerbated by statements such as those at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…" The authors should avoid making overly broad (and potentially incorrect) statements when describing their results.
- Is the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 necessary? In particular, experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may be the most one can expect for certain learning problems (under differential privacy), making it unclear what the bound reveals about this case.
- Some commentary on the possible choices of $\rho$ would be beneficial, allowing readers to understand how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.
- A comparison with the M-estimation procedure of Lei would be valuable, as this paper is not cited, despite likely being relevant to the authors' work.
- There are numerous other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. While randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, it would be helpful to know how these alternative algorithms compare.
- Furthermore, the distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be clarified more explicitly at the beginning of the paper (cf. response to Rev.9).
- A comparison to related work (Lei and those mentioned by another reviewer) is essential for context.
- A closer inspection of [10], which has now appeared, leads me to construe the additional contribution of this paper more narrowly. Although the venues (and hence audiences) for this and [10] differ, the contribution of this paper is twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model, and bounds for density estimation. While the latter are new, the former is essentially contained in [10].
