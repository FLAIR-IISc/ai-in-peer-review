

This paper investigates minimax estimation rates for probability distributions under local privacy constraints, connecting statistical decision theory with differential privacy. The authors establish sharp lower bounds for multinomial and density estimation under α-local differential privacy (LDP), demonstrating how privacy requirements trade off with statistical efficiency. Notably, they show Warner's randomized response mechanism achieves optimal rates for multinomial estimation, and derive new minimax rates for density estimation that differ from classical results.

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution by rigorously analyzing estimation under LDP using minimax decision theory. The proofs are well-structured, and the authors provide matching upper bounds with efficiently implementable algorithms. The main technical contribution is the derivation of sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation under LDP, revealing fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The analysis correctly distinguishes between population estimation (the focus here) and sample estimation (addressed in prior work), which strengthens their lower bounds. However, the paper would benefit from clarifying the distinction between LDP and standard differential privacy, as the manuscript occasionally conflates these concepts (e.g., "providing differential privacy at a level α" on page 4). The restriction to α ≤ 1 in Theorem 1 should be justified, as experimental work suggests α ≈ 1 represents a practical upper bound for many learning problems.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical exposition. The connection between classical survey design (Warner's work) and modern privacy theory is effectively presented. However, the paper could improve by explicitly distinguishing between local privacy (the focus here) and standard differential privacy at the outset, as this is a critical conceptual distinction. The statement that "Warner's randomized response is an optimal way to perform survey sampling while maintaining privacy" would be more precise if qualified as "optimal for the specific LDP setting considered." The discussion of the population vs. sample estimation distinction (addressed in the rebuttal) should be clarified in the main text.

**Originality:** The paper makes two key contributions: (1) a careful exposition of the LDP framework with novel minimax bounds for multinomial estimation, and (2) new minimax rates for density estimation under LDP. While the LDP framework itself is not entirely novel (see [10] which appeared concurrently), the paper provides a more thorough analysis of the minimax rates for specific estimation problems. The density estimation results are particularly novel, showing a different convergence rate than classical minimax results. The paper would benefit from more explicit comparison with [10] to clarify the relative contributions. Additionally, the authors should cite Lei's M-estimation work [18] and address how their orthogonal series estimator compares to his approach.

**Significance:** The results provide fundamental insights into the statistical costs of privacy, identifying precise trade-offs between privacy guarantees and estimation accuracy. This has practical implications for designing privacy-preserving survey mechanisms and statistical procedures. The finding that Warner's 50-year-old method is optimal for certain privacy settings is particularly noteworthy. However, the significance would be enhanced by more detailed discussion of how the results impact practical applications, such as specifying concrete parameter choices for real-world privacy mechanisms.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Provides sharp minimax rates for estimation under LDP, filling a gap in the theoretical understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs
- Demonstrates that classic privacy techniques (randomized response) are optimal in modern privacy frameworks
- Offers new results for density estimation under LDP that differ from classical minimax rates
- Presents efficiently implementable algorithms that match the theoretical bounds

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Fails to clearly distinguish between LDP and standard differential privacy, potentially confusing readers
- Does not sufficiently compare with related work (Lei [18], [10]) to clarify the novelty of contributions
- The restriction to α ≤ 1 in Theorem 1 lacks justification, limiting the practical relevance of the bound
- The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation needs more elaboration

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of privacy-utility trade-offs in statistical estimation. The paper makes a significant technical contribution with its minimax analysis, though it would benefit from greater clarity about the LDP framework and more explicit comparison with recent related work. The results are important for both theoretical and practical aspects of privacy-preserving statistics. With minor revisions addressing the clarity issues and literature comparison, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should clarify the LDP vs. differential privacy distinction and better contextualize their work relative to [10] and Lei's work.
