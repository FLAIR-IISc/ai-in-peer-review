Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

This paper studies estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). In the local model, each user with type xi releases an α-LDP randomized signal about xi; in interactive protocols a user’s message may also depend on previously released signals. The authors analyze this setting through the lens of minimax expected error: an adversary selects a distribution over inputs (types of n users), and we choose an α-LDP local scheme and estimator to minimize the worst-case expected deviation between the target functional of the raw data and its estimate from privatized signals (e.g., sums, aggregates, or averages).

The main contribution is a sharp lower bound on the minimax L2 risk for estimating discrete distributions over d categories. The authors show that classical randomized response achieves this rate, proving its optimality in this setting. They further extend the analysis to density estimation, again establishing matching lower and upper bounds via per-user perturbation mechanisms in which each participant randomizes only her own type.

Overall, the paper is strong and timely. It demonstrates that a simple, well-known method attains the best possible rates under LDP, thereby clarifying the fundamental limits of locally private learning. I believe the NIPS community will find these results valuable, and I recommend acceptance.

That said, I have several reservations and suggestions for clarity and scope:

- Exposition of the minimax framework could be simplified. Early sections introduce substantial notation; since the main results are stated for the L2 norm, it would help to standardize the presentation around L2 throughout, deferring general-norm formalism to an appendix if needed.

- It would be useful to comment on L1 risk. L1 is often the more stringent metric for distribution and density estimation, and even a discussion relating the presented L2 bounds to possible L1 analogs would help readers position the results. Connections to known lower bounds in the central (non-local) DP model would also provide helpful context, even if those bounds do not translate directly.

- The minimax lower bounds appear tailored to the local model. While that focus is reasonable, it would strengthen the paper to discuss whether the techniques extend to broader settings (e.g., communication-constrained but non-private estimation, or hybrid models with limited central trust), and to articulate which proof ingredients are inherently local versus broadly applicable.

- Importantly, while the local model may allow interactivity (responses depending on prior users’ signals), the paper’s results show that a simple non-interactive mechanism—each user randomizing solely based on her own type—achieves the optimal rate (e.g., randomized response for the discrete case). Please state this optimality of non-interaction explicitly and prominently, as it is both practical and conceptually clarifying.

A new concern arose after the FOCS 2013 accepted papers were announced: portions of the current submission, particularly Section 3, appear to extend results that now exist in the FOCS program. In light of this overlap, I still recommend acceptance but with reduced enthusiasm relative to my initial assessment.

In summary: the paper provides sharp minimax lower bounds for locally private estimation, and shows that simple per-user mechanisms—most notably randomized response—match these bounds, establishing optimality. Despite some presentational complexity and some overlap with contemporaneous work, the results are clear, important, and of interest to NIPS.
