This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of prior work available on ArXiV, employing a Fano/LeCam-style argument to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms applied to private data, assuming a distrustful algorithm designer. A key finding is that randomized response emerges as an optimal approach under certain conditions.

Please note this review does not consider the supplementary material.

The work offers valuable insights into the fundamental limitations of learning from privacy-perturbed data. However, some aspects of the presentation lack precision and could potentially mislead readers unfamiliar with the nuances of the results (detailed below). Furthermore, connections to the existing literature on differential privacy appear to be missing.

A critical point is that the focus on local privacy results in a privacy model distinct from standard differential privacy, a distinction that may not be immediately apparent to all readers. This potential for confusion is amplified by statements like the one on page 4 – “Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…” – and the authors should avoid such generalizations when discussing their findings. 

It is unclear if the constraint on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 is strictly necessary. Empirical observations suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may represent an upper limit for certain learning problems when considering differential privacy, raising questions about the applicability of the bound in this regime. 

Adding discussion regarding the practical implications of different choices for $\rho$ would be beneficial, allowing readers to understand how varying utility measures can be integrated into the analysis. 

The paper would benefit from a comparison to the M-estimation procedure presented by Lei, a relevant work currently not cited. Additionally, while randomized response achieves an optimal convergence rate, a discussion of how other histogram estimation methods for discrete data compare would strengthen the analysis.

**Following the rebuttal, I have these additional comments:**

The distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be more clearly articulated, preferably at the beginning of the manuscript (as acknowledged in the response to Rev.9). A more thorough comparison to related work, including Lei’s work and other contributions identified by reviewers, is essential for providing context. Finally, with the recent appearance of [10], I now view the paper’s contribution as consisting of two main components: a rigorous explanation of the local privacy model and novel bounds for density estimation. While the latter represents a new contribution, the former is largely contained within [10], even acknowledging the different intended audiences of the two works.
