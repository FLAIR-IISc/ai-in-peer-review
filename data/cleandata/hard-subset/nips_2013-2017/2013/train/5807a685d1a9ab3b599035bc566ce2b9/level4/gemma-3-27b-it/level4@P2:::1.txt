This paper presents a theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints, leveraging minimax decision theory and differential privacy concepts. The core contribution lies in establishing sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial estimation) and continuous (density estimation) settings, quantifying the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. A notable result is the confirmation that Warner’s randomized response method is optimal for survey sampling with privacy guarantees.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Warner [23] on randomized response, extending it within a rigorous theoretical framework. It connects this classical approach with the more recent developments in differential privacy [11, 1], offering a formal treatment of disclosure risk and its impact on statistical inference. The authors effectively utilize tools from information theory, specifically KL-divergence and mutual information, to derive lower bounds on convergence rates and demonstrate matching upper bounds achievable with computationally efficient algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a mathematically sound analysis of the problem, employing minimax theory and differential privacy in a cohesive manner.
*   **Sharp Results:** Deriving *sharp* minimax rates is a significant accomplishment, providing tight bounds on the performance limits of private estimation.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The reaffirmation of Warner’s randomized response as an optimal strategy has practical implications for survey design and data collection.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the local privacy model and its relationship to differential privacy, which is crucial for understanding the scope of the results.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately contextualizes its contributions within the broader literature on privacy and statistical inference, referencing key works in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Nuance in Interpretation:** The discussion around the implications of the results, particularly regarding the applicability to general differential privacy, could be more precise. Statements linking local privacy to broader differential privacy concepts require careful phrasing to avoid misinterpretation.
*   **Limited Discussion of α:** The sensitivity of the results to the choice of α, specifically the relevance of the bounds when α is close to 1, warrants further investigation and discussion. Current experimental findings suggest a potential limitation in the applicability of the bounds in certain regimes.
*   **Utility Measure Flexibility:** While the paper focuses on specific distance metrics, a brief discussion of how different utility measures could be incorporated into the analysis might broaden its appeal and applicability.
*   **Related Work Gaps:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough engagement with recent related work, specifically citing and comparing to Lei [19] on differentially private M-estimators.
*   **Alternative Algorithms:** A discussion of the performance of alternative estimation algorithms (beyond randomized response and the proposed density estimators) in the context of local privacy would provide a more comprehensive perspective.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The rigorous analysis, sharp results, and clear problem formulation are commendable. While there are minor areas for improvement in terms of clarifying the interpretation of the results and expanding the discussion of related work, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of a fundamental problem in privacy-preserving data analysis.
*   The derived minimax rates provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.
*   The reaffirmation of Warner’s randomized response as an optimal strategy has practical relevance.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its technical contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The discussion around the implications of the results could be more precise to avoid potential misinterpretations.
*   The paper does not fully engage with recent related work in the field.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper, with a request for the authors to address the minor weaknesses identified above, particularly concerning the clarity of the interpretation of the results and the discussion of related work. Addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact on the field.
