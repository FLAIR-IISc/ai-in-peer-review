Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper addresses local privacy—a setting where each user outputs an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of the other *n*-1 users, reporting this signal to the data curator. The authors analyze this setting using a min-max expected error framework. In this framework, an adversary chooses a distribution over inputs (user types), and the goal is to select an α-DP local privacy mechanism that minimizes the distance between an estimate derived from the original data and an estimate derived from the reported signals (typically sums, aggregations, or averages). The paper provides a lower bound for the min-max rate for l₂ distance estimation, assuming users are drawn from a discrete distribution over *d* types. Notably, the authors demonstrate that the simple technique of randomized response achieves this bound, and is therefore optimal. They then extend their analysis to the problem of density estimation, again finding that a perturbation scheme where each user perturbs only their own type meets the established lower bound on the min-max rate.

This is a strong and important paper that shows that classical procedures can be optimal in this setting, achieving proven lower bounds. I believe the NIPS community would find it interesting, and I therefore recommend acceptance.

However, I do have a few reservations. First, the explanation of the min-max bounds could be more accessible; simplifying the presentation would be beneficial. The paper also employs quite cumbersome notation. Given that all bounds are expressed with respect to the l₂ norm, could they not be consistently phrased in terms of this norm? Second, I would appreciate some discussion of the l₁ norm, which is more restrictive, or a comparison of the given bounds to those achievable with l₁ norms, as well as other lower bounds in differential privacy (although I acknowledge these often apply to the more traditional, non-local privacy setting). Lastly, the min-max bounds discussed seem specifically tailored to this local-privacy setting. It would be valuable if the authors had considered a broader range of settings and provided min-max lower bounds for those as well. 

On a positive note, it’s interesting that, despite the local setting allowing users to randomize their reported type based on the signals of the other *n*-1 users, the optimal scheme turns out to be the simple one where each person randomizes their report solely based on their own type. I suggest the authors explicitly state this observation in the text.

I have a further reservation to add. Since the list of accepted papers from FOCS 2013 has been published, I now view this paper as an extension of existing work, particularly the results in Section 3. Therefore, I still recommend acceptance, but with less enthusiasm than before.

In summary, the paper analyzes local privacy using min-max rate bounds, establishes lower bounds on the rate, and demonstrates that randomized response achieves the upper bound – a valuable result.
