SUMMARY
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy, where each datum is privatized before reaching the analyst. The authors combine minimax decision theory with information-theoretic tools (Le Cam’s method and Fano’s inequality) to derive sharp lower bounds on estimation accuracy in the local model, and provide matching upper bounds via efficient mechanisms. Two problems are analyzed in depth: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomials, the results show an effective sample size reduction from n to roughly n α^2 / d (for small alpha), and establish that randomized response is optimal up to constants. For density estimation over Sobolev classes with smoothness β, the non-private rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} degrades under local privacy to (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)}. The paper also designs practical private estimators: a slightly modified randomized response scheme for multinomials, a privatized histogram that is minimax-optimal for Lipschitz densities, and an orthogonal-series estimator achieving the optimal rate for general β. The analysis clarifies when common mechanisms (e.g., adding Laplace noise directly to the raw data) are suboptimal in this local setting.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects classical randomized response (Warner), minimax information-theoretic techniques (Le Cam, Fano, Assouad), and the modern differential privacy literature. The local privacy perspective relates to work on learning in the local model (e.g., randomized response variants and sample-complexity separations between local and central DP). Compared with prior DP results that target release of sample statistics, this work focuses on population risk and derives lower bounds that apply uniformly over privatization channels.

STRENGTHS
- Sharp theory with matching lower and upper bounds that quantify privacy–utility trade-offs in the local model.
- Clear identification of qualitative differences between local and non-private rates, including a change in convergence exponent for density estimation.
- Practical, simple mechanisms (randomized response, privatized histograms, and a bounded orthogonal-series scheme) that achieve the bounds.
- Useful information bounds that can be reused in other problems.
- Conceptually clarifies when classical randomized response is in fact optimal.

WEAKNESSES
- The presentation sometimes conflates “differential privacy” with “local differential privacy,” which may mislead readers unfamiliar with the distinction.
- Some claims are phrased broadly (e.g., “effect of providing differential privacy…”) without emphasizing the local, non-interactive setting.
- The restriction to small alpha in Theorem 1 is not fully discussed; practical regimes with alpha around 1 deserve more careful treatment.
- Limited discussion of alternative mechanisms and empirical behavior; comparisons to related methods (e.g., Lei’s differentially private M-estimators) are missing or brief.
- Overlap with existing arXiv versions may make the incremental contribution appear narrower; it would help to more clearly delineate what is new here (especially for density estimation).

DETAILED COMMENTS AND SUGGESTIONS
- Clarify the privacy model: Early in the introduction, explicitly distinguish local differential privacy (each user privatizes their data before collection) from the central DP model. Throughout, replace generic “differential privacy” with “local differential privacy” where appropriate. For example, the statement at the bottom of page 4 should explicitly refer to local DP to avoid overgeneralization.
- Population vs. sample risk: Clearly state at the outset that the bounds are for population estimation risk under privatized observations, and contrast this with prior DP work that controls error for sample statistics. This will help situate the results and avoid confusion (also raised by other reviewers).
- Assumptions on alpha: Discuss the necessity of the small-alpha regime in Theorem 1. Given that practical deployments often aim for alpha around one, it would be helpful to spell out constants, the crossover behavior, and what the bounds imply when alpha is not asymptotically small.
- Loss functions rho: Provide a short discussion of permissible choices of the metric rho and link the analysis to common utilities (e.g., L1, L2, total variation, or integrated squared error). This will help readers see how to adapt the framework to different goals.
- Related work: Include a more thorough comparison to Lei’s private M-estimators and to other recent local and central DP estimators for densities and histograms. Summarize where the proposed estimators improve rates, where they differ in privacy models (local vs. central), and any trade-offs in constants and practicality.
- Alternative mechanisms: For multinomials, briefly compare randomized response to other local mechanisms (e.g., Laplace perturbation on one-hot encodings, k-ary randomized response, RAPPOR-like schemes). Even if suboptimal, a short discussion contextualizes the optimality claim and guides practitioners.
- Density estimation mechanism design: The paper shows that direct Laplace noise on raw data yields suboptimal rates; it would help to explicitly state the intuition behind why the proposed orthogonal-series scheme avoids the deconvolution bottleneck in the local model.
- Notation and readability: A brief “model summary” box collecting the key definitions (local DP channel, private minimax risk, information bounds) would improve readability. Also ensure constants and dependencies (e.g., on β, B0) are clearly specified.

PROS FOR ACCEPTANCE
- Strong, technically sound results with matching bounds and practical mechanisms.
- Clarifies fundamental limits in an increasingly important privacy model.
- New optimal rates for density estimation under local DP.

CONS AGAINST ACCEPTANCE
- Presentation issues that may confuse readers about the privacy model and scope.
- Incomplete related-work discussion and limited empirical context.
- Perceived overlap with existing arXiv material may narrow the novelty.

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL
- The paper should more prominently and explicitly distinguish population estimation from sample-statistic release (as noted in the authors’ response to another reviewer).
- Please add a direct comparison to related work, including Lei and other methods cited during the discussion.
- After re-reading closely related prior work, the incremental contribution appears primarily in two parts: (i) a careful exposition of the local privacy model within a minimax framework, and (ii) new lower and upper bounds for density estimation under local DP. Emphasizing this delineation will strengthen the paper.

I did not review the supplementary material. Overall, I lean toward acceptance if the authors address the clarity and related-work concerns above.
