SUMMARY: This manuscript is a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv paper. It leverages Fano/Le Cam-style information-theoretic arguments to derive lower bounds for estimators that operate on locally privatized data, where the data curator does not trust the analyst. A consequence is that Warner’s randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy in a precise sense. As a caveat, I did not examine the supplementary material in detail.

pros and cons:
- The results illuminate fundamental limits of learning from data perturbed to satisfy privacy constraints.
- Some of the narrative around the results is imprecise and may mislead casual readers (see below).
- Connections to established differential privacy literature are underdeveloped.

additional comments:
- The focus on local privacy is central to the results and makes the privacy model notably different from the central/standard differential privacy model—this distinction may be lost on many readers. This potential confusion is reinforced by broad statements (e.g., the remark near the end of page 4) that attribute effects to “differential privacy” without explicitly qualifying that the guarantees are local. The authors should avoid general claims that could be read as applying beyond the local model.
- Is the restriction on α in Theorem 1 necessary? Empirical experience suggests α ≈ 1 is often the practical regime in (central) DP learning, so it is unclear how informative the bound is there if it explicitly requires α in a smaller range.
- It would be helpful to discuss possible choices of the loss/metric ρ so readers can see how different utility criteria are accommodated by the analysis.
- How does the proposed density estimator compare to Lei’s private M-estimation approach? That work is not cited, but seems relevant and should be discussed.
- There are many alternative strategies for discrete/histogram-style estimation. Even if randomized response attains the optimal rate, a brief comparison explaining how other algorithms fare would be valuable.

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:
* Please make the population vs. sample statistic distinction explicit and prominent early on (cf. response to Reviewer 9).
* A more thorough related-work discussion (including Lei and other papers raised by reviewers) is important for proper context.
* In light of the now-appeared [10], the incremental contribution here seems narrower. While the venues and audiences differ, the added value is twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model and new bounds for density estimation. The former largely overlaps with [10], whereas the latter appears to be the substantive new contribution.
