This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, which aim to preserve individual data privacy. The authors examine two probability estimation problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. They establish sharp minimax rates of convergence for both problems, demonstrating that local privacy reduces the effective sample size quadratically with the privacy parameter alpha in the discrete multinomial estimation problem. Since alpha is often a small constant, the effective sample size remains of the same order as in the non-private case. In contrast, the authors show that the optimal rate for the non-private setting is unattainable in the density estimation problem when local privacy is enforced.

The results are interesting, and the proofs appear to be correct. To the best of my knowledge, the minimax bound for density estimation under privacy constraints has not been previously explored.

However, I have some comments on the paper. Firstly, the minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely related to previous works on noise complexity for differential privacy, but the authors do not mention this connection. Specifically, the papers by Hardt and Talwar (On the geometry of differential privacy, STOC 2010) and De (Lower bounds in differential privacy, TCC 2012) study worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under differential privacy constraints. These papers consider problems that are highly relevant to the current work, as the probability estimation problem examined in this paper is a special case of the linear counting query studied in those papers. The error measures used are also identical (L2 metric). The primary difference lies in the type of privacy considered: local differential privacy in this paper versus differential privacy in the previous works. Since local privacy imposes stronger constraints than differential privacy, the lower bounds established for differential privacy also apply to local privacy. I would like the authors to clarify whether the bound for local privacy presented in this paper improves upon previous bounds for differential privacy.

Regarding the density estimation problem, the statement of the result is somewhat confusing. The authors claim that the lower bound in the local privacy setting is higher than in the non-private setting. However, the minimax bound established in this paper is specific to the case where the density can be expanded using a trigonometric basis. I would like to know whether the lower bound for the non-private setting (equation 13) holds for the general Sobolev space defined in Definition 1 or only for the special case of trigonometric basis. A fair comparison can only be made if the non-private lower bound holds for the trigonometric basis.

In response to the rebuttal, the authors partially clarify the relationship to previous worst-case lower bounds. However, I would like to point out that the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases, STOC 2013) also considers Mean Square Error. I suggest that the authors include their explanations and missing references in the paper.

In summary, this paper establishes sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function and probability density function estimation with privacy guarantees. The results are interesting, and the paper is well-written. However, the authors should acknowledge and discuss the relevant existing literature on noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy, which is closely related to this work.
