

**Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"**

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, making a significant contribution to the intersection of privacy and statistical inference. The authors characterize the fundamental trade-offs between privacy guarantees and statistical efficiency by establishing sharp minimax convergence rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density estimation) problems. The work builds upon Warner's randomized response framework [23] and connects it to modern differential privacy, while also advancing the theoretical understanding of minimax rates under privacy constraints.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**  
The paper addresses a fundamental problem: how much statistical efficiency is lost when each individual's data is privatized before being observed by the statistician (local privacy). The authors develop a minimax framework to analyze this setting, where privacy is formalized as α-local differential privacy. They derive lower bounds on estimation error that match upper bounds achieved by computationally efficient algorithms. This work complements recent NIPS contributions on privacy and statistics [e.g., 9, 24] by providing the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. While prior work has focused on differential privacy for data release [1, 11], this paper uniquely quantifies the statistical cost of privacy in the local setting, where individuals control their own privacy.

**Strengths**  
*Quality:* The paper excels in technical rigor. The authors provide tight lower and upper bounds for both multinomial and density estimation, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal for discrete estimation. The theoretical arguments are sound, with clear connections between privacy constraints and statistical efficiency. The proofs of the minimax rates are well-structured, and the authors correctly identify the key role of mutual information in establishing privacy-statistics trade-offs.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written for a theoretical contribution. The main results are presented with clarity, and the connection between the technical developments and practical implications (e.g., the optimality of randomized response) is well-articulated. The notation, while somewhat dense, is consistently applied throughout. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, though a brief summary of key techniques in the introduction would enhance accessibility.

*Originality:* This work makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature. While prior work has studied differential privacy in statistical settings [9, 16, 24], this paper is the first to establish sharp minimax rates under local privacy. The demonstration that the classical randomized response mechanism achieves optimal rates is particularly novel and important for practical applications. The density estimation results further reveal how privacy constraints fundamentally alter convergence rates, with exponents shifting from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2) for Sobolev-smooth densities.

*Significance:* The results have substantial theoretical and practical significance. The paper provides a quantitative framework for understanding the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off, which is crucial for designing privacy-preserving surveys and data collection mechanisms. The demonstration that Warner's 1965 method is optimal for local privacy is a profound result with immediate practical relevance. The authors' tools for analyzing privacy-constrained estimation will likely influence future work in private statistics.

**Weaknesses**  
*Clarity:* While the paper is well-written, the introduction could better emphasize the distinction between local and central differential privacy. Some readers may initially confuse the local privacy framework (where each user's data is privatized individually) with central privacy (where a trusted curator applies privacy mechanisms). A more explicit comparison with central privacy would strengthen the presentation.

*Originality:* The paper's most significant limitation is its focus on specific estimation problems (multinomial and density estimation). While these are important, the authors could more explicitly discuss how their framework might extend to other statistical problems (e.g., linear regression, classification). The paper mentions this as a direction for future work, but a brief discussion of potential extensions would strengthen the significance.

*Quality:* The paper could better address the relationship between the results and existing lower bounds in the literature. While the authors cite relevant work [9, 24], they could more thoroughly compare their minimax rates with those in the differential privacy literature for non-local settings [e.g., 2, 16]. The claim that "the optimal privacy-preserving strategy for problems such as survey collection has been known for almost 50 years" is somewhat overstated—while randomized response is optimal for the specific problem studied, the broader question of optimal privacy mechanisms for other problems remains open.

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to the field. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important problem at the intersection of privacy and statistics. The results are significant both for their theoretical novelty and their practical implications for privacy-preserving data collection. While the paper could be strengthened by briefly discussing extensions to other statistical problems and providing a clearer comparison with non-local privacy literature, these are minor concerns for a NIPS paper.

The authors' demonstration that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal for local privacy is a particularly elegant result that should interest the NIPS community. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) and makes a clear contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
