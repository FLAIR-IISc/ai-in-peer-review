SUMMARY: This submission is a reformatted version of an existing ArXiV manuscript, utilizing a Fano/LeCam-style approach to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on private data when the algorithm is untrusted by the data holder. Notably, randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy under certain conditions.

Please note that this review is based solely on the provided manuscript, without consideration of the supplementary material.

PROS AND CONS:

- The results provide valuable insights into the limitations of learning from privacy-preserving perturbed data.
- However, some imprecise commentary on the results may lead to confusion among casual readers (see below).
- Connections to existing differential privacy results appear to be lacking.

ADDITIONAL COMMENTS:

- The focus on local privacy, crucial to the results, distinguishes the privacy model significantly from the differential privacy model. This distinction may not be fully appreciated by readers, potentially exacerbated by statements such as those found at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…". The authors should refrain from making overly broad statements when describing their results.
- The necessity of the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is unclear. Experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may be the maximum achievable value for certain learning problems under differential privacy, making it uncertain what the bound reveals about this case.
- Commentary on possible choices of $\rho$ would be beneficial, allowing readers to understand how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.
- A comparison between the proposed density estimator and the M-estimation procedure of Lei would be valuable, as this paper is not cited but is likely relevant to the authors' work.
- Various alternative approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data exist. While randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, it is unclear how these other algorithms compare.
- A clearer explanation of the distinction between population and sample statistics is necessary at the beginning of the paper (cf. response to Rev.9).
- A comparison to related work, including Lei and other studies mentioned by another reviewer, is essential for context.
- Upon closer inspection of [10], the additional contribution of this paper appears more limited. Although the venues and audiences differ, this paper's contribution is twofold: a detailed exposition of the local privacy model and bounds for density estimation. While the latter are novel, the former is largely contained in [10].
