

The paper "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation" makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing sharp minimax convergence rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints. The authors connect Warner's classical randomized response technique from 1965 with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory, demonstrating that randomized response achieves optimal sample complexity for multinomial estimation. They further develop novel results for density estimation under local privacy, showing that the convergence rates differ fundamentally from classical results due to the privacy constraints.

Regarding Quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide both matching lower and upper bounds for both multinomial and density estimation problems, using appropriate tools from minimax decision theory and differential privacy. The results are well-supported by theoretical analysis, and the paper carefully characterizes the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

For Clarity, the paper is well-organized but contains some cumbersome notation that could be simplified. The authors focus primarily on ℓ₂ norms, which is reasonable, but would benefit from explicitly discussing the relevance of ℓ₁ norms for probability estimation problems (which are more restrictive and commonly used in this context). The explanation of minimax theory could be made more accessible to readers unfamiliar with the framework.

In terms of Originality, this work makes a substantial contribution by establishing sharp minimax rates for local privacy, which differs from prior work on global differential privacy. The connection between Warner's 1965 randomized response and modern minimax theory is particularly novel, and the paper demonstrates that this classical technique is actually optimal for privacy-preserving estimation. The density estimation results further show a fundamental departure from classical minimax rates, providing new insights into the impact of local privacy on statistical efficiency.

For Significance, the results have clear theoretical and practical implications for privacy-preserving statistical inference. The paper advances the state of the art by characterizing the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in a rigorous way, providing a theoretical foundation for privacy-preserving survey design and data collection. The finding that a technique developed nearly 50 years ago is optimal for privacy-preserving estimation is particularly noteworthy for the NIPS community.

Strengths:
- Establishes sharp minimax rates for both discrete and continuous settings under local privacy
- Demonstrates that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation
- Develops efficient algorithms that achieve the lower bounds for density estimation
- Connects classical privacy work with modern differential privacy and statistical decision theory
- Provides a clear characterization of the privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off

Weaknesses:
- Uses cumbersome notation that could be simplified (particularly for readers focused on ℓ₂ norms)
- Lacks explicit discussion of ℓ₁ norms, which are more relevant for probability estimation
- The minimax bounds are tailored to the local privacy setting without broader context
- The connection to Duchi et al.'s FOCS 2013 work (which contains similar results for Section 3) could be more explicitly addressed
- The explanation of the minimax framework could be made more accessible

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While there are some issues with exposition and notation, the core results are important, novel, and well-supported. The paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS, providing both theoretical insights and practical implications for privacy-preserving data analysis. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity concerns and expand on the relationship between the results and ℓ₁ norms.
