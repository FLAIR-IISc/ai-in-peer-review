Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

The paper explores the concept of local privacy, where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based on their type and the signals of the other n-1 users, and then reports their type to the data curator. The authors analyze this setting using the min-max expected error framework, where the adversary selects a distribution over inputs (types for the n users), and we choose an α-DP local scheme to minimize the distance between an estimation derived from the original data and a similar estimation derived from the reported signals. The authors provide a lower bound for the min-max rate for l_2 distance estimations and users drawn from a discrete distribution over d types. They demonstrate that the simple technique of randomized response achieves this bound, making it optimal. The authors then analyze the problem of density estimations, where the lower bound on the min-max rate is met by a perturbation scheme in which each person perturbs only their own type.

The paper is well-written and significant, as it shows that classic procedures can achieve optimal results by meeting certain lower bounds. The NIPS community will likely find it interesting, and I recommend acceptance.

However, I have a few reservations. Firstly, the explanation of min-max bounds could be simplified. The paper also contains cumbersome notations, particularly since all bounds are given for the l_2 norm. It would be more convenient to phrase the bounds with respect to this norm. Additionally, I would appreciate a comment on the l_1 norm, which is more restrictive, or a comparison of the given bounds to l_1 norms, as well as other lower bounds in differential privacy (although these apply more to classical, non-local settings). Furthermore, the min-max bounds discussed seem tailored to the local-privacy setting. I wish the authors had considered a broader set of settings and provided min-max lower bounds for them as well. Nevertheless, I appreciate that the simple scheme, where each person randomizes their report solely based on their type, is optimal in the local setting. I suggest the authors explicitly state this in the text.

A new reservation has arisen since the list of FOCS 2013 accepted papers has been published. The paper now appears to be an extension of an existing paper, particularly the results in Section 3. Although I still recommend acceptance, my recommendation is not as strong as before.

The paper effectively analyzes the framework of local privacy using min-max rate bounds, provides lower bounds on the rate, and demonstrates that the upper bound is met by randomized response. This is a nice result.
