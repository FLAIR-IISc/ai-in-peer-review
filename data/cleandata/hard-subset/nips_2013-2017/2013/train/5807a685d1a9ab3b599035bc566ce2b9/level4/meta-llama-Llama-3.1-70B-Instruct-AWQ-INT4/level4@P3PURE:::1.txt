This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of an ArXiV manuscript, utilizing a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on estimation algorithms that operate on private data when the algorithm is not trusted by the data holder. As a corollary, randomized response is shown to be an optimal strategy in some sense.

As a caveat, I did not review the supplementary material.

The paper has both strengths and weaknesses. On the positive side, the results provide valuable insights into the limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy. However, there are some imprecisions in the commentary on the results that could lead a casual reader to become confused (see below). Furthermore, connections to existing results on differential privacy seem to be missing.

Additional comments:

The restriction to local privacy, which is crucial for the results, makes the privacy model significantly different from the differential privacy model. This distinction may not be fully appreciated by many readers, and statements such as those at the bottom of page 4 ("Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…") may exacerbate this confusion. The authors should avoid making overly broad (and potentially incorrect) statements when describing their results.

The necessity of the restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is unclear. Experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may be the most one can expect for certain learning problems (under differential privacy), so it is unclear what the bound tells us about this case.

Some commentary on the possible choices of $\rho$ would be beneficial, allowing readers to understand how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.

A comparison to the M-estimation procedure of Lei would be useful, as this paper is not cited, but the authors are likely aware of it.

There are many other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. While randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, it would be interesting to see how these other algorithms compare.

Addendum after rebuttal:

* A clearer and more explicit distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be explained at the beginning of the paper (c.f. response to Rev.9).
* A comparison to related work (Lei and those brought up by another reviewer) is essential for context.
* A closer inspection of [10], which has now appeared, suggests that the additional contribution of this paper is more narrow. While the venues (and hence audiences) for this and [10] are different, the contribution of this paper is twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model, and bounds for density estimation. The latter are new, but the former is essentially contained in [10].
