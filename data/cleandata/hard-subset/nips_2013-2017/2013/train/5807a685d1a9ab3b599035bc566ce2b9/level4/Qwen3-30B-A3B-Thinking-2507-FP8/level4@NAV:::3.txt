

The paper investigates local privacy, where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of the other \(n-1\) users, reporting only this signal to the curator. It analyzes the setting via minimax expected error: an adversary selects a distribution over user types, and the curator chooses an α-DP local mechanism to minimize the ℓ₂ distance between estimations derived from the original data and the reported signals (e.g., sums or averages). The authors establish a lower bound for the minimax rate in ℓ₂ estimation for users drawn from a discrete distribution over \(d\) types, demonstrating that randomized response achieves this bound and is thus optimal. For density estimation, they show that a perturbation scheme where each user perturbs only their own type meets the lower bound.  

The work is well-motivated and significant, establishing that classic procedures achieve optimal rates under local privacy. It would interest the NIPS community, warranting acceptance. However, three reservations remain: (1) The explanation of minimax bounds could be simplified, and the pervasive use of cumbersome notation (e.g., expressing bounds solely for ℓ₂ could avoid unnecessary complexity); (2) A discussion comparing ℓ₁ norms (more restrictive) or relating the bounds to existing ℓ₁ lower bounds in non-local DP would strengthen the analysis; (3) The minimax bounds appear tailored to local privacy; extending the framework to broader settings would be valuable. Notably, the paper should explicitly emphasize that optimal performance is achieved when users randomize solely based on their own type (ignoring others' signals), a key insight.  

A new reservation: The FOCS 2013 accepted papers list now includes work closely related to Section 3, suggesting this paper extends prior results. While still recommending acceptance, enthusiasm is somewhat reduced due to this overlap. The core contribution—establishing minimax optimality of randomized response in local privacy for ℓ₂ estimation—remains valuable.
