Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations

The paper explores the concept of local privacy, where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based on their type and the signals of the other n-1 users, and then reports their type to the data curator. The authors analyze this setting using the min-max expected error framework, where an adversary selects a distribution over inputs (types for the n users), and a corresponding α-DP local scheme is chosen to minimize the distance between an estimation derived from the original data and a similar estimation derived from the reported signals. The authors derive a lower bound for the min-max rate for l2 distance estimations and users drawn from a discrete distribution over d types. They demonstrate that the simple technique of randomized response achieves this bound, rendering it optimal. The authors then extend their analysis to the problem of density estimations, where the lower bound on the min-max rate is met by a perturbation scheme in which each person perturbs only their own type.

The paper is well-written and important, as it establishes that classic procedures are optimal, meeting certain lower bounds. The NIPS community will likely find it interesting, and I recommend acceptance.

However, I have several reservations. Firstly, the explanation of min-max bounds could be simplified. The paper's notation is also cumbersome, particularly since all bounds apply to the l2 norm; it would be more intuitive to phrase the bounds with respect to this norm. Secondly, I would appreciate a discussion on the l1 norm, which is more restrictive, or a comparison between the given bounds and l1 norms, as well as other lower bounds in differential privacy (albeit in a classical, non-local setting). Furthermore, the min-max bounds presented seem tailored to the local-privacy setting, and I wish the authors had considered a broader range of settings and provided min-max lower bounds for those as well. On a positive note, the paper shows that in the local setting, where users can randomize the type they report based on the remaining n-1 users, the simple scheme in which each person randomizes the report solely based on their type is optimal; I suggest the authors explicitly state this in the text.

Additionally, considering the recently published list of FOCS 2013 accepted papers, I now perceive the paper as an extension of an existing work, particularly the results in Section 3. While I still recommend acceptance, my endorsement is not as strong as it was initially. The paper's analysis of the local privacy framework using min-max rate bounds, derivation of lower bounds, and demonstration of the optimality of randomized response are all notable contributions.
