This paper investigates the problem of probability estimation under the local privacy model, where each of the *n* users perturbs their individual data to ensure α-differential privacy before reporting it to a central curator. The analysis is conducted within the min-max framework, framed as a game where an adversary chooses a data distribution, and the statistician designs a local privacy mechanism to minimize the expected estimation error.

The authors derive a min-max lower bound on the l2 estimation error for discrete distributions over *d* types. Crucially, they demonstrate that this bound is tight, as it is matched by the classic randomized response mechanism, thereby proving its optimality in this setting. This analysis is extended to the problem of density estimation, where a similar result is shown: a simple, local perturbation scheme achieves the optimal min-max rate.

This is a well-executed and important paper. The primary contribution—demonstrating the optimality of classical mechanisms like randomized response by matching them to tight min-max lower bounds—is a significant finding that will be of great interest to the community. I recommend acceptance.

However, I have several suggestions for improvement that could strengthen the paper:

1.  **Presentation and Clarity:** The exposition of the min-max framework could be simplified for greater clarity. The notation is often cumbersome. For instance, since the derived bounds are specific to the l2 norm, could the results be stated directly in terms of this norm to make them more accessible?

2.  **Scope and Context:** The discussion could be broadened. A comparison to the more restrictive l1 norm, or a discussion on how the presented l2 bounds relate to l1, would be highly valuable. Similarly, situating these results with respect to other lower bounds in the (albeit non-local) differential privacy literature would provide important context.

3.  **Generality and Key Insights:** The min-max bounds appear specifically constructed for the local privacy model. A brief discussion on whether this analytical approach could extend to other privacy settings would be welcome. On a related and very positive note, a key insight is that while the local model permits complex protocols where a user's report can depend on others, the optimal mechanism is a simple one where each user randomizes their report based solely on their own data. This is a powerful finding, and I urge the authors to state this conclusion more explicitly in the main text.

**Post-rebuttal update:** An additional point of consideration has arisen. The list of accepted papers for FOCS 2013 has been published, and it appears there is significant overlap with a paper presented there. This new context positions the current submission, particularly the results in Section 3, more as an extension of existing work. Consequently, while I still believe the paper merits publication, my recommendation for acceptance is now made with this reservation about its incremental novelty.

In summary, the paper provides a valuable analysis of the local privacy model through the lens of min-max rates, establishing sharp lower bounds and proving the optimality of randomized response. The results are solid, though the paper's overall impact is somewhat tempered by recent related work.
