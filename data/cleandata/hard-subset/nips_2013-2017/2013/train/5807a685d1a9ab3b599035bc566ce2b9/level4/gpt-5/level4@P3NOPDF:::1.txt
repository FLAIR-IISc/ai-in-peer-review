Summary:
This paper studies statistical estimation under local privacy, where data holders privatize their data before releasing it to an untrusted algorithm. Using Fano/Le Cam-style arguments, the authors derive information-theoretic lower bounds on the accuracy of any estimator operating on locally privatized data. A key corollary is that randomized response achieves the optimal convergence rate (and can be optimal in a stronger sense in particular settings). The paper also proposes estimators for density estimation within this model. Caveat: I did not review the supplementary material.

Strengths:
- Provides clear, minimax-style lower bounds for estimation with locally privatized data, offering a principled understanding of the statistical price of local privacy.
- Identifies randomized response as rate-optimal, aligning a classic mechanism with modern minimax guarantees.
- For density estimation, the bounds appear new and offer concrete guidance on achievable rates.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- The exposition at times blurs the distinction between local privacy and the standard (central) differential privacy model. This risks misleading readers, especially where the text appears to draw general conclusions about “differential privacy” from results that apply specifically to local privacy.
- The connection to the existing differential privacy literature is underdeveloped. Important related work and contrasts (including with central DP results) are not made explicit.
- Some statements describing the implications of the results are imprecise or too broad (e.g., the comment at the bottom of page 4 about the effect of providing differential privacy at level alpha).
- The restriction on alpha in Theorem 1 is not fully motivated. It is unclear whether the bound is informative for practically relevant regimes (e.g., alpha around 1).
- The paper could better explain the role of the loss/utility functional rho and how different choices (e.g., squared error, total variation, KL) are handled by the analysis.
- The discussion of density estimation would benefit from comparisons to other methods (including M-estimation) and to alternative histogram-based approaches for discrete data.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
1) Scope and model clarity:
- Please make the local privacy model explicit up front and keep it clearly separated from central differential privacy. Emphasize that your bounds and optimality claims pertain to local privacy. Avoid general statements about “differential privacy” unless you qualify that you are in the local model, which can be significantly more restrictive than the central model.
- The statement at the bottom of page 4 about the effect of providing differential privacy at level alpha reads as too general; as written, it risks being incorrect outside the local model. Please restate it to be specific to local privacy and to the problem class and loss under study.

2) Population vs. sample-level quantities:
- Early in the paper, explicitly distinguish between population parameters/risks and sample-based estimators/risks (cf. your response to Reviewer 9). Clarify at first mention which objects are privatized (individual samples) and which are targets of estimation (population parameters), and how this impacts the lower-bound construction.

3) Relation to prior work:
- Add a thorough comparison to related work, including the M-estimation procedure by Lei (and related privacy-preserving estimation methods), as well as other relevant references brought up by reviewers. Position what is new here (particularly for density estimation) versus what is covered in prior work (including the now-appeared reference [10], which seems to overlap on the local privacy framework).
- For density estimation, compare your estimator and guarantees to other approaches, including discrete histogram estimators and alternative local randomization schemes. Even if randomized response is rate-optimal in your setting, it would help readers to understand constants, practical performance, and when other algorithms might be competitive.

4) On the restriction on alpha (Theorem 1):
- Please clarify whether the restriction on alpha is necessary for the proof technique or for the statement to hold. Is it an artifact of the particular packing/Fano argument, or is there a counterexample beyond that range?
- Comment on the regime alpha ≈ 1. Several empirical and theoretical works suggest this is a practically relevant privacy level. Do your lower bounds and matching procedures remain informative here (in both rates and constants)?

5) Choice of rho and utility measures:
- Provide more guidance on admissible rho and how different loss functions map into your lower bounds. A few concrete examples (e.g., squared loss for mean estimation, Hellinger/TV for density estimation) would make the scope and applicability much clearer. Indicate any technical conditions (e.g., convexity, Lipschitzness) needed by your analysis.

6) Randomized response and alternatives:
- While you establish that randomized response achieves the optimal rate, please discuss how other local mechanisms (e.g., k-ary randomizers, optimized privatization maps for specific tasks) compare in terms of constants or adaptivity. For discrete histogram estimation, many algorithms exist; a brief comparative discussion or experiment would provide useful practical context.

7) Technical clarity:
- Where you use a Le Cam or Fano argument, it would be helpful to outline the construction at a high level: the choice of packing, the contraction due to local privacy, and how divergence measures are controlled. This will aid readers who are less familiar with these techniques, even if full details remain in the supplement.
- Please ensure all assumptions (on distributions, smoothness classes for density estimation, sample size regimes, etc.) are stated near the main theorems.

Addendum after rebuttal:
- Please add an explicit, early clarification of population vs. sample statistics and the role of privatization at the sample level.
- Provide a sharper comparison to related work, including Lei’s M-estimation procedure and other references raised by reviewers.
- After reviewing the now-appeared [10], I view the additional contribution of this paper more narrowly: (i) a clear exposition of the local privacy model, and (ii) new bounds for density estimation under local privacy. The latter strikes me as the primary technical novelty; much of the general local privacy framework appears to be covered by [10]. The paper would benefit from framing its contributions accordingly.

Overall assessment:
This paper tackles an important question—the statistical limits of learning under local privacy—and delivers clear minimax lower bounds with matching upper bounds via randomized response. The work is valuable, particularly for density estimation. However, the paper should more carefully delineate the local privacy model, temper general statements about “differential privacy,” and more fully engage with related work. Addressing these clarity and positioning issues, and commenting on practically relevant alpha and choices of rho, would substantially strengthen the paper.
