SUMMARY
This paper is a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript. It uses a Fano/Le Cam–style argument to derive lower bounds for estimation algorithms that operate on private data in the local (untrusted analyst) privacy model. As a corollary, randomized response is shown to be optimal in an appropriate sense for some problems. As a caveat, I did not review the supplementary material.

STRENGTHS
- The results clearly characterize the limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee privacy in the local model.
- The lower-bound framework is clean and leads to interpretable corollaries, including the (conditional) optimality of randomized response.

WEAKNESSES AND CONCERNS
- Some imprecision in the exposition could confuse readers, especially regarding the relationship between local privacy and (global) differential privacy.
- Connections to existing results on differential privacy appear to be missing or too thin, which may lead readers to overgeneralize the conclusions beyond the local model.

DETAILED COMMENTS
- Scope of the privacy model: The restriction to local privacy is essential for the results but makes the model quite different from the standard (central) differential privacy setting; many readers may not appreciate this distinction. This confusion is exacerbated by statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4 (“Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level alpha …”). The paper should avoid overly broad—and possibly incorrect—statements that risk conflating local and global DP guarantees or their statistical consequences. Be explicit throughout about which model a statement pertains to.
- Assumption on alpha in Theorem 1: Is the restriction on alpha necessary? Empirically, for certain learning problems under (global) differential privacy, alpha ≈ 1 may be the most one can expect. It is unclear what the presented bound says about this practically relevant regime; please clarify whether the theorem extends to alpha around 1 or explain why it does not.
- Utility measures: Some commentary on possible choices of rho would help readers see how different utility measures are captured by the analysis and how sensitive the bounds are to this choice.
- Related work (Lei and others): How does the proposed density estimator compare to the M-estimation procedure of Lei? This paper is not cited, but it seems highly relevant. More generally, there are many approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. Randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence in your setting, but how do other algorithms compare in practice or under slightly different assumptions? A brief empirical or theoretical comparison would be valuable.

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL
- Clarify population vs. sample quantities: The distinction between population parameters and sample statistics should be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (cf. the response to Reviewer 9). This will help prevent misinterpretation of what the lower bounds and procedures target.
- Related work comparison: Please include a direct comparison to related work, including Lei and other papers raised by reviewers, to properly situate the contribution.
- Positioning relative to [10]: A closer inspection of [10], which has now appeared, narrows the additional contribution of the current paper. While the venues (and thus audiences) differ, the contribution here is twofold: (1) a careful exposition of the local privacy model and (2) bounds for density estimation. The latter appear to be new; the former is essentially already contained in [10]. Please make this positioning explicit.

Overall, the paper offers a clear lower-bound framework in the local privacy setting and an appealing optimality result for randomized response, but it needs sharper exposition about the privacy model, clearer treatment of practically relevant parameter regimes (alpha around 1), and a more complete discussion of related work and alternative estimators.
