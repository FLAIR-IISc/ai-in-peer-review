This paper provides a detailed study of the estimation of probability distributions in a stringent setting where data is kept private even from the statistician. The authors derive sharp minimax rates of convergence for estimation in these locally private settings, exhibiting fundamental trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate. They also provide tools to allow movement along the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum.

The paper begins by discussing the original motivation for providing privacy in statistical problems, which was first discussed by Warner. The authors then introduce the concept of local privacy, which is a type of privacy that guarantees that any individual providing data has plausible deniability about taking a value x, since any outcome z is nearly as likely to have come from some other initial value x'. They also discuss the concept of differential privacy, which has strong semantic privacy guarantees that make it a good candidate for declaring a statistical procedure or data collection mechanism private.

The authors then introduce the minimax framework used throughout the paper, which is based on the idea of reducing the estimation problem to a testing problem of identifying a point θ ∈ Θ from a collection of well-separated points. They also discuss the concept of α-private minimax rate, which is the central object of interest for this paper.

The paper then presents two main results, which quantify the penalty that must be paid when local privacy at a level α is provided in multinomial estimation and density estimation problems. The first result implies that for estimation of a d-dimensional multinomial probability mass function, the effective sample size of any statistical estimation procedure decreases from n to nα2/d whenever α is a sufficiently small constant. The second result exhibits an interesting departure from standard minimax estimation results, showing that the minimax integrated squared error scales (in the sample size n) as (α2n)−2β/(2β+2) in the locally private case.

The authors also provide efficiently implementable algorithms that attain sharp upper bounds as companions to their lower bounds. They show that Warner's randomized response procedure enjoys optimal sample complexity and that histogram and orthogonal series estimators can achieve the optimal rate of convergence in certain cases.

Overall, the paper provides a comprehensive study of the estimation of probability distributions in locally private settings. The results provide a fundamental understanding of the trade-offs between privacy and convergence rate and offer insights into the design of privacy-preserving statistical inference and estimation procedures.
