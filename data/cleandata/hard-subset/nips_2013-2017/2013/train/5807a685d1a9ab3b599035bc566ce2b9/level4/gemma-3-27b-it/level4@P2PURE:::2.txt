This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation when individual data privacy must be preserved. The authors focus on the setting of local privacy and analyze two estimation problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. For both, they establish sharp minimax rates of convergence. Regarding multinomial estimation, they demonstrate that local privacy introduces a reduction in effective sample size that is quadratic in the privacy parameter α. However, given that α is often a small constant, the effective sample size remains comparable to that of the non-private scenario. For density estimation, the authors show that achieving the optimal rate from the non-private setting becomes impossible under the constraint of local privacy. 

Overall, the results are compelling, and the proofs appear correct to the best of my assessment. To my knowledge, this work represents the first investigation of minimax bounds for density estimation under a privacy constraint.

My primary concern is the close relationship between the minimax bound for multinomial estimation presented here and prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy, which is not adequately acknowledged. Specifically, the papers by Hardt and Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012) examine relevant lower bounds for the error of linear queries under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem considered in this paper can be viewed as a special case of the linear counting queries analyzed in those works, using the same L₂ error metric. The key distinction lies in the privacy model – this paper utilizes local differential privacy, while Hardt & Talwar and De consider central differential privacy. Since local privacy imposes a stronger constraint, lower bounds derived for differential privacy also apply to local privacy. Therefore, it is important to understand whether the bound established here for local privacy improves upon existing bounds for differential privacy.

Concerning the density estimation problem, the statement of the result is somewhat unclear. The authors claim a higher lower bound in the local privacy setting compared to the non-private setting; however, their minimax bound applies specifically to densities expressible with a trigonometric basis. I question whether the non-private lower bound (eq. 13) holds generally for the Sobolev space defined in Definition 1, or only for this special case of trigonometric basis functions. A fair comparison requires that the non-private lower bound also apply to the trigonometric basis case.

Regarding the rebuttal: while the clarification of the relation to prior worst-case lower bounds is helpful, the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also considers Mean Square Error, should be noted. I recommend the authors incorporate these explanations and missing references into their paper.

In summary, this paper presents sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function and probability density function estimation with privacy guarantees. The results are interesting and the paper is well-written, but lacks sufficient acknowledgement of relevant existing results on noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy.
