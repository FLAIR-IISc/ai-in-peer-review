- Paper provides a rigorous minimax analysis of statistical estimation under α-local differential privacy, linking decision theory and differential privacy formalisms.  
- Focuses on two problems: multinomial (discrete) estimation and nonparametric density (continuous) estimation.  
- For multinomial estimation, establishes sharp minimax lower bounds on L1 and L2 errors.  
- Key multinomial finding: α-local privacy effectively reduces sample size from n to about nα²/d (d = number of categories).  
- Warner’s randomized response is shown to achieve the multinomial lower bound — it is minimax optimal.  
- For nonparametric density estimation, local privacy changes the polynomial exponent in the convergence rate (a stronger effect than mere sample-size reduction).  
- Classical rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} for β-smooth densities becomes (nα²)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under local privacy.  
- Practical estimators (histograms and orthogonal series) are provided that achieve these slower, sharp rates.  
- Strength: high-quality, significant contribution giving sharp matching upper and lower bounds quantifying the “price of privacy.”  
- Strength: novel and impactful result that randomized response is minimax optimal and that local privacy fundamentally alters nonparametric rates.  
- Weakness: exposition is dense (Section 2 notation) and could be made more accessible with simpler notation or added intuition.  
- Weakness: the paper should explicitly highlight the important implication that simple, non-interactive mechanisms are optimal.  
- Weakness: would benefit from more contextualization with standard (central) differential privacy results and discussion of extending the minimax framework to other privacy models.  
- Weakness/concern: apparent overlap with the authors’ FOCS 2013 paper on multinomial estimation—needs clarification of novel contributions relative to that work.  
- Recommendation: accept — strong, novel contributions (especially density results), though novelty of multinomial portion is partially tempered by overlap with prior FOCS work.
