SUMMARY: This paper, a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript, employs a Fano/LeCam-style argument to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms operating on private data under the assumption of an untrusted algorithm. A notable corollary is that randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy within this framework. As a caveat, this review does not consider the supplementary material.

PROS AND CONS:
- The results characterize fundamental limitations inherent in learning from privacy-perturbed data.
- The commentary surrounding the results contains some imprecision that could potentially mislead a casual reader (see detailed comments below).
- Connections to existing work on differential privacy appear to be missing.

ADDITIONAL COMMENTS:
- The restriction to local privacy, critical for the results presented, defines a privacy model distinct from the standard differential privacy model. Many readers may not readily appreciate this difference, and statements like those on the bottom of page 4 – “Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…” – may exacerbate this confusion. The authors should avoid broad, and potentially inaccurate, generalizations when discussing the implications of their findings.
- Is the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 truly necessary? Experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may represent the practical limit achievable for certain learning problems under differential privacy; therefore, it is unclear whether the established bound provides useful information in this case.
- Further discussion of possible choices for $\rho$ would be beneficial, allowing readers to understand how different utility measures can be accommodated within the analysis.
- How does this density estimator compare to the M-estimation procedure proposed by Lei? While not cited, the authors should be aware of this related work.
- Numerous approaches exist for histogram estimation with discrete data. While randomized response achieves an optimal convergence rate, a comparative analysis of other algorithms' performance would strengthen the paper.

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:
- The distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (as acknowledged in the response to Rev.9).
- A comparison to related work, including Lei’s work and contributions highlighted by other reviewers, is crucial for providing context.
- A closer examination of [10], which has now appeared, leads me to view the paper’s contribution more narrowly. While the venues (and thus audiences) differ, the contribution lies primarily in a careful exposition of the local privacy model and, secondarily, in bounds for density estimation. The latter is novel, but the former is largely contained within [10].
