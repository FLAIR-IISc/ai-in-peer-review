This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation under the constraint of preserving individual data privacy, specifically focusing on a privacy definition known as local privacy. The authors examine two probability estimation problems: multinomial estimation and density estimation. For both problems, they establish sharp minimax rates of convergence. Notably, for the discrete multinomial estimation problem, they demonstrate that local privacy results in a reduction in the effective sample size, which is quadratic in the privacy parameter alpha. Given that alpha is often considered a small constant, the effective sample size remains of the same order as in the non-private case. In contrast, for the density estimation problem, the authors show that the optimal rate achievable in the non-private setting is no longer attainable when local privacy must be preserved.

Overall, the results are highly interesting. Upon reviewing the proofs, they appear to be correct. To the best of my knowledge, the minimax bound for density estimation under a privacy constraint has not been previously considered.

My primary comment is that the minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely related to prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy, but this connection is not acknowledged. Specifically, two papers, "On the geometry of differential privacy" by Hardt and Talwar (STOC, 2010) and "Lower bounds in differential privacy" by De (TCC, 2012), address highly relevant problems. These papers study worst-case lower bounds for the error of linear queries under the constraint of differential privacy. The probability estimation problem considered in this paper is, in fact, a special case of the linear counting query examined in those papers, with the same L2 metric used to measure error. The key difference lies in the fact that this paper focuses on local differential privacy, whereas Hardt, Talwar, and De consider differential privacy. Since local privacy imposes a stronger constraint than differential privacy, lower bounds for differential privacy also serve as lower bounds for local privacy. I am curious to know whether the bound for local privacy presented in this paper improves upon previous bounds for differential privacy.

Regarding the density estimation problem, the statement of the result is somewhat confusing. The authors claim that the lower bound in the local privacy setting is higher than in the non-private setting. However, the minimax bound established in this paper pertains to the special case where the density can be expanded using a trigonometric basis. I am unsure whether the lower bound for the non-private setting, as given in equation (13), holds for the general Sobolev space defined in Definition 1 or only for the special case of the trigonometric basis. A fair comparison can only be made if the non-private lower bound holds for the trigonometric basis.

Additional comments for the rebuttal:

The feedback partially clarifies the relationship to previous worst-case lower bounds. However, it is worth noting that the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, "The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases" (STOC 2013), also considers Mean Square Error. I suggest that the authors incorporate their explanations and missing references into the paper.

This paper proves sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function (pmf) and probability density function (pdf) estimation with a privacy guarantee. The results are interesting, and the paper is well-written. Nevertheless, there is a notable lack of discussion regarding known results on the noise complexity lower bound for differential privacy, which are highly relevant to this paper.
