### Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

**Summary**

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of statistical estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy. In the local privacy model, each individual's data is privatized before being aggregated, offering a stronger privacy guarantee than the more common central model. The authors connect the tools of minimax statistical theory with the formal definition of local privacy to derive fundamental trade-offs between statistical accuracy and the level of privacy (α).

The paper presents two main results:

1.  **Multinomial Estimation:** For the problem of estimating the parameters of a d-dimensional multinomial distribution, the authors establish sharp minimax rates of convergence. They show that providing α-local privacy incurs a penalty that effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `n * α^2 / d`. A key consequence of this analysis is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal for this task.

2.  **Nonparametric Density Estimation:** For the problem of estimating a probability density function belonging to a Sobolev smoothness class Fβ, the authors demonstrate a more severe consequence of local privacy. The minimax convergence rate changes from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(n * α^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent represents a fundamental shift in the difficulty of the problem, not just a constant-factor loss in sample size.

For both problems, the authors complement their information-theoretic lower bounds with concrete, computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these optimal rates, thus proving the bounds are sharp.

**Strengths and Evaluation Criteria**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the intersection of privacy and machine learning.

*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper's primary strength is its foundational nature. It is one of the first works to rigorously quantify the statistical cost of local privacy using the powerful framework of minimax decision theory. This approach provides a principled way to understand the inherent trade-offs, moving beyond the analysis of specific algorithms. The result that randomized response is optimal is particularly elegant and important, as it provides a modern theoretical justification for a classic, practical technique. The finding for density estimation—that the convergence rate itself changes—is a novel and deep insight, demonstrating that local privacy can fundamentally alter the statistical character of a problem.

*   **Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is very high. The authors adeptly apply sophisticated tools from information theory and statistical decision theory (Le Cam's method, Fano's inequality) to the privacy setting. The claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs, and the matching upper and lower bounds provide a complete picture of the problems studied. The authors are careful to point out subtleties, such as the fact that simple noise-addition mechanisms (like adding Laplace noise to samples) are suboptimal for density estimation, which motivates the more sophisticated mechanism they propose.

*   **Clarity:** While the paper is technically dense, it is generally well-organized. The problem is clearly formulated in the introduction, and the main results are stated upfront. However, the presentation assumes a high level of familiarity with minimax theory, which may make it challenging for some members of the NIPS community. The notation, while precise, is at times cumbersome. A more intuitive walkthrough of the minimax framework and its components in the context of privacy could broaden the paper's accessibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas for potential improvement.

1.  **Accessibility:** As mentioned above, the paper's technical density could be a barrier to some readers. In the final version, the authors might consider adding a brief, high-level paragraph in Section 2 explaining the intuition behind minimax lower bounds (e.g., "to prove no algorithm can be better than X, we construct a 'hard' statistical problem and show that any private algorithm struggles to distinguish between different possibilities...").

2.  **Contextualization:** The draft reviewer correctly notes that the bounds in the paper are related to the broader literature on differential privacy. While the focus is rightly on the *local* model, a short discussion comparing the derived rates to known rates in the *central* model of differential privacy would be valuable. This would help readers better understand the specific price of decentralizing the privacy mechanism.

3.  **Highlighting Practical Implications:** A key result of the paper is that simple, non-interactive mechanisms (where each user randomizes their own data independently of others) are optimal. This is a powerful and practical finding that is somewhat understated. The authors could make this point more explicit in the introduction or discussion, as it is a strong argument for the feasibility of locally private data collection.

4.  **Regarding Concurrent Work:** The field of privacy is moving very quickly. As noted by the draft review, it is important to situate this work with respect to other recent publications. The authors should ensure that the final version clearly delineates its contributions from any closely related work that may have appeared in other top-tier venues (e.g., FOCS 2013). Based on the submission, the work appears novel and significant, but explicitly addressing its relationship to the latest literature would strengthen it further.

**Summary of Recommendation**

This paper addresses an important and timely problem with technical depth and significant results. It provides a foundational theoretical understanding of the costs of local privacy for fundamental statistical tasks. The establishment of sharp minimax bounds and the demonstration that classic, simple mechanisms are optimal are major contributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a foundational, minimax analysis of local differential privacy, a significant and novel contribution.
*   Derives sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial and density estimation.
*   Proves the optimality of the classic and practical randomized response mechanism within a modern privacy framework.
*   Uncovers a fundamental change in the convergence rate for nonparametric density estimation under local privacy, a deep statistical insight.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The presentation is technically dense, which may limit its accessibility to a broader audience without a strong background in statistical theory.
*   The relationship to other very recent work in this fast-moving area could be clarified further.

Despite minor concerns about presentation, the paper's fundamental contributions, technical quality, and the importance of its results strongly support its acceptance. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
