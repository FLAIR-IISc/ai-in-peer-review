This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation under the privacy constraint of local privacy. The authors analyze both multinomial and density estimation problems, establishing sharp minimax rates of convergence for each. For multinomial estimation, they demonstrate a quadratic reduction in effective sample size due to the privacy parameter, α; however, given that α is often small, this reduction is often manageable. Regarding density estimation, the authors show that preserving local privacy renders the optimal non-private convergence rate unattainable. 

Overall, the results are compelling and the proofs appear sound. To the best of my knowledge, the derived minimax bound for density estimation under a privacy constraint represents a novel contribution.

My primary concern relates to the multinomial estimation result. This work bears a close connection to existing literature on noise complexity for differential privacy, a connection that is not explicitly acknowledged. Specifically, the papers by Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012) address relevant lower bounds for linear query error under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem considered here can be viewed as a specific instance of the linear counting queries studied in those works, utilizing the same L2 error metric. Given that local privacy imposes a stronger constraint than differential privacy, lower bounds for the latter also apply to the former. It would be valuable to understand whether the local privacy bound presented here improves upon existing bounds for differential privacy.

Concerning the density estimation result, the statement of the lower bound is somewhat unclear. The authors indicate a higher lower bound in the local privacy setting compared to the non-private setting, but this comparison is specific to densities expressible with a trigonometric basis. I question whether the non-private minimax bound (eq. 13) holds generally for the Sobolev space defined in Definition 1, or only for the trigonometric basis case. A fair comparison requires the non-private bound to apply specifically to the trigonometric basis.

Regarding a potential rebuttal: While the initial response partially addresses the relationship to prior lower bound work, the paper by Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also considers Mean Square Error, should also be discussed. I recommend the authors incorporate these explanations and missing references into the revised manuscript.

In summary, this paper provides valuable and well-presented sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function and probability density function estimation with privacy guarantees. However, the work would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related results on noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy.
