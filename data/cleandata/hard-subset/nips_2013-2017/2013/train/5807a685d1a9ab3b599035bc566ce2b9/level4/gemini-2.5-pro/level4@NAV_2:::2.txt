This paper presents a detailed minimax analysis of probability estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy. The authors study two canonical problems: estimation of discrete multinomial distributions and nonparametric estimation of continuous probability densities. For both settings, the work derives sharp minimax convergence rates that formally characterize the trade-off between the degree of privacy (parameterized by α) and statistical efficiency. For d-dimensional multinomial estimation, a primary result is that local privacy induces a reduction in the effective sample size from `n` to approximately `nα^2/d`. The paper also demonstrates that a variant of Warner's classic randomized response technique from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal. For the nonparametric estimation of β-smooth densities, the authors uncover a more severe penalty: the optimal convergence rate is fundamentally altered, with the exponent degrading from `2β/(2β+1)` in the standard non-private case to `2β/(2β+2)` under local privacy. These theoretical lower bounds are complemented by the analysis of computationally efficient estimators that achieve these new rates.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of fundamental importance: quantifying the statistical cost of enforcing strong privacy guarantees. By establishing sharp minimax rates, the work provides universal, algorithm-independent limits on estimation accuracy, offering a crucial contribution to the fields of privacy-preserving data analysis and statistical theory. The results establish a clear "privacy-utility continuum" that can inform the design of future statistical methods.
*   **Originality:** The application of minimax decision theory to derive sharp rates under local differential privacy is both novel and powerful. While related problems have been studied, the discovery that local privacy changes the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate for density estimation is a particularly surprising and insightful finding. Furthermore, proving the optimality of a classic 50-year-old mechanism (randomized response) within this modern framework is an elegant and compelling result.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and rigorous piece of work. The claims are well-supported by a combination of tools from information theory, such as Le Cam's and Fano's methods, and the specifics of the local privacy definition. The inclusion of both lower bounds and matching upper bounds attainable by practical algorithms makes the paper's conclusions particularly strong and convincing.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is strong overall, but its contribution could be further clarified by better positioning it relative to a closely related line of research and by enhancing the clarity of one of its key comparisons.

1.  **Relation to Lower Bounds for Linear Queries:** The minimax result for multinomial estimation is directly connected to a significant body of work in the theoretical computer science community on lower bounds for answering linear queries under differential privacy (e.g., Hardt & Talwar, STOC 2010; De, TCC 2012). Estimating a multinomial's probability vector is equivalent to a set of counting queries. The current manuscript does not discuss this literature. The authors should:
    *   Acknowledge this line of work and add the appropriate citations.
    *   Discuss the connection between their minimax framework (which is an average-case risk over the data-generating distribution) and the worst-case error bounds typically studied for private query release.
    *   Clarify how their lower bound for the *local* privacy model compares to existing bounds for the *central* privacy model. Since local privacy is a more stringent constraint, a tighter bound would be a significant result, and this comparison should be made explicit to highlight the paper's contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Comparison in Density Estimation:** The central claim for density estimation relies on a comparison between the private minimax rate and the classical non-private rate from Eq. (13). The paper's lower bound is established for a Sobolev class defined specifically via a trigonometric basis. For the comparison to be fully convincing, it would be helpful to briefly state that the cited classical rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` also applies to this specific function class. While this is likely true given the reference to Tsybakov [22], explicitly confirming that the comparison is on equal footing would remove any potential ambiguity for the reader and strengthen the impact of this important result.
