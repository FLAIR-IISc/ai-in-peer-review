Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper analyzes the problem of local privacy – a setting where each user outputs an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of other users, reporting this signal to a data curator. The authors employ the framework of minimax expected error to analyze this setting, considering an adversary who chooses a distribution over inputs (user types) and a mechanism designer who selects an α-DP local privacy scheme to minimize the distance between estimations derived from the original data and those derived from the reported signals (typically sums, aggregations, or averages). They present a lower bound for the minimax rate for l₂ distance estimation with users drawn from a discrete distribution over *d* types. Notably, they demonstrate that the simple randomized response technique achieves this bound, establishing its optimality. The authors then extend this analysis to the problem of density estimation, where a perturbation scheme wherein each user perturbs only their own type also meets the derived lower bound on the minimax rate.

This is a valuable and important contribution, demonstrating that established procedures are, in fact, optimal by meeting fundamental lower bounds. I believe the NIPS community would find the results interesting and therefore recommend acceptance.

However, I do have several reservations. Firstly, the explanation of minimax bounds could be more accessible; simplifying the presentation would improve clarity. The paper also employs somewhat cumbersome notation. Given that all bounds are presented for the l₂ norm, framing them directly with respect to this norm would be preferable. Secondly, a discussion of the l₁ norm – which is more restrictive – or a comparison of the obtained bounds to those using the l₁ norm, as well as existing lower bounds in differential privacy (though often applied in a classical, non-local setting), would be beneficial. Lastly, the minimax bounds appear specifically tailored to this local privacy setting. Exploring a broader range of settings and providing minimax lower bounds for those scenarios would strengthen the work. I appreciate that, despite the local setting allowing users to randomize their reported type based on the signals of others, the optimal scheme ultimately involves each user randomizing independently based only on their own type. Explicitly stating this key observation in the text would enhance the paper.

A further reservation arises from the recent publication of accepted papers from FOCS 2013. I now view this work as an extension of existing results, particularly those presented in Section 3. Therefore, while I still recommend acceptance, my recommendation is somewhat less enthusiastic than before.

In summary, the paper analyzes local privacy through the lens of minimax rate bounds, providing lower bounds on the rate and demonstrating that randomized response achieves the corresponding upper bound – a significant result.
