- Provides detailed theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy, establishing sharp minimax rates for discrete and continuous settings and quantifying privacy–efficiency trade-offs.  
- Demonstrates the optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism for survey sampling under local privacy.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: coherently integrates minimax theory with differential privacy.  
- Achieves sharp, tight minimax convergence rates.  
- Practical relevance: links theoretical results to a widely-used privacy mechanism.  
- Overall clear presentation, though some sections could be simplified.  
- Well-defined focus on the challenging local privacy model distinct from global privacy.  
- Notational burden: heavy and occasionally cumbersome; could be streamlined (e.g., consistent use of ℓ2 norm).  
- Initial minimax introduction is complex and could use more intuition or a simplified example.  
- Limited discussion of alternative norms (e.g., ℓ1) and relation to classical (non-local) DP lower bounds.  
- Scope limitation: should acknowledge broader minimax landscape and limits of applying these bounds to other scenarios.  
- Key optimality insight (randomized response) is not emphasized explicitly enough in the main text.  
- Related work connection (FOCS 2013) should be discussed more clearly to place contributions in context.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem, technically rigorous, practically relevant, well-written, and advances understanding of privacy-preserving inference.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility issues from notation and exposition, limited discussion of alternative norms and broader bounds, and some novelty tempered by prior FOCS 2013 work.  
- Overall assessment: strong, valuable contribution with minor weaknesses; recommendation to accept.
