This paper presents a detailed theoretical study of probability estimation under the constraints of local differential privacy. The authors employ a minimax decision-theoretic framework, using Le Cam's and Fano's methods, to derive sharp lower bounds on the convergence rates of estimators operating on locally privatized data. This approach allows for a precise characterization of the trade-off between the degree of privacy (α) and statistical accuracy. The analysis focuses on two fundamental problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For multinomials, the key result is that local privacy effectively reduces the sample size from n to nα²/d, which establishes the minimax optimality of Warner's classic randomized response mechanism. For density estimation over Sobolev classes, the paper reveals a more substantial cost: the exponent in the polynomial convergence rate is altered, degrading from n⁻²ᵝ/(²ᵝ⁺¹) in the non-private setting to (nα²)⁻²ᵝ/(²ᵝ⁺²) under local privacy. The authors complete their analysis by providing computationally efficient algorithms that achieve these lower bounds, thereby confirming their sharpness.

This review is based on the main text; the supplementary material was not examined in detail.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper rigorously addresses a significant and timely problem by formally characterizing the fundamental statistical costs of enforcing strong, local privacy guarantees. The application of minimax theory is a powerful and appropriate tool for this purpose.
*   The results are technically strong and provide clear, quantitative insights into the privacy-utility trade-off. The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal is an elegant result that connects a foundational technique from the survey literature to the modern differential privacy framework.
*   The analysis of density estimation provides a novel and important finding: local privacy can fundamentally alter the polynomial convergence rate, rather than just impacting constant factors or the effective sample size.
*   The work constitutes a complete contribution by complementing the theoretical lower bounds with matching upper bounds achieved by constructive and practical estimators.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity on Privacy Models:** The paper could be more precise in its terminology to avoid potential confusion between the "local" privacy model used here and the more common "central" model of differential privacy. For readers not deeply familiar with this distinction, the difference is critical but easily missed. For example, statements like "...the effect of providing differential privacy at a level α..." (end of Section 3.1) are overly broad and could be misconstrued. Explicitly and prominently clarifying this distinction early in the paper would significantly improve its clarity.
*   **Contextualization with Related Work:** The discussion of related literature could be expanded to better position the paper's contributions. For instance, while the optimality of randomized response is a key result, a brief comparison to other private mechanisms for estimating discrete distributions would provide valuable context. Furthermore, the density estimation results should be compared with the highly relevant work on private M-estimators by Lei (NIPS 2011), which is currently not cited.
*   **Scope and Practical Implications:**
    *   Theorem 1 on multinomial estimation includes a restriction that α ∈ [0, 1]. As practical applications often consider α in this range (and sometimes slightly larger), it would be beneficial for the authors to comment on the necessity of this constraint and the behavior of the bounds as α approaches 1.
    *   The general framework uses a semi-metric ρ to measure error. A short discussion on how the analysis might adapt to different choices of ρ, corresponding to different utility measures, could help readers appreciate the broader applicability of the techniques.
