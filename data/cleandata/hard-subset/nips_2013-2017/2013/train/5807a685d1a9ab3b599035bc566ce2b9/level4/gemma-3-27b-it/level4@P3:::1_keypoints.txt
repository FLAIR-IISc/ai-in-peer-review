- Paper derives sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy in both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) settings.
- Provides matching lower and upper bounds, showing local privacy imposes a fundamental penalty (reduced effective sample size / slower convergence).
- Proves optimality of Warner’s randomized response mechanism for private survey sampling.
- Leverages minimax decision theory and differential privacy tools to formally characterize privacy–utility trade-offs.
- Builds on classical and recent works (Warner; contemporary differential privacy literature) and fills a gap by formalizing disclosure risk for decision-theoretic analysis.
- Strength: strong theoretical rigor using a solid minimax + differential privacy framework with rigorous guarantees.
- Strength: derived minimax rates are sharp, indicating fundamental, non-improvable trade-offs.
- Strength: practical relevance via the optimality result for a widely used randomized response method.
- Strength: clear problem formulation, definitions of local privacy, and stated assumptions.
- Strength: novel combination of differential privacy and minimax theory for analyzing private estimation.
- Weakness: some claims about applicability to “differential privacy” at large lack precision—local privacy differs importantly and needs qualification.
- Weakness: Theorem 1’s restriction on privacy parameter α and implications for practical α values (e.g., α ≈ 1) need more discussion and exploration for larger α.
- Weakness: sensitivity to the chosen utility metric (squared error) should be addressed by discussing extensions to other semi-metrics ρ.
- Weakness: related work omissions (notably Lei [19] on differentially private M-estimators) and possibly other histogram estimation approaches.
- Weakness: contribution on the local privacy model may be seen as incremental given prior presentations; clarify unique contributions (density estimation bounds, exposition).
- Overall: strong, technically sound paper with insightful results; recommendation is acceptance with minor revisions.
- For acceptance: addresses a fundamental, timely problem; sharp theoretical results; well-written; randomized response optimality is significant.
- Against acceptance: needs clearer connection to broader differential privacy literature, expanded discussion of α, and a more comprehensive related work review.
