

Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations  
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  
The paper investigates local privacy, where each user generates an α-differentially private signal based on their own type and the signals of the other \(n-1\) users, reporting this to the data curator. It analyzes this setting using minimax expected error: an adversary selects a distribution over user types, and the analyst chooses an α-DP local mechanism to minimize the \(\ell_2\) distance between an estimator derived from the original data and one from the reported signals (typically sums, aggregations, or averages). The authors establish a lower bound on the minimax rate for \(\ell_2\) estimation when users are drawn from a discrete distribution over \(d\) types. They demonstrate that randomized response achieves this bound, proving its optimality. The analysis extends to density estimation, where a perturbation scheme—perturbing only one's own type—meets the lower bound.  

The paper is well-motivated and significant, establishing that classic techniques like randomized response are minimax-optimal. Its contributions would interest the NIPS community, supporting acceptance. However, three reservations remain:  
1. The explanation of minimax bounds could be simplified. The notation is cumbersome; since all bounds apply to the \(\ell_2\) norm, they should be explicitly framed in this norm.  
2. A discussion of \(\ell_1\) norms would strengthen the paper, given their greater restrictiveness in differential privacy. Comparisons to \(\ell_1\) bounds or existing non-local minimax results would be valuable.  
3. The minimax bounds appear tailored specifically to local privacy. Extending the analysis to broader settings would broaden the paper's impact.  

Notably, the paper correctly identifies that optimal local privacy requires each user to randomize solely based on their own type (not the other \(n-1\) users' signals), though this key insight should be stated explicitly.  

A new reservation arises: the FOCS 2013 accepted papers list now includes work closely related to Section 3, suggesting this paper extends prior results. Despite this, the paper remains valuable and merits acceptance, though less strongly than initially recommended.  

The paper effectively analyzes local privacy via minimax rate bounds, establishes tight lower bounds, and demonstrates randomized response achieves optimality—a well-executed result.
