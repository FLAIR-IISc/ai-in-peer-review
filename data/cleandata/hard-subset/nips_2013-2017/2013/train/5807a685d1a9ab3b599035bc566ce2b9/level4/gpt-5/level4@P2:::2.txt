This paper analyzes probability estimation under local differential privacy using a minimax decision-theoretic framework. The authors study two settings: (i) discrete multinomial estimation over the simplex, and (ii) nonparametric density estimation in Sobolev classes. They derive sharp minimax rates under α-local privacy and provide private mechanisms that achieve these rates.

For multinomial estimation in d dimensions, the paper shows that local privacy reduces the effective sample size from n to roughly nα^2/d (for small α), yielding tight lower and upper bounds in both ℓ2 and ℓ1 risk. A consequence is that Warner’s randomized response is optimal (up to constants) for locally private survey sampling. For nonparametric density estimation in Sobolev classes with smoothness β, the minimax rate under local privacy changes from the classical n−2β/(2β+1) to (nα^2)−2β/(2β+2), and the authors exhibit computationally efficient histogram (β=1) and orthogonal series mechanisms (β≥1) that achieve these rates.

Quality: The results appear technically sound, with matching lower and upper bounds and careful use of information-theoretic tools (e.g., Fano/Le Cam and mutual information bounds specialized to local privacy). The mechanisms are simple and efficiently implementable. I did not find errors in the arguments I checked.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written. Two clarifications would improve readability: (a) make explicit the regimes in which the multinomial rate represents only a constant-factor degradation (fixed d, constant α) versus severe degradation (growing d), and (b) clarify the function class used in the density results when comparing to the classical nonprivate rate.

Originality: To my knowledge, sharp minimax rates for density estimation under local privacy have not appeared before. The tight characterization for multinomial estimation under local privacy and the optimality of (a variant of) randomized response are also valuable and well supported.

Significance: The work provides fundamental privacy–accuracy tradeoffs with tight constants and constructive mechanisms. It should influence both theory (minimax under privacy constraints) and practice (design of locally private data collection protocols).

Relation to prior work and missing references: The multinomial estimation problem is closely related to worst-case lower bounds for answering linear counting queries under (central) differential privacy, e.g., Hardt & Talwar (STOC’10), De (TCC’12), and Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC’13), which also study mean-squared error. Because local privacy is a stronger constraint than central DP, lower bounds for central DP apply a fortiori to locally private mechanisms. The paper should (i) add these references, (ii) explain precisely how its multinomial lower bound relates to those linear-query bounds, and (iii) state whether the dependence on d and α improves over the central-DP bounds or is incomparable due to the different privacy model (local vs. central) and non-interactive assumption. This will help position the contribution within the differential privacy lower-bound literature.

Comments on specific results:
- Multinomial estimation: The paper’s message would be clearer if it explicitly stated that for fixed d and constant α, the ℓ2 risk degrades only by a constant factor relative to the nonprivate MLE, whereas for large d the effective sample size becomes nα^2/d, which can be significantly smaller. The argument establishing optimality of (a modified) randomized response is clean and informative.
- Density estimation: The lower bound and matching upper bounds are convincing. However, the comparison to the classical nonprivate rate should be aligned on the same function class. The paper defines Fβ via an orthonormal basis and then specializes to the trigonometric basis for the lower bound. Please clarify whether the nonprivate baseline (eq. (13)) is stated and proved for the same trigonometric-based Sobolev class (periodic boundary conditions) or for the general Sobolev class in Definition 1. The comparison is only fair if the nonprivate lower bound holds for the exact same model. The discussion noting that simple additive Laplace perturbation of Xi is suboptimal is useful; the argument via deconvolution constraints is well placed.

Pros:
- Sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation under local privacy, with matching constructive mechanisms.
- Clear articulation of privacy–statistical efficiency tradeoffs and the optimality of randomized response.
- Technically solid and broadly relevant.

Cons:
- Missing or underemphasized connections to central-DP lower bounds for linear queries (Hardt–Talwar 2010; De 2012; Nikolov–Talwar–Zhang 2013), including mean-squared error comparisons.
- Needs clarification on the function class used in the density comparison to the nonprivate baseline.
- Results focus on the non-interactive local model; a brief discussion of potential extensions or limits relative to interactive or central models would be helpful.

Additional comments to the rebuttal:
- The added discussion of worst-case lower bounds is helpful. Please also cite and discuss Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which analyzes mean squared error in sparse/approximate regimes, and clarify how your α- and d-dependence compares in the multinomial setting.

Recommendation: Strong paper with important contributions. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions to address the missing references and to clarify the scope of the density comparison and the multinomial rate interpretation.
