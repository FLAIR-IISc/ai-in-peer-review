

This paper establishes minimax bounds for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, addressing both multinomial and density estimation problems. The results are significant, as they demonstrate sharp rates of convergence and clarify the impact of privacy on statistical efficiency. The authors show that for multinomial estimation, local privacy reduces the effective sample size by a factor of Ω(α²), where α is the privacy parameter (e.g., ε in (ε,0)-local DP). Since α is often small but constant, the effective sample size remains comparable to the non-private case, implying minimal efficiency loss in practice. For density estimation, the authors prove that the optimal non-private rate is unattainable under local privacy, establishing a strictly slower minimax rate. Overall, the technical contributions are novel and well-executed, with proofs appearing correct. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to derive minimax bounds for density estimation under local privacy, making it a valuable addition to the literature.

However, two critical issues require clarification before publication. First, the minimax bound for multinomial estimation is closely related to existing work on noise complexity in differential privacy. Specifically, Hardt and Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012) derive worst-case lower bounds for linear queries (including counting queries) under central differential privacy using the L₂ error metric. The probability estimation problem studied here is a special case of their linear counting query framework. Crucially, local differential privacy imposes a stronger constraint than central DP, so lower bounds for central DP also apply to local DP. The paper should explicitly compare its bound to these prior results and clarify whether the local privacy bound improves upon the central DP bounds (e.g., by a constant factor or under specific conditions). The authors’ current omission of these foundational works (Hardt & Talwar, De) weakens the novelty claim. Additionally, Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which analyzes mean square error under differential privacy, should be cited as it addresses related noise complexity. The rebuttal partially addresses this but fails to integrate the references into the main text.

Second, the density estimation result requires precision. The paper states that the local privacy lower bound exceeds the non-private bound (Section 4), but this comparison hinges on the non-private lower bound in Equation (13). Definition 1 specifies the Sobolev space for the density class, yet Equation (13)’s lower bound is derived for the special case of trigonometric basis expansions (not general Sobolev spaces). For a valid comparison, the non-private lower bound must also be established for the trigonometric basis. The current presentation conflates these settings, making the claim that "the optimal non-private rate is unattainable" potentially misleading. The authors should explicitly state whether Equation (13) applies to the trigonometric basis (as used in the local privacy analysis) or to the broader Sobolev class. If the latter, the comparison is invalid, and the paper must either derive a non-private lower bound for the trigonometric basis or revise the claim.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but these omissions undermine its contribution. The multinomial result’s connection to prior work on DP noise complexity is not merely tangential—it is central to understanding the paper’s novelty. Similarly, the density estimation comparison must be rigorously grounded in the same functional basis. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance pending revisions to clarify the literature context and resolve the basis mismatch in the density estimation analysis. The results themselves are compelling and warrant publication once these clarifications are made.
