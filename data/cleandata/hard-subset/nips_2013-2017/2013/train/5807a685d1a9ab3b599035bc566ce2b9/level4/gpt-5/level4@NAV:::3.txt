Review- Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimations
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The paper studies local privacy—a setting where each user emits an \alpha-differentially private signal as a function of her own type and the signals of the other n-1 users, and reports that to the data curator. The analysis is carried out in a minimax expected-error framework: an adversary chooses a distribution over inputs (types for the n users), and we choose an \alpha-DP local mechanism to minimize the gap between a statistic computed from the true data and the analogous statistic computed from the reported signals (typically sums, aggregates, or averages). The authors then establish a lower bound on the minimax rate for l_2-distance estimation when users are drawn from a discrete distribution over d types. They show that the simple randomized response mechanism achieves this bound and is therefore optimal. The paper then turns to density estimation, where again the lower bound on the minimax rate is matched by a perturbation scheme in which each individual perturbs only her own type.

The paper is clear and significant—it demonstrates that a classic procedure attains the best possible performance by matching the lower bounds. I believe the NIPS community would find it of interest, and I therefore recommend acceptance.

I do have a few reservations. First, stylistically, the exposition of minimax bounds could be simplified. The notation is also rather heavy—since all bounds given are for the l_2 norm, couldn’t the results be stated directly with respect to this norm? Second, I would have appreciated a comment about the l_1 norm, which is more stringent, or a discussion relating the presented bounds to l_1 guarantees, as well as to other lower bounds in differential privacy (which, admittedly, are more aligned with the classical, non-local, setting). Lastly, the minimax bounds considered seem “tailored” to the local-privacy model. It would be valuable to consider a broader family of settings and provide minimax lower bounds for them as well. I do like, however, that although the local model allows users to randomize the reported type based on the remaining n-1 users, the simple scheme in which each person randomizes solely as a function of her own type is optimal; I wish the authors would state this explicitly.

A new reservation: now that the list of FOCS 2013 accepted papers is public, the submission appears to be an extension of an existing paper, especially regarding the results in Section 3. I therefore still recommend acceptance, but less strongly than before.

The paper analyzes local privacy through minimax rate bounds, provides lower bounds on the rate, and shows that randomized response matches the upper bound. A nice result.
