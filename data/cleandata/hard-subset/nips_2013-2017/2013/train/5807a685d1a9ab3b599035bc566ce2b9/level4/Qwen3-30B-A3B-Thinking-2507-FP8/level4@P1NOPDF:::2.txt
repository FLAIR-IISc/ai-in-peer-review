

This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, addressing both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors establish sharp minimax rates, revealing that for multinomial estimation, local privacy reduces the effective sample size quadratically in the privacy parameter ε (typically small), resulting in an effective sample size comparable to the non-private case. For density estimation, they show the optimal non-private rate is unattainable under local privacy.

The results are theoretically significant and well-motivated. The proofs appear sound, and to the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to derive minimax bounds for density estimation under local differential privacy.

My primary concern is the insufficient discussion of prior work on noise complexity for differential privacy, which directly relates to the multinomial estimation results. The problem studied here is a special case of linear counting queries under differential privacy, where the error metric (L₂) matches that of Hardt & Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012). These papers establish worst-case lower bounds for linear queries under *central* differential privacy. Since local differential privacy imposes a stricter constraint than central differential privacy, their lower bounds also apply to the local setting. The authors should clarify whether their local privacy bound improves upon these existing central DP bounds, or if it merely inherits the central DP lower bound as a special case.

Regarding density estimation, the comparison between private and non-private rates requires clarification. The paper’s lower bound (Theorem 3) assumes a density expandable in a trigonometric basis. However, the non-private lower bound (eq. 13) is stated for the general Sobolev space defined in Definition 1. For a valid comparison, the non-private bound must also be derived for the trigonometric basis case. The authors should explicitly confirm whether eq. (13) holds under this specific basis or only for the general Sobolev space.

The rebuttal partially addresses the connection to Hardt & Talwar and De, but overlooks Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also studies mean square error under differential privacy. The authors should incorporate this reference and explicitly discuss how their local privacy bound relates to prior central DP lower bounds.

In summary, the paper presents interesting results on minimax estimation under local privacy, but the lack of engagement with foundational work on differential privacy’s noise complexity weakens the novelty claim. The connection to Hardt & Talwar, De, and Nikolov et al. must be clarified, particularly regarding whether the local privacy bound is tighter than known central DP lower bounds. The density estimation comparison also requires precise specification of the basis assumption. With these revisions, the paper would make a stronger contribution. The technical content is compelling, and the writing is generally clear—only the literature context needs strengthening.
