- Detailed study of distribution estimation when data is kept private even from the statistician, deriving sharp minimax rates and privacy–convergence trade-offs.
- Introduces and formalizes local privacy (plausible deniability) and discusses differential privacy as a stronger semantic guarantee.
- Employs a minimax framework that reduces estimation to hypothesis testing and defines the central object: the α-private minimax rate.
- Main result (multinomial): effective sample size reduces from n to roughly n·α^2/d for d-dimensional multinomial estimation when α is a small constant.
- Main result (density estimation): under local privacy the minimax integrated squared error scales as (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, differing from standard nonprivate rates.
- Provides efficient algorithms matching upper bounds: Warner’s randomized response is sample-optimal; histogram and orthogonal series estimators achieve optimal rates in some regimes.
- Offers tools to move along the privacy–statistical efficiency continuum.
- Strength: Comprehensive theoretical analysis with matching lower and upper bounds and supporting experiments.
- Strength: Clear organization, consistent notation, and lucid exposition of the minimax framework.
- Weakness: Focus restricted to local privacy, which may not cover all practical scenarios.
- Weakness: Commentary can confuse casual readers and lacks stronger connections to existing differential privacy results.
- Weakness: The restriction on α in Theorem 1 may be unnecessary; experiments hint that α ≈ 1 might be the practical limit for some DP problems.
- Originality: Presents a novel perspective on distribution estimation under local privacy and clarifies fundamental trade-offs.
- Significance: Results guide the design of privacy-preserving statistical inference and have implications for ML and data analysis.
- Overall: Well-written and technically sound work that advances understanding of privacy vs. convergence trade-offs.
