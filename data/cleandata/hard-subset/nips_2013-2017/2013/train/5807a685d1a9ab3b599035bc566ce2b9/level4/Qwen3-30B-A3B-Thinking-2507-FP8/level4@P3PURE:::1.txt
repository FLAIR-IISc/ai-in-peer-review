

SUMMARY: This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript that employs a LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on estimation algorithms operating on privately perturbed data when the algorithm is untrusted by the data holder. As a corollary, randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy under certain conditions.  

PROS AND CONS:  
- The results provide a characterization of the fundamental limitations of learning from data perturbed to guarantee privacy.  
- The commentary on the results contains imprecisions that may confuse less familiar readers.  
- The connections to established results in differential privacy remain underdeveloped.  

ADDITIONAL COMMENTS:  
- The restriction to local privacy (a critical aspect of the analysis) substantially differentiates the privacy model from standard differential privacy. Many readers may overlook this distinction, which is exacerbated by statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$..." Such broad claims are potentially incorrect and should be avoided when describing the results.  
- The necessity of the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 warrants clarification. Experimental evidence suggests $\alpha \approx 1$ represents a practical limit for certain learning problems under differential privacy; it is unclear whether the bound remains informative in this regime.  
- A brief discussion of possible choices for $\rho$ would help readers understand how different utility measures are captured within the analysis.  
- The paper omits comparison to Lei's M-estimation procedure for density estimation (a relevant work not cited), despite the authors' likely awareness of it.  
- While randomized response achieves the optimal rate for density estimation, the paper does not address how alternative histogram estimation approaches for discrete data compare.  

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:  
- The distinction between population and sample statistics requires clearer, more explicit exposition at the paper's outset (cf. response to Reviewer 9).  
- A comparative discussion of related work (including Lei's approach and other relevant contributions highlighted by reviewers) is essential for contextualizing the contribution.  
- Upon closer inspection of [10] (now published), the paper's contribution appears more narrowly defined: it offers a careful exposition of the local privacy model and density estimation bounds. The former is largely covered in [10], while the latter represents a novel contribution. Although the venues (and thus audiences) differ, this clarifies the paper's scope.
