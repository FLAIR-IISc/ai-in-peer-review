SUMMARY: This paper presents a NIPS-formatted version of an arXiv manuscript, employing a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive a lower bound on estimation algorithms operating on private data in a setting where the data holder does not trust the algorithm. A key result is that randomized response emerges as an optimal strategy under certain conditions. 
As a caveat, this review does not cover the supplementary material.

PROS AND CONS:
- The results characterize the fundamental limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to ensure privacy.
- The commentary accompanying the results contains some imprecision, which could potentially lead to confusion for a casual reader (see details below).
- The paper appears to lack discussion of connections to existing work on differential privacy.

ADDITIONAL COMMENTS:
- The restriction to local privacy, crucial for the results presented, establishes a privacy model significantly different from the differential privacy model. Many readers may not fully appreciate this distinction. This potential confusion is exacerbated by statements such as the one on the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…" The authors should avoid such broad (and potentially inaccurate) generalizations when describing their findings.
- Is the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 truly necessary? Experimental results suggest that $\alpha \approx 1$ may represent the upper bound achievable for certain learning problems (under differential privacy), making it unclear what insight the bound provides in this common case.
- Providing some discussion of possible choices for $\rho$ would be helpful, allowing readers to understand how different utility measures can be captured within the analysis.
- How does this density estimator compare to the M-estimation procedure proposed by Lei? This paper is not cited, yet the authors should be aware of it.
- Numerous approaches exist for histogram estimation with discrete data. While randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, a comparison of other algorithms’ performance would strengthen the paper.

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:
- The distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (as also noted in the response to Rev. 9).
- A comparison to related work, including Lei’s paper and others raised by other reviewers, is important for providing context.
- After a closer examination of [10], which has now appeared, I believe the contribution of this paper should be viewed more narrowly. While the venues (and therefore audiences) differ, the paper's contribution is twofold: a careful exposition of the local privacy model, and bounds for density estimation. The latter are novel, but the former is largely contained within [10].
