Summary
The paper studies minimax bounds for probability estimation under local privacy constraints. It treats two canonical problems—multinomial (discrete) probability mass function estimation and nonparametric density estimation—and establishes sharp rates of convergence in both settings. For multinomial estimation, the authors show that imposing local privacy reduces the effective sample size by a factor quadratic in the privacy parameter α. When α is a constant, the rate matches the order of the non-private case up to constants. For density estimation, the paper shows that the classical non-private optimal rate is unattainable under local privacy.

Strengths
- The questions are fundamental and timely. Establishing sharp minimax rates under local privacy is of clear interest to both statistics and privacy communities.
- The results for density estimation under local privacy appear to be new, to the best of my knowledge, and the paper gives a clean characterization of the (degraded) optimal rate.
- The presentation is generally clear, and the proofs (at least the parts I checked) appear sound.

Relation to prior work and main concerns
The multinomial estimation result is closely related to known worst-case lower bounds for differentially private linear queries. In particular:
- Hardt and Talwar, On the Geometry of Differential Privacy (STOC 2010)
- De, Lower Bounds in Differential Privacy (TCC 2012)
- Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang, The Geometry of Differential Privacy: The Sparse and Approximate Cases (STOC 2013)

These works study mean squared error (L2) lower bounds for answering collections of (linear) counting queries under central differential privacy. The multinomial estimation problem considered here can be viewed as a special case of answering a set of linear counting queries (the category counts/empirical frequencies). Because local privacy is strictly stronger than central differential privacy—any mechanism that operates by locally privatizing each datum yields an ε-DP mechanism in the central model—their lower bounds also apply to locally private procedures. This makes it important to clarify precisely how the lower bounds and rates in the present paper improve upon, match, or differ from those earlier results. At a minimum, the paper should:
- Explicitly discuss the mapping from multinomial estimation to linear counting queries and the L2 error metric alignment.
- Compare the dependence on α (or ε), dimension, and n to the bounds in Hardt–Talwar, De, and Nikolov–Talwar–Zhang.
- State whether the new lower bounds are strictly stronger in the local model or whether the novelty lies primarily in the statistical framing and sharp constants/rates for multinomial estimation.

Clarity of the density estimation result
The statement for density estimation could be clearer about the function class. The paper’s minimax bounds are established for densities representable in a trigonometric (Fourier) basis, which is a common surrogate for Sobolev spaces. Equation (13) presents a non-private lower bound; however, it is not explicit whether this bound holds:
- for the general Sobolev class defined in Definition 1, or
- specifically for the trigonometric basis subclass used in the private lower bound.

For a fair rate comparison between private and non-private settings, the non-private lower bound should be stated and proved for the exact same function class as the private lower bound (i.e., the trigonometric/Sobolev class used in the private analysis). If the current non-private lower bound only covers the larger Sobolev class, please either specialize it or add a citation showing the same rate for the trigonometric subclass.

Requests for clarification and suggestions
- Please add an explicit discussion comparing your multinomial lower bound to the central-DP lower bounds above, including the precise parametric dependence and whether your bounds are strictly tighter in the local model. If the improvement is primarily conceptual (minimax/statistical framing, sharpness, or matching upper bounds), clarify that as well.
- Include the STOC 2013 paper by Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang in the related work and explain how its MSE bounds relate to your results.
- Make the relationship between α (used here) and the more standard ε explicit early on, and state all rates using consistent notation.
- For multinomial estimation, it would help to include a brief discussion of the optimal mechanisms achieving the upper bounds (e.g., variants of randomized response) and how they match the lower bounds up to constants/log factors.
- For density estimation, please state clearly: (i) the smoothness assumptions, (ii) the precise basis and truncation scheme used by the estimator, and (iii) whether adaptivity to unknown smoothness is possible under local privacy, or if the rates require tuning to the smoothness parameter.
- If known, comment on whether there is a privacy–smoothness trade-off: do smoother classes still suffer the same qualitative separation from the non-private rate under local privacy, or is there a threshold beyond which the gap narrows?

Overall evaluation
This is a strong and interesting paper that establishes sharp minimax rates for two central estimation problems under local privacy. The results for density estimation are novel and important. The paper is generally well written and technically solid.

The main weakness is an incomplete positioning with respect to prior lower bounds on the noise/error complexity for differentially private linear queries, which are directly relevant to the multinomial problem. With appropriate additions to the related work and a careful, explicit comparison to Hardt–Talwar (2010), De (2012), and Nikolov–Talwar–Zhang (2013), along with clarification of the function class used in the density-estimation comparison, I would be enthusiastic about acceptance.
