**Summary**

This paper derives information-theoretic lower bounds for statistical estimation algorithms that operate under the local privacy model, where the data holder does not trust the analyst collecting the data. Using Fano's inequality and LeCam's method, the authors establish fundamental limits on the accuracy of any estimation procedure as a function of the sample size and the privacy level. A key corollary of this analysis is that for discrete distribution estimation, the well-known randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal. The work provides a rigorous characterization of the trade-offs between utility and privacy in this setting.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its rigorous, information-theoretic approach to a significant problem. By establishing fundamental lower bounds, the results characterize the inherent limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee local privacy. This helps to formally quantify the statistical cost of privacy and provides a benchmark against which practical algorithms can be measured. The finding that randomized response is an optimal strategy for certain tasks is an elegant and important result.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the technical results are sound, the paper could be significantly strengthened by improving the clarity of its exposition and providing better context with respect to related work.

1.  **Distinction from Central Differential Privacy:** The paper's focus is on the *local* privacy model, which is a crucial distinction from the more commonly discussed *central* differential privacy model. This distinction may not be immediately apparent to all readers, and the current manuscript could do more to clarify it. For instance, statements such as the one at the bottom of page 4 ("Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…") are overly broad and potentially misleading, as they seem to generalize a result from the local model to the broader concept of differential privacy. The authors should be more precise in their language to avoid such confusion and should explicitly contrast the local and central models early in the paper. Clarifying the difference between population and sample statistics at the outset would also help frame the contribution more clearly.

2.  **Context and Comparison to Related Work:** The paper is missing connections to several relevant works, which makes it difficult to situate the contributions within the existing literature.
    *   The work of Lei on M-estimation under privacy constraints seems highly relevant, yet it is not cited or discussed. A comparison to this work would be valuable.
    *   For the problem of histogram estimation for discrete data, there are many existing approaches. While the paper shows that randomized response achieves the optimal rate of convergence, a discussion of how other competing algorithms perform in this context would provide a more complete picture.
    *   As noted in the addendum, a closer inspection of reference [10] (which has now appeared in a different venue) reveals significant overlap in the exposition of the local privacy model. This narrows the primary contribution of the present manuscript to the novel lower bounds for density estimation. Acknowledging this and clearly delineating the novel aspects of this paper from those in [10] is essential for accurately framing the contribution.

3.  **Scope of the Theoretical Results:**
    *   It is unclear if the restriction on the privacy parameter $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 is necessary. In practice, particularly under central differential privacy, values of $\alpha \approx 1$ are often considered the limit of what is useful for many learning problems. The paper should comment on whether the bound provides insight into this practically relevant regime.
    *   The analysis is framed in terms of a general loss function $\rho$. While this generality is a strength, the paper would be more accessible if it included a brief discussion of concrete choices for $\rho$ (e.g., L1 error, L2 error, KL divergence) and explained how different utility measures can be captured by the framework.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents valuable and technically sound information-theoretic bounds for estimation under local privacy. However, its impact is currently limited by a lack of clarity regarding the specific privacy model and insufficient engagement with related literature. To improve the paper, the authors should (1) more carefully and explicitly distinguish the local privacy model from the central differential privacy model throughout the manuscript, (2) incorporate a thorough discussion and comparison with related work, including the papers by Lei and others mentioned by the reviewers, and (3) clarify the relationship of their contribution to that of reference [10], highlighting that the main novelty lies in the density estimation bounds. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and better communicate its important contributions to the NIPS community.
