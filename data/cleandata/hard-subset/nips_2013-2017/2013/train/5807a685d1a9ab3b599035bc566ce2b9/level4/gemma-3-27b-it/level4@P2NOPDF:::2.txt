This paper investigates minimax bounds for probability estimation problems—multinomial estimation and density estimation—under the constraint of local privacy. The authors establish sharp rates of convergence for both problems. Their analysis of multinomial estimation reveals a quadratic reduction in effective sample size due to the privacy parameter α. However, they note that this reduction is often manageable as α is frequently a small constant, resulting in an effective sample size comparable to the non-private scenario. For density estimation, the authors demonstrate that preserving local privacy renders the optimal non-private convergence rate unattainable.

Overall, the results presented are compelling and the paper is generally well-written. The proofs appear correct to the best of my understanding, and, to my knowledge, this work represents the first consideration of minimax bounds for density estimation under a privacy constraint.

My primary concern lies in the paper’s limited engagement with existing literature on noise complexity for differential privacy, particularly in the context of multinomial estimation. The established minimax bound bears a close relationship to prior work, specifically Hardt and Talwar (STOC 2010) and De (TCC 2012). These papers derive worst-case lower bounds on the error of linear queries under differential privacy. The probability estimation problem analyzed in this paper can be viewed as a specific instance of the linear counting queries considered in those works, and the error metrics employed—the L2 metric—are consistent across all three. The key distinction is the focus on local differential privacy here, versus the standard differential privacy considered by Hardt, Talwar, and De. Given that local privacy imposes a stronger constraint, lower bounds derived for differential privacy also apply to local privacy. I would like to understand whether the local privacy bound presented in this paper offers any improvement over existing bounds for differential privacy.

Regarding density estimation, the phrasing of the result is somewhat unclear. The authors state that the lower bound in the local privacy setting exceeds that of the non-private setting. However, the minimax bound presented is specifically for densities expressible using a trigonometric basis. I question whether the non-private lower bound, as stated in equation (13), holds generally for the Sobolev space defined in Definition 1, or if it is also limited to the trigonometric basis case. A fair comparison requires the non-private lower bound to be applicable to the same function class (trigonometric basis) as the privacy-preserving bound.

**Comments regarding the rebuttal:**

The rebuttal provides partial clarification of the relationship to existing worst-case lower bounds. However, the work of Nikolov, Talwar, and Zhang (STOC 2013), which also utilizes the Mean Square Error metric, should also be acknowledged. I recommend the authors incorporate these explanations and the missing references into the revised manuscript to provide a more comprehensive contextualization of their contributions.

In summary, this paper provides valuable, sharp minimax bounds for probability mass function (PMF) and probability density function (PDF) estimation with privacy guarantees. The results are of interest to the community, and the paper is well written. Nevertheless, a more thorough discussion of relevant literature concerning noise complexity lower bounds for differential privacy would significantly strengthen the work.
