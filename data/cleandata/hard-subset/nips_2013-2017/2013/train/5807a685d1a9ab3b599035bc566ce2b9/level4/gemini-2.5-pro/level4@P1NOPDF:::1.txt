**SUMMARY**

This paper, a NIPS-formatted version of an existing ArXiv manuscript, uses a Fano/LeCam-style argument to derive minimax lower bounds on estimation error in the local privacy model. In this setting, individual data holders do not trust the central aggregator or algorithm curator. As a corollary, the paper demonstrates that randomized response is a rate-optimal strategy for this problem.

Note: This review does not cover the supplementary material.

**PROS AND CONS**

*   **Pro:** The results provide a valuable characterization of the fundamental limitations of learning from data that has been perturbed to guarantee local privacy.
*   **Con:** The commentary surrounding the results contains some imprecisions that could be confusing to readers.
*   **Con:** The paper is missing a discussion of its connections to existing results in the broader differential privacy literature.

**ADDITIONAL COMMENTS**

1.  The paper's focus on **local privacy** is a crucial distinction from the more common centralized model of differential privacy. This difference may not be appreciated by all readers and should be stated more explicitly upfront. This potential for confusion is exacerbated by broad statements like the one at the bottom of page 4: "Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…" The authors should be more precise in their claims, ensuring they are carefully scoped to the local privacy setting to avoid such generalizations.

2.  Is the restriction on $\alpha$ in Theorem 1 necessary? Practical applications of differential privacy often find that $\alpha \approx 1$ is an upper limit for achieving useful utility. It is therefore unclear how informative the theorem's bound is for this important and practical regime.

3.  A discussion of the possible choices for the loss function $\rho$ would be beneficial. This would help readers understand how different utility measures can be captured by the analysis.

4.  How does the proposed density estimator compare to the M-estimation procedure of Lei? This appears to be a highly relevant work, but it is not cited or discussed.

5.  While randomized response is shown to achieve the optimal rate of convergence, there are many other approaches to histogram estimation for discrete data. A comparison with these alternative algorithms would provide valuable context for the results.

**ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL**

After reading the author rebuttal and consulting the cited work, my assessment has been updated as follows:

*   The distinction between population and sample statistics needs to be explained more clearly and explicitly at the beginning of the paper (as noted in the authors' response to Reviewer 9).

*   A comparison to related work (specifically Lei's work and others mentioned by another reviewer) is essential for properly contextualizing the paper's contribution.

*   A closer inspection of reference [10], which has now been published, leads me to view the contribution of this paper more narrowly. While the venues and target audiences for this work and [10] differ, this paper's primary contributions appear to be twofold: 1) a careful exposition of the local privacy model, and 2) new bounds for density estimation. The bounds for density estimation are novel, but the exposition of the local privacy model is largely contained in [10].
