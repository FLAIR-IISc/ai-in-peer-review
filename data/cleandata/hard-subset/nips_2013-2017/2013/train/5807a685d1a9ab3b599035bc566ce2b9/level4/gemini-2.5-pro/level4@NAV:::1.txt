SUMMARY: This work presents a NIPS submission based on a prior ArXiV manuscript. It employs a LeCam-style argument to establish a lower bound for estimation algorithms in the local privacy model, where the data holder does not trust the algorithm curator. A key corollary of this analysis is that randomized response is demonstrated to be an optimal strategy in a particular sense. Please note that this review does not take the supplementary material into account.

PROS AND CONS:
- The paper's results effectively characterize the fundamental trade-offs involved in learning from privacy-preserving perturbed data.
- The discussion surrounding the results lacks precision at times, which may cause confusion for non-expert readers.
- The manuscript fails to adequately connect its findings to the existing literature on differential privacy.

ADDITIONAL COMMENTS:
- The local privacy setting is central to the paper's results, yet it differs significantly from the more common central model of differential privacy—a distinction that may be lost on many readers. This potential for confusion is amplified by statements like the one at the bottom of page 4 ("Thus, for suitably large sample sizes n, the effect of providing differential privacy at a level $\alpha$…"). The authors should refrain from making such general, and potentially inaccurate, statements when contextualizing their findings.
- Is the constraint on alpha in Theorem 1 truly necessary? Empirical studies in differential privacy often show that $\alpha \approx 1$ is a practical and important regime, so it is uncertain how the derived bound informs our understanding of such cases.
- Including a discussion on the potential choices for the loss function $\rho$ would be beneficial. This would help readers understand how the analysis can accommodate different utility measures.
- A comparison to Lei's M-estimation procedure is notably absent. How does the proposed density estimator relate to this work? It is surprising that this highly relevant paper is not cited or discussed.
- Beyond randomized response, numerous other algorithms exist for histogram estimation with discrete data. Although randomized response is shown to achieve the optimal convergence rate, a discussion of how these alternative methods perform would provide valuable context.

ADDENDUM AFTER REBUTTAL:
*   The authors should more clearly and explicitly differentiate between population and sample statistics, preferably early in the paper (as suggested in the response to Reviewer 9).
*   For proper context, a comparison with related work is essential, including the paper by Lei and others identified by another reviewer.
*   A review of reference [10], which has since been published, narrows my view of this paper's contribution. While the target venues and audiences for this manuscript and [10] differ, the current paper’s contribution appears to be twofold: (1) a detailed presentation of the local privacy model and (2) new bounds for density estimation. The bounds are a novel contribution; however, the exposition of the local privacy model is largely already present in [10].
