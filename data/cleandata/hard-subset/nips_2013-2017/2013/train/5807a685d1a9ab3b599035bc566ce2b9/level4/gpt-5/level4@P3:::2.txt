Summary
This paper studies probability estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each datum is privatized before collection. The authors formalize the problem via channels Qi producing private views Zi that satisfy α-local differential privacy, and analyze the α-private minimax risk across two settings: (i) multinomial probability mass function estimation in d dimensions and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. Using information-theoretic lower bounds (Le Cam/Fano) combined with novel divergence bounds tailored to LDP, they derive sharp minimax rates and provide matching, computationally efficient private mechanisms.

For multinomial estimation, they show that under LDP the mean-squared error behaves as min{1, d/(nα^2)}, and the ℓ1 error as min{1, d/√(nα^2)}. Thus, relative to the non-private MSE rate 1/n, LDP induces an effective sample size reduction n → nα^2/d (for small α). They also show a variant of Warner’s randomized response achieves these rates, establishing (with projection to the simplex) its minimax optimality.

For density estimation over Sobolev classes of smoothness β, they show a qualitative shift: the optimal non-private risk n^{-2β/(2β+1)} cannot be attained under LDP. Instead, the optimal LDP rate is (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, and they provide two mechanisms matching this rate: a privatized histogram for β=1 and an orthogonal series estimator with a tailored locally private sampling scheme for general β≥1. They further explain why simple additive Laplace noise to raw data is suboptimal via deconvolution arguments.

Relation to prior work
This work connects classical randomized response (Warner ’65) with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory. It usefully complements the global DP literature on linear query lower bounds and geometry (e.g., Hardt & Talwar STOC’10; De TCC’12; and subsequent Nikolov, Talwar, Zhang STOC’13), as well as the LDP/learning line (Kasiviswanathan et al.). The paper’s lower and upper bounds are in the local model; since LDP is stronger than global DP, prior global DP lower bounds apply but need not be tight here. The paper would benefit from explicitly situating its multinomial results against these global DP noise complexity bounds and clarifying when LDP yields strictly stronger (or incomparable) lower bounds and what is new in the local setting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds are derived via clean information-contraction inequalities under LDP and standard testing reductions; the upper bounds are attained by simple, implementable mechanisms (randomized response with projection; privatized histogram; orthogonal series with a carefully constructed privatization). The arguments appear correct and the rates match, indicating sharpness.
- Completeness: For both problems the paper presents matching bounds and discusses implementability and parameter choices. The connection to deconvolution for additive noise is well made.
- Evaluation: As a theory paper, the claims are backed by proofs. Some constants and regimes (e.g., when min terms switch) could be discussed more explicitly for practitioner intuition.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. A few points merit clarification:
- Comparability in the density setting: Eq. (13) cites the classical non-private rate over Sobolev classes Fβ. Your lower bound in Theorem 2 is stated for densities defined using the trigonometric basis (periodic Sobolev). Please make explicit that the non-private baseline is computed for the same function class (periodic Sobolev with the same boundary assumptions), or state the precise class for which Eq. (13) holds and align it with Definition 1. This will remove any ambiguity about fairness of the comparison.
- Multinomial effective sample size: It would help to state explicitly the dependence on d and α and to discuss common regimes (e.g., fixed d and small α; growing d with n).
- Prior work positioning: Please add a brief discussion of how your multinomial lower bound under LDP relates to worst-case lower bounds for linear counting queries under global DP (Hardt & Talwar 2010; De 2012; and also Nikolov, Talwar, Zhang 2013), noting similarities (ℓ2 error) and differences (local vs global DP, distributional vs adversarial worst case, population vs sample estimation).

Originality
The density estimation results under LDP, including the identification of a different polynomial exponent and matching mechanisms, appear novel and significant. For the multinomial problem, while related lower bounds exist for global DP linear queries, establishing sharp minimax rates in the local model and proving the optimality of (a variant of) randomized response for population estimation are new and practically relevant contributions.

Significance
The work provides a clear quantitative privacy–utility trade-off in two foundational estimation problems and shows how to attain optimal rates with simple mechanisms. The identification of suboptimality of naive additive-noise approaches under LDP is valuable. These results should influence both theory and the design of private data collection protocols.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Please discuss whether your LDP lower bounds for multinomial estimation strictly strengthen global DP lower bounds (or are incomparable) and, if so, where the improvement comes from (e.g., localization, mutual information contraction under LDP).
- Add citations and brief discussion of: Hardt & Talwar (STOC’10), De (TCC’12), and Nikolov, Talwar, Zhang (STOC’13) on MSE lower bounds for linear queries. Also consider clarifying the relationship to Kasiviswanathan et al. on learning under LDP and Smith (STOC’11) on private estimation rates.
- In Section 4, be explicit that your Sobolev class with trigonometric basis corresponds to periodic Sobolev spaces, and ensure Eq. (13) is stated for the same class (citing, e.g., Tsybakov).
- Provide a brief intuitive explanation of the privatization scheme in Section 4.3 (geometry of the randomized sign mechanism), and summarize the constant factors and computational costs.

Pros
- Sharp minimax lower and upper bounds under LDP for two canonical problems.
- Optimality and simplicity of mechanisms (randomized response; histogram; orthogonal series).
- Clear identification of changed rates in nonparametric density estimation.
- Strong, broadly applicable proof techniques (information bounds under LDP).

Cons
- Limited discussion of closely related global DP lower bounds for linear queries and their connection to the presented multinomial results.
- Minor clarity issues regarding the exact function class in the density baseline and practical interpretation of regimes.

Recommendation
Strong accept, contingent on adding the missing references and clarifications above. The paper makes a substantial and well-executed contribution to the theory of private statistical estimation in the local model.
