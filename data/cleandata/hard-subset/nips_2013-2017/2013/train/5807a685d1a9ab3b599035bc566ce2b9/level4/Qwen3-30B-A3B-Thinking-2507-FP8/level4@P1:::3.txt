

Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation

This paper investigates probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, providing sharp minimax convergence rates for both multinomial and density estimation problems. The authors bridge Warner's classical randomized response technique with modern differential privacy and statistical decision theory, demonstrating that Warner's approach achieves optimal sample complexity for multinomial estimation. The paper also reveals a fundamental difference in density estimation rates under local privacy compared to classical settings, with the convergence rate degrading from $n^{-2\beta/(2\beta+1)}$ to $(n\alpha^2)^{-2\beta/(2\beta+2)}$.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a formal minimax framework for local privacy, establishing that the effective sample size in multinomial estimation decreases from $n$ to $n\alpha^2/d$ under $\alpha$-local differential privacy. The authors prove that Warner's randomized response mechanism achieves this optimal rate, demonstrating that a solution known for nearly 50 years remains optimal for privacy-preserving survey sampling. Second, for density estimation, the paper shows that local privacy fundamentally alters convergence rates, requiring different estimation strategies than those used in non-private settings. The authors provide matching upper and lower bounds, demonstrating that their proposed histogram and orthogonal series estimators achieve the optimal rate.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for all claims, satisfying the quality criterion. The authors carefully address both the strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly in the density estimation section where they demonstrate why direct Laplace perturbation fails to achieve optimal rates. The minimax framework is well-motivated and properly positioned relative to prior work in differential privacy and statistical decision theory.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the technical results. The notation is consistent and well-defined, though the density estimation section becomes somewhat dense with technical details. The authors would strengthen the paper by more explicitly contrasting their L2 results with L1 considerations, which are particularly relevant for probability estimation where L1 distance is often more natural. The paper does acknowledge this in Theorem 1, but a brief discussion of the implications of L1 vs L2 norms for practical applications would enhance the clarity.

The originality of the paper is strong. While differential privacy has been extensively studied, the specific application to local privacy with minimax rates for probability estimation represents a novel contribution. The connection between Warner's classical work and modern privacy frameworks provides an important historical context that previous literature has overlooked. The paper properly references related work, including Duchi et al.'s earlier work on local privacy, and clearly distinguishes its contributions from previous NIPS papers.

The significance of the paper is substantial. It provides a quantitative framework for understanding the trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency, which is crucial for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The finding that Warner's 1965 randomized response method remains optimal for survey sampling is particularly noteworthy, as it demonstrates that some fundamental privacy-preserving techniques have stood the test of time. The paper's theoretical results also provide practical guidance for practitioners, showing exactly how much statistical efficiency is lost under local privacy constraints.

A minor concern is the paper's limited discussion of how their results compare to non-local privacy settings. While the authors note that their lower bounds are stronger than prior work, a more explicit comparison to the non-local differential privacy literature would strengthen the paper's positioning. Additionally, the paper could better highlight how their density estimation results differ from classical non-private results.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, providing sharp theoretical bounds that have immediate practical implications for privacy-preserving statistical estimation. The results are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and efficient algorithms, satisfying the quality criterion. The paper's connection between classical and modern privacy techniques is particularly valuable for the NIPS community, as it demonstrates how foundational work can inform contemporary research.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-statistical efficiency trade-offs. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in statistical privacy. I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
