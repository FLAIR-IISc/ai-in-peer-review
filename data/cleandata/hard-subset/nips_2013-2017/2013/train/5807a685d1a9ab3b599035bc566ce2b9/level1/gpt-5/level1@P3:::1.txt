Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies non-interactive local differential privacy (LDP) and its impact on statistical estimation, focusing on (i) d-dimensional multinomial probability estimation and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. It frames the problem in a minimax decision-theoretic setting and derives sharp lower bounds under LDP by combining Le Cam/Fano methods with information-contraction inequalities specific to local mechanisms. Matching upper bounds are provided via simple, computationally efficient procedures: a modified randomized response for multinomials and privatized histogram/orthogonal series estimators for densities. A key conceptual takeaway is an explicit privacy-efficiency trade-off: for multinomials, privacy reduces the effective sample size by a factor ≈ d/α^2; for densities with β smoothness, the non-private rate n^{-2β/(2β+1)} degrades to (α^2 n)^{-2β/(2β+2)}. The results unify and sharpen insights from Warner’s randomized response, differential privacy, and classical minimax theory (Yang & Barron; Tsybakov), and relate to prior DP work (Wasserman-Zhou; Smith; Hardt-Talwar; Chaudhuri et al.; Kasiviswanathan et al.), but in the more stringent local model.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds are derived via an LDP-specific divergence/mutual-information contraction (Proposition 1) plugged into Le Cam and Fano bounds (Lemma 1), a standard but powerful template. The dependence on (e^α−1)^2 is natural in the local model and yields the α^2 scaling that propagates into minimax rates. The multinomial upper bound via randomized response plus projection is straightforward and matches the lower bound in rate. For densities, the privatized histogram (β=1) and a carefully designed α-LDP orthogonal series mechanism (with bounded basis, randomized rounding, and sign-vector sampling) attain the lower bound rates, establishing sharpness. The avoidance of naive Laplace perturbation and the link to deconvolution impossibility (Carroll & Hall) convincingly argue necessity of tailored noise.
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to the longer version [9], but the core ideas, constructions, and rate calculations are sufficiently described to assess plausibility. The sampling in the series mechanism is implementable (uniform over half the hypercube has acceptance probability 1/2, so rejection sampling is efficient).
- Minor technical issues/typos: (a) Equation (8) includes a term √(nα^2) in a bound on mean-squared error; that has incorrect units and is likely a typo. The intended lower/upper rates appear to be Θ(min{1, d/(nα^2)}). (b) In (10), “−xj” seems unintended; it should be “1−xj” (bit flip). (c) In the Sobolev definition, ∑ j^{2β} φ_j^2 ≤ C^2 should be ∑ j^{2β} θ_j^2 ≤ C^2. Addressing these would improve readability. Also, clarify constants c_k and their dependence on k in Section 4.3.
- Assumptions: The non-interactive (memoryless) local model is clearly stated. The role of α in [0,1] is reasonable; a brief discussion of behavior for larger α would be welcome. The series estimator requires knowledge of B0 and β to choose k; adaptivity is not addressed.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-structured: background on minimax testing reductions, LDP definitions, information bounds, then two estimation problems with lower/upper bounds. Notation is dense but standard. As noted, a few typos and omitted constants/conditions (e.g., bounds on ck, explicit proof sketches for Proposition 1 if not assumed from [9]) hinder immediate reproducibility for some readers. The intuitive narrative—privacy contracts information and alters rates—is clear and compelling.

Originality:
High. While prior work has analyzed learning and estimation under (global) DP and local privacy (e.g., statistical query model connections, private ERM), this work provides sharp minimax rates for fundamental estimation tasks under LDP, including the novel finding that privacy changes the polynomial exponent for nonparametric density estimation. The optimality of a suitably tuned randomized response for multinomials is both theoretically and historically satisfying.

Significance:
Substantial. The results quantify the privacy-utility trade-off in a way that can guide practitioners in survey sampling and distributed data collection, where LDP is often the only viable option. The methods (information contraction under LDP) are reusable and likely to influence subsequent lower bound work. The constructive mechanisms for optimal estimation are simple and efficient, increasing practical impact.

Pros:
- Sharp, matching minimax lower/upper bounds in a stringent privacy model.
- Clear identification of effective sample size degradation and exponent changes.
- Elegant and implementable mechanisms (randomized response; LDP-orthogonal series).
- Solid integration of information theory, classical minimax testing, and DP.
- Relevance to NIPS topics in statistical learning, privacy, and theory.

Cons:
- Typos/mismatches in key equations and notation that should be corrected.
- Dependence on known smoothness β; no adaptive/Lepski-type selection or discussion of adaptivity under LDP.
- Focused on non-interactive local channels; a brief comparison to interactive local mechanisms (and whether interactivity changes rates) would strengthen the scope.
- Limited discussion of approximate LDP ((α,δ)) or alternative losses for densities (e.g., L1/TV), which are often of interest.
- Some constants (ck, B choices) and computational burdens when k grows with n could be more explicit.

Questions and suggestions:
- Please correct Equation (8) and (10) and the Sobolev definition typos; provide a short appendix with the exact constants and conditions (ranges of α, dependence of ck on k).
- Can your information-contraction bounds handle (α,δ)-local DP, and if so, do the rates change?
- Does interactivity in the local model improve constants or rates for these problems?
- For densities, can you discuss projection to the density cone (nonnegativity/integration) for the series estimator and its effect on risk?
- Any pathway to adaptive choice of k (unknown β) under LDP with near-minimax rates?

Recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper makes a clear, technically solid, and impactful contribution by establishing and attaining sharp minimax rates under local differential privacy for core estimation problems. With minor clarity fixes and brief additional discussion as suggested, it will be a valuable addition to NIPS.
