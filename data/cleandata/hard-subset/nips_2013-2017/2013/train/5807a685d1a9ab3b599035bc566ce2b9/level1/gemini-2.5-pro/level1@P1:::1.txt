This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the trade-off between statistical accuracy and local differential privacy for two fundamental probability estimation problems: multinomial parameter estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The authors frame the problem within the minimax framework, seeking to characterize the best possible convergence rate for any estimator that satisfies α-local differential privacy.

The main contributions are the derivation of sharp minimax lower bounds and the construction of computationally efficient estimators that achieve these bounds. For estimating a d-dimensional multinomial, the paper shows that the minimax risk scales as `d / (n * alpha^2)`, which corresponds to an effective sample size reduction from `n` to `n * alpha^2 / d`. A key consequence is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is, in fact, minimax optimal in this modern privacy framework. For nonparametric estimation of a density with β derivatives, the authors show a more dramatic effect: the minimax rate degrades from the classical `n^(-2*beta / (2*beta + 1))` to `(n * alpha^2)^(-2*beta / (2*beta + 2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent is a significant finding, demonstrating that privacy imposes more than just a constant-factor penalty in this setting. The authors propose optimal mechanisms, including a privatized histogram for Lipschitz densities and a novel orthogonal series estimator for smoother densities.

This work connects several important lines of research. It grounds the classical work of Warner [23] on survey privacy in the modern, rigorous framework of differential privacy [11]. It extends the growing literature on differentially private data analysis by shifting focus from centralized to local privacy [13] and by providing sharp population-level minimax bounds, which are stronger than the sample-level bounds often considered previously [16, 8]. Most importantly, it brings the powerful tools of minimax statistical theory [26, 22] to bear on the problem of privacy, providing a fundamental characterization of the inherent statistical cost of privacy.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of outstanding technical quality. The claims are supported by rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing both lower bounds (via information-theoretic arguments like Fano's inequality) and matching upper bounds (via the explicit construction and analysis of estimators). This establishes the "sharpness" of the rates, making this a complete and definitive piece of work on these problems. The authors are careful to highlight subtleties, such as the sub-optimality of naive noise-addition mechanisms for density estimation, which underscores the depth of their analysis.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear motivation, a precise problem formulation, and a logical progression of results. The key takeaways—the `n * alpha^2 / d` effective sample size for multinomials and the change in exponent for density estimation—are stated plainly and their implications are discussed lucidly. The writing makes a complex theoretical topic accessible.

*   **Originality:** The contribution is highly original and constitutes a significant conceptual advance. The synthesis of minimax decision theory and local differential privacy is novel and powerful. While prior work had explored private estimation, this paper is among the first to provide a complete and sharp characterization of the fundamental limits. The discovery that local privacy can alter the polynomial rate of convergence for nonparametric problems is a profound insight. The connection drawn to Warner's 50-year-old work is both elegant and surprising.

*   **Significance:** The results are of fundamental importance and are likely to have a major impact on the fields of machine learning, statistics, and data privacy. The paper provides a formal language and a core set of results for quantifying the "price of privacy." As the need for privacy-preserving algorithms grows, this foundational understanding is crucial. The methodology developed here—using information-theoretic bounds on privacy channels to derive minimax rates—is general and can be applied to a wide range of other statistical problems. The proposed estimators are practical, providing a clear path for practitioners who need to implement optimal private estimation schemes. This work represents a clear advance in the state of the art.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

The paper is very strong, and any weaknesses are minor.

1.  The proofs of the main theorems and the key information-theoretic bounds in Proposition 1 are deferred to a longer arXiv version [9]. While understandable given page limits, this makes the paper not fully self-contained. Including a brief, high-level proof sketch of Proposition 1 in an appendix would enhance the paper by giving the reader more intuition about the core technical machinery without requiring them to consult an external reference.

2.  The description of the orthogonal series sampling strategy in Section 4.3 is quite dense. While the mechanism is clearly defined, the motivation behind its specific two-stage structure (sampling `τ_tilde`, then sampling `Z` based on its inner product) could be elaborated upon. A sentence explaining why this more complex scheme is necessary to achieve optimality over, for example, a simpler noise-addition strategy would be helpful for the reader's intuition.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** Provides a fundamental and definitive characterization of the minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy.
*   **Pro:** The results are novel and significant, including the discovery that privacy can change the polynomial convergence rate.
*   **Pro:** The paper is technically flawless, exceptionally clear, and bridges multiple important research areas.
*   **Pro:** The work is foundational and is highly likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.
*   **Con:** (Minor) Key proofs are located in an external document.

In summary, this is a landmark paper that makes a deep and lasting contribution to our understanding of the interplay between privacy and statistical inference. It is technically brilliant, clearly communicated, and highly significant. It is an ideal paper for NIPS and I recommend it for acceptance without hesitation.
