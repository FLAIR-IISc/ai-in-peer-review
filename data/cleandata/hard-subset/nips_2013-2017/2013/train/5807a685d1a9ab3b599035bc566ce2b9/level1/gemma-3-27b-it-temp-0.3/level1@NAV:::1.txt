## Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical accuracy in probability estimation, specifically within the framework of local differential privacy. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems under this privacy constraint. They build upon Warner’s classical work on randomized response, demonstrating its optimality, and connect it to modern differential privacy and minimax theory. This work is highly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a crucial intersection of statistical learning, privacy, and information theory – areas frequently explored at the conference.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents a complex topic. The authors effectively bridge the gap between classical statistical decision theory and the more recent developments in differential privacy. The derivation of minimax rates is rigorous, and the proofs (referenced to a longer version) appear sound. A key strength is the identification of differing convergence rates for multinomial estimation (reduction in effective sample size) versus density estimation (altered polynomial rate), highlighting the nuanced impact of privacy constraints. The achievability results, demonstrating that randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation and proposing efficient estimators for density estimation, further strengthen the contribution. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the derived rates. While the authors mention the trade-offs, a more concrete illustration of how these rates translate into real-world performance for different parameter settings (e.g., varying levels of privacy, different dimensions) would be valuable. Additionally, while the connection to Warner’s work is highlighted, a more thorough discussion of related work in privacy-preserving machine learning beyond differential privacy (e.g., federated learning) could broaden the context. The notation is dense, and while generally clear, some readers might find it challenging to follow all the details.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Technical Contribution:** Provides sharp minimax rates for a fundamental problem under a realistic privacy constraint.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** Rigorous analysis and clear connection to established statistical and privacy frameworks.
*   **Relevance to NIPS:** Addresses a core intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy.
*   **Practical Implications:** Demonstrates optimality of a classical technique and proposes efficient private estimators.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Discussion:** Could benefit from a more detailed analysis of real-world performance implications.
*   **Dense Notation:** May pose a challenge for some readers.
*   **Narrow Scope of Related Work:** Could benefit from a broader discussion of privacy-preserving ML techniques.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. While some improvements in clarity and practical discussion would be beneficial, the technical rigor and relevance to NIPS are substantial.
