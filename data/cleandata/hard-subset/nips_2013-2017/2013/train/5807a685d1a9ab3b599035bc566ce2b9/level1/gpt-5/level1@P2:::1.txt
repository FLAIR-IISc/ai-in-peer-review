Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each sample is privatized before being seen by the statistician. It brings minimax decision-theoretic tools together with LDP to derive sharp rates for two canonical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The authors develop information-contraction inequalities for LDP channels (upper bounds on KL divergence and mutual information after privatization), and combine them with Le Cam/Fano methods to obtain lower bounds. They then design simple, computationally efficient privatization mechanisms and estimators that match these lower bounds up to constants, yielding tight minimax characterizations.

For multinomial estimation in d dimensions, they show that under α-LDP (non-interactive), the effective sample size degrades from n to nα^2/d for small α, with minimax risks Mn(∥·∥2^2) ≍ min{1, d/(nα^2)} and Mn(∥·∥1) ≍ min{1, d/√(nα^2)}. They further show that Warner’s randomized response (with a simple modification) is optimal in this sense, thus formally validating a 50-year-old method as minimax optimal under differential privacy constraints.

For nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1], they establish a change in the minimax rate’s exponent compared to the non-private setting: from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} (classical) to (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under LDP. They design two estimators that attain these rates: a privatized histogram estimator for β=1, and a privatized orthogonal series estimator for β≥1 based on a novel bounded, unbiased privatization of basis evaluations. They also prove that simple Laplace perturbation of raw samples cannot attain the optimal rate (via deconvolution limits).

The work is well situated with respect to differential privacy (Warner; Dwork et al.; Wasserman & Zhou; Hardt & Talwar; Smith) and classical minimax theory (Le Cam, Fano, Tsybakov, Yang & Barron).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds derive from clear information bounds tailored to LDP ((e^α−1)^2 dependence), then standard testing reductions. The upper bounds are obtained via explicit channels and estimators with variance control and bias correction. For both multinomial and Sobolev density classes, the bounds match up to constants, establishing sharp rates. The arguments that Laplace perturbation is suboptimal for density estimation are grounded in deconvolution theory.
- Completeness: The paper treats the non-interactive LDP model thoroughly, covers both lower and upper bounds, and provides implementable procedures (projection onto simplex; histogram; orthogonal series with bounded privatization). Proofs are deferred to the longer version, but the main ideas and constructions are explained.
- Caveats: Results are restricted to the non-interactive local model; interactive LDP protocols can sometimes be more powerful. The density estimators assume uniformly bounded bases and knowledge of constants (B0, β) for tuning k; adaptivity to unknown β under LDP is not developed. No empirical evaluation is provided to assess finite-sample constants and practical performance. Some constants (e.g., c_k in Eq. (20)) are not fully explicit in the main text.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written, with a helpful minimax background and precise definitions. The flow from information bounds to minimax lower bounds is well organized. Mechanism descriptions are concrete.
- Suggestions: Give more intuition for the privatization scheme in Eq. (19) (e.g., a geometric view of randomized sign vectors and why unbiasedness and α-LDP hold). Clarify the role and magnitude of c_k and how to pick B; provide brief guidance on computational cost of the rejection sampling. Discuss practical selection of k when β is unknown, and how privacy-safe model selection could be done in the local setting.

Originality:
- The paper makes a novel and substantial contribution by deriving sharp minimax rates under local DP for both discrete and continuous probability estimation and by identifying an exponent change for density estimation. The optimality of randomized response for multinomial estimation under LDP is a clean and impactful result. The information-contraction lemmas specialized to LDP and their use in minimax lower bounds are original and broadly useful.

Significance:
- High. The results quantify the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency in a widely adopted privacy model, offering both limits and constructive, optimal procedures. The findings inform the design of local privacy mechanisms (e.g., surveys, decentralized data collection) and set benchmarks for future methods in high-dimensional and nonparametric settings. The exponent shift in density estimation under LDP is a striking phenomenon likely to motivate further research.

Pros for acceptance:
- Sharp, matching lower/upper bounds; clean conceptual message.
- Unifies minimax theory with local DP via general information bounds.
- Practical, simple mechanisms (randomized response, histograms, orthogonal series) shown optimal.
- Clarifies limitations of naive perturbation approaches.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited to non-interactive LDP; no exploration of potential gains from interaction.
- Requires knowledge of smoothness β for optimal tuning; no adaptive, private model selection.
- No empirical studies; finite-sample behavior and constants left unillustrated.
- Some technical constants/mechanism details deferred to the longer version.

Recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper is technically solid, original, and significant, with clear exposition and constructive algorithms. Minor improvements could include clarifying constants and offering guidance on adaptive tuning and practical implementation; however, these do not detract from the core contributions.
