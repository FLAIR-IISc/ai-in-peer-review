This paper presents a theoretical investigation into probability estimation under local differential privacy, establishing sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems. The work connects Warner's classical randomized response method with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory, providing a rigorous framework for quantifying the trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the long history of privacy concerns in statistical inference (Warner, 1965) and the recent surge of interest in differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006). The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature – a formal treatment of privacy allowing for decision-theoretic trade-off analysis. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and the contributions of the paper, and the notation is generally well-defined. The formalization of local privacy using likelihood ratios is standard (Evfimievski et al., 2003) and appropriate. The core results – Theorems 1 and 2 – are significant and provide concrete bounds on the achievable accuracy of estimation under privacy constraints.  The connection drawn to Warner’s randomized response as an optimal mechanism for multinomial estimation is a particularly insightful historical link.  The authors successfully leverage existing results from minimax theory (Tsybakov, 2009) and combine them with their privacy-specific bounds to obtain new and meaningful conclusions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper employs a solid theoretical framework based on minimax optimality and differential privacy, providing strong guarantees on the performance of estimators.
*   **Sharp Bounds:**  The derived minimax rates are sharp, demonstrated by both lower and upper bounds, establishing the fundamental limits of estimation under privacy constraints.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The analysis has practical implications for privacy-preserving data analysis, specifically highlighting the trade-offs involved in achieving both privacy and accuracy.  The optimality result for Warner’s method is a strong practical contribution.
*   **Clear Connections:**  The paper effectively connects classical work (Warner, 1965) with modern privacy frameworks (Dwork et al., 2006; Evfimievski et al., 2003), providing a historical context and demonstrating the evolution of the field.
*   **Well-Organized:** The paper is logically structured, with clear definitions, theorem statements, and explanations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notationally Dense:**  The paper is quite dense with notation, which might make it challenging for readers not already familiar with the field. While most notation is defined, the rapid succession of symbols and mathematical expressions can be overwhelming.
*   **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper establishes theoretical optimality, it lacks a detailed discussion of the computational cost of the proposed estimators, especially the orthogonal series estimator.  The practical feasibility of implementing these estimators, particularly for high-dimensional data, isn't thoroughly addressed.
*   **Focus on Specific Basises:** The density estimation results (Theorem 2) are tied to the trigonometric basis. While justified in the context of smoothness, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss how the results generalize to other bases or whether the choice of basis significantly impacts the convergence rates.
* **Missing Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant works, the references to related work on private density estimation, beyond the cited papers, could be more exhaustive. For example, works exploring kernel density estimation under differential privacy might be mentioned.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
*   The results are rigorously proven and provide sharp bounds on the performance of estimators.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem with practical implications for a wide range of applications.
*   The connection to Warner’s work is a compelling and historically important contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The notationally dense style might limit accessibility for some readers.
*   The lack of detailed discussion about computational cost could be a concern for practitioners.
*   The reliance on a specific basis in the density estimation results might limit the generality of the findings.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the NIPS community. The theoretical results are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the paper addresses a timely and important problem. While the paper could benefit from some improvements in terms of accessibility and discussion of computational cost, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the points raised in the weaknesses section. Specifically, I suggest adding a brief discussion on the computational complexity of the estimators and expanding the related work section to include more recent advances in private density estimation.
