- Paper provides a rigorous theoretical study of probability distribution estimation under local differential privacy, deriving sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation and quantifying privacy–utility trade-offs.
- Shows classical mechanisms (e.g., Warner's randomized response) are optimal for some tasks and introduces novel mechanisms (esp. for density estimation), with matching lower and upper bounds and practical algorithms.
- Strength: Technically rigorous with sharp theoretical results and novel use of minimax decision theory in the LDP context.
- Strength: Clearly written and well-organized with accessible background and well-structured proofs.
- Strength: Highly original integration of minimax theory with local differential privacy, advancing the theoretical state of the art.
- Strength: Significant implications for theory and practice by identifying optimal mechanisms and highlighting fundamental privacy-imposed limitations.
- Weakness: Lacks empirical validation on real-world datasets to demonstrate practical performance.
- Weakness: Focus is limited to multinomial and density estimation, with limited discussion of generalization to other tasks.
- Weakness: Some proposed mechanisms (notably for density estimation) are complex; practical implementation and computational efficiency are not fully addressed.
- Argument for acceptance: Makes a significant, novel theoretical contribution by deriving sharp minimax rates under LDP relevant to the community.
- Argument for acceptance: Bridges classical statistical decision theory and modern privacy frameworks, opening directions for future work.
- Argument against acceptance: Limited empirical validation reduces immediate practical impact.
- Argument against acceptance: Narrow problem focus may limit broad appeal.
- Recommendation: Accept, with encouragement to add empirical evaluations and broader applicability discussions in future revisions.
