- Rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy, giving sharp minimax rates for discrete and continuous settings.
- Multinomial result: local privacy level α effectively reduces sample size from n to nα²/d; Warner's randomized response achieves the optimal rate.
- Density result: convergence rate shifts from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} to (α²n)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, with matching upper bounds for β=1 and β>1.
- Provides matching lower and upper bounds, combining minimax theory with information-theoretic privacy bounds and extending Duchi et al. (2013).
- Clarifies the fundamental trade-off between privacy and statistical efficiency and connects randomized response to differential privacy.
- Offers computationally efficient algorithms that attain the optimal rates, making theory practically applicable.
- Insightful linkage between classical survey methodology and modern privacy frameworks; framework is generalizable to other problems.
- Strength: first work to establish sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy.
- Strength: historically and practically significant demonstration of randomized response optimality.
- Weakness: restricts focus to local differential privacy without comparison to central DP models.
- Weakness: practical implementation challenges (high-dimensional data, large d or β computational costs) are not deeply explored.
- Weakness: limited comparison with alternative privacy-preserving density estimators; lacks application-specific privacy-utility analyses.
- Quality: technically sound with rigorous, well-structured proofs and appropriate use of minimax and information-theoretic tools.
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear definitions, consistent notation, and a lucid presentation of randomized response.
- Originality: significant extension of prior work; first minimax analysis proving Warner's method optimal and novel private density estimation rates.
- Significance: establishes fundamental limits on estimation under privacy, with theoretical and practical importance and extensibility.
- Recommendation: strong accept—important, novel, and technically rigorous contribution.
- Acceptance arguments: first sharp minimax rates, optimal randomized response, clear privacy-efficiency trade-offs, computationally efficient methods, and strong theoretical significance.
- Rejection arguments: high technicality may limit accessibility, limited practical discussion, and no central DP comparisons (though authors justify focus).
