This paper tackles the fundamental problem of probability estimation under local differential privacy, providing sharp minimax rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation. The work is well-motivated, connecting Warner’s early work on randomized response to modern differential privacy and minimax theory. The authors successfully demonstrate quantitative trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The paper is structured logically. It begins with a clear introduction outlining the problem and its connection to prior work. The formalization of local privacy using differential privacy is concise and standard. The presentation of the minimax framework and the relevant background lemmas are thorough, even restating necessary results from their earlier work for self-containment. The results for multinomial estimation are particularly strong, showing optimality of Warner’s randomized response. The density estimation results, while more complex, also provide a clear characterization of the rate under privacy constraints.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The work provides a rigorous theoretical treatment of a long-standing problem (privacy-preserving probability estimation) using a powerful combination of tools (differential privacy, minimax theory). The identified trade-offs are novel and quantitatively precise.
* **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears technically sound, with clear derivations and appropriate use of existing results. The lower bounds are particularly convincing, building on Fano's inequality and carefully controlling the divergences. The achievability results, demonstrated through the analysis of randomized response and histogram/orthogonal series estimators, are equally compelling.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are relatively easy to follow. The self-contained presentation of background material is helpful.
* **Significance:** The results are significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. They provide a fundamental understanding of the limits of privacy-preserving statistical inference and guide the development of optimal estimators.  The optimality result for Warner’s randomized response is a particularly impactful finding.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density Estimation Complexity:** While the theoretical results for density estimation are valuable, the practical implementation details of the orthogonal series estimator, particularly the choice of basis functions and the parameter tuning (e.g., the value of *k*), could be elaborated. The computational complexity of the proposed estimators, especially for higher dimensions or smoothness parameters, is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Limited Scope of Density Estimation:** The focus is primarily on densities with bounded derivatives. The performance in more complex scenarios (e.g., densities with heavier tails or singularities) is not addressed.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, a more in-depth discussion of practical considerations and potential challenges in applying these results in real-world settings would be beneficial. For example, the sensitivity of the estimators to the choice of privacy parameter α is not fully explored.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.
* The technical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by theoretical results and practical examples.
* The clarity of the presentation and the thoroughness of the background material make the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* The identified trade-offs between privacy and accuracy are fundamental and have important implications for the design of privacy-preserving data analysis systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical implementation details of the density estimation procedure could be more thoroughly discussed.
* The scope of the density estimation results is limited to densities with bounded derivatives.
* A more in-depth discussion of practical considerations and challenges in applying the results in real-world settings would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and presents novel and impactful results. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised in the "weaknesses" section, specifically elaborating on the computational complexity and practical considerations of the proposed estimators.
