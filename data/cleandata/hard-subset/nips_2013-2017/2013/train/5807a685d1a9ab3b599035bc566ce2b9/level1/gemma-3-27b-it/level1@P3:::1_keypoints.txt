- Presents a thorough study of probability estimation under local differential privacy, proving sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation.  
- Connects Warner’s randomized response with differential privacy and minimax theory in a decision-theoretic framework.  
- Well-motivated, clearly written, and addresses a timely privacy-statistics trade-off problem.  
- Leverages differential privacy to derive minimax convergence rates, filling a gap where prior work focused more on achieving privacy than its statistical cost.  
- Builds on prior work (e.g., Duchi et al.) and ties into classical minimax and differential privacy literature.  
- Focuses on local privacy, guaranteeing protection even against a malicious data collector.  
- Theoretical rigor: strong use of minimax theory and differential privacy, with particularly strong lower bounds.  
- Sharpness: achieves matching upper and lower bounds for key problems, demonstrating estimator optimality.  
- Practical relevance: analyzes randomized response and proposes practical estimators (histogram, orthogonal series), showing randomized response is optimal.  
- Clarity and organization: exceptionally well-written with clear notation and accessible derivations.  
- Strong literature connections and careful positioning of contributions.  
- Detailed technical proofs are available in the referenced work [9].  
- Limited scope: primarily addresses only multinomial and density estimation, limiting generalizability to other problems (e.g., regression, high-dimensional).  
- Computational cost concerns: some estimators (notably orthogonal series) may be computationally heavy and may not scale well.  
- Reliance on basis/smoothness assumptions: sensitivity to choice of orthonormal basis and smoothness conditions is not fully explored.  
- Lack of empirical evaluation: absence of simulations or real-data experiments weakens practical impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, significant, technically sound results on an important problem with potential to influence privacy-preserving methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scope, potential computational impracticality, and missing empirical validation.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality paper recommended for strong acceptance, with suggested future work on scalability and empirical validation; a minor revision adding empirical results would strengthen it.
