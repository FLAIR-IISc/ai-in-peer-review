

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local privacy constraints, connecting Warner's classical work on randomized response with modern differential privacy and statistical decision theory. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation under α-local differential privacy, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper builds on Duchi et al. [9] (which appeared at NIPS 2013) but provides significantly more detailed minimax analysis specifically for probability estimation problems, which was not the focus of the earlier work. Previous NIPS papers on privacy and statistics (e.g., [11, 1, 24]) focused on algorithmic constructions or disclosure risk without providing sharp rates for estimation under privacy constraints, making this work a substantial theoretical contribution to the field.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds for the minimax rates. The authors establish tight bounds for both multinomial and density estimation problems, demonstrating that their lower bounds are matched by efficiently computable estimators. For multinomial estimation, they prove that Warner's randomized response mechanism is minimax optimal, resolving a long-standing question about optimal privacy-preserving estimation. For density estimation, they show a fundamental change in the convergence rate exponent due to privacy constraints, which is a significant theoretical insight. The paper carefully addresses both strengths and weaknesses, noting that direct perturbation of samples is suboptimal for density estimation (a key insight not present in prior work). The technical depth and completeness of the theoretical analysis are excellent.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by connecting Warner's classical work with modern differential privacy. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, with clear statements of theorems and propositions. The paper is logically structured, moving from the theoretical framework to specific estimation problems with clear transitions. The only minor issue is that some of the technical proofs (e.g., in Section 4.1) are referenced to a longer paper [9] rather than included, which slightly reduces self-containment.

**Originality:** This paper makes several highly original contributions. First, it establishes sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, which is a novel theoretical framework for privacy-preserving statistics. Second, it demonstrates that Warner's randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation, a result that was previously conjectured but not rigorously proven. Third, the density estimation results show a fundamental change in the convergence rate exponent (from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2)), which is a significant theoretical insight not present in prior work. The paper thoroughly reviews related work and clearly differentiates from previous NIPS papers (e.g., Duchi et al. [9], Wasserman and Zhou [24]), showing that this work provides substantially new theoretical results.

**Significance:** The results have profound significance for both theory and practice. The identification of the fundamental privacy-statistical efficiency trade-off provides a quantitative framework for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The finding that Warner's randomized response is optimal resolves a 50-year-old question in privacy-preserving survey sampling. The sharp rates for density estimation provide a theoretical foundation for developing better privacy-preserving nonparametric methods. The paper's results will be valuable to both researchers developing new privacy-preserving statistical methods and practitioners who need to balance privacy and utility in real-world applications. The work advances the state of the art by providing the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy.

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous proof of sharp minimax rates for both discrete and continuous probability estimation
- Demonstrates optimality of Warner's randomized response for multinomial estimation
- Reveals fundamental change in convergence rates for density estimation under privacy constraints
- Provides efficiently computable algorithms matching the lower bounds
- Connects classical work (Warner [23]) with modern differential privacy theory

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper relies on referencing a longer version for some proofs (e.g., Theorem 2 proof is referenced to [9])
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges beyond theoretical efficiency
- The density estimation results are limited to the Sobolev class Fβ [C]; extension to other function classes would be valuable

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1. The paper provides the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, a significant theoretical advance.
2. The results have important practical implications for privacy-preserving survey design and statistical inference.
3. The work connects classical privacy work (Warner) with modern differential privacy theory in a novel way.
4. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges.
2. Some proofs are referenced to a longer version rather than included in the paper.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistics. The results are both novel and significant, providing sharp minimax rates that resolve important questions about privacy-utility trade-offs. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of significant importance to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
