- Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy, deriving sharp minimax rates for multinomial and density estimation.  
- Formalizes α-local differential privacy (Evfimievski et al.) and establishes a minimax framework for private estimation.  
- Multinomial estimation result: effective sample size drops from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy; Warner’s randomized response is rate‑optimal.  
- Density estimation result: classical n^{-2β/(2β+1)} rate degrades to (nα²)^{-2β/(2β+2)} under local privacy, with matching upper bounds via histogram and orthogonal series estimators.  
- Technical strengths: tight lower bounds matched by efficient estimators; analysis is technically sound and carefully constructed.  
- Authors identify limitations of naive perturbation methods, showing they can be suboptimal for density estimation.  
- Establishes a clear connection between Warner’s randomized response and modern differential privacy, proving its optimality for survey sampling under local privacy.  
- Originality: first paper to give sharp minimax rates for probability estimation in the local differential privacy setting.  
- Significance: characterizes fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, guiding design of privacy-preserving procedures.  
- Practical implication: supports use of randomized response for multinomial/survey problems despite its age.  
- Weakness: analysis restricted to the non-interactive local privacy model, not covering interactive mechanisms.  
- Weakness: limited empirical validation — few or no extensive experiments provided.  
- Weakness: density results confined to Sobolev spaces with trigonometric bases, which may limit practical generality.  
- Presentation: well-written, clearly organized, consistent notation, and sufficient detail for reproduction.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically rigorous minimax results, connects classical and modern privacy, and provides optimal algorithms.  
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow empirical support and restricted scope of privacy models and function classes.
