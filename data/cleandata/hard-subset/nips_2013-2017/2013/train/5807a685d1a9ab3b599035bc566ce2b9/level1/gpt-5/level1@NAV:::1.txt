This paper studies statistical estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), giving sharp minimax rates for discrete (multinomial) and continuous (Sobolev-smooth) distributions when the analyst observes only privatized views of the data. The authors develop information-theoretic lower bounds tailored to LDP via private versions of Le Cam and Fano (bounding divergences through the privacy parameter α), and provide matching, computationally efficient mechanisms. For d-dimensional multinomial estimation, the risk behaves as d/(nα^2), implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα^2/d; a variant of Warner’s randomized response is shown minimax-optimal. For density estimation over Sobolev classes Fβ on [0,1], privacy changes the polynomial rate from n^{-2β/(2β+1)} (non-private) to (nα^2)^{-2β/(2β+2)}, with optimality achieved by privatized histograms for β=1 and an orthogonal-series mechanism for β≥1 using a carefully designed locally private sampling scheme. The work connects classical randomized response [Warner], differential privacy foundations [Dwork et al.], the LDP model [Kasiviswanathan et al.], geometric/information lower bounds [Hardt & Talwar], and private inference [Wasserman & Zhou; Smith], while focusing on population minimax risk rather than sample query accuracy.

Quality: The technical development is strong. Lower bounds are precise in α, n, d, and β, and the proposed mechanisms attain them up to constants with efficient procedures. The novel mutual information bounds under LDP are particularly useful. Some proofs are deferred and constants (e.g., ck, choices of B) are opaque; finite-sample constants and tuning guidance (e.g., selection of k) could be clarified. No empirical demonstrations are provided.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, though notation is heavy. Suggestions: add pseudocode for the multinomial and orthogonal-series mechanisms; provide intuition for the variational MI bound; summarize regimes (small vs moderate α) and constants; discuss computational aspects of the rejection sampling.

Originality: High. Establishing sharp minimax rates under local (non-interactive) privacy, identifying a change in exponent for density estimation, and proving randomized response optimality in general multinomials are novel and distinguish the work from prior central-DP and query-accuracy results.

Significance: High. Results articulate fundamental privacy–utility trade-offs in the LDP model and provide tools likely to influence future private learning algorithm design.

Pros for acceptance:
- Sharp, matching lower/upper bounds; simple, efficient mechanisms
- Clear conceptual contributions (effective sample size; exponent shift)
- Strong connections to and extensions beyond prior DP theory

Cons for acceptance:
- Scope limited to non-interactive LDP and 1D densities
- Lack of empirical validation and finite-sample guidance
- Some technical assumptions (bounded bases, α∈[0,1]) and constants under-specified

Overall, a strong theoretical contribution; I lean toward acceptance.
