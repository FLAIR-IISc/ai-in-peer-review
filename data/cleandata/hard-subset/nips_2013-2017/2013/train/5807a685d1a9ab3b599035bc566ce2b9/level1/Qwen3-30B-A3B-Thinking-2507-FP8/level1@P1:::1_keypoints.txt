- Rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under α-local differential privacy connecting Warner’s randomized response with modern differential privacy and decision theory.
- Establishes sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy, quantifying privacy–efficiency trade-offs.
- Extends and deepens Duchi et al. [9] by providing detailed minimax analysis specifically for probability estimation.
- Technically sound with rigorous lower and upper bounds and proofs demonstrating tightness of rates.
- Efficiently computable estimators are provided that match the derived lower bounds.
- Proves Warner’s randomized response is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation, resolving a long-standing question.
- Shows privacy changes the density estimation convergence exponent from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2), a fundamental theoretical insight.
- Identifies that direct perturbation of samples is suboptimal for density estimation under local privacy.
- Paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation and reproducible theorem statements.
- Some technical proofs are deferred to a longer version, reducing the paper’s self-containment.
- Original contributions include first sharp minimax rates under local privacy, proof of randomized response optimality, and novel density-rate results.
- Results have significant theoretical and practical implications for designing privacy-preserving statistical methods and survey sampling.
- Connects classical survey methodology (Warner) with contemporary differential privacy theory in a novel way.
- Strength: rigorous treatment of both discrete and continuous estimation problems under privacy constraints.
- Strength: demonstrates matching algorithms and links to classical results, advancing the state of the art.
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical implementation challenges beyond theoretical efficiency.
- Weakness: density estimation analysis is restricted to the Sobolev class; extensions to other function classes would be valuable.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, significant, technically strong, well-written, and practically relevant.
- Arguments against acceptance: some proofs omitted and insufficient discussion of implementation challenges.
- Recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
