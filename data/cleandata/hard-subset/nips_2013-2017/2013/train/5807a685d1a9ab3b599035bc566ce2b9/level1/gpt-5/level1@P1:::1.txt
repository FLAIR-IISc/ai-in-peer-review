Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric and multinomial probability estimation under local differential privacy (LDP). Each user privatizes Xi via a non-interactive channel Q(·|Xi) satisfying α-local DP, and the statistician only observes Zi. The authors bring together information-theoretic minimax lower bounds with privacy constraints to quantify the statistical cost of local privacy and to design optimal mechanisms.

Two main problems are analyzed. (1) Multinomial estimation: For θ ∈ Δd, the private minimax risks in ℓ2 and ℓ1 are shown to scale as min{1, d/(nα2)} and min{1, d/√(nα2)}, respectively, implying an effective sample size reduction n → nα2/d (for small α). A slight variant of Warner’s randomized response achieves these rates, establishing its optimality. (2) Density estimation over [0,1] for Sobolev classes Fβ: classical non-private risk is n−2β/(2β+1), but under LDP the minimax risk becomes (nα2)−2β/(2β+2), i.e., the polynomial exponent worsens. The authors give matching upper bounds via two mechanisms: a privatized histogram that is optimal for β=1 (Lipschitz densities) and a novel privatized orthogonal series estimator that is optimal for all β≥1. They also show that direct Laplace perturbation of raw samples cannot achieve the optimal rates (connecting to deconvolution lower bounds).

This work builds on and unifies strands from the DP literature (e.g., Dwork et al. on global DP; Kasiviswanathan et al. on local/interactive models; Hardt–Talwar on geometry and lower bounds; Smith, Chaudhuri et al., Wasserman–Zhou on statistical estimation with DP) and from information-theoretic minimax analysis (Le Cam, Fano, Assouad, Yang–Barron). The key technical tool is an information-contraction inequality under LDP (KL/mutual information bounds), stated here and developed in the authors’ companion work.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bounds follow a standard packing + Fano/Le Cam route, with novel contraction inequalities tailored to LDP. The upper bounds are constructive and computationally efficient: a modified randomized response for multinomial estimation, Laplace-perturbed histograms with projection, and an elegant privatized orthogonal series mechanism (randomized sign/halfspace selection) achieving unbiasedness and optimal variance scaling. The argument that simple additive noise to Xi is suboptimal for density estimation is well-justified via deconvolution theory.
- Rigor/completeness: Many proofs are deferred to the companion paper, which is acceptable for NIPS but weakens self-containment. Assumptions (non-interactive channels; α∈[0,1]; bounded bases) are clear. Constants are tracked, though practical guidance on their magnitude is limited.
- Evaluation: No empirical results; the contribution is theoretical. Claims are well-supported by analysis.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The minimax setup, notation, and LDP definition are precise. The mechanisms are described concretely, with attention to computational aspects (e.g., simplex projection in O(d)). A few places could benefit from added intuition (e.g., the geometric picture of the orthogonal series mechanism and the role of ck), and a brief complexity analysis of the rejection sampling would help.

Originality
- New minimax characterization under LDP for both multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, including the sharp change in the nonparametric exponent under LDP. The identification of randomized response as minimax-optimal for multinomials in this decision-theoretic sense is crisp and satisfying. The privatized orthogonal series mechanism is particularly novel and tailored to LDP constraints.

Significance
- High. LDP is increasingly relevant in practice (telemetry, crowd-sourcing). The results quantify fundamental privacy–accuracy trade-offs and provide optimal, implementable mechanisms. The polynomial-rate degradation in nonparametric settings under LDP is an important conceptual message likely to influence future theory and practice.

Strengths
- Sharp minimax lower and upper bounds under LDP for two core problems.
- Clean, interpretable rates and effective sample size heuristics.
- Optimality (and simplicity) of randomized response for multinomials.
- Novel, rate-optimal privatization for orthogonal series estimation.
- Clear connection to and extension of prior DP and minimax literature.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope: Focuses on non-interactive LDP. A discussion of whether interaction can improve rates (particularly for density estimation) would be valuable.
- Self-containment: Several key proofs rely on the companion paper. Including more proof sketches or a longer appendix would strengthen the submission.
- Practicality: No experiments. Simple simulations illustrating the rates and constants (e.g., multinomial MSE vs. n, α, d; density MISE vs. n, α, β) would enhance accessibility.
- Mechanism details: Clarify the constant ck in the orthogonal series mechanism, provide bounds uniform in k, and give a concise analysis of sampling efficiency (expected trials) and numerical stability for large k.
- Assumptions: Emphasize the boundedness requirement on basis functions and discuss extensions to unbounded bases or alternative function classes (e.g., Besov).
- Global DP comparison: A short section contrasting these LDP rates with known global DP rates would situate the contribution.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, original theoretical contribution with sharp, matching bounds.
- Relevance and timeliness: LDP is an active topic with practical importance.
- Elegant mechanisms with clear insights (optimality of randomized response).
- Advances understanding of privacy–statistical efficiency trade-offs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on companion paper for proofs.
- Lack of empirical illustration may limit accessibility for some NIPS audiences.
- Limited discussion of interactive protocols and whether rates could improve.

Overall recommendation
Accept. This paper delivers clear, significant theoretical advances on the foundations of learning under local differential privacy, with tight rates and practically meaningful mechanisms. With minor clarifications and, ideally, some empirical illustrations, it will be a strong addition to the NIPS program.
