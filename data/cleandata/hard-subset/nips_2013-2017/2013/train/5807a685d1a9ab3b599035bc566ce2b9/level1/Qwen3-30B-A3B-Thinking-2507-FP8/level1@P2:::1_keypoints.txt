- Rigorous theoretical analysis connecting Warner's randomized response with modern local differential privacy and minimax decision theory.  
- Establishes sharp minimax rates for multinomial and nonparametric density estimation under local privacy.  
- Shows effective sample size drops to nα²/d for multinomial estimation and density estimation rate becomes (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) versus classical n^(-2β/(2β+1)).  
- Provides tight minimax lower bounds and constructs estimators that achieve those bounds.  
- Proves Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation.  
- Develops specialized sampling strategies for density estimation and shows direct perturbation of samples is suboptimal.  
- Technically sound with rigorous proofs and correct application of the minimax framework; distinguishes population vs. sample estimation.  
- Exceptionally clear writing, consistent notation, and a strong explanation of links between historical and modern work.  
- Highly original: first sharp characterization of minimax rates under local privacy and novel insights on randomized response optimality.  
- Significant contribution: clarifies privacy–statistical efficiency trade-offs and offers theoretical tools applicable to other problems.  
- Strengths include sharp rates, optimality proof, efficient algorithms, historical-modern connections, and rigorous upper/lower bounds.  
- Weaknesses: limited experimental validation and potentially hard-to-implement complex sampling for β > 1.  
- Additional weaknesses: limited discussion of computational complexity and practical, real-world implications.  
- Meets NIPS criteria with distinction and recommended for acceptance.
