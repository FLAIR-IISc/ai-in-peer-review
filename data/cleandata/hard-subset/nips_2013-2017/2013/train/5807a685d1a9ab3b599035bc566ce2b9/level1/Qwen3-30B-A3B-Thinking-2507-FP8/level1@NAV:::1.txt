

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, establishing sharp minimax rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems. The authors connect Warner's classical randomized response [23] with modern differential privacy and statistical decision theory, demonstrating that Warner's approach is minimax optimal for multinomial estimation. For multinomial estimation, they show the effective sample size reduces from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy, while for density estimation with β smoothness, the convergence rate changes from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2). The paper provides matching lower and upper bounds with efficiently computable algorithms, addressing a significant gap in understanding privacy-statistical efficiency trade-offs.

The paper excels in technical quality, with rigorous minimax analysis that builds upon recent work in differential privacy [9, 11, 24] while making clear connections to classical survey methodology [23]. The theoretical framework is sound, with careful attention to both lower bounds and achievable upper bounds. The results are novel in the context of NIPS proceedings (comparing to recent papers like Smith [21] and Duchi et al. [9]), and the paper demonstrates significant theoretical advancement in understanding privacy constraints on statistical estimation.

Strengths include the clear exposition of fundamental trade-offs, the elegant mathematical treatment of both discrete and continuous cases, and the practical insight that Warner's 50-year-old method is optimal. The paper also provides efficient algorithms for achieving the theoretical bounds.

Weaknesses include limited discussion of practical implementation challenges beyond the theoretical framework, and the paper could better contextualize how these results compare to more recent NIPS work on privacy (e.g., Chaudhuri et al. [6] and Lei [19]). While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper would benefit from more discussion of real-world applicability.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-statistical efficiency trade-offs. The results are significant for both privacy and statistical theory, with clear implications for designing optimal privacy-preserving estimators. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The work represents a substantial advancement in the field, making it suitable for acceptance at NIPS.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, sharp minimax rates, clear connection to classical work, efficient algorithms, significant implications for privacy-preserving statistics.
Con: Limited practical discussion, could better contextualize within recent NIPS privacy literature.
