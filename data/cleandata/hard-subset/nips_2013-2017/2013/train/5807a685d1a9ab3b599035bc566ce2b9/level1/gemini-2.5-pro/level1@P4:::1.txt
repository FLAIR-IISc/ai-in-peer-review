This paper studies the fundamental trade-offs between statistical efficiency and privacy within the framework of local differential privacy. It leverages minimax decision theory to derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds on the convergence rates for two canonical statistical problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. The main idea is to characterize the minimax risk, `Mn`, for an estimator under the constraint that the data-generating mechanism provides `α`-local differential privacy. This connects the classical work of Warner on randomized response with the modern, formal definition of differential privacy, providing a quantitative understanding of the statistical cost incurred to achieve a certain level of privacy.

For multinomial estimation in `d` dimensions, the paper demonstrates that the minimax mean-squared error scales as `d / (nα^2)`, a significant degradation from the standard `1/n` rate. This result implies that local privacy effectively reduces the sample size from `n` to `nα^2/d`. A key consequence is that Warner's randomized response mechanism, a method proposed nearly 50 years prior, is shown to be minimax optimal.

For nonparametric density estimation over a Sobolev class `Fβ` (characterized by `β` degrees of smoothness), the paper reveals a more severe penalty. The classical minimax rate of `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` is shown to degrade to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. Unlike the multinomial case, here the polynomial exponent of the convergence rate itself changes, indicating a fundamental shift in the problem's difficulty. The paper complements these lower bounds by proposing specific, computationally efficient mechanisms that achieve these rates, including a private histogram estimator for `β=1` and a private orthogonal series estimator for general `β > 1`.

This work builds upon the growing literature at the intersection of privacy and machine learning, particularly works that have formalized differential privacy. However, it distinguishes itself by focusing on the stringent *local* privacy model and by providing sharp minimax rates for estimating *population* parameters, which is a stronger guarantee than estimating sample quantities.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. It adeptly combines tools from information theory (Fano's inequality, KL-divergence bounds) and statistical decision theory (Le Cam's method) to derive tight lower bounds on estimation error. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis presented. The work provides a complete analysis, presenting both lower bounds (impossibility results) and matching upper bounds via constructive, efficient algorithms.

2.  **Originality and Significance:** The contributions are highly original and significant.
    *   It provides the first sharp characterization of the minimax rates for fundamental statistical problems under the strong guarantee of local differential privacy. This establishes a fundamental limit on what any algorithm can achieve.
    *   The connection established between Warner's 1965 randomized response and modern minimax optimality under differential privacy is a deep and elegant insight. It provides a strong theoretical justification for a simple and classical mechanism.
    *   The discovery that local privacy changes the convergence rate's exponent for density estimation is a crucial finding. It demonstrates that the cost of privacy can be more than a simple reduction in effective sample size and highlights that naive perturbation schemes (like adding Laplace noise directly to data points) are provably suboptimal.
    *   These results are of broad importance to both researchers and practitioners. They offer a clear, quantitative guide to the "price of privacy," allowing for principled decisions when designing private data collection and analysis systems. The work is likely to inspire a significant amount of follow-up research.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, defines the local privacy setting, and introduces the minimax framework. The main theorems are stated precisely, and their implications are discussed lucidly. The descriptions of the privacy mechanisms that achieve the upper bounds are clear and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reproducibility:** A notable weakness is that the proofs for the main theorems are deferred to a longer, external paper on arXiv. While standard for conference submissions with page limits, this prevents a fully self-contained verification of the technical results from the submitted manuscript alone. The review must operate on the assumption that the referenced proofs are correct.

2.  **Practicality of Proposed Mechanisms:** The orthogonal series estimator proposed for general `β`-smooth density estimation involves a complex sampling strategy (Section 4.3, procedure 19). While claimed to be efficient, the details of its implementation (e.g., via rejection sampling) are not provided, making it difficult to assess its practical computational cost, especially for a large number of basis functions `k`.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Provides fundamental, sharp minimax rates for estimation under local differential privacy.
    *   Technically deep, combining statistical decision theory with modern privacy concepts.
    *   High originality and significance, with results that are likely to be foundational for future work. The optimality of randomized response is a standout finding.
    *   Excellent clarity and presentation.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The lack of self-contained proofs makes a full technical audit of the submission challenging.
    *   The computational feasibility of the most general proposed algorithm could be substantiated more thoroughly.

Overall, the strengths of this paper are substantial and far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It addresses a fundamental problem with a powerful theoretical framework, yielding results that are both insightful and significant for the NIPS community.
