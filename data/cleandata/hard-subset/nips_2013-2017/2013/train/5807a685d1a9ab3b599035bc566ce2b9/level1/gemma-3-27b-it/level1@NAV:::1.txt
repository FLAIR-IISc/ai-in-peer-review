## Review of “Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation”

This paper investigates the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency in probability estimation under local differential privacy. The authors provide sharp minimax rates of convergence for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation problems, building upon Warner’s pioneering work on randomized response and leveraging recent advances in differential privacy and minimax theory. This work is situated within a growing body of research addressing privacy-preserving statistical inference, notably building on prior work by Dwork et al. [11], Chaudhuri et al. [6], and more recently, Duchi et al. [9] (which the authors themselves utilize extensively). 

The paper’s main contributions are the derivation of explicit minimax rates demonstrating the penalty incurred by enforcing local privacy. For multinomial estimation, the effective sample size is reduced to *nα² / d*, and for density estimation, the convergence rate degrades from *n⁻²β/(2β+1)* to *(nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2)*. Crucially, the authors not only provide lower bounds but also exhibit matching upper bounds achievable through computationally efficient algorithms, including a refined analysis of Warner’s randomized response and a novel orthogonal series estimator.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, rigorously proving its main results with clear connections to established minimax theory. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature and accurately position their contributions within it. The results are significant, as they provide a formal quantification of the privacy-utility trade-off, which is crucial for practical applications of privacy-preserving data analysis. The explicit rates and the demonstration of achievable algorithms are particularly valuable. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, with definitions and notation clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:** While generally clear, the paper’s density and technical detail could make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with minimax theory and differential privacy. Some of the proofs are deferred to an accompanying technical report ([9]), which could hinder reproducibility for some. A more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the derived rates for specific applications would enhance the paper’s impact. While the authors mention the computational efficiency of their algorithms, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (beyond mentioning linear time for projection) would be helpful. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides sharp, theoretically grounded results on a fundamentally important topic.
*   Offers practical algorithms achieving the derived bounds.
*   Connects disparate fields (statistics, cryptography, and privacy).
*   Addresses a challenging and relevant problem with significant implications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   High technicality may limit accessibility.
*   Deferred proofs might hinder reproducibility.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a rigorous analysis of the trade-offs inherent in privacy-preserving statistical estimation. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the results are likely to be influential in guiding future research and practice in this area.
