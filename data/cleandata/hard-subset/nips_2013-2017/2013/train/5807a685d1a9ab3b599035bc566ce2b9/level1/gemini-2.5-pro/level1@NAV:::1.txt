This paper presents a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of probability estimation under the constraint of local differential privacy (LDP). The authors connect the modern framework of differential privacy, which has been extensively studied in computer science, with classical minimax statistical theory. The main contribution is the derivation of sharp minimax convergence rates for two fundamental problems: multinomial probability estimation and nonparametric density estimation. For estimating a d-dimensional multinomial, the authors show that LDP reduces the effective sample size from *n* to approximately *nα²/d*. For estimating a density with β derivatives, they demonstrate a more severe penalty, changing the polynomial convergence rate from the classical *n*⁻²β/(²β⁺¹) to (*nα²*)⁻²β/(²β⁺²). A significant consequence of this analysis is that Warner's classic randomized response mechanism from 1965 is shown to be minimax optimal.

The paper’s strengths are numerous and substantial.
**Pro-acceptance:**
*   **Significance & Originality:** The work is highly significant as it establishes a foundational bridge between two important fields. By providing sharp, non-asymptotic characterizations of the statistical cost of local privacy, it provides fundamental limits that will likely guide future algorithm design. The analysis is novel and leads to deep insights, such as the optimality of a classic mechanism and the sub-optimality of simple additive noise for density estimation.
*   **Quality & Clarity:** The technical quality is excellent. The paper provides a complete analysis, with tight lower bounds complemented by constructive, efficient algorithms that achieve these bounds. The theoretical claims are well-supported and build upon established results from information theory and statistics. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized, making its sophisticated arguments clear and accessible.

The paper’s primary weakness is its purely theoretical focus.
**Con-acceptance:**
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of numerical experiments is a limitation. While this is a theory paper, simulations verifying the derived convergence rates or illustrating the performance gap between the proposed optimal mechanisms and simpler, suboptimal ones (e.g., Laplace perturbation for density estimation) would have strengthened its impact and made the results more tangible for practitioners.

In summary, this is a landmark theoretical paper that provides definitive answers to fundamental questions about the trade-off between privacy and statistical accuracy. The technical contributions are deep, original, and of high importance to both the machine learning and statistics communities. Despite the lack of experiments, the theoretical significance is undeniable. This is a clear and enthusiastic accept.
