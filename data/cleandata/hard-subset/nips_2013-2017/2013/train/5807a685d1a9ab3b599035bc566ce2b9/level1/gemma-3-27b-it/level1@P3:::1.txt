This paper presents a thorough investigation of probability estimation under local differential privacy, establishing sharp minimax rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density) estimation. The work elegantly connects classical privacy notions (Warner’s randomized response) with modern differential privacy and minimax theory, providing a rigorous framework for understanding the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a crucial problem in the age of increasing privacy concerns.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors revisit Warner’s seminal work on randomized response and place it within a formal, decision-theoretic framework.  They leverage differential privacy, a more recent and rigorous formulation of privacy, to derive minimax convergence rates for estimation problems. This is a significant contribution, as prior work often focuses on *achieving* privacy with limited consideration of the resulting statistical penalty. The paper builds upon the work of Duchi et al. [9] (which the authors themselves authored) on statistical inference under differential privacy, providing concrete results for specific estimation problems. It also connects to the broader literature on minimax estimation (e.g., Tsybakov [23], van der Vaart [26]) and differential privacy (e.g., Dwork et al. [11], Chaudhuri et al. [6]). The paper distinguishes itself by focusing on *local* privacy (Evfimievski et al. [13]), where privacy is guaranteed even if the data collector is malicious.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, relying on minimax theory and differential privacy definitions. The lower bounds are particularly strong, demonstrating the inherent limitations imposed by privacy constraints.
* **Sharpness of Results:** The authors achieve matching upper and lower bounds for several key problems (multinomial estimation, density estimation), establishing the optimality of their proposed estimators.
* **Practical Relevance:** The analysis of Warner’s randomized response and the development of practical estimators (histogram-based, orthogonal series) demonstrate the applicability of the results. Showing randomized response is optimal after nearly 50 years is a particularly noteworthy achievement.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The notation is clear, and the derivations are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The authors carefully position their work within the existing literature, citing relevant papers and highlighting the contributions of their work.
* **Detailed proofs are available in [9]:** This is a strong point, as the main paper's focus remains on presenting the results and their implications, deferring the technical details to the linked work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The paper focuses primarily on two specific estimation problems (multinomial, density).  While these are important, the results might not generalize easily to other estimation problems or data types.  Exploring other scenarios (e.g., regression, high-dimensional parameter estimation) would strengthen the work.
* **Computational Cost of Estimators:** While the estimators are theoretically optimal, the computational cost of some (particularly the orthogonal series estimator with its associated sampling scheme) could be substantial, especially for high-dimensional problems. A more thorough discussion of the computational complexity and scalability would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions on Basis Functions:** The density estimation results rely on assumptions about the smoothness of the density and the choice of orthonormal basis functions.  The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not fully explored.
* **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the absence of empirical validation (even with simulations) weakens the practical impact of the work. Demonstrating the performance of the estimators on real or synthetic datasets would build confidence in the results.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents novel and significant theoretical results in a rapidly growing field.
* The work is technically sound, with clear proofs and rigorous analysis.
* The paper addresses an important problem with practical implications.
* The results have the potential to influence the design of privacy-preserving statistical methods.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scope of the paper is somewhat limited.
* The computational cost of some estimators could be prohibitive.
* The lack of empirical evaluation weakens the practical impact of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The theoretical results are rigorous and insightful, and the connections to both classical statistical theory and modern differential privacy are well-articulated. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience. I **strongly recommend acceptance** with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the weaknesses outlined above in future work (specifically, exploring the scalability of the estimators and providing some empirical validation). A minor revision addressing the empirical evaluation would substantially strengthen the paper.
