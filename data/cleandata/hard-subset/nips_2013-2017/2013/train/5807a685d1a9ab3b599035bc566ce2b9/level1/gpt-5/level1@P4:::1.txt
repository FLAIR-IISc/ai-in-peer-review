Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies probability distribution estimation under local differential privacy (LDP), where each data holder privatizes their datum before any interaction with the statistician. It develops sharp minimax rates for two settings: (i) multinomial probability estimation and (ii) nonparametric density estimation over Sobolev classes. The analysis combines classical minimax lower-bounding techniques (Le Cam, Fano) with information-contraction bounds specific to LDP. A key insight is that LDP fundamentally alters sample complexity: in multinomial estimation the effective sample size becomes nα^2/d (for small α), and in nonparametric density estimation the polynomial rate exponent deteriorates from 2β/(2β+1) to 2β/(2β+2). The paper provides matching upper bounds via simple, efficiently implementable mechanisms: a modified randomized response (RR) scheme for multinomials and privatized histogram/orthogonal series estimators for densities.

Relative to prior work, the paper builds on differential privacy (Dwork et al.), LDP (Evfimievski et al., Kasiviswanathan et al.), and geometry/information-theoretic analyses of DP (Hardt–Talwar, De). It distinguishes itself by focusing on population estimation under LDP in a minimax framework, rather than accuracy of specific sample statistics. The analysis relies on information bounds for LDP channels (from the authors’ prior work) to control KL and mutual information after privatization. It connects to classical results on minimax density estimation (Tsybakov) and deconvolution (Carroll–Hall), showing that naive Laplace perturbation of samples yields suboptimal rates.

Quality (technical soundness):
- Lower bounds: The paper uses packing arguments with LDP-specific information contraction (Proposition 1) to control divergences between privatized distributions. For multinomials, it yields rates Mn(Δd, ||·||2^2, α) ≍ min{1, d/(nα^2)} and Mn(Δd, ||·||1, α) ≍ min{1, d/√(nα^2)} for α ∈ [0,1]. For Sobolev densities Fβ, the lower bound Mn(Fβ, ||·||2^2, α) ≥ cβ (nα^2)−2β/(2β+2) matches the upper bounds.
- Upper bounds: The modified RR mechanism is shown α-LDP and leads to unbiased estimation with variance scaling yielding the minimax rate. For densities, a privatized histogram achieves the β=1 rate, and a carefully constructed locally private orthogonal series mechanism (bounded basis, randomized sign vectors with α-LDP sampling) achieves the optimal rate for general β ≥ 1. The paper also explains why direct Laplace perturbation of Xi cannot achieve the lower bound (via deconvolution limits).
- Arguments appear consistent and appropriately reference supporting theory. Some proofs are deferred to the longer version [9], which is acceptable given space, but reduces self-containedness.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, with clear problem formulation, definitions of LDP, minimax risk, and the channels. The mechanisms are described concretely (including parameter choices such as k ≍ (nα^2)1/(2β+2)), and the relationship to classical results is explicit. Minor notational typos appear (e.g., the Sobolev constraint expression showing “ϕ2j” where “ϕj^2” is intended; occasional overloaded symbols), and some constants (ck) are introduced without explicit form, though their role is clear. Providing a brief pseudocode for the orthogonal-series sampling strategy and clarifying the efficiency claims (e.g., expected number of trials in rejection sampling) would further aid reproducibility.

Originality:
The paper makes original contributions by:
- Establishing sharp minimax rates for population estimation under LDP for both multinomial and Sobolev density estimation, including the change in nonparametric rate exponent due to privacy.
- Showing optimality of (a variant of) Warner’s RR for multinomial estimation in the LDP minimax sense.
- Designing locally private orthogonal series mechanisms that achieve the lower bounds, and demonstrating the suboptimality of straightforward additive noise to samples.
These results represent a novel and principled quantification of the privacy–statistical efficiency trade-off in the local model.

Significance:
The results are fundamental and likely to influence both theory and practice of privacy-preserving data analysis:
- They provide clear guidance on the sample complexity costs of LDP.
- They identify optimal or near-optimal mechanisms (RR, privatized histograms/series) with implementable procedures.
- The framework can plausibly extend to other estimation problems.
Given the growing importance of local privacy in large-scale data collection, the contributions are timely and impactful.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Scope restriction: The analysis focuses on non-interactive (memoryless) channels. Discussion of whether interaction can improve rates (and how much) would be valuable.
- Proof accessibility: Several key proofs are deferred to [9]. Including condensed proof sketches for the main theorems would strengthen the standalone nature of the conference version.
- Assumptions: Density estimation assumes support on [0,1] with periodic boundary conditions via trigonometric bases and uniformly bounded bases. A brief discussion on extending to other supports/bases and boundary handling would broaden applicability.
- Finite-sample performance: No empirical evaluation is provided. Small-scale simulations illustrating constants and finite-sample crossover (e.g., α regimes, choice of k) would aid practitioners.
- Mechanism details: The orthogonal-series mechanism depends on constants (B0, ck) and uses rejection/uniform sampling over sign-constrained hypercube vertices. Additional details on computational complexity and parameter calibration would help adoption.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Sharp, technically sound minimax theory under LDP; clear advances over prior DP/LDP literature.
- Matching upper and lower bounds; concrete, efficient mechanisms.
- Clear articulation of privacy–utility trade-offs; broad relevance to NIPS community.
- Novel identification of exponent degradation in nonparametric rates; optimality of RR for multinomials.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on a companion paper for proofs; limited proof details in this version.
- Restriction to non-interactive local privacy; lack of discussion on interactive protocols.
- No experimental validation; practical performance and constants not illustrated.
- Some notation/typo issues and limited discussion on boundary effects and general bases.

Overall, the paper offers substantial, well-founded contributions to the theory of learning under local privacy with clear implications for practice.
