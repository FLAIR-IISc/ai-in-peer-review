

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, establishing sharp minimax convergence rates for both multinomial estimation and density estimation problems. The authors connect minimax decision theory with differential privacy, characterizing the fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency.

The paper begins by formalizing local differential privacy (as defined by Evfimievski et al.) and establishing the minimax framework for private estimation. The main contributions are two-fold: (1) for multinomial estimation, the paper establishes that the effective sample size decreases from n to nα²/d under α-local privacy, showing Warner's randomized response mechanism achieves the optimal rate; (2) for density estimation, the paper demonstrates that the classical minimax rate n⁻²β/(2β+1) degrades to (nα²)⁻²β/(2β+2) under local privacy constraints, with matching upper bounds provided via histogram and orthogonal series estimators.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with carefully constructed lower bounds matched by efficient upper bounds. The authors properly address both the strengths (e.g., optimal rates for multinomial estimation) and limitations (e.g., suboptimality of direct perturbation methods for density estimation) of their results. The paper provides a clear connection between Warner's 1965 work on randomized response and modern privacy frameworks, demonstrating that his classical approach is optimal for survey sampling under local privacy constraints.

The paper's originality is substantial. It provides the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy, distinguishing itself from prior work that focused on different privacy frameworks or sample estimation rather than population estimation. The authors properly situate their work within the NIPS proceedings by referencing relevant papers on differential privacy (e.g., Dwork et al. 2006, Duchi et al. 2013) and clearly articulating how their contribution advances the field.

The significance of the results is considerable. The paper establishes fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency that will guide the design of privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The finding that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation, despite being known for nearly 50 years, has practical implications for survey design. The paper's framework for understanding the privacy-statistical efficiency continuum provides a foundation for future work in privacy-preserving statistical inference.

Potential weaknesses include the focus on the non-interactive setting for local privacy, which, while justified by the authors, may not cover all privacy mechanisms. The paper also lacks extensive experimental validation, though this is expected for a theoretical contribution. The density estimation results are limited to Sobolev spaces with trigonometric basis, which may not cover all practical scenarios, though the authors justify this choice by referencing standard density estimation literature.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with consistent notation and appropriate mathematical rigor. The exposition effectively bridges statistical decision theory and differential privacy, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, including clear descriptions of the proposed estimators and their computational properties.

The main arguments in favor of acceptance are:
- The paper provides the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local differential privacy
- It establishes a fundamental connection between classical privacy work (Warner's randomized response) and modern differential privacy
- The results demonstrate a significant degradation in statistical efficiency under privacy constraints
- The paper provides efficient algorithms that achieve the lower bounds

The main arguments against acceptance are:
- The theoretical focus means limited experimental validation
- The analysis is confined to specific privacy models (non-interactive channels)
- The density estimation results are limited to specific function classes

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of privacy-statistical efficiency trade-offs. The results are technically sound, novel, and significant for the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The paper is clearly written and properly situates its contributions within the existing literature. The authors have successfully bridged classical statistical decision theory with modern differential privacy, providing a framework for understanding the fundamental costs of privacy in statistical estimation. The work is well-suited for NIPS as a high-quality theoretical contribution to a major challenge in machine learning and statistics.
