### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a rigorous, decision-theoretic analysis of the fundamental trade-off between statistical accuracy and privacy. The authors focus on the stringent model of *α-local differential privacy* (LDP), where each individual's data is privatized before being sent to a central aggregator. Using the tools of minimax theory, they derive sharp, non-asymptotic bounds on the best possible estimation error for two canonical statistical problems: (1) estimating the parameters of a discrete multinomial distribution and (2) non-parametric estimation of a continuous probability density function.

The main contributions are both theoretical and constructive. For d-dimensional multinomial estimation, the authors show that LDP incurs a substantial statistical cost, reducing the effective sample size from `n` to `nα^2/d`. A key insight is that Warner's classic 1965 randomized response mechanism is, up to constants, minimax optimal for this problem, providing a modern justification for a foundational technique in privacy. For density estimation over Sobolev classes with smoothness `β`, the authors reveal an even more dramatic consequence of LDP: the minimax convergence rate itself changes, degrading from the classical `n^(-2β/(2β+1))` to `(nα^2)^(-2β/(2β+2))`. This change in the polynomial exponent signifies a more severe penalty for privacy in non-parametric settings. Crucially, the authors complement these lower bounds with computationally efficient algorithms (a privatized histogram and a novel orthogonal series estimator) that achieve these new optimal rates.

The work elegantly bridges classical statistical decision theory (Le Cam, Fano, Tsybakov) with the modern computer science notion of differential privacy, providing a precise, quantitative framework for reasoning about the price of privacy.

### Review

This is an outstanding paper that makes fundamental contributions to the emerging field at the intersection of statistics and privacy. It is technically deep, the results are significant, and the presentation is exceptionally clear.

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality. The application of minimax theory to the local differential privacy setting is executed rigorously. The core of the lower bounds rests on information-theoretic inequalities (Proposition 1, drawing from the authors' companion work [9]), which effectively upper-bound the amount of statistical information that can be extracted under an α-LDP constraint. The derivation of matching upper bounds via explicit, practical algorithms makes the work complete and compelling. The authors are careful to distinguish their focus on *population parameter* estimation from prior work on *sample quantity* estimation, correctly noting that their lower bounds are stronger. The technical arguments, while sophisticated, appear sound. The one minor weakness is the reliance on the longer arXiv version [9] for the proofs of key results like Proposition 1 and Theorem 2. While standard practice for conference submissions, it does make the paper less self-contained.

**Clarity:** The paper is a model of clarity. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, defining the α-private minimax risk framework, and situating their work within the broader context of both classical statistics and modern privacy research. The notation is standard and the progression of ideas is logical, moving from the general framework to the specific applications in multinomial and density estimation. The discussion of why simple mechanisms like adding Laplace noise to the data points are suboptimal for density estimation is particularly insightful and well-argued.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. While the idea of a privacy-utility trade-off was not new, this work was among the first to formalize and quantify it with the full power of minimax decision theory in the local privacy model. The key novel contributions are:
1.  The formulation of the α-private minimax rate and the derivation of general-purpose tools to bound it.
2.  The sharp characterization of the minimax rate for multinomial estimation, which was a new and fundamental result.
3.  The surprising and elegant discovery that Warner's randomized response is an optimal mechanism. This connects a 50-year-old idea to the state-of-the-art in a profound way.
4.  The discovery that LDP changes the polynomial convergence rate for non-parametric density estimation. This is a deep result that highlights a fundamental difference in how privacy impacts parametric versus non-parametric problems.
5.  The design of novel privacy mechanisms, particularly the orthogonal series estimator, that are provably optimal in this new setting.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial and has proven to be lasting. It lays a theoretical foundation for understanding the ultimate limits of statistical inference under local privacy. By providing sharp rates, it gives practitioners a concrete formula to understand the "cost" of achieving a certain level of privacy in terms of the required sample size. For researchers, it established a powerful and widely adopted methodology for analyzing private algorithms. The results for the two specific problems studied are foundational and serve as important benchmarks. The insight about the sub-optimality of naive noise addition has spurred the development of more sophisticated privacy mechanisms. This paper has directly influenced a large body of subsequent work on private data analysis, both in theory and in systems that have deployed local privacy (e.g., by Apple and Google).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Addresses a fundamental problem at the intersection of machine learning, statistics, and privacy with a rigorous and powerful theoretical framework.
*   **Pro:** Delivers sharp, matching upper and lower bounds for two core statistical tasks, which represents a complete and satisfying theoretical contribution.
*   **Pro:** Contains multiple novel and surprising insights, including the optimality of randomized response and the change in convergence rates for non-parametric estimation.
*   **Pro:** Exceptionally well-written and accessible to an expert audience.
*   **Pro:** High potential for significant and lasting impact on the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The reliance on an external arXiv paper [9] for key proofs makes the paper not fully self-contained for verification, though this is a minor point given conference page limits.

In summary, this is a landmark paper that is technically excellent, highly original, and of great significance. It clearly meets and exceeds the high standards for a NIPS publication. I strongly recommend acceptance.
