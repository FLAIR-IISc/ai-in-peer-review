

# Review of "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, connecting Warner's classical randomized response technique with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for two fundamental statistical problems: multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation. They demonstrate that local privacy imposes fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, with the effective sample size decreasing from n to nα²/d in multinomial estimation, and a different convergence rate (nα²)^(-2β/(2β+2)) for density estimation compared to the classical rate n^(-2β/(2β+1)).

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Warner [23] on randomized response and modern differential privacy literature [1, 9, 11, 24], while providing novel theoretical results. The authors establish tight minimax lower bounds and construct efficient estimators that match these bounds, demonstrating that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation. For density estimation, they develop specialized sampling strategies that achieve the optimal rate, showing that direct perturbation of samples is suboptimal.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and careful attention to both lower and upper bounds. The minimax framework is properly applied to the local privacy setting, and the authors correctly acknowledge the distinction between population estimation (their focus) and sample estimation (prior work). The theoretical results are well-supported, with careful attention to the trade-offs between privacy parameter α and statistical efficiency. The paper provides a complete theoretical treatment of the problem, not merely a position paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly define the problem, present the theoretical framework, and logically develop the results. The notation is consistent and well-chosen. The connection between historical work (Warner) and modern theory is particularly well-explained. The paper provides sufficient mathematical detail for experts to verify the results and understand the techniques.

**Originality:** This work makes significant contributions by providing sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy constraints, a problem that has not been previously characterized with such precision. The results extend previous work on differential privacy [9, 11, 24] by providing fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The demonstration that Warner's 50-year-old randomized response method is optimal for multinomial estimation is particularly novel and insightful. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous NIPS work by providing sharp rates rather than merely establishing feasibility or suboptimal rates.

**Significance:** The results provide fundamental understanding of the cost of privacy in statistical estimation, with clear implications for designing privacy-preserving statistical procedures. The paper advances the state of the art in privacy-preserving statistical inference by providing the first sharp rates for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy. The theoretical tools developed here can be applied to other statistical problems, and the paper explicitly identifies a "privacy-statistical efficiency continuum" that can be leveraged to adjust statistical procedures according to privacy needs.

**Strengths:**
- Sharp minimax rates for both multinomial and density estimation under local privacy
- Proof that Warner's randomized response is optimal for multinomial estimation
- Efficient algorithms achieving the theoretical bounds
- Clear connection between historical and modern privacy work
- Rigorous theoretical treatment with both lower and upper bounds

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited experimental validation (the paper is purely theoretical)
- The density estimation approach for β > 1 requires a complex sampling strategy that may be challenging to implement
- Limited discussion of computational complexity beyond "efficiently computable"
- The practical implications for specific real-world applications could be more thoroughly explored

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference, with significant theoretical depth and clear connections to both historical and contemporary work. The results provide fundamental insights into the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, and the paper is well-positioned to influence future work in this area. The theoretical results are of high quality, the presentation is clear, and the significance of the work is substantial for both theoretical and applied researchers in privacy and statistical learning.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria with distinction. It is technically sound, clearly written, highly original, and of significant importance to the field. The authors have made a fundamental contribution to understanding the theoretical limits of privacy-preserving statistical estimation. I recommend acceptance.
