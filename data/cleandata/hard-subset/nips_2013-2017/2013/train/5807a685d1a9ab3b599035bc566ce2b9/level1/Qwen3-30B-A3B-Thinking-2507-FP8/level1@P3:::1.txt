

# Peer Review: "Local Privacy and Minimax Bounds: Sharp Rates for Probability Estimation"

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of probability estimation under local differential privacy constraints, bridging classical statistical decision theory with modern privacy frameworks. The authors establish sharp minimax rates for both discrete (multinomial) and continuous (density estimation) settings, demonstrating fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. Their work builds upon Warner's seminal 1965 randomized response framework while connecting it to the more recent formalization of differential privacy.

The paper's main contributions include:
1. For multinomial estimation: showing that local privacy at level α reduces the effective sample size from n to nα²/d, with Warner's randomized response achieving the optimal rate.
2. For density estimation: establishing that the convergence rate changes from n⁻²β/(2β+1) to (α²n)⁻²β/(2β+2), with matching upper bounds for both Lipschitz densities (β=1) and smoother densities (β>1).

The authors demonstrate strong theoretical rigor, providing both matching lower and upper bounds for both estimation problems. The proof techniques effectively combine minimax theory with information-theoretic bounds for differential privacy, extending the work of Duchi et al. (2013) while making significant new contributions. The paper carefully considers the trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency, providing a clear continuum along which privacy can be traded for utility.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy, resolving an important theoretical question.
- The demonstration that Warner's randomized response is optimal (after minor modifications) is historically significant and practically relevant.
- The authors provide computationally efficient algorithms that achieve the optimal rates, making the theoretical results practically applicable.
- The connection between classical survey methodology and modern privacy frameworks is insightful and well-executed.
- The paper's theoretical framework is generalizable to other statistical problems, as noted in the discussion.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper focuses exclusively on local differential privacy, without comparing with central differential privacy models that might achieve better rates (though this is justified by the authors' focus on local privacy as "the setting in which data is kept private even from the statistician").
- The practical implementation challenges in real-world settings (e.g., handling high-dimensional data, computational costs for large d or β) are not thoroughly explored.
- While the paper mentions that "other potential density estimators" are suboptimal, a more detailed comparison with alternative privacy-preserving density estimation approaches would strengthen the argument.
- The paper doesn't address the issue of privacy-utility trade-offs in terms of specific applications, which would enhance the practical significance.

**Quality Assessment:**
The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs of both lower and upper bounds. The authors carefully evaluate both the strengths and limitations of their approach, acknowledging that their results are for the local privacy model specifically. The minimax framework is correctly applied, and the information-theoretic bounds (Proposition 1) are appropriately used to derive the main results. The connection between classical and modern privacy frameworks is well-supported.

**Clarity Assessment:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The necessary background is provided concisely, with clear definitions of key concepts. The main results are stated precisely with theorems, and the proofs are logically structured. The notation is consistent throughout, and the paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand the technical content. The presentation of the randomized response mechanism (Equation 10) is particularly clear.

**Originality Assessment:**
This work significantly extends previous research on privacy and statistical inference. While Warner's randomized response has been known for decades, the authors provide the first minimax analysis showing its optimality under local privacy. The density estimation results (Theorem 2) are particularly novel, demonstrating a different convergence rate than classical non-private estimation. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work at NIPS (e.g., Duchi et al. 2013, Wasserman and Zhou 2010, Smith 2011) by focusing on population estimation rather than sample estimation, and by providing sharp rates rather than qualitative statements.

**Significance Assessment:**
The results have significant theoretical and practical importance. The paper establishes fundamental limits on statistical estimation under privacy constraints, providing a theoretical foundation for understanding the cost of privacy in statistical applications. The finding that Warner's 50-year-old method is optimal for survey sampling is historically significant and practically valuable. The framework developed can be extended to other statistical problems, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes significant advances in understanding the statistical consequences of local privacy. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important problem at the intersection of statistics and privacy. The results are novel and have clear implications for both theoretical and applied statistics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides the first sharp minimax rates for probability estimation under local privacy
- Demonstrates optimality of Warner's randomized response for multinomial estimation
- Establishes fundamental trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency
- Connects classical survey methodology with modern privacy frameworks
- Provides computationally efficient algorithms achieving the optimal rates

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- The paper is quite technical, which might limit accessibility to some readers
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges
- Does not compare with central differential privacy models (though this is justified by the paper's focus)

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistics. The results are significant, the methodology is sound, and the writing is clear. The work advances the state of the art in understanding the fundamental limits of statistical inference under privacy constraints. I strongly recommend acceptance.
