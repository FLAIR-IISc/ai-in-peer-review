This paper tackles the important problem of probability estimation under local differential privacy, building a theoretical foundation for understanding the inherent trade-offs between privacy and statistical efficiency. The authors connect Warner’s early work on randomized response with modern differential privacy and minimax decision theory, providing sharp convergence rates for both multinomial estimation and nonparametric density estimation. This is a significant contribution, as it formally quantifies the cost of privacy in these fundamental statistical problems. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and addresses a timely and relevant research question.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea is to leverage the framework of minimax optimality to characterize the best possible convergence rates for estimators when data is protected by local differential privacy. The authors build on Warner’s [23] randomized response method, a classical approach to protecting respondent privacy in surveys. They integrate this with the more formal framework of differential privacy introduced by Dwork et al. [11], which provides stronger semantic privacy guarantees. By combining these concepts with minimax theory [25, 26], they derive tight lower and upper bounds on the estimation error. The work builds on their previous paper [9], utilizing results from that work to obtain their bounds. They correctly situate their work within the broader literature on privacy and statistical inference [e.g., 1, 10, 13, 14, 15, 16, 18, 23].

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically rigorous, with clear definitions and careful derivations of the minimax rates. The use of differential privacy and minimax theory is appropriate and well-executed. The proofs, although summarized (with details relegated to a related work [9]), appear sound based on the presented arguments.
* **Novelty & Originality:** The combination of differential privacy with minimax optimality, resulting in sharp convergence rates for probability estimation, is a novel contribution. The demonstration that Warner’s method is optimal in this setting is a surprising and valuable result.  The extension to nonparametric density estimation, and the identification of a different convergence rate than in the non-private case, is also significant.
* **Significance:** The results are important for understanding the fundamental limits of privacy-preserving statistical inference. The quantified trade-offs provide practical guidance for designing privacy-preserving estimators.  The work has the potential to impact fields relying on sensitive data, such as healthcare, finance, and social sciences.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the arguments are presented in a logical and accessible manner. The introduction provides sufficient context and motivation for the work. The authors explain complex concepts with precision.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Reliance on Prior Work:** The paper relies heavily on results presented in a related work [9]. While this is common, it makes it difficult to fully assess the technical soundness of the derivations without access to and careful review of that paper.  More details, even in an appendix, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope:** The paper focuses on specific estimation problems (multinomial and density estimation) and assumes specific privacy mechanisms (local differential privacy). It would be valuable to discuss the limitations of these assumptions and potential extensions to other problems or privacy models.
* **Computational Complexity:**  The practical implications of the proposed estimators, particularly for high-dimensional density estimation, are not fully explored. Discussing the computational cost of the algorithms and potential scalability issues would strengthen the paper.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in a rigorous and insightful manner.
* The results are technically sound, novel, and significant.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
* It makes a substantial contribution to the field of privacy-preserving statistical inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on a related work for detailed proofs raises some concerns about complete verification.
* The scope is somewhat limited, and the practical implications of the proposed estimators could be further explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The technical soundness, novelty, significance, and clarity of the work make it a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions (e.g., including more details in the main paper or an appendix regarding the proofs from [9]).
