This paper proposes an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a recently developed method for model selection in latent variable models that maximizes a tractable lower bound on a "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC), which itself is an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. A key limitation of previous FAB work (e.g., on Mixture Models and HMMs) was the requirement that the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood be block-diagonal. LFMs violate this condition because model parameters are shared across all latent features. The central contribution of this paper is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian for LFMs (Lemma 1), which demonstrates that the resulting FIC has the same functional form as the one for mixture models. This insight allows the authors to derive a FAB algorithm for LFMs (FAB/LFM) that automatically determines the number of latent features. The paper further provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm, interpreting the optimization as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) which inspires a "shrinkage acceleration" technique, and proves a parameter identifiability result which motivates a feature merging post-processing step. The work is positioned as a computationally efficient and accurate alternative to non-parametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) for model selection in LFMs.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper makes a valuable and non-trivial extension to the FAB framework. Overcoming the block-diagonal Hessian limitation significantly broadens the applicability of this inference method. The derivation in Section 2.1, particularly Lemma 1, is the key technical insight and appears sound. The subsequent analysis in Section 3, connecting the E-step to CCCP and addressing parameter identifiability, adds depth to the work and leads to practical algorithmic improvements.

2.  **Significance:** The paper addresses the important problem of model selection for LFMs. Existing methods, such as those based on the IBP, can be computationally demanding. This work presents a compelling alternative that is shown to be faster and often more accurate in terms of prediction and model selection on several real-world datasets. The proposed method's ability to automatically select the number of features without user-tuned hyperparameters is a significant practical advantage. The results suggest FAB/LFM could be a useful tool for researchers and practitioners who use LFMs.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the theoretical development is presented in a logical sequence, and the final algorithm is easy to follow. The relationship to prior work on both FAB and LFMs is well-established, helping the reader to situate the contribution within the field.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough experimental comparison against several state-of-the-art methods (IBP with fast Gibbs sampling, Variational Bayes for IBP, and MEIBP). The evaluation on both synthetic and real-world data convincingly demonstrates the advantages of the proposed FAB/LFM method in terms of computational efficiency, predictive performance (PLL), and its ability to find more compact models (smaller K) while avoiding overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL gap).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Nature:** While the extension is non-trivial, the work is fundamentally an application and extension of the pre-existing FAB framework [5, 6 from the paper]. The core ideas of FIC and its lower-bound optimization are not new.

2.  **Asymptotic Assumptions:** The entire framework relies on an asymptotic approximation (large N). The paper briefly acknowledges this limitation in the discussion of the Libras dataset results, where performance is worse in a small N scenario. A more detailed discussion of the limits of this approximation would be beneficial. For example, how does the quality of the FIC approximation depend on the data dimensionality (D) or the true number of features (K) in relation to the number of samples (N)?

3.  **Experimental Setup:** For the competing IBP-based methods, the noise standard deviation was fixed rather than learned. While the authors state this follows the setup in the original papers for those methods, it could potentially put them at a disadvantage compared to FAB/LFM, which learns the noise precision. A more direct comparison where all methods learn the noise parameter would strengthen the empirical claims.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and technically sound extension of the FAB inference framework to the important class of Latent Feature Models, overcoming a key limitation of prior work.
*   The resulting algorithm is computationally efficient, fully automated, and empirically demonstrates superior or competitive performance against state-of-the-art IBP-based methods.
*   The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and includes insightful theoretical analysis that leads to practical algorithmic refinements.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The contribution could be viewed as an incremental extension of an existing inference framework.
*   The reliance on large-sample asymptotics is a limitation that is not fully explored.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a well-executed piece of research with solid theoretical and empirical contributions that are of clear interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
