This paper proposes a novel extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), allowing for automatic model selection and parameter estimation. The authors demonstrate that the FAB framework can be applied to LFMs by deriving a new form of the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) that is similar to the one used for Mixture Models (MMs). The proposed approach is evaluated on various datasets and compared to state-of-the-art methods, including Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and Variational Bayes (VB) methods.

The paper's main contribution is the derivation of the FIC for LFMs, which enables the application of the FAB framework to these models. The authors show that the FIC for LFMs has the same form as the FIC for MMs, despite the fact that LFMs do not satisfy the conditions required for the FAB framework. This result is significant because it extends the applicability of the FAB framework to a broader class of models.

The paper also presents an efficient algorithm for optimizing the FIC, which is based on the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors propose a shrinkage acceleration method that reduces the computational cost of the algorithm by eliminating irrelevant features in early iterations.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in terms of model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency. The authors show that the FAB framework outperforms state-of-the-art methods, including IBP and VB methods, on various datasets.

However, the paper has some limitations. The mathematical exposition is imprecise in places, and some assumptions are not clearly specified. For example, the asymptotic regime and the scaling of K with N are not clearly defined in Theorem 2. Additionally, the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the predictive improvements come from the model-selection criterion or from the algorithms avoiding worse local optima.

In terms of originality, the paper's technical contribution is novel but incremental, building on prior work on FAB inference. The approach is reasonable and motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument, but it does not revolutionize the field.

Overall, the paper presents a sensible but somewhat incremental method for model selection and parameter estimation in LFMs. While the experimental results are promising, further analysis is needed to fully interpret the reported gains and to clarify the limitations of the proposed approach.
