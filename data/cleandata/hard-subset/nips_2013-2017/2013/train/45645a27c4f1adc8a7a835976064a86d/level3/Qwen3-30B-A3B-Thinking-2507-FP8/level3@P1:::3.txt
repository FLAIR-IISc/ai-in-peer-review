

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a significant theoretical contribution that addresses a gap in the FAB framework. Previous work on FAB inference was limited to models satisfying a specific condition on the Hessian matrix (block diagonal structure), which excludes LFMs. The authors prove through asymptotic analysis that LFMs, despite not meeting this condition, yield FICs with the same form as mixture models (Theorem 2), enabling FAB application to LFMs. This is a novel extension of FAB beyond its original scope of mixture models and HMMs. The paper demonstrates that FAB/LFMs offer automatic hidden state selection (K), parameter identifiability, and computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The theoretical contributions are strong. Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 provide rigorous justification for the FIC form in LFMs, showing that the log ∑ₙ zₙₖ term naturally appears in the log determinant of the Hessian without relying on the assumptions A1 and A2 used for mixture models. The parameter identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) is particularly valuable, as it provides a theoretical basis for post-processing (merging features) that improves model compactness. The CCCP interpretation of the FAB algorithm (Section 3.1) is insightful, leading to a practical shrinkage acceleration method that significantly reduces computational cost (Figure 1).

Empirically, the paper demonstrates FAB/LFMs outperform IBP (Gibbs sampling), variational Bayesian (VB), and MEIBP methods on several datasets in terms of model selection accuracy, prediction performance, and computational efficiency. The results on artificial data (Figure 2) and real-world datasets (Table 1) show FAB consistently achieves better predictive log-likelihoods with smaller K values and lower computational costs. The shrinkage acceleration mechanism (Algorithm 2) proves particularly effective in reducing runtime without compromising model quality.

However, the experimental evaluation has several limitations. The use of MEIBP as a baseline is problematic since it requires non-negative weight matrices W, but the paper compares it with datasets containing negative values (e.g., EEG, Piano), invalidating these comparisons. The noise variance setting for real data (0.75 times standard deviation) appears inconsistent with recommended practices (0.25 times, per references [1,22]), and the block data experiments should have used the known noise value instead of a heuristic. The suspicious result of ~70 features on 49-d Sonar data (Section 4) suggests potential issues with initialization or noise variance that warrant further investigation. The paper also lacks exploration of small-data regimes where asymptotic arguments may fail, which is particularly important given the theoretical foundation of the method.

The discussion of related work is incomplete. The paper should cite and discuss MAD-Bayes (ICML 2013), which considers asymptotics of LFMs through a different limiting case (noise variance going to zero). Reference [21] is incorrectly described as Gibbs sampling when it performs MAP inference, requiring correction. The paper also fails to address potential convergence difficulties in the EM-like procedure and lacks discussion of algorithmic limitations beyond the binary LFM constraint.

The methodology is well-explained, though some experimental details (e.g., number of restarts for VB-IBP, initialization procedures) are insufficiently described. The paper's limitation to binary latent feature models (not applicable to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA) should be acknowledged more prominently.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by extending FAB to LFMs and demonstrating its practical utility. The core ideas are sound, and the empirical results generally support the claims. However, the experimental evaluation requires significant refinement to address the methodological issues noted above. The paper's significance is high given the importance of efficient model selection in latent variable models, but the current experimental presentation undermines the strength of the claims.

The paper's strengths include its theoretical novelty, the practical shrinkage acceleration method, and the parameter identifiability analysis. The weaknesses involve experimental limitations, incomplete related work discussion, and insufficient exploration of the method's boundaries. With careful revision to address the experimental concerns and improve the related work discussion, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, I recommend rejection due to unresolved methodological issues in the experimental evaluation, though I acknowledge the theoretical merit of the work.
