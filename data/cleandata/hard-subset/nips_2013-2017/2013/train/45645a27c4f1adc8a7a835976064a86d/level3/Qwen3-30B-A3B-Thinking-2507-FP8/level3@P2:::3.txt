

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), which had previously not been applicable to LFMs due to the requirement of a specific condition on the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood. The authors show that despite LFMs not satisfying the required condition, their asymptotic analysis reveals that FICs for LFMs have the same form as those for mixture models, enabling the development of FAB/LFMs. This approach offers automatic hidden states selection (determining the number of latent features K) without requiring priors or hand-tunable hyperparameters. The paper demonstrates that FAB/LFMs achieve better computational efficiency than state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods while maintaining competitive model selection and prediction performance.

The work builds on previous FAB applications to mixture models (Fujimaki and Morinaga, AISTATS 2011) and hidden Markov models (Fujimaki and Hayashi, ICML 2012), extending the theoretical framework to a broader class of models. It also relates to nonparametric Bayesian methods for LFMs (Griffiths and Ghahramani, NIPS 2005; Doshi-Velez et al., AISTATS 2009) and MAP-based approaches like MEIBP (Reed and Ghahramani, ICML 2013).

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical contribution is significant: the authors successfully extend FAB inference to LFMs despite the apparent incompatibility with the required Hessian condition, demonstrating that FICs for LFMs share the same form as for mixture models.
- The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, with the automatic selection of K being a valuable practical advantage over existing methods.
- The experimental results generally show FAB/LFMs outperforming IBP, variational methods, and MEIBP in terms of computational efficiency while achieving competitive or better model selection accuracy and prediction performance on several datasets.
- The shrinkage acceleration method significantly reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process.

**Weaknesses:**
- The experimental evaluation contains several flaws that undermine the comparative claims. Most notably, the MEIBP baseline was inappropriately used in comparisons with block data and artificial simulations, as MEIBP requires non-negative features while the block data contains negative values. This invalidates those comparisons.
- The paper lacks sufficient detail about baseline implementations (e.g., for VB-IBP, it's unclear how many restarts were performed or how K was selected), making the comparisons less convincing.
- The paper fails to discuss important limitations: the method is restricted to binary latent feature models (not applicable to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA), and no discussion is provided about potential convergence difficulties with the EM-like procedure.
- The behavior of the method in small-data regimes (where asymptotic arguments may fail) is not explored, which is particularly concerning given the paper's reliance on asymptotic analysis.
- Some experimental results are questionable (e.g., finding ~70 features on 49-dimensional Sonar data with FAB), suggesting possible issues with initialization or hyperparameter settings.
- The paper omits important related work, including MAD-Bayes (Broderick et al., ICML 2013), which also considers small-variance asymptotic approaches for nonparametric LFMs. The reference to [21] incorrectly describes it as Gibbs sampling when it actually performs MAP inference.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and well-executed.
- The automatic model selection capability (determining K) is valuable for real-world applications.
- The method demonstrates clear computational advantages over existing approaches.
- The paper provides useful theoretical insights about parameter identifiability and merge post-processing.

*Con:*
- The experimental evaluation is flawed and lacks sufficient detail to be convincing.
- The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the method or discuss cases where it may underperform.
- The comparison with MEIBP is invalid in key experiments due to the non-negative constraint.
- The paper fails to acknowledge relevant recent work (MAD-Bayes) and contains incorrect descriptions of existing methods.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of FAB inference to latent feature models with promising empirical results. However, the experimental evaluation contains significant flaws that need to be addressed before the paper can be considered for acceptance. The authors should: (1) correct the MEIBP comparisons to use appropriate baselines; (2) provide more detailed experimental descriptions (including implementation specifics for all baselines); (3) discuss the limitations of the method, particularly regarding the binary feature restriction; (4) include a discussion of MAD-Bayes and other relevant recent work; and (5) investigate the questionable results on the Sonar dataset.

With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of latent variable modeling. As it stands, the current experimental evaluation does not sufficiently support the claims made about the method's advantages over existing approaches. I recommend rejection pending substantial revisions to address these methodological issues.
