

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), overcoming a key limitation of previous FAB work that required a specific Hessian condition not satisfied by LFMs. The authors demonstrate through rigorous asymptotic analysis that FIC for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models (despite not satisfying the original Hessian condition), enabling automatic model selection without hand-tuning. The method combines mean-field inference with accelerated feature shrinkage, producing a computationally efficient approach that outperforms state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real datasets in terms of model selection accuracy, prediction, and speed.

The paper excels in clarity and structure, with well-motivated theoretical development and thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets. The authors provide comprehensive comparisons against relevant methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP), demonstrating significant computational advantages while maintaining or improving predictive performance. The method represents a solid, logical extension of prior FAB/FIC work, combining several approximations into a novel, effective framework that achieves automatic dimensionality selection—a key advantage over competing approaches.

The work demonstrates strong quality through theoretically grounded analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) and well-supported empirical results. It makes a significant contribution to the field by extending FAB's applicability to a broader class of models while maintaining theoretical properties like parameter identifiability and automatic feature selection. The paper is highly original in its combination of FAB, mean-field approximation, and shrinkage acceleration for LFMs, with appropriate references to related work.

The primary weakness concerns the lack of analysis regarding failure modes of the combined approximations. While the paper demonstrates success across multiple datasets, it does not systematically explore scenarios where the mean-field approximation, FIC approximation, or shrinkage mechanism might fail (e.g., highly correlated features, specific data distributions, or extreme noise regimes). The authors should address these limitations in the final version.

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear theoretical grounding, compelling empirical results, and significant practical advantages. The minor weakness regarding failure modes does not undermine the substantial technical contribution, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the failure mode analysis. The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection for latent feature models while offering substantial computational benefits.
