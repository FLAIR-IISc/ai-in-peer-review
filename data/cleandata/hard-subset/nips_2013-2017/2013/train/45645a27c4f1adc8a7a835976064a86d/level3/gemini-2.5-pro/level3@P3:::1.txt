This paper proposes extending Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a model selection framework, to binary latent feature models (LFMs). The core idea is to approximate the marginal log-likelihood by integrating out model parameters using a Laplace approximation, which yields a criterion similar to the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). This "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC) includes a complexity penalty term that encourages sparsity in the latent features, allowing for automatic model selection (i.e., determining the number of features K). The authors derive an EM-like algorithm to optimize a lower bound of the FIC.

This work builds directly on previous FAB literature for mixture models and HMMs (e.g., [5, 6]), extending the framework to a model class where the standard block-diagonal Hessian assumption does not hold. The problem of determining the number of latent features is central to LFMs and is most prominently addressed by non-parametric Bayesian approaches, particularly the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). This paper positions FAB/LFM as a computationally efficient and potentially more accurate alternative to IBP implementations like Gibbs sampling, Variational Bayes, and MAP-based estimation (MEIBP).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Practicality:** The paper presents a novel application of the FAB framework to binary LFMs, which is a non-trivial extension. The resulting EM-like algorithm with an integrated complexity penalty is an appealing and practical alternative to more complex non-parametric methods or standard cross-validation. The proposed shrinkage acceleration technique also appears effective at speeding up convergence.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate compelling results. The FAB/LFM method achieves superior or competitive predictive log-likelihoods on held-out data compared to several state-of-the-art IBP implementations, often with significantly faster runtimes.
*   **Sound Motivation:** The approach is reasonably motivated by an asymptotic argument based on the Laplace approximation. This provides a principled, if approximate, basis for the model selection criterion, connecting it to established concepts like BIC.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the application to LFMs is new, the core technical machinery is a direct extension of prior FAB work by Fujimaki et al. ([3], [4] in the provided list; [5, 6] in the paper's bibliography). The main contribution is showing that the FIC for LFMs takes a familiar form, but it doesn't introduce a fundamentally new inference principle.
*   **Mathematical Imprecision:** The mathematical exposition could be tightened in several places:
    *   The definition of the FIC in equation (3) involves a `log(sum_n z_nk)` term. If a particular feature `k` is never used for any data point (i.e., `z_nk = 0` for all `n`), the argument of the logarithm becomes zero, making the objective ill-defined. The paper should clarify how this edge case is handled and what its implications are for the optimization.
    *   Theorem 2, a key theoretical result, lacks a precise specification of the asymptotic regime under which the approximation holds. In particular, the relationship between the number of features K and the number of data points N is not stated. The BIC-style penalty is typically derived assuming a fixed, finite K as N goes to infinity. This seems at odds with the motivation of competing with the IBP, which is premised on a potentially infinite K. The validity of the approximation when K can be large or grow with N is unclear.
    *   There are minor formal errors. For instance, in the discussion of mixture models in Section 1.1, `pk(X | z·k)` should be conditioned only on the subset of data `X` assigned to component `k`, not the entire dataset. In Equation (4), while the expectation `Eq` is correctly placed on the left-hand side, the exposition surrounding the lower-bounding technique could be clearer.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Analysis:** The empirical results are promising but difficult to fully interpret due to a lack of detail.
    *   Table 1 reports that FAB converges much faster than the IBP Gibbs sampler, but the stopping criteria for the sampler are not specified. Gibbs samplers for IBP are typically run for a fixed number of iterations, making a direct comparison of "convergence time" ambiguous. The number of iterations used and the justification for this choice are needed to assess whether the comparison is fair.
    *   It is unclear whether the reported improvements in predictive performance stem from the superiority of the FIC as a model selection criterion or from the algorithm's ability to avoid poor local optima. The authors note that Gibbs sampling for IBP tends to learn too many components, but this could be an artifact of hyperparameter settings or known mixing issues with the sampler. The experiments do not adequately disentangle the quality of the objective function from the quality of the optimization algorithm.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a sensible and useful method for model selection in binary latent feature models. The approach is well-motivated and the empirical results suggest it is a powerful practical tool. However, the contribution feels somewhat incremental, building heavily on the authors' prior work. More importantly, the paper suffers from several points of mathematical imprecision and an experimental evaluation that, while showing strong results, lacks the necessary detail to fully support the claims of superiority over competing methods. The work is promising, but requires significant clarification of its theoretical underpinnings and a more rigorous experimental comparison to be a fully convincing contribution.
