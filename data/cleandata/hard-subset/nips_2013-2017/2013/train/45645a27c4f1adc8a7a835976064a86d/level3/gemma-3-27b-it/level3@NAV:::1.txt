## Review of “Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models”

This paper proposes a novel extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a limitation of prior FAB work which required a block-diagonal Hessian. The authors demonstrate that a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) can be derived for LFMs, leading to an EM-like algorithm with complexity penalties, and showcase improved performance – faster convergence and better predictive likelihoods – compared to several IBP implementations on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The approach is logically sound, building on existing FAB theory and providing a plausible asymptotic argument for extending the method to LFMs. The motivation for automating model selection (dimensionality of latent features) and avoiding the need for manual hyperparameter tuning is well articulated. The connection to Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) offers a clear rationale for the complexity penalties, and the proposed shrinkage acceleration technique based on a Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation is a valuable contribution. 

However, the paper suffers from several shortcomings. While the mathematical exposition is generally clear, it lacks precision in crucial areas. Specifically, Equation (3) raises concerns about the validity of the FIC when all elements of z are zero, potentially leading to an infinite log-sum. Theorem 2's asymptotic regime and scaling assumptions are underspecified – how K (number of latent features) scales with N (number of samples) is unclear, which is critical given BIC’s requirement for a fixed, finite K, contrasting with the IBP’s infinite-K framework. Minor technical errors are present (conditioning in section 2 and a missing expectation in equation (4)).

The experimental evaluation, while promising, is incomplete. Stopping criteria for the FAB algorithm are not clearly defined, making runtime comparisons and truncation decisions for the Gibbs sampler somewhat arbitrary. It remains unclear whether the observed predictive improvements stem directly from the model selection criterion or from the FAB algorithm avoiding suboptimal local optima, a common issue with Gibbs sampling. The tendency of Gibbs sampling to learn an excessive number of components further suggests potential implementation or local-optima related issues.

**Overall:** This is a sensible, albeit incremental, contribution. The core idea is sound and the proposed method shows promise. However, the lack of mathematical rigor in certain areas and the insufficient detail in the experimental analysis hinder a stronger assessment of the reported gains. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Novel extension of FAB inference to LFMs.
* Improved performance compared to existing methods.
* Logically sound theoretical motivation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Imprecise mathematical exposition.
* Underspecified asymptotic assumptions.
* Incomplete experimental evaluation and unclear interpretation of results.
