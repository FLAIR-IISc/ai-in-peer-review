This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian observations. The core theoretical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the complete-data Hessian showing that the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models despite the lack of block-diagonal structure. Specifically, Lemma 1 shows that the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian contributes a sum of log sum_n z_nk terms, yielding Theorem 2 and an FIC that penalizes each feature by D/2 log(sum_n z_nk) plus an O(K log N) term. Substituting this into the usual variational identity produces FICLFM, and a tractable lower bound is optimized using (i) a linearization of the convex penalty, (ii) joint optimization over parameters P instead of the MLE, and (iii) a mean-field q(Z) over binary indicators. The resulting E-step updates are coordinate-wise logistic regressions with a FAB-specific shrinkage term proportional to −D/(2Nπ̃_k), which encourages automatic removal of unused features. The M-step is closed-form as in PPCA, with π_k and π̃_k both set to the empirical usage. 

Beyond establishing the FIC, the paper relates the E-step to a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), proposing accelerateShrinkage: multiple CCCP inner updates per iteration to drive π̃_k down faster and remove irrelevant features early, reducing total runtime. A second analysis proves a form of parameter identifiability under FAB’s regularization: if two columns of W coincide, the optimal solution balances their usages, motivating a merge post-processing step that combines near-duplicate features when it increases the lower bound. 

The approach is positioned relative to prior FAB for mixtures and HMMs [5,6], Indian Buffet Process (IBP) models and their variational and sampling algorithms [10,3,2], and recent MAP/submodular MEIBP [22]. It also contrasts with zero-noise asymptotics (MAD-Bayes) [1] and discusses the relation to BIC [23] and singular learning theory [25,26]. Empirically, the method is evaluated on synthetic and several real datasets, reporting computational time, selected K, and predictive log-likelihoods. FAB typically selects compact models with competitive or superior predictive performance, while being substantially faster than most NPB/VB baselines; MEIBP can be faster in some settings but often underperforms in prediction.

Quality: The technical development is sound and carefully connected to prior FAB theory. The key Hessian lemma is plausible given the model structure; proofs are in the supplement. The optimization bound is well-motivated, and monotone ascent of the lower bound is maintained. The CCCP view is correct and justifies the acceleration. The identifiability result and merge heuristic are reasonable and practically useful. Main caveat: several approximations are combined (asymptotics/Op(1), mean-field q(Z), linearization for the convex penalty, early shrinkage and merging). The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of when these approximations may break (e.g., small N, highly correlated or dense features, extreme noise levels), and from ablations quantifying the contribution of each component (FAB penalty, CCCP acceleration, merging). Also, some baselines fix noise variance whereas FAB learns Λ; reporting FAB results under matched noise settings would strengthen fairness claims.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well structured. Derivations are crisp, and algorithms are easy to follow. Suggestions: state the exact assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text, not only the supplement; include per-iteration computational complexity and memory; clarify how merge thresholds are chosen and how monotonicity is preserved; and discuss practical diagnostics for when FIC ≈ log evidence is reliable.

Originality: Extending FAB/FIC beyond block-diagonal complete-data Hessians to LFMs is a nontrivial and novel theoretical step. The CCCP reinterpretation and the resulting shrinkage-acceleration are conceptually simple but effective. The identifiability result and its use for merging add insight.

Significance: Automatic selection of K with minimal tuning in LFMs is important. The empirical results suggest that FAB/LFM is a strong practical alternative to IBP-style methods, often with better predictive performance and substantially lower cost. The ideas should transfer to other discrete latent variable models and encourage broader use of FAB-style criteria. Limitations include the linear-Gaussian setting and reliance on asymptotics; discussing extensions to non-Gaussian likelihoods or continuous features (e.g., BPCA) would broaden impact.

Specific questions and suggestions:
- Assumptions for Lemma 1: please summarize them (e.g., diagonal Λ, bounded moments, nondegeneracy) and discuss cases where they fail (dense Z, near-collinearity in W).
- Provide an ablation showing effects of: (a) FAB penalty on K selection vs. prior-only regularization, (b) accelerateShrinkage, (c) merge step.
- Report sensitivity to initialization and Tshrink; provide guidance on default choices.
- Fair comparison: some baselines fix noise variance; add a variant where Λ is fixed for FAB, or allow Λ learning in baselines when feasible.
- Clarify runtime scaling with N, D, K; include wall-clock vs. iterations and the effect of early feature pruning.
- Discuss diagnostics for small N where the Op(1) gap may matter; can you detect and mitigate under- or over-shrinkage?
- Explore extension to non-Gaussian likelihoods or count data; does Lemma 1 generalize?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical advance: FIC for LFMs without block-diagonal Hessian, with clear implications.
- Practical, well-engineered algorithm with automatic K selection and strong empirical performance.
- CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability/merge analysis add value beyond a direct FAB port.
- Clear writing and thorough experimental comparisons.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Combined approximations lack a detailed failure-mode analysis; limited guidance for small-sample regimes.
- Some comparative choices (fixed noise in baselines, omission of VB due to time) leave room for doubt about fairness.
- Scope limited to linear-Gaussian LFMs; generality to other likelihoods not demonstrated.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that meaningfully advances FAB methodology and provides a competitive, low-tuning approach to latent feature modeling. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions, adding ablations, and tightening the empirical comparisons.
