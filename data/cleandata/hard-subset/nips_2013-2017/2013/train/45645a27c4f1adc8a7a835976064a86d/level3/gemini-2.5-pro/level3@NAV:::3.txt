This paper presents an EM-based algorithm for inference in binary latent feature models (LFMs) using Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference. The core idea is to leverage a FAB-derived shrinkage criterion, which serves as a model selection penalty, to automatically infer the number of latent features, K, during learning. This approach is positioned as a scalable alternative to nonparametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), which rely on priors for model parsimony. The extension of the FAB framework, previously applied to mixture models and HMMs, to the more complex dependency structure of LFMs is the central contribution.

The paper's strengths are significant. The technical approach appears sound, and applying FAB to LFMs is a novel and valuable contribution. It addresses the important problem of performing efficient inference in LFMs while simultaneously determining model complexity. The algorithm's ability to scale to large datasets and the promising experimental results, which suggest it can outperform IBP-based Gibbs, variational, and MAP methods in runtime and quality, are commendable.

However, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses, primarily in its experimental evaluation and discussion. The evaluation contains critical flaws that invalidate some of the comparisons and undermine confidence in the results. Specifically, the MEIBP baseline was used inappropriately on data with negative values, IBP Gibbs was not provided the known noise variance in a key experiment, and details for the variational baseline are missing. Furthermore, the discussion lacks an analysis of the method's limitations, such as potential convergence issues for the EM procedure, its restriction to binary LFMs, and its behavior in small-data regimes where the asymptotic justification for FAB may not hold. The related work section is also incomplete, notably omitting recent work on small-variance asymptotics for nonparametric LFMs (e.g., MAD-Bayes, ICML 2013) and incorrectly describing reference [21] as Gibbs sampling instead of MAP inference.

In conclusion, while the core methodological contribution is strong and significant, the manuscript is not yet ready for acceptance. The experimental flaws are too severe to be overlooked. Before publication, the authors must conduct a thorough and fair experimental re-evaluation with corrected baseline settings and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. Additionally, they must improve the discussion of related work, correct factual errors in citations, and add a candid discussion of the algorithm's limitations. If these major issues are comprehensively addressed, the paper would represent a solid contribution to the field.
