This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a setting where previous FAB work was not directly applicable. The authors demonstrate that the Fisher Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models, enabling the application of FAB with its desirable properties – automatic hidden state selection, parameter identifiability, and computational efficiency. Empirical evaluation on synthetic and real datasets demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods like IBP and variational inference, particularly in terms of runtime and model selection.

This work builds upon a growing body of research on efficient inference in latent variable models. FAB inference, as presented, offers a deterministic alternative to fully Bayesian nonparametric approaches like IBP, addressing a key limitation of those methods: computational cost. The extension to LFMs is a novel contribution, extending the applicability of FAB beyond mixture models and hidden Markov models. The theoretical analysis demonstrating the equivalence of the FIC form for LFMs and MMs is a significant result, justifying the extension. The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, inspired by a connection to the convex-concave procedure (CCCP), is a valuable practical contribution, demonstrably reducing computational time. The discussion of parameter identifiability and a post-processing merge step further strengthen the theoretical grounding of the method.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness, the clear presentation of the theoretical framework, and the empirical demonstration of competitive performance. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods and position their work as a compelling alternative. The inclusion of both theoretical analysis and empirical validation increases confidence in the findings. The algorithmic details are correctly presented, and the method appears well-motivated. Scaling to large datasets and automated feature selection are both important practical advantages.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the methodology sections are generally clear, the experimental details are insufficiently described. This makes it difficult to fully assess the validity of the comparisons. Specifically, the parameter settings for baseline methods are not always clearly justified. The authors acknowledge this in their response to initial feedback, and addressing this is crucial.

A significant concern relates to the experimental evaluation. The MEIBP baseline was used inappropriately, as it requires non-negative weights, invalidating some comparisons. This has been noted by the authors and must be rectified. Furthermore, the comparison with IBP Gibbs sampling lacks fairness – providing the known noise value to IBP would level the playing field. The VB-IBP experiments lack sufficient detail (e.g., number of restarts). The claim of ~70 features found by accelerated Gibbs on the Sonar dataset is suspicious and requires further investigation. Several recent related works on efficient inference for LFMs are missing from the discussion, including MAD-Bayes (Broderick et al., ICML 2013) and others focusing on small-variance asymptotics. An incorrect description of reference [21] as Gibbs sampling (it's MAP inference) requires correction. 

The method’s limitations are not thoroughly discussed. The paper acknowledges the restriction to binary latent feature models, but doesn't explore the implications of this limitation or potential avenues for extending the method to other data types. The potential for convergence difficulties with the EM-like procedure is also not addressed.  The behavior of the method in small-data regimes, where asymptotic arguments may not hold, is unexplored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of FAB inference to a new model class (LFMs).
*   Sound theoretical foundation, with a clear demonstration of the FIC equivalence.
*   Competitive performance on a range of datasets, particularly in terms of runtime.
*   Practical improvements through the shrinkage acceleration method.
*   Addresses an important problem – efficient inference and model selection in LFMs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently detailed experimental setup and parameter justification.
*   Flawed experimental comparisons (e.g., inappropriate use of MEIBP).
*   Limited discussion of algorithmic limitations and potential failure modes.
*   Missing related work and incorrect reference descriptions.
*   Lack of analysis of small-data performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent feature models. The technical soundness of the approach and the promising empirical results warrant consideration for acceptance. However, the weaknesses related to experimental methodology and the lack of thorough discussion regarding limitations must be addressed.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend a **conditional acceptance**. The authors must address the concerns regarding experimental details, baseline comparisons, related work, and limitations before the paper can be considered acceptable for publication in NIPS. Specifically, the authors *must* fix the issues with the MEIBP baseline, clarify parameter settings for all methods, include a discussion of MAD-Bayes and other relevant recent work, correct the description of reference [21], and provide a more comprehensive discussion of the method’s limitations and potential failure modes. Improved justification and further analysis of the results are essential.
