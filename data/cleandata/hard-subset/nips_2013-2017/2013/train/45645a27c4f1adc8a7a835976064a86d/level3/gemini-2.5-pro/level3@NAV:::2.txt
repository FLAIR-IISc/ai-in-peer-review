This paper presents a method for extending Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the associated Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) to latent feature models (LFMs). This builds directly upon prior work that applied FAB/FIC to models like mixture models, which satisfy a specific block-diagonal Hessian condition. The authors' key theoretical contribution is showing that an FIC of a similar form can be derived for LFMs, even though they do not meet this condition. The proposed framework integrates this criterion with a mean-field variational approximation and a novel shrinkage acceleration technique, yielding an efficient algorithm for model selection in LFMs, a problem typically tackled by non-parametric Bayesian approaches such as the Indian Buffet Process.

The paper has several significant strengths. The work represents a logical, solid extension of the FAB/FIC line of research, combining several known approximations into a novel and effective approach. Its primary significance lies in delivering a more automatic model selection procedure that requires little hand-tuning, which is a valuable contribution for practitioners. The paper is very clearly written, well-structured, and the claims are supported by a thorough empirical evaluation. The authors demonstrate compelling computational efficiency gains and strong model selection performance against a wide range of relevant competing methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The major weakness of the paper is the lack of a critical discussion regarding the method's limitations. The proposed approach is built on a cascade of approximations—the asymptotic FIC, the mean-field factorization of the posterior, and the shrinkage acceleration heuristic. While empirically successful, it is unclear under what conditions this combination of approximations might fail or perform poorly. For example, when is the mean-field assumption too restrictive for this model class? How does the accuracy of the FIC approximation degrade in small-N scenarios? A dedicated section or discussion examining these potential failure modes and the limits of the underlying assumptions would significantly strengthen the paper and provide valuable guidance to users of the method.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, practical, and well-validated method. The strengths in its originality, empirical performance, and clarity are substantial. While the lack of discussion on the method's limitations is a notable concern, it does not invalidate the core contribution and can be addressed in a final version.
