This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a limitation of previous FAB work that required a specific block-diagonal structure in the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite not initially satisfying this condition, exhibit a similar form in their Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), enabling the application of FAB. The resulting FAB/LFMs offer automatic hidden state selection, parameter identifiability, and empirically outperform state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Processes (IBPs) in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.

The work builds upon previous FAB frameworks for mixture models [5, 6] and extends them to a more general class of models. The central theoretical contribution lies in proving that the FIC for LFMs has the same form as that for MMs (Lemma 1, Theorem 2), allowing the adaptation of the existing FAB algorithm.  The authors further analyze the algorithm, relating the E-step to a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and leveraging this connection to propose a shrinkage acceleration method, and demonstrate parameter identifiability leading to a merging post-processing step.  The paper is well-motivated, acknowledging the computational challenges of existing methods for LFM model selection [10, 3, 16] and positioning FAB as a potentially more scalable alternative.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension:** The extension of FAB inference to LFMs represents a valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of this promising Bayesian approximation method.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the demonstration of its similarity to the FIC for MMs provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the competitive performance of FAB/LFMs in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency. The comparison to established IBP methods is particularly insightful.
*   **Algorithm Refinements:** The introduction of the shrinkage acceleration method and the merge post-processing step showcase a thoughtful attempt to optimize and refine the FAB algorithm for LFMs.
*   **Identifiability Analysis**: The discussion of parameter identifiability and its implications for merging latent features is a welcome addition, addressing a crucial theoretical aspect often overlooked in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Imprecise Mathematical Exposition:** Certain aspects of the mathematical derivation require further clarification.  For instance, the log-sum term in equation (3) could potentially be infinite, and the precise asymptotic regime and assumptions underlying Theorem 2 are not fully specified. This lack of rigor undermines the confidence in the theoretical justification.
*   **Scaling Issues with K:** The reliance on a BIC-based approximation appears to imply a fixed, finite dimensionality *K*, potentially conflicting with the inherent infinite dimensionality of IBP models, which is a primary motivation for using non-parametric Bayesian methods.  The authors need to clarify how *K* scales with *N* to justify this approximation.
*   **Minor Formal Errors:** Several minor typographical or notational errors detract from the paper’s polish (e.g., condition on data assigned to component *k* in *pk(X | z·,k)*, missing expectation in equation (4)).
*   **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details, such as specific stopping criteria used for the algorithms. The arbitrary truncation of Gibbs sampler runtime makes runtime comparisons less reliable.
*   **Ambiguous Performance Gains**: It remains unclear whether the observed predictive improvements stem from the model-selection criterion itself or from the FAB algorithm’s ability to avoid worse local optima compared to the IBP methods. The tendency of Gibbs sampling to overfit (learn too many components) suggests potential implementation or local-optima issues with those methods.
*   **Limited comparison with VB:** While IBP methods are extensively compared, a more detailed comparison with modern VB approaches, beyond acknowledging their high computational cost, would strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem – scalable model selection for LFMs – and proposes a novel solution based on a solid theoretical foundation.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the competitive performance of FAB/LFMs compared to state-of-the-art methods.
*   The algorithm refinements (shrinkage acceleration, merge post-processing) show a commitment to improving the practical utility of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical exposition lacks the necessary rigor and clarity.
*   The scalability limitations associated with the BIC-based approximation need to be addressed.
*   The experimental analysis lacks sufficient detail to fully interpret the reported gains.
*   The connection to, and differentiation from, existing VB methods could be further strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a sensible and well-motivated method for LFM model selection, building on prior FAB work in a natural and incremental way. While the theoretical contributions are somewhat limited by imprecise mathematical formulations and potential scaling issues, the empirical results are promising. However, the lack of detailed experimental analysis and the potential ambiguity in the source of the performance gains warrant caution. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the mathematical imprecisions, clarify the scaling behavior of *K* with *N*, provide more detailed experimental details (including stopping criteria), and explore a more thorough comparison with VB methods. Addressing these concerns will significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and increase confidence in its findings.
