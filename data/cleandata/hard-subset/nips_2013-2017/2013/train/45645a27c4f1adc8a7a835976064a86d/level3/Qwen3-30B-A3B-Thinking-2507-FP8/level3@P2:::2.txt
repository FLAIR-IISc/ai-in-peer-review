

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant gap in the FAB framework. Previous work on FAB inference [5,6] was limited to models with block-diagonal Hessians, such as mixture models (MMs), but could not be applied to LFMs due to their shared parameter structure. The authors make a crucial theoretical contribution by showing that LFMs, despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition, exhibit the same FIC form as MMs (Theorem 2). This insight enables the application of FAB to LFMs, which are widely used in applications like link prediction [16]. The paper builds on previous work in FAB for MMs [5,6] and latent feature models [10,17], making a natural extension to a broader class of models.

**Strengths:**

*Quality*: The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified, with Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 providing a solid foundation for the FIC derivation. The authors carefully address the theoretical challenge of applying FAB to LFMs, and their asymptotic analysis shows that FIC for LFMs has the same form as for MMs despite the different model structures. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple state-of-the-art methods (IBP [10], VB [3], MEIBP [22]) across synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper demonstrates significant computational efficiency gains (Figure 1) and better model selection accuracy (Figures 2, 3, Table 1) without compromising predictive performance.

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the theoretical contributions, the algorithmic details, and the experimental results. The FAB/LFM algorithm is described precisely, and the supplementary materials provide rigorous proofs for the main results. The connection between FAB and CCCP (Section 3.1) is particularly well-explained, offering valuable theoretical insights.

*Originality*: The paper makes a novel contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs, which was previously not possible due to the Hessian condition. The key insight that LFMs have the same FIC form as MMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal condition is theoretically interesting and broadens the applicability of FAB. The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) is a practical refinement that significantly improves computational efficiency.

*Significance*: The work addresses an important practical problem in latent feature modeling: automatic model selection without manual tuning. The paper demonstrates that FAB/LFM achieves better model selection accuracy than state-of-the-art methods while being significantly faster (Table 1). The method's ability to automatically select the number of latent features with minimal hyperparameters is particularly valuable for large-scale applications where manual tuning is impractical.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality*: While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper does not sufficiently examine the failure modes of the combined approximations (FIC, mean-field, shrinkage). The key concern is understanding when and why the approximations may fail, especially in scenarios where the assumptions of Lemma 1 or Theorem 2 might not hold. The authors should provide more analysis of the limits of their approach, particularly for small sample sizes where asymptotic approximations may be less accurate.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-written, but the connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation could be strengthened. For instance, the shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) is described, but the paper could provide more intuition about why multiple CCCP steps accelerate the shrinkage effect.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel extension of FAB inference to LFMs, a significant theoretical contribution
- Rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating FIC form equivalence between LFMs and MMs
- Practical algorithm with substantial computational advantages
- Thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets
- Clear writing and well-structured presentation

*Con:*
- Limited analysis of failure modes and limits of the approximations
- Some claims about the superiority of FAB/LFM could be strengthened with more detailed comparisons (e.g., statistical significance tests)
- The connection between the theoretical insights and practical implementation could be more explicit

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field by extending the FAB framework to LFMs, demonstrating both theoretical novelty and practical utility. The extension is logically sound, well-motivated, and supported by thorough experiments. While the paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of the limitations of the approach, the theoretical insights and empirical results are compelling. The method addresses a real problem in latent feature modeling—automatic model selection with minimal tuning—and provides significant computational advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to the NIPS community.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and the empirical results are convincing. The minor weaknesses in the analysis of failure modes do not detract from the overall quality and contribution of the work. This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical application of latent feature models.
