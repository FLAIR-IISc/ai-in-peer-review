

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant gap in the applicability of FAB to models that do not satisfy the specific Hessian condition required for the factorized information criterion (FIC). The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not meeting the condition required for FIC derivation (as in mixture models), the FIC for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models, enabling the application of FAB inference to LFMs. The key contribution is showing that the asymptotic analysis of LFMs' Hessian matrix leads to the same regularization term as mixture models, allowing for automatic hidden state selection without requiring hyperparameters or priors.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical contribution and practical implications. The authors establish a connection between FAB inference for mixture models and LFMs through a careful asymptotic analysis, which is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The proposed shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2), derived from interpreting the FAB updates as a convex concave procedure (CCCP), significantly reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process. Empirical results demonstrate FAB/LFMs' superiority over existing methods in model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency, particularly for large-scale data. The paper also provides a valuable identifiability analysis (Theorem 4), showing how FAB resolves non-identifiability issues in LFMs through the regularization effect, which is an important theoretical contribution often overlooked in latent variable models.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses that limit its impact. The mathematical exposition contains imprecisions that undermine the theoretical foundation: Equation (3) in the FIC for mixture models omits the expectation over q(Z), and the log-sum term ∑ₙzₙₖ could be problematic if all zₙₖ are zero (raising questions about the validity of the FIC). Theorem 2 lacks specification of the asymptotic regime, particularly how the number of latent features K scales with the sample size N, which is crucial since the IBP framework is inherently designed for infinite K. The authors claim FAB achieves automatic model selection, yet the theoretical justification for this property is unclear without specifying how K behaves as N increases.

The experimental section has several issues that weaken the empirical claims. The stopping criteria for FAB and other methods are unspecified, making runtime comparisons arbitrary. The paper reports faster convergence for FAB but doesn't clarify whether the improvements stem from the model selection criterion or from avoiding poor local optima (a common issue with Gibbs sampling in IBP). The comparison with IBP methods is somewhat misleading since IBP methods typically require careful tuning of the concentration parameter α, which the authors set to 3 (a value that may cause overestimation of K). The paper omits a comparison with the variational EM method for LFMs, which would help isolate whether the improvements are due to the FAB framework or the specific algorithm.

The paper's contribution is novel but incremental, extending prior FAB work on mixture models to LFMs. While the extension is meaningful and practically valuable, it doesn't represent a major theoretical leap. The connection to previous work (Fujimaki et al. 2013, 2012) is well-established, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the asymptotic assumptions and their implications for model selection.

In terms of significance, the approach addresses a real practical problem in latent feature modeling: the computational burden of model selection. The shrinkage acceleration method is particularly valuable for large-scale applications, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages. However, the theoretical justification for the asymptotic approximation requires more careful treatment to fully establish the validity of the method.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, but some technical details (like the asymptotic regime for K) are glossed over. The authors make a reasonable case for the method's value, but the mathematical imprecisions and experimental ambiguities prevent the paper from making a stronger contribution to the field.

Pro: Theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs with practical benefits; effective shrinkage acceleration; strong empirical results on predictive performance and computational efficiency.

Con: Mathematical imprecisions in key equations; lack of specification of asymptotic regime (K vs. N); unclear stopping criteria for experiments; potential confusion between model selection benefits and avoidance of poor local optima.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that extends the FAB framework to a new class of models with practical benefits. While the theoretical underpinnings could be more carefully developed, the empirical results and the proposed acceleration method make this a valuable addition to the literature on latent feature models. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the mathematical imprecisions and providing clearer experimental details.
