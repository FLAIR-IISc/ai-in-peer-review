Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixtures/HMMs to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB optimizes a tractable lower bound of a factorized information criterion (FIC) that asymptotically approximates the marginal log-likelihood. Prior FAB work required a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete log-likelihood (true for mixture models), which does not hold for LFMs due to shared parameters across features. The core theoretical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian that yields Lemma 1: log det|F(d)| produces the same log sum_n z_nk complexity term as in mixtures, despite the lack of block-diagonality. Consequently, the FIC for LFMs takes the same form as FIC for mixtures, leading to an FAB algorithm with automatic feature shrinkage and asymptotic identifiability.

Algorithmically, the authors derive a mean-field FAB lower bound; the E-step fixed-point update includes a D/(2Nπ̃_k) term that shrinks rarely used features, the M-step has closed-form updates (as in PPCA), and a shrinkage step removes features that are always on or off. They reinterpret the E-step as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), enabling multiple inner iterations that accelerate shrinkage. A theorem on parameter identifiability motivates a post-hoc merge step to combine redundant features, improving the bound.

Empirically, FAB/LFM is compared with IBP (fast Gibbs), IBP-VB, and MEIBP on synthetic, block data, and several real datasets. FAB is consistently strong in model selection and predictive log-likelihood; runtime is competitive and often favorable, though MEIBP can be faster in some regimes. The paper is clearly written and connects to relevant work: FAB for mixtures/HMMs; IBP and its variants; Watanabe’s theory for singular models; and related asymptotics (MAD-Bayes).

Quality
- Theory: The asymptotic derivation (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is the main contribution and appears sound, with proofs in the supplement. The resulting FIC matches mixtures’ FIC, which is both nontrivial and useful. The lower bound optimization is carefully constructed, with monotonic improvement for the stated updates. The CCCP interpretation is apt; multiple inner steps are a reasonable acceleration. One caveat: the simultaneous update of π and π̃ during acceleration could use a brief convergence argument (the text asserts empirical improvements).
- Empirics: Comparisons are broad and fair overall; synthetic and real datasets cover a range of N and D. It is helpful that they discuss small-N regimes where FIC may be less accurate. A minor concern is fairness in noise-variance treatment: FAB learns Λ, while some baselines fix the noise scale; a sensitivity analysis or allowing baselines to tune/learn noise would strengthen claims.
- Completeness/honesty: The paper discusses assumptions, small-N limitations, and cases where MEIBP runs faster; this is appreciated.

Clarity
The paper is well organized. The core ideas, derivations, and algorithmic steps are clear. Suggestions to improve readability:
- State all assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text (even briefly), and clarify the Op(1) terms’ dependence.
- Provide computational complexity per iteration and memory in terms of N, D, K, and Tshrink.
- Offer guidance on choosing Tshrink and δ, and clarify how merge decisions are made (thresholds and effect on L).
- Explicitly state that the E-step with multiple CCCP updates maintains monotonicity of the lower bound under the proposed schedule.

Originality
Showing that the FIC for LFMs has the same form as for mixtures, despite the lack of block-diagonal Hessian, is novel and insightful. The CCCP view is not entirely new as a technique but is well-leveraged here to accelerate FAB’s intrinsic shrinkage. The identifiability analysis and merge post-processing are thoughtful, albeit incremental relative to the main asymptotic result.

Significance
The work advances automated model selection for LFMs with a theoretically grounded, efficient method that competes with or improves upon state-of-the-art IBP approaches in accuracy and often in runtime. The broader implication—that FIC-style approximations extend beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians—should interest the community and may inspire extensions to other latent variable models. The current scope is linear-Gaussian LFMs with Bernoulli features; generalization to non-Gaussian likelihoods or structured priors would further increase impact.

Pros
- Strong theoretical result: FIC for LFMs matches mixtures’ form via novel Hessian asymptotics.
- Practical, monotone algorithm with E/M updates, shrinkage, and CCCP acceleration.
- Automatic feature selection and improved identifiability; principled merge step.
- Solid empirical performance in model selection and predictive accuracy; competitive efficiency.
- Well written, with useful connections to prior work.

Cons
- Assumptions for Lemma 1 and convergence aspects of accelerated steps are mostly in the supplement; more detail in the main text would help.
- Baseline configuration: fixed noise variance for some methods may disadvantage them; include sensitivity or enable noise learning/tuning.
- Limited exploration beyond linear-Gaussian settings; unclear how readily the approach generalizes.
- Small-N behavior is acknowledged but not deeply characterized; additional diagnostics would be useful.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please summarize the key assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text and discuss their practical plausibility.
- Provide per-iteration and total complexity, and report wall-clock time versus K during shrinkage to quantify acceleration benefits.
- Offer sensitivity analyses for Tshrink, δ, and initialization of K; and compare with ARD/Bayesian PCA baselines for dimension selection.
- Allow baselines to adjust or learn noise variance (or grid-search it) to ensure fairness.
- Clarify the conditions under which the merge step is guaranteed to increase the bound and what tolerance is used for detecting near-duplicate features.

Overall recommendation: Accept
