This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a recently developed Bayesian approximation method for model selection, to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB inference operates by maximizing a computationally tractable lower bound of a “factorized information criterion” (FIC), which converges to a marginal log-likelihood in the large sample limit. Previous work demonstrated FAB’s efficacy in mixture models and hidden Markov models, achieving competitive performance with non-parametric Bayesian and variational Bayesian methods at a lower computational cost. However, a key limitation of FAB was its applicability only to models satisfying a specific condition on the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood—namely, that it must be block diagonal. This paper addresses this limitation, extending the FAB framework to LFMs, which do *not* inherently satisfy this condition. 

The authors’ primary contribution is demonstrating that, despite the violation of the Hessian condition, FICs for LFMs retain the same form as those for mixture models. This is established through careful asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix.  Consequently, FAB/LFMs inherit desirable properties from FAB/MMs, including automatic hidden state selection, parameter identifiability, and monotonic convergence of the lower FIC bound during optimization. Furthermore, the authors present refinements to the FAB algorithm tailored for LFMs, including an analysis relating FAB E-steps to a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), leading to a “shrinkage acceleration” method for reducing computational costs, and a demonstration of parameter identifiability, guiding a post-processing step for merging latent features. The method is evaluated empirically on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technically Sound:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of the Hessian matrix, convincingly demonstrating the applicability of FAB to LFMs despite the absence of the block-diagonal condition. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is clearly explained and well-motivated.
*   **Novel Extension:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs is a valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of this promising Bayesian approximation technique.
*   **Practical Improvements:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method based on the CCCP analysis is a practical refinement that demonstrably improves computational efficiency. The identifiability analysis and resulting merge post-processing step are insightful additions.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of FAB/LFMs in terms of model selection, prediction accuracy, and computational speed. The comparison against established methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) is thorough and supports the authors’ claims.
*   **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a logical manner. 

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the extension of FAB to LFMs is a valuable contribution, it could be argued that it is somewhat incremental. The core idea of extending an existing method to a new domain, while technically sound, might not represent a radical departure from existing research.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While FAB/LFMs are reported to be computationally efficient, the experimental section's table indicates that some methods (particularly MEIBP) were unable to complete within the allowed time limit on larger datasets. A more comprehensive analysis of scalability and computational complexity across a wider range of dataset sizes would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions sensitivity to parameter settings for some of the compared methods, and states default values were used. More detailed discussion on parameter tuning (or lack thereof for FAB) and its impact on performance would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Limited Novelty in Shrinkage:** While the CCCP interpretation and acceleration are useful, shrinkage techniques are not entirely novel in the LFM literature. Further elaboration on how this approach differs from or improves upon existing shrinkage methods would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in Bayesian machine learning—scalable model selection for latent feature models.
*   The technical contribution is solid and well-supported by theoretical analysis and empirical evidence.
*   The authors clearly demonstrate the advantages of FAB/LFMs over existing methods in terms of both performance and efficiency.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to understand, making it accessible to a broad audience within the NIPS community.
*   The practical improvements (shrinkage acceleration, identifiability-guided merging) enhance the usability of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution, while valuable, is somewhat incremental and may not represent a significant breakthrough.
*   The computational limitations observed with some competing methods raise questions about the scalability of FAB/LFMs to very large datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian machine learning. While the contribution is somewhat incremental, the technical soundness, practical improvements, and empirical validation are compelling. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it a pleasure to read. I believe that this paper is a good fit for NIPS and recommend acceptance. The strengths of the work outweigh the relatively minor weaknesses. I recommend that the authors consider addressing the scalability concerns and providing a more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity in a future revision, if accepted.
