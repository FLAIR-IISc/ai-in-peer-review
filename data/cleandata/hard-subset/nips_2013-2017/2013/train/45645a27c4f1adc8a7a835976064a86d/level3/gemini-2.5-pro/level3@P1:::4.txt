This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a model selection framework that maximizes a lower bound on the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which asymptotically approximates the marginal log-likelihood. Prior work on FAB, such as for Mixture Models and HMMs, relied on a crucial assumption: that the Hessian of the complete data log-likelihood is block-diagonal. This assumption does not hold for LFMs due to the sharing of parameters (the basis matrix W) across latent features. The central contribution of this work is to overcome this limitation. Through an asymptotic analysis of the LFM Hessian, the authors show (Lemma 1) that the resulting penalty term in the FIC has the same functional form as that for Mixture Models, namely a sum of `log(sum_n z_nk)` terms. This key insight allows them to derive a valid FIC and a corresponding FAB algorithm for LFMs. The proposed algorithm features desirable properties like automatic feature selection and includes practical refinements such as a shrinkage acceleration step (inspired by a CCCP interpretation) and a post-processing merge step to handle identifiability issues. The work is positioned as a computationally efficient and accurate alternative to non-parametric Bayesian approaches for LFMs, such as those based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivations appear to be sound, with the asymptotic analysis in Lemma 1 forming the core technical contribution that enables the entire framework. The subsequent derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the corresponding lower bound for optimization is clear and well-justified. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical arguments (e.g., convergence of FIC to marginal log-likelihood, identifiability analysis) and a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors are careful in their evaluation, comparing against relevant and state-of-the-art baselines (IBP Gibbs sampling, VB-IBP, MEIBP) on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The results compellingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed FAB/LFM method, particularly in its ability to achieve strong predictive performance and accurate model selection with significantly lower computational cost than many competitors. The paper presents a complete piece of work, from theory to an efficient algorithm and thorough empirical validation.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is stated clearly in the introduction, highlighting the limitations of the existing FAB framework and the challenges posed by LFMs. The theoretical development is presented in a logical sequence, making it easy for the reader to follow how the key challenge of the non-block-diagonal Hessian is addressed. The description of the FAB/LFM algorithm is detailed, and the connection drawn to CCCP for the shrinkage acceleration step provides both theoretical insight and a practical justification for the refinement. The experimental setup is described clearly, and the results are presented in an interpretable manner through tables and figures. The paper provides sufficient detail to understand the contributions and potentially reproduce the work.

**Originality**

The work is original and makes a significant contribution. While it builds upon the existing FAB framework of Fujimaki et al., the extension to LFMs is non-trivial and required a novel theoretical insight. The main originality lies in the asymptotic analysis of the Hessian for LFMs, which demonstrates that the resulting FIC structure is unexpectedly similar to that of simpler models. This finding itself is interesting and could potentially generalize to other models with shared latent structures. The proposed shrinkage acceleration technique, derived from the CCCP perspective, is also a nice, original refinement that enhances the practical utility of the algorithm. The paper correctly and adequately cites related work in both FAB inference and LFM literature, clearly distinguishing its contributions from prior art like IBP and variational methods.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community. LFMs are a fundamental class of models, and inference and model selection for them remain an active area of research. This paper introduces a new algorithm, FAB/LFM, that presents a very compelling alternative to the dominant non-parametric Bayesian (IBP) approaches. The experimental results show that FAB/LFM can achieve state-of-the-art or superior predictive performance and model selection accuracy, often at a fraction of the computational cost of methods like VB-IBP or Gibbs sampling. This combination of accuracy and efficiency makes the proposed method highly valuable for practitioners and researchers working with large-scale data. Furthermore, the theoretical finding that the FIC can be applied to models that do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition may inspire further research into extending the FAB framework to an even wider class of latent variable models.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Technical Contribution:** The paper provides a non-trivial theoretical extension of the FAB framework to LFMs by overcoming the restrictive block-diagonal Hessian assumption. The analysis in Lemma 1 is the key original insight.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed FAB/LFM method is shown to be highly competitive, often outperforming state-of-the-art IBP-based methods in terms of predictive accuracy, model selection, and computational efficiency on a variety of datasets.
*   **Practical Significance:** The method offers a fast, scalable, and fully automated tool for learning with LFMs, which is a significant contribution for a widely used model class. The shrinkage acceleration further improves its practical appeal.
*   **High Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making the complex derivations and algorithmic details easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The contribution, while novel and technically sound, is an extension of an existing framework (FAB) rather than the introduction of a completely new paradigm. This is a very minor point, however, as the extension itself is significant and required substantial new work.
*   The key assumptions underlying the asymptotic analysis in Lemma 1 are detailed in the supplementary materials. While this is standard practice, it means the validity of the core results in the main paper rests on these details.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated contribution that advances the state of the art in inference for latent feature models. I recommend acceptance.
