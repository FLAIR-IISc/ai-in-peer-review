This paper proposes an extension of the factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to latent feature models (LFMs). The authors derive a lower bound of the factorized information criterion (FIC) that converges to the marginal log-likelihood, despite the fact that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by previous FAB frameworks. The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is shown to have desirable properties such as automatic hidden state selection, parameter identifiability, and a monotonic increase in the lower FIC bound through iterative optimization.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough derivation of the FIC for LFMs. The empirical evaluation on synthetic and real datasets demonstrates the superiority of FAB/LFM in terms of predictive performance and computational cost compared to other state-of-the-art methods such as fast Gibbs sampling for the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), variational Bayes (VB), and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP).

The contribution of this paper is somewhat incremental, but it is interesting and relevant to the NIPS community. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the FAB framework and its extension to LFMs. The paper is well-organized, and the authors adequately inform the reader about the theoretical and empirical aspects of the proposed method.

The quality of the paper is high, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is a complete piece of work, and the authors provide a clear conclusion and future directions.

The originality of the paper is moderate, as it builds upon previous work on FAB inference and LFMs. However, the authors provide a novel combination of techniques and a new perspective on the application of FAB to LFMs. The related work is adequately referenced, and the authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm has the potential to be widely applicable in various fields, and the authors provide a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach to the problem of latent feature modeling.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper is well-written and provides a thorough derivation of the FIC for LFMs.
* The empirical evaluation demonstrates the superiority of FAB/LFM in terms of predictive performance and computational cost.
* The contribution is interesting and relevant to the NIPS community.
* The paper is well-organized, and the authors adequately inform the reader about the theoretical and empirical aspects of the proposed method.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The contribution is somewhat incremental, building upon previous work on FAB inference and LFMs.
* The paper may not be of interest to readers who are not familiar with FAB inference or LFMs.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper for publication in NIPS. The authors provide a high-quality contribution that is relevant to the NIPS community, and the paper is well-written and well-organized.
