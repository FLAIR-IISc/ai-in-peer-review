This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM models to latent feature models (LFMs). The key challenge addressed is that prior FAB derivations relied on a block-diagonal structure of the complete-data Hessian, which LFMs violate due to parameter sharing across features. The authors provide an asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) showing that for LFMs, the determinant of the relevant Hessian decomposes to yield the same log sum znk term as in mixtures, leading to an FIC with the same representation and asymptotically matching the marginal log-likelihood. Building on this, they propose an FAB algorithm with a mean-field E-step, interpret the updates as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), introduce a shrinkage-acceleration routine, and prove an identifiability result that justifies a practical merge post-processing step. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets compare to fast Gibbs IBP, VB, and MEIBP, showing superior predictive performance and competitive or lower computational cost for FAB/LFM.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound, with a clear lower-bound construction and monotonic improvement guarantees. The central asymptotic result is convincing, though important assumptions are deferred to the supplement; surfacing the “mild” conditions and a finite-sample discussion in the main text would strengthen the work. The empirical evaluation is broad and uses standard baselines. However, fixing the noise variance for competing methods but learning it for FAB may bias results; a matched setting or sensitivity analysis would improve fairness. Runtime scaling with N and D could be characterized more systematically.

Clarity: The paper is well written and well organized, with derivation steps laid out cleanly and algorithms clearly specified. A brief summary of the key assumptions behind Lemma 1 in the main text would aid readability.

Originality: The core novelty is extending FAB beyond the block-diagonal Hessian regime and proving that the LFM FIC matches the mixture-model form. The CCCP interpretation, shrinkage acceleration, and identifiability-based merging are useful refinements. The contribution is somewhat incremental relative to prior FAB work, but still meaningful.

Significance: This advance makes FAB applicable to a widely used class of models, offers automated model selection without priors, and shows practical gains over IBP/VB/MEIBP. It is relevant to the NeurIPS community.

Pros:
- Solid asymptotic derivation and practical algorithm
- Automatic feature selection, identifiability, and acceleration
- Strong predictive performance and efficiency

Cons:
- Assumptions relegated to supplement
- Potential evaluation bias from unequal noise treatment
- Limited systematic scaling analysis

Overall, I support acceptance.
