Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB is built around the factorized information criterion (FIC), an asymptotically accurate approximation to the marginal log-likelihood obtained via Laplace analysis and factorization. Earlier FAB derivations for mixtures/HMMs relied on a block-diagonal structure of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood, a condition violated by LFMs because parameters are shared across features. The authors show that, despite this, an asymptotic analysis of the per-dimension Hessian in LFMs yields the same crucial log sum_n z_nk penalty as in mixtures: log det F(d) ≈ sum_k log(sum_n z_nk) + O_p(1). This leads to an FIC for LFMs with the same representation as for mixture models, supporting automatic feature shrinkage and asymptotic equivalence to the marginal log-likelihood.

They then derive a practical FAB algorithm for LFMs: a tractable lower bound to FIC using a mean-field posterior over Z, linearization of the logarithmic penalty, and alternating E/M updates. The E-step admits a CCCP interpretation, inspiring a shrinkage-acceleration scheme that helps prune irrelevant features early. The paper further analyzes parameter identifiability, showing FAB’s regularization prefers balanced/merged representations when features collapse, and provides a merging post-processing step. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets compare FAB/LFM against fast Gibbs sampling for IBP, variational Bayes, and MEIBP, showing strong predictive performance with lower computational cost.

This work connects to prior FAB work for mixtures/HMMs (Fujimaki et al.), nonparametric LFMs with IBP and VB (Griffiths & Ghahramani; Doshi-Velez et al.), submodular IBP (MEIBP; Reed & Ghahramani), and asymptotics for singular models (Watanabe), as well as related “MAD-Bayes” asymptotic MAP derivations.

Quality
- Theory: The key technical contribution is Lemma 1 showing the determinant of the per-dimension Hessian contributes the same feature-usage penalty as in mixtures, enabling an FIC with the same form. The resulting Theorem 2 and the corollary give the asymptotic connection to the marginal log-likelihood. The derivations are clear in the main text, with assumptions relegated to the supplement; it would help to summarize the “mild assumptions” more concretely (e.g., bounds on Λ and W, moments of X, conditions preventing degenerate features).
- Algorithm: The FAB lower bound is carefully constructed (linearization of log sum, mean-field q(Z), joint optimization in q, parameters, and linearization variables). The CCCP view is insightful and motivates a principled acceleration. The identifiability analysis and merge step are thoughtful and practically relevant.
- Empirics: Comparisons include strong IBP baselines (fast Gibbs, VB) and MEIBP. FAB/LFM attains competitive or superior predictive log-likelihoods with reduced runtime, especially due to automatic shrinkage and the acceleration. The block data example illustrates feature recovery and pruning. Real-data studies show plausible advantages in PLL and more compact K. The paper honestly notes cases where FAB is less accurate for small N.
- Caveats: The asymptotic guarantees hinge on large N; finite-sample behavior is guided by the lower bound and may depend on initialization. The mean-field assumption over Z may under-estimate posterior dependencies. Some experimental choices potentially favor FAB (e.g., fixing noise variance for IBP/VB/MEIBP while FAB learns it; MEIBP constraints to non-negative data). Reporting sensitivity (e.g., multiple random initializations, effect of Tshrink, tolerance δ) would strengthen claims of robustness.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. The narrative from limitations of prior FAB to the new LFM FIC, algorithmic details, CCCP acceleration, and identifiability is easy to follow. Derivation steps are explicit, and notation is consistent. A brief summary of the assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text would further help readers.

Originality
While FAB and FIC are known for mixtures/HMMs, extending them to LFMs is nontrivial because LFMs violate the earlier Hessian factorization conditions. The central insight that the same log(sum_n z_nk) penalty arises for LFMs is original and useful, as is the CCCP-based acceleration and the identifiability/merging analysis. The contribution is somewhat incremental relative to the FAB line of work but novel within LFMs.

Significance
Automated, prior-insensitive model selection for LFMs with provable asymptotics and practical efficiency is valuable. Practitioners who currently rely on IBP variants or VB may benefit from a scalable alternative with competitive predictive performance and automatic feature pruning. The result also broadens the applicability of FIC beyond mixtures/HMMs, hinting at use in other latent variable models.

Strengths
- Solid asymptotic analysis yielding an LFM FIC identical in form to mixtures, despite violated Hessian conditions.
- Practical FAB algorithm with monotonic lower-bound ascent, automatic feature shrinkage, and CCCP acceleration.
- Identifiability analysis and merging heuristic address a real pain point in LFMs.
- Empirical evidence of better PLL and lower runtime than strong IBP-based baselines on several datasets.
- Clear exposition and thorough derivations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumptions behind Lemma 1: summarize them in the main paper; discuss edge cases (e.g., near-collinear features, extreme πk).
- Finite-sample accuracy: discuss the impact of O_p(1) constants; provide additional small-N stress tests.
- Mean-field q(Z): comment on potential loss from ignoring dependencies; any evidence that structured variational forms do/don’t help?
- Comparisons: ensure fairness by allowing baselines to estimate noise variance; report sensitivity to hyperparameters and initialization (e.g., multiple seeds, variability in K and PLL).
- Complexity: provide per-iteration time complexity with and without acceleration; discuss memory costs for large N, D, K.
- Generality: clarify to what extent the analysis extends beyond Gaussian likelihoods (e.g., Poisson/Bernoulli LFMs).

Arguments for acceptance
- Technically sound extension of a principled asymptotic criterion to an important model family.
- Same FIC form as mixtures is an elegant and generalizable result.
- Algorithmic innovations (CCCP acceleration, merging) make the approach practical and competitive.
- Strong empirical performance with reduced computation and compact models.
- Well written and relevant to the NIPS community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Asymptotic nature; finite-N behavior depends on approximations and initialization.
- Somewhat incremental within the FAB program.
- Experimental setup could be more balanced regarding noise variance and MEIBP settings; additional robustness analyses would help.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper provides a careful and practically useful extension of FAB to LFMs, with solid theory, a clear algorithm, and convincing empirical results. The contribution, while incremental, is meaningful and likely to influence subsequent work on scalable Bayesian model selection for latent feature models.
