

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models to latent feature models (LFMs), overcoming a key limitation that previously restricted FAB to models with block-diagonal Hessian matrices of the complete log-likelihood. The authors prove (Lemma 1) that LFMs, despite violating this condition, yield a factorized information criterion (FIC) with identical form to MMs, enabling FAB/LFM. This is achieved through asymptotic analysis showing the log-determinant of the Hessian relates to ∑ₙ zₙₖ, inducing automatic feature selection and identifiability. The FAB/LFM algorithm incorporates a shrinkage acceleration via CCCP interpretation (Section 3) and merge post-processing (Theorem 4), empirically outperforming IBP, variational Bayes (VB), and MEIBP in model selection, prediction, and efficiency.

**Quality**: The theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified. Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 rigorously establish the FIC form for LFMs, resolving a critical gap in FAB applicability. The derivation of the FAB/LFM algorithm (Section 2.2) is technically sound, with clear monotonic convergence guarantees. Experiments (Section 4) comprehensively evaluate computational speed, model selection accuracy (K), and predictive performance (PLL/TLL) across synthetic and real datasets. Results consistently demonstrate FAB/LFM’s superiority: it achieves higher PLL than MEIBP and VB while requiring significantly less computation (e.g., 50x faster than MEIBP on USPS), selects more compact models (smaller K), and mitigates overfitting (smaller PLL-TLL gap). Minor limitations include slightly poorer performance on small N (Libras) where FIC approximation weakens, and omission of VB due to excessive runtime—both acknowledged by the authors.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. Key insights (e.g., the Hessian analysis in Lemma 1, CCCP interpretation in Section 3) are presented logically with precise notation. Supplementary materials provide rigorous proofs for all main results, enabling reproducibility. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1–2) are detailed and implementable. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem by contrasting FAB’s automation advantages against prior NPB/VB methods for LFMs, and the related work section properly cites foundational NIPS papers (e.g., Griffiths & Ghahramani 2006 for IBP, Fujimaki 2012 for FAB/MMs).

**Originality**: While FAB inference itself is not new (Fujimaki et al., NIPS 2012), extending it to LFMs represents a meaningful theoretical advance. The core novelty lies in proving FIC’s form for LFMs *without* the block-diagonal Hessian condition—a non-trivial extension beyond prior work. The paper clearly differentiates from related approaches: MAD-Bayes (Broderick et al., ICML 2013) relies on noise-variance limits, while FAB/LFM leverages asymptotic Bayesian marginalization. The shrinkage acceleration via CCCP (Section 3) and identifiability-based merge post-processing (Theorem 4) are novel refinements that enhance practical utility. Though incremental in scope, the contribution is significant for advancing scalable Bayesian model selection in latent variable models.

**Significance**: FAB/LFM addresses a critical bottleneck in LFMs: high computational cost of state-of-the-art NPB methods (IBP, VB) for large-scale data. By enabling automatic feature selection without hyperparameters or priors, it offers a practical, efficient alternative for real-world applications (e.g., link prediction, dimensionality reduction). The empirical results demonstrate tangible benefits: better predictive accuracy (PLL), reduced model complexity (smaller K), and orders-of-magnitude speedups (e.g., 50 hours → 1 hour on USPS). This advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference, directly benefiting practitioners. The identifiability guarantee (Theorem 4) also resolves a key theoretical issue in LFMs, enhancing reliability.

**Weaknesses**: The experimental comparison omits VB for large datasets (due to runtime), which slightly weakens the computational efficiency claim. While the small-N limitation (Libras) is noted, a deeper analysis of FIC approximation error bounds would strengthen the theoretical foundation. The paper does not discuss sensitivity to initialization, though the random initialization and convergence guarantees mitigate this.

**Pro/Con Summary**:  
*Pro*: Rigorous extension of FAB to LFMs; novel theoretical insights (Lemma 1, Theorem 4); superior empirical performance (accuracy, speed, compactness); clear exposition; practical relevance to scalable Bayesian learning.  
*Con*: Incremental in methodology (builds on FAB); limited analysis of small-N regime; omission of VB in large-scale experiments.

**Recommendation**: This paper makes a strong, well-executed contribution to the field. It resolves a key theoretical gap in FAB inference while delivering significant practical improvements over existing methods. The quality of theory, clarity of presentation, and empirical validation meet NIPS standards. The significance for scalable Bayesian model selection is clear, with immediate applicability to real-world data analysis. Accept.
