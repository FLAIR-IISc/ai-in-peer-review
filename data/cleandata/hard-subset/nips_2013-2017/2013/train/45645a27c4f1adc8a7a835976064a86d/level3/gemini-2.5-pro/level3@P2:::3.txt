### **Summary**

This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a model selection framework that provides a computationally tractable criterion, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which approximates the marginal log-likelihood in the large-sample limit. Previously, FAB was limited to models like Gaussian mixtures or HMMs, where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. The key theoretical contribution here is to show that, despite LFMs not satisfying this condition, their FIC takes a similar form, enabling the development of a FAB algorithm. The resulting algorithm is an EM-like procedure that iteratively updates model parameters and the posterior over the binary latent features. A crucial feature is a "model-induced regularization" term that automatically shrinks the number of active latent features, thus performing model selection without relying on priors like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). The authors also introduce a shrinkage acceleration method based on a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation. Experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets suggest the proposed FAB/LFM method can outperform several state-of-the-art IBP variants (Gibbs sampling, variational Bayes, and MAP estimation) in terms of computational efficiency and predictive performance, particularly on larger datasets.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper's technical approach appears sound. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is the core theoretical contribution and seems correct, extending the FAB framework to a new and important class of models. The resulting EM-like algorithm is well-described, and the connection to CCCP for accelerating shrinkage is a clever and practical refinement. The claims of automatic model selection and scalability are supported by the algorithm's structure.

However, the quality of the experimental evaluation is a major weakness. Several issues cast doubt on the reported comparisons and conclusions:
1.  The MEIBP baseline is designed for non-negative data, yet it was applied in experiments (e.g., artificial simulations, block data) where this assumption may not hold. This makes the comparison invalid.
2.  In the block data experiment, the ground-truth noise variance was known. For a fair comparison, the IBP Gibbs sampler should have been provided with this value rather than a heuristic one (0.75).
3.  The VB-IBP baseline used a finite truncation, but details like the number of restarts or the truncation level are missing. It is also unclear why an infinite variational version was not used for a more direct comparison.
4.  Some reported results are suspicious. For instance, the accelerated Gibbs IBP method finding ~70 features on the 49-dimensional Sonar dataset seems highly improbable and likely points to a problem with initialization or incorrect noise variance settings in the baseline implementation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is reasonably well-written and organized. The methodology in Sections 2 and 3 is presented clearly, guiding the reader from the theoretical derivation of the FIC to the final algorithm. The notation is consistent.

However, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. As noted above, critical parameters and settings for the baseline methods are either omitted or poorly justified. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of its limitations. There is no mention of potential convergence difficulties (e.g., local optima) for the EM-like procedure, nor is there a discussion of the method's behavior in small-data regimes where the asymptotic arguments central to FAB may not hold.

**Originality:**
The primary contribution—applying the FAB framework to LFMs—is novel. Prior work on FAB focused on mixture models and HMMs. This extension is non-trivial and broadens the applicability of this inference paradigm. While the resulting algorithm shares similarities with variational EM for LFMs, the FAB-derived regularization term for model selection is distinct from prior-based regularization and provides a fresh perspective on model selection in these models.

**Significance:**
The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of performing efficient inference in LFMs while simultaneously determining the number of latent features. If the claims hold up, the proposed method could be a valuable, scalable alternative to computationally intensive nonparametric Bayesian methods like IBP-based Gibbs sampling. The method's ability to automatically infer model complexity without hand-tunable hyperparameters is highly desirable. However, the significance of the empirical results is currently diminished by the flaws in the experimental evaluation.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental section requires a major overhaul. Baselines must be used correctly (e.g., MEIBP on appropriate data) and configured for fair comparison (e.g., providing known noise variance to IBP). More details on all experimental settings are needed. The suspicious results should be investigated and explained.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The authors should add a discussion on the limitations of their method. This includes:
    *   The restriction to *binary* latent features, which means it cannot be directly applied to models like probabilistic PCA or factor analysis.
    *   The reliance on asymptotic arguments. An experiment or discussion on performance when N is small would strengthen the paper.
    *   The non-convex nature of the objective function and the potential for local optima.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The related work section should include a discussion of "MAD-Bayes" (Broderick et al., ICML 2013), which also explores asymptotic regimes for nonparametric LFMs, albeit from a small-variance perspective. This would provide valuable context for the paper's contribution.
*   **Minor Corrections:**
    *   The paper states the standard deviation for real data was set to 0.75, citing [2, 22]. My understanding is that a common heuristic from this line of work is to set the noise standard deviation to 0.25 times the standard deviation of the training data. This should be clarified and corrected if necessary.
    *   The description of reference [22] (MEIBP) in the text is correct, but the key points provided to me noted a potential mischaracterization of another reference as Gibbs sampling when it was a MAP method. The authors should double-check their characterizations of all baseline methods to ensure accuracy.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and non-trivial extension of the FAB inference framework to latent feature models.
*   The proposed EM-like algorithm with automatic model selection is computationally efficient and scalable.
*   Addresses an important problem in machine learning: inferring model complexity in LFMs.
*   The CCCP-based acceleration method is a nice algorithmic contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation contains significant flaws that undermine the claims of empirical superiority over state-of-the-art baselines.
*   The paper lacks a crucial discussion of its own limitations, particularly concerning the asymptotic assumptions and the restriction to binary features.
*   Important and highly relevant related work (e.g., MAD-Bayes) is not discussed.

In its current form, the paper's promising theoretical ideas are not backed by a sufficiently rigorous empirical evaluation. A thorough revision addressing the experimental flaws and expanding the discussion of limitations is necessary for this work to be acceptable.
