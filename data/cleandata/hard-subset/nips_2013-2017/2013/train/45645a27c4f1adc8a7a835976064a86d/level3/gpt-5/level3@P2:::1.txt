Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously developed for mixture and HMM-like models, to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian likelihoods. The central technical step is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood. Lemma 1 asserts that log det of the per-dimension Hessian contributes a sum of log feature-usage counts, yielding a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs with the same functional form as for mixtures. This enables an EM-like algorithm that maximizes a tractable lower bound to the FIC, leading to automatic feature shrinkage and selection. The authors further (i) reinterpret the FAB E-step as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), proposing a “shrinkage acceleration” that performs multiple CCCP iterations per E-step to prune features earlier; and (ii) analyze parameter identifiability, showing FAB encourages balanced usage of redundant features and suggesting a post-hoc merge step.

Experiments compare FAB/LFM to IBP-based methods (Gibbs sampling, variational IBP, MEIBP) and to a variational EM baseline, reporting better model selection (closer to true K), competitive or improved held-out likelihoods, and lower runtimes than several IBP implementations.

Strengths
- Clear and reasonable motivation: extending FAB to a broader class (LFMs) that do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition used in earlier FAB work.
- Main technical idea is plausible: the Hessian determinant asymptotics produce the same log-usage penalty as for mixtures, despite parameter sharing. If correct, this materially widens the applicability of FIC/FAB.
- Algorithmic contributions are practical: the CCCP interpretation and shrinkage acceleration reduce wall-clock time by pruning features early; the identifiability discussion and merge heuristic address non-uniqueness that often plagues LFMs.
- Empirical results show consistent model selection and predictive benefits on synthetic and real datasets; the shrinkage acceleration provides measurable speedups.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Asymptotic regime and assumptions are underspecified. Theorem 2 and Lemma 1 are stated with “mild assumptions” deferred to the supplement. It is crucial to spell out: (i) whether K is fixed as N→∞ (as in BIC-like derivations) or allowed to grow, and if the latter, at what rate; (ii) regularity/singularity conditions for the Fisher information; and (iii) whether feature usage proportions stay bounded away from 0 and 1 so that log sums are well-defined. Much of the practical appeal is model selection over K, but the presented asymptotics appear to require a fixed finite K, which conflicts with the IBP motivation.
- Edge cases of the FIC term. The log ∑n znk penalty is undefined when a feature is unused or always on. The paper acknowledges post-hoc shrinking/removal, but the derivation relies on those terms being well-behaved during optimization. Please clarify how the lower bound and its updates avoid or handle these boundary cases rigorously, not just heuristically.
- Mathematical precision. A few places are imprecise or contain minor errors:
  - In the mixture model discussion, pk(X|z·k) should condition only on data assigned to k; as written it could be misread as using all data. Clarify conditioning and notation for assigned subsets. 
  - Equation (4) appears to mix expectations—ensure the RHS includes the appropriate Eq[·] (currently it reads as if an expectation is missing).
  - The statement that “asymptotically ignores priors” is context-dependent; please qualify when the prior-induced regularization is negligible versus finite-N effects.
- Attribution/originality. The contribution is primarily an extension of prior FAB work to LFMs, with a new asymptotic Hessian analysis. This is useful but incremental. Related asymptotic criteria (e.g., MAD-Bayes) and nonparametric methods (IBP variants) are discussed, but it would help to more clearly delineate how the present asymptotics differ from zero-noise limits and from BIC-like treatments for non-regular models.
- Experimental analysis leaves ambiguity about sources of gains:
  - Stopping criteria and runtime accounting: FAB uses a relative improvement threshold δ and Tshrink iterations; IBP samplers typically require burn-in and thinning. Please report exact stopping rules for each method, number of sweeps/iterations, and include sensitivity to δ and Tshrink. Otherwise runtime comparisons can be arbitrary.
  - Fairness in likelihood comparison: some baselines fix noise variance while FAB learns it; this can disadvantage baselines. Either allow baselines to learn noise or tune via validation.
  - Hyperparameter sensitivity: IBP’s α strongly affects K; show sensitivity or cross-validate α to ensure overestimation is not an implementation artifact.
  - Ablations: to isolate the effect of the model-selection criterion vs. optimization, report (i) EM/variational EM initialized identically and run with the same CCCP/shrinkage schedule; (ii) FAB without acceleration; (iii) using K chosen by FAB but optimizing parameters with EM (and vice versa).
- Identifiability and merge. Theorem 4 is interesting, but the conditions and the precise notion of “identifiability” could be clearer. The proposed merge step is heuristic; please provide a condition guaranteeing L increases after merging and quantify how often this occurs.

Quality
Technically sound in spirit, but important details are relegated to the supplement. The asymptotic argument looks plausible, yet the lack of explicit assumptions on K, feature usage, and regularity makes it hard to fully validate. The algorithmic components are reasonable and empirically effective.

Clarity
Generally well-written, with helpful intuition for shrinkage and CCCP. However, several notational ambiguities and missing assumptions detract. Please tighten the exposition around the mixture model derivation, the exact lower bound used, and the boundary cases of the log-usage term. Minor typos: “mean-filed,” “this this update,” etc.

Originality
Moderately original. The main novelty is extending FAB to LFMs via a new Hessian asymptotic and leveraging CCCP for acceleration. Conceptually, it builds on established FAB ideas.

Significance
Useful for practitioners who want automated model selection in LFMs without nonparametric machinery. If the asymptotic results are made precise, the work could broaden FAB’s scope. Empirical gains are promising but would be more convincing with stronger baselines and ablations.

Questions for the authors
- Specify the asymptotic regime: is K fixed? If K grows, what conditions ensure Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 still hold?
- Provide explicit assumptions for Lemma 1 and a sketch of proof in the main paper.
- How do you guarantee the algorithm avoids states where ∑n µnk ∈ {0, N} so that the FIC remains valid during optimization?
- Can baselines be allowed to learn the noise variance, and can you report sensitivity to IBP α?
- Please include ablations isolating the effect of CCCP-based acceleration and the merge step on both speed and predictive performance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Extends a principled model-selection framework to a broader class (LFMs).
- Practical, fast algorithm with automatic feature selection and good empirical behavior.
- CCCP interpretation and acceleration are neat and useful.

Arguments against acceptance
- Asymptotic theory and assumptions are under-specified in the main text.
- Some mathematical imprecision and edge-case handling left heuristic.
- Experimental comparisons do not fully control for stopping criteria, hyperparameters, and likelihood fairness; ablations are limited.
- Incremental relative to prior FAB work.

Overall recommendation
Borderline leaning reject. The approach is sensible and potentially impactful, but the paper needs clearer and more rigorous specification of the asymptotic assumptions, tighter mathematics, and more careful empirical methodology to substantiate the claimed advantages. With these revisions, it could be a strong contribution.
