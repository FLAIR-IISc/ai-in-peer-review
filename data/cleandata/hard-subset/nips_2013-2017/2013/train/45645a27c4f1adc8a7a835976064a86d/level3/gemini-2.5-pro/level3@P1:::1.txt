### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously applied to mixture models and HMMs, to the domain of latent feature models (LFMs). The core idea is to derive a model selection criterion, termed the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which approximates the marginal log-likelihood. This is achieved by applying a Laplace approximation to the complete data log-likelihood, where a key technical contribution (Lemma 1) shows that the resulting penalty term for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models, despite the shared parameters across features. This FIC leads to an EM-like algorithm where the E-step is modified with a complexity penalty term that encourages sparsity and automatically selects the number of latent features. The authors further provide analysis connecting their E-step to the convex-concave procedure (CCCP), leading to a practical acceleration technique. They also discuss the parameter identifiability of their method. The empirical evaluation compares the proposed FAB/LFM against several state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations, demonstrating superior performance in terms of predictive likelihood and computational efficiency on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a compelling experimental evaluation where the proposed FAB/LFM consistently outperforms several IBP-based methods (Gibbs sampling, VB, MEIBP) in terms of predictive log-likelihood (Table 1). Furthermore, it often does so with significantly lower computational cost and learns a more compact model (smaller K). The smaller gap between training and predictive likelihood suggests the method's regularization is effective at preventing overfitting.

2.  **Reasonable and Well-Motivated Approach:** The core idea of using an asymptotic approximation of the marginal likelihood to derive a practical model selection criterion is sound. The extension of the FAB framework to LFMs, which do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption of earlier work, is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The resulting algorithm is elegant, inheriting the simplicity of EM while incorporating an automatic and principled complexity penalty.

3.  **Useful Algorithmic Refinements:** The paper goes beyond the basic derivation of the algorithm. The CCCP interpretation provides a deeper understanding of the optimization and motivates a practical "shrinkage acceleration" technique, which Figure 1 shows to be highly effective. The discussion on parameter identifiability and the subsequent merge post-processing step is also a thoughtful addition that addresses a key theoretical and practical issue in latent variable models.

**Weaknesses and Questions**

1.  **Incremental Originality:** While the extension to LFMs is novel and technically non-trivial, the core conceptual framework is a direct application of the authors' previous work on FAB ([5], [6] in the paper). The contribution lies in demonstrating that the FAB principle can be applied to a more complex model class, rather than introducing a fundamentally new inference paradigm.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Precision:** The mathematical exposition could be tightened in several places, which raises concerns about the rigor of the underlying theory.
    *   **Asymptotic Regime:** The derivation relies on an asymptotic approximation akin to the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). However, the precise asymptotic regime is not specified. BIC-style arguments typically assume a fixed, finite number of parameters (K) as the number of data points (N) goes to infinity. This seems at odds with the motivation of competing with the IBP, which is designed for a potentially infinite number of features. The authors should clarify the assumptions of Theorem 2, particularly regarding how K is allowed to scale with N.
    *   **Mathematical Details:** There are minor but notable formal errors. For example, in the definition of FIC for MMs (Eq. 3), the term `log(sum_n z_nk)` is undefined if a feature k is never used (`sum_n z_nk = 0`). While in expectation this might be handled, the formulation is imprecise. Similarly, the lower bound in Equation (4) appears to be missing an expectation `E_q[...]` on the left-hand side. In Section 1.1, the description of `p_k(X|z.k)` seems to imply it conditions on the full dataset X, whereas it should only depend on the data points assigned to component k. These points reduce confidence in the technical soundness.

3.  **Ambiguity in Experimental Comparison:**
    *   **Stopping Criteria:** The runtime comparison in Table 1 is difficult to interpret fully. While the convergence criterion for FAB/EM is specified (`delta = 10^-4`), the criteria for the competitor methods are not. For MCMC-based methods like the IBP Gibbs sampler, performance is highly dependent on the number of iterations. It is unclear if the sampler was run for a fixed number of iterations or until some convergence diagnostic was met. This makes it hard to judge if the speed comparison is fair.
    *   **Source of Improvement:** It is not entirely clear whether the superior predictive performance of FAB stems from the FIC objective itself or from a more effective optimization procedure that avoids the poor local optima that can plague Gibbs samplers. The observation that IBP tends to learn many more components could be a symptom of hyperparameter settings (e.g., `alpha=3` is mentioned as potentially causing overestimation) or getting stuck in a particular mode of the posterior. A more detailed analysis or ablation study would be needed to disentangle the benefits of the objective function from those of the optimization algorithm.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a sensible and effective method for model selection in latent feature models. The approach is a logical, albeit incremental, extension of prior work. The empirical results are strong, showing clear improvements over established non-parametric Bayesian methods in both speed and predictive accuracy. However, the paper is weakened by a lack of precision in its theoretical claims, particularly regarding the asymptotic assumptions underlying the FIC derivation. Furthermore, the experimental analysis could be strengthened to provide a more conclusive interpretation of the performance gains.

Despite these weaknesses, the proposed method is practical, performs well, and addresses the important problem of model selection in LFMs. It is a solid contribution that is likely to be of interest to practitioners and researchers in the field. I lean towards acceptance, but would strongly encourage the authors to address the issues of theoretical clarity and experimental ambiguity in their final version.
