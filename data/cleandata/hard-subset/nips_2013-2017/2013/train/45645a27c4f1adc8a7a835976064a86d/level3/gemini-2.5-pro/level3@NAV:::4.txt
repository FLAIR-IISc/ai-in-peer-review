This paper extends the framework of factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and hidden Markov models to the important class of latent feature models (LFMs). FAB inference, introduced in prior work by Fujimaki et al., is an efficient model selection method that maximizes a factorized information criterion (FIC), which is a tractable lower bound that converges to the marginal log-likelihood. The key technical challenge addressed here is that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption on the complete likelihood, a condition previously required for deriving the FIC. The authors' central contribution is a novel asymptotic analysis showing that, despite this violation, a FIC for LFMs can be derived and surprisingly takes the same functional form as the one for mixture models.

The paper's primary strengths lie in its technical quality, clarity, and strong empirical results. The theoretical derivation that bypasses the Hessian condition is non-trivial and is presented clearly with sufficient detail. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed FAB/LFM against strong and relevant baselines, including fast Gibbs sampling for the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), Variational Bayes (VB), and the more recent Maximum-Expectation IBP (MEIBP). The results are compelling, demonstrating that FAB/LFM consistently achieves superior predictive performance with a significantly lower computational cost on both synthetic and real-world datasets. This suggests the method is a practical and powerful alternative for inference in LFMs. Additional contributions, such as the CCCP-inspired shrinkage acceleration and the analysis of parameter identifiability, further strengthen the work.

The main weakness is that the contribution, while valuable, could be viewed as incremental. It applies an existing inference framework (FAB) to a new, albeit important, model class. However, the technical work required for this extension is significant and the finding that the FIC structure is preserved has broader implications for the applicability of FAB.

In summary, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that presents a solid and useful extension of an interesting inference technique. The demonstrated advantages in performance and computational efficiency make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I support the paper's acceptance.
