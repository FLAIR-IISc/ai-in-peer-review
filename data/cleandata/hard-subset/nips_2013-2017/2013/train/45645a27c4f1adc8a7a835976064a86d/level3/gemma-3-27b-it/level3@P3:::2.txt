This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a key limitation of previous work. The authors convincingly demonstrate that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) can be applied to LFMs despite not initially satisfying the conditions required for its derivation, through a careful asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix. The resulting FAB/LFM framework offers a more automated model selection procedure – specifically, determining the dimensionality of latent features – and achieves substantial computational efficiency gains. The thorough empirical evaluation against a range of competing methods, including IBP variants and variational approaches, strengthens the claims. The paper is well-written, logically structured, and builds upon prior FAB/FIC work in a natural and effective manner.

**Summary & Related Work:** The introduction effectively positions the work within the existing landscape of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of prior FAB inference regarding the Hessian matrix condition and highlights the computational burden of existing LFM approaches like IBP and VB. The detailed explanation of FIC for mixture models provides necessary context. The related work section is comprehensive, referencing key papers in both FAB inference and infinite LFMs, demonstrating the authors’ awareness of the relevant literature. The connection to MAD-Bayes is appropriately noted, highlighting the differing asymptotic assumptions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification for applying FIC to LFMs, presented through Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, appears solid. The analysis of the Hessian matrix and the derivation of the FIC are convincingly presented.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The introduction of shrinkage acceleration, inspired by a CCCP interpretation, demonstrably improves the computational performance of the algorithm (Figure 1). This is a significant practical contribution.
*   **Automated Model Selection:** The method's ability to automatically select the number of latent features without hand-tuning is a major advantage, simplifying the modeling process and reducing the risk of overfitting.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the method's effectiveness across various scenarios. The comparison against strong baselines is particularly commendable. The results consistently show strong performance in terms of both model selection accuracy and predictive performance.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the theoretical developments and understand the algorithmic details. The notation is clear, and the explanations are concise yet comprehensive.
*   **Identifiability Analysis:** The discussion of parameter identifiability and the proposed merging post-processing step are valuable contributions, addressing potential issues with non-uniqueness in the learned parameters.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Approximation Failure Modes:** The most significant concern is the lack of detailed discussion regarding the potential failure modes of the combined approximations (FIC, mean-field inference, shrinkage). While the authors acknowledge the asymptotic nature of the derivations, they do not thoroughly investigate the conditions under which these approximations might break down or lead to inaccurate results. Specifically, the sensitivity of the method to deviations from the assumptions underlying the Hessian analysis (Lemma 1) warrants further investigation. Under what circumstances would the approximation of the log determinant become inaccurate, and what impact would this have on the performance of the algorithm?
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Although the method aims for automation, some parameters (e.g., δ, Tshrink) still require tuning. A more thorough sensitivity analysis of these parameters could strengthen the argument for its automation.
*   **Computational Cost Discussion:** While the shrinkage acceleration is shown to be effective, a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of each step in the algorithm (e.g., E-step, M-step, shrinkage) would be helpful for understanding the overall efficiency gains.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound extension of FAB inference to LFMs.
*   Significant computational efficiency gains demonstrated empirically.
*   Automated model selection capability simplifies the modeling process.
*   Thorough empirical evaluation against strong baselines.
*   Well-written and clearly organized presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the potential failure modes of the approximations.
*   Some parameter tuning still required despite the aim for automation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The authors successfully extend FAB inference to LFMs, addressing a significant limitation of previous work and achieving impressive results in terms of computational efficiency and model selection accuracy. While the lack of detailed discussion regarding the failure modes of the approximations is a concern, it does not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding approximation failure modes. Specifically, the authors should consider adding a section discussing the limitations of the method and potential scenarios where it might perform poorly, along with suggestions for mitigating these issues.
