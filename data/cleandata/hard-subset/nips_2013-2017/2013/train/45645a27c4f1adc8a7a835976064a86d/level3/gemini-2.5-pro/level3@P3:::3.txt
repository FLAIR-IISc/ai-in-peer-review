### Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary**

This paper introduces an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to binary latent feature models (LFMs). The core contribution is an EM-based algorithm that optimizes a tractable lower bound on a "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC), which approximates the marginal log-likelihood. A key feature of this approach is a shrinkage penalty derived from the FIC that allows the model to automatically infer the number of latent features, K, during optimization. The authors position this method as a deterministic and scalable alternative to nonparametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), arguing that it achieves parsimony through a model-selection criterion rather than an explicit prior. Experimental results on several datasets are presented to demonstrate that the proposed FAB/LFM method can outperform Gibbs sampling, variational, and MAP-based IBP methods in terms of runtime, prediction, and model selection accuracy.

**Quality and Technical Soundness**

The technical approach appears sound. The derivation extending the FAB framework from mixture models to LFMs, which do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption of prior FAB work, is the paper's main theoretical contribution. Lemma 1, which shows that the determinant of the Hessian for LFMs yields a complexity term of the same form as in mixture models, is a key and interesting result. The resulting EM-like algorithm with its associated E-step, M-step, and shrinkage mechanism is well-defined and the monotonicity of the objective function provides a convergence guarantee to a local optimum. The analysis connecting the updates to a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) is insightful and provides good motivation for the proposed shrinkage acceleration technique.

However, while the core methodology is strong, the paper is weakened by a superficial experimental evaluation and a lack of critical discussion. The claims of empirical superiority are not fully supported due to several flaws in the experimental design and reporting.

**Clarity**

The paper is reasonably well-written and organized. The methodology sections explaining the derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the steps of the algorithm are generally clear and easy to follow. However, the clarity of the experimental section is poor. Crucial details about the setup and configuration of baseline methods are either missing or unclearly justified, making the results difficult to interpret and reproduce. For instance, the specifics of the VB-IBP implementation (e.g., number of restarts, justification for using a finite version) are not provided. The post-submission author feedback, while helpful, still leaves the experimental methodology in need of clearer explanation within the main paper.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper addresses the significant and practical problem of performing efficient inference in latent feature models while simultaneously determining the appropriate model complexity (K). Applying the FAB framework to LFMs is a novel extension of prior work, which was limited to models like mixtures and HMMs. This work is significant because it provides a scalable, deterministic alternative to MCMC-based nonparametric methods, which can be computationally expensive and suffer from slow mixing on large datasets. The model's ability to automatically prune irrelevant features is a highly desirable property for unsupervised feature learning.

However, the paper misses an opportunity to connect its asymptotic approach to other related work on small-variance asymptotics for nonparametric models. A discussion of methods like MAD-Bayes (Broderick et al., ICML 2013) would provide valuable context and better position the paper's contribution within the field.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Extension:** The application of the FAB inference framework to latent feature models is a novel and non-trivial contribution.
2.  **Scalability:** The proposed EM-like algorithm is computationally efficient and demonstrates potential for scaling to large datasets where methods like Gibbs sampling may be too slow.
3.  **Automatic Model Selection:** The method provides an elegant, integrated mechanism for inferring the number of latent features without resorting to cross-validation or complex nonparametric priors.
4.  **Sound Theoretical Basis:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the formulation of the optimization algorithm appear technically correct and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses and Required Changes**

1.  **Flawed Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is the paper's most significant weakness and requires a major overhaul.
    *   **Inappropriate Baseline Usage:** The MEIBP baseline is designed for models with non-negative weight matrices (W). Applying it to simulations and real-world datasets where this assumption is violated makes these comparisons invalid and misleading.
    *   **Unfair Comparisons:** In the block images experiment, the IBP Gibbs sampler should have been provided with the known true noise variance for a fair comparison, rather than being handicapped with a heuristic setting. Baselines should be run under their most favorable conditions.
    *   **Suspicious Results:** The reported result that accelerated Gibbs sampling finds ~70 features on the 49-dimensional Sonar data is highly suspect and likely points to a flawed baseline implementation (e.g., poor initialization, incorrect noise variance). Such results cast doubt on the validity of the comparisons.
    *   **Insufficient Detail:** The experiments lack sufficient detail for reproducibility. For example, the VB-IBP experiments should clarify why a finite variational version was used instead of an infinite one and report details like the number of restarts.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on Limitations:** The paper presents a one-sided view of the proposed method.
    *   There is no discussion of potential convergence issues, such as sensitivity to initialization and local optima, which are common in EM-like procedures.
    *   The paper fails to discuss scenarios where FAB might underperform. For instance, in small-data regimes where asymptotic assumptions may not hold, or for problems with complex posterior landscapes where Gibbs sampling might find better solutions.
    *   The method is limited to binary LFMs with Gaussian noise and cannot be applied to other common models like factor analysis or probabilistic PCA. This limitation should be explicitly stated.

3.  **Missing and Incorrect References:**
    *   Reference [21] (Reed and Ghahramani) is incorrectly described as a Gibbs sampling method; it performs MAP inference via submodular optimization. This must be corrected.
    *   The paper should cite and discuss relevant recent work on efficient inference for LFMs, particularly the MAD-Bayes framework, which shares a similar asymptotic motivation.
    *   A minor but important correction: The standard deviation setting for real data is cited as 0.75 times the data's standard deviation, referencing [1, 22]. The commonly recommended setting in those contexts is 0.25. This should be corrected or justified.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a novel and promising method for scalable inference in binary latent feature models. The core technical ideas are strong and address an important problem. However, the paper is significantly undermined by a weak and flawed experimental evaluation that fails to provide convincing evidence for the claimed superiority of the method. Furthermore, the lack of discussion regarding limitations and key related work prevents a balanced assessment of the contribution.

For the paper to be acceptable, the authors must substantially revise the experimental section to include fair, well-justified, and reproducible comparisons. The identified errors in baseline usage and referencing must be fixed, and the discussion should be expanded to include the method's limitations and its relationship to other asymptotic inference techniques.
