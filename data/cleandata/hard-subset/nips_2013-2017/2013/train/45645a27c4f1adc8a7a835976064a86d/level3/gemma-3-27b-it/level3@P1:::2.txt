## Review of “Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models”

This paper presents a compelling extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The authors address a key limitation of previous FAB work – its inapplicability to models lacking a specific Hessian matrix condition – and demonstrate that FICs for LFMs have a similar form to those of mixture models, enabling the application of FAB. They further introduce an acceleration technique based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and establish parameter identifiability, offering a principled approach to feature merging. The work is grounded in a strong theoretical foundation and validated through extensive experimentation.

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature. It clearly connects to prior FAB work on mixture models [5, 6] and establishes the challenge of extending this framework to LFMs [9, 17]. The discussion of related work, including Indian Buffet Processes [10, 11] and variational Bayesian methods [3], is comprehensive and accurately reflects the state-of-the-art. The citations are relevant and appropriately used. The authors correctly identify that existing nonparametric Bayesian (NPB) and VB methods, while effective, often suffer from high computational costs. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical derivations, particularly Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, appear rigorous and well-motivated. The connection to the FIC and its asymptotic properties is clearly established. The inclusion of proofs and assumptions in the supplementary materials strengthens the technical validity of the claims.
* **Novelty and Originality:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs, a class of models previously excluded due to Hessian matrix constraints, is a significant contribution.  The proposed shrinkage acceleration technique, leveraging a CCCP interpretation, adds further novelty. The identifiability analysis and feature merging post-processing are insightful extensions.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly explain the core concepts and mathematical derivations, making the work accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian inference and latent variable models. The notation is consistent and well-defined.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, encompassing both synthetic and real-world datasets. The comparison against strong baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed FAB/LFM approach. The analysis of computational speed, model selection accuracy, and prediction performance provides a well-rounded assessment of the method’s strengths.
* **Significance:** The work addresses a practical challenge in LFM learning – efficient model selection – and offers a fully automated solution with minimal hand-tuning. The demonstrated improvements in computational efficiency, particularly with the shrinkage acceleration, are valuable. The potential for broader applicability to other latent variable models, as suggested by the authors, further enhances the significance of the contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Approximation Limitations:** While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the combined approximations (FIC, mean-field, shrinkage) empirically, a more thorough discussion of their potential failure modes and the conditions under which they might break down would be beneficial. Specifically, exploring the impact of deviations from the assumptions underlying the FIC derivation would strengthen the analysis. 
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** Although the authors state that the method has limited hyperparameter tuning, the choice of parameters within the acceleration technique (e.g., *Tshrink*) or for the baselines could influence the results. A brief sensitivity analysis on these parameters would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of FAB inference to a challenging class of models.
* The theoretical contributions are significant, and the empirical validation is compelling.
* The proposed method offers a practical solution to a real-world problem – efficient model selection in LFMs.
* The clarity of the presentation and thoroughness of the experimentation are commendable.
* The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature and builds upon existing work in a meaningful way.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The potential limitations of the combined approximations warrant further investigation and discussion.
* A brief sensitivity analysis of key parameters could provide additional insight into the robustness of the method.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and latent variable models. The strengths of the work far outweigh the weaknesses, and I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS 2013. The authors have presented a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated approach to a challenging problem. Addressing the limitations regarding the approximation analysis and parameter sensitivity could further strengthen the paper.
