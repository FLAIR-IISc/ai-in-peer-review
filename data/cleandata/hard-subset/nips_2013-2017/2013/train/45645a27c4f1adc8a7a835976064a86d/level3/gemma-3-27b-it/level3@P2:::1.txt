This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a limitation of previous FAB work that required a block diagonal Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite not strictly meeting this condition, exhibit similar properties allowing for FAB application. They derive a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs and propose an efficient algorithm with a shrinkage acceleration step and a merging post-processing strategy. Empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods in terms of model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.

The work builds upon recent advances in FAB inference [5, 6] and addresses a significant challenge in extending its applicability. The core idea of leveraging asymptotic analysis to derive a tractable criterion for model selection is well-motivated and potentially impactful. The connection to BIC is clearly articulated, and the resulting algorithm shares desirable characteristics like automatic hidden state selection and parameter identifiability. The analysis of the Hessian matrix and the derivation of the FIC for LFMs represent a valuable technical contribution, extending the scope of FAB inference to a broader class of models. The proposed acceleration technique and merging post-processing further enhance the practical utility of the approach.

However, several aspects of the paper require attention. The mathematical exposition, while generally clear, contains some imprecisions. Specifically, equation (3) presents a potential issue: the log-sum term may be undefined if all $z_{nk}$ are zero, raising concerns about the validity of the FIC derivation. Furthermore, Theorem 2 lacks crucial details regarding the asymptotic regime and underlying assumptions. The scaling of K with N is not explicitly addressed, and the application of a BIC-based approximation seems more suited for fixed, finite K, potentially conflicting with the infinite-K spirit of IBP models. Minor formal errors exist, such as the conditioning in $p_k(X | z_{.,k})$ which should only be on data assigned to component k, and a missing expectation symbol in equation (4).

The experimental evaluation, while thorough, suffers from some limitations. The stopping criteria for the algorithms are not clearly specified, making a direct comparison of runtime performance somewhat arbitrary, particularly for methods like Gibbs sampling that rely on truncation. The interpretation of the predictive improvements is also unclear. It’s difficult to ascertain whether the gains stem solely from the model selection criterion or from the FAB algorithm effectively navigating local optima that might trap IBP methods. The authors acknowledge potential implementation issues with Gibbs sampling, but a more detailed analysis of convergence behavior and sensitivity to initialization would strengthen the conclusions. 

The paper positions itself relative to IBP methods, highlighting computational efficiency and predictive accuracy. While the results demonstrate advantages in these areas, the choice of α=3 for the IBP implementations might bias the comparison, potentially leading to an overestimation of K. A more careful parameter tuning strategy for the baselines would enhance the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, the discussion of the relationship between FAB and the MEIBP method [22] is somewhat limited; a more in-depth comparative analysis would be valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel extension of FAB inference to LFMs.
*   Derivation of a tractable FIC for LFMs.
*   Efficient algorithm with acceleration and merging techniques.
*   Competitive performance against state-of-the-art IBP methods.
*   Theoretical justification based on asymptotic analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Imprecise mathematical exposition in certain sections.
*   Lack of detail regarding asymptotic regime and assumptions in Theorem 2.
*   Ambiguity in experimental stopping criteria and runtime comparisons.
*   Unclear attribution of predictive gains to model selection vs. optimization.
*   Potentially biased comparison due to IBP parameter settings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The work addresses a relevant problem in Bayesian model selection for LFMs.
*   The technical contribution, while incremental, extends the applicability of FAB inference.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The mathematical exposition contains some imprecisions.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more rigorous analysis.
*   The novelty, while present, is somewhat incremental.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a sensible and well-motivated approach to extending FAB inference to LFMs. While the technical contribution is incremental and some aspects of the mathematical exposition and experimental analysis could be improved, the work demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature and offers a promising new method for model selection in latent feature models. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the identified shortcomings. Specifically, the authors should clarify the asymptotic assumptions, address the potential issue with equation (3), and provide more detailed information about the experimental setup, including stopping criteria and parameter tuning for baseline methods.
