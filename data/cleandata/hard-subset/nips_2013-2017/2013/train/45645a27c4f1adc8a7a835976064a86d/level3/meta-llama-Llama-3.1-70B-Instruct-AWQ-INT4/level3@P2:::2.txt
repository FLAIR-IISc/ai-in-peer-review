This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), providing a more automatic model selection procedure that requires little hand tuning. The authors achieve this by deriving a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs, which has the same form as that for mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the specific condition required for FAB inference.

The paper is clearly written and well-structured, building upon previous work on FAB inference and FIC. The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation of their method against a wide range of competing methods, including state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods. The results demonstrate significant computational efficiency gains on both synthetic and real datasets.

The proposed method combines several approximations, including the FIC, mean-field inference, and accelerated feature shrinkage. While the authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their approach, a major concern is the lack of discussion on when and how these combined approximations may fail. It is essential to examine the failure modes and limits of these approximations to understand the method's robustness and applicability.

The paper's strengths include:

* A logical and solid extension of prior FAB/FIC work
* A novel and effective approach to automatic model selection
* Significant computational efficiency gains on synthetic and real datasets
* Thorough empirical evaluation against a wide range of competing methods
* Clear and well-structured writing

However, the paper's weaknesses include:

* Unclear discussion on the limitations and potential failure modes of the combined approximations
* Lack of theoretical analysis on the robustness of the method to model misspecification or non-standard data distributions

To address these concerns, the authors could provide additional theoretical analysis or empirical evaluations that examine the method's performance under various scenarios, such as:

* Model misspecification: How does the method perform when the true model is not an LFM?
* Non-standard data distributions: How does the method perform when the data distribution is not Gaussian or has outliers?
* High-dimensional data: How does the method perform when the number of features is very large?

By addressing these concerns, the authors can strengthen their method and provide a more comprehensive understanding of its limitations and applicability.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective approach to automatic model selection for LFMs.
* The method demonstrates significant computational efficiency gains on synthetic and real datasets.
* The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation against a wide range of competing methods.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks a clear discussion on the limitations and potential failure modes of the combined approximations.
* The method's robustness and applicability to various scenarios are not thoroughly examined.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with the condition that the authors address the concerns mentioned above in their final version.
