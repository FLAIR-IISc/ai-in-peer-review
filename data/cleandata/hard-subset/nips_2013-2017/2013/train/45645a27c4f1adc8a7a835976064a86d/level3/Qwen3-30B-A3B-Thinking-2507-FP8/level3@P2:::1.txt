

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), which have previously not been amenable to FAB due to the absence of the block diagonal Hessian condition required for the original FAB framework. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying this condition, their Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) has the same form as for mixture models, enabling FAB inference for LFMs. The paper presents an FAB/LFM algorithm with shrinkage acceleration, establishes theoretical properties including parameter identifiability, and demonstrates improved model selection accuracy and computational efficiency compared to existing methods like IBP, VB, and MEIBP.

## Quality

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by showing that LFMs can be analyzed using the FAB framework despite not meeting the original Hessian condition. The asymptotic analysis in Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 provides a solid foundation for the approach. However, there are concerns about the mathematical precision. Equation (3) in the paper may be problematic since log(∑n znk) could be infinite if all znk are zero, raising questions about the validity of the FIC. Theorem 2 lacks specification of the asymptotic regime, particularly how K scales with N, which conflicts with IBP's motivation for infinite K. The authors should clarify these theoretical assumptions. The experimental results support the claims but suffer from insufficient detail about stopping criteria, making runtime comparisons less meaningful.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from motivation to theory to experiments. The technical exposition is mostly accessible, though there are minor issues. Section 2 incorrectly states pk(X|z·k) should condition only on data assigned to component k (it should be data assigned to component k), and equation (4) is missing an expectation. The algorithm descriptions are clear, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The supplementary materials appear to contain rigorous proofs that address many of the theoretical concerns.

## Originality

The core contribution is a reasonable extension of prior FAB work ([3], [4]) to LFMs. While the extension is novel in its application to LFMs, the approach itself is incremental rather than transformative. The key theoretical insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying the original Hessian condition is interesting, but the main contribution appears to be the application of an existing framework to a new class of models. The shrinkage acceleration method is a practical contribution, but the underlying CCCP interpretation is not entirely novel.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in model selection for LFMs, which has been primarily handled by computationally expensive methods like IBP. The empirical results show that FAB/LFMs achieve better predictive performance with lower computational cost than state-of-the-art methods, particularly for large-scale data. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the incremental nature of the contribution. The reported improvements in predictive likelihood and computational efficiency are meaningful, but it's unclear whether they stem from the model selection criterion or from the algorithm avoiding worse local optima (as Gibbs sampling tends to learn too many components).

## Overall Assessment

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- The paper successfully extends FAB inference to LFMs, addressing a significant gap in the literature.
- The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the relationship between LFMs and the FAB framework.
- The experimental results demonstrate clear practical benefits over existing methods.
- The shrinkage acceleration technique is a useful practical contribution.
- The parameter identifiability analysis offers important theoretical insights for latent variable models.

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- The theoretical foundation requires clarification regarding the asymptotic regime and assumptions.
- The paper contains minor formal errors that need correction.
- Experimental results lack sufficient detail about stopping criteria, making runtime comparisons less convincing.
- The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, building directly on prior FAB work.
- It's unclear whether the reported gains come from the model selection criterion or from algorithmic improvements.

This paper presents a reasonable extension of existing FAB work to LFMs with practical benefits in computational efficiency and model selection. While the theoretical contribution is not groundbreaking, the practical value is significant for large-scale applications of LFMs. The paper would be stronger with more precise mathematical statements, clearer specification of asymptotic assumptions, and more detailed experimental methodology. With these improvements, this work would be a solid contribution to the field.

Overall, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical and experimental concerns raised. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the application of Bayesian model selection methods to latent feature models, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims.
