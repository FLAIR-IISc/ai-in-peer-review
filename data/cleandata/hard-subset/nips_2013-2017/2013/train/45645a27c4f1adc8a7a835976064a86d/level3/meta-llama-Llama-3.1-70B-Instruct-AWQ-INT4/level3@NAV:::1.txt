This paper proposes a novel model-selection criterion for binary latent feature models that integrates out parameters via Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), yielding an Expectation-Maximization (EM)-like algorithm with complexity penalties. The approach is reasonable and motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. The authors demonstrate improved held-out/predictive likelihoods and faster runtimes compared to several Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The technical contribution of this paper is novel but incremental, essentially an extension of prior Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) work. However, the mathematical exposition is imprecise in places, such as equation (3), where the log-sum of z may be infinite if all zeros, raising questions about the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC). Additionally, Theorem 2 lacks specification of the asymptotic regime and assumptions, particularly how K scales with N; the BIC-based approximation seems to require fixed finite K, conflicting with IBP's infinite-K motivation.

The experimental results (Table 1) report faster convergence for FAB, but stopping criteria are unspecified, making runtime comparisons and Gibbs sampler truncation arbitrary. Furthermore, it is unclear whether predictive improvements come from the model-selection criterion or from algorithms avoiding worse local optima; Gibbs's tendency to learn too many components suggests implementation/local-optima issues.

Overall, this is a sensible but somewhat incremental method, with some imprecise math and insufficient experimental analysis to fully interpret the reported gains.

Arguments Pro Acceptance:

1. The paper proposes a novel model-selection criterion for binary latent feature models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The approach is reasonable and motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument.
3. The authors demonstrate improved held-out/predictive likelihoods and faster runtimes compared to several IBP implementations.

Arguments Con Acceptance:

1. The technical contribution is incremental, building on prior FAB work.
2. The mathematical exposition is imprecise in places, which may raise concerns about the validity of the results.
3. The experimental results are not thoroughly analyzed, and the stopping criteria are unspecified, making it difficult to fully interpret the reported gains.

Recommendation:

I would recommend accepting this paper, but with the condition that the authors address the imprecise math and provide a more thorough analysis of the experimental results. Additionally, the authors should clarify the stopping criteria and provide a more detailed comparison with other methods.
