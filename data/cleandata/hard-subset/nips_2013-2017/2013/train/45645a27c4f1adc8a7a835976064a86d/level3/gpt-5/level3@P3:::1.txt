Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to linear-Gaussian latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features. The key technical step is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian with respect to per-dimension parameters θd that yields a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs with the same complexity terms as in FAB for mixture models (MMs): a global O(DK log N) term and, crucially, per-feature penalties of the form (D/2) log(∑n znk). This leads to an EM-like optimization of a tractable lower bound with an additional shrinkage term that automatically prunes unneeded features. The authors further reinterpret the E-step as a CCCP update and introduce a shrinkage-acceleration heuristic and a post-hoc merge operation to resolve non-identifiability. Experiments compare FAB to several IBP implementations (accelerated Gibbs, VB, MEIBP) and to variational EM on synthetic and real data, reporting improved held-out likelihoods and faster runtimes in many settings.

The paper builds directly on prior FAB work for MMs and HMMs ([5],[6]), IBP-based LFM inference ([10],[2],[3],[22]), and related asymptotics (BIC [23], singular learning theory [25], and “MAD-Bayes” [1]). The main conceptual novelty is showing that the same FIC structure arises for LFMs despite parameter sharing across features.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: provides a computationally simple, automatic model selection mechanism for LFMs that does not rely on priors or hand-tuning.
- Technical idea: Lemma 1/Theorem 2 argue that LFM FIC inherits the per-feature log-occupancy penalty that drives principled shrinkage. The CCCP view is insightful and leads to a practical acceleration.
- Algorithmic practicality: EM-like updates with closed-form M-step, monotone lower-bound ascent, built-in pruning/merging, and a stopping criterion.
- Empirical evidence: on several datasets, FAB attains competitive or better predictive log-likelihoods with smaller K and often lower runtime than IBP variants; qualitatively cleaner features in the block-data study.
- Reasonable asymptotic motivation: the FIC approximation and its shrinkage behavior are plausibly justified in large-N regimes.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Asymptotic regime underspecified. Theorem 2 does not state clearly the assumptions and scaling: Are D and K fixed as N→∞? Does K grow with N (as in IBP, where E[K] can scale with log N)? The BIC-style Laplace approximation typically assumes fixed finite parameter dimension; using it to motivate automatic model selection for potentially growing K needs explicit conditions. Please state the precise regime (e.g., N→∞ with K, D fixed; bounded per-feature occupancy; regularity assumptions) in the main text, not only in the supplement.
- Handling empty/full features. The FIC contains −(D/2) log(∑n znk). If ∑n znk=0, log 0=−∞ and the criterion becomes ill-defined; the paper later notes this but relies on ad hoc pruning. Precisely define the domain over Z where FIC is valid, how the algorithm avoids these configurations during optimization, and whether a disciplined cap (e.g., replacing counts by max{∑n znk, 1}) alters guarantees.
- FAB lower bound equations need more care. Equation (4) appears to miss an explicit expectation on the right-hand side and is ambiguous in notation; please correct and state the inequality with all expectations and linearization points. In Section 1.1, pk(X|z·,k) should condition only on data assigned to component k (those n with znk=1), not on the entire X; clarify the factorization and conditioning precisely.
- Theorem 2 and Lemma 1. The key equality log det|F(d)|=∑k log(∑n znk)/N+Op(1) is central. The assumptions are relegated to the supplement; at least summarize them (e.g., bounded moments, non-degenerate design, identifiability conditions) and discuss when they fail (collinearity, near-zero noise variance). Also specify whether the Op(1) term is uniform over Z and K, which matters for model comparison.
- Attribution of gains. It is unclear whether predictive improvements are due to the FIC-based model selection versus algorithmic advantages (better avoidance of poor local optima). An ablation that runs: (a) variational EM with the same CCCP/shrink/merge mechanics but without the FIC penalty, and (b) FAB without shrinkage acceleration, would help isolate the source of improvements. The tendency of Gibbs to overestimate K is consistent with local-optima/implementation issues rather than an inherent inferiority.

Clarity
- Overall writing is informative but imprecise in places. Examples: equation (3) domain issues; Eq. (4) notation; some typos (e.g., “mean-filed”). The main assumptions behind Theorem 2 and Theorem 4 should be promoted to the main text. State the exact stopping criteria for all baselines.
- The CCCP interpretation is helpful; consider consolidating the alternative derivations (Laplace vs CCCP) and clearly stating what is guaranteed (monotonicity of the lower bound) and what is heuristic (accelerateShrinkage, merge).

Originality
- The contribution is novel but incremental. It extends the FAB machinery from MMs/HMMs to LFMs, preserves the same FIC structure via a new Hessian argument, and adds practical refinements (CCCP acceleration, merging). Given substantial prior FAB work ([5],[6]) and the rich IBP literature, the conceptual advance is modest but meaningful for this model class.

Significance
- Practical impact could be good: a simple, fast, and automatic method for choosing K in LFMs is valuable, especially when IBP implementations are computationally heavy. However, clearer theoretical assumptions and stronger, more controlled empirical analysis are needed to fully establish robustness and generality.

Experimental evaluation
- Runtime comparisons: stopping criteria and truncations for baselines (IBP Gibbs, VB, MEIBP) are not fully specified; different initial Ks and fixed noise variances for some methods complicate fairness. Please standardize stopping rules (e.g., wall-clock to a target held-out likelihood), report truncation levels for IBP, and provide sensitivity to initialization and α in IBP.
- Report confidence intervals or variability over multiple seeds for PLL/TLL and K; include statistical tests where appropriate.
- Diagnostics: plot K vs iteration/time, and training vs validation likelihood to show overfitting control; include an ablation to quantify the effect of the FIC penalty.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact asymptotic regime (scaling of K with N) under which Theorem 2 holds?
- Is the Op(1) remainder uniform over Z and K? Can you bound it to justify model comparisons?
- How do you guarantee FIC is well-defined during optimization when ∑n znk∈{0, N}? Is there a principled safeguard?
- Can you provide ablations that separate the contributions of FIC, CCCP acceleration, and merging?
- How sensitive are results to noise variance learning vs fixing, and to the choice of Tshrink and δ?

Pros
- Sensible, well-motivated extension of FAB to LFMs with a clear complexity penalty and automatic model selection.
- Simple, fast algorithm with monotone lower-bound improvements and useful practical refinements.
- Competitive to better predictive performance and smaller K across several datasets.

Cons
- Asymptotic assumptions and regimes insufficiently specified; tension with IBP’s infinite-K motivation.
- Mathematical exposition imprecise in places; minor formal errors.
- Experimental setup lacks some controls, making it hard to attribute gains and to ensure fair runtime comparisons.

Overall assessment
A sensible and practically appealing method with a plausible asymptotic argument and promising empirical results. The contribution is incremental relative to prior FAB work, and the math/exposition need tightening, particularly around the asymptotic regime and domain issues. With clarified assumptions and stronger controlled experiments, this could be a solid addition; as it stands, it is borderline but promising.
