

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), presenting an EM-based algorithm that automatically infers the number of latent features K through FAB shrinkage. The authors demonstrate that FAB/LFMs offer automatic feature selection and better computational efficiency than existing methods, with experiments showing superior performance on large datasets. The work builds on prior FAB applications to mixtures and HMMs, but extends it to LFMs despite their failure to satisfy the standard Hessian block-diagonal condition.

Strengths include a technically sound approach with correct algorithmic details, clear methodology, and a novel application of FAB to LFMs. The paper addresses an important problem in scalable Bayesian inference for LFMs, and the experimental results on large datasets demonstrate significant computational advantages. The theoretical analysis showing FIC equivalence between LFMs and mixtures is particularly valuable.

However, several significant weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. The experimental evaluation contains critical flaws: MEIBP was inappropriately applied to non-negative data, the IBP Gibbs baseline should have received the known noise value in block data experiments, and the accelerated Gibbs result on Sonar data appears suspicious. The paper lacks discussion of algorithmic limitations, convergence difficulties, and performance in small-data regimes where asymptotic arguments may fail. The method's restriction to binary LFMs (excluding factor analysis and PCA) is not adequately addressed. The experimental details are insufficient, and the paper misses key related work including MAD-Bayes (ICML 2013) and recent advances in efficient LFM inference. Reference [21] is incorrectly described as Gibbs sampling (it performs MAP inference).

The paper would benefit from addressing these issues before acceptance. While the core idea is novel and the technical approach is sound, the current experimental evaluation lacks rigor and thoroughness. The authors must clarify baseline settings, fix reference descriptions, include MAD-Bayes, and provide a more complete analysis of limitations and small-data behavior.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to FAB inference but requires substantial revision to strengthen its experimental evaluation and discussion of limitations before acceptance. The technical content is strong, but the evaluation shortcomings prevent it from making a compelling empirical case for its advantages.
