

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a significant advancement that addresses a notable gap in the FAB framework. Previous work on FAB inference was limited to models satisfying a specific Hessian condition (block diagonal structure), which excluded LFMs. The authors demonstrate through Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 that LFMs, despite not satisfying this condition, yield FICs with the same form as mixture models. This theoretical insight enables the development of a fully automated model selection procedure for LFMs through FAB/LFMs, which requires little hand-tuning and offers automatic dimensionality selection.

The paper makes several notable contributions: First, it provides a rigorous theoretical foundation showing that LFMs' FIC has the same form as mixture models' FIC. Second, it introduces a novel FAB/LFM algorithm combining mean-field inference and accelerated feature shrinkage. Third, it offers theoretical insights into the algorithm's properties, including parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) and a CCCP interpretation that enables computational acceleration.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare FAB/LFMs against a wide range of competing methods including IBP, VB, EM, and MEIBP on synthetic and real datasets. The results consistently demonstrate FAB/LFMs' superiority in terms of computational efficiency (with significant speedups from the proposed acceleration), model selection accuracy, and predictive performance. The paper clearly shows that FAB/LFMs achieve better predictive performance (higher PLL) with lower computational cost than alternatives, particularly on larger datasets. The experiments also validate the effectiveness of the model shrinkage and merge post-processing procedures.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-structured. The mathematical derivations are clear and logically presented, with supplementary materials providing rigorous proofs. The algorithms (1 and 2) are described precisely, enabling reproducibility. The discussion of related work is comprehensive, appropriately citing key prior contributions to FAB, IBP, and latent feature modeling.

The contribution represents a logical and solid extension of prior FAB/FIC work. The authors successfully combine several approximations (mean-field inference, accelerated feature shrinkage, and the novel insight about FIC form) into a coherent framework that advances the state of the art in automatic model selection for latent feature models.

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical contribution is significant: The demonstration that LFMs yield the same FIC form as mixture models despite different model structures is novel and important.
- The method is truly automatic, eliminating the need for hand-tuned hyperparameters for model selection.
- The computational efficiency gains are substantial (demonstrated in Figure 1 and Table 1), making the method practical for large-scale applications.
- The thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and metrics provides strong evidence for the method's superiority.
- The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:**
- The major concern is that the paper does not sufficiently address when and why the combined approximations (FIC, mean-field, shrinkage) might fail. While the authors note the importance of the condition 0 < ∑n μ*ₙₖ/N < 1 for Theorem 4, they do not provide concrete examples or analysis of failure modes.
- The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the method to initialization, particularly given that the authors note "the initial number of latent features was set to min(N,D)" without analyzing how different initializations affect the final model.
- The theoretical analysis assumes certain conditions (e.g., bounded joint marginal log-likelihood), but the paper doesn't provide practical guidance on when these conditions might be violated in real-world applications.

**Assessment:**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by comprehensive empirical results. The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, though more detailed analysis of failure modes would strengthen this aspect.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and well-written. The notation is consistent, and the algorithms are described precisely. The supplementary materials provide the necessary mathematical details.

**Originality:** This is a novel contribution that successfully extends FAB inference to LFMs, a class of models previously outside the scope of FAB. The theoretical insight that LFMs yield the same FIC form as mixture models is particularly noteworthy.

**Significance:** The method offers a practical, fully automated solution for model selection in latent feature models with significant computational advantages over existing methods. The ability to automatically determine feature dimensionality without hand-tuning is highly valuable for practitioners. The theoretical insights regarding identifiability and CCCP interpretation also advance the field's understanding of latent variable models.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. While the authors should address the failure modes concern in the final version, the current paper is strong, well-supported, and represents a meaningful advance over prior work. The method's computational efficiency, automatic model selection capability, and strong empirical performance make it a valuable contribution to NIPS.
