This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and Gaussian observations. The key technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the complete-data Hessian that shows the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models (MMs), despite LFMs not satisfying the previous block-diagonal Hessian condition. The resulting FIC yields the same model-induced regularization term proportional to sum_k log sum_n z_nk, enabling automatic feature shrinkage and identifiability without priors. Building on this, the authors derive a practical FAB algorithm for LFMs, introduce a mean-field q(Z) and a CCCP interpretation of the E-step that motivates a shrinkage-acceleration routine, and propose a merge post-processing step based on an identifiability analysis. Empirically, FAB/LFM is compared to IBP (fast Gibbs), VB-IBP, and MEIBP on synthetic, block patterns, and several real datasets, showing strong model selection, predictive performance, and competitive to improved computational efficiency.

Relation to prior work: FAB was previously developed for MMs and HMMs [Fujimaki et al.], where FIC arose under a block-diagonal Hessian assumption; this paper removes that barrier for LFMs by proving that, asymptotically, log det F(d) decomposes into the same per-feature log-count terms. The work is positioned against nonparametric LFMs (IBP [Griffiths & Ghahramani], VB-IBP [Doshi-Velez et al.], MEIBP [Reed & Ghahramani]) and asymptotic MAP approaches (MAD-Bayes [Broderick et al.]). The CCCP interpretation aligns with standard convex-concave procedures [Yuille & Rangarajan] and links to variational mean-field updates.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The core Lemma/Theorem establishing that log det F(d) contributes D/2 sum_k log sum_n z_nk (up to Op(1)) is central and appears well-motivated; rigorous proofs are deferred to the supplement. The derivation of the FAB lower bound follows established FAB methodology, with an additional mean-field approximation for tractability. The CCCP-based acceleration and the monotonic bound increase provide a principled algorithmic framework with a natural stopping criterion.
- Empirical support: Experiments include controlled synthetic data (including the classic “blocks” dataset) and real datasets, with consistent gains in model selection (smaller, more accurate K), predictive log-likelihood, and computational cost compared to IBP/VB. MEIBP is sometimes faster per wall time but tends to underperform in PLL; FAB is competitive on large N and exhibits smaller TLL–PLL gaps (less overfitting).
- Caveats: Some baselines fix noise variance whereas FAB learns it; this could advantage FAB in likelihood-based comparisons. MEIBP settings appear sensitive and sometimes required special initialization. Results emphasize median runs; variance across runs is not reported. The asymptotic approximation may be less accurate at small N (acknowledged).

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The progression from asymptotic analysis to algorithm to refinements is easy to follow. The CCCP interpretation is insightful and sheds light on the shrinkage mechanism. A few points could be clarified: state the precise assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text, provide complexity per iteration with/without acceleration, and discuss the effect of learning/fixing noise variance on fairness of comparisons.

Originality:
- Conceptually, showing that FIC for LFMs matches that of MMs despite the non-block-diagonal Hessian is a neat and nontrivial extension of FAB theory. The CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration and identifiability/merge analysis are practical and theoretically motivated refinements. While FAB itself is not new, this application to LFMs overcomes a previously stated limitation and broadens applicability.

Significance:
- LFMs are widely used; automatic feature selection without priors or hyperparameters, with identifiability and a provable monotonic objective, is valuable. The empirical improvements in predictive performance and model compactness, plus speedups from early shrinkage, suggest that FAB/LFM could be adopted by practitioners. The work advances understanding of asymptotic Bayesian criteria beyond mixtures.

Strengths:
- Solid asymptotic derivation leading to the same FIC structure as in MMs.
- Practical, monotonic algorithm with closed-form M-step and a clear CCCP-based E-step.
- Automatic feature shrinkage and parameter identifiability, with a principled merge heuristic.
- Empirical results demonstrate strong model selection and prediction; computational efficiency competitive with or better than alternatives in many cases.
- Clear exposition and good positioning relative to IBP/VB/MEIBP and MAD-Bayes.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions for Lemma 1 are only in the supplement; summarizing them (e.g., conditions on Λ, W scaling, boundedness) would strengthen confidence.
- Fairness of comparisons: baselines with fixed noise variance vs. FAB with learned Λ may bias likelihood metrics; consider a control experiment with fixed Λ for FAB or reporting RMSE/MAE on reconstructions.
- Sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameters (e.g., Tshrink, δ) is not explored; provide ablations and robustness plots.
- Scalability: include explicit time/space complexity and wall-time scaling with N, D, K; report performance on larger-scale datasets if possible.
- Report variance across random seeds and statistical significance of PLL differences.
- Discuss applicability beyond Gaussian noise and binary features; can the analysis extend to non-Gaussian likelihoods or real-valued Z?

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a known limitation of FAB and broadens its scope to LFMs with a clean theoretical result.
- Well-designed algorithm with monotonic bound and effective shrinkage acceleration.
- Strong empirical performance in model selection and prediction, with competitive efficiency.
- Clear, careful writing and useful insights (CCCP view, identifiability).

Cons against acceptance:
- Some experimental choices may favor FAB (noise variance learning); limited analysis of sensitivity/variance.
- Asymptotic theory may be less convincing at small N, and the assumptions are not fully visible in the main text.
- Incremental for readers already familiar with FAB, though still a meaningful extension.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a substantive and well-supported extension of FAB to LFMs, provides principled algorithmic refinements, and demonstrates practical benefits. With minor clarifications on assumptions and experimental fairness, it will be a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
