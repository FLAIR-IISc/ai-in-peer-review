This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The FAB framework, previously applied to models like mixture models and HMMs, provides a method for model selection by maximizing a tractable lower bound on a "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC), which asymptotically approximates the marginal log-likelihood. A key limitation of prior FAB work was its reliance on a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete log-likelihood, a condition not met by LFMs where parameters are shared across features. The central technical contribution of this work is a novel asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) demonstrating that the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs decomposes in a way that yields an FIC with the same form as that for mixture models. This insight enables the development of an EM-like algorithm, FAB/LFM, that automatically determines the number of latent features, K, through a "model induced regularization" term that penalizes model complexity. This approach positions FAB/LFM as a scalable, deterministic alternative to nonparametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), which achieve similar goals through priors over infinite feature spaces.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of performing efficient inference and model selection in LFMs. The extension of the FAB framework to LFMs is a novel contribution that broadens the applicability of this inference paradigm. By providing a scalable, deterministic alternative to sampling-based or complex variational methods for IBP, the work is of significant interest to researchers working with latent feature models, particularly for large-scale datasets.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The core technical derivation showing that the FIC for LFMs has a similar form to that of mixture models appears sound and is the key insight of the paper. The authors further provide valuable analysis, including a CCCP interpretation of the E-step, which cleverly motivates a shrinkage acceleration technique that demonstrably improves runtime. The discussion of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) and the resulting merge post-processing step is a thoughtful addition that addresses a key theoretical issue in LFM learning and translates it into a practical algorithmic refinement.

3.  **Performance:** The experiments, despite some flaws discussed below, demonstrate that the proposed FAB/LFM method can be computationally efficient and effective. On several large real-world datasets, it achieves competitive or superior predictive performance compared to IBP-based methods, often with significantly reduced computational time and a more parsimonious model (smaller K).

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weaknesses of the paper lie in its experimental evaluation, which contains several methodological flaws, and a lack of discussion regarding the method's limitations.

1.  **Experimental Flaws and Baseline Comparisons:**
    *   The comparison with MEIBP [22] is inappropriate for several datasets. MEIBP is designed for non-negative data and requires a non-negative basis W. Applying it to data with negative values is invalid and may explain its poor performance. This should be corrected, and MEIBP should only be compared on appropriate non-negative datasets.
    *   The VB-IBP baseline uses a finite truncation of the IBP. A fairer comparison would involve an infinite variational IBP method. Furthermore, crucial details like the number of restarts for the VB method are missing, making the results difficult to interpret.
    *   For the block data experiment, the IBP Gibbs sampler was given a heuristic noise value, whereas the true value was known and used for the FAB method. For a fair comparison, the Gibbs sampler should have also been provided with the true noise variance.
    *   Some experimental results appear questionable. For instance, the result for accelerated Gibbs sampling on the Sonar dataset (not shown in the main paper but mentioned in the key points) finding ~70 features seems highly suspicious and may indicate issues with initialization or parameter settings for that baseline.
    *   There is a minor factual error in parameter settings: the recommended noise standard deviation for real data in related works is typically 0.25 times the data's standard deviation, not 0.75 as stated.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on Limitations:**
    *   The method's effectiveness relies on asymptotic arguments. The paper does not explore or discuss its behavior in small-data regimes where the FIC may be a poor approximation of the marginal likelihood. An empirical evaluation on smaller datasets would be valuable.
    *   The paper does not discuss potential convergence issues. As an EM-like procedure, the algorithm is susceptible to local optima, but this is not mentioned.
    *   The scope of the method is limited to binary latent feature models. It cannot be applied to models with real-valued latent variables like factor analysis or probabilistic PCA. This limitation should be explicitly stated.

3.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of other related work on asymptotic analysis for nonparametric models. In particular, the MAD-Bayes framework (Broderick et al., ICML 2013), which also considers small-variance asymptotics for LFMs, is a highly relevant piece of work that should be cited and discussed.

**Summary of Recommendations:**

This paper presents a novel and potentially impactful method for scalable inference in latent feature models. The theoretical contribution is solid and the proposed algorithm is well-motivated. However, the experimental validation is not rigorous enough to fully support the claims of superiority over state-of-the-art methods.

For the paper to be acceptable for publication, the authors must significantly revise the experimental section to ensure fair and appropriate comparisons with all baselines. This includes using baselines correctly (e.g., MEIBP on non-negative data), providing them with fair parameter settings (e.g., known noise variance), and including sufficient detail for reproducibility. Furthermore, the authors should add a dedicated discussion section addressing the limitations of their approach, including its reliance on asymptotic theory, potential for local optima, and its restriction to binary latent features. Finally, the related work section should be updated to include a discussion of relevant contemporary methods like MAD-Bayes.
