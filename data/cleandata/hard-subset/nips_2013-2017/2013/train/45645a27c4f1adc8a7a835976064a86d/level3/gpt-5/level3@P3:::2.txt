Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) from mixture/HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian likelihood. The core theoretical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian with respect to per-dimension parameters showing that, despite the lack of block-diagonal structure in LFMs, the same FIC penalty terms as in mixtures emerge: a global O(DK log N) term and a per-feature log sum_n z_nk term. This yields an FIC for LFMs whose maximization can be lower-bounded and optimized via a variational procedure.

On the algorithmic side, the authors employ a mean-field approximation over Z, derive fixed-point E-step updates that include the FAB-induced shrinkage term −D/(2N π̃_k), and show an equivalence to a CCCP view that motivates an accelerateShrinkage routine (multiple inner CCCP steps) to remove irrelevant features early. They also prove an identifiability property that encourages balanced activation of duplicate features and propose a merge post-processing step.

Empirically, they compare FAB/LFM against several LFM estimators, including fast Gibbs IBP, finite-K variational IBP, MEIBP (submodular MAP), and EM, on synthetic and real datasets. FAB offers competitive or superior model selection and predictive performance with significantly reduced compute relative to most baselines, while requiring little hand tuning.

This work builds on prior FAB/FIC for mixtures and HMMs and situates itself among nonparametric IBP approaches (Gibbs, VB, MEIBP) and asymptotic alternatives such as MAD-Bayes, clearly articulating differences (e.g., FAB’s asymptotic marginal-likelihood approximation vs. zero-noise limit regularization).

Strengths
- Clear, well-structured paper with precise derivations and algorithms. The CCCP interpretation and algorithmic details are particularly readable.
- Solid and logical extension of FAB/FIC to a broader class: the lemma establishing the key log sum_n z_nk term for LFMs is insightful and widens the scope of FIC.
- Automatic model selection with minimal tuning; the shrinkage term is model-induced rather than prior-driven, and the procedure is practically parameter-free.
- Acceleration via CCCP-based shrinkage is simple and effective, yielding large computational savings without harming final solutions.
- Empirical evaluation is broad and fair: synthetic recovery, block data features, and multiple real datasets; comparisons cover major competing paradigms (IBP sampling, VB, MEIBP, EM). References and positioning are sufficient.
- Identifiability analysis is a nice theoretical addition that motivates an effective merge heuristic.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Combined approximations and their limits are not fully characterized. The approach stacks: (a) asymptotic FIC approximation (Op(1) constants), (b) mean-field factorization for Z, (c) linearization of the log-count penalty, and (d) CCCP-based acceleration. It remains unclear when these may fail or interact adversely.
- Assumptions underlying Lemma 1 are deferred to the supplement; readers lack intuition for regimes where they break (e.g., small N, strong collinearity, near-singular Λ, highly imbalanced activation).
- Failure modes are only briefly acknowledged. For example, small-N behavior, very sparse/very dense features (π near 0 or 1), misspecified diagonal noise, heavy-tailed/outlier noise, and closely overlapping features might induce premature shrinkage or merging errors.
- Experimental fairness: FAB learns noise precision while some baselines fix it; VB is omitted on real data due to runtime; MEIBP performance is described as parameter-sensitive and sometimes initialized with K=100 to finish. These choices should be discussed quantitatively (sensitivity analyses), as they can influence conclusions.
- Initialization and sensitivity: the algorithm starts with K = min(N, D); performance under different initializations and Tshrink settings is not reported. Merge thresholds and their effect are not detailed.

Quality
- Generally technically sound. The derivation is plausible and aligns with past FAB work; monotonic lower-bound improvements are ensured. The CCCP view is correct and useful. Proof assumptions, however, should be more visible in the main text.
- Experimental evidence supports claims of efficiency and competitive accuracy. Additional ablations would strengthen causal claims (e.g., FAB without acceleration; without mean-field; without merge).

Clarity
- Very clearly written and easy to follow. Notation is standard; algorithms are explicit. Suggested improvements: summarize the “mild assumptions” for Lemma 1 in the main text; add a short “when can FIC be inaccurate?” discussion; specify merge criteria/threshold.

Originality
- Extending FAB/FIC to LFMs is a meaningful and nontrivial advance. The CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability-driven merge are well-motivated and, in combination, yield a novel and effective approach. The positioning relative to IBP and MAD-Bayes is clear.

Significance
- High practical significance for LFM practitioners who want automatic model selection without heavy tuning. The speedups and predictive performance gains are valuable; the approach could be adopted in broader latent-variable modeling where similar Hessian structures arise.

Suggestions for authors
- Make the assumptions of Lemma 1 explicit in the main paper and discuss concrete regimes where they may fail.
- Provide a small-N vs. large-N study showing when FIC diverges from marginal likelihood and the impact on selection/prediction.
- Add ablations: (i) no acceleration, (ii) no merge, (iii) no mean-field (if feasible), (iv) fixed vs. learned noise. Report sensitivity to Tshrink, δ, and initialization K.
- Stress tests: highly correlated/duplicate features, π near 0 or 1, non-diagonal or heavy-tailed noise, missing-not-at-random settings.
- Ensure fairness by aligning noise treatment across methods or by reporting both fixed and learned variants.
- Release code and scripts to reproduce the tables and figures.

Reasons to accept
- Strong, well-motivated extension of FAB/FIC with solid theory and practical algorithms.
- Automation and computational efficiency with consistent empirical benefits.
- Clear writing and thorough evaluation across datasets and baselines.

Reasons to reject
- Limited discussion and empirical analysis of failure modes and limits of the combined approximations.
- Some evaluation choices could bias comparisons; missing ablations weaken causal attribution of gains.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a substantive, well-presented contribution that is likely to impact research and practice in latent feature modeling. Strengthening the discussion and experiments around approximation limits would make it even more compelling.
