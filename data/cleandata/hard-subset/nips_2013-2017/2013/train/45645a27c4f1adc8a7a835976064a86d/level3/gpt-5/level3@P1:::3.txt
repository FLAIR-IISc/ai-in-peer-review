This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to linear-Gaussian latent feature models (LFMs) with Bernoulli features. The key theoretical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood showing that the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models: a complexity term of the form (D/2)∑k log ∑n znk arises, despite the lack of block-diagonal structure assumed in earlier FAB work. Building on this, the authors derive an EM-like FAB algorithm with: (i) a mean-field variational E-step yielding a fixed-point update with a data-dependent shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k) that automatically prunes features; (ii) a standard M-step; (iii) a CCCP interpretation enabling a shrinkage-acceleration procedure; and (iv) an identifiability analysis that motivates post-hoc merging of near-duplicate features. Empirically, FAB reportedly matches or outperforms accelerated Gibbs IBP, variational IBP, and the submodular MAP method (MEIBP) on several datasets in terms of model selection, predictive likelihood, and runtime.

Relation to prior work: FAB has previously been developed for mixtures and HMMs; this paper is, to my knowledge, the first to show that the same FIC structure emerges for LFMs by a direct Hessian asymptotic argument. On the nonparametric/LFM side, the paper compares to Gibbs IBP, variational IBP, and MEIBP (submodular MAP). It should also discuss small-variance/mode-seeking asymptotics for LFMs and mixtures (e.g., MAD-Bayes, ICML 2013), which provide an alternative prior-induced regularization view, and clearly distinguish its asymptotics (large N, prior-ignoring) from small-variance limits.

Strengths
- Novel and nontrivial theoretical extension: Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are insightful and technically sound; recovering the same FIC form as mixtures without block-diagonal Hessian assumptions is surprising and important.
- Practical algorithm with provable monotonic lower-bound ascent; the CCCP view is elegant and supports a simple, effective shrinkage acceleration.
- Automatic model selection and improved identifiability: the complexity term induces parsimonious solutions and provides a principled merging heuristic.
- Empirical promise: on several medium/large datasets, FAB attains strong predictive likelihood with compact K and favorable runtimes.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Scope limitation: the method is restricted to binary-feature LFMs with linear-Gaussian likelihoods. It does not apply to real-valued latent factors (factor analysis/PPCA) or non-Gaussian likelihoods. Please clarify the path (if any) to broader feature models (e.g., Poisson, logistic, or continuous features).
- Convergence/local optima: the EM/CCCP procedure can get stuck; the paper lacks a discussion of initialization, restarts, and convergence pathologies. Provide diagnostics, number of restarts, and sensitivity analyses.
- Small-N behavior: since guarantees are asymptotic in N, behavior on small datasets is not analyzed. Include experiments or discussion showing when FAB underperforms IBP samplers/variational methods and how to detect this.
- Experimental fairness:
  - MEIBP requires nonnegative W; several simulations and the block-data comparison appear invalid for this baseline without enforcing W ≥ 0. Either enforce nonnegativity for all relevant methods or omit those comparisons.
  - Noise variance: for fairness, Gibbs/VB baselines should be given the same noise model as FAB. In block-image experiments, they should be run with the known noise level rather than a heuristic. Also, the recommended real-data setting in the cited works is 0.25× the data std-dev, not 0.75; please correct and re-run where needed.
  - Variational IBP: only a finite K variational version is used; please report details (K range, number of restarts, priors) and consider the infinite variational approach where applicable.
  - The accelerated Gibbs result reporting ~70 features on 49-d Sonar is suspect; likely due to poor initialization or mismatched noise variance. Please revisit with multiple seeds, correct noise, and report variance across runs.
  - Reference description: MEIBP (Reed & Ghahramani, ICML 2013) is a MAP/submodular optimization method, not Gibbs; please correct the text and ensure hyperparameters and constraints are matched to its assumptions.
Clarity
- Overall writing is clear and well organized. However, more experimental detail is needed: data preprocessing, splits, hyperparameters, initialization, stopping criteria, and number of restarts per method. Report wall-clock time on identical hardware, and complexity per iteration in terms of N, D, K.
- Clearly state all assumptions required for Lemma 1 (currently deferred to the supplement) and discuss their practical reasonableness.
Originality
- Extending FAB to LFMs with a precise FIC derivation and CCCP-based acceleration is novel relative to prior FAB work on mixtures/HMMs. Please add a discussion of small-variance asymptotics (MAD-Bayes and related) to sharpen the contrast.
Significance
- If the empirical advantages hold under corrected baselines and settings, this is a meaningful advance: an automated, scalable alternative to nonparametric LFMs that avoids tuning priors. The identifiability insight is also useful in practice.

Requested fixes before acceptance
- Correct the MEIBP description and ensure fair, valid comparisons respecting its constraints; rerun affected experiments or remove invalid ones.
- Provide detailed experimental methodology, multiple restarts, and variance across runs; use matched/known noise settings for all baselines.
- Add discussion and citation of small-variance asymptotics (e.g., MAD-Bayes) and clarify how FAB differs in assumptions and outcomes.
- Analyze small-N regimes and convergence behavior; include at least one case where FAB underperforms and explain why.
- Clarify the limitations to binary-feature LFMs and discuss extensions or why they are nontrivial.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical contribution (FIC for LFMs).
- Simple, fast algorithm with automatic feature selection and provable monotone improvement.
- Good empirical performance and compact models on large datasets.
- Useful identifiability analysis and practical merging heuristic.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental methodology has fairness issues and some invalid baseline use; missing details and ablations.
- Limited model scope (binary features, Gaussian likelihood).
- No systematic small-N or convergence analysis.
- Missing/incorrect related-work discussion and reference descriptions.

Overall: promising contribution with solid theory and an attractive algorithm, but experimental evaluation and related-work positioning need significant tightening. I would lean toward acceptance if the authors correct the experimental flaws, fix baseline descriptions/settings, and add the requested analyses; otherwise, I recommend rejection.
