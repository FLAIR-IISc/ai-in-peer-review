This paper extends the factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture and hidden Markov models, to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB inference relies on maximizing a computationally tractable lower bound of a factorized information criterion (FIC), which, in the limit of large sample sizes, converges to the marginal log-likelihood. A key limitation of prior FAB work was its applicability only to models possessing a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete log-likelihood – a condition LFMs do not naturally satisfy. The authors overcome this hurdle by providing an asymptotic analysis demonstrating that the FIC for LFMs retains the same form as that for mixture models, thereby extending the applicability of FAB inference. The paper also introduces a shrinkage acceleration method based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation of the FAB updates, and establishes parameter identifiability for FAB/LFMs. Extensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real datasets showcases the benefits of the proposed approach.

The work builds upon a strong foundation in Bayesian inference and model selection. The connection to previous work on FAB inference [5, 6], Indian Buffet Processes [10, 11], and variational Bayes [3] is clearly articulated. The authors appropriately highlight the computational challenges associated with existing methods for LFM model selection, motivating the need for a more efficient alternative. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, and the demonstration of its structural similarity to the mixture model case, represent a valuable theoretical contribution. Furthermore, the insightful analysis connecting FAB E-steps to the CCCP framework and subsequent acceleration method are well-motivated and empirically validated. The discussion around parameter identifiability and the proposed merging post-processing step adds further depth to the work.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The technical soundness is high; the derivations appear rigorous and the arguments are logically presented. The authors are careful to address potential limitations and provide supporting theoretical analysis. The experimental section is comprehensive, encompassing both synthetic data for controlled evaluation and real-world datasets to demonstrate practical utility. The comparison against strong baselines – fast Gibbs sampling for IBP, VB, and MEIBP – provides a compelling case for the advantages of the proposed FAB/LFM approach, particularly regarding computational efficiency and predictive performance. The writing is clear and well-organized, and the paper is adequately informative, though the supplementary material will undoubtedly be necessary for a complete understanding of the detailed derivations.

However, there are a few areas that could be improved. While the theoretical contribution is solid, the work feels somewhat incremental. The FIC framework itself isn't novel, and the extension to LFMs, while non-trivial, primarily involves demonstrating that the FIC retains a familiar form. A deeper exploration of *why* this form is preserved, beyond the asymptotic analysis of the Hessian, would be beneficial. The performance gains demonstrated in the experiments, while positive, aren’t always dramatic. A more thorough ablation study, specifically isolating the impact of the shrinkage acceleration and merge post-processing steps, would strengthen the empirical evaluation. Additionally, the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the noise standard deviation in the real data experiments (fixed at 0.75) could be discussed further. A brief discussion of the assumptions underlying Lemma 1 and their potential impact on the applicability of the method would also be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Extends a promising Bayesian inference framework (FAB) to a new class of models (LFMs).
*   Provides a theoretically sound justification for applying FAB to LFMs despite the violation of the original Hessian condition.
*   Demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Introduces a computationally efficient acceleration method based on CCCP.
*   Addresses parameter identifiability and proposes a post-processing step to improve model quality.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and technically sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The contribution, while valuable, is somewhat incremental.
*   Performance gains are not consistently overwhelming.
*   Ablation studies and sensitivity analysis could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the incremental nature of the contribution, this paper represents a solid and well-executed piece of work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the results are promising. The extension of FAB inference to LFMs addresses a practical limitation of the original framework and offers a valuable tool for researchers working in this area. I believe this paper is a suitable contribution to NIPS and recommend acceptance. A minor revision addressing the points raised above (e.g., deeper discussion of Lemma 1 assumptions, more detailed ablation studies) would further strengthen the manuscript.
