

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant gap in the applicability of FAB to more complex latent variable models. Previous FAB work [5,6] was limited to models satisfying a specific Hessian condition, which LFMs do not inherently satisfy. The authors make a key theoretical contribution by showing through asymptotic analysis that LFMs can still leverage FAB inference because their factorized information criterion (FIC) has the same form as for mixture models (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2), despite LFMs lacking the block-diagonal Hessian structure assumed in prior work. This is a significant theoretical insight that broadens the applicability of FIC/FAB methods.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it proposes a novel FAB/LFM algorithm that automatically selects the number of latent features (K) without requiring hand-tuned hyperparameters, a major advantage over existing non-parametric Bayesian (NPB) methods like Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [10,11] and variational Bayesian (VB) approaches [3]. The FAB/LFM framework achieves automatic feature selection through a "model-induced regularization" effect, where irrelevant features shrink to zero during optimization. Second, the authors introduce a clever shrinkage acceleration technique using convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation, which significantly reduces computational costs by eliminating irrelevant features earlier in the optimization process (Algorithm 2). Third, they provide a theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability for LFMs (Theorem 4), which leads to a natural merge post-processing step that improves model compactness.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, comparing FAB/LFM against multiple strong baselines including IBP [2], VB [3], and MEIBP [22] on synthetic and real-world datasets. The results demonstrate clear computational advantages (Figures 1-2), superior model selection accuracy (especially for larger N), and better predictive performance (Table 1). Notably, FAB/LFM achieves competitive predictive performance (PLL) with significantly lower computational costs, and consistently selects more compact models (smaller K) than alternatives. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their merge post-processing step in improving model representation.

The paper is well-written, logically structured, and makes a solid theoretical contribution that builds upon and extends prior FAB work [5,6]. The connection between FAB/LFM and the CCCP framework provides valuable theoretical insights that explain the algorithm's behavior. The related work section properly contextualizes the contribution within the NIPS literature, particularly noting the distinction between FAB and other approaches like MAD-Bayes [1].

The paper's primary weakness is the lack of detailed analysis of the method's failure modes and limitations. While the authors show strong empirical results, they do not systematically investigate scenarios where the FAB/LFM approach might fail or where the assumptions (e.g., asymptotic behavior, specific Hessian properties) might not hold. The paper states that "FAB/LFMs have several desirable properties" but doesn't provide sufficient discussion of when these properties might not hold. For instance, it would be valuable to examine cases with strong feature correlations, small sample sizes (N), or non-Gaussian noise structures where the asymptotic approximation might be less accurate. The authors acknowledge that for small N, FAB might perform worse than NPB methods (as observed in Libras dataset), but they don't provide a more systematic analysis of the regime where the asymptotic assumptions break down.

The paper also lacks a detailed discussion of the practical sensitivity of the algorithm to initialization, particularly since the authors note that the initial number of latent features is set to min(N,D). It would be helpful to understand how sensitive the final model selection is to this initial choice.

In terms of significance, this work advances the state of the art in model selection for latent feature models by providing a computationally efficient method that requires minimal hand-tuning. The automatic model selection capability addresses a key practical limitation of current NPB methods. The theoretical insights about FIC for LFMs also contribute to our broader understanding of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field. The extension of FAB to LFMs is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The computational efficiency gains are particularly valuable for real-world applications with large-scale data. While the failure modes analysis could be strengthened, the paper's strengths in theoretical contribution, empirical validation, and clear writing make it a compelling contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations of the asymptotic assumptions.
