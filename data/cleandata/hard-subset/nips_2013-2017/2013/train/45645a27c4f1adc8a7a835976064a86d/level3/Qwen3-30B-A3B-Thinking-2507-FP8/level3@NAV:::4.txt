

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a key limitation in previous FAB applications that required a block-diagonal Hessian matrix condition not satisfied by LFMs. The authors derive a lower bound of the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) applicable to LFMs, demonstrating that FIC for LFMs has the same representation as for mixture models despite the Hessian condition violation. The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by proving that LFMs' FIC converges to marginal log-likelihood, and introduces a shrinkage acceleration method that significantly reduces computational cost while maintaining predictive performance.

The paper excels in technical quality (Quality criterion) with rigorous asymptotic analysis and thorough derivations. The authors carefully address both theoretical properties (e.g., parameter identifiability) and practical implementation (e.g., the CCCP interpretation and merge post-processing). The empirical evaluation on synthetic and real datasets thoroughly compares FAB/LFM against fast Gibbs sampling, variational Bayes, and MEIBP, demonstrating superior predictive performance and lower computational costs. The paper is exceptionally well-written (Clarity criterion), with clear notation and logical flow that would enable replication.

The contribution (Originality criterion) is somewhat incremental but highly relevant to the NIPS community, as it expands FAB's applicability to an important class of models (LFMs) that have seen growing interest in Bayesian nonparametric methods. The significance (Significance criterion) is strong as FAB/LFM offers automated model selection without hyperparameters, addressing a key challenge in LFMs where dimensionality selection is often computationally expensive.

The paper's minor weakness is the incremental nature of the contribution, though this is offset by the elegant theoretical insight that LFMs share the same FIC representation as mixture models despite different structural assumptions. The authors properly acknowledge related work, including the Indian Buffet Process and previous FAB applications.

Overall, this is a high-quality, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The theoretical insights are well-supported by empirical results, and the practical implications for scalable latent feature modeling are significant. I strongly support acceptance as it advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection for latent variable models.
