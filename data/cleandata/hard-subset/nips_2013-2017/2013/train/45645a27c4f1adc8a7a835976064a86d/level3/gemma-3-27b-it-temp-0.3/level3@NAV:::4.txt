## Review of “Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models”

This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference – previously limited to models with a block-diagonal Hessian – to the more general class of Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core contribution lies in deriving a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs that, despite violating the original Hessian condition, maintains the same form as the FIC for mixture models. The authors demonstrate the theoretical properties of this new FIC, including convergence to the marginal log-likelihood, automatic hidden state selection, and parameter identifiability. Empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets shows that FAB/LFM outperforms competitors like fast Gibbs sampling for IBP, variational Bayes, and MEIBP in terms of predictive performance and computational efficiency.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The derivations are thorough and clearly presented, making the complex mathematical arguments accessible. The authors carefully address the limitations of previous FAB approaches and convincingly demonstrate how their work overcomes these hurdles. The inclusion of a shrinkage acceleration method, inspired by a connection to the Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), further enhances the practical utility of the proposed method. The experimental setup is comprehensive, comparing FAB/LFM against a strong set of baselines on diverse datasets. The results consistently favor FAB/LFM, supporting the claims of improved performance and efficiency. 

While the contribution is somewhat incremental – extending an existing framework rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach – it is nonetheless interesting and highly relevant to the NIPS community. The extension of FAB to LFMs broadens the applicability of this promising inference technique, and the theoretical insights provided regarding the FIC and parameter identifiability are valuable. The demonstrated improvements in both computational cost and predictive accuracy are significant. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and clear derivations.
*   Empirically validated improvements over existing methods.
*   Addresses a practical limitation of FAB inference.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Relevant to the NIPS audience interested in Bayesian inference and latent variable models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental rather than revolutionary.
*   The experimental results, while positive, do not represent a dramatic leap in performance.



Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a solid and valuable contribution to the field, addressing a specific limitation of an existing technique and demonstrating its improved performance in a rigorous and well-documented manner.
