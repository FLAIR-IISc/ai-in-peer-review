## Review of “Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models”

This paper presents a novel extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The authors address the challenge of efficient inference and automatic model selection (determining the number of latent features, K) in LFMs, a problem traditionally tackled by computationally expensive nonparametric Bayesian (NPB) methods like IBP. They demonstrate that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs has a form similar to that of Mixture Models, enabling the application of FAB inference. The core contribution lies in adapting FAB to a setting where its initial assumptions don’t directly hold, and demonstrating its practical benefits in terms of runtime and inference quality on several datasets.

The technical approach appears sound, and the algorithmic details are clearly presented. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, and the subsequent development of the FAB algorithm, are well-motivated and logically structured. The inclusion of a shrinkage acceleration step and a merging post-processing procedure further enhances the method’s efficiency and robustness. The experimental results demonstrate that FAB outperforms Gibbs sampling, variational methods, and MEIBP on some datasets, particularly in terms of runtime. The ability to automatically infer K is a significant advantage.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge limitations, the discussion is insufficient. There’s a lack of analysis regarding scenarios where FAB might underperform compared to, for example, Gibbs sampling, or potential convergence difficulties of the EM-like procedure. The method’s restriction to binary LFMs is a significant limitation not fully addressed. The experimental evaluation, while promising, is flawed. The MEIBP baseline is inappropriately used due to its non-negativity constraint, and the IBP Gibbs sampler isn’t given a fair comparison in the block image experiments. Furthermore, the VB-IBP experiments lack crucial details, and the reported results for the accelerated Gibbs sampler on the Sonar data are suspect. 

Several recent related works, such as MAD-Bayes, are missing from the discussion. Additionally, there are minor inaccuracies in the description of reference [21] and the recommended standard deviation setting. The authors’ response to feedback did not fully address concerns regarding experimental methodology and baseline justification.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel application of FAB to LFMs, promising empirical results on some datasets, efficient inference and automatic K selection.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Insufficient discussion of limitations, flawed experimental evaluation, missing related work, minor inaccuracies.

**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but requires significant revisions to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered for acceptance. Specifically, the experimental evaluation needs to be thoroughly revised, missing related work must be included, and a more comprehensive discussion of limitations is necessary.
