This paper presents an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB inference is a model selection method that maximizes a lower bound on the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. A key limitation of previous FAB methods, developed for mixture and hidden Markov models, was the requirement that the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood be block-diagonal. LFMs violate this condition due to parameters being shared across all latent features.

The central contribution of this work is a novel asymptotic analysis of the LFM's Hessian matrix. The authors demonstrate, via Lemma 1, that despite the violation of the block-diagonality condition, the log determinant of the Hessian yields a model complexity term identical in form to that found in mixture models. This insight allows them to derive an FIC for LFMs that has the same representation as FIC for mixture models, thereby making the FAB framework applicable. The resulting FAB/LFM algorithm provides automatic model selection (i.e., determines the number of latent features) and is shown to have desirable properties like parameter identifiability. The authors also introduce a shrinkage acceleration technique, derived from a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation of the E-step, which significantly improves computational efficiency. The method is empirically validated against strong baselines from the non-parametric Bayesian literature, including fast Gibbs sampling for the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), Variational Bayes for IBP, and Maximum-Expectation IBP (MEIBP), showing superior predictive performance and lower computational cost on several datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The core theoretical result (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is a non-trivial extension of the FAB framework and appears to be sound. The claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world data. The authors are careful to compare against relevant state-of-the-art methods and demonstrate consistent improvements in predictive log-likelihood, model compactness (selected K), and computational time. The inclusion of an ablation-style study showing the benefit of the shrinkage acceleration (Figure 1) strengthens the paper's practical claims. The authors are also honest about the limitations of the asymptotic approach, noting that performance may degrade in small-N scenarios, as observed in one of the experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts by clearly motivating the problem and situating the work within the context of previous FAB and LFM literature. The technical derivations are presented logically, and the key insight—that the `log(sum(z_nk))` regularization term naturally emerges from the LFM Hessian—is highlighted effectively. The algorithmic description is clear, and the connection between the E-step update and CCCP provides valuable insight into the optimization procedure. The experimental setup and results are presented transparently, making the work easy to understand and assess.

**Originality:** The work is an extension of the existing FAB framework, but the contribution is novel and significant. Applying FAB to models that do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption was an open problem. The paper's main originality lies in the technical derivation that overcomes this obstacle for LFMs. This finding suggests that the FIC representation may be more broadly applicable than previously thought. The proposed shrinkage acceleration technique and the analysis of parameter identifiability, which motivates a practical merging post-processing step, are also novel contributions that enhance the overall method.

**Significance:** This work is highly significant for researchers working on Bayesian inference and latent variable models. It provides a scalable and highly effective method for performing model selection in LFMs, a widely used model class. The FAB/LFM algorithm presents a compelling alternative to non-parametric Bayesian approaches like the IBP, which are often computationally demanding. The empirical results demonstrate that FAB/LFM can achieve better predictive performance with a more compact model representation and at a fraction of the computational cost of competing methods. This makes it a practical and attractive tool for a wide range of applications. The theoretical insights may also spur further research into applying asymptotic Bayesian methods to other complex models.

### Summary of Review

This paper successfully extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) framework to Latent Feature Models, which was not previously possible due to structural properties of the model's likelihood. The authors provide a solid theoretical derivation showing that the resulting Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes on the same elegant form as in mixture models, leading to a "model-induced regularization" effect that automatically prunes irrelevant features. The proposed algorithm is efficient, practical, and demonstrates strong empirical performance against established baselines.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and technically sound solution to extend the applicability of FAB inference to the important class of Latent Feature Models.
*   The resulting FAB/LFM algorithm is computationally efficient and empirically outperforms strong baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) in predictive accuracy and model selection.
*   The paper is very clearly written, with thorough derivations and a well-executed experimental section.
*   The work is of high significance to the community, offering a practical and powerful tool for inference in LFMs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The contribution could be viewed as somewhat incremental, as it extends an existing inference framework rather than proposing a new one. However, the extension itself is non-trivial and impactful.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution. The technical novelty, practical utility, and clear presentation make it an excellent fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
