

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a gap in FAB's applicability to models that don't satisfy the Hessian block-diagonal condition required for prior FAB work. The authors demonstrate that LFMs share the same FIC structure as mixture models despite lacking this condition, deriving a novel FAB/LFM algorithm with automatic feature selection and parameter identifiability. The work builds on prior FAB frameworks for mixture models [5,6] and extends them to LFMs, showing empirical improvements in predictive likelihood and computational efficiency over IBP implementations.

Strengths include a theoretically sound asymptotic argument for the FIC derivation (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2), the practical benefit of automatic model selection without hyperparameters, and compelling experimental results showing FAB/LFM's superior predictive performance and faster convergence. The shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) is particularly valuable for large-scale applications.

However, significant weaknesses undermine the paper's technical contribution. The mathematical exposition contains imprecisions: Equation (3) is problematic when all z·k=0 (raising concerns about FIC validity), and Theorem 2 lacks specification of the asymptotic regime (particularly how K scales with N), conflicting with IBP's infinite-K motivation. Section 2's pk(X|z·,k) should condition only on data assigned to component k, and Equation (4) misses an expectation. Experimental comparisons suffer from unspecified stopping criteria (Table 1), making runtime comparisons arbitrary, and it's unclear whether predictive improvements stem from the model-selection criterion or better local optima avoidance (Gibbs' tendency to learn too many components suggests implementation issues).

While the approach is reasonable and motivated, the technical contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The paper would benefit from clearer theoretical assumptions, more precise mathematical exposition, and better experimental controls to isolate the source of performance gains. The significance is limited by these technical shortcomings, though the method is practically useful for LFM applications.

Overall, this is a well-executed incremental advance with solid experimental results, but the mathematical imprecisions and insufficient theoretical justification prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. I recommend rejection (weak reject) due to insufficient novelty and technical rigor.
