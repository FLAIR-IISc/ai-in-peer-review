This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to binary latent feature models, proposing a novel model-selection criterion. The central idea is to derive a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) by integrating out model parameters using a Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) approximation. This results in an efficient EM-like algorithm with an inherent complexity penalty that automatically determines the number of active latent features. This work is a direct extension of prior FAB research on mixture models [5, 6] and presents a deterministic alternative to non-parametric stochastic methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

The main strength of the paper is its well-motivated and practical approach. The method provides an elegant, principled way to perform model selection in latent feature models, and the resulting algorithm is empirically strong. The experiments demonstrate compelling improvements in held-out predictive likelihoods and significantly faster runtimes when compared to several IBP implementations. The ability to automatically prune irrelevant features within an optimization framework is a valuable contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that temper its impact. First, the technical contribution is incremental, being a fairly direct application of the existing FAB framework to a new model class. More importantly, the mathematical exposition contains imprecisions that undermine the theoretical claims. For example, the definition of FIC in equation (3) is potentially ill-defined if a feature is never used. A crucial omission is in Theorem 2, which lacks a clear specification of the asymptotic regime; in particular, it is unclear how the number of features K is assumed to scale with the data size N. The BIC-like derivation relies on an assumption of fixed K, which is fundamentally at odds with the infinite-feature motivation of the IBP models against which the method is benchmarked.

The experimental validation is also inconclusive. The claims of faster convergence (Table 1) are difficult to interpret, as the stopping criteria for the baseline methods are not specified, making the truncation of Gibbs samplers seem arbitrary. Furthermore, it is not clear whether the improved predictive performance stems from the superiority of the FIC itself or from the deterministic algorithm simply avoiding the poor local optima to which Gibbs samplers can be prone. In summary, while the paper presents a sensible and promising method, its contribution is limited by its incremental nature, imprecise mathematical formalism, and an experimental analysis that is not rigorous enough to fully support its claims.
