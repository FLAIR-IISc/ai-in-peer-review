

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously applicable only to mixture models and HMMs, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The authors address a key limitation in the existing FAB framework: it required the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood to be block-diagonal, a condition not satisfied by LFMs. Through careful asymptotic analysis, they demonstrate that LFMs still yield FICs with the same form as mixture models, despite not meeting the original Hessian condition. This theoretical insight enables them to develop FAB/LFMs, which offer automatic feature selection, parameter identifiability, and computational advantages over existing methods.

The paper builds upon Fujimaki's earlier work on FAB for mixture models [5, 6] and extends it to the more complex LFM setting. While LFMs have been studied using non-parametric Bayesian methods (e.g., Indian Buffet Process [10, 11]) and variational Bayes [3], these approaches often suffer from high computational costs. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation showing that FAB can be successfully applied to LFMs, with Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 being the key theoretical contributions. They also introduce a practical acceleration technique based on CCCP interpretation that significantly reduces computational cost.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with thorough theoretical derivations and empirical validation. The asymptotic analysis is well-motivated and carefully presented, with supplementary materials providing rigorous proofs. The experimental results consistently demonstrate FAB/LFM's superiority over state-of-the-art methods in model selection accuracy, prediction performance, and computational efficiency across synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors responsibly acknowledge limitations (e.g., the FIC approximation may be less accurate for small N).

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The mathematical derivations are well-organized, with clear notation and logical flow. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the experimental setup is well-documented. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics and parameter settings. The writing is accessible to NIPS readers familiar with latent variable models.

The originality of the work is moderate but significant. While the extension of FAB to LFMs builds upon prior work, the theoretical insight that LFMs yield FICs with the same form as mixture models despite not meeting the original Hessian condition is novel. The CCCP interpretation and shrinkage acceleration method represent valuable practical contributions. The paper carefully contextualizes its contribution against related work, particularly noting how it differs from MAD-Bayes [1] and maximum-expectation IBP [22].

The significance of this work is substantial. LFMs are widely used in applications like link prediction [16], and the computational bottleneck has limited their scalability. FAB/LFM addresses this by providing a method that automatically selects model complexity with lower computational cost than existing approaches. The paper demonstrates clear advantages in both model selection and predictive performance across multiple datasets, suggesting practical utility for practitioners. The parameter identifiability property is particularly valuable for ensuring reliable model interpretation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a theoretically sound extension of FAB to LFMs despite initial theoretical limitations
- Derives a practical algorithm with rigorous theoretical foundation
- Demonstrates superior performance in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency
- Offers novel insights into automatic feature selection and parameter identifiability
- Well-written with thorough experimental validation

Arguments against acceptance:
- The core contribution is somewhat incremental (extending FAB to a new class of models)
- Limited comparison to some state-of-the-art methods (VB was omitted due to computational time)
- The FIC approximation may be less accurate for small N, though this is acknowledged

Overall, this is a strong NIPS paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. The theoretical insights are sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. The authors successfully bridge a theoretical gap in the FAB framework while providing a practical algorithm with demonstrable advantages. The work is relevant to the NIPS community and advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference for latent feature models.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's contribution is significant enough to warrant publication, and its technical soundness and clarity make it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
