This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a limitation of prior FAB work which required a specific block-diagonal structure in the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite lacking this structure, exhibit a similar form in their factorized information criterion (FIC), allowing for the application of FAB. The resulting FAB/LFMs offer automated model selection (dimensionality of latent features), parameter identifiability, and promising empirical performance, outperforming state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods in terms of model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.

This work builds upon the recent advancements in FAB inference [5, 6], which provides a computationally efficient alternative to traditional Bayesian methods for model selection. While previous FAB implementations were restricted to models like mixture models, this paper significantly broadens the applicability of the framework to a more general class of models – LFMs – commonly used in diverse applications like link prediction and dimensionality reduction [9, 17]. The core contribution lies in demonstrating the applicability of FAB to LFMs through an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix and deriving the corresponding FIC.  Beyond the theoretical contribution, the authors propose a shrinkage acceleration method, inspired by a connection to the convex-concave procedure (CCCP) [29], to further improve computational efficiency and a merging post-processing step that leverages the parameter identifiability properties.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The extension of FAB to LFMs is a conceptually sound and technically well-motivated idea. The theoretical analysis, particularly Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, provides a rigorous justification for applying FAB to this new class of models. The introduction of the shrinkage acceleration and merging post-processing steps are valuable additions that address practical considerations and further enhance the algorithm's performance.  The empirical results, while needing further clarification (see weaknesses below), demonstrate the potential of FAB/LFMs to outperform existing methods in terms of both computational speed and accuracy. The emphasis on automated model selection and the resulting parsimony are also noteworthy benefits. The discussion of parameter identifiability and its implications for post-processing is a strong theoretical contribution.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail and contains inconsistencies. While comparisons are made with several relevant baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP), the experimental setup and hyperparameter choices are not thoroughly explained or justified, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison.  Specifically, the use of MEIBP, which inherently assumes non-negative weights, in experiments with datasets containing negative values introduces a significant confounding factor. The claim of accelerated Gibbs finding ~70 features on Sonar data seems questionable and requires more scrutiny, potentially indicating issues with initialization or noise variance settings. The reference to [21] is inaccurate and needs correction. Furthermore, the paper does not adequately address potential convergence difficulties for the EM-like optimization procedure employed, and the algorithmic limitations (e.g., restricted to binary latent features) are not thoroughly discussed.  The lack of exploration of behavior in small-data regimes, where the asymptotic assumptions may not hold, is also a concern. The omission of recent relevant work, such as MAD-Bayes [1] and other small-variance asymptotic analyses for nonparametric LFMs, diminishes the comprehensiveness of the related work discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of FAB inference to a new and important class of models (LFMs).
*   Sound theoretical analysis justifying the use of FAB for LFMs.
*   Practical algorithmic improvements (shrinkage acceleration and merging post-processing).
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating potential performance gains over existing methods.
*   Addresses the important problem of efficient and automated model selection in LFMs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently detailed and flawed experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of discussion of algorithmic limitations and potential failure modes.
*   Omission of relevant related work.
*   Inaccuracies in reference descriptions.
*   Potential convergence issues with the EM-like optimization procedure not addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of latent feature modeling. The theoretical work is solid, and the proposed algorithm has the potential to be a powerful tool for model selection and inference. However, the weaknesses in the experimental evaluation and the lack of discussion regarding limitations raise concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **conditional acceptance**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by providing a more detailed and rigorous experimental evaluation, correcting the inaccuracies in the references, including the missing related work, and discussing the potential limitations of the proposed method. Specifically, clarifying the experimental setup, justifying hyperparameter choices, and performing a more thorough comparison with relevant baselines are crucial.  Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and justify its inclusion in NIPS.
