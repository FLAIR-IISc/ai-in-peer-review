### Summary

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously applied to mixture models, to the setting of Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core idea is to derive a model selection criterion, termed the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), by applying an asymptotic approximation (akin to BIC) to the marginal log-likelihood over both observed and latent variables. The key technical contribution is showing that even though LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption required in prior FAB work, a similar FIC can be derived. This FIC includes a complexity penalty term for each feature that depends on the log of the number of data points assigned to it (`log(sum_n z_nk)`). The authors then derive a tractable lower bound on this FIC and propose an EM-like algorithm for its optimization. This algorithm automatically prunes inactive features, thus performing model selection. The paper also introduces an acceleration technique based on a CCCP interpretation of the updates. The proposed FAB/LFM method is empirically compared to several Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations, demonstrating superior performance in terms of predictive likelihood, model compactness, and computational speed on several datasets.

### Quality

The paper presents a technically interesting and plausible approach. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is the main contribution and appears reasonable. However, the technical presentation has some weaknesses that undermine the paper's soundness.

-   The central asymptotic argument in Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 is not presented with sufficient rigor. The assumptions under which these results hold are relegated to the supplementary material, but their implications are crucial. For instance, standard BIC-style approximations assume a fixed model dimension (K) as the number of samples (N) grows. It is unclear if the authors' derivation holds under different regimes (e.g., K growing with N), which would be important for a method positioned as an alternative to infinite-capacity models like the IBP.
-   There are points of imprecision in the mathematical exposition. For example, the FIC objective in Eq. (3) includes the term `Eq[log(sum_n z_nk)]`. If a feature `k` is unused across all data points, the argument of the logarithm is zero, making the objective ill-defined. While the optimization procedure may avoid this, the formulation itself requires clarification.
-   The experimental evaluation, while showing promising results, is not entirely convincing. The comparison of runtimes is difficult to interpret without explicit details on the convergence criteria used for FAB/EM (`delta=10^-4` is mentioned but its sensitivity is not discussed) and the number of iterations run for the IBP Gibbs sampler. Truncating an MCMC sampler is not equivalent to an optimization algorithm converging, making direct time comparisons problematic. It is also unclear whether the performance gains are due to the superiority of the FIC criterion or because the EM-like optimization avoids poor local optima that the compared MCMC samplers may have struggled with. The observation that IBP finds too many features could also be a consequence of hyperparameter choices or specific sampler implementation details.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the overall algorithm is explained logically. The connection to previous FAB work is established, and the proposed extensions for LFMs are highlighted. However, the clarity could be improved in several areas:

-   As mentioned under Quality, the formal statements of the main theoretical results (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) are not precise enough in the main text. Key assumptions and the exact asymptotic regime should be specified for the reader to fully assess the claims.
-   There are minor notational inconsistencies. For example, in the discussion of FIC for MMs in Section 1.1, `pk(X|z·k)` seems to imply conditioning on the entire dataset X, whereas it should likely condition only on the subset of data associated with component k. Similarly, the lower bound in Equation (4) appears to be missing an expectation `Eq[]` around the final term in the parenthesis. These small issues, while not critical, detract from the paper's polish.

### Originality

The work is a novel application and extension of the FAB framework. While the core idea of using asymptotic approximations for the marginal likelihood is not new (e.g., BIC), and the FAB framework itself has been previously published by the authors for other models [5, 6], its derivation for LFMs is non-trivial and original. The authors successfully show how to handle a model structure that violates the assumptions of their prior work. This positions FAB as a potentially general methodology for model selection in latent variable models, competing with non-parametric Bayesian approaches. The shrinkage acceleration technique and the identifiability analysis are also nice, original contributions that enhance the practicality of the method. The contribution can be seen as somewhat incremental, but it is a technically meaningful step that broadens the applicability of the FAB framework.

### Significance

The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of determining the number of latent features in LFMs. Current state-of-the-art methods are often based on the IBP, which can be computationally expensive and sensitive to hyperparameters. The proposed FAB/LFM offers a compelling alternative that is deterministic, computationally efficient, and free of tunable hyperparameters. The empirical results suggest that the method is not only faster but can also lead to more compact models with better predictive performance. If the method proves to be robust, it is likely to be used by practitioners and could inspire further research into asymptotic methods for model selection as a practical alternative to non-parametric Bayes. The analysis connecting the FAB updates to CCCP and the discussion on identifiability are also of broader interest.

### Arguments for acceptance:

1.  The paper tackles a significant problem: automatic model selection for latent feature models.
2.  It proposes a novel extension of the FAB framework to LFMs, which is technically non-trivial as it required circumventing the block-diagonal Hessian assumption from prior work.
3.  The resulting algorithm is practical, efficient, and demonstrates strong empirical performance against established IBP-based methods.
4.  The method provides a valuable, alternative perspective on model selection that is deterministic and avoids the complexities of MCMC-based inference.

### Arguments against acceptance:

1.  The contribution is somewhat incremental, building heavily on the authors' previous work on FAB.
2.  The theoretical justification for the core asymptotic approximation lacks precision in the main text, particularly regarding the assumptions on how model complexity (K) scales with data size (N).
3.  The experimental comparison, while promising, is not fully rigorous in its comparison of runtimes and its ability to disentangle the effects of the objective function versus the optimization algorithm.

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a sensible and useful method. While the mathematical rigor and experimental analysis could be strengthened, the novelty of extending FAB to LFMs and the strong empirical results make a good case for its acceptance.
