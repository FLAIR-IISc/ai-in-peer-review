

Summary: This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a significant advancement since previous FAB work was limited to models satisfying a specific Hessian condition (not met by LFMs). The authors demonstrate that despite this condition not being satisfied, the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models. The approach leverages a mean-field approximation for latent variable inference and introduces a shrinkage acceleration method that dramatically reduces computational cost. The paper makes a compelling case that FAB/LFMs offer automatic hidden state selection, parameter identifiability, and superior performance in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods like Indian Buffet Processes.

Quality: The paper presents thorough theoretical analysis, including rigorous proofs for the key lemmas and theorems in the supplementary materials. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) across synthetic and real-world datasets with respect to computational speed, model selection accuracy, and predictive performance. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach by noting that FIC approximation may be less accurate for small N, which is reflected in the experimental results. The evaluation metrics (computational time, selected K, predictive log-likelihood, training log-likelihood) are well-chosen and appropriate for the problem.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written. The authors effectively motivate their approach by carefully explaining the limitations of previous FAB work and demonstrating how their extension overcomes these limitations. The technical details are presented logically, with clear definitions and notation. The experimental results are well-presented and easy to interpret, with Figure 1 particularly effective in illustrating the computational benefits of the shrinkage acceleration method. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Originality: The core contribution is the theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs, which is non-trivial given that LFMs do not satisfy the Hessian condition required for previous FAB work. The authors demonstrate that the FIC for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models, revealing a broader applicability of the FIC framework. The shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation is also a novel contribution that significantly improves computational efficiency. This represents a meaningful generalization of the FAB framework rather than a simple application of existing techniques.

Significance: The paper addresses a practically important problem in latent feature modeling—automatically selecting the number of latent features without manual tuning. The proposed FAB/LFM method outperforms state-of-the-art alternatives in computational efficiency while achieving superior or comparable model selection and prediction performance. The automatic feature selection capability is particularly valuable for large-scale applications where manual tuning of K would be impractical. The parameter identifiability result (Theorem 4) provides a theoretical foundation for the method's success and offers a natural guide for post-processing latent features.

Strengths:
- Theoretical novelty in extending FAB to LFMs despite the Hessian condition
- Rigorous proofs for key theoretical contributions
- Practical computational improvements through shrinkage acceleration
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets
- Automatic model selection without hyperparameters
- Parameter identifiability result that addresses an important theoretical concern

Weaknesses:
- The paper could better address the theoretical limitations of the FIC approximation for small N, though this is acknowledged in the results (noted in Libras case).
- While the paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness, it would be valuable to include a more detailed analysis of when the method might fail or perform suboptimally, particularly regarding the assumptions underlying the Hessian approximation in Lemma 1.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of latent variable models. The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is significant and well-supported, the experimental results are compelling, and the method offers practical advantages in terms of computational efficiency and automatic model selection. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and represents a meaningful advancement over previous work in model selection for latent feature models. I recommend acceptance.
