This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB is a recently developed method for model selection that has demonstrated strong performance for mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models. The method works by maximizing a computationally tractable lower bound of a factorized information criterion (FIC), which converges to the marginal log-likelihood in the large sample limit. A key limitation of the original FAB framework is its reliance on the assumption that the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal, a condition that LFMs do not satisfy.

The main contribution of this work is to overcome this limitation. The authors conduct an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian for LFMs and show, in their Lemma 1, that the resulting FIC for LFMs has the same functional form as the FIC for MMs. This is a non-trivial finding that enables the application of the FAB framework to this important class of models. Building on this theoretical result, the paper develops a complete algorithm (FAB/LFM), introduces a shrinkage acceleration technique derived from a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation, and analyzes the parameter identifiability of the method. The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing FAB/LFM against state-of-the-art methods, including those based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), such as fast Gibbs sampling, Variational Bayes (VB), and the maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP). The results demonstrate that the proposed method often achieves superior performance in model selection and prediction, typically with a lower computational cost.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to a broader and more complex class of models (LFMs) that do not meet the original block-diagonal Hessian assumption. The derivation showing that the FIC for LFMs preserves the same form as for MMs is elegant and impactful, suggesting wider applicability of the FIC framework.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing. FAB/LFM is shown to outperform or be competitive with several state-of-the-art IBP-based methods. Notably, it demonstrates better predictive log-likelihoods and more compact model representations (a smaller number of selected features, K) while being computationally more efficient in most cases. The smaller gap between training and predictive likelihoods also suggests that the method is less prone to overfitting.

3.  **Practical Algorithmic Refinements:** The paper introduces valuable practical improvements. The CCCP interpretation of the E-step is insightful and leads to an effective acceleration technique for shrinking the model, which Figure 1 shows significantly reduces computation time. Furthermore, the analysis of parameter identifiability provides theoretical justification for a post-processing step to merge redundant features, which is a useful and principled addition.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the technical derivations are thoroughly explained. The authors effectively guide the reader from the theoretical underpinnings of FAB to the specific challenges of LFMs and their proposed solutions.

### Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement

The primary contribution is the extension of an existing method (FAB) to a new model class. While the technical work required for this extension is substantial and the result is not obvious, the work could be viewed as an incremental, though highly valuable, advancement of the FAB framework rather than the introduction of a completely new paradigm.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims, particularly Lemma 1 and Theorems 2 and 4, appear well-supported by the proofs provided in the supplementary material. The experimental methodology is solid, and the comparisons to relevant baselines are fair. The authors are also careful to note a case in a small-sample scenario (Libras dataset) where their method is outperformed, which speaks to a complete and honest evaluation.

*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally clear. The motivation is well-established, the theoretical development is easy to follow, and the proposed algorithm is clearly detailed. The connection between the FIC penalty term and the automatic shrinkage of features is explained particularly well.

*   **Originality:** The work is original in its application and extension of FAB inference. While it builds directly on previous work on FAB, the analysis required to apply it to LFMs is novel and overcomes a significant hurdle. The finding that the model complexity term `log(sum_n z_nk)` emerges naturally despite the shared parameters in LFMs is a key insight. The proposed acceleration technique is also a novel contribution.

*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant for researchers and practitioners working with latent feature models. FAB/LFM provides a powerful, computationally efficient, and fully automated alternative to widely-used non-parametric Bayesian techniques for determining model dimensionality. Given its strong performance, the method is likely to be adopted by others and could inspire further extensions of asymptotic Bayesian methods to other complex models.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper that presents a theoretically sound and practically useful extension of factorized asymptotic Bayesian inference. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method, FAB/LFM, advances the state of the art in model selection for latent feature models, offering improved accuracy and efficiency over established baselines. The paper is well-written, the ideas are interesting, and the results are compelling. I strongly support its acceptance.
