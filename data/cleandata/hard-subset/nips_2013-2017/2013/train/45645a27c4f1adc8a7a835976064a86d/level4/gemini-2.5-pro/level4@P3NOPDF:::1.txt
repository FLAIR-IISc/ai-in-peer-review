### Summary

This paper introduces a novel model selection criterion, the Feature Information Criterion (FIC), for binary latent feature models. The approach is conceptually similar to variational Bayes but differs in a key aspect: instead of assuming a factorized posterior over both latent variables and parameters, it approximately integrates out the model parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), while maintaining a variational distribution over the latent features. A primary advantage of this method is its computational efficiency. Unlike more expensive Gibbs sampling or fully-variational Bayes inference schemes for the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), the proposed algorithm resembles an Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure with additional complexity penalties, making it potentially more scalable. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores and competitive runtimes compared to several existing IBP implementations.

### Strengths

The proposed approach is a well-motivated and pragmatic solution to the challenging problem of inference in IBP models. By replacing the difficult integration over parameters with a BIC approximation, the authors derive a computationally tractable algorithm that scales better than sampling-based alternatives. The connection to the EM algorithm is intuitive and provides a clear path for optimization. The empirical results, showing consistent improvements in predictive likelihood, suggest that the method is effective in practice.

### Weaknesses and Major Concerns

While the core idea is appealing, the paper is weakened by a lack of theoretical precision and an experimental analysis that does not fully support its central claims.

**1. Lack of Theoretical Rigor:**
The mathematical foundations of the method are not presented with sufficient clarity, and key assumptions seem to be unstated or problematic.

*   **Asymptotic Regime in Theorem 2:** The justification for using the BIC relies on Theorem 2, which states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated." However, the asymptotic regime is not clearly defined. Specifically, what are the assumptions on the number of features, K, as the number of data points, N, grows? Since the BIC is applied to each feature's parameters individually, the approximation seems to require that the number of data points assigned to each feature grows to infinity. This implicitly assumes a fixed, finite K, which contradicts the primary motivation for using a non-parametric prior like the IBP, where the number of features is expected to grow with the data. This crucial point needs to be addressed, as it calls into question the theoretical justification for applying this method to IBP models.

*   **Potential for Infinite FIC Score:** In Equation (3), the term `log(\sum_i z_{ik})` is undefined if a feature `k` is not used by any data point (i.e., `z_{ik} = 0` for all `i`). A non-degenerate variational distribution `q` would assign a non-zero probability to this event. Could the authors clarify why this does not cause the FIC score to be infinite in practice?

**2. Incremental Novelty:**
The technical contributions appear to be an incremental extension of the "fast and accurate" (FAB) variational Bayes framework developed in prior work ([3], [4]). While the application of BIC in this context is new, the overall variational structure and optimization approach build heavily on these existing methods.

**3. Ambiguous Experimental Analysis:**
The experimental results are promising but difficult to interpret conclusively.

*   **Unclear Source of Improvement:** The source of the significant improvements in predictive likelihood is not clearly disentangled. The paper attributes the gains to its superior model selection criterion. However, it is also plausible that the differences arise from optimization artifacts, such as different methods getting trapped in qualitatively different local optima. This concern is amplified by the observation that the Gibbs sampler learns an "excessively large" number of features on some datasets. In principle, an ideal sampler should not suffer from such a systematic bias, suggesting that the baseline implementation may have its own convergence or mixing issues. To strengthen the claims, the authors should provide more analysis to rule out confounding factors like initialization strategies or other implementation details.

*   **Unfair Runtime Comparisons:** The runtime comparisons, particularly with the Gibbs sampler, are potentially misleading. The stopping criterion for the sampler is not specified, making it unclear if it was run to convergence. Given that MCMC methods can be run for arbitrary lengths of time, comparing runtimes without a well-defined convergence diagnostic makes the efficiency claims difficult to substantiate. While FAB is reported to be much faster, its performance on predictive likelihood is substantially worse, suggesting it may have converged prematurely to a poor solution.

### Minor Points and Questions

*   In Section 2, the definition `p_k(X | z_{·,k})` appears to depend on the entire dataset `X`. Should this instead be conditioned only on the subset of data points assigned to feature `k`? Please clarify.
*   In Equation (4), the left-hand side should likely be an expectation with respect to the variational distribution `q`, i.e., `E_q[...]`.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a sensible and computationally efficient method for inference in binary latent feature models. However, its contributions are undermined by a lack of precision in its theoretical arguments and an experimental evaluation that does not sufficiently isolate the source of the reported improvements. The novelty also appears somewhat incremental over prior FAB work. While the approach is promising, the paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could clarify the asymptotic assumptions, provide a more rigorous justification for the FIC score, and conduct a more controlled experimental analysis to demonstrate that the performance gains are truly due to the proposed model selection criterion and not to optimization artifacts.
