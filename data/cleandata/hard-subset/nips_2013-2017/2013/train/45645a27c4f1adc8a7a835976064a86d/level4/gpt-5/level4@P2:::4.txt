Summary
The paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and hidden Markov models to latent feature models (LFMs). The key technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood for LFMs, showing that the factorized information criterion (FIC) takes the same form as in mixture models, despite the absence of the block-diagonal Hessian condition previously required for FAB. This yields a FAB algorithm for LFMs (FAB/LFM) that optimizes a tractable lower bound via alternating updates, incorporates a mean-field approximation for binary features, and leverages a CCCP-based acceleration for automatic shrinkage of irrelevant features. The authors also analyze identifiability and propose a merge post-processing step. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets compare FAB/LFM against IBP-based methods (Gibbs sampling, MEIBP) and variational Bayes (VB), demonstrating accurate model selection, competitive or superior predictive performance, and favorable computational cost.

Relation to prior work
FAB has previously been shown effective for model selection in MMs and HMMs without hand-tuned priors, converging to marginal log-likelihood asymptotically. For LFMs, nonparametric Bayesian approaches (e.g., IBP, MEIBP) and VB are standard for inferring the number of features but can be computationally demanding. The paper’s main novelty is showing that the FIC for LFMs matches that for MMs under an asymptotic Hessian analysis (Lemma 1), thereby enabling FAB in a setting where prior FAB assumptions do not hold. The work is also related to MAD-Bayes but differs in its asymptotic regime (large N rather than vanishing noise variance).

Strengths
- Clear, principled extension: Derives an FIC for LFMs and shows asymptotic equivalence to the MM case, enabling FAB beyond previously admissible models.
- Automatic model selection: The FIC term induces shrinkage of unnecessary features, achieving compact models without priors or tuning.
- Algorithmic refinements: CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration significantly reduces runtime in practice; the identifiability analysis motivates a merge post-processing step.
- Empirical validation: Across synthetic and real data, FAB/LFM typically selects the correct or more compact K, with strong predictive log-likelihood and competitive speed.
- Theoretical grounding: Results include asymptotic consistency of the FIC with marginal log-likelihood and discussion of identifiability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Incremental scope: Conceptually an extension of prior FAB frameworks, albeit nontrivial due to the lack of block-diagonal Hessian structure in LFMs.
- Reliance on asymptotic assumptions: Key results (e.g., Lemma 1) depend on “mild assumptions” deferred to the supplement; practical robustness when assumptions are weakened is not fully explored.
- Mean-field approximation: While standard, the factorization over Z may limit posterior fidelity; the impact on model selection accuracy is discussed but not deeply analyzed.
- Fairness and coverage of baselines: Some baselines fix the noise variance and VB is omitted on larger datasets due to runtime, which may affect comparative conclusions. MEIBP is inapplicable to some datasets (negative entries), and its performance is noted as sensitive to settings.
- Sensitivity analysis: Limited discussion of sensitivity to initialization, the number of CCCP/shrinkage steps (Tshrink), and stopping criteria.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent derivation and a plausible asymptotic argument leading to the FIC form. The algorithm and its monotonic improvement property are clearly stated. Some core assumptions and proofs are in the supplement, and additional robustness checks would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. Derivations are presented clearly, with helpful algorithmic summaries. A concise statement of the assumptions behind Lemma 1 in the main text would improve readability.

Originality
Moderately original. The main contribution is extending FAB to LFMs by establishing that the FIC retains the MM form. While building on prior FAB work, the analysis that overcomes the Hessian condition is nontrivial and of interest.

Significance
Meaningful for practitioners working with LFMs who need automated model selection at scale. The method’s ability to compete with IBP-based models while being computationally efficient is valuable. The identifiability insight and merging heuristic are useful in practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- State the key assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text, with a brief discussion of their plausibility and failure modes.
- Add sensitivity analyses (initialization, Tshrink, stopping tolerance) and an ablation of the CCCP-based acceleration and merge step.
- Where possible, include a small VB result on a medium-scale dataset, or clarify the computational trade-offs more explicitly.
- Discuss applicability beyond linear-Gaussian LFMs and outline extensions (e.g., non-Gaussian likelihoods).

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution enabling FAB in LFMs with the same FIC form as MMs.
- Automatic, prior-free feature selection with clear computational benefits.
- Strong empirical performance and practical algorithmic refinements.
- Relevance to the community using LFMs and nonparametric alternatives.

Arguments against acceptance
- Primarily an extension rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.
- Asymptotic assumptions and mean-field approximation could limit accuracy in small-sample regimes; limited robustness analysis.
- Baseline comparisons constrained by runtime or applicability, leaving some gaps.

Overall assessment
A clear, well-executed extension of FAB to LFMs with both theoretical and practical value. Despite its incremental nature, the contribution is nontrivial and the empirical results are convincing. I recommend acceptance.
