This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), building upon prior work demonstrating its effectiveness for mixture models. FAB inference offers a computationally tractable approach to model selection by maximizing a lower bound of the factorized information criterion (FIC), which approximates the marginal log-likelihood. The key contribution lies in adapting this framework to LFMs, overcoming limitations present in previous applications of FAB which required a block diagonal Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that LFMs satisfy a similar condition, enabling the application of FAB. They further introduce a shrinkage acceleration method inspired by a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and show parameter identifiability within the FAB/LFM framework. Empirical evaluations demonstrate improved performance compared to state-of-the-art Indian Buffet processes in terms of model selection, prediction accuracy, and computational efficiency.

This work is a logical and well-executed extension of FAB inference to a more complex model class. The authors clearly explain the theoretical foundations, particularly how the FIC is adapted for LFMs despite the differences in model structure compared to mixture models. The inclusion of theoretical analysis regarding parameter identifiability and the derivation of the shrinkage acceleration method add significant value. The experimental results are comprehensive, utilizing both synthetic and real-world datasets and comparing against a diverse set of relevant methods (IBP variants, VB, and EM). The authors appropriately acknowledge potential limitations and provide sufficient references to related work, including recent NIPS publications [10, 22]. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the accompanying theoretical analysis (identifiability, convergence) are strong points.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method significantly improves computational performance, addressing a key limitation of existing LFM inference methods.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation on a variety of datasets, demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the subject matter. 
*   **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Efficient and automated model selection for LFMs is a challenging and important problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Approximation Stack:** The method relies on a sequence of approximations (FIC, mean-field, shrinkage). While justified, the potential for cumulative error and scenarios where these approximations break down are not fully explored. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of these approximations, and perhaps an analysis of their impact under specific conditions, would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the authors emphasize the automation of FAB, it would be valuable to briefly discuss the sensitivity of the results to initial conditions or other minor parameter settings. 
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons are extensive, the inclusion of more recent or highly optimized IBP implementations could further strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of FAB inference to LFMs.
*   The proposed method demonstrably improves computational efficiency and achieves competitive performance compared to existing approaches.
*   The work addresses a significant problem in the field of latent variable modeling.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The cumulative effect of approximations requires more thorough investigation.
*   The absence of a detailed analysis of potential failure modes or sensitivity to parameter settings.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of latent variable modeling. The proposed method is theoretically well-founded, computationally efficient, and empirically effective. While the limitations related to the approximations used should be acknowledged, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
