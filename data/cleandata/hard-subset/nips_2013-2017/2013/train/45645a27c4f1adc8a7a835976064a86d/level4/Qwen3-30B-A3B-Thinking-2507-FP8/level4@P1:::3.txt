

Summary: This paper introduces Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference for latent feature models (LFMs), extending a recently developed framework originally applied to mixture models and hidden Markov models. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the specific Hessian condition required for FAB inference in previous work, LFMs share the same asymptotic behavior as mixture models with respect to the factorized information criterion (FIC). The FAB/LFM approach automatically selects the number of latent features without requiring prior specifications or hand-tuned hyperparameters, while offering computational advantages over existing nonparametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the theoretical foundation for applying FAB to LFMs, deriving novel properties including automatic feature selection and parameter identifiability, and introducing a shrinkage acceleration technique that significantly reduces computational cost.

Quality: The technical content is sound and well-justified. The asymptotic analysis in Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 correctly establishes the equivalence between FICs for LFMs and mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications. The derivation of the FAB/LFM algorithm and its theoretical properties (including the CCCP interpretation in Section 3) is rigorous and well-supported. The empirical evaluation demonstrates clear computational advantages over competing methods, with FAB achieving superior model selection accuracy and predictive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The supplementary materials provide thorough proofs of the main theoretical results.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, and the connection between the theoretical analysis and algorithmic implementation is well-maintained. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is sufficiently detailed for replication. However, some experimental details would benefit from clarification, particularly regarding the implementation choices for baseline methods and the handling of noise variance parameters.

Originality: The application of FAB inference to latent feature models represents a significant extension of the existing framework. While FAB has been successfully applied to mixture models and hidden Markov models, the authors convincingly demonstrate that the theoretical foundation extends to LFMs despite the apparent lack of the required Hessian condition. The derivation of FIC for LFMs having the same form as for mixture models (Theorem 2) is particularly novel. The shrinkage acceleration technique based on the CCCP interpretation (Algorithm 2) is also a valuable contribution. The paper properly contextualizes this work within the NIPS literature, particularly with respect to recent advances in nonparametric Bayesian inference.

Significance: The paper addresses an important practical challenge in latent feature modeling—efficient inference with automatic model selection. The ability to automatically determine the number of latent features without hyperparameters or prior specifications is highly valuable for real-world applications, especially with large-scale datasets. The demonstrated computational advantages over state-of-the-art IBP methods (particularly in terms of feature selection accuracy and reduced runtime) make this approach appealing for practitioners. The theoretical insights into parameter identifiability and the natural merge post-processing procedure also contribute to the broader understanding of latent variable models.

Strengths:
- Theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs with rigorous asymptotic analysis
- Automatic feature selection without priors or hyperparameters
- Significant computational efficiency gains through shrinkage acceleration
- Clear empirical demonstration of superior performance on synthetic and real-world data
- Novel insights into parameter identifiability and merge post-processing

Weaknesses:
- The experimental evaluation of MEIBP is problematic as the synthetic data (block data and artificial simulations) contains negative values, while MEIBP is designed for non-negative linear Gaussian models (W must be positive). This invalidates the comparison for these datasets.
- The paper does not adequately discuss limitations of the approach, particularly in small-sample regimes where asymptotic assumptions may not hold.
- The comparison with MAD-Bayes (a related asymptotic approach for LFMs) is missing, despite the paper's focus on asymptotic methods.
- The experimental setup for VB-IBP lacks sufficient detail regarding implementation choices (e.g., number of restarts, initialization).
- The handling of noise variance parameters for baseline methods (e.g., setting to 0.75 for real data) requires better justification.

The authors have addressed some concerns in their feedback, but key issues remain regarding experimental methodology and proper contextualization with related work. The reference to [21] as "Gibbs sampling" is incorrect—this work implements MAP inference, not sampling, and should be corrected.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution that extends the FAB framework to a new class of models with significant practical implications. The theoretical insights and computational advantages are compelling, though the experimental evaluation requires more careful execution and contextualization. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models and would be a valuable addition to NIPS.
