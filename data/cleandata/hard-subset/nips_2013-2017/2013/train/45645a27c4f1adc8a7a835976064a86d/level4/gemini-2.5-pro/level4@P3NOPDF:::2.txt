### Summary

This paper introduces a novel and efficient method for model selection in latent feature models (LFMs). The core contribution is the generalization of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC)—techniques well-established for mixture models—to the LFM domain. The proposed approach uniquely combines a mean-field approximation for inferring the latent variables with an accelerated shrinkage mechanism to effectively determine the global number of features. A key advantage of this method is its high degree of automation, significantly reducing the need for manual parameter tuning. The authors demonstrate the method's efficacy on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing significant gains in computational efficiency compared to existing approaches.

### Quality

The paper's technical quality is high. The authors present a rigorous empirical evaluation, including a thorough analysis of the results. The proposed method is benchmarked against a wide range of competing techniques across multiple datasets, providing a comprehensive assessment of its performance. The work is well-contextualized with sufficient and appropriate references to prior literature.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making the technical contributions and experimental results easy to follow. The exposition of the method, from the theoretical underpinnings to the final algorithm, is logical and coherent.

### Originality and Significance

The work represents a solid and logical generalization of previous research on FAB inference and FIC for model selection. While the individual components (FIC, mean-field variational inference, shrinkage priors) are not new, the primary contribution lies in their novel and effective combination to tackle the challenging problem of model selection in LFMs. The resulting algorithm is both computationally efficient and effective, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

A potential area for improvement would be a more in-depth discussion of the method's limitations. The proposed algorithm relies on a confluence of approximations—namely, the factorized information criterion, the mean-field assumption, and the shrinkage mechanism. It would be highly valuable for the reader to understand the potential failure modes. For instance, under what conditions might the interplay between these approximations lead to suboptimal performance? An analysis that "pushes the method until it breaks" would strengthen the paper by clearly delineating the boundaries of its applicability and providing practitioners with a clearer understanding of when it is most likely to succeed.
