

Summary: This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), which have not previously been amenable to FAB due to their failure to satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for FAB's derivation. The authors prove that LFMs share the same FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) structure as mixture models despite this condition, enabling FAB's application to LFMs. The paper introduces an efficient FAB/LFM algorithm with shrinkage acceleration, which automatically selects the number of latent features while offering computational advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The work builds directly on prior FAB research for mixture models (Fujimaki & Morinaga, AISTATS 2013; Fujimaki et al., ICML 2012) and extends it to a broader class of latent variable models.

Strengths: The paper excels in quality, presenting rigorous theoretical analysis supported by thorough empirical evaluation. The key contribution—showing that LFMs share the same FIC form as mixture models despite violating the Hessian condition—represents a significant theoretical advance. The authors provide detailed proofs (in supplementary materials) and demonstrate FAB/LFM's superiority across multiple datasets in terms of model selection accuracy, prediction performance, and computational efficiency. The shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) is particularly well-motivated and effectively reduces computational cost without sacrificing accuracy. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate comparisons to related work (IBP, VB, MEIBP), and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Originality and significance are strong. While FAB has been applied to mixture models, extending it to LFMs required novel theoretical insights. The authors convincingly demonstrate that FAB/LFM offers automatic feature selection (without hand-tuning hyperparameters), parameter identifiability, and computational efficiency—advantages that address key limitations of existing methods like IBP and VB. The method's ability to select compact models with better predictive performance (as shown in Table 1) makes it valuable for practical applications.

Weaknesses: While the theoretical foundation is solid, the paper would benefit from a more thorough investigation of the method's limitations. The authors claim FAB/LFM achieves "automatic hidden states selection" and "parameter identifiability," but they don't explore scenarios where these properties might fail (e.g., for highly correlated features or specific data distributions). A sensitivity analysis examining how the method performs under varying conditions (e.g., different noise levels, feature correlations) would strengthen the claims. Additionally, while the shrinkage acceleration is effective, the paper lacks a clear theoretical justification for why multiple CCCP iterations (Algorithm 2) accelerate convergence—this could be addressed by analyzing the convergence rate of the CCCP-based optimization.

The paper's experimental section is strong but could be more comprehensive. For instance, the comparison with MEIBP in the real-world datasets (Table 1) shows FAB's superior predictive performance, but the authors don't explicitly explain why MEIBP's performance is worse despite its speed advantage. A brief discussion of the underlying reasons (e.g., MEIBP's sensitivity to initial K values or its non-identifiability issues) would enhance the interpretation of results.

The paper could also better clarify the relationship between FAB/LFM and existing Bayesian methods. While the authors note FAB's "asymptotic ignorance of priors," they don't sufficiently contrast this with other approaches that incorporate priors (e.g., IBP). A brief discussion of when FAB/LFM's approach might be preferable to prior-informed methods would help contextualize the contribution.

Recommendation: This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to model selection in latent feature models. The extension of FAB to LFMs is well-motivated, rigorously justified, and empirically validated. The method offers clear advantages in computational efficiency and model selection accuracy over state-of-the-art approaches. While the paper could benefit from a more thorough exploration of the method's limitations and a deeper discussion of its relationship to prior work, these are minor concerns that do not undermine the paper's strong contribution. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in the field. I recommend acceptance.
