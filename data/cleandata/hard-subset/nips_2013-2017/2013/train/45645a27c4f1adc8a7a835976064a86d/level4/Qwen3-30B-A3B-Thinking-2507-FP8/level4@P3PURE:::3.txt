

Summary:  
This paper introduces an inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation \(\mathbf{x}_n\) is represented by a \(K\)-dimensional binary vector \(\mathbf{z}_n\) indicating the presence of latent features. The goal is to infer \(\mathbf{z}_n\) for all observations while learning model parameters, including the number of latent features \(K\). The proposed method employs an EM-based algorithm with a shrinkage mechanism leveraging Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference—a model selection criterion recently developed for mixture models and HMMs. Unlike nonparametric Bayesian approaches (e.g., Indian Buffet Process), FAB does not impose prior-induced parsimony but instead uses an asymptotic approximation to the log marginal likelihood, incorporating a model selection term that encourages shrinkage of \(K\) (via an upper bound). The method demonstrates superior scalability and inference quality over Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation for IBP on large datasets, with reduced runtime.  

Quality:  
The technical approach is sound, with correct algorithmic details. The ability to handle large-scale data while automatically inferring \(K\) is a significant strength.  

Clarity:  
The methodology is clearly presented. However, experimental details lack sufficient clarity (addressed in Weak Points).  

Originality:  
While FAB was previously applied to mixture models and HMMs, its adaptation to LFMs constitutes a novel contribution.  

Significance:  
The work addresses a key challenge: efficient inference in LFMs with automatic selection of \(K\).  

Weak Points:  
1. **Limitations unaddressed**: The paper omits discussion of scenarios where the algorithm may underperform compared to vanilla Gibbs sampling (e.g., due to sensitivity to initialization or hyperparameters).  
2. **Convergence concerns**: No analysis of convergence behavior for the EM-like procedure.  
3. **Scope restriction**: Limited to binary LFMs; not applicable to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.  
4. **Inadequate experimental evaluation**:  
   - The MEIBP baseline (reference [22]) requires a non-negative loading matrix \(\mathbf{W}\), yet synthetic and block data experiments use general \(\mathbf{W}\) (invalidating comparison). Synthetic data must be generated with non-negative \(\mathbf{W}\) for fair MEIBP comparison.  
   - For block images, the IBP Gibbs sampler should use the known noise standard deviation (not the heuristic 0.75), as FAB is provided with this value.  
   - VB-IBP experiments rely on the finite variational version; the infinite variant should be used for fairness. Experimental settings (e.g., restarts, hyperparameter tuning) are insufficiently described.  
   - The Gibbs sampler’s discovery of 70 latent features on 49-dimensional Sonar data is surprising and likely stems from poor initialization or mis-specified noise variance.  
5. **Small-data regime**: No discussion or experiments for small-data settings where the asymptotic FAB approximation may fail.  
6. **Missing literature**: Recent work on small-variance asymptotic inference for nonparametric LFMs (e.g., "MAD-Bayes" from ICML 2013) is unaddressed.  
7. **Reference error**: Line 70 incorrectly cites [21] as Gibbs sampling; it describes MAP inference for IBP (like [22]).  

Other Comments:  
- Line 349: The recommendation in [1,22] for noise standard deviation is \(0.25 \times \text{std}(\mathbf{x})\), not 0.75.  
- The MAD-Bayes work (ICML 2013) should be discussed to contextualize the FAB approach.  

Comments after Author Feedback:  
The authors addressed some concerns, but critical issues remain:  
- Experimental methodology (for FAB and baselines) requires clearer justification (e.g., hyperparameter choices, initialization effects).  
- Reference [21] still mischaracterized as Gibbs sampling; it describes MAP inference.  
- The MAD-Bayes reference must be included to properly position the work.  

Final Recommendation:  
The core idea—applying FAB to LFMs for scalable inference with automatic \(K\) selection—is promising and addresses a meaningful problem. However, the experimental evaluation contains critical flaws (invalid MEIBP comparison, inadequate small-data analysis) and lacks discussion of limitations. The paper must be revised to: (1) correct experimental methodology and comparisons, (2) address all cited weaknesses (especially reference [21] and MAD-Bayes), and (3) discuss limitations and small-data behavior before acceptance.
