This paper introduces a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. It shares similarities with variational Bayes, but instead of assuming a factorized posterior, it approximates parameter integration using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihoods compared to existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach is well-motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument and represents a sensible direction for research. A key advantage over other IBP inference methods is its computational efficiency: it corresponds to an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with added complexity penalties, rather than the more computationally demanding sampling or variational Bayes techniques. 

However, the technical contributions, while novel, appear incremental, building directly upon the Factor Analysis with Bayesian (FAB) work of [3] and [4]. 

Several parts of the exposition lack precision, impacting clarity. For instance, equation (3) involves a log sum which becomes infinite when all z values are zero. Given that this scenario has non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution q, the Finite Information Criterion (FIC) score should frequently be infinite; this issue is not addressed. 

Furthermore, Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood can be asymptotically approximated, but the specific asymptotic regime and underlying assumptions are not fully defined. In particular, the behavior of K (the number of components) as N (the dataset size) increases is unclear. Since the BIC is applied component-wise, the theorem appears to necessitate a fixed, finite K, implying an infinite number of activations per feature. This contradicts the core motivation for using the IBP model, which assumes an infinite number of components and continually discovers new ones with increasing data.

Minor issues include: in section 2, pk(X | z{\cdot, k}) should likely only consider data points assigned to component k, rather than the entire dataset; and equation (4) should include an expectation on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results in Table 1 show the proposed method achieving higher predictive likelihoods in shorter timeframes compared to alternatives. While FAB exhibits significantly faster completion in some cases, interpreting this result is difficult due to the unspecified stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler, which appears arbitrary.

Although the improvements in predictive likelihood are notable, the source of these gains remains unclear. The results would be more convincing with evidence attributing the difference specifically to the model selection criterion, rather than to variations in convergence to local optima—a factor likely influenced by implementation details such as initialization strategies. The Gibbs sampler’s tendency to learn an excessively large number of components in certain datasets supports the possibility of suboptimal convergence, as it should, in theory, exactly compute the likelihood being approximated by FAB.

In summary, the proposed approach is reasonable and demonstrates practical improvements in running time and predictive likelihood. However, the contributions are somewhat incremental, the mathematical presentation could be more precise, and further analysis is needed to confidently interpret the experimental results.
