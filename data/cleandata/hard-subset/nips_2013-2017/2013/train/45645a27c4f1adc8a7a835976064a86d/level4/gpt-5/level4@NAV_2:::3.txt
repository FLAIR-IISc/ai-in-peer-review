Summary:
The paper introduces an inference procedure for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation xn is represented by a K-dimensional binary vector zn with znk = 1 indicating the presence of feature k. The objective is to infer all latent feature assignments z together with model parameters. The proposed EM-style algorithm incorporates a shrinkage mechanism that both learns the latent features and automatically determines the number of features K. Conceptually, this is akin to nonparametric Bayesian LFMs such as the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), but here parsimony is achieved not via priors but through a model selection principle—Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference—originally developed for mixture models and HMMs. FAB relies on approximating the marginal log-likelihood by an expected complete-data log-likelihood plus a complexity penalty that induces shrinkage of latent features, enabling one to set an upper bound on K and learn the effective K during inference. On several large-scale datasets, the authors report that FAB performs favorably relative to Gibbs sampling, variational methods, and MAP-based IBP approaches in terms of runtime and inference quality.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound, and the algorithmic components are coherently presented. The ability to scale to large datasets while automatically selecting K is compelling.

Clarity:
The methodological exposition is generally clear. Some aspects of the experimental setup and choices could be better specified (see below).

Originality:
While building on prior FAB work for mixture models and HMMs, the extension to LFMs is new and nontrivial.

Significance:
The work targets an important problem: efficient inference in LFMs with automatic determination of the number of features.

Weak Points:
- The paper does not clearly articulate limitations. When, if ever, would the method underperform relative to standard Gibbs sampling?
- Convergence behavior and potential pathologies of the EM-like procedure are not discussed.
- The method targets binary LFMs and does not directly apply to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.
- The empirical evaluation is not fully comprehensive and contains issues (see comments below).
- Behavior in the small-sample regime, where asymptotic arguments may be unreliable, is insufficiently explored or discussed.
- Some recent work on efficient LFM inference is missing from the discussion (see below).
- MEIBP [22] is only applicable to nonnegative linear-Gaussian models (W constrained to be nonnegative). Thus, the artificial and block-data comparisons are not valid MEIBP baselines unless W is constrained to be nonnegative in those experiments.

Other Comments:
- For the block image data, the FAB method is given the true/known noise level; for fairness, the IBP Gibbs sampler should also be provided the same noise level rather than the 0.75 std-dev heuristic.
- For VB-IBP, the infinite variational formulation could be used; the experiments employ a finite-K variational version. Please provide more details on VB settings (e.g., number of restarts).
- The accelerated Gibbs sampler reportedly finds around 70 features on the 49-dimensional Sonar data; this seems implausible and likely stems from unfavorable initialization and/or suboptimal noise variance hyperparameters.
- Line 70: the cited work [21] is not a Gibbs sampling method but a MAP/submodular approach (similar in spirit to [22]). Please correct the description.
- Please discuss recent small-variance asymptotic methods for nonparametric LFMs, e.g., “MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes” (ICML 2013), and relate them to your approach.

Minor comments:
- Line 349: For real data, [1,22] recommend setting the noise std-dev to 0.25 times the empirical std-dev of the examples across dimensions, not a fixed 0.75.

Comments after the author-feedback:
The rebuttal addressed several points. A few items still require revision before acceptance:
- Clarify and justify experimental methodology for both the proposed method and baselines, including explanations for atypical behaviors (e.g., fixed α potentially inflating K in Gibbs, or mis-specified noise variance), and provide enough detail to ensure reproducibility.
- Correct the mischaracterization of [21] as sampling; it performs MAP inference.
- Add and discuss the MAD-Bayes reference for the IBP.
Overall, the LFM FAB inference is interesting, and its scalability and automatic K selection are attractive. The empirical section would benefit from more careful design and clearer reporting.
