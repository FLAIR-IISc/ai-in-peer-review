This paper introduces a new model-selection criterion for binary latent feature models. It resembles variational Bayes, but instead of positing a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximately integrates out the parameters via a BIC-based approximation. The method yields better held-out likelihoods than several existing IBP implementations.

The approach is reasonable and supported by a plausible asymptotic motivation. Its primary advantage over other IBP inference schemes is computational: it effectively reduces to an EM algorithm augmented with complexity penalties, avoiding the higher cost of sampling or standard variational Bayes.

The technical contributions appear novel but incremental, essentially extending the FAB framework of [3] and [4].

Some of the exposition is confusing because certain mathematical statements seem imprecise. In equation (3), the log of the sum of the z values diverges when all entries are zero. Since that event has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution q, why isn’t the FIC score always infinite?

Theorem 2 claims that the marginal likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated as” a particular expression, but the asymptotic regime and assumptions are not specified. In particular, how is K assumed to scale as N increases? Because BIC is applied component-wise, the result appears to require a fixed finite K so that each feature’s activation count grows without bound. Under the IBP, however, the number of components is infinite and new features continue to appear as data grow, so assuming a finite K removes a key motivation for using the IBP.

Minor points: in Section 2, shouldn’t p_k(X | z_{·,k}) involve only the datapoints assigned to component k, rather than the entire dataset? In equation (4), the left-hand side should be an expectation.

In the quantitative results in Table 1, the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihoods in less time than competing approaches. Although FAB sometimes converges orders of magnitude faster, the comparison is hard to interpret because the stopping criterion is unspecified. In particular, when to terminate the Gibbs sampler appears arbitrary.

The improvements in predictive likelihood are substantial, but what is their source? The results would be more persuasive with evidence that the gains arise from the model-selection criterion itself, rather than from different algorithms settling into better or worse local optima, which likely depend on implementation details (initialization strategies, etc.). The observation that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets suggests local-optima issues, since in principle it computes the exact likelihood that FAB only approximates.

Overall, the proposed method is sensible but somewhat incremental. Some of the mathematics lack precision. The experiments show improvements in runtime and predictive likelihood over prior work, but, as argued above, there is insufficient analysis to clearly interpret these results.
