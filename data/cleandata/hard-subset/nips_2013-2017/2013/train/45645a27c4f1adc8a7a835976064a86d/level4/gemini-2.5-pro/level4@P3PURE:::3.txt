### Summary

This paper introduces an EM-based inference algorithm for the binary latent feature model (LFM). In this model, each observation is represented by a K-dimensional binary vector indicating the presence or absence of K latent features. The primary goal is to infer these binary feature vectors for all observations, along with other model parameters. The proposed algorithm employs a shrinkage mechanism, derived from the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, to learn the feature assignments and simultaneously infer the number of active latent features (K) from a pre-specified upper bound. This approach is conceptually similar to non-parametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), but it replaces an explicit prior with a model selection penalty term added to the expected complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate on several large datasets that their method can outperform standard inference techniques for the IBP, including Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation, in terms of both computational efficiency and inference quality.

### Review

The paper addresses the important problem of scalable inference in latent feature models, and the proposed method's ability to simultaneously handle large datasets and infer model complexity is highly appealing. The application of the recently proposed Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) framework to latent feature models is novel. The technical presentation of the methodology is reasonably sound and the algorithmic details appear correct. However, the experimental evaluation has several significant weaknesses that undermine the paper's conclusions and must be addressed.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Invalid Experimental Comparison:** A critical flaw exists in the comparison with the MEIBP baseline [22]. The MEIBP algorithm is specifically designed for non-negative linear Gaussian models, where the loading matrix W must be positive. The paper's experiments on artificial and block data appear to use a real-valued W, which violates MEIBP's modeling assumptions and invalidates the comparison. A fair evaluation would require generating synthetic data with a positive loading matrix, consistent with the constraints of the MEIBP model.

2.  **Insufficiently Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup lacks rigor and fairness. For example, in the block images experiment, the proposed FAB algorithm is supplied with the known noise value, while the IBP Gibbs sampler is not, instead relying on a heuristic. For an equitable comparison, all methods should be given access to the same information. Furthermore, the experimental details for the VB-IBP baseline are sparse; it is unclear why the finite variational version was used instead of the infinite one, and key parameters like the number of restarts are not reported.

3.  **Questionable Baseline Performance:** The performance reported for some baseline methods seems suspect and may indicate suboptimal implementation or tuning. For instance, it is surprising that the accelerated Gibbs sampler discovers approximately 70 latent features on the 49-dimensional Sonar dataset. This result suggests potential issues with initialization, convergence, or poorly specified hyperparameters (e.g., noise variance or the concentration parameter alpha). The paper would be much stronger if the baselines were more carefully tuned to ensure they represent formidable competition.

4.  **Lack of Discussion on Limitations:** The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithm. There is no analysis of potential failure modes, scenarios where it might perform worse than methods like Gibbs sampling, or potential convergence difficulties associated with the EM-like procedure.

5.  **Scope and Asymptotic Assumptions:** The algorithm is limited to binary latent features and is not applicable to continuous models like factor analysis or probabilistic PCA. Additionally, since the method is based on an asymptotic argument, its performance in the small-data regime is unclear. Some discussion or experiments addressing this would strengthen the paper.

6.  **Omission of Relevant Work:** The paper fails to discuss some recent and highly relevant work on efficient inference in non-parametric LFMs, such as "MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes" (ICML 2013), which also explores small-variance asymptotic approximations.

**Minor Points and Corrections:**

*   **Incorrect Citation:** Line 70: Reference [21] is incorrectly described as a Gibbs sampling method. It is a MAP estimation algorithm for the IBP, similar in nature to reference [22]. This description must be corrected.
*   **Heuristic Correction:** Line 349: The heuristic suggested in [1, 22] for setting the standard deviation on real-world data is to use 0.25 times the standard deviation of the data across all dimensions, not a fixed value of 0.75.

**Comments After Author Feedback:**

The author feedback has addressed some of my questions. However, several key issues remain that must be resolved before the paper can be accepted.

Specifically, the experimental methodology for both the proposed method and the baselines requires significant clarification. The authors should provide justifications for hyperparameter choices and investigate why certain baseline algorithms performed poorly (e.g., using a fixed alpha for the Gibbs sampler may have led to an overestimation of K, or noise variance hyperparameters may have been improperly set). Furthermore, the paper must be updated to correct the description of reference [21] (as a MAP inference method, not sampling) and to include a discussion of the relevant MAD-Bayes paper.

In conclusion, the proposed inference method for LFMs is interesting, and its scalability is a significant advantage. However, the paper's central claims are not yet supported by a sufficiently careful and rigorous experimental evaluation. Acceptance is contingent on addressing the aforementioned experimental and textual issues.
