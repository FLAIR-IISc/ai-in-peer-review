Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to binary latent feature models (LFMs). FAB maximizes a tractable lower bound to a factorized information criterion (FIC) that asymptotically approximates the marginal log-likelihood while inducing data-driven model selection. Prior FAB work focused on mixture models and HMMs; those models satisfy a block-diagonal Hessian condition that simplifies the Laplace approximation. The main theoretical contribution here is to show, via an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian for LFMs, that the same FIC form arises even though the block-diagonal condition does not hold (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2). This yields a FAB objective for LFMs identical in form to that for mixtures, with a crucial log-sum term that shrinks irrelevant features and makes parameters identifiable.

Algorithmically, the paper develops an EM-like procedure with: (i) a mean-field E-step over binary features; (ii) a CCCP interpretation that motivates a shrinkage-acceleration routine; and (iii) a post-hoc merge step justified by an identifiability analysis. Compared to nonparametric Bayesian approaches such as the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) with Gibbs sampling, variational inference (VB), and the MEIBP MAP procedure, FAB avoids priors/hyperparameters, selects K automatically, and is designed for scalability.

Experiments on synthetic data, block images, and several real datasets compare FAB to accelerated Gibbs sampling for IBP, finite VB, and MEIBP (when applicable). Results show FAB is competitive or superior in predictive performance and model selection, often with lower computational cost, and its shrinkage acceleration yields substantial speedups.

Strengths:
- Theory: A clear and nontrivial asymptotic result (Lemma 1) showing the FIC for LFMs matches that for mixtures, despite parameter coupling. The corollary establishes asymptotic tightness of the FIC. The identifiability analysis and merge criterion are well-motivated.
- Algorithm: Monotone lower-bound optimization, an interpretable CCCP view, and a practical shrinkage-acceleration routine that removes irrelevant features early. The method is fully automatic (no priors/hyperparameters) and includes natural stopping criteria.
- Empirics: Demonstrates scalability and competitive or improved model selection and prediction versus strong baselines on several datasets. The shrinkage mechanism clearly impacts efficiency.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Scope and limitations: The method targets binary LFMs with Gaussian noise and diagonal precision. It does not directly handle real-valued latent factors (e.g., FA/PPCA), non-Gaussian likelihoods, or structured priors on features. Please discuss these limitations explicitly and outline potential extensions.
- Small-sample regime: Since the approach relies on asymptotics, behavior for small N is unclear. The paper briefly notes potential degradation on small N (Libras) but lacks a systematic analysis. Please add experiments or a discussion clarifying when FAB may underperform IBP/VB (e.g., highly multimodal posteriors, weak signal).
- Convergence and initialization: Although each step increases the lower bound, the procedure is non-convex and initialization matters. Provide a short discussion on sensitivity to initialization, the impact of Tshrink, and any heuristics (restarts, annealing) you found useful. Clarify whether merge operations always increase the bound and how you check that.
- Assumptions behind Lemma 1: The main text should state the key “mild assumptions” enabling the determinant decomposition (currently only in the supplement). This will help readers assess applicability.
- MEIBP comparisons: MEIBP assumes nonnegative W; some synthetic and block-data comparisons appear invalid under that constraint. Either (i) generate nonnegative W for MEIBP, or (ii) omit MEIBP on datasets violating its assumptions and explain why. As is, those baselines are not comparable.
- Noise variance and fairness: FAB sometimes uses the true or a tuned noise variance, while IBP/VB/MEIBP are set to heuristics. For fairness, give all methods the same noise standard deviation when it is known, and document hyperparameter tuning. The report of ~70 features on 49-dim Sonar likely reflects suboptimal α/noise settings; please justify or adjust.
- VB-IBP configuration: You compare to a finite-K VB variant. An infinite variational IBP exists; if not used, please justify. Also report restarts and sensitivity for VB, and clarify all priors/hyperparameters.
- Related work: Please discuss small-variance asymptotics for LFMs (MAD-Bayes: Broderick et al., ICML 2013), which also derive scalable MAP-like objectives with feature-shrinkage behavior, and clarify similarities/differences to FAB (e.g., prior-induced vs model-induced regularization).
- Citations/descriptions: The manuscript appears to describe MEIBP (Reed & Ghahramani, 2013) as a sampling method in one place; it is a MAP approach via submodular optimization. Please correct. Also ensure the noise-setting heuristic matches the cited recommendations (the 0.75 SD heuristic vs 0.25×global SD discrepancy noted).

Quality:
- The derivations are technically sound and interesting. The CCCP interpretation and identifiability result add value. Some experimental comparisons need better controls (noise settings, priors, MEIBP applicability) to support the strongest claims.

Clarity:
- The methodology is mostly clear, with helpful algorithm boxes. Please surface the key assumptions and complexity discussion (per-iteration cost and dependence on N, D, K) in the main text. Expand the experimental section to detail all hyperparameters, initializations, restarts, and stopping criteria for each baseline.

Originality:
- Extending FAB from mixtures/HMMs to LFMs is novel and nontrivial. The CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability-driven merging are also appealing contributions.

Significance:
- Automatic K selection with a scalable, prior-free objective is valuable for latent feature modeling. If the empirical comparisons are tightened and the limitations are clarified, the impact on practitioners working with large datasets could be substantial.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical extension with clear practical implications.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with monotone improvement and automatic feature selection.
- Competitive or better empirical performance and substantial speedups due to shrinkage acceleration.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some baseline comparisons are not methodologically fair (MEIBP applicability; noise settings; VB configuration), weakening empirical claims.
- Limited discussion of small-N behavior and convergence/initialization issues.
- Restricted to binary LFMs with Gaussian noise; broader applicability not demonstrated.

Minor comments:
- Clarify the exact data preprocessing and noise setting protocol across methods; consider providing code or a detailed appendix to ensure reproducibility.
- Use consistent notation (e.g., z_{nk}) and ensure all symbols are defined upon first use.
- Fix the description of MEIBP as MAP, not sampling, and include the MAD-Bayes reference.
