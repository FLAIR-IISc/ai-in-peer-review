This paper introduces a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. The method resembles variational Bayes, but instead of a fully factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximately integrates out parameters using a BIC-style penalty. Empirically, it yields higher held-out likelihoods than several existing IBP implementations.

The approach is sensible and supported by a plausible asymptotic argument. A key practical advantage over standard IBP inference is computational: the algorithm reduces to an EM procedure with additional complexity penalties, avoiding the heavier cost of sampling or standard variational Bayes.

The technical contributions appear novel but incremental, largely extending the FAB framework of [3] and [4].

Some parts of the exposition are unclear due to imprecise mathematics:
- In equation (3), the log of the sum of z values becomes negative infinity when all entries are zero. Since this event has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate q, why doesn’t the FIC score go to negative infinity? Please clarify the handling of zero-activation cases (e.g., via conventions, smoothing, or conditioning).
- Theorem 2 claims that the marginal likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated as” a given expression, but the asymptotic regime and assumptions are not specified. In particular, how does K behave as N grows? Applying BIC per component suggests a fixed finite K so that each feature’s number of activations tends to infinity. Under the IBP prior, however, the number of components is unbounded and grows with data, so assuming finite K seems to remove a core motivation for using the IBP. Please make explicit whether K is fixed, grows slowly with N, or is otherwise controlled, and how this reconciles with the IBP setting.

Minor points:
- In Section 2, shouldn’t pk(X | z·,k) condition only on the data assigned to component k, rather than the entire dataset?
- In equation (4), the left-hand side should be an expectation.

Empirical results (Table 1) indicate that the proposed method attains higher predictive likelihood in less time relative to alternatives. However, interpretation is hindered by missing details:
- FAB sometimes finishes orders of magnitude faster, but the stopping criteria for all methods are unclear. In particular, the Gibbs sampler’s stopping rule appears arbitrary; without a principled convergence or effective sample size target, wall-clock comparisons are hard to assess.
- The observed gains in predictive likelihood are sizable, but it is not evident whether they stem from the proposed model selection criterion or from differences in optimization behavior (e.g., sensitivity to local optima, initialization schemes, learning-rate schedules). Evidence disentangling these effects would strengthen the claims. For instance, controlled ablations (same initialization, identical update ordering, matched stopping criteria), optimization diagnostics (objective traces, multiple restarts), or experiments where the model selection penalty is toggled while keeping the optimizer fixed would help. The fact that the Gibbs sampler sometimes learns a very large number of components suggests issues with local optima or poor mixing; in theory, it should accurately reflect the likelihood that FAB approximates.

Overall assessment: The proposed idea is reasonable and practically attractive, but the contribution feels somewhat incremental relative to prior FAB work. Some mathematical statements need tightening, especially regarding the asymptotic regime and edge cases in the objective. The experiments are promising in both runtime and predictive likelihood, yet additional analysis is needed to attribute improvements to the model selection criterion rather than optimization artifacts and to make runtime comparisons fair and interpretable. Clarifying these points would significantly strengthen the paper.
