This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously applied to models like mixture models, to binary latent feature models (LFMs). The core technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the LFM's complete-data log-likelihood. This analysis demonstrates that the resulting Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes a similar form to the FIC for mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition previously required for FAB. The authors derive an efficient EM-like algorithm that maximizes a lower bound on the FIC, and they demonstrate its superior performance in terms of model selection, prediction, and computational speed compared to several state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The work addresses the important and challenging problem of model selection in latent feature models. Extending the FAB framework to this more complex model class, which does not satisfy the original assumptions, is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Computational Efficiency:** A key advantage of the proposed method is its computational profile. The resulting algorithm is analogous to EM with an additional penalty term that encourages model compactness, making it significantly faster than more computationally intensive sampling-based or variational inference algorithms for non-parametric models like the IBP.
3.  **Empirical Performance:** The experiments show that FAB/LFM achieves strong predictive log-likelihood scores on held-out data across several real-world datasets, often with substantial reductions in computation time compared to baseline methods. The observation that FAB achieves a smaller gap between training and predictive likelihoods suggests its regularization is effective at preventing overfitting.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **Originality:** The technical contribution, while novel and non-trivial, can be viewed as an incremental extension of the prior FAB framework developed in [5, 6] to a new model class.
2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** Some aspects of the mathematical formulation could benefit from further clarification to enhance rigor.
    *   In Equation (3), the FIC objective includes the term `Eq[log(sum_n z_nk)]`. If the variational distribution `q(Z)` assigns a non-zero probability to the event that a feature `k` is inactive for all `n` (i.e., `sum_n z_nk = 0`), the FIC would be negative infinity. Could the authors clarify how this is handled or what constraints are placed on `q` to avoid this issue?
    *   The asymptotic regime for Theorem 2 is not precisely defined. Specifically, it is unclear how the number of latent features `K` is assumed to scale with the number of data points `N`. The derivation, which relies on a BIC-like approximation for each feature, seems to imply a fixed, finite `K` so that the number of observations per feature grows with `N`. This assumption seems to diverge from the motivation of non-parametric Bayesian models like the IBP, where the number of features is expected to grow with the data.
    *   Minor points: In the discussion of FIC for MMs in Section 1.1, `log p_k(X|z_.k)` appears to use the entire dataset `X`, whereas for mixture models this term typically involves only the data points assigned to component `k`. Additionally, the left-hand side of Equation (4) seems to be missing an expectation `E_q[...]`.
3.  **Interpretation of Experimental Results:** While the quantitative results are strong, a deeper analysis would make them more conclusive.
    *   The comparison of computation times is challenging to interpret fairly, as the stopping criteria for the different algorithms are not specified. In particular, determining convergence for a Gibbs sampler is often heuristic, which may not be directly comparable to the tolerance-based criterion used for FAB.
    *   The source of the significant improvement in predictive likelihood is not fully disentangled. It is difficult to determine if the gains are due to the superiority of the FIC as a model selection criterion, or if the FAB optimization procedure is simply more effective at avoiding poor local optima than the competing algorithms. For instance, the IBP Gibbs sampler's tendency to learn an excessively large `K` on some datasets could indicate mixing problems rather than a fundamental flaw in the Bayesian posterior it is sampling from. Providing evidence that separates the quality of the objective from the quality of the optimization would strengthen the paper's claims.
