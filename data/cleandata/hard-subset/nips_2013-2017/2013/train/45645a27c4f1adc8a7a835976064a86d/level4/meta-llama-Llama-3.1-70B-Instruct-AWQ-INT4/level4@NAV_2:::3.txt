The paper presents an inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), which express each observation as a K-sized binary vector. The goal of inference is to learn the latent features for all observations, along with other model parameters. The presented EM-based algorithm uses a shrinkage mechanism to learn the latent features and infer the number of latent features (K). This approach is similar in spirit to nonparametric Bayesian latent feature models, such as the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). However, the algorithm does not use prior distributions inducing model parsimony but instead uses a model selection criterion, Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference (FAB), recently introduced in the context of mixture models and HMMs.

The FAB idea is based on expressing the log marginal likelihood in terms of the expected complete log-likelihood plus a model selection term that encourages shrinkage of the number of latent features (by specifying an upper bound on K and learning the correct K during inference). On some large datasets, FAB is shown to outperform other inference methods, including Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation methods for the IBP, in terms of running time and inference quality.

The technical quality of the paper appears to be sound, and the algorithmic details seem correct. The ability to deal with large datasets and learn the number of latent features is very appealing. However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithm, such as cases where it might perform worse than vanilla Gibbs sampling. Additionally, the experimental evaluation is not thorough enough and seems flawed at some points.

The algorithm is limited to binary latent feature models and cannot be applied to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA. The experimental evaluation does not provide a thorough comparison with other recent works on efficient inference in LFMs. Furthermore, the MEIBP algorithm proposed in [22] is applicable only to non-negative linear Gaussian models, making the artificial simulation and block data experiments invalid for the MEIBP baseline.

The paper could be improved by discussing the limitations of the proposed algorithm, providing a more thorough experimental evaluation, and including recent works on efficient inference in LFMs. Additionally, the experimental methodology for the proposed method and the baseline needs to be better explained, and justifications/explanations about why certain algorithms behaved in a certain way should be provided.

In terms of the four criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on originality, as the application of FAB to latent feature models is novel. However, it scores lower on clarity, as some of the experimental details are unclear. The paper scores well on significance, as it addresses an important problem in efficient inference in latent feature models. However, it scores lower on quality, due to the limitations and areas for improvement mentioned above.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel application of FAB to latent feature models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The algorithm is able to deal with large datasets and learn the number of latent features, making it a valuable tool for practitioners.
* The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm and its properties.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithm, which could be a significant drawback in certain scenarios.
* The experimental evaluation is not thorough enough and seems flawed at some points, which could impact the validity of the results.
* The algorithm is limited to binary latent feature models, which could limit its applicability in certain domains.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of latent feature models, but could be improved by addressing the limitations and areas for improvement mentioned above.
