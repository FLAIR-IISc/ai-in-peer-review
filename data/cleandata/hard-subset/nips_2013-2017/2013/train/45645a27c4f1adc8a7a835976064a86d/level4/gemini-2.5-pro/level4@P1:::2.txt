### Review

**Summary**

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to the domain of Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The FAB method and its associated Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) provide an efficient framework for model selection, but their application has previously been limited to models, such as mixture models, where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood has a block-diagonal structure.

The central contribution of this work is a novel asymptotic analysis that generalizes the FAB framework to LFMs, which do not satisfy this structural condition. The authors show that the FIC for LFMs surprisingly takes the same tractable form as that for mixture models. The resulting algorithm, FAB/LFM, uses a mean-field approximation for inference over the latent features and introduces an effective shrinkage acceleration technique. A key advantage of the proposed approach is its ability to automatically determine the number of latent features with minimal hyperparameter tuning. The authors provide empirical evidence on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating significant improvements in computational efficiency and competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods based on the Indian Buffet Process.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis (Lemma 1, Theorems 2 and 4) and a comprehensive set of experiments. The method is thoroughly evaluated against a wide range of competing approaches on multiple datasets, demonstrating its practical advantages. The provided analysis of parameter identifiability is also a valuable theoretical contribution that motivates a principled post-processing step for merging features. The paper appears to be a complete piece of work, and related literature is adequately cited.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The motivation for extending FAB to LFMs is well-articulated, and the technical derivations are presented in a logical sequence. The resulting algorithm is described with sufficient detail for the reader to understand its implementation.

**Originality and Significance**

This work is a solid and non-trivial generalization of previous research on FAB inference. While it combines several known techniques (e.g., Laplace approximation, mean-field inference, CCCP), the core technical contribution—deriving the FIC for LFMs via a novel analysis of the Hessian—is original and enables the application of this efficient framework to a broader and more complex class of models. The proposed shrinkage acceleration technique is an important practical refinement that further enhances the method's utility.

The significance of this work lies in providing a new, highly efficient, and automated tool for model selection in LFMs, a crucial and often difficult problem. The experimental results suggest that FAB/LFM can match or outperform state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian methods in prediction and model selection accuracy, often at a fraction of the computational cost. This strong combination of performance, speed, and automation makes the method a valuable contribution to the field, and it is likely to be used by other researchers and practitioners.

**Areas for Improvement**

The paper could be strengthened by a discussion of the method's limitations and potential failure modes. The proposed approach relies on a combination of several approximations (the asymptotic nature of FIC, the mean-field factorization of the posterior over latent variables, and the shrinkage acceleration heuristic). It would be beneficial to understand the conditions under which these approximations might break down or interact negatively. For example, how does performance degrade in low-data regimes where the asymptotic justification for FIC is less secure, or on problems with strong posterior dependencies that violate the mean-field assumption? An investigation into these aspects would provide a more complete picture of the method's applicability.
