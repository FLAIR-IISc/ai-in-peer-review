## Review for NIPS 2013 - Binary Latent Feature Model with Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation *x<sub>n</sub>* is represented by a binary latent vector *z<sub>n</sub>* indicating the presence or absence of *K* latent features. The proposed Expectation-Maximization (EM)-based algorithm simultaneously learns the latent features *z<sub>n</sub>* and infers the number of latent features *K*, utilizing a shrinkage mechanism. While inspired by nonparametric Bayesian LFM approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), the method departs from explicit prior distributions favoring model parsimony. Instead, it leverages Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference (FAB), a recently developed technique for model selection in mixture models and Hidden Markov Models. FAB formulates the log marginal likelihood as the expected complete log-likelihood plus a model selection term promoting shrinkage of *K* by enforcing an upper bound and learning an optimal value during inference. Experimental results on large datasets demonstrate that the FAB-based algorithm outperforms Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and IBP MAP estimation in terms of both runtime and inference quality.

**Quality:**

The technical quality of the paper appears sound, and the algorithmic details are presented clearly and correctly. The ability to handle large datasets and simultaneously infer the optimal number of latent features is a significant strength.

**Clarity:**

The methodological description is well-written and easy to follow. However, the experimental details require further clarification, as noted below.

**Originality:**

The application of the recently proposed FAB inference framework to latent feature models constitutes a novel contribution. While FAB has been explored in other contexts (mixture models, HMMs), its adaptation to LFMs represents a valuable extension.

**Significance:**

The paper tackles an important problem: efficient inference in latent feature models, including the challenging task of inferring the correct number of latent features.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper lacks a thorough discussion of its limitations. Specifically, it would benefit from addressing potential scenarios where the proposed algorithm might underperform compared to simpler methods like vanilla Gibbs sampling. Furthermore, a discussion of convergence properties, given the EM-like nature of the algorithm, is missing. The current formulation is restricted to binary latent feature models and cannot be readily extended to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA. 

The experimental evaluation, while promising, lacks robustness and thoroughness. It's unclear how the algorithm behaves in the small-data regime where asymptotic justifications may not hold; this should be addressed through discussion or additional experiments. The paper also overlooks relevant recent work on efficient inference in LFMs (see comments below). 

A critical flaw exists in the experimental comparison with the MEIBP algorithm [22]. MEIBP is applicable only to non-negative linear Gaussian models (requiring a positive-definite loading matrix *W*), while the synthetic and block data experiments in this paper do not adhere to this constraint. To enable a valid comparison, the synthetic dataset should be generated with a positive *W*.

**Other Comments:**

*   For the block image data, to ensure fair comparison, the IBP Gibbs sampler should utilize the same noise level provided to the FAB algorithm, rather than the 0.75 standard deviation heuristic.
*   The experiments comparing against VB-IBP [2] would be strengthened by employing the infinite variational version instead of a finite one. Furthermore, the experimental setup for VB-IBP lacks sufficient detail (e.g., number of restarts).
*   The discovery of 70 latent features by accelerated Gibbs sampling on the 49-dimensional Sonar data is surprising. This likely stems from poor initialization and/or an inadequately tuned noise variance.
*   Line 70 incorrectly identifies reference [21] as being about Gibbs sampling; it actually describes MAP estimation for the IBP. This requires correction.
*   The paper should acknowledge recent advancements in efficient inference leveraging small-variance asymptotics in nonparametric LFMs, such as the work presented in “MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes” (ICML 2013).

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 349: The recommended standard deviation for real data, as suggested in [1, 22], is 0.25 times the standard deviation of examples across all dimensions, not 0.75.

**Comments after Author Feedback:**

The authors' feedback addressed some initial concerns. However, several points require further attention before acceptance. 

Specifically, the experimental methodology – for both the proposed method and the baseline algorithms – requires more detailed explanation. Justifications or explanations are needed for observed algorithmic behaviors (e.g., why the Gibbs sampler used a fixed alpha, potentially leading to an overestimated *K*, or why hyperparameters were chosen as they were). 

Reference [21] still incorrectly states Gibbs sampling; it performs MAP inference and needs to be corrected.

Finally, the reference to MAD-Bayes inference for the IBP should be included.

**Overall Assessment:**

The proposed inference method for LFMs is interesting, and its scalability to large datasets coupled with its ability to infer *K* are appealing. However, the experimental evaluation requires significant refinement to substantiate the claims adequately. A more rigorous and well-justified experimental analysis is essential for acceptance.
