Summary:
The authors propose an extension of factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), enabling efficient model selection. FAB inference has been successfully applied to mixture models, and this work generalizes it to LFMs by leveraging a mean-field approximation for latent inference and accelerated shrinkage of the global number of features. The method offers increased automaticity in model selection, reducing the need for manual tuning.

Quality:
The paper presents thorough analysis and evaluates the proposed method against a range of competing approaches on multiple data sets. The references provided are sufficient, and the results demonstrate the method's effectiveness.

Clarity:
The paper is well-written and structured, making it easy to follow. The authors have done an excellent job of explaining the technical details and providing necessary background information.

Originality/Significance:
The work represents a logical extension and solid generalization of previous research in model selection for LFMs using FAB inference. By combining several approximations into a novel method, the authors have created a approach that works well. However, it would be beneficial to investigate the limitations of these approximations and understand when they may lead to poor performance. The authors should consider "pushing" the method to its limits to identify potential failure cases.

Strengths:

1. Efficient model selection: The proposed method enables automatic model selection with minimal manual tuning.
2. Generalization of FAB inference: The authors have successfully extended FAB inference to LFMs, expanding its applicability.
3. Accelerated shrinkage: The method's accelerated shrinkage technique significantly reduces computational cost.

Weaknesses:

1. Approximation limitations: The authors should investigate the limitations of the approximations used (FIC, mean-field, shrinkage) and their potential impact on performance.
2. Sensitivity analysis: It would be beneficial to conduct a sensitivity analysis to understand how the method's performance changes with different parameter settings and data characteristics.

Arguments for acceptance:

1. The method offers a significant improvement in computational efficiency while maintaining good performance.
2. The authors have provided thorough analysis and evaluation of the proposed method.
3. The work represents a valuable contribution to the field of model selection for LFMs.

Arguments against acceptance:

1. The method relies on several approximations, which may lead to poor performance in certain scenarios.
2. The authors have not thoroughly investigated the limitations of the approximations used.
3. The method's sensitivity to parameter settings and data characteristics is not well understood.

Recommendation:
Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above by investigating the limitations of the approximations and conducting a sensitivity analysis. Additionally, they should provide more details on the potential failure cases and how to mitigate them.
