This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The goal is to perform efficient inference and model selection simultaneously, specifically determining the number of latent features, K. The FAB framework is based on an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood, which yields a tractable objective function called the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC). A key contribution is showing that the FIC for LFMs takes a form similar to that for Mixture Models, despite LFMs having shared parameters across features and thus not meeting the block-diagonal Hessian condition previously assumed necessary for FAB. The resulting EM-style algorithm includes a "model-induced" regularization term that encourages shrinkage of irrelevant features, effectively performing model selection. The paper also introduces an acceleration technique based on a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation and provides an analysis of parameter identifiability. The method is empirically compared against several Indian Buffet Process (IBP) based approaches on synthetic and real-world datasets, where it is shown to be computationally efficient while achieving strong predictive and model selection performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of scalable inference and model selection for LFMs. Extending the FAB framework to this model class is a novel contribution. The key theoretical insight in Lemma 1, which shows how the necessary shrinkage term arises from the Hessian of the LFM likelihood, is elegant and broadens the applicability of the FAB methodology. The proposed method presents a compelling and principled alternative to computationally intensive non-parametric Bayesian methods like the IBP, which is of significant interest to the community.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is well-motivated, and the resulting algorithm is clearly presented. The theoretical analyses, including the CCCP interpretation that motivates the shrinkage acceleration and the discussion of parameter identifiability, add depth and rigor to the work. The guarantee of monotonic improvement of the objective function is a desirable property.
*   **Clarity:** The methodology is generally well-explained. The connection between the derived FIC and the automatic shrinkage of features is clearly articulated, making the mechanism behind the model selection intuitive.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The primary weaknesses of the paper lie in the experimental evaluation and a lack of discussion regarding the method's limitations. These should be addressed to strengthen the paper's contribution.

1.  **Experimental Comparison with MEIBP:** The comparison with the Maximum-Expectation IBP (MEIBP) method [22] appears to be flawed. The MEIBP algorithm is designed for models with non-negativity constraints on the feature matrix (W), but the artificial simulations and block data experiments seem to use a standard LFM with real-valued W. This makes the comparison on these datasets invalid. To rectify this, the authors should either a) generate synthetic data appropriate for MEIBP (i.e., with W ≥ 0) for a fair comparison, or b) remove the invalid MEIBP results and clarify why it was not an appropriate baseline for those specific experiments.

2.  **Fairness of Baseline Comparisons:** The experimental setup for the baseline methods could be improved to ensure a fairer comparison.
    *   For the block data experiment, the proposed FAB algorithm is given the true noise value, whereas the IBP sampler uses a heuristic value (0.75). The IBP sampler should also be provided with the true noise value to ensure a level playing field.
    *   The paper uses a finite variational version of IBP [3]. It would be more appropriate to compare against an infinite variational method if possible, or at least to justify the choice of the finite version.
    *   The experimental details for the baselines are sparse. For instance, were restarts used for the VB-IBP algorithm? How was the IBP hyperparameter `alpha` set? An `alpha` value of 3, as mentioned, could lead to an overestimation of K, potentially explaining the large number of features found by the Gibbs sampler on some datasets (e.g., 70 features on the Sonar data). More detailed reporting and justification of hyperparameter settings are needed.

3.  **Missing Discussion of Limitations:** The paper would benefit from a dedicated discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.
    *   **Asymptotic Assumptions:** The FAB framework is based on a large-sample (N → ∞) approximation. How does the method perform in small-data regimes where this assumption may not hold? Some discussion or an experiment on this would be valuable.
    *   **Convergence:** Like any EM-style algorithm, the proposed method is susceptible to local optima. A discussion of this issue, its potential impact on performance, and any heuristics used for initialization would be welcome.
    *   **Model Scope:** The current work is limited to binary LFMs with a Gaussian likelihood. Acknowledging this and discussing potential challenges or avenues for extending the framework to other observation models or real-valued features (e.g., Factor Analysis) would place the contribution in a broader context.

4.  **Omission of Related Work:** The paper misses an important and highly relevant piece of recent work: "MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes" (Broderick et al., ICML 2013). This work also proposes an efficient, MAP-based asymptotic inference method for LFMs. A discussion of this method and how FAB/LFM relates to it is essential for properly situating the paper's contribution.

**Minor Issues:**

*   The description of the noise-setting heuristic for real-world data seems to misquote the original papers [2, 22]. The authors should double-check and correct this detail.
*   The author feedback indicates a potential mischaracterization of a cited work. Please ensure all references and descriptions of prior methods are accurate.

**Summary of Review:**

This paper presents a novel, efficient, and theoretically-grounded algorithm for inference and model selection in latent feature models. The extension of the FAB framework is a solid contribution, and the empirical results are promising. However, the experimental evaluation contains significant flaws, particularly in the comparison to the MEIBP baseline and the general setup for other competing methods. A thorough revision of the experiments to ensure fairness, along with a more comprehensive discussion of the method's limitations and its relation to other asymptotic inference techniques, is necessary before the paper can be considered for publication.
