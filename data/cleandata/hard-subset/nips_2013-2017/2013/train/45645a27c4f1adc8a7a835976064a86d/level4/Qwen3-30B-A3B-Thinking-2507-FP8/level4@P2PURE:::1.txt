

This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. The approach resembles variational Bayes but instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximately integrates out the parameters using the BIC. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing IBP implementations.  

The proposed method appears reasonable and is motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. Its main advantage over other IBP inference methods is computational efficiency: it corresponds to an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, avoiding the higher cost of sampling or variational Bayes approaches.  

The technical contributions appear novel but incremental, essentially extending the FAB work of [3] and [4].  

Several aspects of the exposition are unclear due to mathematical imprecision. In Equation (3), the log-sum of the $z$ values is infinite if all values are zero. Since this occurs with nonzero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution $q$, why is the FIC score not always infinite?  

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated as" a specific expression, but it omits the asymptotic regime and key assumptions. Crucially, it is unclear how $K$ (the number of features) behaves as $N$ (data size) increases. The BIC is applied per component, implying a fixed finite $K$, which requires all feature activations to approach infinity. However, the IBP model inherently assumes an infinite number of components, with new features continuously observed as $N$ grows. Thus, fixing $K$ undermines a primary motivation for using the IBP.  

Minor issues: In Section 2, $p_k(X \mid z_{\cdot,k})$ should condition only on data points assigned to component $k$, not the entire dataset. Equation (4) requires an expectation on the left-hand side.  

In Table 1, the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores with reduced runtime compared to alternatives. While FAB runs faster by orders of magnitude in some cases, the results are ambiguous due to unspecified stopping criteria for the Gibbs sampler, which introduces arbitrariness in convergence.  

The predictive likelihood improvements are significant, but the source of the difference is unclear. The results would be more convincing if evidence showed the gains stem from the model selection criterion rather than algorithmic artifacts (e.g., Gibbs sampling converging to suboptimal local optima due to implementation details like initialization). The observation that Gibbs sampling learns an excessive number of components on some datasets supports this concern, as it suggests the issue lies with local optima—since Gibbs should exactly compute the marginal likelihood that FAB approximates.  

Overall, the approach is reasonable but incremental, with some mathematical imprecision. The experimental results show improvements in runtime and predictive likelihood, but insufficient analysis prevents clear interpretation of these results.
