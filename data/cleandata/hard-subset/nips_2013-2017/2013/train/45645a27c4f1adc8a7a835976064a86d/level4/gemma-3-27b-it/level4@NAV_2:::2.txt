The authors propose a method for efficient model selection in latent feature models (LFMs) by extending factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the factorized information criterion (FIC). FAB and FIC are established techniques for mixture models, approximating the marginal log-likelihood through factorization and Laplace approximation for tractable model selection. This work generalizes FAB to LFMs, employing a mean field approximation for latent variable inference and an accelerated shrinkage technique to promote feature selection. A key characteristic of the approach is its automation, requiring minimal manual parameter tuning. The authors demonstrate significant computational efficiency gains through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

The presented results are supported by thorough analysis, and the method is rigorously evaluated against a diverse set of competing approaches across multiple datasets. The referencing is comprehensive and appropriate.

The paper is clearly written and well-structured, facilitating understanding of the proposed method and its underlying principles.

This work represents a logical and well-executed extension of existing research in model selection for LFMs, leveraging FAB inference and FIC. The combination of several approximations into a novel and effective method is a noteworthy contribution. 

A potential area for future work could involve a more detailed investigation into the limitations of the proposed approach. Specifically, exploring scenarios where the interplay of the FIC approximation, mean field assumption, and shrinkage technique might lead to suboptimal performance – essentially, identifying the conditions under which the method “breaks” – would strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem—efficient model selection for LFMs—with a novel and theoretically grounded approach.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in computational efficiency compared to existing methods, particularly IBP-based approaches.
*   The clarity of the presentation and thoroughness of the analysis contribute to the paper's overall quality.
*   The theoretical analysis provides insights into the behavior of the proposed method, including parameter identifiability and a principled approach to feature merging.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on multiple approximations raises questions about the robustness of the method under various conditions. Further investigation into potential failure modes would be valuable.
*   While the paper presents strong empirical results, a more in-depth analysis of the sensitivity of the method to different data characteristics could be beneficial.
