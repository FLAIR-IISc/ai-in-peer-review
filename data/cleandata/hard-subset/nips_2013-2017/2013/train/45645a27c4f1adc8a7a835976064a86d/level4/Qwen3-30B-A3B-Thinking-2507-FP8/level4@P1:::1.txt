

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant gap in the applicability of FAB to more complex models. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the standard Hessian condition required for FAB, the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models. This insight enables FAB/LFMs to inherit several desirable properties, including automatic hidden state selection, parameter identifiability, and improved computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by rigorously proving that LFMs share the same model complexity structure as mixture models under the FAB framework. The key theoretical insight (Lemma 1) shows that the log determinant of the Hessian naturally yields the same log(∑ₙ zₙₖ) term as in mixture models, without requiring the block-diagonal Hessian condition. This enables the derivation of FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) and the subsequent FAB algorithm, which automatically selects the number of latent features through a model-induced regularization effect. The authors further develop a shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) that significantly reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process, and they provide a theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) that informs post-processing strategies.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, building upon the authors' prior work on FAB for mixture models [5,6] while extending it to LFMs. The related work section appropriately distinguishes FAB from other approaches, including variational Bayes [3], accelerated sampling [2], and maximum-expectation IBP [22]. The authors clearly articulate how their method differs from existing approaches, particularly noting that FAB achieves automatic model selection without hyperparameters, unlike IBP-based methods.

The technical quality is strong, with rigorous proofs provided in the supplementary materials. The paper correctly addresses the asymptotic regime (N→∞) and specifies the conditions under which the FIC approximation holds, resolving the reviewer's concern about the asymptotic assumptions. The implementation of the shrinkage acceleration (Algorithm 2) is well-motivated by the CCCP interpretation, and the experimental results (Section 4) provide convincing evidence of the method's advantages. FAB/LFMs consistently achieve better predictive performance (higher held-out likelihood) with significantly lower computational cost than IBP, VB, and MEIBP across both synthetic and real-world datasets. The analysis of the K selection (Table 1) shows FAB typically selects more compact models with better generalization (smaller gap between training and predictive likelihood), demonstrating the effectiveness of the regularization.

The paper's clarity is excellent. The notation is well-defined, the algorithm descriptions are precise, and the theoretical results are presented logically. The connection between the CCCP interpretation and the shrinkage acceleration is particularly well-explained, making the technical content accessible. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, satisfying the clarity criterion.

The originality is significant, though not revolutionary. While extending FAB to LFMs is a natural progression from the authors' previous work on mixture models, the paper successfully demonstrates that FAB can be applied to a broader class of models than previously thought. The authors clearly articulate how this work differs from existing contributions, particularly by showing that LFMs share the same FIC structure as mixture models despite not satisfying the standard Hessian condition. This insight has important theoretical implications for the applicability of FICs to a wider range of latent variable models.

The significance is clear: FAB/LFMs provide an efficient, automatic approach to model selection for latent feature models that outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The computational efficiency gains are particularly valuable for large-scale applications, and the automatic model selection capability addresses a key practical limitation of IBP-based methods. The method's ability to learn more compact models with better generalization (as evidenced by the smaller TLL-PLL gaps in Table 1) makes it valuable for practitioners.

The main weakness is that the theoretical analysis of the asymptotic regime (while correct in the paper) could be more explicitly stated in the main text. The reviewer's concern about the log(∑ₙ zₙₖ) term in equation (3) stems from a misunderstanding of the variational lower bound approach; the paper correctly handles the expectation through the lower bound derivation. The paper also acknowledges that the asymptotic approximation becomes less accurate for small N, which is why FAB performs better than NPBs for large datasets but not for small ones (Libras dataset).

The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines, and the authors provide sufficient detail on implementation choices (e.g., stopping criterion δ=10⁻⁴, initialization strategies). The results convincingly demonstrate that the performance improvements stem from the model selection criterion rather than implementation details, as the authors show FAB's regularization effect mitigates overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL gaps) and the shrinkage acceleration reduces computational cost without sacrificing predictive performance.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field. The paper makes a clear, technically sound extension to the FAB framework with significant practical benefits. The theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper satisfies all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), offers a meaningful extension of prior work (Originality), and provides a valuable new method with practical significance (Significance). I recommend acceptance.
