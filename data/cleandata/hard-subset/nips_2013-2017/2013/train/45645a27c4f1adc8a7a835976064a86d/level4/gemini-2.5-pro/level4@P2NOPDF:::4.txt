This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a recent and effective model selection method, to the domain of Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The FAB framework, which has shown strong results for mixture models and hidden Markov models, works by maximizing a lower bound on the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), an approximation of the marginal log-likelihood.

A key limitation of the original FAB framework is its requirement that the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood be block-diagonal. Latent feature models do not satisfy this condition, which has previously precluded the application of FAB. The main contribution of this work is to overcome this limitation. The authors successfully derive a tractable lower bound of the FIC for LFMs, demonstrating that it takes a form analogous to the FIC for mixture models. This insight allows for the efficient application of FAB to this broader and important class of models.

The proposed method, FAB/LFM, is empirically evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Its performance is compared against several established baselines for LFMs, including fast Gibbs sampling with an Indian Buffet Process (IBP) prior, Variational Bayes with an IBP, and Maximum-Expectation IBP (MEIBP). The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of FAB/LFM, showing superior performance in predictive accuracy while also being more computationally efficient than the competing methods.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a thorough and detailed derivation of the FIC lower bound for LFMs, making the technical contribution clear and verifiable.
2.  **Novel Contribution:** The extension of FAB to models with non-block-diagonal Hessians is a significant technical achievement. It broadens the applicability of a powerful model selection framework to an important class of models where it was previously not viable.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and the results are compelling. The proposed FAB/LFM method consistently outperforms strong baselines on multiple datasets, not only in terms of model performance but also in computational efficiency. This provides strong evidence for the practical utility of the approach.

**Points for Consideration:**
While the work is a direct extension of the existing FAB framework, its theoretical contribution—circumventing the block-diagonal Hessian requirement—is non-trivial and significantly expands the scope of FAB. The practical implications, demonstrated by the strong empirical results, justify the focus on this extension.

**Conclusion:**
In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension to an important inference framework. The method is clearly explained, the derivations are thorough, and the empirical results are convincing. This work will be of significant interest to researchers working on Bayesian nonparametrics, model selection, and latent feature modeling. I recommend acceptance.
