This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features. The main idea is to derive a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs by analyzing the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood and showing that, asymptotically, the FIC takes the same functional form as in mixture models. Leveraging this, the authors propose a practical FAB algorithm for LFMs that alternates an E-step (with a mean-field approximation on Z), an M-step, and a shrinkage step that prunes inactive features. They relate the E-step updates to a convex–concave procedure (CCCP), propose a shrinkage acceleration heuristic, and argue that FAB yields automatic feature selection and parameter identifiability. Empirically, they compare against several Indian Buffet Process (IBP) baselines (Gibbs, VB, MEIBP) and report improved predictive likelihoods and substantially lower run times in several datasets.

Relation to prior work: FAB has previously been developed and analyzed for mixture models and HMMs. The paper’s technical novelty lies in extending the FIC machinery to LFMs that do not satisfy the usual block-diagonal Hessian condition, via an asymptotic analysis showing that log det terms decompose into ∑k log∑n znk. Algorithmically, the resulting FAB procedure resembles EM with additional complexity penalties and an automatic shrinkage mechanism, in contrast to more expensive sampling or VB methods commonly used with IBPs.

Strengths:
- A plausible asymptotic argument that yields an FIC for LFMs with the same regularization terms (log∑n znk) as in mixture models.
- A simple, scalable algorithm that is computationally similar to EM and includes an effective pruning mechanism. The CCCP view is helpful and the shrinkage acceleration is sensible in practice.
- Empirical results often show better predictive likelihoods and significantly reduced computation time compared to several IBP implementations.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Mathematical precision and assumptions. Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated as” a certain expression, but the asymptotic regime and assumptions are not stated clearly. In particular, how is K treated as N grows? The argument seems to require a fixed finite K so that ∑n znk → ∞ for each active feature (as in BIC-style approximations applied per component). Under the IBP, K grows with N and new features continue to appear; assuming fixed K removes a key motivation for nonparametric modeling. Please state precise conditions (e.g., K fixed vs. growing with N, lower bounds on ∑n znk, regularity conditions on Λ and W) under which Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 hold.
- Potential divergence in the FIC term. In equation (3), the FIC includes −(Dk/2) log∑n znk. If ∑n znk = 0, log 0 = −∞ and the overall FIC becomes +∞. Since under any nondegenerate q there is nonzero probability that all znk = 0 for some k, why doesn’t Eq[log∑n znk] = −∞ make the FIC infinite? Do you impose constraints or smoothing (e.g., ε offsets) to avoid this? Please clarify the conditions under which the lower bound remains finite.
- Exposition details. In Section 2, shouldn’t pk(X|z·k) involve only the data assigned to component k, rather than the entire dataset? In equation (4), there appears to be a missing expectation on the left-hand side. These points make the exposition harder to follow.
- Experimental protocol. In Table 1, FAB often finishes much faster and yields higher predictive likelihoods. However, the stopping criteria for all methods are not stated in sufficient detail to enable a fair comparison. In particular, the time at which the Gibbs sampler is stopped can be arbitrary. Please specify stopping rules (e.g., effective sample size or convergence diagnostics for MCMC; tolerance on the lower bound or change in parameters for optimization-based methods) and ensure comparable termination across methods.
- Source of predictive gains. The improvements in predictive likelihood are encouraging, but it is unclear whether they stem from the model selection criterion or from differences in optimization and susceptibility to local optima. The observation that the Gibbs sampler sometimes learns too many components suggests local optima/poor mixing rather than a principled model comparison advantage, since in theory it targets the true posterior (which FAB approximates). Additional evidence would strengthen the claims, e.g.,:
  - Compare FAB against a variational EM/EM baseline with the same K (fixed a priori or selected by cross-validation) and identical initializations, to isolate the effect of the FIC-based shrinkage.
  - Report variability across multiple random initializations for all methods.
  - Provide ablations (with/without the CCCP-based acceleration and merge post-processing) to show their contributions.

Quality: The high-level derivation is plausible and interesting, but some key assumptions and edge cases (e.g., log 0) are not treated rigorously in the main text. The algorithm is sound and practically motivated.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized, but several equations are imprecise or underspecified. Clarifying the asymptotics, the role of K, and the conditions that keep the bound finite would improve readability and rigor.

Originality: The contribution is novel but incremental—an extension of prior FAB work to LFMs with a useful acceleration heuristic and identifiability discussion.

Significance: The method is practically appealing; the empirical results suggest meaningful gains in both speed and predictive performance. With clarified theory and more controlled comparisons, the impact would be stronger.

Pros:
- Scalable EM-like inference with automatic feature selection.
- Sensible asymptotic justification and CCCP interpretation.
- Strong empirical performance in several datasets.

Cons:
- Asymptotic regime and assumptions insufficiently specified.
- Potential divergence issue in the FIC when features are inactive.
- Some imprecision in equations/notation.
- Experimental stopping criteria and source of gains need clearer analysis.

Overall: A reasonable and practically useful extension of FAB to LFMs with promising results, but the paper would benefit from tighter mathematical assumptions and more carefully controlled experiments to substantiate the claimed advantages.
