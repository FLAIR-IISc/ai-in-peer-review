This paper introduces a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, offering an alternative to variational Bayes by approximating parameter integration via the BIC. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood compared to existing IBP implementations.

The proposed method is well-motivated by asymptotic reasoning and offers a compelling computational advantage; it resembles an EM algorithm with added complexity penalties, contrasting with the greater expense of sampling or variational Bayes techniques. However, the technical contributions, while novel, appear incremental, building directly on previous work such as FAB [3, 4]. 

The exposition contains some ambiguities due to imprecision in the mathematical formulations. For example, equation (3) involves a logarithmic sum that becomes infinite when all input values are zero – a scenario with non-zero probability under any non-degenerate posterior – raising the question of why the resulting FIC score isn't consistently infinite. Furthermore, Theorem 2 describes an asymptotic approximation of the marginal likelihood without clearly defining the asymptotic regime or underlying assumptions, particularly concerning the behavior of *K* as *N* increases. The application of BIC to each component suggests a fixed, finite *K*, potentially conflicting with the IBP model’s inherent ability to generate an infinite number of components as data accumulates, thereby diminishing a core motivation for utilizing the IBP.

Several minor points require clarification. In section 2, the likelihood *p<sub>k</sub>(X | z<sub>⋅, k</sub>)* should likely consider only data points assigned to component *k*, not the entire dataset. Additionally, an expectation symbol is missing on the left-hand side of equation (4). 

The quantitative results in Table 1 show the proposed method achieving higher predictive likelihoods in shorter runtimes. While FAB demonstrates substantially faster completion in some instances, interpreting these results is hampered by the unspecified stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler, appearing somewhat arbitrary. 

The observed improvements in predictive likelihood are noteworthy, but their source remains unclear. A more thorough analysis is needed to determine whether these gains stem from the model selection criterion itself, or from differences in how each algorithm converges to local optima – a factor likely influenced by implementation details such as initialization. The Gibbs sampler’s tendency to learn an excessive number of components in certain datasets supports the possibility of suboptimal convergence, given its theoretical ability to compute the exact likelihood approximated by FAB.

In summary, while the approach is sensible and potentially valuable, its contribution is somewhat incremental. The mathematical presentation requires refinement, and further analysis is necessary to confidently interpret the experimental results.
