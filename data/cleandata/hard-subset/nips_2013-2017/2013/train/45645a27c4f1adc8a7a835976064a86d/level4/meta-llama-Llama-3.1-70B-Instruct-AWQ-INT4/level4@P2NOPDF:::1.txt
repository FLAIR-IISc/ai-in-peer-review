This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, which is analogous to variational Bayes but integrates out the parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach is motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument and appears to be a reasonable extension of existing methods. Its primary advantage over other IBP inference methods is its computational efficiency, as it corresponds to an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, rather than relying on more expensive sampling or variational Bayes algorithms.

While the technical contributions seem novel, they are essentially an extension of the FAB work of [3] and [4], making them somewhat incremental. However, some parts of the exposition are confusing due to imprecise math. For instance, in equation (3), the log sum of the z values is infinite if all values are zero, which has a nonzero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution q. This raises the question of why the FIC score is not always infinite.

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood can be asymptotically approximated, but it lacks clarity on the asymptotic regime and assumptions. Specifically, it is unclear how K is assumed to behave as N increases. Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem seems to require a fixed finite K, which contradicts the IBP model's assumption of an infinite number of components. This assumption removes one of the primary motivations for using the IBP.

Minor points of note include the omission of an expectation on the left-hand side of equation (4) and the potential error in section 2, where pk(X | z{., k}) should only include data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset.

The quantitative results in Table 1 show that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores in less time compared to alternative methods. However, the lack of a specified stopping criterion makes it challenging to interpret these results, particularly for the Gibbs sampler. The improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, but it is unclear whether these differences are due to the model selection criterion or the algorithms getting stuck in worse local optima. Further analysis is necessary to determine the source of these improvements and to provide more convincing evidence for the proposed approach.

Overall, the proposed approach appears reasonable, but its novelty is somewhat incremental. While the experimental results show improvements in running time and predictive likelihood, further analysis is required to interpret these results accurately.
