This paper proposes a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. It resembles variational Bayes, except that instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximately integrates out the parameters using a BIC-style penalty. Empirically, the method achieves improved held-out likelihood compared to several existing IBP implementations.

The approach is reasonable and motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. Its main practical advantage over existing IBP inference methods is computational: it reduces to an EM algorithm augmented with complexity penalties, avoiding the higher costs of sampling or full variational Bayes.

That said, the technical contribution feels novel but incremental—largely an extension of the FAB framework in [3] and [4]. Some parts of the exposition were confusing due to imprecise mathematics:

- In equation (3), taking the log of the sum of z entries diverges to −∞ if all entries are zero. Since this event has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution q, why doesn’t the FIC score collapse to −∞?
- Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated as …” without specifying the asymptotic regime or assumptions. In particular, what is the behavior of K as N grows? Because a BIC-style penalty is applied per component, the argument appears to require a fixed finite K so that the number of activations for every feature goes to infinity. Under the IBP, however, the number of components is infinite and new components continue to appear with more data. Assuming finite K undermines one of the main motivations for using the IBP in the first place.

Minor points:
- In Section 2, shouldn’t pk(X | z·,k) be defined using only those data points assigned to component k, rather than the entire dataset?
- In equation (4), there should be an expectation on the left-hand side.

Experimental results (Table 1) show higher predictive likelihood in less time relative to alternatives. FAB sometimes finishes orders of magnitude faster, but the stopping criterion is unspecified, making the comparison hard to interpret; in particular, when to stop the Gibbs sampler seems arbitrary. More importantly, while the gains in predictive likelihood are substantial, it is unclear whether they stem from the proposed model selection criterion or from differences in optimization dynamics (e.g., local optima, initialization, or other implementation details). The observation that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets suggests issues with local optima or premature stopping; in theory, if run sufficiently long, Gibbs sampling targets the true posterior and marginal likelihood that FAB only approximates.

Overall: the proposed approach makes sense and is computationally attractive, but feels somewhat incremental relative to FAB. Parts of the math require clarification, especially the conditions underlying the asymptotic approximation and the handling of degenerate z configurations. The empirical results are promising in both runtime and predictive likelihood, but stronger analysis and clearer experimental protocols (e.g., explicit stopping criteria, diagnostics for convergence, sensitivity to initialization, and ablations isolating the model selection penalty) are needed to convincingly attribute the gains to the proposed criterion rather than to optimization artifacts.
