This paper introduces a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. It is similar to variational Bayes, but instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximately integrates out the parameters using a BIC-based approach. The authors report improved held-out likelihoods relative to several existing IBP implementations.

The approach appears reasonable and is motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. Its main practical advantage over other IBP inference methods is computational: it reduces to an EM-style algorithm with additional complexity penalties, avoiding the higher costs of sampling or standard variational Bayes.

The technical contributions seem novel but incremental—essentially an extension of the FAB framework in [3] and [4].

Some parts of the exposition are confusing due to imprecise mathematics. In equation (3), the logarithm of the sum of z values diverges to −∞ if all entries are zero. Because this event has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution q, why doesn’t the FIC objective blow up to −∞?

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated as” a certain expression, but the asymptotic regime and assumptions are not specified. In particular, how does K behave as N grows? Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem seems to require a fixed, finite K so that the number of activations of every feature goes to infinity. Under the IBP, the number of components is infinite and new components continue to appear as the dataset grows, so assuming a finite K removes one of the motivations for using the IBP.

Minor points:
- In Section 2, shouldn’t pk(X | z·,k) include only the data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset?
- In equation (4), there should be an expectation on the left-hand side.

In the quantitative results in Table 1, the proposed method attains higher predictive likelihoods in less time than competing methods. While FAB converges orders of magnitude faster in some cases, it is unclear how to interpret this because the stopping criterion is not specified. In particular, the decision of when to stop the Gibbs sampler appears arbitrary.

The gains in predictive likelihood are substantial, but their source is unclear. The results would be more persuasive if there were evidence that the improvements stem from the model selection criterion itself rather than from different algorithms getting trapped in poorer local optima—an effect that likely depends on implementation details such as initialization strategies. The observation that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets suggests a local optima issue, since in principle it targets the exact likelihood that FAB is approximating.

Overall, the proposed approach is sensible but somewhat incremental. Some of the mathematics is imprecise. The experiments show improvements in runtime and predictive likelihood over prior methods, but, as noted above, there is insufficient analysis to clearly interpret these results.
