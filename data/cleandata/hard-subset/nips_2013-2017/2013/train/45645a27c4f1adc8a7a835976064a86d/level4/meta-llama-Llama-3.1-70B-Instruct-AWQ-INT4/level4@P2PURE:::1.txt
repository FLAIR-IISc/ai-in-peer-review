This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, which is an extension of variational Bayes that approximately integrates out the parameters using the BIC, rather than assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach is motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument and seems like a reasonable thing to do. Its main advantage relative to other methods for IBP inference is that it corresponds to an EM algorithm with some additional complexity penalties, rather than the more expensive sampling or variational Bayes algorithms, making it computationally more efficient.

The technical contributions appear to be novel but incremental, as they are essentially an extension of the work presented in [3] and [4]. However, some parts of the exposition are unclear due to imprecise math. For instance, equation (3) implies that the log sum of the z values would be infinite if all the values are zero, which has a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution q. This raises the question of why the FIC score is not always infinite.

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood can be asymptotically approximated, but it lacks clarity on the asymptotic regime and assumptions. Specifically, it is unclear how K is assumed to behave as N increases. Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem seems to require a fixed finite K, so that the number of activations of every feature would approach infinity. However, under the IBP model, the number of components is infinite, and new components would continue to be observed as the amount of data increases, which undermines one of the motivations for using the IBP.

Some minor points also need to be addressed. In section 2, pk(X | z{\cdot, k}) should only include the data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset. Additionally, equation (4) is missing an expectation on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results in Table 1 show that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores in less time compared to alternative methods. However, the stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler is not specified, making it difficult to interpret the results. The improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, but it is unclear whether the difference is due to the model selection criterion or the algorithms getting stuck in worse local optima. The latter is likely to depend on the implementation details, such as initialization strategies. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components in some datasets suggests that it may be a problem with local optima, as in theory it should be exactly computing the likelihood that FAB is approximating.

Overall, the proposed approach seems reasonable, but it is somewhat incremental. Some of the math appears imprecise, and while the experimental results show improvements in running time and predictive likelihood, further analysis is needed to interpret these results convincingly.
