Summary:
This paper proposes an EM-based inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation xn is associated with a binary vector zn indicating the presence of K latent features. The key contribution is to combine EM with a shrinkage mechanism derived from Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to jointly infer the latent feature assignments and automatically determine K. Unlike nonparametric Bayesian LFMs (e.g., the Indian Buffet Process, IBP), FAB does not rely on priors for sparsity; instead, it uses an asymptotic approximation of the marginal likelihood that decomposes into an expected complete log-likelihood plus a model selection penalty, which encourages pruning of superfluous features. On several large datasets, the proposed method is reported to outperform Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP-based IBP methods in both speed and estimation quality.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound and the algorithmic details are coherent. The integration of a principled shrinkage term into an EM framework is elegant, and the ability to scale to large datasets while inferring K is compelling. However, important aspects of the experimental methodology and baselines require clarification and, in some cases, correction.

Clarity:
The methodological presentation is generally clear. Some experimental settings, choices of hyperparameters, and baseline configurations are insufficiently documented, making it hard to assess fairness and reproducibility.

Originality:
FAB has been introduced previously for mixture models and HMMs; applying it to LFMs is, to my knowledge, novel and worthwhile.

Significance:
Efficient inference for LFMs with automatic model size selection is an important problem. If validated with a more rigorous empirical study, this approach could be impactful for large-scale applications.

Weaknesses:
- Lack of limitations analysis. The paper does not discuss scenarios where the method might underperform relative to standard Gibbs sampling (e.g., highly multimodal posteriors, strong local optima).
- Convergence considerations. As an EM-like procedure, sensitivity to initialization and the potential for poor local optima should be discussed, with practical guidance or diagnostics.
- Model scope. The method is limited to binary LFMs; it does not directly address continuous factor models such as factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.
- Small-sample behavior. Since FAB relies on asymptotic approximations, the performance in small-data regimes is unclear; discussion or experiments are needed.
- Experimental evaluation. Several aspects of the empirical study are incomplete or problematic (see below), undermining some of the conclusions.
- Related work coverage. Some recent advances in efficient LFM inference are missing from the discussion (see below).

Experimental concerns and requests for clarification:
- MEIBP applicability (Ref. [22]). MEIBP is designed for non-negative linear–Gaussian models (W ≥ 0). Comparisons on synthetic and block-image datasets that violate this assumption are invalid as baselines. If MEIBP is included, the data generation and real-data preprocessing should ensure non-negativity of W (or features) to respect the model.
- Noise variance fairness. For the block images, the FAB method is given the true noise level. For a fair comparison, the IBP Gibbs sampler should be provided the same value rather than a 0.75 std-dev heuristic.
- Variational IBP settings (Ref. [2]). Consider using the infinite variational formulation instead of a finite truncation. Provide full details: truncation level (if any), priors, initialization strategies, number of restarts, and convergence criteria.
- Surprising Sonar result. The accelerated Gibbs sampler reportedly discovers ~70 latent features on 49-dimensional Sonar data. This likely reflects poor initialization and/or misspecified noise variance or hyperparameters. Please investigate and report the sensitivity to initialization and noise settings.
- Fixed hyperparameters and K inflation. If the Gibbs sampler uses a fixed α (IBP mass parameter), justify the choice and analyze its effect on K. Otherwise, infer α or conduct sensitivity analyses.
- Runtime reporting. Ensure consistent hardware, stopping criteria, and implementation details across methods. Report wall-clock times with variance across runs.
- Evaluation metrics. Clarify the metrics used to assess “inference quality” (e.g., held-out likelihood, reconstruction error, predictive performance), and include uncertainty estimates.

Related and missing references:
- MAP-based small-variance asymptotics for nonparametric LFMs: “MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes,” ICML 2013, which provides efficient objective-driven alternatives closely related in spirit to FAB-style penalized formulations. A discussion contrasting FAB with small-variance/“hard” Bayesian limits would strengthen the paper.
- Please correct the description of Ref. [21]: it performs MAP inference for the IBP, not Gibbs sampling (similar in spirit to Ref. [22]).

Minor comments:
- Real-data noise setting: The recommendation in [1, 22] is not to fix the noise std-dev to 0.75, but to set it to 0.25 times the empirical std-dev of the examples across all dimensions.
- Provide pseudocode or a concise algorithmic summary with stopping criteria and pruning schedule for reproducibility.
- Report how K evolves during training (feature birth/pruning curves), which would illuminate the shrinkage behavior.

After author feedback:
The feedback addressed some questions, but several issues remain to be fixed before acceptance:
- Expand and justify the experimental methodology for both the proposed method and all baselines. Explain anomalous behaviors (e.g., inflated K for Gibbs with fixed α; effects of noise hyperparameters).
- Correct Ref. [21] as a MAP method, not a sampler.
- Include and discuss MAD-Bayes-style small-variance approaches for IBP/LFMs, clarifying conceptual and empirical differences.
- Revisit comparisons with MEIBP under valid assumptions (non-negative models), or remove those comparisons if they cannot be made fairly.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-motivated and technically solid application of FAB to LFMs, offering an attractive and scalable alternative that can automatically select K. The empirical study, however, needs stronger controls, clearer reporting, and fairer baseline configurations. With these issues addressed—especially correcting baseline assumptions, clarifying hyperparameters, and adding analysis of convergence and small-sample behavior—the paper would make a valuable contribution.
