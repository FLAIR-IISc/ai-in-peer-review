Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features. The central technical result is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood showing that the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same form as that for mixture models: the model complexity includes a −(D/2)∑k log(∑n znk) term. This induces automatic shrinkage of rarely used features and yields a tractable lower bound that can be maximized via an EM-like algorithm. The authors further (i) relate the FAB E-step to a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), deriving an acceleration that speeds shrinkage of irrelevant features, and (ii) prove an identifiability property that motivates a post hoc merge step. Empirically, the method outperforms several IBP-based approaches (Gibbs, variational IBP, MEIBP) in model selection, predictive likelihood, and often in wall-clock time.

Relation to prior work
FAB has previously been developed for mixtures and HMMs, where block-diagonal Hessians make FIC derivations straightforward. LFMs lack that structure, and the paper’s Lemma 1 shows the same complexity form nevertheless emerges asymptotically. The work is compared to IBP-based inference (Gibbs sampling, variational IBP, MEIBP), and to MAP-asymptotic approximations like MAD-Bayes. Conceptually, the approach resembles BIC-like asymptotics applied componentwise, but here the key log-activation penalty arises from the Hessian structure in LFMs rather than a priori model dimension counting.

Strengths
- A clear and useful extension of FAB to a widely used model class. The Hessian analysis (Lemma 1/Theorem 2) is interesting and suggests broader applicability of FIC beyond mixtures/HMMs.
- The resulting algorithm is simple, hyperparameter-free, and EM-like, with automatic feature selection driven by the −(D/2) log activation term.
- The CCCP interpretation and shrinkage acceleration are practical and lead to large runtime savings.
- The identifiability analysis is valuable for LFMs and leads to an intuitive merge post-processing step.
- Solid empirical results: consistently strong predictive log-likelihoods and competitive or better runtime, often with more compact models (smaller K).

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
Quality/technical soundness
- Asymptotic regime: Theorem 2 states that the marginal log-likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated,” but the assumptions are only sketched. Please make precise: (i) N → ∞ with what behavior of K (fixed, slowly growing, or data-dependent)? The log ∑n znk penalty suggests each active feature must have ∑n znk → ∞; this seems to require fixed finite K or at least that the number of active features grows slowly enough. How does this mesh with the infinite-component motivation of the IBP?
- Conditions for Lemma 1: The supplementary assumptions are key. Please state in the main text the identifiability/regularity, boundedness, and eigenvalue/separation conditions under which log det|F(d)| decomposes into ∑k log(∑n znk)/N + Op(1).
- Handling zero/always-on features: In Eq. (3)/(8), log(∑n znk) is −∞ when a feature is never used and log N when always used. In expectation under any nondegenerate q(Z), there is positive mass on ∑n znk = 0. How is Eq[log ∑n znk] defined/avoided? The later algorithmic sections introduce a lower bound via linearization (Eq. (4)) with π̃k > 0 and an explicit removal of features at the boundaries (∑n µnk/N ∈ {0,1}). Please surface this early and explain that (a) the algorithm never evaluates the problematic expectation directly, (b) the bound remains finite, and (c) boundary features are eliminated so that the approximation remains valid.
- Minor derivations: In Eq. (4), an expectation is missing on the left-hand side; please add it. In Sec. 1.1, pk(X|z·k) should be over points assigned to k (not the entire dataset) for mixtures; clarifying this will avoid confusion.
- Prior usage: FAB is said to asymptotically ignore priors; please delineate precisely where priors matter (η(πk) term) and when they are dominated by the −(D/2) log activation penalty.

Clarity
- More care with notation and indexing would help (e.g., consistent use of boldface for vectors, clear definition of H(q), Op(1)). Explicitly state the monotonicity guarantee for the alternating updates and the stopping criterion (δ) in the main text.
- The CCCP view is insightful; briefly explain why multiple inner CCCP steps accelerate feature shrinkage and how Tshrink impacts both runtime and convergence.

Originality
- The work is primarily an extension of prior FAB theory to LFMs, but the asymptotic Hessian argument and the CCCP-based acceleration are nontrivial and practically meaningful. The identifiability/merge perspective is a nice addition.

Significance
- If the assumptions hold, the approach offers a practical alternative to IBP inference with fewer tunables and strong model selection. The empirical results are promising, but stronger evidence is needed to attribute gains to the model selection criterion rather than optimization artifacts.

Experimental evaluation
- Fairness/compute budgets: FAB often finishes far faster, but stopping criteria differ across methods. Please specify stopping rules for all baselines and, ideally, enforce comparable compute budgets (e.g., fixed wall-clock or iteration caps) and report performance as a function of time.
- Initialization/local optima: Since all methods can be sensitive to initialization, report variability over multiple random restarts for FAB/EM/MEIBP and show means/variances. The observation that Gibbs learns too many components could reflect convergence/stopping issues; show trace plots or effective sample sizes.
- Ablations to isolate model selection: To support the claim that gains come from the FIC penalty, compare FAB to EM with (i) fixed K matched to FAB’s selected K and (ii) an oracle K (or a sweep over K with selection by validation likelihood). Report PLL gaps under identical K to separate selection from optimization effects.
- Baseline parity: Some baselines fix the noise variance while FAB learns it; consider matching this setting or re-running baselines with learned noise if available. For IBP, α was fixed at 3; sensitivity analyses would help. Clarify MEIBP applicability to nonnegative data and how constraints were handled.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually clean extension of FAB to LFMs with a compelling asymptotic argument.
- Simple, hyperparameter-free algorithm with automatic feature selection and identifiability-aware post-processing.
- Strong empirical performance in predictive likelihood and often in runtime.

Cons:
- Asymptotic assumptions and regimes are underspecified; the role of K vs N is critical and should be explicit.
- Some mathematical imprecision (expectations, boundary cases), which could be clarified.
- Experimental methodology needs tighter controls (stopping criteria, initialization variability, ablations) to convincingly attribute the gains to the proposed criterion.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a useful and practically relevant extension of FAB with promising results. Clarifying the asymptotic assumptions and tightening the experimental protocol would strengthen the contribution.
