## Review of “Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models”

This paper presents a novel inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), aiming to efficiently learn latent feature assignments and the dimensionality of the latent space. The approach extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously applied to mixture models and hidden Markov models, to the LFM context. The core idea is to leverage a model selection criterion – FAB – to induce sparsity in the latent feature space without relying on explicit priors, offering a computationally attractive alternative to existing methods like Indian Buffet Processes (IBP). The algorithm demonstrates promising performance on several datasets, outperforming Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation methods for the IBP in terms of runtime and model selection.

**Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is generally sound. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, particularly Lemma 1 which addresses the condition previously limiting FAB's applicability, appears mathematically rigorous. The proposed acceleration mechanism based on a connection to the convex-concave procedure (CCCP) is a valuable contribution. The algorithmic details are clear and appear correctly implemented. The ability to scale to large datasets and simultaneously learn the model dimensionality is a significant strength.

**Clarity:** The methodology section is well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the core concepts of FAB inference and its adaptation to LFMs. However, the experimental setup could benefit from more detailed explanations (see Weaknesses below). The notation is generally consistent, though potentially challenging for readers unfamiliar with the FAB framework.

**Originality:** Applying the FAB framework to latent feature models represents a novel contribution. While the method builds upon prior work on FAB for other models, extending it to LFMs, which do not naturally satisfy the initial assumptions, is a significant step. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the proposed shrinkage acceleration method further enhance the originality.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a crucial problem in latent feature modeling: efficient inference and automatic model selection. LFMs are widely used in various applications, and the proposed method offers a potentially scalable and effective solution. The ability to avoid hand-tuning hyperparameters, a common challenge in Bayesian nonparametrics, is also a notable advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Discussion of Limitations:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the algorithm’s limitations. Specifically, under what conditions might the method perform poorly compared to alternative approaches like Gibbs sampling? What are the potential drawbacks of the asymptotic approximation used in FAB, particularly in small-sample regimes?
* **Convergence Concerns:** As an EM-like procedure, convergence properties deserve more attention. A discussion of potential convergence issues and strategies to address them (e.g., different initialization schemes, convergence criteria) would strengthen the paper.
* **Model Restriction:** The current algorithm is limited to binary latent feature models. Addressing this limitation or discussing potential extensions to continuous-valued latent features would broaden the applicability of the work.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, lacks sufficient detail and justification for certain choices. The parameter settings for baseline methods (e.g., α for IBP, noise variance for VB) are not always clearly explained or justified.
* **Small Sample Regime:** The paper does not adequately address the algorithm’s performance in scenarios where the asymptotic assumptions may not hold. Experiments with smaller datasets or a discussion of the potential impact of finite sample size would be valuable.
* **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks recent work on efficient inference in LFMs, specifically “MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes” (Broderick et al., ICML 2013), which presents a complementary approach based on small-variance asymptotics.
* **MEIBP Applicability:** The comparison with MEIBP (Reed & Ghahramani, ICML 2012) is flawed. MEIBP is designed for non-negative linear Gaussian models (with positive loadings), rendering the comparisons on synthetic and block image data invalid. The experimental setup should be adjusted to reflect the constraints of MEIBP, or the comparison should be omitted.
* **Inconsistent Reference:** Reference [21] is incorrectly described as relating to Gibbs sampling; it actually proposes a MAP estimation framework. This should be corrected.

**Further Comments & Specific Points:**

* For the block image data, ensuring a fair comparison requires using the same noise variance for all algorithms, including the IBP Gibbs sampler.
* For VB-IBP, utilizing the infinite variational version would provide a more robust comparison. More detailed reporting of experimental settings, such as the number of restarts used, is needed.
* The reported number of latent features discovered by accelerated Gibbs sampling on the Sonar dataset (70 for a 49-dimensional dataset) seems unusually high and warrants further investigation. The authors should examine the impact of initialization and noise variance on this result.
* Line 349: The suggested noise variance setting of 0.25 times the standard deviation across dimensions aligns with best practices and should be clarified.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound extension of FAB inference to latent feature models.
* The proposed method offers a scalable and efficient alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametrics for LFM inference.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on several datasets.
* The shrinkage acceleration mechanism represents a valuable contribution to reducing computational cost.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail and justification, potentially compromising the validity of the results.
* The paper does not adequately address the algorithm’s limitations or performance in small-sample regimes.
* The comparison with MEIBP is flawed, and the omission of recent relevant work weakens the discussion of related work.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising new approach to inference in latent feature models. While the technical quality is high and the potential impact is significant, several issues related to experimental methodology and a lack of comprehensive discussion need to be addressed before acceptance. **I recommend acceptance with major revisions.** Specifically, the authors should carefully review the experimental setup, address the limitations of the algorithm, include relevant related work, and correct the inaccurate reference. A more thorough and nuanced discussion of the results would significantly strengthen the paper.
