Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and sequential latent-variable models to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB maximizes a tractable lower bound to the factorized information criterion (FIC), which asymptotically equals the marginal log-likelihood, enabling automated model selection without hand-tuned priors. FAB had previously required a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood, a condition not satisfied by LFMs due to parameter sharing across features.

The core technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian in LFMs (Lemma 1) showing that log det|F(d)| contributes the same log(sum_n z_nk) regularizer as in mixture models, even without the block-diagonal assumption. This leads to an FIC for LFMs with the same form as for MMs (Theorem 2), and thus to a FAB algorithm for LFMs. The algorithm employs a mean-field variational approximation on Z, yields closed-form M-steps for W, Λ, and b, and updates feature inclusion probabilities via a fixed-point equation that includes the FIC-induced shrinkage term. The authors reinterpret the E-step updates as a convex–concave procedure (CCCP), which motivates a shrinkage-acceleration routine to remove irrelevant features early, reducing computation. They further analyze identifiability, proving that FAB prefers balanced (and thus more identifiable) solutions and motivating a post-hoc merge step for overlapping features.

Empirically, on synthetic and real datasets, FAB/LFM is compared to fast Gibbs sampling for the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), variational IBP, and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP). FAB generally achieves superior model selection, competitive or better predictive performance, and favorable computational cost, particularly benefitting from the shrinkage acceleration.

Strengths:
- Conceptual advance: The derivation that the FIC for LFMs has the same asymptotic form as for MMs without the block-diagonal Hessian is elegant and broadens the scope of FAB.
- Algorithmic clarity and guarantees: The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm monotonically increases a lower bound, yields automatic feature selection, and includes practical speedups (CCCP-based shrinkage).
- Identifiability analysis: Theorem 4 offers insight into parameter identifiability and provides a principled basis for feature merging.
- Empirical results: Demonstrations on synthetic and real datasets show strong model selection and predictive performance with good computational efficiency.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions and scope of Lemma 1: The main lemma is pivotal yet its “mild assumptions” are deferred to the supplement. Please list the assumptions explicitly in the main text and discuss their practical scope (e.g., dependence on Λ being diagonal, conditioning on π, bounds on W, or moments of X). Clarify whether the result extends to non-Gaussian likelihoods or non-diagonal noise.
- Finite-sample behavior: The theory is asymptotic (Op(1) gaps). It would help to quantify finite-N effects: e.g., empirical calibration of the gap between FIC and marginal log-likelihood, and sensitivity when N is small relative to D and K.
- Mean-field approximation: Since mean-field on Z can be restrictive in LFMs, please discuss its impact. Ablations contrasting FAB with and without the factorization (where feasible) or comparisons to structured approximations would strengthen the case.
- Fairness of experimental comparisons: The paper notes that some competing implementations do not learn noise variance and fixes it (e.g., σ=1 synthetic, 0.75 real). Please clarify whether FAB also fixes σ or learns it; if it learns σ, include a control run with σ fixed to the same value, or allow competitors to learn an analogous noise parameter if possible. Also discuss the effect of the IBP hyperparameter α (set to 3) on K estimation, and report sensitivity to α.
- Computational complexity: Provide per-iteration and total complexity in terms of N, D, and K, and quantify the empirical speedup from the shrinkage acceleration (e.g., time to reach a target bound) beyond the illustrative figures. Detail the choice and sensitivity of Tshrink and the stopping tolerance δ.
- Convergence and initialization: Since the algorithm finds local optima, discuss initialization strategies, robustness across random starts, and variance (e.g., report medians and quartiles for K and PLL/TLL over multiple runs).
- Merge post-processing: While Theorem 4 motivates merging identical or near-identical features, formalize the criterion for merging in finite samples (e.g., thresholds on ||w_k − w_l||) and show that merging increases the bound in practice (include a small ablation).
- Positioning vs related asymptotics: Clarify differences from MAD-Bayes (which considers vanishing noise) and its implications for the regularizer, and relate your result to classical MDL/BIC for singular models (Watanabe), emphasizing why the O(∑_k log ∑_n z_nk) complexity is the right scale for LFMs.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound overall, with clear derivations of the lower bound, CCCP interpretation, and identifiability argument. However, important assumptions are relegated to the supplement; bringing them forward would improve rigor and reproducibility.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. Consider tightening the exposition around Lemma 1/Theorem 2, summarizing assumptions, and adding a brief complexity section. Minor typographical issues exist in the current draft but are easily fixable.
- Originality: Novel extension of FAB to LFMs by overcoming the Hessian factorization requirement; the CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability-guided merging are thoughtful additions.
- Significance: The contribution broadens FAB to a widely used class of models and demonstrates practical benefits in model selection and prediction. The approach is likely to influence subsequent work on scalable, automated structure learning in latent variable models.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical insight that generalizes FAB to LFMs with a clear, reusable form of FIC.
- Practical algorithm with monotonic improvement, automatic feature selection, and acceleration.
- Solid empirical evidence of improved model selection and competitive prediction with good speed.
- Identifiability analysis and actionable post-processing.

Cons against acceptance:
- Key assumptions underlying the main lemma are not prominently stated in the main text.
- Some experimental choices raise fairness questions (noise variance, IBP hyperparameters).
- Limited analysis of sensitivity, initialization, and finite-sample behavior.
- Merge heuristic lacks a fully specified acceptance criterion in the main paper.

Questions for the authors:
1) Precisely list the assumptions for Lemma 1 and discuss their practical range (e.g., non-diagonal Λ, non-Gaussian likelihoods).
2) Did FAB learn noise variance in the real-data experiments? If so, please report results with σ fixed for parity.
3) How sensitive are results to initialization and Tshrink? Provide variance across runs.
4) What is the per-iteration time complexity and memory footprint? How does acceleration change it empirically?
5) Can the method extend to count or binary observations (e.g., Poisson/Bernoulli LFMs)?

Recommendation:
I lean toward acceptance. The paper offers a meaningful theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs, practical algorithmic advances, and convincing empirical results. Addressing the clarity of assumptions and fairness of comparisons would strengthen an already solid submission.
