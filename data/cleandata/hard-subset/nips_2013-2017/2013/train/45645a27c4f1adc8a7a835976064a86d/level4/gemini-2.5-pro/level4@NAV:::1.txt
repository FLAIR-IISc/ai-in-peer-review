This paper introduces a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. The method is analogous to variational Bayes, but with a key distinction: instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it utilizes the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) to approximately integrate out the parameters. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves superior held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing implementations of the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

The proposed methodology is well-founded and motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. Its primary advantage over other IBP inference methods is its computational efficiency; it is structured as an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with additional complexity penalties, which avoids the higher computational costs of sampling or full variational Bayes algorithms.

The technical contributions, while novel, appear to be incremental, as they primarily serve as an extension of the FAB framework developed in [3] and [4].

Certain elements of the mathematical exposition are confusing due to a lack of precision. For instance, in Equation (3), the log-sum term becomes infinite if all z values are zero. As this state has a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution q, it is unclear why the FIC score is not always infinite.

Furthermore, Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated," but it does not specify the asymptotic regime or the underlying assumptions. Specifically, the behavior of K (the number of components) as N increases is not defined. The application of BIC to each component individually seems to presuppose a fixed, finite K, which would cause the number of activations for each feature to approach infinity. This assumption of a finite K undermines one of the core motivations for using the IBP, where the number of components is infinite and grows as more data is observed.

On a more minor note, in Section 2, the term pk(X | z{·, k}) should likely be conditioned only on the data points assigned to component k, not the entire dataset X. Additionally, the left-hand side of Equation (4) appears to be missing an expectation operator.

Regarding the quantitative results in Table 1, the proposed method attains higher predictive likelihood scores in less time than the alternative methods. However, the interpretation of these results is challenging because the stopping criteria for the algorithms are not specified. This makes the runtime comparisons, especially with FAB which can be orders of magnitude faster, difficult to assess, as the point at which to terminate the Gibbs sampler, for example, seems arbitrary.

The improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, but the paper does not adequately investigate their source. The results would be more compelling if there were evidence that the performance gains stem from the model selection criterion itself, rather than from competing algorithms becoming trapped in worse local optima. The latter is a distinct possibility, as outcomes can depend heavily on implementation details like initialization strategies. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessive number of components on some datasets suggests it may be struggling with local optima, particularly since it is, in theory, computing the exact likelihood that FAB approximates.

In conclusion, the proposed approach is conceptually sensible but represents a somewhat incremental contribution. Some of the mathematical derivations seem imprecise. While the experimental results show improvements in both runtime and predictive likelihood over previous methods, the analysis provided is insufficient to confidently interpret these findings.
