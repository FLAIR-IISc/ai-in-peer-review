Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models and HMMs to latent feature models (LFMs). The key theoretical result is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood that yields a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs with the same form as in mixture models, despite the lack of block-diagonal structure typically required by earlier FAB derivations. Building on this, the authors propose a practical FAB algorithm for LFMs that (i) uses a mean-field approximation over binary feature indicators, (ii) alternates between E- and M-steps to maximize a tractable lower bound on FIC, (iii) incorporates a CCCP-based view of the E-step to motivate a shrinkage acceleration scheme that prunes irrelevant features early, and (iv) provides a merging post-processing step supported by an identifiability analysis. Empirical comparisons to Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods (Gibbs, VB), MEIBP, and EM demonstrate favorable model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency on synthetic and real datasets.

This work is well situated relative to prior art: FAB for mixtures and HMMs (Fujimaki & Morinaga; Fujimaki & Hayashi), nonparametric Bayesian LFMs via the IBP (Griffiths & Ghahramani; Doshi-Velez et al.) including accelerated Gibbs and VB, and MEIBP (Reed & Ghahramani). The asymptotic perspective is related to MAD-Bayes (Broderick et al.) but differs in not relying on a zero-noise limit. The use of CCCP (Yuille & Rangarajan) clarifies the optimization dynamics, and the discussion of model-induced regularization connects to Watanabe’s singular learning theory.

Quality:
- Theory: The central Lemma shows that the log-determinant of the Hessian with respect to per-dimension parameters θd behaves as sum_k log(sum_n znk)/N + O_p(1), which underpins an FIC identical in form to that for mixtures. Theorem 2 follows to approximate the marginal complete log-likelihood, and the resulting FIC is optimized via a lower bound with guarantees of monotonic improvement for the alternating updates. The identifiability result (Theorem 4) is valuable, indicating that FAB prefers balanced, smaller-K ML solutions and supports the merging heuristic. While the main proofs and “mild assumptions” are deferred to the supplement, the claims are plausible; however, the paper would benefit from a clearer statement of the precise regularity and asymptotic conditions (e.g., growth rates, boundedness, nonsingularity conditions) required for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2.
- Algorithm: The mean-field E-step yields closed-form fixed point updates; the M-step is standard (PPCA-style updates for W, Λ, b). The CCCP interpretation justifies multiple inner E-steps to accelerate shrinkage. The monotonicity of the lower bound for each update is stated; for the merge post-processing, the authors check an increase in the bound—a good safeguard.
- Empirics: Experiments cover synthetic (including block data) and several real datasets, comparing prediction and model selection with run-time. The ablation on shrinkage acceleration is informative. Some implementation choices for baselines (e.g., fixing noise variance, specific α for IBP, MEIBP applicability to non-negative data) could bias results; a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with explicit algorithms and update equations. A few improvements would help:
- State all assumptions for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 in the main text (even briefly), and clarify when FIC ceases to approximate the marginal log-likelihood (e.g., features always on/off).
- Unify notation (e.g., consistent use of µ, π, π̃), and fix minor typos.
- Clarify the conditions under which the merge step is attempted and how often it triggers in practice.

Originality:
The derivation of an LFM FIC with the same penalization structure as mixtures—without the block-diagonal Hessian assumption—is novel and nontrivial. The CCCP-based acceleration and the identifiability-driven merge post-processing are also new contributions in this context.

Significance:
Automated model selection in LFMs without hyperparameter tuning, with competitive or improved predictive performance and reduced computational cost, is of broad interest. The approach provides a practical alternative to IBP-based methods, particularly attractive when scalability and automation matter. The theoretical insight may extend FAB to other discrete latent variable models.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical contribution (FIC for LFMs) with intuitive implications (automatic shrinkage, identifiability).
- Practical, principled algorithm with monotonic lower-bound improvements.
- Convincing empirical gains in model selection accuracy and prediction, often with lower K and shorter run-time.
- CCCP interpretation leading to a useful acceleration.
- Merge heuristic grounded in theory.

Cons:
- Key assumptions for the asymptotic results are not fully spelled out in the main text.
- Evaluation fairness could be improved: fixed noise variance for some baselines, sensitivity to IBP α, and MEIBP’s constraints may affect conclusions.
- Limited stress testing: behavior under small N or severe model mismatch (non-Gaussian noise, highly correlated or redundant features) is not fully explored.
- The impact of the mean-field assumption on accuracy is not isolated via ablation.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Explicitly list the assumptions for Lemma 1 (e.g., moment conditions, growth of K with N, boundedness) and discuss finite-sample implications. Are there scenarios where the O_p(1) terms are large enough to alter model selection?
- Provide sensitivity analyses: (i) varying noise variance and learning it across all baselines (or at least report results where α for IBP is inferred); (ii) initialization of K and µ; (iii) the Tshrink parameter and number of CCCP inner loops.
- Add small-N experiments to show where FAB may underperform and articulate guidelines for practitioners.
- Report per-iteration complexity and wall-clock comparisons normalized by implementation (e.g., MATLAB vs. optimized code), if possible.
- Clarify the merge criterion and provide statistics on how often merges occur and their effect on PLL/TLL.
- If space permits, include a brief discussion comparing to MAD-Bayes and why the proposed asymptotics may be preferable in moderate-noise regimes.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and timely contribution that advances FAB to a new and important class of models. The paper is well executed, with meaningful theoretical and practical advances and strong empirical evidence. With clearer articulation of the theoretical assumptions and modest expansions to the empirical analysis for fairness and robustness, this would be a strong addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
