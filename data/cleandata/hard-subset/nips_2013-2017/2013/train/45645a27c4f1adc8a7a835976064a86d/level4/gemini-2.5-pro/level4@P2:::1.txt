This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), providing a novel criterion for model selection—specifically, for determining the number of latent features. The proposed method approximates the marginal log-likelihood by integrating out the model parameters using an approach similar to the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), but adapted for the structure of LFMs. This results in an algorithm that resembles a penalized version of Expectation-Maximization (EM), offering a computationally efficient alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). The authors demonstrate empirically that their method achieves strong performance on held-out data compared to several existing IBP implementations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary advantage of this work is its computational efficiency. By framing the problem within an EM-like framework with specific complexity penalties, the authors develop a scalable algorithm. This is a significant practical benefit over MCMC sampling or standard variational inference for IBP models, which can be prohibitively slow on large datasets.

2.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The proposed method is a sensible and well-motivated extension of prior work on FAB inference. The core technical contribution—the asymptotic analysis in Lemma 1 showing that the LFM complexity term resembles that of mixture models—is novel and extends the applicability of the FAB framework to a more complex and widely used model class.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling. Across several real-world datasets, the FAB/LFM method consistently achieves high predictive log-likelihoods and identifies more compact models (i.e., fewer latent features) than the competing methods. The smaller gap between training and predictive likelihood suggests the method's regularization is effective at preventing overfitting.

4.  **Algorithmic Refinements:** The paper introduces useful practical enhancements. The proposed shrinkage acceleration, based on a CCCP interpretation, demonstrably reduces computation time. Furthermore, the analysis of parameter identifiability provides a natural justification for a post-processing step to merge redundant features.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Clarity and Rigor of Asymptotic Analysis:** The paper's theoretical justification could be stated more precisely. Theorem 2 claims an asymptotic approximation of the marginal likelihood, but the asymptotic regime is not clearly defined. Specifically, how is the number of features, K, assumed to behave as the number of data points, N, grows? The derivation seems to rely on a fixed, finite K, as the BIC-like penalty for each feature's parameters requires that the number of data points activating that feature approaches infinity. This assumption appears to contradict one of the primary motivations for using IBP, where the number of discovered features is expected to grow with the data. Clarifying the underlying assumptions would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical claims (Clarity, Quality).

2.  **Interpretation of Experimental Comparisons:** While the empirical results are impressive, their interpretation is challenging for the following reasons:
    *   **Running Time:** The claim of being orders of magnitude faster is difficult to evaluate without a clear description of the stopping criteria for all methods. The criterion for the FAB algorithm is well-defined, but it is not specified how convergence was determined for the IBP Gibbs sampler, making a direct comparison of wall-clock times potentially misleading.
    *   **Source of Performance Gains:** It is unclear whether the improved predictive likelihood is a result of the superior model selection criterion (FIC) or if the optimization procedure is simply more effective at avoiding poor local optima than the compared methods. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets suggests it may be struggling with optimization issues. The paper would be more convincing if it could provide analysis that disentangles the effect of the model selection criterion from the effects of the optimization algorithm (e.g., initialization, local optima).

3.  **Potential Issues in the FIC Formulation:** In the discussion of the FIC for mixture models (Equation 3), the term `log(sum_n z_nk)` is undefined if a particular feature `k` is not used by any data point (i.e., `sum_n z_nk = 0`). While the variational distribution `q` may prevent this in expectation, this edge case could cause numerical instability or theoretical issues. A brief clarification on how this is handled would be helpful.

**Minor Points:**

*   In Equation (4), the expectation `E_q` appears to be missing on the left-hand side of the inequality.
*   In Section 1.1, the notation `p_k(X|z_{·,k})` for mixture models seems to imply that the k-th component's likelihood is evaluated on the entire dataset `X`, rather than on the subset of data points assigned to that component. This could be a source of confusion for the reader.

**Summary:**

The proposed approach is a logical and valuable contribution to the literature on model selection for latent feature models. The method is computationally efficient and performs very well empirically. However, the originality is somewhat incremental, building directly on previous FAB research. The paper's primary weaknesses lie in the lack of precision in its theoretical claims and the ambiguity in the interpretation of its experimental results. Addressing the questions regarding the asymptotic assumptions and providing a more controlled comparison would substantially strengthen this work.
