This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to binary latent feature models (LFMs). The core idea is to develop a model selection criterion, termed the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), by applying a Laplace approximation to the marginal likelihood over the model parameters. This results in a tractable lower bound on the FIC that can be optimized efficiently using an EM-like algorithm. The authors demonstrate experimentally that their method, FAB/LFM, achieves competitive or superior performance on held-out data compared to several Indian Buffet Process (IBP) based methods, often with significantly lower computational cost.

The primary strength of the paper is its proposal of a practical and computationally efficient algorithm for model selection in LFMs. The approach is well-motivated by an asymptotic argument and provides a principled alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods. A key advantage of FAB/LFM over competing approaches, such as those based on the IBP, is its computational profile. The resulting algorithm resembles an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, making it significantly faster than more expensive MCMC sampling or standard variational inference techniques.

While the approach is promising, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form, primarily related to theoretical clarity and the interpretation of the experimental results.

**Theoretical Clarity:**
Some parts of the theoretical exposition are confusing due to what appears to be mathematical imprecision.

1.  **FIC Definition:** In Equation (3), the penalty term `log(sum_n z_nk)` is undefined (approaches negative infinity) if a feature `k` is not used by any data point (i.e., `sum_n z_nk = 0`). Since this event would have a non-zero probability under a non-degenerate variational distribution `q`, it is unclear why the FIC objective is not always negative infinity. The paper should clarify how this edge case is handled in the derivation and optimization.

2.  **Asymptotic Assumptions:** The statement of Theorem 2 is vague. It claims an asymptotic approximation for the marginal likelihood but does not clearly specify the asymptotic regime or the underlying assumptions. In particular, it is unclear how the number of features, K, is assumed to behave as the number of data points, N, grows. The derivation, which applies a BIC-like penalty to each feature, seems to implicitly assume a fixed, finite K, ensuring that the number of data points activating each feature grows to infinity. This assumption is a significant departure from the motivation for non-parametric models like the IBP, where the number of features is expected to grow with the data. This point deserves clarification and discussion.

**Minor Points on Notation:**
*   In the discussion of Mixture Models (MMs) in Section 1.1, the notation `pk(X|z·k)` is ambiguous. It seems to imply that the likelihood for the k-th component depends on the entire dataset X, whereas it should typically only depend on the subset of data points assigned to that component.
*   The expectation operator `Eq[...]` appears to be missing from the left-hand side of the inequality in Equation (4).

**Experimental Evaluation:**
The empirical results are promising but difficult to fully interpret without further analysis.

1.  **Runtime Comparison:** The experiments show that FAB/LFM is often orders of magnitude faster than the baselines. However, this comparison is potentially misleading. While the FAB algorithm has a clear convergence criterion, the paper does not specify when the MCMC-based methods (e.g., the IBP Gibbs sampler) were stopped. The stopping point for a sampler can be arbitrary, making direct speed comparisons problematic.

2.  **Source of Performance Gains:** The improvements in predictive likelihood are substantial, but the paper does not provide a clear analysis of their source. It is difficult to disentangle whether the gains stem from the superiority of the FIC criterion itself, or from differences in the optimization and inference procedures. For example, some baseline methods may be more prone to converging to poor local optima, an issue that could depend heavily on implementation details like initialization. A more convincing case would involve an analysis that isolates the effect of the model selection criterion from the optimization algorithm. The fact that the IBP sampler selects a very large number of features on some datasets might point to slow mixing or specific hyperparameter settings rather than a fundamental flaw in the non-parametric Bayesian approach itself.

**Overall Assessment:**
In summary, this paper presents a computationally efficient method for model selection in latent feature models. The approach is a sensible and logical extension of previous work on FAB inference. However, the technical contribution feels incremental, and the theoretical presentation lacks precision in some key areas. Furthermore, the experimental analysis is not deep enough to fully substantiate the claims of superiority over other methods. While the results are promising, the paper would be significantly stronger if the authors addressed the theoretical ambiguities and provided a more careful and nuanced interpretation of the empirical comparisons.
