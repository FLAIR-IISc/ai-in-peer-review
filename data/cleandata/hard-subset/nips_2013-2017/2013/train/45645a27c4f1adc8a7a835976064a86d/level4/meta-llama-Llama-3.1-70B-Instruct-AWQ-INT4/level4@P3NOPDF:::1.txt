This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, which is an extension of variational Bayes. Instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, the authors approximately integrate out the parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). The proposed approach demonstrates improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The authors' motivation for this approach is rooted in a plausible asymptotic argument. The main advantage of this method over other IBP inference techniques is its computational efficiency, as it corresponds to an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, rather than relying on more expensive sampling or variational Bayes algorithms.

While the technical contributions of this paper appear to be novel, they are essentially incremental and build upon the work of [3] and [4]. The exposition is generally clear, but some mathematical derivations require further clarification. For instance, equation (3) involves the log sum of z values, which would be infinite if all values are zero. Since this has a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution q, it is unclear why the FIC score would not always be infinite.

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood can be asymptotically approximated, but the asymptotic regime and assumptions are not clearly defined. Specifically, it is unclear how K is assumed to behave as N increases. Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem seems to require a fixed finite K, allowing the number of activations of every feature to approach infinity. However, under the IBP model, the number of components is infinite, and new components would continue to be observed as the data increases, which undermines one of the motivations for using the IBP.

Additionally, there are some minor points that require attention. In section 2, pk(X | z{·, k}) should only include the data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset. Furthermore, equation (4) is missing an expectation on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results in Table 1 demonstrate that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores in less time compared to alternative methods. However, the interpretation of these results is hindered by the lack of a specified stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler. It is unclear when to stop the sampler, which may lead to arbitrary decisions. Moreover, the improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, but it is unclear whether these improvements are due to the model selection criterion or the algorithms getting stuck in worse local optima. The latter is likely to depend on the implementation details, such as initialization strategies.

To strengthen the results, it would be beneficial to provide evidence that the difference in predictive likelihood is due to the model selection criterion rather than the algorithms converging to suboptimal solutions. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components in some datasets suggests that it may be getting stuck in local optima, which undermines the comparison with the proposed method.

In conclusion, the proposed approach is reasonable and demonstrates improvements in running time and predictive likelihood compared to previous approaches. However, further analysis is required to fully interpret these results and understand the contributions of the model selection criterion.
