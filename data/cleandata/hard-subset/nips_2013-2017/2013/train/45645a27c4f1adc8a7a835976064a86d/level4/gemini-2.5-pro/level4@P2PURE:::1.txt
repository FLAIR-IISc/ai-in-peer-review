This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. The method is analogous to variational Bayes, but instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximates the integration of the parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing implementations of the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

The proposed approach is well-motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument and presents a reasonable alternative for IBP inference. Its main advantage is computational: unlike more expensive sampling or standard variational Bayes algorithms, this method corresponds to an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties.

The technical contributions appear to be novel but incremental, as they are a direct extension of the FAB framework presented in [3] and [4].

Several parts of the exposition are confusing due to a lack of mathematical precision. For example, in Equation (3), the log-sum of the z values is negative infinity if all values for a given feature are zero. Since this configuration has a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution q, it is unclear why the FIC score is not always negative infinity.

Furthermore, Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated," but the asymptotic regime and its underlying assumptions are not specified. In particular, the behavior of K (the number of features) as N (the number of data points) increases is not defined. Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem appears to require a fixed, finite K, which would ensure that the number of activations for every feature approaches infinity. This assumption undermines a core motivation for using the IBP, which posits an infinite number of components that can grow as more data is observed.

I have a few more minor points:
*   In Section 2, should the likelihood term `pk(X | z{·, k})` be conditioned only on the data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset?
*   In Equation (4), the left-hand side appears to be missing an expectation.

In the quantitative results (Table 1), the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores in less time compared to alternative methods. However, interpreting these results is difficult. While the FAB method is orders of magnitude faster in some cases, the stopping criteria for the algorithms are not specified, making a direct comparison of runtimes inconclusive. The decision of when to stop the Gibbs sampler, in particular, seems arbitrary.

More importantly, while the improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, their origin is unclear. The results would be more convincing if the authors provided evidence that the performance difference is due to the proposed model selection criterion itself, rather than an artifact of certain algorithms getting stuck in worse local optima. The latter often depends on implementation details such as initialization strategies. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets suggests it may be suffering from issues with local optima, especially since in theory it should more accurately compute the likelihood that FAB is approximating.

In summary, the proposed approach is sensible but its contribution is somewhat incremental. The mathematical exposition lacks precision in key areas. Although the experimental results show improvements in running time and predictive likelihood, the analysis is insufficient to confidently interpret these results and attribute the gains to the proposed method.
