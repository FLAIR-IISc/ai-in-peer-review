This paper introduces a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, offering an alternative to variational Bayes. The core idea is to approximate parameter integration using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) rather than assuming a factorized posterior distribution. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to existing implementations of the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

The proposed approach is intuitively sound and well-motivated by asymptotic arguments. A key advantage over existing IBP inference methods is its computational efficiency; the method effectively corresponds to an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with added complexity penalties, avoiding the higher computational cost of sampling-based or full variational Bayes techniques. While the technical contributions appear novel, they are incremental, building upon previous work on Factor Analysis with Bayesian priors (FAB) [3, 4].

However, several aspects of the presentation and technical justification require clarification. The mathematical notation lacks precision in places. For example, equation (3) involves a log-sum of ‘z’ values which becomes infinite when all values are zero. Given that a non-degenerate variational distribution *q* assigns non-zero probability to such configurations, it is unclear why the resulting Factor Information Criterion (FIC) score would not consistently be infinite. 

Furthermore, Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood can be *asymptotically approximated*, but lacks a precise definition of the asymptotic regime and underlying assumptions. Specifically, the behavior of *K* (the number of components) as *N* (the dataset size) grows is not specified. Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem implicitly assumes a fixed, finite *K*, implying that the number of activations for each feature approaches infinity. This assumption is problematic in the context of the IBP, which inherently models an infinite number of components, continuously adding new features as the data size increases. Fixing *K* therefore undermines a central motivation for employing the IBP.

Several minor technical points should also be addressed. In Section 2, the likelihood term *p<sub>k</sub>(X | z<sub>•,k</sub>)* appears to be calculated over the entire dataset, whereas it should only include data points assigned to component *k*. Additionally, equation (4) appears to lack an expectation operator on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results presented in Table 1 demonstrate that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores with reduced computational time. While FAB consistently finishes faster, often by several orders of magnitude, the interpretation of this result is hampered by the unspecified stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler. Determining when to terminate a Gibbs sampler is somewhat arbitrary, and this choice may significantly influence the results. 

The observed improvements in predictive likelihood are encouraging, but their source remains unclear. To strengthen the claims, the authors should provide evidence demonstrating that these gains are specifically attributable to the proposed model selection criterion, rather than differences in convergence to local optima. Implementation details such as initialization strategies can strongly affect the behavior of sampling-based methods, and thus the observed differences may reflect such factors. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components in some datasets supports the possibility of convergence issues, as the Gibbs sampler should theoretically compute the exact likelihood approximated by FAB.

In summary, the proposed approach is a reasonable and potentially valuable extension to existing IBP inference techniques. However, the paper would benefit from more rigorous mathematical justification, clearer exposition, and a more thorough analysis of the experimental results to better isolate the contribution of the proposed model selection criterion. The incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the issues identified above, warrants a careful consideration of its overall impact.
