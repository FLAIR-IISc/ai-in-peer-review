## Review

**Summary:**

This paper introduces an Expectation-Maximization (EM)-based inference algorithm for the binary Latent Feature Model (LFM), where each observation *x<sub>n</sub>* is represented by a binary latent vector *z<sub>n</sub>* indicating the presence or absence of *K* latent features. The algorithm learns the latent features *z<sub>n</sub>* and infers the number of latent features *K* through a shrinkage mechanism. While motivated by nonparametric Bayesian LFM approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), the method utilizes the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference (FAB) framework – recently applied to mixture models and Hidden Markov Models – rather than explicit prior distributions for model parsimony. FAB achieves shrinkage by expressing the log marginal likelihood in terms of the expected complete log-likelihood and a model selection term, effectively bounding *K* during inference.  Experimental results on large datasets demonstrate that the FAB-based algorithm outperforms competing methods like Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation for the IBP, both in terms of runtime and inference quality.

**Quality:**

The technical quality of the work appears sound, and the algorithmic details seem correct. The ability to handle large datasets and simultaneously learn the optimal number of latent features is a significant strength.

**Clarity:**

The methodology is presented clearly. However, details regarding the experimental setup require further clarification, as noted below.

**Originality:**

The application of the recently proposed Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to latent feature models represents a novel contribution. 

**Significance:**

The paper tackles an important problem: efficient inference in latent feature models, including automatic determination of the number of latent features. 

**Weak Points:**

*   The paper lacks a discussion of potential limitations. Are there scenarios where the proposed algorithm might underperform compared to standard Gibbs sampling?
*   The convergence properties of the EM-like procedure are not addressed. Potential difficulties or considerations regarding convergence should be discussed.
*   The algorithm is currently limited to binary LFMs and cannot be directly extended to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks thoroughness and contains potential flaws (detailed below).
*   The behavior of the algorithm in the small-data regime, where asymptotic approximations may not hold, is unclear and should be discussed or investigated.
*   Relevant recent work on efficient inference in LFMs has not been adequately cited and discussed.
*   The comparison with the MEIBP algorithm [22] is invalid as it is only applicable to non-negative linear Gaussian models with a positive loading matrix *W*. The synthetic dataset and block data experiments require a positive *W* for a fair comparison.

**Other Comments:**

*   For the block image data, given that the noise level is known and provided to the FAB algorithm, the IBP Gibbs sampler should also be provided with this value, rather than relying on the 0.75 standard deviation heuristic, to ensure a fair comparison.
*   For VB-IBP [2], the infinite variational version should be considered in the experiments instead of the finite version. Furthermore, the experimental settings for VB-IBP require more detailed description, including the number of restarts used.
*   The discovery of 70 latent features by accelerated Gibbs sampling on the 49-dimensional Sonar data is surprising and likely due to poor initialization and/or improper noise variance specification.
*   Line 70: Reference [21] describes MAP estimation for the IBP, not Gibbs sampling. This should be corrected.
*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of recent work on efficient inference using small-variance asymptotics in nonparametric LFMs, such as "MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes" (ICML 2013).
*   Line 349: The suggestion in [1,22] was to set the standard deviation to 0.25 times the standard deviation of examples across all dimensions, rather than 0.75.

**Comments after Author Feedback:**

The authors’ response addressed some of the initial concerns. However, several points require further attention before acceptance:

*   The experimental methodology, for both the proposed method and the baselines, needs to be explained in greater detail. Justifications and explanations for the behavior of different algorithms are required (e.g., the impact of a fixed alpha on the Gibbs sampler’s estimated *K*, or potential issues with hyperparameter tuning).
*   Reference [21] should be corrected to accurately reflect that it describes MAP inference, not sampling.
*   The reference to MAD-Bayes inference for the IBP should be included. 

The proposed inference method for LFMs is promising, particularly its scalability to large datasets and its ability to infer *K*. However, the experimental evaluation needs to be more rigorous and carefully considered.
