

This paper addresses a significant limitation in Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference for latent feature models (LFMs). FAB, a model selection method that maximizes a lower bound on the factorized information criterion (FIC), has demonstrated strong performance for mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models (HMMs). However, its applicability has been restricted to models where the Hessian of the complete likelihood is block diagonal—a condition violated by LFMs. The authors successfully extend FAB to LFMs, deriving a valid lower bound for FIC that maintains the same representation as in MMs despite the absence of the block-diagonal Hessian constraint. This technical contribution is both novel and well-executed, with clear derivations that are thoroughly explained in the paper.

The authors validate their approach through extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing FAB/LFM against state-of-the-art methods including fast Gibbs sampling (with Indian Buffet Process), variational Bayes (VB), and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP). Results consistently demonstrate that FAB/LFM outperforms competitors in both predictive accuracy and computational efficiency. Notably, the method achieves superior predictive performance while reducing computational costs, a critical advantage for large-scale applications. The experimental design is robust, with thorough ablation studies and clear presentation of metrics.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with logical flow and precise mathematical exposition. The derivation steps are accessible and rigorously justified, making the technical contribution transparent to readers. The extension from FAB/MMs to FAB/LFMs represents a meaningful incremental advance, bridging a key gap in Bayesian model selection for non-block-diagonal latent structure models. While the core idea is incremental, the elegance of the solution and its empirical validation elevate the work beyond a mere technical extension.

The significance of this work for the NIPS community is substantial. LFMs are widely used in applications like collaborative filtering, topic modeling, and feature selection, yet scalable model selection methods for them remain scarce. By adapting FAB—a method proven effective in other domains—to LFMs, the authors provide a practical, theoretically grounded tool that addresses a clear gap in the literature. The computational efficiency gains are particularly relevant for modern machine learning challenges involving high-dimensional data.

Minor suggestions for improvement: The discussion of the Hessian condition could be slightly more explicit in the introduction to immediately contextualize the contribution. Additionally, while the real-world results are compelling, including one concrete application example (e.g., in a specific dataset like MovieLens) would further strengthen the practical relevance. However, these are minor refinements to an already strong paper.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of Bayesian inference for latent feature models. The technical extension is sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the presentation is clear. The results are convincing, and the method offers tangible benefits for practitioners. The work aligns well with NIPS’ focus on principled, scalable machine learning methods. I recommend acceptance without reservation.
