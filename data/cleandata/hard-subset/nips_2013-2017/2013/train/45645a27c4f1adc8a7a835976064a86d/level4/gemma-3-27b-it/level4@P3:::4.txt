This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a limitation of the existing FAB framework which required a block-diagonal Hessian matrix for complete log-likelihoods. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite not satisfying this condition, exhibit a similar factorized information criterion (FIC) structure to mixture models (MMs), enabling the application of FAB. They present a theoretical analysis, derive a lower bound for the FIC in LFMs, and propose a shrinkage acceleration method inspired by a connection to the convex-concave procedure (CCCP). Experimental evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate competitive performance in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency compared to methods like Indian Buffet Processes (IBP), variational Bayes (VB), and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP).

This work builds upon recent advances in FAB inference [5, 6] and extends its applicability to a broader class of latent variable models. FAB offers an attractive alternative to traditional Bayesian non-parametric (NPB) and variational Bayesian (VB) approaches by aiming for automatic model selection and avoiding the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning. The paper effectively situates itself within the landscape of LFM inference, referencing related work such as IBP-based methods [10, 11, 16, 19, 22] and the “MAD-Bayes” approach [1], noting key differences in assumptions and methodology. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the demonstration of its equivalence to the MMs case are valuable contributions. The CCCP-inspired acceleration and parameter identifiability analysis further strengthen the theoretical foundation of the proposed approach. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs appears rigorous, with Lemma 1 providing a crucial link to the established FAB framework for MMs.
*   **Novelty:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs represents a significant advance, addressing a previously identified limitation and opening up new avenues for applying this efficient inference technique.
*   **Practical Improvements:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, motivated by the CCCP analysis, demonstrates a practical improvement in computational efficiency.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets convincingly demonstrate the competitiveness of FAB/LFMs, showing strong performance in prediction and model selection.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the complex theoretical concepts and algorithmic details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost Comparison Nuance:** While the paper claims improved computational efficiency, the comparison in the experiments lacks some detail. The specific hardware used and a more detailed breakdown of the runtime for each method would be valuable. The statement that MEIBP was much faster, but had worse PLL results, could be further explored – a trade-off analysis would strengthen the claim of FAB's superiority.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity of Competitors:** The authors mention fixing the noise standard deviation for some competing methods due to limitations in their implementations. This introduces a potential bias in the comparison. A discussion of the sensitivity of these methods to this parameter would be beneficial.
*   **Merge Post-processing Detail:** While the merge post-processing step is described, more details on the threshold used for determining feature overlap and its impact on performance would be helpful.
*   **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** The “mild assumptions” mentioned in Lemma 1 could be explicitly stated and discussed. This would strengthen the theoretical grounding and clarify the scope of applicability of the proposed method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of a promising inference technique (FAB) to a challenging problem (LFM inference).
*   The experimental results provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness and efficiency.
*   The clear writing and thorough explanation of the method make the paper accessible to a broad audience.
*   The proposed acceleration technique addresses a practical limitation of FAB inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost comparisons could be more comprehensive.
*   The reliance on fixed parameters for competing methods introduces a potential bias.
*   The details of the merge post-processing step could be expanded upon.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of latent variable modeling. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed method is novel and practical, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is well-suited for NIPS and **recommend acceptance**.
