### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a model selection method that maximizes a lower bound on a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which asymptotically converges to the marginal log-likelihood. Previous work has shown its effectiveness for models like Mixture Models (MMs) and Hidden Markov Models. However, the applicability of FAB has been limited to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal.

The central contribution of this work is to overcome this limitation for LFMs. The authors conduct an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix for LFMs and demonstrate (Lemma 1) that, despite not having a block-diagonal structure, the resulting FIC takes on a form identical to that of MMs. This key theoretical insight allows them to derive a FAB algorithm for LFMs (FAB/LFM), which facilitates automatic selection of the number of latent features. The paper further enhances the proposed algorithm with a shrinkage acceleration method inspired by a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation and analyzes the model's parameter identifiability, which motivates a post-processing step for merging redundant features. The authors provide empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing FAB/LFM to state-of-the-art methods based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), including fast Gibbs sampling, Variational Bayes (VB), and Maximum-Expectation IBP (MEIBP). The experiments suggest that FAB/LFM achieves competitive or superior performance in model selection and prediction, often at a significantly lower computational cost.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The core of the paper is the non-trivial theoretical extension of the FAB framework. The derivation showing that the FIC for LFMs, which violate the block-diagonal Hessian assumption, has the same form as that for MMs is elegant and significant. This result may pave the way for applying FAB to a broader class of latent variable models with shared parameters, which is a valuable contribution to the community.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against several relevant and strong baselines on a variety of datasets. The results consistently demonstrate that FAB/LFM is highly competitive. It often achieves better predictive log-likelihood and more compact models (smaller K) than IBP-based methods, while being substantially faster. The clear advantage in the trade-off between performance and computational cost is a major strength.

3.  **Practical Algorithmic Refinements:** The paper goes beyond the main theoretical derivation to propose practical improvements. The interpretation of the E-step updates as a CCCP is insightful and leads directly to the "accelerateShrinkage" procedure. As shown in Figure 1, this addition provides a significant practical speed-up by pruning irrelevant features early in the optimization process. This makes the algorithm more appealing for real-world applications.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the limitations of prior FAB methods, presenting their theoretical solution, deriving the algorithm, and analyzing its properties. The exposition is easy to follow for readers familiar with Bayesian inference and latent variable models.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement)**

1.  **Scope of Originality:** While the technical derivation for LFMs is novel, the work is fundamentally an extension of the existing FAB framework [5, 6] to a new model class. The paper's impact could be strengthened by a discussion on the generality of their approach. Could the analytical technique used for the LFM Hessian be applied to other important models that also violate the block-diagonal assumption (e.g., models with other forms of parameter sharing)? Such a discussion would broaden the perceived contribution from a specific model extension to a more general methodological advance.

2.  **Experimental Setup Details:** In the experiments, the noise variance was fixed for the baseline methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP), whereas it appears to be learned in the proposed FAB/LFM method (as part of the precision matrix Λ). This discrepancy could potentially give FAB/LFM an unfair advantage. The authors should either justify this experimental choice or, ideally, report results where the baselines are also allowed to learn the noise variance to ensure a more direct comparison.

3.  **Limitations of Asymptotic Approach:** The method relies on an asymptotic approximation (N → ∞). The authors commendably acknowledge that in small-N scenarios, the FIC approximation may be inaccurate, leading to poorer performance compared to non-parametric Bayesian methods (as observed with the Libras dataset). It would be beneficial for practitioners if the authors could provide more intuition or analysis regarding the sample size at which the asymptotic regime becomes reliable.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a solid and well-executed contribution. It successfully extends the promising FAB inference framework to the important class of Latent Feature Models by overcoming a key theoretical hurdle. The work is technically sound, supported by strong and comprehensive experiments, and includes practical algorithmic enhancements that improve its utility. While the contribution could be framed as an incremental advance on the FAB framework, the technical novelty required for the extension and the impressive empirical results make it a valuable addition to the literature on Bayesian model selection. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

The paper will be of great interest to researchers working on Bayesian inference, model selection, and latent variable models. I support its acceptance.
