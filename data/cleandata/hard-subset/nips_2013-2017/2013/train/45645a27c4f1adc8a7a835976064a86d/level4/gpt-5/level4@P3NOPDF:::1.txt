Summary
The paper proposes a model selection criterion for binary latent feature models that approximates marginalization over parameters using a BIC-style penalty, yielding an EM-like algorithm with added complexity terms. Empirically, the method outperforms several IBP baselines in held-out likelihood while being faster than sampling- or variational-based approaches. The idea is sensible, computationally attractive, and motivated by asymptotic considerations, and it extends prior FAB-style work [3,4].

Strengths
- Clear motivation: replacing expensive posterior inference over parameters with a BIC-like approximation is appealing and leads to a simple EM-like procedure.
- Empirical promise: the method often achieves higher predictive likelihood than competing IBP implementations, sometimes with substantial speedups.
- Practicality: the approach appears straightforward to implement and scale.

Main concerns
1) Asymptotic justification and assumptions
- Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated as …,” but the asymptotic regime and assumptions are not specified. Please state clearly:
  - What tends to infinity (N, per-feature counts Nk, both)?
  - How K behaves with N. The proof sketch and per-component BIC penalties seem to require fixed, finite K with Nk → ∞ for all retained components.
  - Under the IBP, K grows like O(α log N); features continue to appear with more data. If your approximation assumes fixed K (or effectively prunes to a finite K), please justify why this is appropriate and under what regimes the approximation remains accurate for IBP-like growth of K.
  - Whether the BIC penalty is valid when per-feature effective sample sizes Nk are small or vary widely across features.

2) Objective definition and degeneracies
- Equation (3): if the term involves log(sum_n z_{nk}) (or similar), it is −∞ when a feature has no activations. Under any nondegenerate q, this event has nonzero probability, so why doesn’t the FIC become −∞ (or undefined)? If you implicitly exclude zero-usage features, add an explicit convention (e.g., features with Nk=0 are pruned, or a small ε inside the log). Please formalize the domain and treatment of zero-activation features so the objective is always well-defined.
- More generally, if the penalty includes log Nk terms, please clarify how you handle Nk=0 and very small Nk, and whether the approximation includes higher-order corrections in those regimes.

3) Relation to prior FAB work
- The technical novelty appears incremental relative to [3,4]. Please detail the specific extensions for binary latent features/IBP (e.g., new penalties, tighter bounds, different pruning rules) and discuss when the proposed criterion reduces to or differs from FAB.

Empirical evaluation
- The method shows higher predictive likelihood in less time, which is encouraging. However, it remains unclear whether gains stem from the model selection criterion per se or from optimization dynamics and implementation details.
  - Stopping criteria: Results are difficult to interpret without matched and clearly specified stopping rules. For FAB and EM-like methods, define convergence thresholds; for Gibbs/variational baselines, report stopping conditions or run-time budgets. Provide predictive likelihood vs wall-clock curves so readers can compare across time.
  - Local optima vs criterion: To attribute improvements to the selection criterion, consider:
    - Ablation: run the same EM-like optimization with and without the BIC/FIC penalty (or with different penalties) from the same initializations.
    - Multiple restarts: report mean/variance across seeds for all methods.
    - Synthetic data with known ground-truth features: show feature recovery, K estimation, and predictive likelihood as N increases.
    - Compare to collapsed samplers or more advanced variational methods, controlling for compute budget, and verify that Gibbs does not systematically overfit due to hyperparameter choices or insufficient mixing.
  - Feature cardinality: Report the learned number of active features and activation histograms for each method/dataset; correlate predictive gains with differences in K and sparsity.
  - Hyperparameters and priors: State how α and other priors are set/learned. Since Gibbs can inflate K under certain priors, ensure fairness across methods.

Clarity and correctness issues
- Section 2: p_k(X | z·,k) should involve only the subset of data assigned to component/feature k, not the entire dataset, unless there is a specific coupling you intend; please clarify the factorization and conditioning.
- Equation (4): the left-hand side should be an expectation if it represents an expected complete-data log-likelihood or a variational bound. Please add the missing expectation operator and define q precisely.
- Notation: define all quantities (e.g., Nk, K+, z·,k) at first use. Make explicit what is optimized in E and M steps under the proposed criterion.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide the exact asymptotic statement for Theorem 2, including rates (e.g., remainder terms O_p(1)) and conditions under which the per-feature BIC penalty is valid when K grows with N.
- Clarify the handling of zero-activation and rarely-activated features in both the objective and the algorithm (e.g., pruning rules, thresholds).
- Add ablation and sensitivity studies as noted above to separate the effect of the model selection criterion from optimization and initialization effects.
- Include convergence/time plots and matched stopping criteria. Ensure all baselines use comparable compute budgets and data passes.
- Discuss limitations: when per-feature sample sizes are small, the BIC approximation may be poor; note when this affects performance and whether regularization or alternative penalties help.

Minor comments
- Fix the potential divergence in Equation (3) by defining the domain or adding a small constant inside the log, and document the choice.
- There are a few typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., “quantiative” → “quantitative”; missing expectations). A careful pass would help readability.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a practical and plausible criterion with promising empirical performance and computational advantages. However, the theoretical assumptions and asymptotic regime are insufficiently specified, parts of the objective appear ill-defined in edge cases, and the empirical analysis does not yet isolate the contribution of the model selection criterion from optimization artifacts. With clarified theory and stronger experimental controls/ablations, this could be a valuable contribution. In its current form, I view it as an incremental extension of FAB with unresolved technical and empirical questions. My inclination is weak reject, but I would be persuaded by revisions that address the above points.
