This paper introduces a novel model selection criterion, the Feature-specific Information Criterion (FIC), for binary latent feature models based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). The proposed method can be viewed as a hybrid approach that draws from both variational Bayes and asymptotic approximations. It resembles variational inference by optimizing a lower bound on the marginal likelihood, but rather than assuming a fully factorized posterior over both latent variables and parameters, it analytically integrates out the model parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). The authors demonstrate that this approach achieves superior held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing IBP inference algorithms.

The core idea is well-motivated and presents a practical trade-off between computational efficiency and model complexity. A key advantage of this approach is its computational profile; inference corresponds to an EM-like algorithm with additional complexity penalties, making it significantly more efficient than more computationally intensive methods like Gibbs sampling or standard variational Bayes.

Despite its appeal, the paper has several weaknesses in its theoretical exposition and experimental analysis.

**Clarity of Technical Contributions**

While the proposed method is useful, the technical contribution appears to be an incremental extension of the Factorial Asymptotic Bayes (FAB) framework previously introduced in [3] and [4]. More critically, the mathematical exposition contains several points that are imprecise or require clarification:

1.  **Potential for Infinite FIC Score:** In Equation (3), the term involving the logarithm of the sum of `z_ik` appears to be undefined (approaching negative infinity) if all `z_ik` for a given data point `i` are zero. As this state would have a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution `q`, it is unclear why the FIC score is not consequently infinite. The authors should address how this case is handled.

2.  **Undefined Asymptotic Regime:** Theorem 2, which provides the asymptotic justification for the method, is stated without defining the asymptotic regime or its underlying assumptions. It is crucial to specify how the number of latent features, K, is assumed to behave as the number of data points, N, grows. The use of BIC for each feature seems to imply a regime where K is fixed and the number of data points assigned to each feature grows to infinity. This assumption is at odds with the fundamental non-parametric premise of the IBP, where the number of features is unbounded and is expected to grow with the data. This discrepancy undermines the theoretical motivation for applying a BIC-based approximation in this setting.

3.  **Minor Technical Issues:**
    *   In Section 2, the definition of `p_k(X | z_{.,k})` seems to incorrectly condition on the entire dataset X, rather than just the subset of data points associated with feature k.
    *   The left-hand side of Equation (4) appears to be missing an expectation with respect to the variational distribution `q`.

**Experimental Evaluation**

The empirical results in Table 1 show that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihoods in less time than several competitors. However, the interpretation of these results is difficult due to a lack of methodological rigor in the experimental setup.

1.  **Unspecified Stopping Criteria:** The paper does not specify the convergence or stopping criteria used for the baseline algorithms. This makes the runtime comparisons difficult to interpret. For instance, the runtime of a Gibbs sampler is highly dependent on the number of iterations, which appears to have been an arbitrary choice. Without a clear and fair basis for comparison (e.g., running all methods until convergence of the predictive likelihood on a validation set), the claims about computational efficiency are not fully supported.

2.  **Ambiguous Source of Improvement:** While the improvements in predictive likelihood are noteworthy, the paper does not provide sufficient analysis to attribute these gains directly to the proposed FIC criterion. The performance of these complex models is often sensitive to optimization issues, such as initialization and local optima. It is plausible that the observed differences are due to the optimization landscape of the different objective functions rather than the inherent quality of the model selection criterion itself. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of features on some datasets suggests that the baselines may not have been run to convergence or were otherwise hampered by optimization problems. To strengthen the claims, the authors need to provide evidence that the improvement is a direct result of the FIC and not an artifact of the optimization procedure.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a computationally efficient and intuitively appealing method for model selection in IBP models. However, its contribution appears incremental over prior work. More importantly, the paper suffers from a lack of mathematical precision in its theoretical justification and insufficient analysis in its experimental evaluation. The concerns regarding the asymptotic assumptions and the ambiguity in the experimental comparisons make it difficult to fully assess the method's contributions. For the paper to be acceptable, the authors would need to substantially revise the theoretical sections for clarity and rigor and provide a more thorough and well-controlled experimental analysis.
