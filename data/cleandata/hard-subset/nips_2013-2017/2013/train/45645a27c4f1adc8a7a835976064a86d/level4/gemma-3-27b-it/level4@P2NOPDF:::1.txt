This paper introduces a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, presenting an alternative to variational Bayes. Instead of assuming a factorized posterior, the authors approximate parameter integration using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). They demonstrate improved held-out likelihoods compared to existing Infinite Beta Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach is well-motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument and represents a sensible direction for research. A key advantage over other IBP inference methods is its computational efficiency; it manifests as an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with added complexity penalties, avoiding the higher cost of sampling or full variational inference. However, the technical contributions appear incremental, building directly on previous work from the authors ([3, 4]) related to Factor Analysis with Bayesian (FAB) priors. 

Several aspects of the presentation require clarification. The mathematical notation occasionally lacks precision, leading to potential ambiguities. For example, equation (3) contains a log-sum that becomes infinite when all z values are zero – a scenario with non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution. The paper does not address why this does not invariably result in an infinite FIC score. 

Furthermore, Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood can be asymptotically approximated, but lacks crucial details regarding the asymptotic regime and underlying assumptions. Specifically, the behavior of *K* (the number of components) as *N* (the dataset size) increases is undefined. The application of the BIC to each component individually suggests a fixed, finite *K*, requiring an infinite number of activations per feature as *N* grows. This constraint conflicts with the core motivation of using an IBP model, which allows for an infinite number of components and continuous discovery of new features with increasing data.

Several minor technical points should also be addressed. In section 2, the likelihood *p<sub>k</sub>(X | z<sub>•,k</sub>)* should arguably be calculated only on the data points assigned to component *k*, rather than the entire dataset. Additionally, equation (4) appears to be missing an expectation operator on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results presented in Table 1 demonstrate that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihoods with faster execution times compared to alternative methods. While FAB exhibits significantly faster completion times, interpreting this result is difficult given the unspecified stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler. The choice of when to terminate the sampler seems somewhat arbitrary.

The reported improvements in predictive likelihood are encouraging, but the source of these gains remains unclear. To strengthen the conclusions, further analysis is needed to determine whether the improvements are attributable to the model selection criterion itself, or merely to differences in convergence to local optima. The fact that the Gibbs sampler often learns an excessively large number of components in some datasets suggests susceptibility to local optima – particularly since, in theory, the Gibbs sampler should compute the exact likelihood that FAB approximates.

In summary, while the proposed approach is conceptually sound and offers computational benefits, its contributions are somewhat incremental, and the presentation contains ambiguities. The experimental results demonstrate improvements in both running time and predictive likelihood; however, further investigation is necessary to confidently attribute these gains to the novel model selection criterion and rule out the influence of algorithmic convergence behavior.
