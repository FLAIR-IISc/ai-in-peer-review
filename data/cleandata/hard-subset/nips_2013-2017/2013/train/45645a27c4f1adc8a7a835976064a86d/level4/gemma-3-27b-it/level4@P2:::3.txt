The paper presents an inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation is represented by a binary vector indicating the presence of latent features. The algorithm aims to learn these latent features and the number of features (K) simultaneously, employing a Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference approach. This differs from standard nonparametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) by utilizing a model selection criterion rather than priors to induce model parsimony. The method demonstrates competitive performance, particularly in terms of running time and inference quality on large datasets, when compared to Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation methods for the IBP.

The technical quality appears sound, with a logically presented algorithm and seemingly correct implementation details. The ability to handle large datasets and automatically determine the dimensionality of the latent space is a significant strength. The paper is generally clear in its methodological explanation, though experimental details require further clarification. The proposed method is novel in its application of the recently developed FAB framework to LFMs. Addressing the important problem of efficient inference in LFMs, including model selection (determining K), constitutes a valuable contribution.

However, several weaknesses and points for improvement exist. The paper lacks a discussion of potential limitations. For example, under what conditions might the algorithm perform poorly compared to standard Gibbs sampling? Convergence issues inherent in EM-like procedures are not addressed. The algorithm’s restriction to binary latent features limits its applicability to models like factor analysis or probabilistic PCA. The experimental evaluation, while extensive, could be more rigorous. Specifically, the behavior of the algorithm in the small-data regime, where the asymptotic arguments may not hold, is not discussed or empirically investigated. Relevant recent work on efficient inference in LFMs is not fully acknowledged. Critically, the comparison with MEIBP [22] is flawed as that algorithm is only applicable to non-negative data, and the synthetic and block data experiments do not meet this requirement.

Specific comments include: the noise variance in the block image data experiment should be consistent across all methods for a fair comparison. The VB-IBP experiments should utilize the infinite variational version rather than the finite one, and the experimental settings require more detail (e.g., the number of restarts). The reported number of latent features discovered by accelerated Gibbs sampling on the Sonar data seems unusually high and warrants investigation regarding initialization and noise variance settings. Reference [21] incorrectly describes the work as Gibbs sampling; it is, in fact, a MAP estimation method. The related work section should include a discussion of "MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes" (ICML 2013), which explores efficient inference using small-variance asymptotics for nonparametric LFMs. Minor revisions include correcting the noise variance suggestion on line 349.

Following author feedback, some concerns were addressed, but further improvements are necessary before acceptance. The experimental methodology for both the proposed method and baselines needs clarification and justification for observed behaviors. For example, explain why certain algorithms exhibited specific performance characteristics (e.g., overestimated K due to a fixed IBP alpha, or improperly tuned noise variance hyperparameters). Correct the description of reference [21] to reflect its focus on MAP inference. Incorporate a citation to the MAD-Bayes inference work.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The proposed inference method is interesting and offers a compelling approach to efficient LFM inference, particularly its ability to scale to large datasets and simultaneously infer the number of latent features.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient rigor and justification, potentially undermining the claimed advantages of the method. The flawed comparison with MEIBP and omission of relevant related work raise concerns about the completeness and accuracy of the evaluation. The absence of a discussion regarding limitations and convergence behavior weakens the overall contribution.

**Overall Assessment:** Reject with request for major revisions. The core idea is promising, but the experimental validation and presentation require substantial improvement before the paper can be considered a significant contribution to the field. A more thorough and carefully justified experimental analysis, alongside addressing the identified limitations and incorporating the suggested related work, is crucial for acceptance.
