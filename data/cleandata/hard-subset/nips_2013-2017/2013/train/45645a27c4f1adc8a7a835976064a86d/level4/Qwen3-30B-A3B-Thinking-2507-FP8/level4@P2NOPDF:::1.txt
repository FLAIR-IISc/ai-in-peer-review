

This paper introduces a model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, specifically targeting inference in the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). The approach extends variational Bayes by approximating parameter integration via the BIC, avoiding the need for a factorized posterior. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihoods compared to existing IBP implementations, with computational efficiency gained through an EM-like algorithm incorporating complexity penalties—offering a faster alternative to sampling or variational methods.

The technical contribution appears novel within its scope but is largely incremental, building directly on the FAB framework from [3, 4]. While the asymptotic motivation for the criterion is plausible, several critical gaps undermine the theoretical foundation. First, Equation (3) contains a log-sum-exp term that diverges to infinity when all \(z_{n,k} = 0\) (a non-zero probability event under any non-degenerate \(q\)), rendering the FIC score undefined. This fundamental issue requires clarification. Second, Theorem 2 claims the marginal likelihood is "asymptotically approximated as" a specific form, yet omits the asymptotic regime, key assumptions, and the behavior of \(K\) (the number of features) as \(N\) (data size) grows. Crucially, the BIC application per component assumes fixed finite \(K\), contradicting the IBP’s core motivation—where \(K\) is infinite and grows with data. Assuming fixed \(K\) invalidates the rationale for using the IBP, as new features should continue emerging with more data.

Minor technical inconsistencies further weaken the exposition. In Section 2, \(p_k(X \mid z_{\cdot,k})\) should restrict to data points assigned to component \(k\), not the full dataset. Equation (4) requires an expectation operator on the left-hand side. 

The empirical results (Table 1) show the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihoods in less time than alternatives. However, the runtime comparison is problematic: FAB’s speed advantage (orders of magnitude) is not contextualized by a specified stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler. The arbitrary termination point for sampling-based methods invalidates direct runtime comparisons. More critically, the reported likelihood improvements cannot be conclusively attributed to the model selection criterion. The Gibbs sampler’s tendency to learn excessively large component counts (evident in some datasets) suggests suboptimal local optima—likely due to implementation details (e.g., initialization)—rather than a flaw in the FAB approximation. Since Gibbs theoretically computes the exact marginal likelihood (which FAB approximates), the observed differences may stem from algorithmic behavior, not the criterion itself. Without controlled experiments isolating the criterion’s impact (e.g., using identical optimization procedures), the results remain ambiguous.

In summary, the paper proposes a computationally efficient inference approach for IBP with modest empirical gains. However, the theoretical analysis is incomplete due to unresolved mathematical issues (Equation 3, Theorem 2’s assumptions), and the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor to distinguish the contribution of the model selection criterion from algorithmic artifacts. The work is technically sound but incremental, with the strongest claim being the speed advantage. To strengthen the contribution, the authors must: (1) resolve the divergence in Equation 3, (2) clarify the asymptotic regime for Theorem 2 (e.g., specify whether \(K\) is fixed or grows with \(N\)), and (3) conduct ablation studies to isolate the criterion’s effect from optimization dynamics. Without these, the results cannot robustly support the methodological claims.
