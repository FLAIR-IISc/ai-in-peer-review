This paper introduces a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, offering an alternative to variational Bayes by approximating parameter integration through the use of the BIC. The authors demonstrate improvements in held-out likelihood compared to existing IBP implementations.

The proposed method is well-motivated by asymptotic reasoning and offers a computational advantage over existing IBP inference techniques. Specifically, it frames the inference as an EM algorithm with added complexity penalties, avoiding the computational cost of sampling or full variational Bayes. However, the technical contributions, while novel, appear incremental, building directly upon the FAB work presented in [3] and [4]. 

Several aspects of the presentation require clarification. The mathematical notation lacks precision in places; for example, equation (3) contains a logarithmic sum that becomes infinite when all values are zero, a scenario with non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution. The paper does not address why the resulting FIC score isn't consistently infinite. Furthermore, Theorem 2 states an asymptotic approximation of the marginal likelihood without clearly defining the asymptotic regime or underlying assumptions, particularly regarding the behavior of *K* as *N* increases. The application of the BIC to each component individually suggests a fixed, finite *K*, which contrasts with the infinite component nature of the IBP model and diminishes a key motivation for its use. 

Minor technical points include a potential scope issue in section 2, where *p<sub>k</sub>(X | z<sub>•, k</sub>)* appears to consider the entire dataset rather than only data points assigned to component *k*. Additionally, an expectation appears to be missing on the left-hand side of equation (4).

The quantitative results in Table 1 show that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihoods with faster runtimes. While FAB demonstrates significantly faster completion in some instances, the lack of a defined stopping criterion makes interpreting these results difficult, raising concerns about arbitrary decisions in terminating the Gibbs sampler.

The observed improvements in predictive likelihood are noteworthy, but the source of these gains remains unclear. The results would be strengthened by evidence attributing the improvements specifically to the model selection criterion, rather than differences in convergence to local optima – a factor likely influenced by implementation details such as initialization strategies. The Gibbs sampler’s tendency to learn an excessive number of components in certain datasets supports the possibility of local optima issues, given its theoretical ability to compute the exact likelihood that FAB approximates.

In summary, the proposed approach is reasonable and potentially valuable, but its contributions are somewhat incremental. The mathematical presentation could benefit from increased rigor, and further analysis is needed to confidently interpret the experimental results and isolate the impact of the new model selection criterion.
