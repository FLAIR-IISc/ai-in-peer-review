Summary
This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB maximizes a tractable lower bound of a factorized information criterion (FIC) that converges to the marginal log-likelihood in the large-sample limit. A key limitation of prior FAB work was the requirement that the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood be block diagonal. The authors show, via an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian for LFMs, that the resulting FIC for LFMs has exactly the same form as for MMs, despite the fact that LFMs do not satisfy the earlier block-diagonal condition. Building on this, they derive a FAB algorithm for LFMs that inherits desirable properties such as automatic feature selection and parameter identifiability. They further relate the FAB E-step to a convex–concave procedure (CCCP) and propose a shrinkage-acceleration scheme that reduces computational cost in practice. Experiments on synthetic and real data compare FAB/LFM against fast Gibbs sampling for IBP-based models, variational Bayes (VB), and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP), demonstrating advantages in model selection accuracy, prediction, and runtime.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: the analysis showing that the FIC for LFMs matches that of MMs, even without the block-diagonal Hessian condition, is elegant and useful.
- Practical algorithm: the FAB/LFM procedure provides automatic determination of the number of latent features and includes a principled shrinkage mechanism.
- Acceleration: the CCCP interpretation motivates an effective shrinkage-acceleration step, yielding substantial computational savings.
- Empirical results: on both synthetic and real datasets, the method achieves strong model selection and predictive performance with competitive or superior runtime, often outperforming IBP, VB, and MEIBP baselines.
- Clarity: the paper is well written and the derivations are thoroughly explained.

Weaknesses
- The contribution, while solid, is somewhat incremental in that it extends FAB from MMs to LFMs without introducing a fundamentally new modeling framework.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The paper appears technically sound. The asymptotic arguments are well motivated, and the algorithm is derived carefully. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and supports the claims about accuracy and efficiency.
- Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The derivation steps are transparent, and the algorithmic components (including the shrinkage and merging procedures) are described clearly.
- Originality: Extending FAB from MMs to LFMs is an incremental but meaningful advance. The identification that the FIC has the same form as in MMs despite the lack of a block-diagonal Hessian is a noteworthy insight.
- Significance: The ability to perform automated model selection in LFMs with improved efficiency and accuracy is valuable, and should be of interest to the NIPS community.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical insight with a clean extension of FAB to LFMs.
- Automatic feature selection and identifiability properties.
- Effective acceleration (CCCP-based shrinkage).
- Convincing empirical results across synthetic and real datasets.
- Clear and careful presentation.

Cons for acceptance
- The contribution is primarily an extension of an existing approach and thus somewhat incremental.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a well-executed paper that presents a compelling extension of FAB to LFMs, with both theoretical and practical contributions. The results are convincing, the method is efficient, and I believe it will interest the NIPS community. I support acceptance.
