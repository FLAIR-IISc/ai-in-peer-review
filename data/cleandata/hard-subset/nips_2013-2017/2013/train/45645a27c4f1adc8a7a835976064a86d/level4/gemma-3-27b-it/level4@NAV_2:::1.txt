This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a limitation of prior FAB work that required a specific condition on the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that LFMs satisfy a similar Hessian structure to mixture models, enabling the application of FAB. They further present theoretical results regarding parameter identifiability and propose a shrinkage acceleration method based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) to improve computational efficiency. Empirical evaluation demonstrates competitive performance in model selection, prediction, and speed compared to state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods.

The core idea of extending FAB inference to LFMs is well-motivated and represents a logical step in developing this Bayesian approximation technique. The theoretical analysis establishing the similarity in Hessian structure between LFMs and mixture models (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is a key contribution, enabling the application of established FAB principles. The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, leveraging a CCCP interpretation, offers a practical improvement to computational efficiency, and the identifiability analysis provides valuable theoretical insight. The experimental results, particularly the improved predictive likelihoods and reduced computation times, are encouraging.

However, the paper suffers from occasional imprecision in its mathematical presentation, which hinders clarity. For instance, the potential for infinite values in equation (3) when all z values are zero is not addressed, and the asymptotic assumptions underlying Theorem 2 require further clarification, specifically regarding the behavior of K as N grows. The connection to the IBP model and the implications of assuming a finite K within the asymptotic analysis should be more thoroughly discussed, as it potentially undermines the motivation for using an infinite-dimensional prior. Minor notational inconsistencies, such as the scope of the summation in section 2, also detract from the paper’s polish.

Arguments for acceptance:
* **Novel Extension of FAB:** The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to a new class of models (LFMs) where it was previously inapplicable.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The analysis of the Hessian structure and the identifiability result are valuable theoretical contributions.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method offers a practical improvement in computational speed.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing IBP methods, particularly in terms of predictive likelihood and runtime.

Arguments against acceptance:
* **Mathematical Imprecision:** Occasional imprecision and lack of clarity in the mathematical derivations make the paper difficult to follow in places.
* **Asymptotic Assumptions:** The asymptotic assumptions underlying key theoretical results require more rigorous justification, especially concerning the behavior of the model dimensionality (K).
* **Limited Analysis of Results:** While the experimental results are promising, a more in-depth analysis is needed to determine whether the performance improvements are due to the model selection criterion itself or to differences in optimization behavior (e.g., local optima). 

Overall, this is a solid contribution with clear potential. Addressing the concerns regarding mathematical precision and providing a more thorough analysis of the experimental results would significantly strengthen the paper. The work is a valuable addition to the literature on Bayesian inference for latent variable models, and I lean towards acceptance, contingent on addressing the identified weaknesses.
