Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the factorized information criterion (FIC) from mixture and HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs). The key technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood in LFMs, showing that, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition used in earlier FAB derivations, the resulting FIC has the same functional form as in mixture models. This yields the same desirable regularization term that drives automatic pruning of irrelevant features and supports parameter identifiability. The authors further interpret the FAB E-step as a convex–concave procedure (CCCP), which motivates a shrinkage acceleration scheme that removes irrelevant features early to reduce computation. They also analyze identifiability and propose a merge post-processing step guided by this analysis.

Empirically, the method is compared against strong baselines for LFMs, including Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants (Gibbs sampling and variational Bayes), maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP), and a variational EM baseline, on synthetic and several real datasets. Results show competitive or superior model selection and predictive performance, with substantial computational speedups, and demonstrate the approach’s automated nature (minimal hyperparameter tuning). The paper is well-situated with respect to prior NIPS and related work on FAB for mixtures/HMMs and nonparametric Bayesian models for LFMs (IBP, VB, MEIBP).

Quality:
- Theory: The asymptotic derivation (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is central and well-motivated, with proofs deferred to the supplement. The resulting FIC matches the mixture-model form and underpins automatic feature shrinkage and identifiability. The optimization is based on a tractable lower bound that is increased monotonically, and the CCCP interpretation is clear and useful.
- Empirics: The evaluation spans synthetic and real datasets, with comparisons to a broad set of baselines. The reported gains in computational efficiency are significant, and the method typically learns compact models with strong predictive log-likelihoods. References are sufficient.

Clarity:
The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The intuition for why the LFM FIC matches the mixture-model form despite the lack of block-diagonal structure is helpful, and the algorithmic presentation (including accelerateShrinkage and merge) is easy to follow. Figures and experimental descriptions are clear.

Originality:
This is a logical and solid generalization of FAB/FIC to LFMs. Demonstrating that the LFM FIC reduces to the same form as for mixtures is a noteworthy theoretical insight. The CCCP-based acceleration and the identifiability-guided merge step are practical contributions that complement the theoretical development.

Significance:
Automated, scalable model selection for LFMs is important, given the costs and tuning burden of IBP/VB methods. The proposed FAB/LFM approach offers a compelling alternative with strong empirical performance and reduced computation, and it is likely to be useful to practitioners and researchers working with latent feature models.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Multiple approximations interact (FIC asymptotics, mean-field factorization, shrinkage acceleration, and merging). It would be valuable to “push” the method until it breaks: characterize regimes where performance degrades (e.g., small N, highly collinear features, extreme noise levels, or heavy-tailed data) and how each approximation contributes. An ablation study would clarify the individual impact of mean-field, shrinkage acceleration (Tshrink), and merging on accuracy, runtime, and selected K.
- The key assumptions behind Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are in the supplement; summarizing their practical scope in the main text (and providing diagnostics for when they may be violated) would strengthen the paper.
- Since the approach asymptotically ignores priors, guidance on behavior in small-sample regimes and how the prior term compares to the FAB regularizer would help readers anticipate limitations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical contribution: FIC for LFMs with the same regularization structure as mixtures, enabling automatic feature selection and identifiability.
- Practical algorithm with monotonic lower-bound improvements, CCCP interpretation, and effective shrinkage acceleration.
- Strong empirical results across multiple datasets, with substantial computational gains and compact models.
- Minimal hyperparameter tuning; good clarity and organization; thorough related work.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited exploration of failure modes and sensitivity to the interacting approximations.
- Practical scope of the asymptotic assumptions is mostly deferred to the supplement.

Overall, this is a well-executed, substantive extension of FAB to LFMs, with both theoretical and practical value. The paper would be further strengthened by a more explicit analysis of when its approximations may lead to poor performance.
