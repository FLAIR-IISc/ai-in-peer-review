This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion, the Feature Inclusion Criterion (FIC), for binary latent feature models. The approach is analogous to variational Bayes, but instead of assuming a fully factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximates the integration of the parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing implementations for the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

**Strengths:**

The proposed approach is well-motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument and presents a sensible alternative to existing methods. The primary advantage over other IBP inference techniques is computational efficiency. The method corresponds to an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, which is substantially less expensive than Gibbs sampling or standard variational Bayes algorithms. The experimental results show that the proposed method can achieve higher predictive likelihood scores in less time than several alternative methods.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The main weaknesses of the paper lie in the imprecise mathematical exposition and the interpretation of the experimental results.

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Precision:** Some of the paper's theoretical claims are not clearly defined, making them difficult to assess.
    *   In Equation (3), the term `log(sum_i z_ik)` is negative infinity if all `z_ik` are zero for a given feature `k`. Since this configuration has a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution `q`, it is unclear why the FIC score is not always infinite. This point requires clarification.
    *   Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated," but the asymptotic regime and its underlying assumptions are not specified. In particular, how is the number of features `K` assumed to behave as the number of data points `N` gets larger? The use of BIC for each component individually seems to require a fixed, finite `K`, so that the number of activations for every feature would approach infinity. This assumption runs counter to a key motivation for using the IBP, where the number of features is infinite and new features are expected to be discovered as more data is observed.

2.  **Ambiguity in Experimental Analysis:** While the quantitative results in Table 1 are promising, the analysis is not thorough enough to fully interpret them.
    *   The comparison of running times is inconclusive because the stopping criteria for the various algorithms are not specified. For instance, the runtime of a Gibbs sampler is often determined by a user-defined number of iterations, making direct speed comparisons with a convergence-based method difficult to interpret without further details.
    *   The source of the improved predictive likelihood is ambiguous. The improvements could be due to the proposed model selection criterion, but they could also be an artifact of the optimization procedure (e.g., better initialization or avoidance of poor local optima). The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets suggests it may be struggling with local optima, which would weaken the validity of the comparison. The results would be more convincing if the authors provided evidence that the performance gains are specifically due to the FIC criterion rather than implementation details.

3.  **Incremental Novelty:** The technical contributions appear to be an incremental extension of the Factorial Asymptotic Bayes (FAB) framework from references [3] and [4].

**Minor Points:**

*   In Section 2, shouldn't the likelihood term `p_k(X | z_{·,k})` be conditioned only on the data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset `X`?
*   In Equation (4), the left-hand side appears to be missing an expectation with respect to the variational distribution `q`.

In summary, the proposed approach is sensible but feels somewhat incremental. The lack of precision in the theoretical development and the ambiguity in the experimental analysis are significant concerns that should be addressed.
