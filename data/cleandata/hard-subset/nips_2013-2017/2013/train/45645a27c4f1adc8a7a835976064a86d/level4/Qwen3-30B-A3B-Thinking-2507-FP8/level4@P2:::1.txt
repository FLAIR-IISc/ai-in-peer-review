

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a gap in previous FAB applications that required the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood to be block diagonal. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying this condition, their Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) takes the same form as for mixture models (MMs), enabling FAB application to LFMs. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) asymptotic analysis showing FIC for LFMs matches MMs despite different model structures; (2) an FAB algorithm for LFMs with automatic feature selection through model-induced regularization; and (3) a shrinkage acceleration method that significantly reduces computational cost while maintaining performance.

The paper is well-motivated by the need for efficient model selection in LFMs, where existing methods like Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations suffer from high computational costs. The authors successfully relate their approach to prior work: FAB inference for MMs [5,6] achieves comparable model selection accuracy to state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian (NPB) methods with less computation, while the paper extends this framework to LFMs. The connection to variational inference and convex-concave procedures (CCCP) is well-explained, particularly regarding how FAB's E-step resembles variational inference in restricted Boltzmann machines.

The main strengths of this work include:
- Clear theoretical derivation showing FIC for LFMs has the same form as for MMs despite different model structures, which is a significant contribution to understanding the general applicability of FIC.
- The introduction of a shrinkage acceleration method that drastically reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early in optimization (Algorithm 2), with empirical validation showing substantial speedups without compromising performance.
- Demonstrated parameter identifiability for FAB/LFMs (Theorem 4), which resolves non-identifiability issues in standard EM for LFMs and enables post-processing through feature merging.
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing FAB/LFMs achieve better predictive performance with lower computational cost compared to IBP, variational EM, and MEIBP across artificial and real-world datasets.

The paper's main weaknesses include:
- Some theoretical statements lack precision. Theorem 2 claims "log p(X,Z|M) = J(Z,P̂) + Op(1)" without specifying the asymptotic regime or assumptions about how K behaves as N increases. For IBP applications, K is typically allowed to grow with N, but the paper's analysis seems to assume fixed finite K, which contradicts one of IBP's main motivations (handling infinite feature spaces).
- The claim that "log ∑n znk is infinite if all znk=0" in Equation (3) is misleading. The paper uses expectations Eq[log ∑n znk], which are finite under any non-degenerate variational distribution q, as the authors correctly note in their derivation.
- The experimental comparison lacks sufficient analysis to distinguish whether performance gains stem from the model selection criterion or implementation details. For example, the Gibbs sampler's tendency to select excessive features in some datasets suggests local optima issues rather than limitations of the FAB framework.
- The stopping criterion for computational comparisons (δ=10−4) is not thoroughly justified, making it difficult to assess whether the speed advantage is due to algorithmic efficiency or arbitrary stopping conditions.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that the approach achieves automatic feature selection with computational efficiency comparable to variational methods but with better predictive performance. The shrinkage acceleration method is particularly noteworthy for its practical impact.

The authors should clarify the asymptotic assumptions in their theoretical results, particularly regarding the relationship between K and N. While the paper demonstrates good empirical results, the explanation of why FAB achieves better predictive performance than competing methods would be strengthened by additional analysis isolating the contribution of the model selection criterion from other factors like optimization algorithms or initialization strategies.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound extension of FAB inference to LFMs with clear theoretical justification and practical benefits. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate improved computational efficiency and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. The work is well-written, well-organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent feature models. While the theoretical analysis could be more precise, the paper's strengths substantially outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
