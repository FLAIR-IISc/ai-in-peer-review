Summary:
The authors propose an efficient method for model selection in latent feature models, based on factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the factorized information criterion (FIC). While FAB and FIC are established techniques for mixture models—where FIC approximates the model's log-likelihood via factorization and the Laplace approximation to enable tractable model selection—the primary contribution here is their generalization to latent feature models. This generalization employs a mean-field approximation for inference over the latent features and introduces an accelerated shrinkage mechanism to determine the global number of features.

A key advantage of this approach is the high degree of automation in model selection, requiring minimal manual tuning. The method's significant gains in computational efficiency are demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Quality:
The paper presents a thorough analysis, evaluating the proposed method against a wide range of competing approaches across multiple datasets. The work is well-supported by sufficient references.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well structured.

Originality/Significance:
This work is a logical and solid generalization of previous research on model selection for latent feature models using FAB inference and FIC. Its novelty lies in the successful combination of several approximation techniques into a single, effective method.

However, the analysis could be strengthened by exploring the limitations of this approach. Given that multiple approximations (FIC, mean-field, shrinkage) are playing a role simultaneously, it would be valuable to understand the conditions under which they might interact to produce poor performance. An analysis of the method's failure modes—in effect, "pushing" the method until it breaks—would provide a clearer picture of its robustness and practical boundaries.
