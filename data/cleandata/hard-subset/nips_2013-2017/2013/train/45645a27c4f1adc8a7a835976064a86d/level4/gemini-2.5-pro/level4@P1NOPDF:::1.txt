This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. The method is analogous to variational Bayes; however, rather than assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximates the marginal likelihood of the parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach is well-motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. Its primary advantage over other methods for IBP inference is computational efficiency. The resulting algorithm is structured like an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, making it less costly than typical sampling or variational Bayes algorithms.

The technical contributions appear novel but incremental, as they primarily extend the Feature-Allocation-BIC (FAB) framework of [3] and [4].

Certain parts of the exposition are confusing due to what appears to be mathematical imprecision. In Equation (3), the term involving `log(sum_k z_nk)` is undefined (negative infinity) if all `z_nk` are zero for a given data point `n`. Since this configuration would have a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution `q`, it is unclear why the proposed FIC score is not always negative infinity.

The theoretical justification also lacks clarity. Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated," but the asymptotic regime and underlying assumptions are not specified. In particular, it is not stated how the number of features, K, is assumed to behave as the number of data points, N, grows. Since the BIC is applied to each feature individually, the theorem seems to implicitly require K to be fixed and finite, ensuring that the number of data points associated with each feature approaches infinity. This assumption, however, contradicts a key motivation for using the IBP, which posits an infinite number of features that grows with the dataset.

There are also a few minor points regarding the mathematical formulation. In Section 2, the likelihood term `p_k(X | z_{., k})` seems to be defined over the entire dataset X. Shouldn't this term instead be conditioned only on the subset of data points assigned to feature k? Additionally, the left-hand side of Equation (4) appears to be missing an expectation with respect to the variational distribution `q`.

In the quantitative results in Table 1, the proposed method is shown to achieve higher predictive likelihood scores in less time than several alternatives. However, the lack of specified stopping criteria makes these results difficult to interpret. The decision of when to terminate the Gibbs sampler, for example, can be arbitrary and significantly impact both runtime and performance.

While the improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, their source is unclear. The results would be more convincing if the authors provided evidence that the improvement stems from the superiority of their model selection criterion, rather than from optimization issues like algorithms getting stuck in different local optima. The latter is often highly dependent on implementation details such as initialization strategies. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of features on some datasets suggests that optimization is a confounding factor, since a well-mixed sampler should, in theory, converge to the true posterior that the proposed method only approximates.

In summary, the proposed approach is well-motivated but appears to be an incremental extension of prior work. The mathematical presentation lacks precision in key areas, which raises questions about the method's theoretical underpinnings. While the experimental results show improvements in runtime and predictive likelihood, they are not analyzed in sufficient depth to convincingly attribute these gains to the proposed criterion, as opposed to optimization or implementation artifacts.
