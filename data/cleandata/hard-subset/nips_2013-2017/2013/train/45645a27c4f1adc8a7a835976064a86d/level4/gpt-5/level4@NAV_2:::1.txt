This paper introduces a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, akin to variational Bayes but replacing a fully factorized posterior assumption with an asymptotic integration over parameters via a BIC-like treatment. The authors report improved held-out likelihood relative to several existing IBP-based methods.

The approach appears sensible and is grounded in a plausible asymptotic justification. Its main practical advantage over prior IBP inference strategies is computational: it reduces to an EM-style routine augmented with explicit complexity penalties, avoiding the heavier costs of sampling-based or standard variational Bayes procedures.

The technical contributions seem original yet incremental, essentially extending the FAB methodology of [3] and [4].

Some aspects of the presentation are confusing due to what appears to be imprecise mathematics. In Eq. (3), the log of the sum over z becomes undefined (i.e., diverges) when all entries are zero. Since this event has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution q, why doesn’t the FIC objective blow up?

Theorem 2 claims the marginal likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated as” a given expression, but the asymptotic regime and assumptions are not clearly specified. In particular, how does K scale with N? Because the BIC-like treatment is applied per component, the result seems to rely on a fixed, finite K so that each feature’s activation count grows without bound. Under an IBP prior, the number of features is unbounded and new features emerge as N increases, so assuming fixed K sidesteps one of IBP’s core motivations.

Minor issues: in Section 2, shouldn’t pk(X | z·,k) involve only the data assigned to component k rather than the entire dataset? And in Eq. (4), an expectation seems to be missing on the left-hand side.

In the quantitative results (Table 1), the proposed method attains higher predictive likelihoods in less time than competing approaches. While FAB finishes much faster in several cases, it is difficult to interpret this without a clear stopping criterion. In particular, the choice of when to halt the Gibbs sampler seems somewhat arbitrary.

The gains in predictive likelihood are notable, but it is unclear what drives them. Stronger evidence would be helpful to separate improvements due to the model selection criterion itself from those caused by different local optima—effects that likely hinge on implementation details such as initialization. The observation that the Gibbs sampler learns an overly large number of components on some datasets suggests local optima issues; in principle, it should be estimating the likelihood that FAB is approximating.

Overall, the proposed approach is reasonable but somewhat incremental. Parts of the analysis appear imprecise. The experiments show advantages in runtime and predictive performance over prior methods, but, as noted above, additional analysis is needed to clearly attribute these improvements.
