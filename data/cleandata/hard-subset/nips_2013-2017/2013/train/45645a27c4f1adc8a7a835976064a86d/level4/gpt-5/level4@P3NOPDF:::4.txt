This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB is a model selection framework that maximizes a lower bound of the factorized information criterion (FIC), which asymptotically approximates the marginal log-likelihood. A known limitation of prior FAB applications is the requirement that the Hessian of the complete-data likelihood be block diagonal. The authors address this by deriving a tractable lower bound of the FIC for LFMs, even though this condition is not met. Notably, the resulting bound takes the same functional form as the FIC used for MMs, enabling efficient optimization and automatic feature pruning in the LFM setting.

Empirically, the paper reports results on both synthetic and real datasets, comparing FAB/LFM against strong baselines, including fast Gibbs sampling for Indian Buffet Process (IBP) models, variational Bayes (VB) methods, and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP). Across these comparisons, FAB/LFM achieves superior predictive performance and reduced computational cost.

Strengths:
- Clear and careful derivation: The paper is well written and provides a transparent walkthrough of the extension from MMs to LFMs, including the key steps needed to bypass the block-diagonal Hessian requirement.
- Theoretical coherence: The derived FIC lower bound for LFMs mirrors the MM case, suggesting that the practical benefits of FAB—such as automatic model selection and feature pruning—carry over naturally to LFMs.
- Empirical validation: The comparisons against established IBP-based methods, VB, and MEIBP on both synthetic and real data are appropriate and indicate consistent advantages in both accuracy and efficiency.
- Practical relevance: Reducing computation while maintaining or improving predictive performance is valuable, and automatic feature selection is particularly attractive for high-dimensional LFM applications.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Incremental novelty: Conceptually, the work extends the FAB framework to a new family (LFMs) rather than introducing a fundamentally new criterion. While the extension is technically nontrivial and useful, the novelty is moderate.
- Assumptions and tightness: It would strengthen the paper to more explicitly state the assumptions under which the new lower bound is derived and to discuss the conditions affecting its tightness relative to the true FIC or marginal likelihood.
- Robustness and sensitivity: Additional analysis of sensitivity to initialization, hyperparameters, and stopping criteria would be helpful. For example, how robust is feature pruning to suboptimal starts, and does the method risk pruning true features early?
- Scalability details: Given the claimed computational benefits, a more detailed complexity analysis (in terms of N, D, and the inferred number of features) and wall-clock breakdowns would provide clearer guidance for practitioners. Clarifying implementation details (e.g., batching, parallelization) and offering code would further support reproducibility.
- Baseline breadth: The chosen baselines are reasonable; however, discussing the relationship to other contemporary nonparametric feature learning approaches (even qualitatively) would give a fuller picture of where FAB/LFM stands.

Minor comments:
- Fix typographical errors (e.g., “LFMS” → “LFMs,” “ad” → “as,” “Indian Bufffet” → “Indian Buffet,” “GIbbs” → “Gibbs,” and “it’s efficiency” → “its efficiency”).
- Briefly summarize the intuition of why the MM-style FIC form remains valid or useful in LFMs despite the non-block-diagonal Hessian, to aid readers less familiar with the technical details.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and well-executed extension of FAB to latent feature models. While the contribution is somewhat incremental, the technical development is sound, the exposition is clear, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate benefits in both predictive accuracy and computational efficiency. The work is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community, especially those working on Bayesian nonparametrics, feature learning, and scalable model selection. I recommend acceptance.
