

Summary:  
The authors introduce a novel algorithm for efficient model selection in latent feature models (LFMs), leveraging factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the factorized information criterion (FIC). While FAB and FIC are established for mixture models—where FIC approximates log-likelihood via factorization and Laplace approximation for tractable model selection—their key contribution generalizes these techniques to LFMs. This involves employing a mean-field approximation for latent inference and an accelerated shrinkage mechanism for the global feature count. The approach significantly enhances automation, minimizing manual tuning, and demonstrates substantial computational gains on both synthetic and real-world datasets.  

Quality:  
The evaluation is comprehensive, featuring rigorous analysis across multiple datasets and comparisons against diverse state-of-the-art baselines. Statistical significance of results is appropriately addressed, and the literature review is sufficiently thorough, covering relevant prior work in model selection for LFMs.  

Clarity:  
The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written, with logical flow from problem formulation to methodological details, experiments, and conclusions. Technical concepts are explained accessibly, and figures effectively support key insights.  

Originality/Significance:  
This work constitutes a meaningful advancement by extending FAB/FIC to LFMs—a non-trivial generalization that addresses a critical computational bottleneck in the field. The fusion of mean-field inference, accelerated shrinkage, and FIC into a unified framework represents a substantial methodological step beyond prior extensions. Its significance lies in enabling scalable model selection for LFMs, which has been hindered by prohibitive computational costs in practice. This paves the way for broader application of LFMs in large-scale settings.  

Major Strengths:  
1. **Computational Efficiency**: The method achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups over competitors (e.g., 10–100× faster on real datasets), making model selection feasible for large-scale problems.  
2. **Automation**: The reduced need for manual hyperparameter tuning is a practical advantage over existing approaches.  
3. **Empirical Validation**: Results on diverse datasets (e.g., image, text, and biological data) robustly demonstrate superior efficiency without sacrificing accuracy.  

Critical Concerns:  
While the paper convincingly establishes the method’s efficacy, a deeper exploration of failure modes is needed. Specifically:  
- **Approximation Sensitivity**: The method relies on three key approximations (FIC, mean-field, shrinkage). It would strengthen the contribution to analyze *when* these approximations degrade performance—e.g., under high feature correlation, sparse data regimes, or extreme model complexity. A controlled "stress test" (e.g., systematically varying data sparsity or feature dependencies) would clarify the method’s boundaries.  
- **FIC Justification**: The paper assumes FIC’s validity for LFMs but does not rigorously justify why the factorization assumptions hold here (unlike in mixture models where they are well-established). A brief theoretical discussion or empirical validation of FIC’s accuracy in LFM contexts would bolster confidence.  
- **Comparison Scope**: While comparisons include several baselines, the absence of recent scalable alternatives (e.g., variational methods tailored for LFMs) leaves room for ambiguity about relative advantages.  

Minor Suggestions:  
- Clarify the "accelerated shrinkage" mechanism: How does it interact with mean-field updates? A brief pseudocode step would aid reproducibility.  
- In Table 2, explicitly state whether computational gains are measured in absolute time (e.g., seconds) or relative speedup to contextualize "significant."  
- Discuss potential limitations in model interpretability—does the shrinkage mechanism bias feature selection?  

Overall Assessment:  
This is a strong, well-executed contribution that addresses a genuine need in scalable model selection for LFMs. The method is technically sound, empirically validated, and offers clear practical benefits. The minor concerns regarding approximation robustness and theoretical grounding are surmountable with targeted revisions. The work advances the field significantly and merits publication at NIPS.  

Recommendation:  
**Accept** (with minor revisions addressing the critical concerns above). The paper’s novelty, thorough evaluation, and practical impact align well with NIPS’ focus on impactful, rigorous machine learning research. The proposed revisions would further solidify its contribution without requiring major rework.
