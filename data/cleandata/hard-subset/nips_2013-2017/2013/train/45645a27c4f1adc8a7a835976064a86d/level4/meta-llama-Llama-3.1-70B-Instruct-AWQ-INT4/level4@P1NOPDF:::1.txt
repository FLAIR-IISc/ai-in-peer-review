This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, which is similar to variational Bayes but with a key difference: instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximately integrates out the parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach appears reasonable and is motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. Its main advantage over other methods for IBP inference is that it corresponds to an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, rather than relying on more expensive sampling or variational Bayes algorithms.

The technical contributions seem novel but incremental, essentially extending the work of [3] and [4]. However, some parts of the exposition are confusing due to imprecise math. For instance, in equation (3), the log sum of the z values is infinite if all values are zero, which has a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution q. This raises the question of why the FIC score is not always infinite.

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated as" a certain expression, but it does not specify the asymptotic regime or the underlying assumptions. In particular, it is unclear how K is assumed to behave as N increases. Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem appears to require a fixed finite K, such that the number of activations of every feature approaches infinity. However, under the IBP model, the number of components is infinite, and new components would continue to be observed as the amount of data increases. Assuming a finite K removes one of the motivations for using the IBP.

Some minor points also need to be addressed. In section 2, pk(X | z{·, k}) should only include the data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset. Additionally, equation (4) is missing an expectation on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results in Table 1 show that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores in less time compared to alternative methods. However, it is unclear how to interpret this result, as the stopping criterion for the algorithms is not specified. The fact that FAB finishes faster by orders of magnitude in some cases seems arbitrary, particularly for the Gibbs sampler. It is unclear when to stop the sampler, and the results may depend on the implementation details, such as initialization strategies.

While the improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, it is unclear where the difference comes from. The results would be more convincing if there was evidence that the difference is due to the model selection criterion rather than the algorithms getting stuck in worse local optima. The latter is likely to depend on the implementation details, and the fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components in some datasets suggests that it is a problem with local optima.

In conclusion, the proposed approach seems reasonable, but it is somewhat incremental. Some of the math appears imprecise, and while the experimental results show improvements in running time and predictive likelihood, further analysis is needed to interpret these results convincingly.
