This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB is a recently developed model selection method that has demonstrated strong performance with mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models, maximizing a computationally tractable lower bound of a factorized information criterion (FIC) that converges to the marginal log-likelihood. A key limitation of existing FAB methods is their applicability only to models satisfying a condition requiring the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood to be block diagonal. This paper addresses this limitation by extending FAB to LFMs, despite the fact that LFMs do not satisfy this condition. The authors effectively derive a lower bound for the FIC in LFMs, demonstrating that it shares the same representation as the FIC for MMs. They present results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing FAB/LFM to alternative methods including fast Gibbs sampling with Indian Buffet processes, variational Bayes (VB), and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP). The results illustrate the advantages of FAB/LFM, demonstrating superior performance in both prediction and computational efficiency.

The paper is well-written and the authors provide a thorough description of the derivation of their method. The extension of FAB from MMs to LFMs represents an incremental, yet interesting, contribution. The results presented support the efficacy of the proposed approach and suggest it would be of interest to the NIPS community. Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a compelling idea, supported by convincing results. I recommend its acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Extends a promising Bayesian inference method (FAB) to a new and relevant model class (LFMs).
*   Theoretical justification is provided, showing the FIC for LFMs has a similar form to MMs, despite not meeting the original assumptions.
*   Empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of FAB/LFMs compared to established methods in terms of both accuracy and computational cost.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the technical details accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental, building on existing FAB work rather than presenting a radically new approach. However, removing a key restriction on applicability is a valuable step.
