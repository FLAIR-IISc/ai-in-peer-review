

The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), demonstrating that FAB's factorized information criterion (FIC) applies to LFMs despite their failure to satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for prior FAB applications (e.g., mixture models). The key theoretical contribution (Lemma 1) proves that the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs asymptotically yields the same regularization term (∑ₙ zₙₖ) as in mixture models, enabling FAB/LFMs to inherit desirable properties: automatic feature selection (via the −D/(2Nπ̃ₖ) term), parameter identifiability (Theorem 4), and convergence guarantees. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) includes a CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration (Algorithm 2) that reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early. Empirical results show FAB/LFMs outperform IBP (with Gibbs sampling), VB, and MEIBP in model selection accuracy (K), prediction (PLL), and computational efficiency on synthetic and real-world datasets (e.g., block data, EEG, USPS), while achieving smaller model complexity (K) and better generalization (smaller TLL-PLL gap). This work builds on Fujimaki et al. (NIPS 2012, 2013) for FAB in mixtures/HMMs and extends it to LFMs, addressing a gap noted in Griffiths & Ghahramani (NIPS 2005) and Doshi-Velez et al. (AISTATS 2009).

**Quality:** The technical derivation is sound, with rigorous asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) and correct algorithmic implementation. The CCCP interpretation (Section 3) and shrinkage acceleration (Algorithm 2) are well-motivated. The experimental design (Section 4) adequately addresses computational speed, model selection, and prediction, though the MEIBP comparison requires clarification (see Weak Points).

**Clarity:** The methodology (Section 2) is clearly presented, with precise notation and logical flow. However, experimental details need refinement: the rationale for fixing noise variance (0.75 for real data) is not justified, and the VB-IBP comparison should specify whether the infinite variant was used (per Doshi-Velez et al., AISTATS 2009). The block data analysis (Figure 3) would benefit from explicit noise variance settings.

**Originality:** The application of FAB to LFMs is novel, as prior FAB work (Fujimaki et al., NIPS 2012, 2013) only covered mixture models. The theoretical insight (Lemma 1) that LFMs share the same FIC form as mixtures despite violating the block-diagonal Hessian condition is significant. The paper appropriately contrasts with related work (e.g., MAD-Bayes [1] and MEIBP [22]), though MAD-Bayes should be cited in the related work (Section 1.1) as it also uses asymptotic approximations for LFMs.

**Significance:** The work addresses a critical gap in scalable latent feature inference: automating feature selection (K) without priors, while achieving state-of-the-art efficiency (Figure 1). FAB/LFMs enable large-scale applications (e.g., EEG, USPS) where IBP/Gibbs sampling becomes intractable, and the identifiability guarantees (Theorem 4) resolve a known issue in LFM learning. The method’s dual advantage (automatic K selection + speed) is a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**  
- Theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs via Lemma 1 is elegant and well-justified.  
- Practical acceleration via CCCP (Algorithm 2) significantly reduces runtime without sacrificing accuracy.  
- Empirical results demonstrate consistent superiority in K selection, prediction, and efficiency across datasets.  
- Clear connection to prior FAB work (Fujimaki et al.) and LFMs (Griffiths & Ghahramani, IBP).

**Weaknesses:**  
- **MEIBP comparison validity:** MEIBP requires non-negative W (Reed & Ghahramani, ICML 2012), but the synthetic/block data experiments used arbitrary W (not positive), making the MEIBP comparison invalid for these datasets. The authors should either re-run experiments with positive W or remove MEIBP from these comparisons.  
- **Experimental gaps:**  
  - No analysis of small-N behavior (where asymptotic assumptions may fail), despite claims of "automatic selection" (Section 2.2).  
  - Insufficient detail on VB-IBP settings (e.g., restarts, K range), and no comparison to infinite VB-IBP (Doshi-Velez et al., AISTATS 2009).  
  - The 70-feature result for Sonar (Table 1) requires justification (e.g., sensitivity to noise variance or initialization).  
- **Theoretical scope:** The method is limited to binary LFMs (Equation 5), excluding factor analysis/PCA. A brief discussion of broader applicability would strengthen the contribution.  
- **Reference correction:** Line 70 misattributes [21] as Gibbs sampling; it is MAP inference (Reed & Ghahramani, ICML 2012).  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution (extending FAB to LFMs) with compelling empirical validation. The weaknesses are resolvable via minor revisions (e.g., clarifying MEIBP comparison, adding small-N analysis). The significance of automated, scalable LFM inference aligns with NIPS’ focus on impactful, generalizable methods.
