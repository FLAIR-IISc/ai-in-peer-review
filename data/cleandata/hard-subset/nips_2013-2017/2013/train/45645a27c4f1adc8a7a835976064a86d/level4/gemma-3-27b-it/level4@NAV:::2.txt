## Review:

This paper introduces an algorithm and approximation technique designed to enhance model selection efficiency within latent feature models (LFMs). The approach leverages factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference alongside the factorized information criterion (FIC), building upon established techniques commonly applied to mixture models. Specifically, the work extends the FIC – which approximates model log-likelihood through factorization and Laplace approximation – to the LFM context, employing a mean field approximation for latent variable inference and incorporating accelerated shrinkage to determine the optimal number of selected features.

A key strength of the method lies in its automation, minimizing the need for manual parameter tuning. The authors demonstrate substantial gains in computational efficiency through experiments conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets. 

The presented results are supported by a comprehensive analysis, and the method is rigorously evaluated against a diverse set of competing techniques across multiple datasets. The provided references are adequate. 

The paper is well-written and logically organized, contributing to its overall clarity.

This work represents a natural and impactful generalization of existing FAB- and FIC-based model selection methods for LFMs. The combination of several approximation techniques into a single, effective method is a noteworthy accomplishment. 

However, a more detailed discussion regarding the potential limitations of the method would be valuable. Specifically, exploring scenarios where the interplay of approximations (FIC, mean field, shrinkage) might lead to suboptimal performance – and identifying conditions under which the method's assumptions are violated – would strengthen the paper. The authors propose an algorithm/approximation method for efficient model selection for latent feature models by using factorized asymptotic bayesian (FAB) inference and the factorized information criterion (FIC). Results are shown on synthetic and real data, and the gain in computational efficiency is significant.
