This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, which is similar to variational Bayes but with a distinct approach to handling parameters. Instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, the authors approximately integrate out the parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). They demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach appears to be a reasonable extension of existing methods, motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. The main advantage of this method relative to other IBP inference techniques is its computational efficiency, as it corresponds to an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, rather than relying on more expensive sampling or variational Bayes algorithms.

While the technical contributions seem novel, they are essentially an extension of the FAB work presented in [3] and [4], and thus can be considered incremental. However, some parts of the exposition are confusing due to imprecise mathematical formulations. For instance, in equation (3), the log sum of the z values is infinite if all values are zero, which has a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution q. This raises the question of why the FIC score is not always infinite.

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated as" a certain expression, but it lacks clarity on the asymptotic regime and assumptions. Specifically, it is unclear how K is assumed to behave as N increases. Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem seems to require a fixed finite K, implying that the number of activations of every feature would approach infinity. However, under the IBP model, the number of components is infinite, and new components would continue to be observed as the amount of data increases. This assumption of finite K removes one of the primary motivations for using the IBP.

Additionally, there are some minor points that require attention. In section 2, pk(X | z{., k}) should only include the data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset. Furthermore, equation (4) is missing an expectation on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results in Table 1 demonstrate that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores in less time compared to alternative methods. While FAB finishes faster by orders of magnitude in some cases, the stopping criterion is not specified, making it challenging to interpret this result. The choice of when to stop the Gibbs sampler appears arbitrary, particularly in the context of the proposed method.

Although the improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, it is unclear where the difference originates from. The results would be more convincing if there was evidence that the difference is due to the model selection criterion rather than the algorithms getting stuck in worse local optima. The latter issue likely depends on the implementation details, such as initialization strategies. The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components in some datasets suggests that it is a problem with local optima, as it should be exactly computing the likelihood that FAB is approximating.

In conclusion, the proposed approach seems reasonable, but it is somewhat incremental. Some of the mathematical formulations appear imprecise, and while the experimental results show improvements in running time and predictive likelihood, further analysis is necessary to interpret these results accurately.
