

This paper introduces a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, analogous to variational Bayes but differing in that it approximates parameter integration via BIC rather than assuming a factorized posterior. The authors demonstrate superior held-out likelihood scores compared to existing IBP implementations.  

The approach is reasonably motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument, with the primary computational advantage being that it reduces to an EM algorithm with complexity penalties—avoiding the higher cost of sampling or variational Bayes methods.  

The technical contributions appear novel but incremental, essentially extending the FAB framework from [3] and [4].  

Several mathematical ambiguities weaken the presentation. Equation (3) involves a log sum of zero-valued z variables, which would be infinite under any nondegenerate variational distribution q; yet the FIC score is not addressed as infinite in this scenario. Theorem 2 claims an asymptotic marginal likelihood approximation without specifying the asymptotic regime, assumptions, or how K scales with N. Given BIC is applied per component, the theorem implicitly assumes fixed finite K, contradicting the IBP’s core motivation (infinite components with growing data), as new features should continue emerging with larger N.  

Minor issues include: Section 2’s definition of pk(X | z·,k) should restrict to data points assigned to component k, not the full dataset; Equation (4) requires an expectation on the left-hand side.  

Table 1 shows the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihoods faster than alternatives. While FAB runs orders of magnitude quicker in some cases, the results lack interpretability due to unspecified Gibbs sampler stopping criteria, which introduces arbitrariness. The likelihood improvements are significant, but it remains unclear whether they stem from the model selection criterion or inferior local optima in competing algorithms (e.g., Gibbs sampling overfitting by learning excessive components, which should theoretically avoid such issues).  

Overall, the approach is sound but incremental, with some mathematical imprecision. While experimental results indicate gains in runtime and predictive likelihood, insufficient analysis prevents clear attribution of these gains to the proposed criterion versus implementation-dependent local optima.
