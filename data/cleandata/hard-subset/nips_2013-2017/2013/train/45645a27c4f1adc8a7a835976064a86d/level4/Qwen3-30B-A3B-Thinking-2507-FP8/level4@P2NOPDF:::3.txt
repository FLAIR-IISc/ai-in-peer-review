

Summary:  
This paper introduces an EM-based inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation \(x_n\) is represented by a binary vector \(z_n\) of length \(K\), with \(z_{nk}=1\) indicating the presence of the \(k\)-th latent feature. The method learns both the latent features \(z_n\) and the model complexity \(K\) via a shrinkage mechanism grounded in Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference—a recently proposed framework for model selection in mixture models and HMMs. Unlike nonparametric Bayesian approaches (e.g., IBP), FAB avoids explicit priors by incorporating a model selection term into the log marginal likelihood, encouraging parsimony through an upper-bound on \(K\). The algorithm demonstrates efficiency on large datasets, outperforming Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation for IBP in both speed and inference quality.  

Quality:  
The technical approach appears sound, with algorithmic details correctly specified. The dual capability of handling large-scale data and inferring \(K\) is a significant strength.  

Clarity:  
The methodology is clearly presented. However, experimental details require clarification (addressed below).  

Originality:  
While FAB has been applied to mixture models and HMMs, its adaptation to LFMs represents a novel contribution.  

Significance:  
The work addresses a key challenge—efficient LFM inference with automatic model selection—which is highly relevant for high-dimensional data analysis.  

Weak Points:  
1. **Limitations and Failure Cases**: The paper omits discussion of scenarios where the algorithm underperforms (e.g., compared to Gibbs sampling), or potential convergence issues inherent to EM-like procedures.  
2. **Scope Restrictions**: The method applies solely to *binary* LFMs, excluding extensions to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.  
3. **Experimental Rigor**:  
   - The comparison with MEIBP [22] is invalid: MEIBP requires a *non-negative* loading matrix \(W\), but the synthetic and block data experiments used real-valued \(W\). Simulations should have enforced \(W \geq 0\) for a fair comparison.  
   - For block images, the IBP Gibbs sampler should use the known noise standard deviation (not the heuristic 0.75) to ensure fairness.  
   - VB-IBP [2] employed a finite variational approach; the infinite variant should be considered, and experimental settings (e.g., restarts) require full description.  
   - The Gibbs sampler’s reported 70 features on 49-dimensional Sonar data appears implausible (likely due to poor initialization or noise variance specification).  
4. **Asymptotic Validity**: No analysis or experiments address the algorithm’s behavior in small-data regimes where the asymptotic assumptions of FAB may not hold.  
5. **Related Work**: Recent methods like MAD-Bayes [ICML 2013] (which uses small-variance asymptotics for nonparametric LFMs) are not discussed, despite conceptual relevance.  
6. **Reference Correction**: Line 70 misattributes [21] as Gibbs sampling; it actually describes MAP inference for IBP.  

Other Comments:  
- Line 349: The recommended noise standard deviation for real data is 0.25×sample standard deviation (not 0.75), as per [1,22].  
- The paper should acknowledge that FAB’s efficacy relies on asymptotic arguments, necessitating caution in low-data settings.  

Comments after Author Feedback:  
The authors addressed some concerns, but further revisions are required for acceptance:  
1. **Experimental Justification**: Clarify why baselines (e.g., Gibbs) yielded specific results (e.g., fixed \(\alpha\) leading to overestimated \(K\), or suboptimal noise hyperparameters).  
2. **Reference Correction**: Explicitly state that [21] uses MAP, not sampling.  
3. **Literature Coverage**: Include MAD-Bayes [ICML 2013] in the related work to contextualize the FAB approach.  
4. **Methodology Transparency**: Fully describe experimental protocols for all methods (e.g., noise settings, restarts, initialization).  

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and scalable solution for LFM inference with automatic \(K\) selection. Its potential is compelling, but the current experimental evaluation lacks rigor and completeness. Addressing the above points would substantially strengthen the contribution. The method’s ability to handle large datasets while inferring \(K\) is a notable advancement, warranting acceptance *after* the requested revisions.
