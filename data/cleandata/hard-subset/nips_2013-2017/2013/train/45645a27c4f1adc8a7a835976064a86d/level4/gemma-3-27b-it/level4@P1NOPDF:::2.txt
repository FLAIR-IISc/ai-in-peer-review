Summary:
The authors propose an algorithm and approximation method for efficient model selection in latent feature models, leveraging factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the factorized information criterion (FIC). FAB and FIC are well-established techniques for mixture models, where the latter approximates a model’s log likelihood through factorization and Laplace approximation to enable tractable model selection. The key contribution of this work is the generalization of FAB to latent feature models, employing a mean field approximation for latent variable inference and accelerated shrinkage of the global number of selected features. 

A notable feature of the approach is its increased automation in model selection, requiring minimal manual tuning. The authors demonstrate the method’s effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing significant gains in computational efficiency.

Quality:
The presented results include thorough analysis, and the method is evaluated comprehensively against a wide range of competing approaches on multiple datasets. The referencing is sufficient.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well-structured.

Originality/Significance:
This work appears to be a logical and robust generalization of previous research in latent feature model (LFM) selection using FAB inference and FIC. Specifically, the authors effectively combine several approximations into a novel method that performs well in practice. 

It would be valuable to understand the conditions under which these approximations might lead to suboptimal performance – essentially, to identify the limits of the method and how it behaves when "pushed" to its breaking point, given the simultaneous interplay of FIC, mean field approximation, and shrinkage.
