

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), overcoming a key limitation of prior FAB work. While FAB inference—previously applied to mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models—requires the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood to be block diagonal (a condition not satisfied by LFMs), the authors prove via Lemma 1 that LFMs yield the same FIC form as MMs. This hinges on a novel asymptotic analysis showing log det |F^(d)| = ∑_k log(∑_n z_nk)/N + O_p(1), enabling FAB/LFMs to inherit FAB’s desirable properties: automatic feature selection (via regularization independent of priors), parameter identifiability, and a monotonic increase in the FIC lower bound. The paper further introduces a CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration (Algorithm 2) that drastically reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early, and a merge post-processing step to resolve identifiability issues.

The work builds directly on Fujimaki et al.’s FAB framework for MMs [5,6] and addresses a gap in scalable model selection for LFMs. Prior methods like variational Bayes (VB) [3] and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP) [22] require manual tuning of latent dimension K and suffer from high computational costs, particularly for large-scale data. FAB/LFMs eliminate this hyperparameter and achieve better efficiency without sacrificing accuracy, as demonstrated across synthetic and real-world datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS, Libras). Key empirical results include: (1) 3–5× faster convergence than VB (Fig. 1), (2) superior model selection accuracy (e.g., 95% vs. 82% for MEIBP on EEG data), and (3) stronger predictive performance (higher PLL) with reduced overfitting (smaller PLL-TLL gaps than competitors, Table 1). The authors rigorously derive theoretical guarantees (Theorems 2–4) and provide detailed algorithmic steps (Algorithms 1–2), with supplementary materials supporting the proofs.

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality:** The Hessian analysis (Lemma 1) is a significant theoretical advance, demonstrating FIC’s applicability beyond block-diagonal models. This extends FAB’s scope to a broader class of latent variable models.  
- **Significance:** FAB/LFMs offer a practical solution to a critical bottleneck in LFMs (automatic dimensionality selection), with clear computational and accuracy benefits over state-of-the-art methods. The CCCP interpretation for acceleration is both novel and impactful.  
- **Quality:** The derivation is technically sound, with thorough proofs and empirical validation. The comparison to relevant baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) is fair and comprehensive.  
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, with clear notation, logical flow, and effective use of figures (e.g., Fig. 3 showing feature extraction). The supplementary materials adequately address technical details.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The Gaussian noise assumption (Eq. 5) limits applicability to non-Gaussian LFMs, though the authors acknowledge this as a current constraint.  
- The merge post-processing (Section 3) is empirically validated but lacks theoretical guarantees for all datasets; its necessity could be further justified.  
- The comparison to recent IBP variants (e.g., Reed & Ghahramani’s submodular optimization [22]) is thorough, but a brief discussion of how FAB/LFMs might integrate with non-Gaussian extensions would strengthen the scope.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pros:*  
- First FAB extension to LFMs despite non-block-diagonal Hessian, with rigorous theoretical foundation.  
- Practical acceleration mechanism (CCCP-based shrinkage) reduces computational cost without accuracy loss.  
- Demonstrates clear empirical superiority in model selection, prediction, and efficiency across diverse datasets.  
- Aligns with NIPS’ focus on scalable Bayesian inference and model selection.  

*Cons:*  
- Limited to Gaussian LFMs (though a common starting point).  
- Merge post-processing requires additional validation for non-synthetic settings.  

**Overall:** This is a high-quality contribution that advances both theory and practice in Bayesian model selection. The paper’s theoretical insight (Lemma 1) resolves a key limitation of FAB inference, while the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its utility for LFMs. The writing is clear, the methodology is sound, and the significance for scalable Bayesian learning is evident. The minor limitations do not undermine the core contribution. The work is well-positioned for NIPS, addressing a persistent challenge in latent variable modeling with a novel, principled approach. **Recommendation: Accept.**
