Summary:
This paper proposes an inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation xn is represented by a binary vector zn of length K, with znk = 1 indicating the presence of feature k in observation n. The goal is to infer the latent features Z and model parameters, including the effective number of features K. The method builds on Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference previously developed for mixture models and HMMs. It uses an EM-like procedure together with a shrinkage mechanism derived from a factorized information criterion (FIC) to automatically select K while estimating parameters. Unlike nonparametric Bayesian approaches such as the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), FAB does not rely on priors to induce sparsity; instead, it optimizes a tractable lower bound to an asymptotic approximation of the marginal likelihood that includes a feature-wise complexity penalty encouraging shrinkage. On several larger datasets, the FAB approach is reported to outperform Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP-style IBP methods in both runtime and inference quality.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound and the algorithmic details are coherent. The ability to scale to large datasets while jointly inferring K is attractive. However, the paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of limitations and convergence behavior, and from a more careful and transparent experimental methodology.

Clarity:
The methodological exposition is generally clear. Some experimental details, however, are underspecified or potentially inconsistent (see comments below), which makes it difficult to fully assess the empirical claims.

Originality:
The approach leverages the recently introduced FAB framework, extending it from mixture models and HMMs to LFMs. This application to LFMs appears novel.

Significance:
Efficient inference for LFMs with automatic model selection is an important problem. If the empirical advantages hold under fair and properly controlled comparisons, the method could be of broad interest.

Weak Points:
- The paper does not discuss limitations of the proposed algorithm. Are there regimes where it underperforms relative to plain Gibbs sampling or other IBP methods?
- Convergence behavior is not examined in depth, despite the EM-like nature of the procedure. Potential issues (local optima, sensitivity to initialization) should be discussed.
- The approach is restricted to binary LFMs and does not apply to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.
- The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently thorough and appears flawed in places (see below).
- In small-sample regimes where the asymptotics behind FIC may not hold, it is unclear how the method behaves; discussion or targeted experiments would help.
- Some recent work on efficient LFM inference is not discussed (see below).
- The MEIBP baseline [22] assumes non-negative linear Gaussian models (W ≥ 0). The artificial and block-data comparisons do not enforce this, making those MEIBP comparisons invalid. For a fair comparison, synthetic data should be generated with non-negative W.

Other Comments:
- For the block images experiment, since the noise level is known (and supplied to FAB), the IBP Gibbs sampler should also be given the same noise value rather than the 0.75 standard deviation heuristic.
- For VB-IBP, the infinite variational formulation could be used; the paper appears to use a finite-K VB variant. Experimental settings for VB (e.g., number of restarts) should be detailed.
- The accelerated Gibbs sampler reportedly discovers about 70 latent features for the 49-dimensional Sonar data; this seems surprising and likely due to poor initialization and/or mis-specified noise variance.
- Line 70: Reference [21] concerns MAP inference for the IBP, not Gibbs sampling; please correct the text accordingly.
- Please discuss “MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes” (ICML 2013), which develops small-variance asymptotics for nonparametric LFMs and is relevant to efficient inference.

Minor comments:
- Line 349: For real data, [1, 22] recommend setting the noise standard deviation to 0.25 times the empirical standard deviation across all dimensions, not a fixed 0.75.

Comments after the author feedback:
The feedback addresses several questions, but some issues remain and should be fixed before acceptance:
- Clarify and justify all experimental settings for both the proposed method and baselines (e.g., fixed alpha in Gibbs possibly inflating K; noise variance hyperparameters and their impact). Explain anomalies in behavior where applicable.
- Correct the description of [21] as MAP inference rather than sampling.
- Include the MAD-Bayes reference and discuss its relation to your approach.

Overall, the proposed FAB inference for LFMs is interesting, and its ability to scale while inferring K is appealing. However, the empirical evaluation needs to be more carefully designed and reported to substantiate the comparative claims.
