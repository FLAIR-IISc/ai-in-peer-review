Summary
This paper introduces a new model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. Conceptually, it is akin to variational Bayes, but instead of assuming a fully factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximately integrates out the parameters using a BIC-style penalty. The method is implemented as an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties and is empirically shown to improve held-out likelihood relative to several existing IBP implementations.

Strengths
- The approach is reasonable and backed by a plausible asymptotic motivation.
- A key practical advantage is computational: the method reduces to an EM-like procedure with complexity penalties, avoiding the higher cost of sampling-based or full variational Bayes inference.
- The empirical results report improved predictive likelihoods and faster runtimes in several cases.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty appears incremental: the technical ideas extend the FAB framework of [3] and [4] rather than introducing a fundamentally new principle.
- Some mathematical exposition is imprecise or unclear:
  - Equation (3): the log of the sum of z values diverges to −∞ if all z are zero. Since this event has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution q, why doesn’t the FIC score become −∞ almost surely?
  - Theorem 2: the statement “can be asymptotically approximated as” lacks a precise asymptotic regime and assumptions. In particular, what is the behavior of K as N grows? Because the BIC is applied per component, the derivation seems to assume a fixed, finite K so that the number of activations for every feature goes to infinity. Under the IBP prior, by contrast, the number of components is infinite and new components continue to appear with more data. Assuming a finite K appears to undermine one of the main motivations for using the IBP.
- Experimental analysis is hard to interpret:
  - Table 1 shows that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihoods in less time than alternatives. FAB finishes much faster in some cases, but the stopping criteria are unspecified, making the comparison ambiguous. In particular, when to stop the Gibbs sampler seems arbitrary.
  - The source of the predictive likelihood gains is unclear. It would be more convincing to demonstrate that improvements stem from the model selection criterion itself rather than from different tendencies of the algorithms to get stuck in local optima, which are sensitive to implementation details (e.g., initialization). The observation that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets suggests local optima issues, especially since, in principle, it should be targeting the exact likelihood that FAB approximates.

Minor comments
- Section 2: shouldn’t p_k(X | z_{·,k}) involve only the data points assigned to component k rather than the entire dataset?
- Equation (4): the left-hand side appears to be missing an expectation.
- Please correct typos and ensure consistent notation; some definitions and conditioning sets could be clarified.

Overall assessment
The proposed approach is sensible and practically appealing, but the contribution feels somewhat incremental and some of the mathematical details are imprecise. The empirical results are promising in terms of runtime and predictive likelihood, yet additional analysis is needed to attribute the improvements to the proposed model selection criterion rather than to optimization artifacts and stopping rules. Clarifying the asymptotic assumptions (especially the role of K vs. N), tightening the derivations (e.g., Equation (3)), and standardizing experimental protocols (including explicit stopping criteria and ablations to isolate the effect of the criterion) would substantially strengthen the paper.
