

Summary:  
This paper introduces an inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation \(\mathbf{x}_n\) is represented by a \(K\)-dimensional binary vector \(\mathbf{z}_n\) indicating the presence of latent features. The algorithm learns both the latent features \(\mathbf{z}_n\) and the model dimension \(K\) via an expectation-maximization (EM) framework augmented with a shrinkage mechanism. While conceptually related to nonparametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), the method diverges by employing Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference (FAB)—a model selection criterion from mixture models and HMMs—rather than a prior-driven approach. FAB expresses the log marginal likelihood as the expected complete log-likelihood plus a regularization term that encourages feature shrinkage via an upper-bound on \(K\). The authors demonstrate FAB’s superiority over Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation for IBP on large datasets, citing improved runtime and inference quality.  

Quality:  
The technical foundation appears sound, with correct algorithmic details. The ability to handle large-scale data while automatically inferring \(K\) is a notable strength.  

Clarity:  
The methodology is clearly presented. However, experimental details require greater precision (detailed below).  

Originality:  
While FAB was previously applied to mixture models and HMMs, its adaptation to LFMs constitutes a novel contribution.  

Significance:  
The problem addressed—efficient LFM inference with automatic \(K\) selection—is important for scalable latent feature learning.  

Weak Points:  
- **Limitations unaddressed**: No analysis of scenarios where the algorithm might underperform relative to Gibbs sampling (e.g., specific data structures or noise regimes).  
- **Convergence concerns**: The EM-like procedure’s convergence behavior is not discussed, despite potential instability.  
- **Model restriction**: The method applies only to binary LFMs, excluding broader frameworks like factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.  
- **Experimental shortcomings**: Evaluations lack rigor. Key issues include:  
  - The MEIBP baseline [22] requires positive loadings \(W\), yet synthetic data and block experiments use non-positive \(W\), invalidating comparisons. Synthetic data should enforce positive \(W\) for fair benchmarking.  
  - For block images, the IBP Gibbs sampler should use the known noise value (not the heuristic 0.75 standard deviation) to match FAB’s setup.  
  - VB-IBP [2] uses a finite variational approach, but the infinite variant would be more appropriate; experimental parameters (e.g., restarts) are underspecified.  
- **Small-data regime**: The asymptotic FAB theory may not hold for small datasets. The paper lacks analysis or experiments for such cases.  
- **Related work omission**: Recent methods like MAD-Bayes [ICML 2013] for nonparametric LFMs are not discussed, despite methodological relevance.  

Other Comments:  
- The Gibbs sampler’s reported 70 latent features on 49-dimensional Sonar data appears anomalous; this likely stems from poor initialization or mis-specified noise variance.  
- Line 70 mischaracterizes [21] as Gibbs sampling—[21] uses MAP inference for IBP (like [22]). This must be corrected.  
- The reference [1,22] suggests setting noise standard deviation to 0.25×empirical data std-dev (not 0.75), per Line 349.  

Post-Author Feedback:  
The authors addressed some concerns, but critical revisions remain:  
1. **Experimental methodology** requires clearer justification (e.g., why Gibbs used a fixed \(\alpha\) leading to overestimated \(K\), or how noise hyperparameters were set).  
2. **Reference [21]** must be corrected to state it uses MAP inference, not sampling.  
3. **MAD-Bayes [ICML 2013]** must be cited to contextualize the FAB approach.  

Overall, the FAB method for LFMs is promising—its scalability and automatic \(K\) selection are appealing. However, the experimental evaluation requires significant strengthening to validate claims. The paper’s core contribution is interesting, but the current evaluation lacks sufficient rigor to support its conclusions.
