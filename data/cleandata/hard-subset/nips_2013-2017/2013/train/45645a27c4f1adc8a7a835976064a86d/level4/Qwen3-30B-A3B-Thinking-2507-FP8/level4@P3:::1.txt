

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), specifically addressing model selection for binary feature models like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). The authors show that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications (to mixture models), the FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models. This insight enables automatic feature selection through "model-induced regularization" without hyperparameters or priors, while maintaining theoretical guarantees of FAB inference. The authors develop a practical FAB algorithm for LFMs with a novel shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation, and demonstrate parameter identifiability properties with a merge post-processing step.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that builds upon Fujimaki and Morinaga's FAB framework for mixture models [5, 6]. The core insight that LFMs share the same FIC structure as mixture models despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition is novel and well-supported by the asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2). The authors properly contextualize their work against related approaches, including variational Bayes [3], MAP estimation [22], and sampling-based IBP [10]. The connection to CCCP [29] provides theoretical grounding for the proposed acceleration method, and the identifiability results (Theorem 4) address an important theoretical gap in latent feature model learning.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. Methodologically, the FAB/LFM framework achieves automatic model selection (feature dimensionality K) without hand-tuning hyperparameters, which is a significant advantage over existing IBP methods. The experimental results show consistent improvements in predictive likelihood (Table 1) with substantially reduced computational costs (Figure 1), particularly for larger datasets. The shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) effectively reduces computational complexity by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process. The theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability and the merge post-processing step provide valuable insights for practical implementation.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that require attention. The mathematical exposition contains imprecisions that undermine the theoretical contribution. For instance, Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated" but fails to specify the asymptotic regime (e.g., how K behaves as N increases). Under the IBP model, K is infinite, so the finite K assumption in the theoretical analysis contradicts one of the primary motivations for using LFMs. The authors should clarify whether their analysis assumes fixed K or K = O(N^α) for some α < 1. Additionally, Equation (3) contains the problematic term log(∑_n z_nk), which is undefined (infinite) when all z_nk = 0, and the paper fails to address how the FAB framework handles this case under non-degenerate q.

The experimental evaluation could be strengthened. While Table 1 shows FAB's computational advantage, the stopping criterion (δ = 10^{-4}) is not sufficiently justified, and the comparison with Gibbs sampling is potentially misleading since the Gibbs sampler's convergence is highly sensitive to initialization and implementation details. The authors should provide more concrete evidence that the performance improvements are due to the model selection criterion rather than implementation differences. For example, they could compare FAB with the same optimization procedure as other methods (e.g., using the same initialization strategy) to isolate the effect of the FIC-based regularization.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by its incremental nature. While the extension of FAB to LFMs is technically interesting, it builds directly on prior FAB work [5, 6] and does not introduce fundamentally new concepts. The improvements over IBP methods are primarily computational rather than theoretical or methodological. The authors claim that FAB "automatically selects feature dimensionality K" without priors, but this property is shared with other nonparametric Bayesian methods like IBP, which also select K through posterior inference.

Pro: 
- Novel extension of FAB framework to LFMs with theoretical justification
- Demonstrates improved computational efficiency with shrinkage acceleration
- Provides theoretical insights into parameter identifiability and merge post-processing
- Empirical results show consistent predictive performance gains

Con:
- Theoretical analysis contains imprecisions regarding asymptotic behavior and K
- Experimental comparison lacks sufficient detail to isolate the effect of the model selection criterion
- The contribution is incremental rather than transformative
- The paper does not sufficiently address how the method handles the infinite K nature of IBP

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound extension of FAB inference to latent feature models with practical benefits in computational efficiency and model selection. While the theoretical contribution is somewhat incremental, the empirical results are compelling and the method offers a valuable alternative to existing IBP approaches. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the mathematical imprecisions and providing a more thorough experimental comparison that isolates the effect of the FAB model selection criterion. Given the clear empirical advantages and reasonable theoretical foundation, I recommend acceptance, though the authors should address the concerns raised in the review.
