### Summary

This paper introduces a novel inference algorithm for the binary latent feature model (LFM), where each observation is represented by a binary vector indicating the presence or absence of `K` latent features. The primary goal is to infer these feature assignments for all data points while simultaneously learning other model parameters. The authors propose an EM-based algorithm that incorporates a shrinkage mechanism to not only learn the feature assignments but also to automatically infer the number of active latent features, `K`.

While this objective is similar to that of nonparametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), the proposed method does not rely on sparsity-inducing priors. Instead, it employs the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, a model selection criterion recently applied to mixture models and HMMs. FAB approximates the log marginal likelihood with the expected complete log-likelihood plus a penalty term that encourages model parsimony. This allows the algorithm to start with a generous upper bound on `K` and automatically prune unnecessary features during inference. Experimental results on large datasets suggest that this FAB-based approach can outperform several common IBP inference methods (Gibbs sampling, variational inference, MAP estimation) in terms of both runtime and inference quality.

### Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

The technical quality of the paper appears sound, and the algorithmic details are presented correctly. The paper is generally well-written and clear, especially in its description of the methodology. The core contribution—applying the FAB framework to latent feature models—is novel and addresses the significant practical problem of performing scalable inference while determining model complexity. The algorithm's demonstrated ability to handle large datasets is a particularly appealing and important feature.

### Weaknesses and Detailed Comments

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily centered on the experimental evaluation and a lack of discussion regarding the method's limitations. The experimental methodology needs to be improved and better justified before the paper can be accepted.

**1. Flaws in Experimental Comparisons:**

*   **Invalid MEIBP Comparison:** The comparison against MEIBP [22] appears to be fundamentally flawed. The MEIBP algorithm is specifically designed for non-negative linear-Gaussian models where the loading matrix `W` must be positive. The synthetic and block data experiments, which seem to use a real-valued `W`, are therefore not valid use cases for this baseline. For a valid comparison, the synthetic data should have been generated with a non-negative loading matrix.
*   **Unfair Baseline Settings:** The experimental setup for the IBP Gibbs sampler on the block images data is unfair. The proposed FAB algorithm was provided with the true noise variance, whereas the Gibbs sampler used a heuristic value (0.75 standard deviation). For a rigorous comparison, all methods should be given access to the same information or have their hyperparameters tuned with equal care.
*   **Insufficient Details for VB-IBP:** The experimental details for the Variational Bayes IBP (VB-IBP) baseline [2] are insufficient. The paper should clarify whether the finite or infinite variational version was used and specify crucial parameters like the number of restarts, which can significantly impact performance.

**2. Questionable Results and Lack of Analysis:**

*   Some results for the baseline methods are surprising and may indicate issues with their implementation or hyperparameter settings. For example, the discovery of approximately 70 latent features by the accelerated Gibbs sampler on the 49-dimensional Sonar data is highly suspect and likely points to poor initialization or an improperly set noise variance.
*   The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing a more detailed analysis of why the baseline algorithms performed as they did. For instance, did the Gibbs sampler use a fixed alpha hyperparameter that might have led to an overestimation of `K`? A deeper discussion of the hyperparameter sensitivity of all methods is needed.

**3. Missing Discussion of Limitations:**

*   The paper lacks a critical discussion of the proposed algorithm's limitations. Under what conditions might it perform worse than standard Gibbs sampling? What are the potential convergence issues, given its EM-like nature?
*   The FAB framework relies on an asymptotic argument. The paper should discuss or empirically investigate the algorithm's behavior in the small-data regime where this assumption may not hold.
*   The method is restricted to binary latent features, unlike more general models like factor analysis or probabilistic PCA. This limitation should be explicitly stated and discussed.

**4. Necessary Corrections and Missing Literature:**

*   **Incorrect Citation:** The description of reference [21] on line 70 is incorrect. This work performs MAP estimation for the IBP, similar to reference [22]; it is not a Gibbs sampling method. This must be corrected.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper should discuss and compare its work to other recent methods for efficient LFM inference. A notable omission is the small-variance asymptotic approach presented in "MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes" (ICML 2013), which is highly relevant.
*   **Minor Correction:** On line 349, the heuristic for setting the standard deviation for real-data in [1, 22] was suggested to be 0.25 times the standard deviation of the data across all dimensions, not a fixed value of 0.75.

In conclusion, the proposed inference method is interesting, and its scalability is appealing. However, the experimental evaluation is not thorough enough and contains several flaws that undermine the paper's conclusions. These issues must be carefully addressed.
