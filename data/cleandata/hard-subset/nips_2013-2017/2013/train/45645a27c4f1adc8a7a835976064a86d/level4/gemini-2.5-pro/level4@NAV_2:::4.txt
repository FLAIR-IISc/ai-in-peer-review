This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a method for model selection in latent variable models, to the domain of latent feature models (LFMs). The FAB framework, which maximizes a lower bound on a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) that approximates the marginal log-likelihood, has previously shown strong performance for models like mixture models. However, its application was limited to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. The central contribution of this work is to demonstrate, through asymptotic analysis, that the FIC for LFMs takes a form identical to that of mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition. This insight allows the authors to develop a complete FAB algorithm for LFMs (FAB/LFM) that performs automatic selection of the number of latent features. The authors compare their method against state-of-the-art approaches, including Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants, on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims, particularly Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 which form the basis for extending FAB to LFMs, are the key to the paper's contribution and appear correct. These theoretical results are complemented by a thorough empirical evaluation on multiple datasets, which convincingly supports the claims of superior performance in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency compared to established IBP-based methods. The analysis is further strengthened by insightful connections to the CCCP framework, leading to a practical acceleration technique, and a proof of parameter identifiability under the FAB objective.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of first motivating the problem by explaining the limitations of the existing FAB framework and then systematically deriving the FIC for LFMs. The resulting FAB/LFM algorithm is described in sufficient detail.
*   **Originality:** While the work builds upon the existing FAB framework, its extension to LFMs is a novel and non-trivial contribution. The key originality lies in showing how to bypass the restrictive block-diagonal Hessian assumption, which significantly broadens the applicability of this promising inference paradigm. The proposed shrinkage acceleration mechanism and the post-processing merge step based on the identifiability analysis are also original refinements.
*   **Significance:** The work is significant as it presents a highly competitive alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods for learning the structure of LFMs. The ability to perform model selection automatically, without tunable hyperparameters and with lower computational cost than methods like IBP, is a major practical advantage. Researchers and practitioners using LFMs are likely to find this method useful and build upon it. The results suggest that FAB/LFM advances the state of the art in balancing model complexity and predictive accuracy for this class of models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The primary contribution is an extension of an existing framework. Although the technical derivation required to apply FAB to LFMs is novel and overcomes a significant hurdle, the conceptual foundation is inherited from prior work on FAB. This is a minor point, however, as the extension is well-motivated and impactful.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension of the FAB framework. The theoretical contributions are elegant, and the extensive experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed FAB/LFM method over current state-of-the-art alternatives. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field of approximate Bayesian inference and latent variable modeling. I recommend acceptance.
