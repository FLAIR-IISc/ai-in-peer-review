

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant limitation of the original method. FAB, a model selection approach that maximizes a lower bound of the factorized information criterion (FIC), has demonstrated strong performance for mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models (HMMs) by converging to the marginal log-likelihood. However, FAB currently requires the complete likelihood's Hessian to be block diagonal—a condition not satisfied by LFMs. The authors successfully overcome this barrier by deriving a valid lower bound for FIC applicable to LFMs, proving it shares the same representation as FIC for MMs.  

The technical contribution is well-motivated and clearly presented. The derivation steps are thoroughly explained, with sufficient mathematical rigor to support the extension. The authors validate their approach through extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing FAB-LFM against established methods including fast Gibbs sampling for the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), variational Bayes (VB), and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP). Results consistently demonstrate FAB-LFM’s superiority: it achieves higher predictive accuracy while significantly reducing computational cost, particularly in large-scale settings. The empirical evidence is compelling and directly addresses the practical limitations of existing alternatives.  

This work represents a meaningful incremental advance. While the extension of FAB to LFMs is not a paradigm shift, it effectively bridges a critical gap in the applicability of a well-established inference framework. The authors convincingly show that FAB-LFM inherits FAB’s computational efficiency and model-selection advantages while broadening its scope to a major class of probabilistic models (LFMs), which are widely used in applications like collaborative filtering and feature learning. The clarity of the exposition and the robustness of the experimental validation strengthen the paper’s contribution.  

The writing is clear and accessible, with a logical flow from problem statement to derivation to evaluation. The comparison to relevant baselines is comprehensive and fair, and the results are presented with appropriate statistical rigor. The authors avoid overstating claims, instead grounding their conclusions in the empirical evidence. The paper’s focus on both theoretical soundness and practical performance aligns well with NIPS' emphasis on impactful machine learning research.  

While the core idea is incremental, its execution is strong and the results are persuasive. The extension resolves a concrete technical limitation, enabling FAB to serve as a viable alternative to more computationally intensive methods (e.g., Gibbs sampling) in LFM contexts. For the NIPS community, this work offers a practical, efficient tool for model selection in latent feature learning—a domain where scalable and theoretically grounded inference remains highly relevant. The paper’s accessibility ensures it will be useful to both theoretical researchers and practitioners.  

No major flaws were identified. The methodological extension is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the writing is polished. The authors have made a clear case for the value of their approach. The paper is well-suited for acceptance at NIPS, as it delivers a coherent, well-executed contribution that advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian inference for structured models. I recommend acceptance.
