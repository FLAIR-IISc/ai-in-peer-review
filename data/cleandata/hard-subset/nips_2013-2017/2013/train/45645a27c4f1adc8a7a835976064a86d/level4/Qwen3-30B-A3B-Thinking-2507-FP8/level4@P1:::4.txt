

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant theoretical gap in the FAB framework. Previous work on FAB inference [5, 6] has been limited to models satisfying a specific condition requiring the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood to be block diagonal. The authors demonstrate that LFMs do not satisfy this condition, yet through rigorous asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix (Lemma 1), they show that the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models (MMs). This theoretical insight enables the development of FAB/LFMs, which inherit several desirable properties from FAB/MMs, including automatic hidden state selection (through model-induced regularization), parameter identifiability, and a monotonic increase in the lower FIC bound during optimization.

The paper's main contributions are threefold. First, it provides a theoretically sound extension of FAB to LFMs despite the absence of the block-diagonal Hessian condition. Second, it introduces a novel shrinkage acceleration method based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation that significantly reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process. Third, it establishes parameter identifiability for FAB/LFMs, which enables a natural merge post-processing step that further improves model compactness and performance.

The theoretical contributions are well-supported by rigorous proofs (provided in the supplementary materials) and are properly contextualized within the existing literature on nonparametric Bayesian methods and latent feature models. The paper thoroughly relates FAB/LFMs to related work, particularly the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [10, 11], variational Bayes (VB) [3], and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP) [22]. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from MAD-Bayes [1], which relies on a different asymptotic regime (noise variance approaching zero).

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with well-justified claims supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation. The experimental section provides comprehensive comparisons across synthetic and real-world datasets, evaluating computational efficiency, model selection accuracy, and predictive performance. The results consistently demonstrate FAB/LFMs outperforming state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) in terms of model selection accuracy, computational cost, and predictive performance (as measured by log-likelihood on missing data). The authors also provide an insightful analysis of the regularization effects in FAB/LFMs, showing how it mitigates overfitting compared to competing methods.

The paper is clearly written with logical organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is particularly well-explained, and the supplementary materials provide necessary mathematical details. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the experimental setup is well-justified.

In terms of originality, this work makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending the FAB framework to LFMs despite the absence of the required Hessian condition. The paper's key insight—that LFMs can still achieve the same FIC form as MMs through asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix—represents a novel theoretical advance that broadens the applicability of FAB inference. The shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation is also a valuable practical contribution that significantly improves computational efficiency.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it addresses a critical limitation in FAB inference while providing a method that is both theoretically sound and practically efficient. The automatic model selection capability (without hyperparameters) and computational efficiency make FAB/LFMs particularly valuable for large-scale data applications, where current state-of-the-art methods (like IBP and VB) become prohibitively expensive. The parameter identifiability property also provides important theoretical guarantees that are lacking in many existing approaches.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental theoretical question in Bayesian inference with significant practical implications for latent variable modeling. The work advances the state of the art in model selection for latent feature models and provides a framework that could be extended to other model classes.

Strengths:
- Theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs despite the absence of the required Hessian condition
- Rigorous proof of FIC equivalence to mixture models
- Novel shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods
- Clear presentation of both theoretical and practical contributions

Weaknesses:
- The paper assumes a Gaussian noise model, which limits its applicability to non-Gaussian settings
- The identifiability theorem requires certain technical conditions that may not hold in all practical scenarios
- The merge post-processing step, while theoretically sound, could benefit from more detailed analysis of its impact on different types of data

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian inference while providing a practical algorithm with clear advantages over existing methods. The work is well-executed, well-motivated, and makes a substantial contribution to the literature on latent feature models. I believe it would be of great interest to the NIPS community and recommend acceptance.
