### **Review**

**Summary**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). In LFMs, each observation is represented by a set of binary latent features. The central challenge is to infer these features and, crucially, the total number of features (K) from data. The proposed method, FAB/LFM, tackles this by maximizing a tractable lower bound on the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), a model selection criterion that approximates the marginal log-likelihood. This results in an efficient, EM-like algorithm that simultaneously learns model parameters and performs model selection by shrinking the number of active latent features. The authors claim that this approach avoids the high computational cost of traditional nonparametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) while achieving superior performance in model selection, prediction, and speed on several large-scale datasets.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses the important problem of scalable inference and model selection for LFMs. The application of the FAB framework to LFMs is a novel contribution, especially since LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition that simplified the derivation of FAB for previous models like mixture models. The theoretical work showing that the FIC for LFMs nonetheless takes a similar form is a key result.
*   **Scalability:** A major strength of the proposed method is its computational efficiency. By framing the problem as an optimization of a tractable objective, the algorithm can scale to large datasets where sampling-based IBP methods can be prohibitively slow.
*   **Algorithmic Refinements:** The paper includes valuable practical and theoretical enhancements. The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, inspired by a CCCP interpretation of the optimization, appears to significantly reduce computation time. The analysis of parameter identifiability provides theoretical justification for the model's behavior and informs a practical post-processing step for merging redundant features.

**Weaknesses**

The paper is promising, but its claims are weakened by significant issues in the experimental evaluation and a lack of discussion on the method's limitations.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section, while extensive, has several issues that question the validity and fairness of the comparisons.
    *   **Invalid Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with MEIBP [22] seems fundamentally flawed. The MEIBP algorithm is designed for models with non-negative feature loadings (W ≥ 0). However, the artificial and block data experiments appear to use a standard LFM with real-valued W. Applying MEIBP in this setting is inappropriate and makes the resulting comparison invalid. A fair comparison would require generating synthetic data that respects the non-negativity constraint.
    *   **Lack of Detail and Potentially Suboptimal Baselines:** The configuration of the baseline methods is insufficiently described. For the VB-IBP [3], the authors used a finite version and do not specify crucial details like the number of restarts, making the results difficult to reproduce or interpret. Furthermore, the hyperparameters for the IBP Gibbs sampler seem poorly tuned. For example, on the Sonar dataset, the sampler finds an unusually large number of features (K≈70), which may be an artifact of a badly specified noise variance or prior hyperparameter (α). A more careful tuning of baselines is needed to make a convincing case for the superiority of FAB/LFM.
    *   **Unfair Hyperparameter Settings:** In the block data experiment, the true noise value is known and provided to the FAB/LFM algorithm. For a fair comparison, this value should have also been provided to the IBP Gibbs sampler instead of relying on a heuristic value (std-dev = 0.75).

*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper presents the method in a very positive light but fails to discuss its potential limitations.
    *   **Asymptotic Assumptions:** The FAB framework relies on large-sample asymptotic arguments. Its performance in small-data regimes, where these assumptions may not hold, is not investigated. In such cases, fully Bayesian methods that properly account for uncertainty might be expected to perform better. A discussion or experiment on this trade-off is missing.
    *   **Convergence:** As an EM-like algorithm, the method is susceptible to local optima. There is no discussion of this issue or of initialization strategies to mitigate it.
    *   **Model Scope:** The work is confined to binary latent features. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the potential for extending this framework to other models like Factor Analysis or PPCA, which use real-valued latent variables.

*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper omits a highly relevant, concurrent publication: "MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes" (Broderick et al., ICML 2013). This work also develops an efficient inference method for nonparametric LFMs based on asymptotic arguments and should be discussed and contrasted with the proposed FAB framework.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   To strengthen the paper, the experimental evaluation must be revised. Please ensure all baseline comparisons are valid (e.g., use appropriate data for MEIBP) and fair (e.g., provide known noise values to all methods). Provide complete details of the experimental setup for all baselines to ensure reproducibility.
*   Please add a discussion section that addresses the limitations of the proposed method, including its reliance on asymptotic theory, potential convergence issues, and the scope of its applicability.
*   The related work section should be updated to include and discuss the MAD-Bayes paper.
*   There appears to be an incorrect citation or description of prior work. The review process noted a potential mischaracterization of an IBP inference method as sampling-based when it was MAP-based. Please carefully review all citations and their descriptions for accuracy.
*   Minor correction: On Line 349, the paper states the noise standard deviation was set to 0.75 for real data, following [2, 22]. The suggestion in those papers was not to use a fixed value but one proportional to the overall standard deviation of the data (e.g., 0.25 times the std-dev). Please clarify or correct this.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and potentially very useful algorithm for a significant problem. However, the experimental evidence is not yet convincing due to the issues outlined above. If these issues, particularly those concerning the experimental evaluation and discussion of limitations, are thoroughly addressed, the paper could be a strong contribution.
