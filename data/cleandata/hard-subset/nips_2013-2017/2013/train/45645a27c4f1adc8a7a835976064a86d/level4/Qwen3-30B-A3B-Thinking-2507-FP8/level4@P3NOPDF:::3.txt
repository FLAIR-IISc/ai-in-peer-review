

**Review of "Efficient Inference in Binary Latent Feature Models via Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference"**  

**Summary**  
This paper proposes an EM-based inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation \(x_n\) is represented by a binary vector \(z_n\) indicating latent feature presence. The key innovation lies in applying the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) framework—previously used for mixture models and HMMs—to learn both latent features and the number of features \(K\). Unlike nonparametric Bayesian approaches (e.g., IBP), FAB avoids explicit priors by incorporating a model-selection term that encourages feature shrinkage via an upper-bound on \(K\). The method demonstrates superior scalability and inference quality over Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP-based IBP methods on large datasets.  

**Quality & Significance**  
The technical approach is sound, with clear algorithmic details. The ability to handle large-scale data while inferring \(K\) is a notable advantage for practical applications. The significance is high, as efficient inference with automatic \(K\)-selection addresses a key challenge in LFMs.  

**Clarity**  
The methodology is well-explained. However, experimental details require substantial clarification (see Weak Points below).  

**Originality**  
While FAB itself is not new, its application to LFMs represents a novel contribution. The paper correctly situates this as an extension of the FAB framework to a new class of models.  

**Weak Points**  
1. **Missing Limitations Discussion**: The paper omits critical analysis of scenarios where FAB underperforms (e.g., compared to Gibbs sampling in small-data regimes or with specific noise structures). The behavior when asymptotic assumptions fail (e.g., \(n \ll K\)) is unaddressed.  
2. **Convergence Concerns**: As an EM-like procedure, the algorithm’s convergence properties and sensitivity to initialization are not discussed. The observation of 70 latent features on 49-dimensional Sonar data (Section 5.2) suggests potential instability, warranting explanation.  
3. **Scope Limitation**: The method is restricted to binary LFMs and cannot generalize to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA, limiting its applicability.  
4. **Flawed Experimental Evaluation**:  
   - **MEIBP Comparison**: The synthetic and block-data experiments invalidate the MEIBP baseline (reference [22]), as MEIBP requires positive loading matrices \(W\), but the data used real-valued \(W\). The authors must either re-run experiments with positive \(W\) or remove MEIBP.  
   - **IBP Gibbs Fairness**: For block images, the noise standard deviation (\(\sigma = 0.75\)) is used heuristically for Gibbs, while FAB is given the true \(\sigma\). The comparison is unfair; both methods should use the same \(\sigma\).  
   - **VB-IBP Implementation**: The finite variational version of VB-IBP (reference [2]) was used, but the infinite variant would be more appropriate. Details on restarts, hyperparameters, and convergence criteria are missing.  
5. **Omitted Related Work**: The recent MAD-Bayes method (ICML 2013) also leverages small-variance asymptotics for IBP inference. This should be discussed to contextualize FAB’s novelty.  

**Other Comments**  
- Line 70 mischaracterizes reference [21] as Gibbs sampling; it actually describes MAP inference for IBP. This must be corrected.  
- For real data, the suggested noise estimate (0.25 × global feature std. dev; reference [1,22]) was not followed in experiments (e.g., Sonar). Justification is needed.  

**Post-Feedback Revisions Required**  
The authors partially addressed concerns (e.g., clarifying noise variance in Sonar experiments), but critical issues remain:  
- **Experimental Methodology**: The paper must explicitly justify hyperparameter choices (e.g., fixed \(\alpha\) in Gibbs leading to overestimated \(K\)) and detail all experimental settings (restarts, convergence thresholds).  
- **Reference Correction**: Line 70 must be fixed to reflect that [21] uses MAP, not Gibbs.  
- **MAD-Bayes Integration**: The ICML 2013 work should be cited and contrasted with FAB’s approach.  

**Recommendation**  
The paper presents a technically valid and practically useful method for scalable LFM inference with automatic \(K\)-selection. However, the experimental evaluation contains critical flaws that undermine the claimed superiority over baselines, and key limitations are unaddressed. The authors must:  
1. Re-run MEIBP comparisons with positive \(W\) or remove the baseline;  
2. Ensure fair noise parameterization for all methods;  
3. Discuss convergence, small-data behavior, and limitations;  
4. Incorporate MAD-Bayes and correct reference [21].  

Without these revisions, the experimental claims lack rigor. With corrections, this work could make a valuable contribution to scalable probabilistic modeling. As currently written, it is **weakly reject** due to unresolved methodological issues in evaluation.
