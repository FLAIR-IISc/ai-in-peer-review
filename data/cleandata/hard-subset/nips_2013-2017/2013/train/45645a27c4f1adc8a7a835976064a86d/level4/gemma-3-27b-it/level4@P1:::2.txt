This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs). FAB inference, previously applicable to models like mixture models (MMs) satisfying specific Hessian matrix conditions, offers a computationally tractable lower bound of a “factorized information criterion” (FIC) that converges to a marginal log-likelihood in the large sample limit. The authors demonstrate that the FIC for LFMs has a similar form to that of MMs, enabling the application of FAB to this broader class of models. FAB/LFMs offer desirable properties – automatic hidden state selection, parameter identifiability, and improved computational efficiency – and empirically outperform state-of-the-art Indian Buffet processes in model selection, prediction, and speed.

The paper begins with a clear introduction to FAB inference and its limitations, motivating the extension to LFMs. It then presents a theoretical analysis showing the applicability of FIC to LFMs despite the absence of the condition required for MMs. The authors further analyze the FAB E-step, relating it to a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and proposing a shrinkage acceleration method to reduce computational cost. Finally, they demonstrate parameter identifiability and offer a merging post-processing step for latent features.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, demonstrating its equivalence to that of MMs despite differing assumptions, is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, based on the CCCP interpretation, demonstrably improves computational performance, addressing a key limitation of existing LFM methods like IBP.
*   **Automated Model Selection:** The method’s ability to automatically select the dimensionality of latent features, without manual hyperparameter tuning, is a valuable characteristic.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents thorough experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing FAB/LFMs to a range of competing methods, including IBP, VB, and MEIBP.
*   **Identifiability Analysis:** The analysis of parameter identifiability and the proposed merging post-processing step contribute to a more robust and interpretable model.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Approximation Stack:** The method relies on several approximations (FIC, mean field, shrinkage). While each individually is reasonable, the cumulative effect on performance under challenging conditions or when approximations break down isn’t fully explored. Investigating scenarios where these approximations lead to poor performance would strengthen the work.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Although the method aims for automation, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of results to initialization and other implementation details would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Negative Results:** The paper primarily focuses on showcasing the strengths of FAB/LFMs. Including a discussion of cases where the method *doesn’t* perform well, or comparing against stronger baselines on specific datasets, would provide a more balanced assessment.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded extension of FAB inference to LFMs.
*   The proposed method offers significant computational advantages over existing approaches.
*   The comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrates strong performance across various datasets.
*   The analysis of parameter identifiability and the merging post-processing step enhance the method’s robustness and interpretability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on multiple approximations warrants further investigation into potential failure modes.
*   A more in-depth discussion of parameter sensitivity and negative results would provide a more complete picture of the method’s strengths and limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of latent feature modeling. The theoretical analysis is sound, the proposed method is computationally efficient, and the experimental results are compelling. While further investigation into the robustness of the method under various conditions would be beneficial, the overall quality of the work warrants acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper.
