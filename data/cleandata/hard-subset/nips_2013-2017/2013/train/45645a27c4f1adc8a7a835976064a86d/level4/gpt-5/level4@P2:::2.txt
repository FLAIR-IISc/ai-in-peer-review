Summary:
The paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs) by deriving a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs. Although previous FAB results required block-diagonal Hessian structures that LFMs do not satisfy, the authors show via an asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) that the key FIC term log(sum_n z_nk) arises for LFMs as it does for mixture models. Building on this, they propose an efficient FAB algorithm for LFMs that combines: (i) a tractable lower bound of the FIC using mean-field variational distributions over Z, (ii) an E-step update interpretable as a convex–concave procedure (CCCP), and (iii) an accelerated shrinkage scheme that removes irrelevant features early. The method offers automatic feature-number selection, monotonic improvement of the lower bound, and parameter identifiability. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets compare against IBP variants (including fast Gibbs, VB, and MEIBP) and EM, showing substantial improvements in model selection accuracy and competitive or better predictive performance with lower computational cost.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The asymptotic derivations are clearly stated, with proofs deferred to the supplement. The resulting FIC for LFMs mirrors that for mixture models despite the lack of block-diagonal Hessians, which is a nontrivial insight. The optimization maximizes a rigorous lower bound and guarantees monotonic improvement. The CCCP interpretation clarifies the role of the shrinkage term, and the identifiability analysis motivates a merge post-processing step.
- Empirical evaluation: The comparisons span synthetic simulations (including the canonical “blocks” data) and several real datasets. Runtime and model selection metrics are reported, along with predictive log-likelihoods under missingness. The acceleration strategy demonstrably reduces cost without harming outcomes.
- Caveats: The key “mild assumptions” underpinning Lemma 1 are referenced but not summarized in the main text; bringing more of that intuition forward would help readers assess applicability. As the authors note, FIC accuracy may degrade for small N, where nonparametric Bayesian methods can be preferable. The mean-field factorization and linearization used to bound Eq[log sum z] may introduce bias; an ablation quantifying their impact (e.g., with/without acceleration, different Tshrink) would strengthen the case. Some baseline runs were capped by time limits or required manual initialization (e.g., MEIBP), which complicates fairness; clarifying convergence criteria and sensitivity would be useful.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with consistent notation and a helpful algorithm summary. The CCCP viewpoint and shrinkage rationale are easy to follow. A brief, intuitive sketch of why log det|F(d)| yields the log(sum_n z_nk) term, and a concise statement of the main assumptions from the supplement, would improve accessibility. Minor editorial polish (removing duplicated phrases, tightening some long sentences) would further enhance readability.

Originality:
The work provides a thoughtful and technically novel extension of FAB from mixture models to LFMs by establishing that the same FIC structure applies despite different Hessian properties. The CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability-guided merging are also useful refinements. The contribution is positioned clearly relative to IBP, VB, MEIBP, and MAD-Bayes.

Significance:
Automatic model selection for LFMs without hand-tuned hyperparameters and with strong computational efficiency is valuable and broadly relevant. The method appears practically useful for medium to large datasets, with consistent improvements in selected K and predictive performance in many settings. The reliance on asymptotics and mean-field approximations may limit accuracy for small N or highly non-Gaussian settings, but the overall impact remains strong.

Pros:
- Nontrivial theoretical extension: FIC for LFMs with the same key regularizer as in mixture models.
- Practical, automated model selection with monotonic bound optimization and identifiability.
- CCCP interpretation and shrinkage acceleration substantially reduce compute.
- Strong empirical performance across synthetic and real datasets.

Cons and suggestions:
- State and discuss the core assumptions behind Lemma 1 in the main text; add intuition for when they might fail.
- Provide ablations for the acceleration (Tshrink), the merge step, and the effect of the mean-field factorization on accuracy and runtime.
- Clarify fairness and convergence across baselines (e.g., initialization, time caps, noise variance choices), and report sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss regimes where FAB/LFM underperforms (e.g., small N), possibly including a “stress test” to illustrate failure modes.
