Summary:
The paper presents an inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs), where each observation x<sub>n</sub> is represented by a K-dimensional binary vector z<sub>n</sub>, indicating the presence or absence of the k-th latent feature. The algorithm learns the z<sub>n</sub> for all observations, alongside other model parameters, using an EM-based approach with a shrinkage mechanism to infer the number of latent features (K). This approach is motivated by nonparametric Bayesian latent feature models like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), but instead leverages a recently introduced framework – Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) Inference – originally developed for mixture models and Hidden Markov Models. FAB expresses the marginal log-likelihood in terms of the expected complete log-likelihood, coupled with a model selection criterion that encourages shrinkage of K. Experiments on large datasets demonstrate that FAB outperforms other inference methods, including Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation for the IBP, in terms of computational time and inference quality.

Quality: The technical quality appears sound and the algorithmic details seem correct. The algorithm’s ability to handle large datasets and simultaneously learn the number of latent features is particularly appealing.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear in its methodological descriptions. However, certain experimental details require further clarification (discussed below).

Originality: The proposed method builds upon the recently introduced FAB inference framework and applies it to latent feature models, which is a novel contribution.

Significance: The paper addresses an important problem – efficient inference in latent feature models, including the inference of the number of latent features. 

Weak Points:
- The paper lacks a discussion of the algorithm’s limitations. Are there scenarios where the algorithm might perform worse than standard Gibbs sampling?
- There is no discussion of potential convergence difficulties, given the EM-like procedure.
- The algorithm is currently limited to binary latent feature models and cannot be directly applied to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.
- The experimental evaluation could be more thorough. Certain aspects seem flawed (see comments below).
- The behavior of the algorithm in the small-data regime (where the asymptotic argument may not hold) is unclear. A discussion or additional experiments exploring this would be beneficial.
- Some recent work on efficient inference in LFMs is not discussed (see comments below).
- The MEIBP algorithm [22] is applicable only to non-negative linear Gaussian models (where W must be positive, not real-valued). Consequently, the artificial simulation and block data experiments are not directly comparable with MEIBP. If a comparison with MEIBP is maintained, the synthetic dataset should be generated with a positive loading matrix W.

Other Comments:
- For the block image data, to ensure a fair comparison, the IBP Gibbs sampler should be provided with the same noise value used by the FAB algorithm, rather than relying on the 0.75 standard deviation heuristic.
- For VB-IBP [3], an infinite variational version could be employed (the experiments used a finite variational version). The experimental setup for VB lacks sufficient detail (e.g., the number of restarts).
- The Gibbs sampler's discovery of approximately 70 latent features on the 49-dimensional Sonar data is surprising. This may be due to poor initialization or an inadequately specified noise variance.
- Line 70: Reference [21] describes a MAP estimation approach for the IBP, not Gibbs sampling. Please revise the text to reflect this accurately.
- The paper would benefit from a discussion of recent work on efficient inference using small-variance asymptotics for nonparametric LFMs (e.g., “MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes” in ICML 2013).
- Line 349: The suggestion in [1, 22] for setting the standard deviation was to use 0.25 times the standard deviation of the data across all dimensions, not a fixed value of 0.75.

Comments after the author feedback: The authors' response addressed several questions raised in the original review. However, some areas require further attention before the paper can be considered for acceptance. Specifically:
- The experimental methodology (for both the proposed method and the baselines) needs further elaboration, with justifications for the observed behavior of different algorithms (e.g., potential issues with initialization or hyperparameter settings for the Gibbs sampler, or the impact of noise variance choices).
- Reference [21] still incorrectly describes MAP inference as sampling; please correct this.
- The reference to MAD-Bayes inference for the IBP should be included.

The proposed inference method for LFMs is interesting, and its scalability to large datasets alongside its ability to infer K is valuable. However, a more rigorous and carefully executed experimental evaluation is necessary.

Arguments for Acceptance:
* Novel application of the FAB framework to LFMs.
* Good scalability to large datasets.
* Automatic model selection (inferring K).
* Competitive performance compared to existing methods.

Arguments against Acceptance:
* Experimental evaluation lacks thoroughness and contains potential flaws.
* Limited discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and potential convergence issues.
* Restricted to binary latent feature models.
* Lack of engagement with recent related work (e.g., MAD-Bayes).
