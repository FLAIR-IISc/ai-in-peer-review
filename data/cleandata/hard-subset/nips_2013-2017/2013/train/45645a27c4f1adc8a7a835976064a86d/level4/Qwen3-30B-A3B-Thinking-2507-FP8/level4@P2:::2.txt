

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant gap in the application of FAB to models that do not satisfy the standard Hessian condition required for factorized information criterion (FIC) derivation. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite lacking the block-diagonal Hessian structure assumed in previous FAB work for mixture models, yield FICs with identical form to those for mixture models. This theoretical insight enables the development of FAB/LFMs, which achieve automatic model selection (determining latent feature dimensionality) without manual tuning, while maintaining computational efficiency and improved performance over state-of-the-art methods like Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and variational Bayesian approaches.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, through rigorous asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2), the authors establish that LFMs share the same FIC structure as mixture models, despite not satisfying the standard assumptions. This is a significant theoretical advancement that broadens the applicability of FAB inference. Second, the authors develop a practical FAB/LFM algorithm incorporating a mean-field approximation for latent variables and a novel shrinkage acceleration mechanism (Algorithm 2) that significantly reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process. Third, they establish parameter identifiability for FAB/LFMs (Theorem 4), providing theoretical justification for the observed empirical performance and enabling an effective merge post-processing step that further improves model compactness.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing FAB/LFMs against multiple state-of-the-art methods (IBP, variational Bayesian, MEIBP) across synthetic and real-world datasets. The results consistently demonstrate FAB/LFMs' superiority in model selection accuracy, computational efficiency, and predictive performance, particularly for larger datasets. The shrinkage acceleration mechanism (Figure 1) proves especially effective, significantly reducing computational time without compromising model quality. The empirical validation of identifiability and the merge post-processing step (Section 3) provides valuable theoretical and practical insights for latent feature modeling.

The paper excels in quality, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and metrics (model selection accuracy, computational speed, predictive performance). The authors carefully address the trade-offs between different approaches and provide thorough comparisons with relevant prior work. The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-structured arguments, clear notation, and effective use of figures to illustrate key concepts. The writing is precise and accessible to the NIPS audience.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs, a non-trivial generalization that requires novel theoretical insights. While the core FAB framework builds on previous work (Fujimaki & Morinaga, 2012), the application to LFMs represents a significant extension that is not merely a "simple combination" of existing techniques but requires new theoretical results. The authors adequately reference related work, including recent NIPS papers on latent feature models (Griffiths & Ghahramani, 2011; Miller et al., 2009), and clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior methods.

The significance of this work is substantial. By enabling fully automated model selection for LFMs with reduced computational cost, FAB/LFMs address a critical bottleneck in applying latent feature models to large-scale datasets. The method's ability to consistently achieve better model selection accuracy while reducing computational requirements makes it valuable for both researchers and practitioners working with latent feature models. The identifiability results also provide important theoretical grounding for the practical performance observed.

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas for improvement. The authors could provide more detailed analysis of the theoretical limitations of their approach. Specifically, it would be valuable to explore scenarios where the asymptotic assumptions might not hold (e.g., small sample sizes or highly non-Gaussian data) and how the method might behave in such cases. The paper mentions this briefly in the real-world data experiments (Libras case), but a more systematic exploration would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from a clearer discussion of the relationship between the shrinkage effect induced by FAB (independent of prior) and the prior-based regularization. The current explanation in Section 2.2 is somewhat brief, and a more detailed comparison would help readers understand the relative contributions of these two regularization mechanisms.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a theoretically sound contribution to model selection for latent variable models, with strong empirical validation and clear practical implications. The work advances the state of the art in latent feature modeling by providing a principled, efficient, and fully automated approach to model selection.

Strengths:
- Theoretical contribution: Novel extension of FAB to LFMs with rigorous asymptotic analysis
- Practical impact: Significant computational efficiency gains through shrinkage acceleration
- Empirical validation: Comprehensive experiments across multiple datasets and metrics
- Theoretical grounding: Identification of parameter identifiability and post-processing strategy
- Clear presentation: Well-structured, well-written, and accessible

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of theoretical limitations under non-asymptotic conditions
- Brief treatment of the relationship between FAB-induced shrinkage and prior-based regularization
- Some experimental details could be more thoroughly explained (e.g., parameter settings)

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in latent feature modeling. The paper makes a meaningful theoretical extension of FAB inference, provides a practically useful algorithm with significant efficiency gains, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in machine learning. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
