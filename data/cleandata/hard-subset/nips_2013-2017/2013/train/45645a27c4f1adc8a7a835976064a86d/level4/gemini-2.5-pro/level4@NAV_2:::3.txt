### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to latent feature models (LFMs). The core idea is to develop an EM-like algorithm that learns the binary latent features for each data point and simultaneously infers the total number of features, K. This approach is positioned as an alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). Unlike the IBP, which uses priors to encourage model parsimony, the proposed FAB method relies on a factorized information criterion (FIC). This criterion approximates the log marginal likelihood and includes a term that naturally promotes shrinkage of unused latent features. The authors derive this criterion for LFMs, propose an efficient inference algorithm, and demonstrate through experiments on large datasets that their method can outperform standard IBP inference techniques (including Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation) in terms of both computational efficiency and performance.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper's technical foundation appears sound, and the derivation of the FAB algorithm for LFMs is well-presented. The method's ability to scale to large datasets while automatically selecting the number of latent features is a significant and appealing strength.

**Clarity:** The methodological sections of the paper are generally clear and well-organized. However, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail, making it difficult to fully assess the validity and reproducibility of the results. These ambiguities are detailed further below.

**Originality:** The work builds upon the recently established FAB framework, previously applied to mixture models and HMMs. The application and theoretical justification of FAB for latent feature models—which do not meet the block-diagonal Hessian condition of prior work—is a novel and valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The paper tackles the important problem of scalable inference in latent feature models. By providing an efficient method that also determines model complexity, this work presents a practical tool that is likely to be used by and built upon by other researchers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   A novel and non-trivial extension of the promising FAB inference framework to latent feature models.
*   The proposed algorithm is scalable and addresses a key challenge in LFMs: automatically determining the number of features.
*   Empirical results, particularly on large-scale datasets, suggest strong performance compared to established baselines.

**Arguments for Rejection (Weaknesses that need to be addressed):**
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Modes:** The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of the proposed algorithm. For instance, are there scenarios where the asymptotic assumptions fail and methods like Gibbs sampling would be more appropriate? A discussion on convergence properties and sensitivity to local optima, common in EM-like methods, is also absent.
*   **Scope of Applicability:** The method is currently restricted to binary latent feature models and is not applicable to continuous models like factor analysis or probabilistic PCA. This limitation should be acknowledged.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation, while promising, contains several weaknesses.
    *   The comparison with MEIBP [22] seems invalid, as MEIBP is designed for models with non-negativity constraints on the feature loading matrix (W), a condition not met by the synthetic datasets used in the experiments.
    *   The performance in the small-data regime, where the asymptotic justification for FAB may not hold, is not explored.
    *   The paper omits discussion of other relevant and recent work on efficient LFM inference.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to discuss or compare against other asymptotic approaches to LFM inference, such as "MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes" (Broderick et al., ICML 2013), which is a highly relevant piece of prior art.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Baseline Comparisons:** The experimental comparisons require more care to ensure fairness.
    *   In the block images experiment, the IBP Gibbs sampler should be given the true noise value for a fair comparison, as was done for the FAB algorithm, instead of relying on a heuristic.
    *   The setup for the variational IBP baseline is under-specified. The authors should clarify whether a finite or infinite variational approach was used and provide key details such as the number of restarts.
    *   The reported number of latent features (K=70) discovered by accelerated Gibbs sampling on the 49-dimensional Sonar data seems unusually high and may point to sub-optimal initialization or hyperparameter settings for the baseline.

2.  **Correction of Prior Work:** The characterization of reference [22] (Reed & Ghahramani) should be corrected. This work proposes an efficient MAP estimation method, not a sampling-based one.

3.  **Minor Correction:** The paper states that for real data, the noise standard deviation was set to 0.75 following [2, 22]. The suggestion in those papers (e.g., [1, 22]) is typically to set it relative to the data's standard deviation (e.g., 0.25 times the std-dev), not to a fixed value. This should be clarified.

### Concluding Remarks

In summary, this paper introduces an interesting and potentially impactful inference method for latent feature models that is scalable and automates model selection. However, the manuscript in its current form requires substantial revisions to be acceptable. The primary concerns lie with the experimental evaluation, which needs to be made more rigorous, with fairer baseline comparisons and corrected experimental setups. Additionally, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of its limitations and a more complete comparison to related work in the literature. Addressing these issues would solidify the paper's contribution to the field.
