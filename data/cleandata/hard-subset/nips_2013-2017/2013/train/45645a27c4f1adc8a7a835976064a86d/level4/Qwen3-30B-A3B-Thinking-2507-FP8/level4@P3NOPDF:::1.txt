

This paper introduces a model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, framed as a modification of variational Bayes that replaces the factorized posterior with a BIC-based parameter integration. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood over existing IBP implementations, with computational efficiency stemming from an EM-like algorithm incorporating complexity penalties. While the approach is plausible and motivated by asymptotic arguments, the contribution is largely incremental, extending prior FAB work [3,4]. Key concerns undermine the technical validity and interpretability of the results.

The most severe issue lies in the mathematical formulation. Equation (3) defines a log-sum over binary assignments \(z_{nk}\), which becomes \(-\infty\) when all \(z_{nk} = 0\) for a feature \(k\). Crucially, this scenario has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution \(q\), rendering the FIC score undefined (infinite) in practice. The paper fails to address this critical flaw, which invalidates the proposed criterion. Similarly, Theorem 2 claims an asymptotic marginal likelihood approximation but omits the asymptotic regime and assumptions. The proof requires a *fixed finite* \(K\) (number of features), contradicting the IBP’s core motivation: an infinite, data-driven feature set where new components emerge with increasing \(N\). Under the IBP, \(K \rightarrow \infty\) as \(N \rightarrow \infty\), making the finite-\(K\) assumption inconsistent with the model’s intended use. This undermines the theorem’s relevance to the IBP context.

Expository clarity is also compromised. Section 2 incorrectly defines \(p_k(X \mid z_{\cdot,k})\) as the likelihood over the *entire dataset*, whereas it should condition only on data points assigned to component \(k\). Equation (4) lacks an expectation operator on the left-hand side, violating standard notation for variational objectives. These inconsistencies suggest insufficient mathematical rigor.

The experimental results (Table 1) report higher predictive likelihood and faster runtime than alternatives like Gibbs sampling and FAB. However, the runtime comparisons are misleading: FAB’s speed advantage is attributed to an unspecified stopping criterion for Gibbs sampling. Without clear termination rules (e.g., iterations, convergence thresholds), the timing differences cannot be fairly interpreted. More critically, the likelihood improvements remain unexplained. The Gibbs sampler’s tendency to infer excessively large \(K\) (evident in Table 1) suggests it may be trapped in poor local optima—precisely the issue the proposed criterion aims to mitigate. Thus, the observed gains could stem from FAB’s algorithmic stability (avoiding bad optima) rather than the model selection criterion itself. The paper provides no evidence distinguishing these mechanisms (e.g., by fixing initialization or comparing local optima across methods), weakening the causal claim.

While the computational efficiency is a valid practical advantage, the incremental novelty and unresolved theoretical flaws prevent this from being a compelling contribution. The core motivation (infinite \(K\)) is contradicted by the fixed-\(K\) analysis, and the experimental validation lacks the necessary controls to isolate the effect of the proposed criterion. The work would benefit from either: (1) resolving the mathematical inconsistencies (e.g., handling zero-assignment cases), (2) revising the asymptotic analysis to accommodate \(K \rightarrow \infty\), and (3) conducting ablation studies to verify that likelihood gains are due to the criterion, not algorithmic artifacts.

In summary, the paper presents a computationally efficient variant of existing IBP inference but suffers from fundamental mathematical errors, inconsistent theoretical grounding, and unconvincing experimental interpretation. The results cannot be conclusively attributed to the model selection criterion, and the technical contributions fall short of the novelty required for NIPS. I recommend rejection.
