Summary:
The authors propose an efficient algorithm for model selection in latent feature models, leveraging factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the factorized information criterion (FIC). Notably, FAB and FIC are well-established methods for mixture models, where the latter approximates a model's log likelihood through factorization and Laplace approximation, enabling tractable model selection. The primary contribution of this work is the generalization of FAB to latent feature models, which utilizes a mean field approximation for latent inference and accelerates shrinkage of the global number of features selected.

A key feature of this approach is its ability to automate model selection with minimal manual tuning. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their method on both synthetic and real-world data, showcasing significant gains in computational efficiency.

Quality:
The presented results are thorough and well-analyzed, with the method being evaluated against a diverse range of competing approaches on multiple datasets. The references provided are sufficient to support the authors' claims.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and effectively conveys the proposed method and its underlying principles.

Originality/Significance:
This work appears to be a logical extension and solid generalization of previous research in model selection for latent feature models using FAB inference and FIC. The authors' novel combination of several approximations (FAB, FIC, mean field, and shrinkage) yields a method that performs well in practice. However, it would be beneficial to investigate the scenarios in which these approximations may lead to suboptimal performance, essentially "pushing" the method to its limits. Understanding the potential failure modes of this approach would provide valuable insights into its robustness and reliability.
