**Summary**

This paper proposes an efficient approximation method for model selection in latent feature models (LFMs). The approach extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference and the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), techniques previously established for mixture models. The core contribution is the generalization of FAB to the LFM setting. This is achieved by employing a mean-field approximation for inferring the latent variables and an accelerated shrinkage mechanism to prune the global set of features.

A key advantage of the proposed method is its automated nature, which requires minimal manual parameter tuning for model selection. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing significant gains in computational efficiency compared to existing approaches.

**Quality**

The paper presents a thorough empirical analysis. The proposed method is rigorously evaluated against a comprehensive set of competing approaches across multiple datasets. The work is well-situated within the existing literature, and the references are appropriate and sufficient.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and logically structured, making the technical contributions easy to follow.

**Originality and Significance**

The work represents a solid and logical generalization of previous research on model selection, particularly the application of FAB inference and FIC to LFMs. The main novelty lies in the successful integration of several approximation techniques—namely FIC, mean-field inference, and accelerated shrinkage—into a single, effective framework.

While the proposed combination of approximations performs well empirically, the paper could be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of its limitations. Given that multiple approximations are acting in concert, an analysis of scenarios where their interactions might lead to degraded performance would be valuable. For instance, exploring the method's breaking points or identifying specific data characteristics that challenge the underlying assumptions would provide a more complete understanding of its robustness and applicability.
