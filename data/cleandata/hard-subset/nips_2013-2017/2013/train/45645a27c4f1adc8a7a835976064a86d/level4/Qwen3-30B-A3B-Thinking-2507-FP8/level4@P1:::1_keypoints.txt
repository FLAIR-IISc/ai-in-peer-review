- Extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), filling a key applicability gap.  
- Shows LFMs, despite not meeting the standard block-diagonal Hessian condition, yield the same factorized information criterion (FIC) form as mixture models.  
- Lemma 1: proves the log determinant of the Hessian produces the same log(∑ₙ zₙₖ) complexity term as in mixture models.  
- Theorem 2: derives the FIC for LFMs and leads to a FAB algorithm that automatically selects latent feature count via model-induced regularization.  
- Proposes a shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) that eliminates irrelevant features early, significantly reducing computational cost.  
- Theorem 4: provides theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability and guides effective post-processing strategies.  
- Positions work clearly relative to prior FAB mixture work and contrasts FAB with VB, accelerated sampling, and IBP/ME approaches.  
- Technical quality is strong: rigorous proofs in supplement and correct handling of the asymptotic N→∞ regime and FIC conditions.  
- Experimental results: FAB/LFMs achieve higher held-out likelihoods and much lower computational cost than IBP, VB, and MEIBP on synthetic and real data.  
- Model selection behavior: FAB selects more compact models with better generalization (smaller training-to-predictive likelihood gap).  
- Clarity: well-defined notation, precise algorithm descriptions, and a clear CCCP interpretation linking to shrinkage acceleration.  
- Originality: significant and natural extension of prior FAB work—broadens applicability though not revolutionary.  
- Significance: offers an efficient, automatic model-selection method for LFMs, valuable for large-scale applications.  
- Main weakness: asymptotic approximation limitations for small N should be emphasized more in the main text; FAB underperforms nonparametric Bayes for small datasets.  
- Experimental methodology is solid with appropriate baselines and implementation details, supporting that improvements arise from the model/regularization rather than tuning.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound, clear, and significant contribution; reviewer recommends acceptance.
