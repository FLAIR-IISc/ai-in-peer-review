- Introduces a new model-selection criterion for binary latent feature models that approximates parameter integration with BIC rather than a factorized posterior.  
- Demonstrates improved held-out likelihoods compared to existing IBP implementations.  
- Approach is well-motivated by an asymptotic argument and is a sensible research direction.  
- Key practical advantage: computational efficiency—reduces to an EM algorithm with complexity penalties instead of costly sampling/variational methods.  
- Technical novelty is incremental, building directly on prior FAB work ([3], [4]).  
- Exposition lacks precision: equation (3)’s log-sum becomes infinite when all z are zero, which is non-negligible under variational q and is not addressed.  
- Theorem 2 is vague about the asymptotic regime and assumptions—especially how K (number of components) behaves as N grows.  
- Applying BIC component-wise seems to require fixed, finite K, conflicting with IBP’s intent of infinitely many components discovered with more data.  
- Minor technical errors: pk(X | z·,k) should likely consider only data assigned to component k; equation (4) should include an expectation on the left-hand side.  
- Quantitative results (Table 1) show higher predictive likelihoods and faster runtime, but comparisons are undermined by an unspecified/stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler.  
- The source of predictive gains is unclear—could be the model-selection criterion or differences in convergence/local optima influenced by implementation and initialization.  
- Evidence that the Gibbs sampler learns too many components on some datasets suggests possible suboptimal convergence rather than a flaw in FAB’s approximation.  
- Overall: reasonable and practically beneficial approach, but contributions are somewhat incremental, mathematical presentation needs tightening, and experiments need deeper analysis to isolate causes of improvement.
