

This paper introduces a model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, resembling variational Bayes but differing in that it approximately integrates out parameters via the BIC rather than assuming a factorized posterior. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to existing IBP implementations. While the approach is plausible—motivated by a reasonable asymptotic argument—the primary computational advantage lies in its EM-like structure with complexity penalties, avoiding the expense of sampling or variational Bayes methods.  

The technical contributions appear incremental, essentially extending the FAB framework from [3] and [4]. Several exposition issues undermine clarity. In Equation (3), the log-sum-exp of all-zero z values would diverge to infinity. Since any non-degenerate variational distribution q assigns non-zero probability to such z, the FIC score should theoretically be infinite—yet this is unaddressed.  

Theorem 2 claims the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated as" a specific expression, but omits critical details: the asymptotic regime, assumptions, and behavior of K as N grows. Given BIC is applied per component, the theorem implicitly assumes fixed finite K. This contradicts the IBP’s core motivation (infinite components, with new features emerging as data increases), rendering the asymptotic justification inconsistent with the model’s intended use.  

Minor issues persist: In Section 2, $p_k(X \mid z_{\cdot,k})$ should condition only on data points assigned to component $k$, not the full dataset. Equation (4) requires an expectation on the left-hand side.  

Quantitative results (Table 1) show the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihoods faster than alternatives. While FAB converges orders of magnitude quicker in some cases, the lack of a standardized stopping criterion for Gibbs sampling makes runtime comparisons ambiguous—termination appears arbitrary. More critically, the performance gap’s source is unclear. The results could stem from the model selection criterion itself, or from Gibbs sampling getting trapped in poor local optima (evidenced by excessive component counts in some datasets). Since Gibbs theoretically computes the exact marginal likelihood that FAB approximates, the observed differences likely reflect implementation-dependent local minima rather than the criterion’s efficacy. Thus, the claimed improvements lack sufficient justification.  

In summary, the approach is conceptually sound but incremental, with imprecise mathematics and insufficient analysis of experimental results. While runtime and predictive likelihood gains are reported, the evidence does not conclusively attribute these to the proposed criterion rather than algorithmic artifacts. The work makes a modest contribution but requires stronger theoretical and empirical grounding to justify its novelty.
