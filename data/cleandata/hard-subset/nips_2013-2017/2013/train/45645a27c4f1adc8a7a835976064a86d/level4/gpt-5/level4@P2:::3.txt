Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models and HMMs to binary latent feature models (LFMs). The key theoretical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood showing that the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same form as that for mixture models, even though LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition used previously. Building on this, the authors develop an EM-like algorithm that maximizes a tractable lower bound to the FIC with a mean-field approximation on Z. The algorithm includes a shrinkage term that prunes irrelevant features and automatically selects K, and it relates the E-step updates to a convex–concave procedure (CCCP), which motivates a shrinkage-acceleration heuristic. The paper also analyzes identifiability and proposes a merging post-processing step when redundant features are detected. Empirically, the method is compared to IBP-based Gibbs sampling, variational IBP (VB), and MEIBP, reporting advantages in model selection, prediction, and runtime on several datasets.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound, and the derivations are consistent with prior FAB work. The link between the Hessian structure and the FIC penalty for LFMs is interesting, and the CCCP interpretation is insightful. The algorithm is simple to implement (EM-like updates) and the shrinkage mechanism is well-motivated. However, some experimental choices limit the strength of the empirical claims (see below), and the paper would benefit from more discussion of failure modes and convergence behavior, especially in small-sample regimes where asymptotics are weaker.

Clarity:
The methodology is generally clear and well organized. The roles of the shrinkage term and the CCCP-based acceleration are explained, and the identifiability discussion is helpful. That said, several experimental details are underspecified (e.g., VB settings, hyperparameters, restarts), and a few baselines are not evaluated under comparable conditions. There is also a citation/description error for [21].

Originality:
Applying FAB to LFMs is novel and nontrivial, given that LFMs do not meet the earlier Hessian-factorization assumptions. The CCCP perspective and the identifiability-based merge heuristic add useful insights. The work complements nonparametric Bayesian approaches such as the IBP and is related to recent small-variance asymptotics (e.g., MAD-Bayes), which should be discussed explicitly.

Significance:
Automatic feature selection with scalable inference for LFMs is important. If validated with fair and comprehensive experiments, the proposed approach could be a practical alternative to IBP-based methods, especially on large datasets where sampling and VB can be costly. The identifiability analysis and merge post-processing may also be of broader interest.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs, yielding the same FIC structure as in mixture models.
- EM-like algorithm with a principled shrinkage term that automatically prunes features and selects K.
- CCCP interpretation leading to a practical acceleration strategy.
- Identifiability analysis and a justified merge post-processing step.
- Promising empirical results suggesting good speed–accuracy trade-offs.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Limited discussion of when the algorithm may underperform (e.g., small N or severe model mismatch).
- Convergence issues and potential local optima in the EM-like procedure are not discussed.
- The method targets binary LFMs; extensions to real-valued factor analysis/PPCA are not addressed.
- Experimental methodology has fairness issues and missing details (below).
- Some baselines (e.g., MEIBP) are used in settings that violate their assumptions.

Comments on experiments and related work:
- MEIBP applicability: MEIBP assumes nonnegative linear-Gaussian models (W ≥ 0). The synthetic and block-data setups with real-valued W invalidate MEIBP as a baseline there. Either ensure W ≥ 0 in data generation or omit MEIBP from these comparisons.
- Noise variance fairness: For block images, the FAB method uses the known noise. For fairness, the IBP Gibbs sampler should be given the same noise standard deviation rather than a fixed 0.75 heuristic.
- VB-IBP settings: Please clarify whether the infinite variational IBP was considered; if only finite-K VB was used, justify this choice. Report restarts, initialization, priors, and stopping criteria.
- Surprising behavior: Accelerated Gibbs discovering ~70 features on 49-dim Sonar is surprising and likely due to initialization and/or noise variance settings (and possibly α). Please justify hyperparameter choices (e.g., fixed α = 3) and discuss sensitivity.
- Small-sample behavior: Since the method relies on asymptotics, include experiments or discussion for small N to illustrate limitations and practical guidance.
- Related work: Discuss small-variance asymptotics for LFMs (e.g., MAD-Bayes: ICML 2013), which provide efficient MAP-like procedures and regularization terms derived from limiting noise variance.
- Citation correction: [21] performs MAP inference, not Gibbs sampling; please correct the text accordingly.
- Minor: For real data, [1,22] recommend setting the noise std to 0.25 times the empirical std across dimensions, not a fixed 0.75.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated extension of FAB to LFMs with solid theory and practical algorithmic insights.
- Automatic feature selection without priors or hand-tuned hyperparameters.
- Empirical results show competitive or superior accuracy with favorable runtime on large datasets.
- Added value via identifiability analysis and merging heuristic.

Cons for acceptance:
- Experimental methodology needs tightening to ensure fair and valid comparisons (MEIBP setup, noise variance, VB details).
- Limited analysis of convergence behavior and failure cases, particularly for small N.
- Scope limited to binary LFMs; broader applicability is not demonstrated.

Post-rebuttal comments:
The rebuttal addressed some questions, but a few issues should be fixed before acceptance:
- Provide clearer experimental methodology for all methods and justify hyperparameter choices (e.g., α for Gibbs IBP, noise variance).
- Correct the description of [21] as MAP, not sampling.
- Include discussion of MAD-Bayes and relate its asymptotics to FAB.
Overall, the method is interesting, scalable, and potentially impactful, but the empirical evaluation should be made more careful and transparent.
