

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a key gap in prior work where FAB was limited to models satisfying specific Hessian conditions (e.g., mixture models). The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite violating these conditions, yield FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) forms identical to mixtures via a novel asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix (Lemma 1). This enables FAB/LFMs to automate feature selection (K) without priors, achieve parameter identifiability, and outperform state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency on synthetic and real-world datasets. The work builds on Fujimaki et al.'s FAB framework for mixtures/HMMs [5,6] and advances it to a broader class of latent variable models.

**Strengths**  
*Quality:* The technical foundation is sound. The asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) rigorously establishes FIC equivalence between LFMs and mixtures, resolving a key theoretical hurdle. The FAB/LFM algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated, with clear monotonic convergence guarantees and a practical shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) that significantly reduces runtime without sacrificing accuracy (Fig. 1). Empirical results (Section 4) demonstrate consistent advantages in model selection (K estimation), predictive performance (PLL), and computational cost across diverse datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS), with FAB achieving smaller K and reduced overfitting (smaller PLL-TLL gaps).  
*Originality:* The extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and significant. While prior work applied FAB to mixtures/HMMs [5,6], LFMs lack the required Hessian structure, making this application non-trivial. The paper clearly differentiates itself from IBP-based methods [3,10,11,22] by using a *model-selection criterion* (FIC) instead of priors for automatic K-selection.  
*Significance:* The method directly addresses a critical bottleneck in LFMs—scalable model selection for large-scale data. The computational gains (e.g., FAB completes USPS in 3.7 hours vs. MEIBP’s 50+ hours) and superior model compactness (smaller K) make it highly relevant for real-world applications. The identifiability result (Theorem 4) also resolves a key theoretical limitation in LFM learning.  
*Clarity:* The methodology (Sections 2–3) is well-structured, with clear algorithmic steps and insightful CCCP interpretation (Section 3.1). Theoretical results are rigorously supported in the supplement.

**Weaknesses**  
*Clarity/Experimental Rigor:* The MEIBP comparison is invalid for synthetic/block data (Section 4.1) since MEIBP requires non-negative W [22], but experiments generate arbitrary W (e.g., block data in Fig. 3). The authors should either exclude MEIBP from these comparisons or re-run experiments with positive W. Similarly, the noise variance setting (0.75 for real data) lacks justification; FAB uses true noise (σ=1 for synthetics), so IBP should too for fairness.  
*Scope/Limitations:* The paper omits discussion of small-sample regimes (N small), where asymptotic assumptions (Lemma 1, Corollary 3) may fail. The draft review rightly notes that FAB’s performance in such cases (e.g., Libras dataset) is unclear, and the authors should address this.  
*Related Work:* MAD-Bayes [1] (ICML 2013), which uses small-variance asymptotics for LFMs, is not discussed despite its relevance to FAB’s theoretical approach. This weakens the novelty claim.  
*Algorithm Scope:* The method is limited to *binary* LFMs (Eq. 5), excluding standard factor analysis/PCA. The authors should clarify this constraint and its implications.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro:* Novel extension of FAB to LFMs; theoretically grounded; superior scalability and model selection; strong empirical validation on real data.  
*Con:* Invalid MEIBP comparisons; unaddressed small-N limitations; omission of MAD-Bayes; restricted to binary LFMs.  

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a compelling contribution to scalable Bayesian model selection for LFMs. With minor revisions—addressing MEIBP’s positivity constraint (e.g., re-running block data experiments with W ≥ 0), justifying noise variance settings, adding discussion of small-N behavior, and citing MAD-Bayes—the work would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As currently written, the experimental validity concerns slightly outweigh the otherwise high quality, but these are fixable. I recommend *acceptance with minor revisions*.
