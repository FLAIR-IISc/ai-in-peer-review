

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), demonstrating that FAB/LFMs share the same Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) form as Mixture Models despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for FAB in previous work. The authors provide an asymptotic analysis showing that LFMs' FIC has the same form as MMs, enabling FAB inference to be applied to LFMs with desirable properties including automatic hidden state selection and parameter identifiability. The work builds upon the FAB framework introduced in [5,6] for MMs and extends it to LFMs, which have previously been addressed by non-parametric Bayesian (NPB) and variational Bayesian (VB) methods [3,10,16].

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound extension of FAB inference to LFMs with rigorous asymptotic analysis. The authors prove that FIC for LFMs has the same form as for MMs (Theorem 2), and provide empirical evidence of improved model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods. However, some mathematical details are imprecise: Equation (3) contains a log sum of z values that would be infinite if all z values are zero (which has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate q), raising concerns about the FIC score being always infinite. Theorem 2 lacks clarity on the asymptotic regime and how K behaves as N grows, which is critical since the IBP model assumes an infinite number of features with new features being observed as data increases.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly explains the FAB framework and its extension to LFMs. However, the exposition becomes confusing in places due to imprecise mathematics. For instance, in equation (4), there should be an expectation on the left-hand side, and in section 2, pk(X|z·k) should include only data points assigned to component k rather than the entire dataset.

Originality: The extension of FAB inference to LFMs represents a meaningful technical contribution that addresses a gap in the literature. However, the contribution is somewhat incremental as it essentially applies the FAB framework from [5,6] to a new class of models (LFMs) rather than introducing fundamentally new theoretical concepts.

Significance: The proposed FAB/LFMs approach offers significant computational advantages over existing methods by using an EM-like algorithm with complexity penalties rather than expensive sampling or variational inference. The empirical results demonstrate improved computational efficiency and predictive performance, with FAB consistently achieving better predictive likelihoods with lower computational cost. However, the improvements in predictive likelihood could be better justified as being due to the model selection criterion rather than implementation details or local optima issues, as the Gibbs sampler's tendency to learn excessively large numbers of components suggests local optima problems.

Strengths: Rigorous asymptotic analysis demonstrating FIC equivalence between LFMs and MMs; automatic hidden state selection; parameter identifiability; significant computational advantages over state-of-the-art methods; clear experimental results showing improved predictive performance.

Weaknesses: Some mathematical imprecision in key equations; lack of clarity on asymptotic regime and K's behavior as N grows; potential confusion between model selection criterion improvements and local optima issues; the extension is somewhat incremental rather than transformative.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of latent variable models by extending the FAB framework to LFMs, but the mathematical precision could be improved. The experimental results are promising but would be more convincing with a clearer demonstration that the improvements stem from the model selection criterion rather than implementation specifics.
