This paper introduces a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, which is an extension of variational Bayes methods but uses the BIC to approximately integrate out parameters. The authors demonstrate superior held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing IBP implementations.

The proposed approach appears to be a reasonable extension of existing methods, with a plausible asymptotic argument to support it. Its main advantage over other IBP inference methods lies in its computational efficiency, as it corresponds to an EM algorithm with additional complexity penalties, rather than relying on more expensive sampling or variational Bayes algorithms.

While the technical contributions seem novel, they are essentially an incremental extension of the FAB work presented in [3] and [4]. However, some parts of the mathematical exposition are unclear, such as equation (3), where the log sum of z values can be infinite if all values are zero, which has a non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution q. This raises questions about why the FIC score is not always infinite.

Theorem 2's statement that the marginal likelihood can be asymptotically approximated is unclear, as it does not specify the asymptotic regime or assumptions, particularly regarding the behavior of K as N increases. Since the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem appears to require a fixed finite K, which contradicts the IBP model's assumption of an infinite number of components.

Minor issues include the potential need to modify pk(X | z{\cdot, k}) in section 2 to only include data points assigned to mixture component k, and the omission of an expectation on the left-hand side of equation (4).

The quantitative results in Table 1 show that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores in less time compared to alternative methods. However, the lack of a specified stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler makes it difficult to interpret the results, particularly given the significant difference in running times. The improvements in predictive likelihood are substantial, but it is unclear whether these differences are due to the model selection criterion or the algorithms getting stuck in local optima, which may depend on implementation details such as initialization strategies.

Overall, the proposed approach is reasonable but somewhat incremental, with some imprecise mathematical formulations. While the experimental results demonstrate improvements in running time and predictive likelihood, further analysis is needed to interpret these results and understand the source of the differences.
