This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core contribution is the derivation of a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs, which serves as an objective function for model selection. The authors show that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required in previous FAB applications (e.g., for mixture models), the resulting FIC has a similar form. This criterion includes a complexity penalty that encourages sparsity and allows for the automatic selection of the number of latent features. The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is an iterative procedure similar to EM, which the authors show is computationally efficient. The paper demonstrates empirically that their method achieves better predictive performance and model selection accuracy than several state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations, often with significantly lower computational cost.

### Strengths

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary advantage of the proposed method is its computational efficiency. The FAB algorithm is framed as an EM-like optimization procedure with additional regularization terms, which can be much faster than the MCMC sampling or complex variational inference required for traditional IBP-based models. The experimental results in Table 1 strongly support this claim.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments show that FAB/LFM consistently achieves competitive or superior predictive log-likelihood scores compared to several IBP variants on a range of real-world datasets. The observation that FAB/LFM often selects a more compact model (smaller K) while maintaining high predictive accuracy suggests it effectively mitigates overfitting.

3.  **Novel Theoretical Extension:** The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to a more complex model class. The derivation showing that the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs yields a penalty term `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)` (Lemma 1) is a key technical contribution that expands the applicability of FAB inference.

4.  **Practical Refinements:** The paper introduces valuable practical improvements, such as the shrinkage acceleration technique derived from a CCCP interpretation and the post-processing merge step motivated by an identifiability analysis. These additions make the algorithm more robust and efficient.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Clarity of Asymptotic Assumptions:** The theoretical justification for the FIC relies on an asymptotic approximation (Theorem 2), but the nature of this asymptotic regime is not clearly defined. Specifically, how is the number of latent features, K, assumed to behave as the number of data points, N, grows? The per-feature BIC-like penalty `(D/2) * log(sum_n z_nk)` seems to require that the number of data points activating each feature grows to infinity. This implies a fixed, finite K, which seems to contradict one of the primary motivations for using IBP-style models, where the number of features can grow with the data. A more detailed discussion of the assumptions and the limitations of this asymptotic view would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Interpretation of Experimental Comparisons:**
    *   **Runtime:** The comparison of computation times is difficult to interpret definitively. The FAB/LFM algorithm has a clear convergence criterion based on the change in its objective function. However, for MCMC-based methods like the IBP Gibbs sampler, the stopping point is often arbitrary. It is unclear how the runtime for the IBP sampler was determined. A more controlled comparison might involve plotting performance (e.g., PLL) as a function of wall-clock time for all methods.
    *   **Source of Performance Gains:** The results would be more convincing with a deeper analysis of *why* FAB/LFM outperforms the other methods. Is the improvement primarily due to the FIC being a better model selection criterion than the exact Bayesian marginal likelihood (which IBP approximates), or is it because the FAB optimization scheme is more effective at navigating the complex posterior landscape and avoiding poor local optima? The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets could indicate mixing issues or getting trapped in a poor mode, rather than a fundamental flaw in the Bayesian approach itself.

3.  **Incremental Originality:** The work is presented as a direct extension of the previous FAB framework developed by the same authors [5, 6]. While applying this framework to LFMs is a valuable and non-trivial contribution, the core ideas are not entirely new, which limits the paper's originality.

### Minor Comments and Suggestions for Clarification

*   In Equation (3), the term `log(sum_n z_nk)` is undefined if a feature `k` is not used by any data points (i.e., `sum_n z_nk = 0`). Under a variational distribution `q`, this configuration could have a non-zero probability. Please clarify how this potential issue is handled, particularly during optimization where a feature's usage count might temporarily drop to zero.
*   In Section 1.1, when describing the FIC for Mixture Models, the likelihood `p_k(X|z_·k)` seems to be defined over the entire dataset `X`. Should this instead be defined only over the subset of data points assigned to component `k`?
*   There appears to be a minor typo in Equation (4). The left-hand side uses `-sum_k (Dk/2) Eq[log sum_n z_nk]`, but the `Eq[...]` should likely be outside the logarithm to apply Jensen's inequality, i.e., `-sum_k (Dk/2) log(Eq[sum_n z_nk])`. Please verify this expression.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a well-motivated and computationally efficient method for model selection in latent feature models. The theoretical extension of the FAB framework is sound, and the empirical results are impressive, showing clear advantages in speed and predictive accuracy over established IBP methods. However, the paper could be strengthened by providing a more precise discussion of its underlying asymptotic assumptions and a more nuanced analysis of the experimental results to better isolate the source of performance gains. Despite some concerns about the incremental nature of the contribution, the practical utility and strong performance of the proposed method make it a significant contribution to the field.
