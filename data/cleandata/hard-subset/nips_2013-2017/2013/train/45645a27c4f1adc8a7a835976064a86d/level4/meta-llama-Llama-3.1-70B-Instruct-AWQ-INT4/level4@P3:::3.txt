Summary:

The paper proposes an inference algorithm for binary latent feature models (LFMs) based on Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference. The algorithm uses a shrinkage mechanism to learn the latent features and infer the number of latent features (K). The FAB approach is novel in the context of LFMs and has been previously applied to mixture models and Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The algorithm is shown to outperform other inference methods, including Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation methods for the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), on large datasets.

Quality:

The technical quality of the paper appears to be sound, and the algorithmic details seem correct. The ability to handle large datasets and learn the number of latent features is a significant advantage of the proposed method.

Clarity:

The paper is reasonably clear in its methodology, but some experimental details are unclear and require further explanation. For instance, the initialization of the parameters, the choice of hyperparameters, and the convergence criteria for the algorithm could be more explicitly stated.

Originality:

The application of FAB inference to LFMs is novel, and the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of latent feature models. However, the FAB framework itself has been previously proposed for other models.

Significance:

The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning, namely, efficient inference in latent feature models while also inferring the number of latent features. The proposed algorithm has the potential to be widely applicable and could have a significant impact on the field.

Weak Points:

1. Limitations of the proposed algorithm: The paper does not discuss the limitations of the proposed algorithm, such as cases where it might perform worse than vanilla Gibbs sampling.
2. Convergence difficulties: The paper does not discuss the possible difficulties in convergence, given that it is an EM-like procedure.
3. Limited to binary latent feature models: The algorithm is limited to binary latent feature models and cannot be applied to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.
4. Experimental evaluation: The experimental evaluation is not thorough enough, and some experiments seem flawed (e.g., the MEIBP algorithm is not applicable to the synthetic dataset).
5. Small-data regime: The paper does not discuss how the algorithm will behave in the small-data regime, where the asymptotic argument does not hold.
6. Recent works on efficient inference in LFMs: The paper does not discuss recent works on efficient inference in LFMs, such as MAD-Bayes inference for the IBP.

Other Comments:

1. The MEIBP algorithm proposed in [22] is applicable only to non-negative linear Gaussian models, and the synthetic dataset should have been generated accordingly.
2. For block images data, the IBP Gibbs sampler should have been given the same noise value as the FAB algorithm for fairness.
3. The experimental settings for VB-IBP are not described in enough detail, and the infinite variational version could have been used.
4. The accelerated Gibbs sampling discovered an unusually large number of latent features on the Sonar data, which may be due to bad initialization or a badly specified noise variance value.
5. The reference [21] is about MAP estimation, not Gibbs sampling, and should be corrected.

Minor Comments:

1. Line 349: The suggestion in [1,22] was not to set the standard deviation equal to 0.75 but to set it equal to 0.25 times the standard deviation of examples across all dimensions.

After Author-Feedback:

The feedback answered some of the questions, but there are still a few things that need to be fixed or improved before the paper can be accepted. Specifically:

1. Experimental methodology: The experimental methodology for the proposed method and the baseline needs to be better explained, and there should be justifications/explanations about why certain algorithms behaved in a certain way.
2. Reference [21]: The reference [21] does MAP inference, not sampling, and should be corrected.
3. MAD-Bayes inference: The reference on MAD-Bayes inference for the IBP should be included.

The proposed inference method for LFMs is interesting, and the fact that it scales to large datasets (in addition to inferring K) is appealing. However, the experimental evaluation should have been more carefully done.
