This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. It extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to these models, offering an alternative to methods like variational Bayes and Indian Buffet Processes (IBP). The core idea is to approximate the marginal likelihood using a criterion inspired by the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), enabling efficient model selection and parameter estimation. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing IBP implementations.

The proposed approach appears well-motivated by an asymptotic argument and offers a compelling trade-off between computational efficiency and model selection accuracy. Relative to other IBP inference techniques, it leverages the structure of an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, augmented with complexity penalties, rather than relying on computationally demanding sampling or variational Bayes methods.

The technical contributions are novel, representing an extension of the FAB work presented in [5, 6], and successfully apply this framework to latent feature models. However, the novelty is perhaps incremental, building directly upon established concepts. 

Some aspects of the exposition require clarification. In equation (3), the log sum of the *z* values could become infinite if all values are zero, which, under a nondegenerate variational distribution *q*, has non-zero probability. This raises a question about the robustness of the FIC score. Furthermore, Theorem 2 states a marginal likelihood approximation, but the specific asymptotic regime and underlying assumptions are not fully articulated. Specifically, the behavior of *K* (the number of latent features) as *N* (the sample size) grows larger is unclear. Since the BIC-like criterion is applied to each component individually, the theorem seems to implicitly assume a fixed, finite *K*. However, the strength of IBP-based models lies in their infinite dimensionality; assuming a finite *K* potentially undermines a key motivation for employing the IBP.

Several minor points could enhance clarity. In section 2, the expression *p<sub>k</sub>(X | z<sub>⋅, k</sub>)* should arguably only incorporate the data points assigned to mixture component *k*, rather than the entire dataset *X*. Additionally, in equation (4), an expectation should be explicitly present on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results in Table 1 demonstrate that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores with reduced computational time compared to alternative methods. While FAB exhibits significantly faster convergence in certain cases, interpreting these results requires caution as the stopping criterion is not explicitly defined. The termination of the Gibbs sampler, in particular, appears somewhat arbitrary.

The observed improvements in predictive likelihood are encouraging, but further analysis is needed to pinpoint their origin. It would be beneficial to determine whether the gains stem from the model selection criterion itself or from differences in the algorithms’ susceptibility to local optima. The fact that the Gibbs sampler frequently converges to solutions with an excessively large number of components in some datasets suggests a potential issue with local optima, especially given its theoretical ability to compute the exact likelihood that FAB approximates.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of FAB inference to latent feature models.
*   Improved computational efficiency compared to existing IBP methods.
*   Demonstrated improvements in predictive likelihood on benchmark datasets.
*   Clear and logical presentation of the theoretical framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Technical contributions are incremental.
*   Some mathematical details require further clarification (e.g., potential for infinite FIC scores).
*   Asymptotic assumptions require more precise definition.
*   Limited analysis to isolate the source of performance improvements (model selection vs. local optima).



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of latent feature modeling. While the novelty is incremental and some aspects of the exposition could be refined, the method demonstrates promising performance and offers a valuable alternative to existing approaches. Addressing the concerns outlined above would strengthen the paper significantly. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
