

This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, resembling variational Bayes but approximating parameter integration via BIC instead of assuming a factorized posterior. The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to existing IBP implementations. The approach is reasonably motivated by an asymptotic argument and offers computational advantages over sampling or variational Bayes methods, as it corresponds to an EM algorithm with complexity penalties.  

The technical contributions appear novel but incremental, essentially extending the FAB work of [3] and [4]. Several mathematical concerns require clarification:  
- Equation (3) computes the log-sum-exp of the \(z\) values; if all \(z_{ik}\) are zero (which has nonzero probability under any nondegenerate \(q\)), this expression becomes infinite. Why is the FIC score not always infinite?  
- Theorem 2 claims the marginal likelihood "can be asymptotically approximated as" a specific form but omits the asymptotic regime, key assumptions, and the behavior of \(K\) as \(N \to \infty\). Given the IBP's infinite-component nature, assuming finite \(K\) contradicts the model's motivation, as new components would continue emerging with more data.  

Minor exposition issues include:  
- In Section 2, \(p_k(X \mid z_{\cdot,k})\) should refer only to data points assigned to component \(k\), not the entire dataset.  
- Equation (4) requires an expectation over the variational distribution on the left-hand side.  

The quantitative results in Table 1 show higher predictive likelihood and faster runtime than alternatives. However, the significant speed advantage of FAB (orders of magnitude in some cases) lacks interpretability due to unspecified stopping criteria for the Gibbs sampler, making the runtime comparison arbitrary. Crucially, the predictive likelihood improvements are not convincingly attributed to the model selection criterion itself. The Gibbs sampler's tendency to learn excessively large component counts in some datasets suggests results may stem from local optima (e.g., due to initialization) rather than the criterion, as the sampler should theoretically compute the exact marginal likelihood that FAB approximates.  

While the approach is conceptually sound, the incremental novelty, mathematical imprecision, and insufficient analysis of experimental results undermine the claims. The improvements in likelihood and speed are not sufficiently justified as direct consequences of the proposed criterion.
