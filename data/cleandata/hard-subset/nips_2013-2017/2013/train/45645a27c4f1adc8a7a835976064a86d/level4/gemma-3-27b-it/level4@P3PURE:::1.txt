This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models, presenting an alternative to variational Bayes. Instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, the authors approximately integrate out the parameters using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). They demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach is well-motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument and appears reasonable. A key advantage over other IBP inference methods is its computational efficiency; it corresponds to an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with added complexity penalties, rather than the more computationally intensive sampling or variational Bayes techniques. While the technical contributions appear novel, they are incremental, representing an extension of the Factor Analysis with Bayesian (FAB) work presented in [3] and [4].

Several aspects of the exposition require clarification, as the mathematical notation lacks precision. For example, in equation (3), the log-sum term involving the z values becomes infinite if all z values are zero. Given that this scenario has non-zero probability under any nondegenerate variational distribution q, the authors should explain why the proposed Free Information Criterion (FIC) score does not consistently evaluate to infinity. 

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood can be asymptotically approximated, but the specific asymptotic regime and underlying assumptions are not clearly defined. Critically, the behavior of K (the number of components) as N (the dataset size) grows larger needs to be specified. The application of the BIC to each component individually suggests a fixed, finite K, implying that the number of activations for each feature approaches infinity. This contradicts the core premise of the IBP model, which allows for an infinite number of components and continued discovery of new features as data increases. Assuming finite K therefore diminishes one of the primary motivations for employing the IBP.

Minor points include: in section 2, the term pk(X | z{\cdot, k}) should arguably consider only the data points assigned to mixture component k, rather than the entire dataset. Furthermore, an expectation symbol should be present on the left-hand side of equation (4). 

The quantitative results in Table 1 indicate that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores with reduced computation time. While FAB completes significantly faster in some instances, interpreting this result is challenging due to the unspecified stopping criterion. The decision to halt the Gibbs sampler appears arbitrary, and the excessively large number of components learned in some datasets suggests a potential issue with local optima, as the Gibbs sampler should, in theory, precisely compute the likelihood that FAB approximates. 

The improvements in predictive likelihood are noteworthy, but the source of these gains remains unclear. The results would be more compelling with evidence demonstrating that the improvements are attributable to the model selection criterion itself, rather than differences in how effectively each algorithm avoids suboptimal local optima – a factor likely influenced by implementation details such as initialization strategies. Overall, the proposed approach is sensible but represents an incremental advancement. Some mathematical expressions require greater precision, and the experimental results, while promising, require further analysis to confidently attribute observed performance differences to the proposed method.
