This paper proposes a novel model selection criterion for binary latent feature models. The approach resembles variational Bayes, but instead of assuming a factorized posterior over latent variables and parameters, it approximates parameter integration using the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). The authors demonstrate improved held-out likelihood scores compared to several existing Indian Buffet Process (IBP) implementations.

The proposed approach appears reasonable and is motivated by a plausible asymptotic argument. A key advantage over other IBP inference methods is its computational efficiency: it corresponds to an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with added complexity penalties, rather than relying on more computationally expensive sampling or variational Bayes techniques. 

The technical contributions seem novel, though incremental, representing an extension of the Factor Analysis with Bayesian (FAB) work in [3] and [4]. 

Some parts of the exposition were unclear due to imprecision in the mathematical formulation. Specifically, in equation (3), the log-sum of the *z* values becomes infinite if all values are zero. Given that this scenario has non-zero probability under any non-degenerate variational distribution *q*, why doesn't the proposed Free Information Criterion (FIC) score consistently yield infinite values? 

Theorem 2 states that the marginal likelihood “can be asymptotically approximated as” a certain form, but the asymptotic regime and underlying assumptions are not clearly defined. In particular, the behavior of *K* as *N* increases is not specified. Because the BIC is applied to each component individually, the theorem appears to require a fixed, finite *K*, implying that the number of activations for each feature approaches infinity. However, the IBP model inherently assumes an infinite number of components, with new components potentially observed as the amount of data grows. Constraining *K* to be finite therefore undermines a key motivation for employing the IBP.

Regarding minor points, in section 2, *p<sub>k</sub>(X | z<sub>•,k</sub>)* should arguably include only the data points assigned to mixture component *k*, rather than the entire dataset. Furthermore, equation (4) should include an expectation on the left-hand side.

The quantitative results in Table 1 demonstrate that the proposed method achieves higher predictive likelihood scores with faster runtimes compared to alternative methods. While FAB completes significantly faster in some cases, this result is difficult to interpret without a clearly defined stopping criterion. The chosen stopping point for the Gibbs sampler seems arbitrary.

The improvements in predictive likelihood are significant, but the source of these gains remains unclear. The results would be more convincing if evidence indicated that the improvements stem from the model selection criterion itself, rather than differences in how the algorithms converge to local optima – which likely depend more on implementation details such as initialization strategies. (The observation that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessively large number of components on some datasets suggests a problem with local optima, as it should, in theory, accurately compute the likelihood that FAB is approximating.)

In summary, the proposed approach appears sound but is somewhat incremental. The mathematical presentation could be more precise. The experimental results show improvements in both runtime and predictive likelihood, but further analysis is needed to confidently interpret these gains.
