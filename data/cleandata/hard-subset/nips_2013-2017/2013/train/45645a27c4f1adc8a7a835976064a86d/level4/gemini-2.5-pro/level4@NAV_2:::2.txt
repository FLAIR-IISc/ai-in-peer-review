This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a method for model selection, from its established application in mixture models to the domain of Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The primary contribution is the derivation of a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs. This is a non-trivial extension, as LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition previously thought necessary for this framework. The authors demonstrate that the resulting model complexity term for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models. The proposed algorithm, FAB/LFM, combines this asymptotic approximation with a mean-field variational approach for inference and a novel shrinkage acceleration method to efficiently and automatically determine the number of latent features. The method is positioned as a computationally efficient alternative to non-parametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by derivations (notably Lemma 1 and Theorems 2 and 4) and a comprehensive set of experiments. The empirical evaluation is robust, comparing the proposed FAB/LFM method against several relevant baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) across multiple synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating strong performance in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.
*   **Originality:** The work represents a novel and logical generalization of the FAB framework. The key insight that allows the extension of FIC to LFMs is original and significant. Furthermore, the proposed shrinkage acceleration scheme, inspired by a CCCP interpretation, and the merge post-processing step, guided by an identifiability analysis, are valuable and novel additions that improve the practical utility of the algorithm.
*   **Significance:** The proposed method offers a promising, highly automated, and computationally efficient alternative to established non-parametric Bayesian methods for discovering the latent dimensionality in LFMs. Given the high computational cost of methods like IBP, this work could be impactful for researchers and practitioners working with large-scale data, advancing the state-of-the-art in scalable Bayesian inference for this model class.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively explain the theoretical motivation, detail the derivation of the FIC for LFMs, and present the resulting algorithm in a logical sequence that is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary weakness is the limited discussion of the method's potential failure modes. The algorithm relies on a combination of several approximations: the asymptotic nature of the FIC, the mean-field assumption for the variational posterior, and the iterative linearization in the acceleration step. A discussion of scenarios where these combined approximations might lead to poor performance would strengthen the paper. For instance, while the experiments are comprehensive, an analysis that explicitly tests the limits of the method (e.g., in small-N regimes where the asymptotic approximation is less accurate, as hinted at in the Libras dataset results) would provide a more complete picture of its applicability and limitations.
