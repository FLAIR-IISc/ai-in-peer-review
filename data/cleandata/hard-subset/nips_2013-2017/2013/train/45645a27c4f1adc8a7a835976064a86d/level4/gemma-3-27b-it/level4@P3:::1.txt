This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a limitation of the original FAB framework that required a specific condition on the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that LFMs satisfy a similar condition, allowing for the application of FAB. They also present theoretical results on parameter identifiability and propose a shrinkage acceleration method, and empirically show improved performance compared to state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods in terms of model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.

This work builds upon the recent FAB framework [5, 6] and extends its applicability to a broader class of models. The core idea of approximating the marginal log-likelihood via a computationally tractable criterion is well-motivated and aligns with a broader trend in Bayesian inference towards efficient, deterministic approximations. The extension to LFMs is a logical next step, given the limitations of existing IBP inference methods for large-scale datasets. The proposed shrinkage acceleration method and analysis of parameter identifiability are valuable additions. The connection to the convex-concave procedure (CCCP) [29] provides a useful perspective on the optimization process. 

The paper's strengths lie in its clear motivation, theoretical grounding, and empirical validation. The theoretical analysis, while potentially dense, attempts to justify the applicability of FAB to LFMs. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in terms of both computational efficiency and predictive performance. The comparison to several relevant baselines, including IBP and variational Bayes methods, is thorough. The addition of the acceleration method and analysis of identifiability further strengthen the contribution.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The exposition occasionally lacks precision, leading to confusion. For example, the discussion around equation (3) and the potential for infinite values requires clarification. The asymptotic regime underlying Theorem 2 is not explicitly stated, and the assumptions regarding the behavior of *K* as *N* grows larger need to be clearly defined. The relationship between the derived FIC and the original motivations for using an IBP (i.e., allowing for an infinite number of components) is not fully explored. 

Specific concerns regarding the mathematical notation and presentation include: a potential error in equation (4) (missing expectation), and a question regarding the scope of the summation in *pk(X | z{\cdot, k})* in section 2. Additionally, the empirical evaluation could be strengthened. The stopping criterion for the Gibbs sampler is not clearly specified, making the comparison to FAB’s runtime less convincing. While improvements in predictive likelihood are reported, further analysis is needed to attribute these gains specifically to the model selection criterion versus differences in optimization convergence (e.g., getting stuck in local optima). The fact that the Gibbs sampler learns an excessive number of components in some datasets raises concerns about convergence and implementation details.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of the FAB framework to LFMs, expanding its applicability.
*   Theoretical justification for applying FAB to LFMs, including identifiability analysis.
*   Empirically demonstrated improvements in computational efficiency and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art IBP methods.
*   The proposed shrinkage acceleration method significantly reduces computational cost.
*   The work addresses a practical limitation of existing IBP inference techniques.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The exposition is occasionally imprecise, hindering clarity.
*   The asymptotic assumptions and the relationship to the IBP’s infinite component setting are not fully clarified.
*   The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient analysis to definitively attribute performance gains to the model selection criterion.
*   The technical contribution, while valid, is somewhat incremental.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian non-parametric modeling. While the paper is not without its weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them. The theoretical grounding, empirical validation, and practical improvements offered by the proposed method are significant. I believe that with minor revisions to clarify the exposition and strengthen the empirical analysis, this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should: 1) Clearly state the assumptions underlying Theorem 2, particularly concerning the behavior of K as N increases. 2) Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations and applicability of the derived FIC in the context of infinite models like the IBP. 3) Elaborate on the stopping criterion used for the Gibbs sampler and provide a more thorough analysis of the experimental results to disentangle the effects of model selection from optimization convergence. 4) Clarify the mathematical notation where ambiguity exists (e.g., expectation in Eq. 4, scope of summation in Section 2).
