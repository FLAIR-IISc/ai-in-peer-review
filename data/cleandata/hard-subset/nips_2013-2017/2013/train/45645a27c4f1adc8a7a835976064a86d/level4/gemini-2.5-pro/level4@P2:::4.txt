This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a model selection framework that operates by maximizing a lower bound on the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. Prior work has demonstrated its effectiveness for models like Mixture Models (MMs) and Hidden Markov Models. However, the applicability of FAB has been restricted to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal.

The central contribution of this work is to overcome this limitation for LFMs. Through an asymptotic analysis of the LFM Hessian matrix (Lemma 1), the authors show that the resulting FIC takes on the same functional form as that for MMs, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal condition. This theoretical insight allows them to derive the FAB/LFM algorithm, which inherits desirable properties from other FAB methods, such as automatic selection of the number of latent features and parameter identifiability. The authors further enhance the algorithm by introducing a shrinkage acceleration method inspired by a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation. The method is evaluated against state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating strong performance in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of the paper is the successful extension of the FAB framework to a class of models (LFMs) that do not meet its original structural assumptions. The derivation showing that the log-determinant of the LFM Hessian yields the same penalty term as in MMs is elegant and non-trivial. This finding suggests that the FIC may be more broadly applicable than previously thought, which is an important insight for the community.

2.  **Practical and Well-Motivated Algorithm:** The paper translates the theoretical results into a practical algorithm. The analysis connecting the FAB E-step to CCCP is insightful and leads directly to a novel acceleration technique that demonstrably reduces computation time. Furthermore, the theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability provides a principled justification for the proposed feature-merging post-processing step.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare FAB/LFM against relevant and strong baselines, including fast Gibbs sampling for IBP, Variational Bayes (VB) for IBP, and the more recent MEIBP. The results consistently show that FAB/LFM is competitive or superior in terms of model selection accuracy (estimating the correct number of features), predictive performance (higher log-likelihood on held-out data), and computational speed.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining the limitations of prior work, detailing their theoretical derivations, and presenting the final algorithm and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The method's theoretical guarantees are asymptotic. As the authors honestly acknowledge in the conclusion of the experimental section, the FIC approximation may be less accurate in small-sample (small N) regimes. While the method performs well across several datasets, it would be beneficial to include a more direct discussion or characterization of its limitations when the asymptotic assumptions are not well met.

2.  **Incremental Aspect:** While the technical contribution is significant, the work can be viewed as an extension of an existing framework (FAB) to a new model class. However, this is a minor point, as the extension required overcoming a fundamental theoretical barrier, making the contribution more substantial than a straightforward application.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a novel and non-trivial theoretical extension of the FAB inference framework, broadening its applicability.
*   The resulting FAB/LFM algorithm is practical, efficient (especially with the proposed acceleration), and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance.
*   The paper is technically sound, well-written, and supported by a strong set of experiments.
*   The work addresses the important and challenging problem of model selection in latent feature models, which is of broad interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The method's performance relies on asymptotic assumptions, and its behavior in small-data scenarios is a potential limitation.
*   The work builds upon a pre-existing framework, which could be perceived as incremental (though this is a weak argument given the technical depth).

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. The authors successfully tackle a key limitation of the promising FAB framework, extending it to the widely used class of latent feature models. The theoretical work is sound, the resulting algorithm is practical and efficient, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper is well-written and will be of significant interest to researchers working on latent variable modeling, Bayesian nonparametrics, and approximate inference. I strongly support its acceptance.
