This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), building on prior work that successfully applied FAB – and its associated factorized information criterion (FIC) – to mixture models. FAB offers a computationally tractable approach to model selection by maximizing a lower bound on an approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. The key contribution lies in adapting this framework to LFMs, a setting where previous applicability was limited by restrictions on the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that FICs for LFMs exhibit a similar form to those of mixture models, enabling the extension. They further introduce a shrinkage acceleration method to reduce computational cost and analyze parameter identifiability, providing a foundation for merging post-processing of latent features. Empirical evaluation demonstrates improved computational efficiency and competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods like Indian Buffet Processes.

The paper is technically sound, presenting a clear derivation of the FIC for LFMs and a well-defined algorithm. The results are presented thoroughly, with comparisons to a diverse set of competing methods across multiple datasets, including both synthetic and real-world data. The references are comprehensive and demonstrate awareness of relevant prior work. The authors clearly articulate the novelty of their approach as a logical and effective generalization of existing techniques. 

The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the technical details and understand the key contributions. The notation is consistently used, and the mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient clarity. The organization into sections dedicated to analysis and refinements – including the CCCP interpretation, shrinkage acceleration, and identifiability – is particularly effective. The inclusion of algorithmic details and explanations of the merging post-processing further enhances clarity.

The work addresses a significant problem in LFM model selection – the computational burden of existing Bayesian non-parametric and variational Bayesian approaches. Extending the FAB framework offers a promising avenue for scaling these models to larger datasets. The emphasis on automation, reducing the need for manual hyperparameter tuning, is a valuable contribution. The inclusion of the accelerated shrinkage method and the analysis of parameter identifiability strengthens the practical utility and theoretical grounding of the work. While the approach builds upon established concepts, the combination and generalization to LFMs represent a novel contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Successfully extends FAB inference and FIC to LFMs, overcoming limitations of previous applications.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Demonstrates significant speedups compared to competing methods, making LFM model selection more practical for large datasets.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Provides rigorous analysis of the Hessian matrix and establishes parameter identifiability.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Achieves competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   **Practical Impact:** Reduces the need for manual hyperparameter tuning, simplifying the model selection process.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Approximation Stack:** The method relies on multiple approximations (FIC, mean-field, shrinkage). While justified, a more thorough discussion of the potential limitations and failure modes arising from their combined effect would be beneficial. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of each approximation would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Failure Case Analysis:** The paper lacks a focused investigation into scenarios where the approximations may lead to suboptimal performance. Exploring conditions under which the method breaks down would provide a more complete picture of its capabilities.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of latent feature modeling. The authors have successfully extended a powerful inference technique to a new domain, addressing a significant practical challenge. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. While a more in-depth discussion of the potential limitations of the approximations would be valuable, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
