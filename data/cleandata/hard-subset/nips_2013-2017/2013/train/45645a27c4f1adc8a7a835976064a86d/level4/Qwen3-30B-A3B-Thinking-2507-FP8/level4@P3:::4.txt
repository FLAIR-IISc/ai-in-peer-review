

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant limitation of the existing FAB framework. While FAB has been successfully applied to mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models (HMMs), it was previously restricted to models where the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood is block diagonal. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, which do not satisfy this condition, can still leverage FAB through a novel asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix. Their key theoretical contribution shows that FIC (factorized information criterion) for LFMs takes the same form as for MMs, enabling FAB/LFMs to inherit desirable properties like automatic hidden states selection, parameter identifiability, and monotonic convergence.

The paper builds on recent work by Fujimaki and colleagues on FAB for MMs [5, 6] and extends it to LFMs, which have been primarily addressed using nonparametric Bayesian (NPB) methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [10, 11] and variational Bayes (VB) [3]. Unlike these methods, FAB/LFMs do not require tuning hyperparameters or priors for model selection, and they automatically determine the optimal number of latent features through a regularization effect inherent to the FIC approximation.

The theoretical contributions are well-motivated and rigorously presented. The authors' key insight—that Lemma 1 shows log det |F(d)| contains the log ∑n znk term without requiring the block-diagonal Hessian condition—effectively bridges the gap between FAB for MMs and LFMs. The paper also provides valuable theoretical insights into FAB/LFMs' properties: (1) the connection to convex-concave procedure (CCCP) that enables a novel shrinkage acceleration method, and (2) the identifiability theorem that provides a theoretical basis for a merge post-processing step.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare FAB/LFMs against state-of-the-art methods including IBP with fast Gibbs sampling [2], variational Bayes with finite K [3], and maximum-expectation IBP (MEIBP) [22] on artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets. The results consistently demonstrate FAB/LFMs' superiority in model selection accuracy (particularly for large N), prediction performance (higher predictive log-likelihoods), and computational efficiency. The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) significantly reduces computational costs by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process, as evidenced in Figure 1.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and well-supported, demonstrating that the FIC framework is more general than previously thought.
2. The paper provides rigorous proofs for key theoretical claims, with supplementary materials offering additional details.
3. The proposed shrinkage acceleration method is practical and significantly reduces computational costs without compromising performance.
4. The empirical results are compelling, showing consistent advantages across multiple datasets and evaluation metrics.
5. The paper clearly articulates the connection between FAB/LFMs and previous work on MMs, demonstrating how the same FIC form enables similar desirable properties.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of when FAB/LFMs might not perform as well. The authors note that for small N, FIC approximation may be inaccurate, but it's unclear how this limitation affects practical applications.
2. The comparison with VB methods is somewhat limited, as the authors note that VB was omitted due to computational time (Section 4). A more thorough comparison would strengthen the claims about computational efficiency.
3. The connection between the theoretical identifiability result (Theorem 4) and the practical merge post-processing could be more clearly explained in the main text.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and sufficient technical detail for experts to reproduce the results. The authors have thoroughly addressed related work, including recent advances in LFMs like MEIBP and accelerated IBP methods.

This work makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. By extending the FAB framework to LFMs, the authors have created a method that offers both theoretical elegance and practical advantages over existing approaches. The automatic model selection capability without hyperparameter tuning represents a meaningful advance in the field, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its superiority in terms of model selection accuracy, prediction performance, and computational efficiency.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and thorough empirical evaluation; it's clearly written with well-organized presentation; it's novel in extending FAB to LFMs despite the theoretical challenges; and it's significant as it provides a practical, efficient model selection method for a broad class of latent variable models with immediate applicability to real-world problems.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models and would be of interest to the NIPS community.
