Summary:
The paper presents an inference algorithm for the binary latent feature model (LFM), in which each observation x_n is represented by a K-dimensional binary vector z_n, where z_nk = 1 indicates the presence of the k-th latent feature for observation n. The inference goal is to estimate the z’s for all observations (along with the other model parameters). The proposed EM-based algorithm incorporates a shrinkage mechanism to learn the z’s and to infer the number of latent features K. This is conceptually related to nonparametric Bayesian latent feature models such as the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). However, rather than using priors to induce parsimony, the method relies on a model selection criterion—Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) Inference—recently introduced for mixture models and HMMs. FAB expresses an approximation to the log marginal likelihood as the expected complete log-likelihood plus a model selection term that encourages shrinkage in the number of latent features, allowing one to set an upper bound on K and learn the effective K during inference. On large datasets, the paper reports that FAB outperforms Gibbs sampling, variational inference, and MAP estimation for the IBP in both runtime and inference quality.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound, and the algorithmic details are consistent with an EM-like procedure augmented with a shrinkage term. The ability to handle large datasets while simultaneously learning K is appealing.

Clarity:
The methodological exposition is generally clear. Some experimental details, however, are insufficiently explained (see comments below), which makes it difficult to fully interpret the empirical results.

Originality:
While FAB has been proposed previously in the context of mixture models and HMMs, applying FAB to latent feature models is, to my knowledge, novel.

Significance:
The paper targets an important problem: efficient inference in latent feature models with automated determination of the number of latent features.

Weaknesses:
- No discussion of limitations. Are there regimes where the proposed algorithm underperforms vanilla Gibbs sampling?
- Limited discussion of convergence challenges, given the EM-like nature of the procedure.
- The method is restricted to binary latent feature models and does not extend to factor analysis or probabilistic PCA.
- The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently thorough and appears flawed in several places (see below).
- In the small-data regime, where asymptotic approximations may be unreliable, the behavior of the algorithm is unclear; this warrants discussion and/or experiments.
- Some recent work on efficient inference for LFMs is not discussed (see below).
- The MEIBP algorithm in [22] applies only to nonnegative linear Gaussian models (the loading matrix W must be nonnegative). Consequently, the synthetic and block-image experiments—where W is not constrained to be nonnegative—are not valid comparisons for MEIBP. If MEIBP is to be included as a baseline, the synthetic data should be generated with nonnegative W.

Other comments:
- Block-image data: since the noise variance is known and provided to FAB, for fairness the IBP Gibbs sampler should be given the same noise variance rather than the heuristic of 0.75 times the empirical standard deviation.
- VB-IBP [2]: the infinite variational version could (and arguably should) be used; the experiments rely on a finite variational approximation. Experimental settings for VB lack sufficient detail (e.g., number of restarts).
- The accelerated Gibbs sampler reportedly discovers about 70 latent features on the 49-dimensional Sonar data. This is surprising and suggests potential issues with initialization and/or the noise variance specification.
- Line 70: Reference [21] performs MAP estimation for the IBP, not Gibbs sampling (same as [22]). Please correct the text accordingly.
- Recent related work: please discuss small-variance asymptotics for nonparametric LFMs, e.g., “MAD-Bayes: MAP-based Asymptotic Derivations from Bayes” (ICML 2013).

Minor comments:
- Line 349: For real data, [1, 22] suggest setting the noise standard deviation to 0.25 times the empirical standard deviation of the examples across all dimensions, not to 0.75.

Comments after author feedback:
The feedback addressed some questions. A few issues should be fixed or improved before acceptance:
- The experimental methodology (for both the proposed method and baselines) needs clearer description and justification. Explain anomalous behavior where relevant—for example, whether a fixed alpha in the Gibbs sampler led to overestimation of K, or whether noise variance hyperparameters were mis-specified.
- Correct the description of [21]: it performs MAP inference, not sampling.
- Include and discuss the MAD-Bayes reference for IBP-based inference.

Overall, the proposed inference method for LFMs is interesting, and its scalability and ability to infer K are strong contributions. However, the experimental evaluation requires more careful design, fairer baselines, and clearer reporting.
