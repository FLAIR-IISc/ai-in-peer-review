

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

This paper introduces a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a key limitation of the original FAB framework that only applied to models with a block-diagonal Hessian matrix. The core novelty lies in Lemma 1, which demonstrates that LFMs, despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition, still yield the same FIC form as MMs. This theoretical insight is bold and non-obvious, fundamentally expanding the applicability of FAB inference.

The authors make three substantial contributions: (1) proving that LFMs can utilize the same FIC form as MMs despite different structural assumptions; (2) providing a CCCP interpretation of the FAB optimization that leads to a practical shrinkage acceleration method; and (3) establishing identifiability properties for LFMs that enable a post-processing merge step. The theoretical foundation is strong, with Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 providing the key insight that makes the entire approach possible.

The paper excels in its practical implementation of the theoretical insights. The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) is particularly noteworthy for drastically reducing computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process. This is a rare case where theoretical insight directly translates to a practical computational advantage. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate FAB's superiority in both computational efficiency and model selection accuracy compared to state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) across multiple datasets.

The paper's main strength is its conceptual novelty. While previous work on model selection for LFMs (e.g., IBP, VB) has focused on computational efficiency or model accuracy, this paper introduces a fundamentally different approach to model selection that is fully automated (no hyperparameters) and theoretically grounded. The insight that LFMs can use the same FIC form as MMs despite different structural properties is a significant theoretical contribution that opens up new directions for Bayesian inference.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with the theoretical contributions logically developed and supported by rigorous proofs (in the supplementary materials). The experimental section comprehensively evaluates the method against multiple baselines across artificial, synthetic, and real-world datasets, demonstrating consistent advantages in computational speed and model selection accuracy.

The paper's main weakness is that it doesn't fully explore why the Hessian condition isn't satisfied for LFMs but the FIC form still holds. While Lemma 1 provides the mathematical justification, a more intuitive explanation of this phenomenon would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the paper could better contrast with more recent methods for LFMs beyond the ones cited (though it does compare against state-of-the-art approaches).

Despite these minor points, the paper makes a substantial contribution to the field. The extension of FAB inference to LFMs is not just a minor technical improvement but represents a conceptual advance in Bayesian model selection. The theoretical insight that LFMs can use the same FIC form as MMs is bold and significant, and the practical implementation with shrinkage acceleration provides immediate benefits for real-world applications.

In terms of significance, this work addresses a fundamental limitation in existing model selection methods for latent variable models. The ability to automatically select model complexity without hyperparameters or priors is highly valuable for practitioners, and the computational efficiency makes it applicable to large-scale problems. The paper's theoretical contributions also advance our understanding of Bayesian inference for latent variable models.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS should prioritize. It's not just a minor improvement to existing methods but introduces a fundamentally new perspective on model selection that could influence future work in Bayesian inference. The combination of strong theoretical insight with practical implementation makes it particularly compelling.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Groundbreaking theoretical insight extending FAB to LFMs
- Novel CCCP interpretation leading to practical computational acceleration
- Strong theoretical grounding with rigorous proofs
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing clear advantages
- Fully automated model selection without hyperparameters

**Weaknesses:**
- Could provide more intuition for why Hessian condition isn't satisfied but FIC form holds
- Limited comparison with very recent methods for LFMs
- Minor details in experimental setup could be clarified

This paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection by addressing a fundamental limitation of existing methods. The conceptual novelty and practical impact make it a strong candidate for NIPS.
