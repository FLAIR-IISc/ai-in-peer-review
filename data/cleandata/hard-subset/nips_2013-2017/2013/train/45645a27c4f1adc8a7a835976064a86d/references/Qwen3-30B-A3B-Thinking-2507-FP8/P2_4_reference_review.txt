

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously developed for mixture models (MMs), to latent feature models (LFMs). The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications, they can still derive the same FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) form as MMs. This is achieved through Lemma 1, which shows that log det |F(d)| contains the same log(∑n znk) term as MMs. The paper then develops an FAB/LFM algorithm with shrinkage acceleration via CCCP interpretation and a merge post-processing step for identifiability. The authors show FAB/LFM achieves better model selection accuracy with lower computational cost than state-of-the-art methods.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations. The key contribution is Lemma 1, which establishes the surprising result that LFMs, despite not satisfying the conditions A1 and A2, still yield the same FIC form as MMs. This is well-justified through asymptotic analysis and supported by Theorem 2. The authors also provide important theoretical insights about identifiability (Theorem 4) and the CCCP interpretation of the FAB E-step.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The artificial simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of the shrinkage acceleration (Figure 1), while the block data experiment (Figure 3) clearly shows FAB's ability to extract true features without irrelevant ones. The real-world data experiments (Table 1) consistently show FAB achieving better predictive performance with lower computational cost than competitors, particularly for large datasets. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., FAB's performance degrades for small N in Libras dataset), showing appropriate self-critique.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the work within the literature. The theoretical development is presented logically, building from prior FAB work to the novel extension to LFMs. Key definitions (like the FIC and FAB framework) are well-explained, and the notation is consistent and clear. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are precise and include important implementation details like the shrinkage acceleration step. The figures effectively complement the text, with Figure 3 particularly illustrating the practical advantage of FAB's feature selection.

## Originality
This work makes a significant original contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, a domain where the method was previously thought inapplicable. The core insight—that LFMs still yield the same FIC form as MMs despite lacking the block-diagonal Hessian structure—is novel and theoretically important. The CCCP interpretation of the FAB E-step (Section 3.1) is a particularly elegant theoretical contribution that also leads to practical improvements. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and subsequent merge post-processing provide valuable theoretical and practical insights for LFMs that are new to the literature.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Model selection for LFMs is a critical problem in many applications (e.g., link prediction), and existing methods (like IBP and VB) suffer from high computational costs. The paper demonstrates that FAB/LFM achieves better model selection accuracy with significantly lower computational cost than state-of-the-art methods. The automatic feature selection without hyperparameters is particularly valuable for practitioners. The results show clear advantages in both accuracy and speed, especially for large-scale datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS), where FAB consistently outperforms competitors in predictive performance while using fewer latent features.

## Weaknesses and Suggestions
While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas for improvement:

1. The paper could more explicitly explain *why* LFMs yield the same FIC form despite lacking the block-diagonal structure. While Lemma 1 provides the mathematical justification, a more intuitive explanation would strengthen the contribution.

2. The experiments omit VB due to computational cost, but it's a standard baseline for LFMs. Including a brief comparison (even if only for small datasets) would provide a more complete picture of FAB's advantages.

3. The merge post-processing (Section 3.2) is important for identifiability but could be more thoroughly explained. The paper states "we empirically found that a few merging operations were likely to occur," but doesn't quantify this or provide more insight into when merging is necessary.

4. The theoretical connection to MAD-Bayes (mentioned in Related Work) could be elaborated upon, as it's a related asymptotic approach that might offer additional context.

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The theoretical insights are novel and well-justified, the algorithm is well-designed and practical, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The paper addresses an important problem (automatic model selection for LFMs) with a clean, theoretically grounded approach that delivers practical benefits in terms of computational efficiency and model accuracy.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria exceptionally well. The quality is strong with rigorous theory and thorough experiments. The clarity is excellent with well-organized presentation. The originality is significant, extending FAB to a new domain with novel theoretical insights. The significance is clear, with practical implications for large-scale latent feature modeling.

I recommend this paper for acceptance at NIPS. It represents a meaningful advance in Bayesian model selection for latent feature models, with both theoretical depth and practical utility that will be valuable to the machine learning community. The authors have made a strong case for the value of their approach through careful theoretical development and convincing empirical results.

## Pro/Con Summary
**Pros:** 
- Novel extension of FAB to LFMs with rigorous theoretical justification
- Significant computational advantages over state-of-the-art methods
- Automatic model selection without hyperparameters
- Strong experimental validation across multiple datasets
- Theoretical insights on identifiability and CCCP interpretation

**Cons:**
- Could better explain why LFMs yield same FIC form despite lacking block-diagonal structure
- Omission of VB comparisons (though understandable due to computational cost)
- Merge post-processing could be more thoroughly explained

This paper is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection for latent feature models and deserves publication at NIPS.
