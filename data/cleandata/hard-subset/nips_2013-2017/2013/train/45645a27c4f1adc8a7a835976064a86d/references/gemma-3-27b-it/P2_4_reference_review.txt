## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB offers a computationally efficient Bayesian approximation for model selection (determining latent feature dimensionality) without requiring hand-tuned hyperparameters. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions for FAB (block diagonal Hessian), a theoretical analysis reveals the underlying FIC structure remains the same. They further contribute by relating FAB E-steps to a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) which inspires a shrinkage acceleration method, and by establishing parameter identifiability with implications for post-processing and feature merging.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The core contribution – extending FAB to LFMs and demonstrating the surprising similarity in FIC structure despite violating initial assumptions – is valuable. Lemma 1 is a key result and well-justified.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The development of the shrinkage acceleration based on the CCCP interpretation is a significant practical improvement, addressing a potential bottleneck of the FAB approach.  The empirical results (Figure 1) clearly demonstrate its effectiveness.
* **Identifiability Analysis:**  The analysis of parameter identifiability is a strong theoretical aspect, providing insights into potential issues with MLE and justifying the FAB approach's regularization effects. The suggested merge post-processing is a clever application of this analysis.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate competitive performance against established methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) on both synthetic and real datasets, highlighting the benefits of FAB in terms of both accuracy and computational cost.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and derivations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in LFM Application:** While extending FAB is a worthwhile contribution, the application to LFMs itself doesn't introduce fundamentally new modeling capabilities.  LFMs have been extensively studied, and the primary contribution lies in providing a different *inference* method.
* **Experimental Scope:** While comprehensive, the experiments are somewhat limited in scope. The comparison to other methods isn't consistently across all datasets. For example, VB is omitted from the real-world data section. A more thorough comparison, especially regarding the sensitivity of methods to parameter settings, would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity of Comparisons:** The authors acknowledge fixing the noise standard deviation for some comparisons due to limitations in the other methods.  This introduces a potential bias in the comparison and should be discussed more carefully. While acknowledging the limitations is good, the impact on the results needs further consideration.
* **Merge Post-processing Limited Impact:** The experiments don’t demonstrate a significant impact from the merge post-processing. The authors note this, but further investigation into scenarios where merging provides substantial benefits would be valuable.




**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound. The derivations are well-explained, and the theoretical analysis is convincing. The empirical results support the claims, though more thorough comparisons would strengthen this aspect. The completeness is adequate; a deeper dive into the limitations of the FIC approximation for smaller N would be valuable.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The notation is standard, and the explanations are accessible.  The connection between CCCP and the acceleration method is well-explained.
* **Originality (7/10):** Extending FAB to LFMs is a novel application, and the demonstration of the consistent FIC structure is a valuable theoretical insight. However, the overall approach builds heavily on existing FAB work, and the LFM application itself is not radically new.
* **Significance (7/10):** The paper advances the state-of-the-art in efficient Bayesian inference for LFMs. The computational advantages offered by FAB, particularly with the acceleration technique, are significant. The identifiability analysis also provides a valuable theoretical foundation. However, the impact may be limited to researchers specifically interested in FAB and efficient LFM inference.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a theoretically sound extension of a promising inference framework (FAB) to a relevant model class (LFMs).
* Offers practical improvements in computational efficiency through the shrinkage acceleration.
* Provides theoretical justification for the method’s regularization properties via identifiability analysis.
* Demonstrates competitive performance in experiments.

**Con:**

* Limited novelty in the specific application of FAB to LFMs.
* Experimental scope could be broader and more consistent.
* The impact of the merge post-processing is not fully demonstrated.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. While the application to LFMs isn’t groundbreaking in itself, the theoretical contributions, practical improvements, and solid experimental validation make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings. Addressing the minor weaknesses regarding the experimental scope and more in-depth discussion of the fixed noise parameter in comparisons would further strengthen the paper. The work provides a valuable tool for researchers working on efficient Bayesian inference for latent feature models.
