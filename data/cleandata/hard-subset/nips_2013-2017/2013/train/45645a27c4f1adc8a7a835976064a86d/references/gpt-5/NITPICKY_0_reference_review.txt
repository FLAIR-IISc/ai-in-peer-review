Summary
This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs) with Gaussian likelihood and Bernoulli features. The core claim (Lemma 1) is that the Hessian of the negated complete log-likelihood w.r.t. per-dimension parameters induces the same asymptotic log-count penalty ∑k log(∑n znk) that underlies the FIC for mixture models, despite the Hessian not being block-diagonal in LFMs. This yields an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2), a tractable lower bound via linearization and mean-field over Z, and an EM-like FAB algorithm that automatically shrinks irrelevant features. The paper further interprets the E-step as a CCCP, proposes a shrinkage acceleration heuristic, and states an “identifiability” result (Theorem 4) motivating a post hoc merging step. Experiments compare FAB against IBP variants, VB, and MEIBP on synthetic and real datasets.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: showing that the FIC structure for MMs transfers to LFMs is interesting and potentially impactful. If correct and general, Lemma 1/Theorem 2 extend the scope of asymptotic Bayesian model selection beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians.
- Automation: the FAB objective yields data-driven shrinkage without tuning hyperparameters, and the CCCP view is useful for designing accelerations.
- Empirical promise: on several datasets FAB appears to produce compact models and competitive or better predictive log-likelihood with reasonable runtime. The CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration is practical.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical correctness and clarity:
- Critical derivation details are deferred to the supplement. Lemma 1 is central; the “mild assumptions” under which log det|F(d)| = ∑k log(∑n znk/N) + Op(1) holds must be clearly stated in the main text, along with at least a proof sketch. Given LFMs are singular, the regularity of the complete likelihood and nonsingularity of Fisher information at the MLE need explicit conditions (e.g., bounds away from degenerate πk, Λd > 0, no collinearity in WZ, etc.).
- Equation inconsistencies:
  - Eq. (11): µnk = g(cnk + η(πk) − D/2Nπ̃k). The intended term appears to be − D/(2Nπ̃k), not −(D/2N)π̃k; the text suggests smaller π̃k increases shrinkage, which only holds for −D/(2Nπ̃k). Please fix the typography and parentheses.
  - CCCP update (14): µtnk = g(cnk + η(πk) − D/∑n µt−1nk). This is missing a factor 1/2 if derived from L(q) = −(D/2)∑k log∑n µnk (the derivative is −D/(2∑n µnk)). Please reconcile Eq. (11), Eq. (13), and Eq. (14).
  - Eq. (10): the constant terms “− 2D + K log N” are unexplained and dimensionally odd. Given J(Z,P) includes |P| and a −(DK/2) log N term (Eq. (8)), please define |P| explicitly (DK + 2D + K?) and show how these constants arise in the lower bound.
  - Notation switches (Θ vs P), missing definitions (e.g., DZ, Dk earlier), and typos (mean-filed) hinder reproducibility.
- Monotonicity claims: The paper states the algorithm monotonically increases the FIC lower bound in every single step. However, accelerateShrinkage runs multiple CCCP updates using a stale ck (computed before inner updates) and updates π and π̃ within the inner loop. It is unclear whether these operations preserve monotonicity of L. If monotonicity is not guaranteed with acceleration, please qualify the claims and/or give conditions under which the bound increases.
- Mean-field approximation: Although common, moving from Eq. (1) to a mean-field q(zn) breaks the exact variational representation. The paper asserts asymptotic closeness to the marginal likelihood (Cor. 3), but the effect of mean-field on the asymptotic gap is not discussed. Please clarify whether Cor. 3 holds with mean-field q and under what conditions.
- Identifiability: Theorem 4 is quite weak: it shows that if w·k = w·l, then the average activations are equal at stationary points. This does not establish parameter identifiability or uniqueness of ML/FAB solutions; it addresses a specific degeneracy. The proposed merge heuristic relies on approximate equality of columns but lacks a statistically principled criterion (e.g., likelihood-ratio or bound increase guarantees under a threshold). Please temper the identifiability claim and/or strengthen the analysis.

Experimental methodology:
- Fairness: FAB learns Λ while competing baselines often use fixed noise variance (1 or 0.75); this confers an advantage. A fair comparison would either:
  - fix Λ for FAB to the same values, or
  - enable/approximate noise learning for baselines (e.g., empirical Bayes or cross-validated noise).
- Hyperparameter tuning: IBP’s α is fixed to 3; MEIBP is highly sensitive to initialization; VB is omitted due to runtime. Claims of superiority are weakened without proper tuning or reporting performance under reasonable hyperparameter ranges and computational budgets. Consider reporting wall-clock vs. performance trade-offs with tuned baselines.
- Reporting: The paper references Table 1 and Figures 1–3, but they are not included here. It is hard to judge significance without numbers, error bars, and statistical tests. Please report:
  - average and variance (over seeds) of K, PLL/TLL, and runtimes;
  - hardware specs and implementation details (e.g., MATLAB version, BLAS, tolerance settings);
  - per-iteration complexity and convergence diagnostics.
- Shrinkage/merge details: Exact equality ∑n µnk/N ∈ {0,1} is unlikely in mean-field; what thresholds are used in practice? How sensitive are results to thresholds and Tshrink? For merge, what distance thresholds are used and how often does merging increase the bound?

Relations to prior work:
- “MAD-Bayes” (Broderick et al.) is related but differs in asymptotics; the contrast could be sharpened (e.g., zero-noise limit vs. large-N Laplace on complete likelihood).
- There is related asymptotic model selection literature for singular models (Watanabe’s WAIC/SWAIC) that could be cited and contrasted beyond [25,26].

Writing and formatting:
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies: mean-filed → mean-field; NBP/NPB used inconsistently; MEIPB vs MEIBP; grammar (“conduct two analysis”, “on early learning phase”). Reference formatting is incomplete (missing years/pages, inconsistent venues). Please adhere to NeurIPS style.

Pros
- Interesting theoretical direction extending FIC to LFMs.
- Practical, simple algorithm with built-in shrinkage and a plausible acceleration.
- Empirical indications of compact models and good PLL in several datasets.

Cons
- Key theoretical claims are underspecified in the main text; derivations and assumptions lack transparency.
- Mathematical inconsistencies in equations (missing factors, ambiguous terms).
- Monotonicity and correctness of the acceleration step are not established.
- Experimental comparisons are not convincingly fair or comprehensive; important baselines omitted/tuned inadequately; missing quantitative tables/figures.
- Presentation issues (notation, typos, references).

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is promising and could be impactful, the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation issues to recommend acceptance. I encourage the authors to fix the derivations and inconsistencies, clarify assumptions and guarantees, tighten the experimental protocol for fairness, and improve the exposition. Confidence: high.
