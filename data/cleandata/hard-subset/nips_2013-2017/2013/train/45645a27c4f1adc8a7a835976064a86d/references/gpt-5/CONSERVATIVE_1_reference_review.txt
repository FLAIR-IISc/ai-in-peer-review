Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM settings to linear-Gaussian latent feature models (LFMs). The key technical claim (Lemma 1) is that the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian of the complete-data likelihood asymptotically decomposes into a sum of log feature-occupancy terms, yielding an FIC for LFMs that matches the MM case. This leads to an automated feature-selection effect without priors. The authors derive a tractable lower bound and an EM-like algorithm with a mean-field E-step, interpret E-step updates as a CCCP procedure, propose a shrinkage acceleration, and add a merge post-processing justified by an identifiability analysis (Theorem 4). Experiments against IBP Gibbs, variational IBP, MEIBP, and EM show the method selects smaller K and achieves competitive or better predictive log-likelihood, with substantial speed-ups from the acceleration.

Strengths
- Ambitious and potentially impactful extension of FAB to LFMs; if correct, the asymptotic equivalence of FIC terms across MMs and LFMs is a notable insight.
- Algorithm is conceptually simple, with closed-form M-step and a monotone bound-optimization structure (for the core steps).
- CCCP interpretation is reasonable and the shrinkage acceleration appears pragmatically useful.
- Empirical results show promising model selection and predictive performance, with smaller K and good behavior on several datasets.
- The work aims at automation (no hand-tuned hyperparameters), which is desirable for practitioners.

Weaknesses and concerns
Theory and assumptions
- The paper’s central step (Lemma 1) is only stated with “mild assumptions” and its proof is deferred to the supplement. The conditions under which log det|F(d)| equals sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)/N + Op(1) are nontrivial in LFMs, where parameters are shared across features and the Hessian is not block diagonal. Without explicit assumptions in the main text (e.g., boundedness, non-degeneracy, feature occupancy scaling, properties of Λ and W), it is hard to assess correctness or scope.
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 3 claim asymptotic closeness of the FIC to the marginal likelihood. However, the implemented algorithm introduces a mean-field restriction on q(Z), making the optimized objective a lower bound of the FIC, not of the true marginal likelihood. The text asserts the asymptotic gap is constant, but this is not justified under the mean-field restriction. A quantification of the gap induced by mean-field is needed.
- Identifiability (Theorem 4) is stated for a restricted situation and shows equal weights imply equal occupancies. This is far from full identifiability; in LFMs, redundancies and near-duplicates are a broader issue. The subsequent merge heuristic based on Euclidean distance is ad hoc; its theoretical effect on the objective is not characterized.
- Monotonicity is claimed for the core alternating updates, but the accelerateShrinkage (multiple CCCP steps), feature pruning, and merging operations can alter the objective and model dimension. The paper asserts pruning increases L via reduced complexity, but no proof is provided for the specific thresholds or numerical practices; merging is only tested “if the lower bound increases” without guarantees. A formal statement of conditions maintaining monotonicity would improve rigor.

Empirical evaluation
- Fairness of comparisons is questionable. FAB learns the noise variance; baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) are run with fixed noise (and in some cases different defaults per original code). This can materially affect predictive log-likelihood. Either allow all methods to learn noise (where applicable) or fix noise for FAB too.
- The IBP hyperparameter α is fixed to 3 by default, which may bias model complexity. Tuning α or reporting sensitivity would be fairer.
- VB is omitted on real data “because of long time,” which limits the strength of the claims about accuracy/cost trade-offs. Likewise, more modern baselines (e.g., BPFA, spike-and-slab factor analysis, scalable SVI-style IBP variants) are not considered.
- Initializing K at min(N, D) can be very large and will affect time comparisons across methods differently (MEIBP’s performance appears highly sensitive to initial K). Provide ablations over initial K and report iteration counts and wall-clock time on identical hardware.
- Statistical reporting is thin: results are averaged over few seeds; CIs or standard deviations are not consistently provided; the block-data example shows only one configuration. Given the asymptotic nature of the theory, finite-sample behavior should be examined more carefully.
- Scalability analysis is missing. There is no derivation of per-iteration computational complexity or empirical scaling plots versus N, D, and true K. Memory usage with large K initialization should be discussed.

Clarity and reproducibility
- Many crucial details (assumptions for Lemma 1, proofs, merge routine) are in the supplement; the main paper does not make them sufficiently transparent to evaluate. Some notation and wording (e.g., the relationship between π and π̃, thresholds for pruning μnk close to 0/1) could be clarified.
- Sensitivity to Tshrink and δ is not reported. Code availability is not stated.

Questions for the authors
- Please state in the main paper the exact assumptions required for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, and briefly sketch why they hold in typical LFM regimes.
- How does the mean-field restriction on q(Z) affect the claimed asymptotic equivalence to the marginal likelihood? Can you bound the additional gap?
- Do accelerateShrinkage, pruning, and merging preserve monotonic increase of the optimized lower bound? If not, how do you control for possible decreases?
- Can you rerun the real-data comparisons with either learned noise for all methods (when available) or fixed noise for FAB, and with sensitivity analyses for α in IBP and initial K in MEIBP?
- Provide complexity analysis and scaling plots; include ablations on Tshrink and initialization.

Pros
- Novel and interesting extension of FAB to LFMs with a potentially widely applicable FIC form.
- Practical algorithm with sensible acceleration and automatic model selection.
- Promising empirical results and smaller inferred K.

Cons
- Central theoretical claim depends on unstated assumptions; proofs relegated to supplement.
- Mean-field approximation undermines the clean asymptotic story without quantified impact.
- Comparisons to baselines are not fully fair; important baselines and sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Monotonicity and correctness of pruning/merging steps not theoretically justified.
- Limited scalability analysis and weak statistical reporting.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is promising and could be impactful if the theoretical foundations are made explicit in the main text and the empirical evaluation is strengthened with fair, thorough comparisons and clearer scalability analysis. I encourage the authors to address the concerns above; with stronger theory presentation and fairer experiments, this work could be suitable for a future iteration.
