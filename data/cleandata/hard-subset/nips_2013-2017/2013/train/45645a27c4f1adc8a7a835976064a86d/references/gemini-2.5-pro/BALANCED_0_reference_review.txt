### Summary

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for model selection that maximizes a tractable lower bound of the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. Prior work on FAB was limited to models like Mixture Models (MMs) where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. LFMs do not satisfy this condition due to parameter sharing across features.

The central contribution of this paper is a theoretical result (Lemma 1) demonstrating that, through an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian, the FIC for LFMs takes a form analogous to that of MMs. This allows the development of a FAB algorithm for LFMs (FAB/LFM) that can automatically determine the number of latent features without priors or tunable hyperparameters. The authors further provide two important analyses: 1) they interpret the E-step as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), leading to a practical "shrinkage acceleration" algorithm that speeds up convergence, and 2) they prove that FAB resolves the parameter non-identifiability issue in LFMs, which motivates a principled post-processing step for merging redundant features. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data show that FAB/LFM is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant and Novel Contribution:** The paper successfully extends the applicability of the FAB framework to a new, important class of models (LFMs). Overcoming the limitation of the block-diagonal Hessian assumption is a non-trivial technical achievement that broadens the scope of this promising model selection paradigm. This work builds nicely on previous FAB papers (e.g., [5, 6]) and represents a solid step forward for this line of research.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The core theoretical derivation in Section 2.1, particularly Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, appears sound and is the key enabler for the entire method. The insight that the log-determinant of the LFM Hessian yields the same `log(sum_n z_nk)` complexity term as in MMs is elegant. The connection between the resulting FIC and the marginal log-likelihood (Corollary 3) provides strong theoretical justification for the approach.

3.  **Practical and Methodological Refinements:** The paper provides more than just a straightforward application of the FAB recipe. The CCCP interpretation of the E-step is insightful and leads to a practically valuable acceleration technique that is shown to significantly reduce computation time (Figure 1). Furthermore, the analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) addresses a fundamental issue in LFMs and provides a principled basis for the proposed feature-merging post-processing step. These refinements demonstrate a deep understanding of both the optimization and the statistical properties of the model.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed. They convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method from multiple angles:
    *   The synthetic data experiment clearly shows superior model selection accuracy compared to baselines, especially in the large N regime where the asymptotic theory is most relevant.
    *   The block data experiment provides a clear qualitative demonstration of the method's ability to recover true underlying features while effectively pruning irrelevant ones.
    *   The real-world missing data experiments show that FAB/LFM achieves strong predictive performance, often with more compact models (smaller K) and reduced computational cost compared to IBP-based methods. The smaller gap between training and prediction log-likelihoods also suggests the method's regularization is effective at preventing overfitting.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on Theoretical Assumptions:** Lemma 1 is central to the paper's claims, but it rests on "some mild assumptions" that are deferred to the supplementary materials. While this is understandable due to space constraints, it would strengthen the main paper to briefly summarize the nature or intuition behind these assumptions (e.g., "assuming sufficient separation between feature vectors W and non-degeneracy of the data distribution..."). This would give the reader more immediate confidence in the result without breaking the flow.

2.  **Deeper Comparison with Related Asymptotic Methods:** The paper mentions MAD-Bayes [1] as another asymptotic approach but distinguishes it primarily by its reliance on a zero-noise limit. A more detailed comparison would be beneficial. For example, how do the resulting penalty terms or optimization procedures differ? Does MAD-Bayes also exhibit the same "model-induced regularization" effect? A slightly deeper discussion could better contextualize the contribution of FAB within the landscape of asymptotic Bayesian methods.

3.  **Limitations of Asymptotic Approximation:** The entire framework is predicated on a large-sample (N → ∞) limit. The authors rightly acknowledge in the final paragraph that the approximation may be less accurate for small N. The results on the "Libras" dataset are noted as a potential instance of this. It would be valuable to elaborate on this limitation. Is there a rule of thumb or a diagnostic for when N is large enough? Could the authors provide more insight into why the method underperformed on that specific dataset? A more explicit discussion of the method's failure modes would make for a more complete and honest evaluation.

4.  **Practical Sensitivity to Initialization:** The algorithm starts with an initial number of features K, set to min(N,D). While the method is designed to shrink this number, the computational cost of the initial iterations will scale with this choice. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the overall runtime to the initial K would be a useful addition for practitioners. For instance, does starting with a much larger K significantly slow down convergence, even if the final result is the same?

### Decision

**Accept.**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. It extends a novel inference framework (FAB) to a widely used class of models (LFMs) by overcoming a significant technical hurdle. The work is theoretically sound, methodologically insightful (with the CCCP interpretation and identifiability analysis), and supported by a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to requests for additional clarification and discussion, which do not detract from the core value of the contribution. The paper is well-written, tackles an important problem, and the proposed method is likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners working with latent variable models.
