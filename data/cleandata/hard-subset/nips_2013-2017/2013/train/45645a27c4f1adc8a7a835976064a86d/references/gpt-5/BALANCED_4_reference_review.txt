Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models (MMs) and HMMs to latent feature models (LFMs). The key technical claim is Lemma 1: under mild assumptions, the log-determinant of the Hessian of the (negated) complete-data log-likelihood with respect to per-dimension parameters θd yields a complexity term ∑k log(∑n znk) up to Op(1), even though LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian structure previously required by FAB. This leads to an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) that has the same form as for MMs: a data-fit term penalized by DK/2 log N and (D/2)∑k log(∑n znk). Based on this FIC, the authors develop a practical FAB algorithm using a tractable lower bound: mean-field q(Z), linearization of −log∑n znk (as in prior FAB work), and joint optimization over q, parameters P = {W,b,Λ,π}, and linearization variables. The derived E-step yields a logistic fixed-point update for µnk with a shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k), inducing automatic feature pruning. The authors interpret the E-step updates as a CCCP (concave-convex) procedure and propose a shrinkage acceleration routine (multiple CCCP steps) to eliminate irrelevant features earlier. They also prove an identifiability result (Theorem 4) implying that FAB resolves certain non-identifiabilities (e.g., duplicate columns in W) by balancing feature usage; this motivates a merge post-processing step.

Empirically, the paper compares FAB/LFM to IBP-based methods (accelerated Gibbs, VB), MEIBP (MAP via submodular maximization), and variational EM (with only prior-based shrinkage). Experiments on synthetic data, block images, and several real datasets show: (i) the acceleration reduces runtime markedly without hurting model selection; (ii) FAB tends to select compact K and achieves strong predictive log-likelihood (PLL), often outperforming IBP variants and EM while being much faster than VB, though slower than MEIBP in wall-clock time; and (iii) on large N, EM and FAB attain similar PLL, but FAB prefers smaller K due to the FIC regularizer.

Quality
Strengths:
- Theoretical contribution is nontrivial: establishing that the same FIC complexity term arises for LFMs despite shared parameters across features is surprising and important. If Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 hold under broadly applicable conditions, this meaningfully broadens the reach of FAB beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians.
- The algorithm is principled (lower bound on FIC, monotonic steps), simple to implement, and leverages CCCP to speed up shrinkage. The identifiability perspective is insightful, and the merge heuristic is reasonable and grounded.
- Empirical results are fairly comprehensive across synthetic and real data, including feature recovery (blocks), model selection, and PLL. The acceleration shows clear practical value.

Weaknesses / concerns:
- The core assumptions for Lemma 1 are pushed to the supplement and only described as “mild.” Because this lemma underpins the entire extension, the main paper should provide a clear statement, intuition, and at least a proof sketch. For instance: what regularity, concentration, or design assumptions on Z and X are required? How crucial is Gaussian noise/diagonal Λ? Does the result extend to non-Gaussian linear LFMs or correlated noise?
- The bound L(q,P,π̃) (Eq. 10) includes constants and terms (e.g., “−2D + K log N”) whose origin is not fully explained; a concise derivation or pointer is needed to ensure reproducibility and to verify monotonicity when combining E/M, acceleration, shrinkage, and merge steps.
- Fairness of empirical comparisons could be improved. For IBP/VB/MEIBP, noise variance was fixed (following prior code), whereas FAB/EM learn Λ; this can influence PLL substantially. Hyperparameters for IBP (e.g., α) were not tuned per-dataset; MEIBP behavior is acknowledged to be sensitive. Some modern IBP variational/split-merge baselines are missing.
- The computational complexity per iteration and memory footprint are not analyzed. Given the focus on scalability, a clear O(NKD) (or similar) analysis, plus the effect of Tshrink and merging, would help situate the method versus MEIBP/IBP/VB.
- The CCCP-based accelerateShrinkage makes multiple inner updates to µ and π within an E-step. While each CCCP step increases the linearized objective, does L (the true lower bound) provably increase at each inner step? The paper claims monotonic increase overall, but the guarantee with acceleration and pruning/merging should be stated precisely.
- The identifiability result is specialized to exact equality of columns (w·k = w·l). In practice, near-duplicates are more common; the paper relies on a distance threshold and checking L-improvement, which is reasonable, but sensitivity analysis is not reported.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. However:
  - State the assumptions of Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 in the main text and provide intuition (e.g., structure of F(d) via Schur complements leading to occupancy-like factors).
  - Clarify the exact lower bound (Eq. 10): constants, dependence on N and K, and which terms are dropped as Op(1).
  - Provide a short complexity analysis and a discussion of numerical stability (e.g., handling π̃k near zero in −D/(2Nπ̃k)).
  - Specify stopping criteria and monotonicity guarantees when using accelerateShrinkage and merge.
  - Briefly discuss initialization sensitivity and report variance across runs.

Originality
- High. Extending FAB to LFMs without the block-diagonal Hessian assumption, and showing the same FIC structure emerges, is novel. The CCCP interpretation and acceleration are also useful insights.

Significance
- Potentially high. LFMs are widely used; an automated, prior-insensitive, and efficient model-selection procedure is attractive. If the theoretical conditions hold broadly and the empirical advantages persist with stronger baselines and fairer settings (noise learned across methods), this could influence practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and surprising theoretical result (FIC structure for LFMs).
- Practical, simple algorithm with monotonic optimization and effective automatic model selection.
- Clear empirical benefits in PLL and compactness; substantial speedups over VB and competitive behavior overall.
- Insightful CCCP view and identifiability/merge analysis.

Cons against acceptance
- Core lemma’s assumptions and proof are deferred; limited intuition in the main text.
- Some fairness issues and missing/tuned baselines (e.g., learned noise for IBP/VB/MEIBP, hyperparameter tuning).
- Limited complexity analysis and guarantees for the accelerated/shrinkage/merge steps.
- Limited sensitivity analyses (initialization, Tshrink, near-duplicate merging).

Suggestions for improvement
- Move the assumptions of Lemma 1 and a proof sketch into the main text, with intuition about why log-det F(d) yields ∑k log occupancy.
- Provide per-iteration time/memory complexity; discuss numerical safeguards (e.g., π̃k ← max(π̃k, ε)).
- Strengthen baselines: allow noise learning where possible; tune IBP α; include additional IBP variational methods if feasible; report sensitivity to MEIBP initialization.
- Report variability across seeds and sensitivity to Tshrink and the merge threshold; include ablation without merge/acceleration.
- Clarify monotonicity guarantees when using accelerateShrinkage and merging; show L vs. iteration plots including pruning/merging events.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and practical algorithm are compelling, and the empirical results are promising. Addressing the clarity and evaluation issues above would make this a solid accept.
