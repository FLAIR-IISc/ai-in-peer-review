

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously applicable only to models with block-diagonal Hessians (e.g., mixture models), to latent feature models (LFMs). The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models. This enables automatic model selection (determining the number of latent features) without priors or hyperparameters. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) theoretical derivation showing FIC for LFMs has the same form as for MMs despite different model structure, (2) a practical FAB/LFM algorithm with a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation, and (3) a proof of parameter identifiability that enables a merge post-processing step.

This work builds on Fujimaki et al.'s FAB inference for mixture models [5,6] and connects to the broader literature on Bayesian nonparametric models like the Indian Buffet Process [10,11,17]. It addresses a gap in the FAB framework that has limited its applicability to more complex latent variable models, which is a significant limitation given the widespread use of LFMs in applications like link prediction [16].

## Quality

The theoretical contributions are strong, particularly Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, which establish the key insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as MMs despite structural differences. The proof of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) is important for latent variable models and provides a theoretical justification for the merge post-processing step. The connection to CCCP (Convex Concave Procedure) offers a valuable new perspective on the FAB optimization.

However, some theoretical assumptions require more scrutiny. The proof of Lemma 1 relies on "mild assumptions" (not fully specified in the main text) about the Hessian structure, and the paper lacks discussion about how sensitive the results are to violations of these assumptions. The asymptotic results (FIC converging to marginal log-likelihood) are theoretically sound but not thoroughly validated experimentally—most experiments use moderate N (up to 2000), not large N where the asymptotic behavior should dominate. The claim that FAB "asymptotically ignores priors" is well-supported theoretically but could be better contextualized with respect to other asymptotic methods.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive but has some limitations. The comparison with MEIBP is problematic because MEIBP is designed for non-negative data, yet the authors applied it to general data (including negative values) without adaptation. The computational cost comparisons might be misleading since some baselines (like IBP) have highly optimized implementations that aren't reflected in the Matlab-based implementation used for FAB. The lack of statistical significance testing across multiple random seeds (only 5 for real data) is a minor weakness.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The mathematical derivations are precise, and the algorithm descriptions are detailed. However, some key theoretical points could be better motivated. For instance, the paper states "the log ∑n znk term naturally appears in log det |F(d)| without A1 and A2" but doesn't provide intuition for why this happens in LFMs despite the shared parameters. The CCCP interpretation in Section 3 is particularly well-explained.

The experimental section could be clearer about the specific implementation details of baselines, especially regarding parameter choices for methods like IBP and VB. The paper mentions using "original codes released by the authors" but doesn't specify which versions or parameter settings were used, which would be important for reproducibility.

## Originality

The core theoretical insight—that LFMs satisfy the same FIC form as MMs despite structural differences—is genuinely novel and represents a significant extension of the FAB framework. The connection to CCCP for acceleration is also a novel contribution that provides both theoretical insight and practical benefit. The merge post-processing based on identifiability is a valuable practical extension.

However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work on Bayesian nonparametric latent feature models. While the authors cite [10,11,17,22,3], they don't clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods like the variational inference for IBP [3] or MEIBP [22] in terms of theoretical foundations. The claim that FAB "does not have a hand-tunable hyper-parameter" is misleading—π̃k is a linearization parameter that requires tuning, though it's less sensitive than priors in other methods.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. LFMs are widely used in applications like link prediction and collaborative filtering, and the high computational cost of existing Bayesian nonparametric methods has limited their scalability. FAB/LFM offers a theoretically grounded approach to model selection with reduced computational cost, which could enable broader adoption of LFMs for large-scale applications. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods in both model selection accuracy and computational efficiency.

The paper makes a compelling case for FAB's advantages, particularly in the real-world data experiments where FAB consistently achieved better predictive performance (higher PLL) with lower computational cost than alternatives. The observation that FAB achieves smaller differences between training and predictive log-likelihood suggests it effectively mitigates overfitting, which is crucial for practical applications.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to extending the FAB framework to a broader class of latent variable models. The theoretical insights are well-founded and the practical implementation is well-executed. The main weaknesses are the limited validation of the asymptotic behavior and some minor issues with the experimental comparisons. However, these do not undermine the core contributions.

**Pro:** Theoretical extension of FAB framework to LFMs is novel and well-supported; practical algorithm with acceleration method; comprehensive experimental evaluation; important theoretical results on identifiability.

**Con:** Asymptotic claims not fully validated in experiments; some experimental comparisons could be more rigorous; limited discussion of assumptions behind theoretical results.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models and should be accepted for NIPS. The work addresses a genuine limitation in the FAB framework and provides both theoretical insights and practical benefits for model selection in LFMs. The paper's significance is high, and it advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian model selection for latent variable models.
