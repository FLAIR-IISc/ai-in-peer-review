## Peer Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with Mixture Models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a Bayesian approximation method for model selection, aiming for automation and scalability. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions required for FAB application (block diagonal Hessian), a theoretical analysis reveals their FICs have a similar form to those of MMs. This allows the authors to adapt the FAB algorithm for LFMs, retaining its desirable properties – automatic model selection, monotonic improvement, and computational efficiency.  The paper further provides analytical insights into the FAB E-step via a connection to the Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), leading to a shrinkage acceleration method, and demonstrates parameter identifiability, guiding a post-processing merging step for latent features.

**Related Work & Context:**

The introduction accurately situates the work within the context of Bayesian model selection, specifically highlighting the computational drawbacks of traditional approaches like Nonparametric Bayesian (NPB) and Variational Bayesian (VB) methods, which FAB aims to address. The discussion of FIC for MMs provides a necessary foundation. The positioning of this work against existing LFM methods like IBP and MEIBP, emphasizing FAB’s potential for scalability, is well done. The authors correctly identify the challenges in applying FAB to LFMs due to the lack of block diagonal structure in the Hessian.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The key strength is the derivation showing the applicability of FAB to LFMs despite the theoretical differences from MMs. Lemma 1 is central to this and convincingly justifies the extension.
* **Algorithmic Innovation:** The shrinkage acceleration method, inspired by the CCCP analysis, is a valuable practical contribution that addresses a potential bottleneck in the FAB algorithm.  The parameter identifiability analysis and resulting merging post-processing are also insightful and improve the robustness of the algorithm.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is comprehensive, including synthetic data, a benchmark dataset (block data), and real-world datasets. The comparison against several baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) is thorough and demonstrates FAB's competitive performance in terms of accuracy and scalability. The ablation study showing the benefits of the shrinkage acceleration is particularly strong.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. Mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise yet sufficiently detailed.
* **Automated Model Selection:**  The promise of an automated and computationally efficient model selection method for LFMs is highly significant.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions & Limitations:**  The paper mentions “mild assumptions” related to Lemma 1 but doesn't delve deeply into their implications. A clearer discussion of these assumptions and their potential impact on the applicability of the method would be beneficial.  What types of LFM datasets might violate these assumptions?
* **Sensitivity to Initial Conditions:** While the experiments mention initialization, the sensitivity of the algorithm to different initializations could be further investigated and discussed.
* **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the experimental results show FAB’s speedup with the shrinkage acceleration, a theoretical analysis of the computational complexity of the FAB algorithm (with and without acceleration) compared to the baselines would strengthen the paper.
* **Merge Post-processing Details:** The details of the merge post-processing algorithm (described as being in the supplementary materials) are somewhat opaque from the main text. Providing more insight into the criteria used for merging and its impact on performance would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Settings:**  The authors mention fixed noise standard deviations for certain experiments, deviating from the FAB approach's goal of automation.  Justification for these choices, or an exploration of methods for automatic noise level estimation, would be useful.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims.  The paper feels complete, though the assumption discussion could be expanded. The authors are careful to present both strengths and weaknesses, although the weakness discussion could be more detailed.
* **Clarity:**  Generally very clear. The mathematical notation is well-defined.  A more detailed description of the merge post-processing would improve clarity.
* **Originality:**  The extension of FAB to LFMs is a novel contribution. The connection to CCCP and the resulting acceleration technique are also original. The identifiability analysis and merging step add further originality.
* **Significance:**  The potential for automated and scalable model selection in LFMs is highly significant. The method could be widely applicable in various domains, particularly those dealing with high-dimensional data.  The improvements in computational efficiency and the mitigation of overfitting are valuable contributions.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and a novel extension of a promising inference framework.
* Thorough empirical validation demonstrating competitive performance and scalability.
* Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
* Addresses a challenging problem (model selection for LFMs) with a practical and efficient solution.

**Against:**

* The discussion of assumptions and limitations could be more detailed.
* The merge post-processing details are somewhat lacking.
* A more formal computational complexity analysis would be valuable.


**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and latent feature modeling. The theoretical results are compelling, the algorithm is well-designed, and the experimental validation is thorough. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and has the potential to be highly impactful in the community.  I recommend the authors address the minor weaknesses identified above in a revision if possible.
