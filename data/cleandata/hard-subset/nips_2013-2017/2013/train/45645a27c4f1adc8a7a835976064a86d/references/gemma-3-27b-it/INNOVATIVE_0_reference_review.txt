This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a key limitation of the original method. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications, a similar FIC structure emerges through asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1). This allows FAB to be applied to a broader class of models with its inherent benefits – automatic model selection, computational efficiency, and asymptotic marginal likelihood convergence. The introduction of a shrinkage acceleration method leveraging a connection to the CCCP framework, and the analysis of parameter identifiability, further strengthen the work.

The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs seems well-motivated and the proof sketches provided (with full proofs in the supplementary material) inspire confidence. The connection to CCCP is insightful, and the resulting acceleration technique appears promising. The identifiability analysis is a valuable addition, addressing a potential concern with the method. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential avenues for future work, adding to the credibility of the research.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are generally easy to follow. The explanation of the FAB algorithm and its components is clear, and the inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a helpful overview. The motivation for each step is well-articulated, making it easy to understand the rationale behind the proposed approach.

The originality of this work is high. Extending FAB inference to LFMs is a genuinely novel contribution. While the individual components (FAB, LFMs, CCCP) are known, their combination and the theoretical justification for applying FAB to non-block-diagonal Hessian models represent a significant advance.  The insights regarding the connection between FIC and model complexity, and its implications for automated model selection, are also particularly original.

The significance of this work is substantial. LFMs are widely used, and current inference methods (NPB, VB) can be computationally expensive, particularly for large-scale datasets.  FAB offers a potentially more efficient alternative, potentially unlocking applications previously intractable with existing methods. The automatic model selection property is also a major benefit, reducing the need for tedious hyperparameter tuning. The empirical results, while not exhaustive, demonstrate the promise of the method, and the speed-up provided by the shrinkage acceleration is crucial for practical applicability. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** Extends FAB to a new model class (LFMs), overcoming a significant limitation of the original framework.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** Provides a rigorous theoretical justification for the extension, based on asymptotic analysis.
* **Practical Benefits:** Offers a potentially faster and more automated inference method for LFMs, with the shrinkage acceleration providing a significant speedup.
* **Identifiability Analysis:** Addresses a critical theoretical concern and provides insights into model merging.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, organized, and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. While the synthetic data and block data results are encouraging, more extensive evaluation on larger and more realistic datasets is desirable. 
* **Computational Cost of Acceleration:** While the acceleration method appears effective, a detailed analysis of its computational complexity compared to existing methods would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the method to initialization and other parameters (e.g., δ) is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of FAB inference. The authors have addressed a significant limitation of the original method and demonstrated its potential for broader applicability. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive, the theoretical contributions and potential practical benefits are substantial.  I believe this paper represents a significant advance in the field and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, despite the minor limitations regarding empirical validation. I strongly lean towards acceptance and would be comfortable with a publication even with the present level of validation. The boldness of the theoretical work and the potential for impact outweigh the need for absolutely exhaustive empirical checks.
