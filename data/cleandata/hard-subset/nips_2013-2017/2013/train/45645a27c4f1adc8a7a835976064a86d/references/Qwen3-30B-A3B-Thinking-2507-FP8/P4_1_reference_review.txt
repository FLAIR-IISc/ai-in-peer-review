

Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a significant limitation of the current FAB framework which was previously only applicable to models satisfying a block diagonal Hessian condition (e.g., Mixture Models). The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying this condition, their Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) takes the same form as for Mixture Models due to a key theoretical insight (Lemma 1), which shows log det |F(d)| = ∑_k log(∑_n z_nk)/N + Op(1). This enables FAB to be applied to LFMs for the first time, providing automatic model selection (determining the number of latent features K) without hyperparameters or priors.

The work builds on previous FAB applications to Mixture Models [5, 6] and related work on Infinite Latent Feature Models using Non-Parametric Bayesian (NPB) methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [9, 10, 11] and variational Bayesian (VB) approaches [3]. While IBP and VB methods have been used for LFMs, they suffer from high computational costs. The authors demonstrate that FAB/LFMs achieve comparable or better model selection accuracy with significantly lower computational cost.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contribution: Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are significant advances that demonstrate the broader applicability of FIC/FAB beyond the block diagonal Hessian condition. This resolves a key theoretical limitation of the FAB framework.
2. Algorithmic innovation: The CCCP interpretation of FAB updates and the shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) provide a practical way to reduce computational cost while maintaining accuracy, with empirical results showing significant speedups.
3. Practical value: FAB/LFMs achieve better model selection accuracy (estimating K more accurately) and better predictive performance (higher log-likelihoods) compared to state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) across multiple datasets.
4. Rigorous analysis: The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and merge post-processing step provide important theoretical insights for handling non-identifiable solutions in LFMs.

Weaknesses:
1. Small N limitations: The paper acknowledges FIC approximation may be inaccurate for small N, and experiments on the Libras dataset show FAB performing worse than NPB methods. This limitation should be more thoroughly discussed.
2. Algorithm complexity: The FAB/LFM algorithm (Algorithm 1) involves multiple steps (E-step, M-step, shrinkage, merge), which may be less accessible to practitioners compared to simpler alternatives.
3. Limited comparison with very recent methods: The paper compares with established baselines but does not discuss more recent advances in scalable LFMs (e.g., recent work on stochastic IBP approximations).

Pro and Con for Acceptance:
*Pro: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, provides rigorous proofs and experimental validation of significant practical advantages, and addresses a meaningful limitation of current Bayesian model selection methods.
*Con: The small N performance issue is somewhat concerning, and the paper could better contextualize its contribution relative to the most recent advances in scalable LFMs.

Overall Assessment:
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to Bayesian model selection. The key insight that LFMs can use the same FIC form as MMs despite not satisfying the block diagonal Hessian condition is novel and significant. The experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages of FAB/LFMs over existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and model selection accuracy. The paper is technically sound, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The work directly addresses a meaningful limitation in the current FAB framework and offers a solution with clear practical benefits for large-scale applications. The paper is clearly within NIPS scope and makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. I recommend acceptance.
