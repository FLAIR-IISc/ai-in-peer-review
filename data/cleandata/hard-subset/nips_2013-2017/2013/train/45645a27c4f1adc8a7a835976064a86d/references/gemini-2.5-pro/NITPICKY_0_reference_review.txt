This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The main challenge is that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption that simplified the derivation of the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for models like Gaussian Mixtures. The authors' main theoretical contribution is to show that, through an asymptotic analysis of the LFM's Hessian matrix, the resulting FIC has the same functional form as that for Mixture Models. This FIC includes a complexity penalty term, `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)`, which encourages shrinking the number of effective latent features. Based on this, the paper develops the FAB/LFM algorithm, an alternating optimization procedure. The authors further provide a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation of the E-step, which inspires an acceleration technique. Finally, they analyze the parameter identifiability of their method and propose a feature-merging post-processing step. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data compare FAB/LFM to several baselines, including Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants, suggesting the proposed method achieves strong performance in terms of model selection and prediction with lower computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Extension:** The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to LFMs, an important and widely used class of models. The theoretical analysis to handle the non-block-diagonal Hessian (Lemma 1) is non-trivial and forms the core contribution of the work.
2.  **Algorithmic Refinements:** The connection drawn between the E-step and CCCP is insightful. It not only provides a deeper understanding of the optimization dynamics but also leads to a practical "shrinkage acceleration" algorithm that demonstrably reduces computation time (Figure 1).
3.  **Identifiability Analysis:** The discussion on parameter identifiability is a valuable addition. It addresses a known issue in LFMs and provides a theoretical basis (Theorem 4) for a sensible post-processing step (merging features), which is a sign of a carefully considered approach.

**Weaknesses:**

While the paper presents interesting ideas, it is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from minor presentational issues to significant problems in the experimental evaluation.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper lacks the polish expected for a top-tier publication.
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted, with numerous entries like "In ICML,, , ,". References [10] and [11] appear to be the same work.
    *   There are several grammatical errors and awkward phrases (e.g., "Further we conduct two analysis", "does not affect to the approximation").
    *   Notation is not always clearly defined before use (e.g., `Θ` appears in Eq. (10) without definition). The description of the E-step update (Eq. 11) as a "closed-form solution" is inaccurate; it is a set of coupled fixed-point equations.

2.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The paper's core theoretical claims hinge on Lemma 1 and Theorem 4, but their assumptions and proofs are relegated entirely to the supplementary material. The phrase "Under some mild assumptions" is used to justify Lemma 1, but without stating these assumptions in the main text, it is impossible for the reader to assess the result's validity or scope. A rigorous paper should, at a minimum, state the key assumptions for its main results.

3.  **Algorithmic Justification:** The proposed `accelerateShrinkage` algorithm (Algorithm 2) includes an ad-hoc step: "in practice, we update π along with π̃ for further acceleration". This modification is not justified by the CCCP interpretation. It is unclear if this preserves the monotonic ascent property of the original objective function `L`, and it makes the proposed acceleration seem less principled.

4.  **Fundamentally Flawed Experimental Comparison:** The experimental section contains critical flaws that undermine the paper's conclusions about performance.
    *   The most significant issue is the inconsistent treatment of the noise parameter `λ`. The authors state that competing methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) had their noise standard deviation fixed, while the proposed FAB/LFM was allowed to learn this parameter. This represents a major, unfair advantage for FAB/LFM. Improved predictive log-likelihood (PLL) could easily be a result of better noise modeling rather than better feature selection. A fair comparison requires all models to operate under the same conditions—either all learn the noise variance or all have it fixed.
    *   The handling of baselines is questionable. The IBP hyperparameter `α` was set to a single value with the caveat that it "might cause overestimation of K," which is not a substitute for proper tuning. Furthermore, MEIBP was omitted from the block data experiment because its "parameter setting was very sensitive," which is an insufficient reason to exclude a key state-of-the-art competitor.

5.  **Weakness in Theoretical Claims:** The identifiability argument (Theorem 4) states that `w*k = w*l` implies equal feature usage at a stationary point. While interesting, this does not prove that the algorithm avoids non-identifiable solutions, only that at one specific type of degenerate solution, the feature usages are balanced. The claim that merging features "increases L" is stated without proof.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the important problem of model selection in LFMs from a novel asymptotic Bayesian perspective.
*   The derivation showing that the LFM FIC resembles the MM FIC is an elegant theoretical result.
*   The CCCP-based acceleration method is a useful practical contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental evaluation is critically flawed due to an unfair comparison regarding the noise parameter, invalidating the central claims of superior predictive performance.
*   The paper suffers from poor presentation, including a sloppily formatted reference list and numerous grammatical errors.
*   Core theoretical results are not self-contained, with key assumptions and proofs entirely absent from the main body of the paper.
*   The handling of baseline methods in the experiments is unconvincing.

In its current state, the paper does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The weaknesses, particularly the flawed experimental setup, are too significant to ignore. While the underlying ideas have merit, the work requires a major revision to properly support its claims with a fair and rigorous evaluation, as well as a thorough cleaning of the presentation.
