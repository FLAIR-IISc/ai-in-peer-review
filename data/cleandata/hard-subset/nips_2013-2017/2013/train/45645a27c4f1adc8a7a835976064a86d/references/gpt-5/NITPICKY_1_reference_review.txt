Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary feature matrix Z and linear-Gaussian emission. The core theoretical claim is Lemma 1: despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition used in prior FAB work, the Hessian determinant of the per-dimension likelihood contributes a regularization term of the form sum_k log sum_n z_nk (up to asymptotically negligible terms), which leads to an FIC for LFMs identical in form to the one for mixtures. Theorem 2 then provides an asymptotic approximation of the marginal complete log-likelihood with a complexity penalty −(DK/2) log N − (D/2) sum_k log sum_n z_nk. On the algorithmic side, the paper derives a lower bound with a mean-field q(Z) and proposes an EM-like FAB algorithm: a fixed-point E-step update with an additional shrinkage term −D/(2N π̃_k) that encourages automatic pruning, closed-form M-step updates, an explicit shrinkage step, and a CCCP interpretation yielding an “accelerateShrinkage” routine. Finally, an identifiability result argues FAB favors smaller-K ML solutions and motivates a merge post-processing step. Experiments compare FAB with EM, IBP variants, VB, and MEIBP, reporting competitive model selection and predictive performance and improved speed via the acceleration.

Strengths
- Solid and interesting theoretical goal: extending FAB to LFMs that violate prior block-diagonal conditions. If correct, Lemma 1 is a nontrivial and valuable result that broadens applicability of FIC-style approximations.
- The FIC for LFMs matches the MM case, leading to automatic feature shrinkage without hand-tuned hyperparameters; this is attractive in practice.
- The CCCP view of the E-step is insightful and motivates a simple, effective acceleration that appears to reduce runtime substantially.
- The identifiability analysis and the merge post-processing provide a principled way to handle degeneracies (duplicate features), leveraging the regularizer to prefer smaller-K solutions.
- The algorithm is simple to implement (mean-field Bernoulli q(Z), PPCA-like M-step) and has a natural monotone lower-bound optimization structure (at least in the core FAB loop).

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Key theory deferred to the supplement. Lemma 1 is labeled as holding under “mild assumptions” with no statement of those assumptions or even a sketch. The precise form in Eq. (6) is ambiguous as typeset (“log det|F(d)| = sum_k log (sum_n z_nk)/N + O_p(1)? or something else?). Theorem 2 depends critically on this, and without a clear statement and main proof ideas, it is difficult to assess correctness.
- Signs/constant factors are inconsistent across equations. The transition from Lemma 1 to Theorem 2 (−DK/2 log N and −D/2 sum_k log sum_n z_nk) suggests a decomposition of the Hessian determinant into N and cluster-size terms; however Eq. (10) then contains “− 2D + K log N,” which does not match |P| or the earlier complexity accounting. This undermines confidence in the derivation of the practical lower bound.
- Mean-field approximation on Z is introduced for tractability, but the resulting bound no longer upper-bounds the marginal likelihood and monotonicity is only argued for some steps. The CCCP-based accelerateShrinkage updates modify π and π̃ jointly; the paper admits this is for “further acceleration,” but it is unclear whether global monotonic improvement of the same objective is preserved.
- Shrinkage/removal criteria use exact sums ∑n µ_nk/N ∈ {0,1}, which will almost never occur with sigmoids; in practice a threshold must be used and is not specified. This affects both correctness (the FIC ill-conditioning claim) and reproducibility.
- Identifiability/merging: Theorem 4 is again deferred to the supplement and stated with strong conditions; the proposed merge rule (set w_k ← 2w_k, µ_k ← µ_k+µ_l) is ad hoc unless fully justified (e.g., does it preserve the likelihood? how are π, λ updated? what about constraints on µ ∈ [0,1]?).
- Robustness to local optima is not discussed. Given the CCCP/E-step fixed-point nature and heavy shrinkage term, sensitivity to initialization and Tshrink could be substantial.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous typographical and notational issues make the paper hard to follow and raise concerns about precision:
  - Eq. (6) formatting ambiguous; Eq. (10) has unexplained “−2D + K log N”; Eq. (11) lacks parentheses (−D/2Nπ̃_k vs −D/(2Nπ̃_k)); Algorithm 2 line 2 shows “− 121 w>·k,” which appears to be a typesetting error for −(1/2) 1 w>·k; “mean-filed” instead of mean-field; inconsistent boldface and vector/matrix notation; multiple cases of missing subscripts/superscripts spacing.
  - P denotes both parameter set and probability density p(X,Z|P); sometimes Θ used instead of P.
  - Several claims use undefined terms or assumptions (“bounded joint marginal log-likelihood” in Theorem 2).
- Experimental section omits critical details: thresholds for shrinkage/merging, stopping criteria for accelerateShrinkage, number of CCCP inner iterations beyond Tshrink, and how µ is initialized exactly.

Originality
- Extending FAB/FIC to LFMs via a new Hessian determinant analysis is novel to my knowledge. The CCCP view is a nice connection, though similar expansions/linearizations are standard in mean-field contexts.
- Related asymptotic work (e.g., MAD-Bayes) is cited, but the distinction could be sharpened (noise-variance-to-zero vs large-N Laplace around ML in singular models). ARD-style penalties, spike-and-slab, or automatic feature selection approaches in linear-Gaussian latent factor models could be discussed as points of comparison.

Significance
- If the theoretical claims are correct, the result is significant: an automatic, hyperparameter-free model selection mechanism for LFMs with competitive performance. The acceleration is practically useful.
- However, experimental comparisons are not fully convincing:
  - Fairness: FAB learns λ while other baselines had fixed noise variance; this advantages FAB. Hyperparameters for IBP (e.g., α=3) are not tuned; VB is omitted due to runtime; MEIBP is declared inapplicable on negative data; some runs are truncated at 50 hours, which biases results without reporting partial progress or quality/runtime curves.
  - Reporting: No variance/error bars; often a single run or “median over 10” is shown without dispersion. Some datasets, settings, and preprocessing details are missing (Table 1 not visible here).
  - Task coverage: LFMs are often benchmarked on link prediction and binary matrices; here results focus on reconstruction and missing value imputation for real-valued data; no ablations on the impact of the CCCP acceleration vs core FAB, or sensitivity to N and D.

Pros
- Conceptually interesting extension of FAB to LFMs with a theoretically motivated penalty.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with an effective acceleration.
- Identifiability insight and merge post-processing address a real practical issue.
- Promising empirical results on several datasets.

Cons
- Critical theory and assumptions are hidden in the supplement; equation inconsistencies and notation errors hamper verification.
- Algorithmic monotonicity and correctness of acceleration/merging steps are not rigorously established.
- Experimental comparisons raise fairness and completeness concerns; limited reporting of variability.
- Many typographical/formatting/notation mistakes.

Questions for the authors
1. Please state the exact assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text and clarify Eq. (6) precisely (parentheses, dependence on N). Provide a sketch of the proof.
2. Reconcile Eq. (10) constants with Theorem 2: where do “−2D + K log N” come from? What is |P| here?
3. Does accelerateShrinkage (with simultaneous π, π̃ updates) preserve monotonic increase of the same lower bound? If not, can you bound degradation?
4. What thresholds do you use for removing features (µ̄_k≈0 or 1) and for merging (distance threshold)? How sensitive are results to these?
5. Can you provide results where baselines also learn noise variance, or fix FAB’s λ to match baselines, to ensure fairness?

Minor issues and typos (non-exhaustive)
- “mean-filed” → mean-field.
- Eq. (11): write −D/(2Nπ̃_k) explicitly.
- Algorithm 2 line 2: “−121w>·k” likely “−(1/2)1 w>·k”.
- Inconsistent use of boldface and vector notation; define 1 as an all-ones vector once.
- Clarify whether Op(1) is O_p(1) (probability) or deterministic O(1).
- Use consistent notation for parameters P vs Θ; define |P|.
- Several references missing years/venues; ensure complete citations.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper likely contains a solid idea, but the accumulation of technical ambiguities, inconsistent equations, lack of clearly stated assumptions, and fairness/reporting issues in experiments prevent me from confidently endorsing acceptance. I encourage the authors to clean up the presentation, move key assumptions/results into the main text, strengthen the theoretical guarantees for the accelerated steps, and provide fairer, more thorough empirical comparisons. Confidence: high.
