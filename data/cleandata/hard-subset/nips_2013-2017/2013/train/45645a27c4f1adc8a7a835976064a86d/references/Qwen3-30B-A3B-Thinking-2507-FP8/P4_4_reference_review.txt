

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), which previously did not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for FAB application. The authors prove that LFMs have the same Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) form as mixture models (MMs), despite not meeting the prior conditions, enabling FAB inference for LFMs with automatic model selection. Key contributions include theoretical analysis showing the equivalence of FIC for LFMs and MMs, a shrinkage acceleration method based on Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation, and identifiability analysis with merge post-processing.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical results (Lemma 1, Theorem 2, Theorem 4) are rigorously proven with appropriate assumptions, and the supplementary materials provide necessary details. The FAB/LFM algorithm monotonically increases the lower bound, guaranteeing convergence to a local maximum. The experiments comprehensively compare FAB/LFMs with relevant baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) on synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that FAB's asymptotic approximation may be less accurate for small N. The results consistently show FAB/LFMs achieve better model selection accuracy with lower computational cost than state-of-the-art methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The theoretical derivations follow a logical progression, with appropriate references to related work. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is described with precise pseudocode, and the experimental setup is adequately detailed. The CCCP interpretation of FAB updates is particularly well-explained, making a complex optimization method accessible. The paper provides sufficient information for expert reproducibility, with clear notation and well-structured sections.

**Originality:** The extension of FAB inference to LFMs is a significant theoretical contribution, as LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition that previously limited FAB application. The key insight that LFMs have the same FIC form as MMs despite this condition is novel and well-justified. The CCCP interpretation of FAB updates and the resulting shrinkage acceleration method represent a novel practical contribution. The identifiability analysis for LFMs and the merge post-processing step provide valuable theoretical insights not present in previous work. The paper properly contextualizes these contributions against relevant NIPS work [5,6] and IBP methods [10,11,22].

**Significance:** The paper addresses a critical problem in latent feature modeling: the high computational cost of model selection for LFMs. FAB/LFMs provide fully automated model selection without hyperparameters, with better accuracy and lower computational cost than existing methods. The shrinkage acceleration method is particularly impactful for large-scale applications. The results on real-world datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS) demonstrate practical utility, with FAB consistently achieving better predictive performance and more compact model representations. The theoretical insights into identifiability and model shrinkage have broader implications for latent variable models.

**Strengths:**
- Novel extension of FAB to LFMs with rigorous theoretical justification
- Practical algorithm with significant computational improvements through shrinkage acceleration
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets
- Clear theoretical insights into identifiability and model merging
- Well-organized and clearly written presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of FAB's performance in small-N scenarios where asymptotic approximation may be less accurate
- The paper does not extensively explore sensitivity to the shrinkage acceleration parameter Tshrink
- Some baselines (like VB) were excluded due to computational constraints, though this is reasonable given the computational focus

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. It successfully extends FAB inference to a broader class of models (LFMs) with theoretical justification, practical implementation, and empirical validation. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with clear significance for both theory and practice.

Pro: Novel theoretical extension with rigorous proofs; practical algorithm with significant computational benefits; comprehensive empirical validation; clear theoretical insights into identifiability.
Con: Limited discussion of small-N performance; some baselines excluded due to computational constraints.

The paper meets all criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission, with significant theoretical contributions and practical impact. It advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection for latent variable models in a demonstrable way.
