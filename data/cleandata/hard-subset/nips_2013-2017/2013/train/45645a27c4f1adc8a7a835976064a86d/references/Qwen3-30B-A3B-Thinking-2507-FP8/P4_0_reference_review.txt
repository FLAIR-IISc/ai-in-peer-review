

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously developed for mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models, to latent feature models (LFMs). The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications, they can derive FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) for LFMs that has the same form as for MMs. The key insight is Lemma 1, which shows that the log determinant of the Hessian contains a term log ∑ₙ zₙₖ that enables automatic model selection. The paper then develops an FAB algorithm for LFMs, with a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation, and proves parameter identifiability.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, a broader class of models than previously considered. The theoretical analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 2, Corollary 3) is rigorous and well-supported, with detailed proofs provided in supplementary materials. The authors correctly relate their work to previous FAB frameworks for MMs (Fujimaki & Morinaga, 2012; Fujimaki & Hayashi, 2013) and to related work on latent feature models (Griffiths & Ghahramani, 2006; Miller et al., 2009), demonstrating how their approach differs from non-parametric Bayesian methods like IBP and VB.

The paper's technical quality is strong. The derivation of FIC for LFMs is mathematically sound, and the theoretical claims are well-supported. The FAB/LFM algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly described, with the shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) based on CCCP interpretation providing a practical and effective implementation. The identifiability theorem (Theorem 4) is an important theoretical contribution that addresses a significant issue in latent variable models, and the merge post-processing provides a natural solution to the non-identifiability problem.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, comparing FAB/LFMs with state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) on artificial and real-world datasets. The results consistently demonstrate FAB/LFMs' advantages: better model selection accuracy (especially for larger N), lower computational cost, and reduced overfitting (smaller differences between training and predictive log-likelihoods). The shrinkage acceleration method significantly reduces computational cost without compromising accuracy, as shown in Figure 1.

The paper demonstrates significant practical impact by addressing a key limitation in latent feature modeling: the high computational cost of existing model selection methods. FAB/LFMs provide fully automated model selection without hyperparameters, which is particularly valuable for large-scale data. The theoretical insights (CCCP interpretation, identifiability) provide deeper understanding of the FAB framework and its applicability.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical derivation to algorithm development to experimental evaluation. The related work section appropriately contextualizes the contribution relative to previous NIPS publications.

The main weakness is limited explanation of why LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal condition still yield the same FIC form as MMs. While Lemma 1 provides the mathematical justification, the paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of this counterintuitive result. Additionally, the practical impact of the merge post-processing is not fully demonstrated in the real-world experiments, as it's only briefly mentioned in the artificial simulations.

The paper makes a significant contribution to Bayesian model selection by expanding FAB to LFMs. The theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method. The work advances the state of the art in model selection for latent feature models by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient method that achieves better model selection accuracy.

Strengths: 
- Novel extension of FAB to LFMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-supported proofs
- Practical shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation
- Strong experimental results demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods
- Clear organization and writing

Weaknesses:
- Limited intuitive explanation of why LFMs yield the same FIC form as MMs
- Incomplete demonstration of merge post-processing impact in real-world experiments

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to Bayesian inference and model selection, with both theoretical depth and practical utility. It meets all NIPS quality standards and represents a significant advance in the field. I recommend acceptance.
