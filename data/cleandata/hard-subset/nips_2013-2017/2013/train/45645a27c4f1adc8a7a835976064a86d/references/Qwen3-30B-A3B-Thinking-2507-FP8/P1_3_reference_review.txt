

# Peer Review: "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

## Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously applicable only to models with a block-diagonal Hessian structure (like Mixture Models), to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The key theoretical insight is Lemma 1, which demonstrates that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition that MMs do, their Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) takes the same form as for MMs. This enables FAB inference to automatically select the number of latent features without priors or hyperparameters. The paper develops an FAB/LFM algorithm with a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP, proves parameter identifiability, and introduces a merge post-processing step. Experimental results show FAB/LFM achieves superior model selection accuracy and computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods like IBP, VB, and MEIBP.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution (Originality & Significance):** The paper makes a significant theoretical advance by extending FAB inference to LFMs, a class of models that does not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition previously required. Lemma 1 is particularly novel as it demonstrates the log ∑n znk term naturally appears in log det |F(d)| without the block-diagonal assumptions, which is counterintuitive. This insight has broad implications for extending FAB to other latent variable models.

**Algorithmic Innovation:** The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) based on CCCP is well-motivated and empirically shown to drastically reduce computational cost without sacrificing accuracy. The paper demonstrates this through compelling computational time comparisons (Figure 1), showing that the acceleration enables early elimination of irrelevant features.

**Practical Impact:** The experimental results across artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets consistently show FAB/LFM achieves better model selection accuracy (especially for large N) and better predictive performance than competing methods, while maintaining low computational cost. The ability to automatically select compact models with minimal overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL differences) is particularly valuable for practitioners.

**Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous proofs for key theoretical claims (identifiability, FIC convergence) and carefully discusses the theoretical foundations of FAB's automatic model selection mechanism.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity of Key Insight:** While Lemma 1 is central to the paper, its derivation and implications could be made more accessible. The connection between the Hessian structure and the appearance of log ∑n znk without the block-diagonal condition needs a more intuitive explanation for readers less familiar with asymptotic Bayesian inference.

**Comparison Context:** The paper could better contextualize how FAB/LFM differs from existing methods beyond computational efficiency. For example, it states FAB/LFMs "have parameter identifiability" but doesn't clearly explain how this differs from identifiability properties of other methods or why it matters practically.

**Limitations Discussion:** The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss limitations. It would be valuable to address how the method performs with very high-dimensional data (D >> N), with non-Gaussian noise models, or with complex feature interactions that might violate the assumptions in Lemma 1.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough experimental validation. The proofs in supplementary materials appear rigorous, and the experiments are comprehensive across multiple datasets and comparison methods. The authors are honest about limitations (e.g., "FAB would perform worse than NPBs in small N scenarios").

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written but could improve in explaining key theoretical insights. The notation is sometimes inconsistent (e.g., using "N" for both number of samples and another quantity in different contexts), and the presentation of Algorithm 1 could benefit from more detailed comments about each step.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, which was previously an open problem. The connection to CCCP for acceleration is also novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly the FAB/MM work in [5,6].

**Significance:** This work is highly significant as it addresses a key limitation of FAB inference (applicability to only a narrow class of models) and extends it to LFMs, which are widely used in applications like link prediction. The computational efficiency gains with the shrinkage acceleration make the approach practical for large-scale data, which is a major barrier for current state-of-the-art methods like IBP.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that extends the applicability of FAB inference to a broader class of models. The algorithmic innovations and experimental results demonstrate clear practical benefits. The work advances the state of the art in Bayesian inference for latent variable models in a demonstrable way, with implications for both theory and practice.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Provide a more intuitive explanation of why Lemma 1 holds without the block-diagonal condition.
2. Clarify how FAB/LFM's parameter identifiability differs from other methods and why it matters.
3. Add a discussion of limitations, particularly for high-dimensional or non-Gaussian settings.
4. Improve the clarity of Algorithm 1 with more detailed comments about each step.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models, with both theoretical depth and practical utility. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
