This paper introduces Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference for Latent Feature Models (LFMs), extending previous work successful with Mixture Models (MMs). The core contribution is demonstrating that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions required for FAB (block-diagonal Hessian), the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) exhibits a similar form to that of MMs, enabling the application of FAB. The authors further present analysis relating FAB E-steps to a Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP), leading to a shrinkage acceleration method, and demonstrate parameter identifiability with implications for post-processing latent features.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, relying on Lemma 1, appears logically presented and the subsequent algorithm builds upon this foundation. The connection to CCCP is insightful and the proposed acceleration method seems well-motivated. The identifiability argument and resulting merging post-processing are also a valuable contribution. The theoretical claims are supported by experimental results, although the experimental setup could be more extensive (see weaknesses). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and discuss potential failure modes. The proofs are deferred to the supplementary materials, which is standard practice but necessitates a thorough review of that material for a complete assessment.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written, with a logical flow of ideas. However, the mathematical notation is dense, and some sections (particularly those involving the derivations) require careful reading. Providing more intuition behind the mathematical steps would improve accessibility. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical benefits of the FAB/LFM algorithm is reasonably well explained. The algorithmic descriptions are clear, but could benefit from pseudocode. The discussion of related work is comprehensive, adequately referencing relevant publications.

**Originality:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs is a novel contribution, as the initial FAB framework was limited to models with a block-diagonal Hessian. Demonstrating the applicability of FAB to a broader class of models is a significant advancement. The CCCP-inspired acceleration method and the identifiability analysis further enhance the originality of the work. The paper appropriately positions itself relative to existing methods like IBP and VB, highlighting the advantages of the FAB approach (automation, computational efficiency).

**Significance:** The work addresses a relevant problem—efficient model selection and inference in LFMs—and provides a promising solution. The potential for reduced computational cost compared to existing methods (IBP, VB) is significant, especially for large-scale datasets. The automatic model selection capability is also valuable. The theoretical analysis of identifiability and the proposed merging post-processing offer a deeper understanding of the model and potential improvements in performance. The experimental results, while not exhaustive, suggest that FAB/LFMs are competitive with state-of-the-art methods. The paper could potentially influence future research in Bayesian nonparametrics and latent variable modeling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension of FAB:** Successfully extends FAB inference to a new class of models (LFMs).
*   **Theoretical Soundness:**  Provides a rigorous derivation of the FIC for LFMs and supports it with a crucial lemma.
*   **Computational Efficiency:**  Introduces a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP, reducing computational cost.
*   **Identifiability Analysis:** Offers insights into parameter identifiability and proposes a merging post-processing strategy.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Generally well-written and organized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Mathematical Density:**  The mathematical derivations can be challenging to follow without a strong background in the relevant areas.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section could be more extensive. The number of datasets is relatively small, and the comparison with other methods is not always comprehensive. A wider range of datasets and more detailed ablation studies would strengthen the results.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of different parameter settings (e.g., δ, Tshrink) on the performance of the algorithm is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Supplementary Materials Reliance:** A significant portion of the theoretical justification and experimental details are relegated to the supplementary materials, making it difficult to fully assess the paper without a detailed review of those materials.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of FAB inference to LFMs.
*   The proposed acceleration method and identifiability analysis are valuable contributions.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the competitiveness of FAB/LFMs.
*   The work addresses a relevant problem and has the potential to impact the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical density and reliance on supplementary materials may hinder accessibility.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly investigated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and latent variable modeling. The technical soundness, originality, and potential significance of the work are compelling. While the paper has some weaknesses, they are not substantial enough to warrant rejection. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, primarily focusing on improving the clarity of the mathematical derivations and expanding the experimental evaluation. Specifically, the authors should consider adding pseudocode for the algorithms and providing more detailed ablation studies in the supplementary materials.
