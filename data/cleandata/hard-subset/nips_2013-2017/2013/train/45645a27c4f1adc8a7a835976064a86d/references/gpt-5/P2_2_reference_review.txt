Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian observations. The core technical claim is Lemma 1: the log-determinant of the Hessian for each output dimension’s parameters contributes, asymptotically, a sum over features of log(∑n znk), even though LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumptions used in prior FAB work. This yields an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) that has the same form as for mixture models, leading to a practical FAB algorithm: a tractable lower bound using a linearization of −log(∑n znk) and mean-field q(Z). The E-step updates have a CCCP interpretation, which motivates a shrinkage-acceleration routine. The authors also analyze identifiability, showing FAB favors balanced feature usage and justifying a merge post-processing. Experiments on synthetic and several real datasets compare FAB to IBP (Gibbs), VB (limited), and MEIBP, showing competitive or superior model selection and predictive log-likelihood with reduced K and reasonable runtime, especially with acceleration.

Quality
- Theory: The main theoretical contribution is the asymptotic equivalence (Lemma 1 → Theorem 2) that recovers the same FIC regularizer as in mixtures. This is plausible and interesting, but the paper relies critically on assumptions deferred to the supplement and described in the main text only as “mild.” For a NeurIPS audience, at least a clear statement of the assumptions, their scope (e.g., scaling regimes for N, K, and usage counts), and a proof sketch or intuition are needed in the main paper to assess soundness. Similarly, the step from (7) to the practical lower bound (10) combines Laplace asymptotics, linearization, and mean-field; the effect on approximation tightness and convergence guarantees deserves more explicit discussion.
- Algorithm: The E-step fixed-point is standard for mean-field Bernoulli-Gaussian LFMs plus the FAB-induced shrinkage term D/(2Nπ̃k). The CCCP view is correct and gives a neat explanation of why repeated inner CCCP steps accelerate shrinkage. The identifiability theorem (Theorem 4) addresses an important issue (non-uniqueness of ML solutions) and the merge heuristic is reasonable, but again conditions are deferred; the guarantee that merging strictly increases the bound when W columns coincide should be stated precisely.
- Experiments: The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate the shrinkage acceleration effect and FAB’s ability to recover K for larger N. On real data, FAB often attains better PLL with smaller K. However, fairness is a concern: the baselines’ codes do not learn the noise variance, while FAB estimates Λ. Fixing σ for baselines but learning it for FAB can materially advantage FAB in likelihood metrics. Either ensure all methods learn σ or fix Λ across methods and compare other metrics (e.g., RMSE/Brier on held-out entries) to avoid this confound. VB is omitted on large datasets due to runtime; that is understandable, but results would be stronger with a smaller dataset where VB is included. Reporting per-iteration complexity and scaling (in N, D, K) would also help substantiate the “drastically reduces” cost claim.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with a coherent progression from asymptotics to algorithm to analysis and experiments. Key equations are clear, and the CCCP interpretation is illuminating. That said:
- State the assumptions of Lemma 1 in the main text and give intuition (e.g., effective sample sizes per feature usage contributing to curvature).
- Clarify the regime of Corollary 3 (how K may grow with N) and what the Op(1) hides.
- Detail the merge criterion and prove monotonic improvement in the bound under the stated conditions; specify the distance threshold used.
- Discuss initialization sensitivity (K initialized to min(N,D), Tshrink) and stopping criteria in more depth.

Originality
- Extending FAB to LFMs that violate prior block-diagonal conditions, and showing the FIC maintains the same log-usage penalty, is a novel and interesting theoretical result.
- The CCCP lens leading to a practical shrinkage-acceleration is a nice, original insight.
- The identifiability analysis plus merge post-processing is a useful contribution in a space where non-identifiability is common.
Related work is appropriately cited (IBP variants, MEIBP, MAD-Bayes), though a more direct empirical comparison to modern variational IBP/stick-breaking methods would strengthen positioning.

Significance
Automated model selection for LFMs with competitive accuracy and lower computational and tuning burden than nonparametric Bayesian methods is valuable. If the asymptotic argument is correct and the empirical gains hold under fair conditions, this can impact practitioners who need scalable binary latent feature models. The theoretical message that FIC extends beyond block-diagonal Hessians could motivate further applications of FAB.

Pros
- Conceptually neat and potentially generalizable asymptotic result (FIC form persists for LFMs).
- Practical algorithm with closed-form updates and an effective acceleration scheme.
- Identifiability discussion and merge post-processing address a real pain point.
- Empirical results suggest good model selection and predictive performance with compact K.

Cons
- Critical assumptions and proof details are not presented in the main paper, limiting the ability to assess soundness.
- Experimental fairness issues around noise variance learning; some baselines disadvantaged.
- Limited inclusion of VB results and lack of thorough scalability characterization.
- Merge step is heuristic in practice; conditions and thresholds need clearer specification.

Questions and suggestions for authors
1) State the precise assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text and provide a short proof sketch/intuition. Under what scaling of K and feature usage counts does Op(1) remain bounded?
2) Does the FIC approximation require that all features have nontrivial usage (∑n znk neither 0 nor N)? How is the approximation impacted during shrinkage when some π̃k approaches 0 or 1?
3) Please harmonize noise variance treatment across methods or add metrics less sensitive to σ. Can you re-run FAB with fixed σ to match baselines and report PLL/RMSE?
4) Report per-iteration complexity and wall-clock scaling vs. N, D, K; include an ablation of Tshrink and merge.
5) Provide sensitivity to initialization (K0, µ) and a small-data regime where FIC may be less accurate.

Overall assessment
A strong and promising paper with a novel theoretical extension of FAB and a well-motivated, practical algorithm. I lean toward acceptance if the authors clarify the assumptions/proofs in the main text and address the experimental fairness; otherwise, it is borderline due to evaluation concerns rather than conceptual flaws.
