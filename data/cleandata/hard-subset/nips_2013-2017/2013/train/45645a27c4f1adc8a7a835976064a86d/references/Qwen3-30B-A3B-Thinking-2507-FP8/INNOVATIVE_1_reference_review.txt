

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a class of models that do not satisfy the block diagonal Hessian condition required by previous FAB applications to mixture models. The authors demonstrate that despite this apparent mismatch, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models, which is a striking theoretical insight. The key novelty lies in Lemma 1, which shows that the log determinant of the Hessian matrix (which determines model complexity in FIC) asymptotically takes the same form for LFMs as for MMs, despite LFMs lacking the structural assumptions that previously enabled this form.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs, (2) A novel CCCP-based interpretation of the FAB E-step that enables a shrinkage acceleration method, (3) Theoretical guarantees about parameter identifiability and a merge post-processing procedure, and (4) Comprehensive empirical validation showing FAB/LFMs outperform state-of-the-art methods in model selection accuracy while being computationally more efficient.

The paper builds upon the authors' previous work on FAB for mixture models [5, 6] and correctly situates itself within the broader context of latent feature models and nonparametric Bayesian methods [9, 10, 17, 22]. The relationship to related work is well-explained, particularly regarding how FAB differs from other approaches like Variational Bayesian inference [3] and Maximum-Expectation IBP [22].

**Strengths:**
1. **High novelty:** The theoretical insight that FIC takes the same form for LFMs as for MMs despite LFMs violating the assumptions that previously led to this form is genuinely novel and significant. This fundamentally changes how we view the applicability of FAB inference.
2. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides rigorous proofs (in supplementary materials) for all key claims, including the asymptotic equivalence of FICs and the identifiability guarantees.
3. **Practical impact:** The shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP significantly reduces computational cost in practice, making FAB/LFMs applicable to larger-scale problems.
4. **Comprehensive evaluation:** The experimental section thoroughly compares FAB/LFMs with multiple state-of-the-art methods across synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating clear advantages in model selection accuracy and computational efficiency.
5. **Theoretical insights:** The identifiability analysis and merge post-processing procedure provide valuable theoretical insights that go beyond mere algorithmic improvement.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The explanation of why the block diagonal Hessian condition was previously necessary for FAB applications could be clearer to better highlight the novelty of the extension to LFMs.
2. While the paper demonstrates computational speedups, a more detailed Big-O analysis of the complexity would strengthen the claims.
3. The limitations of the shrinkage acceleration method (e.g., potential for suboptimal convergence in certain cases) could be more thoroughly discussed.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by extending FAB inference to a new class of models (LFMs) that were previously thought to be outside FAB's scope. The theoretical insight that FIC takes the same form for LFMs as for MMs despite LFMs violating the structural assumptions is remarkable and opens up new possibilities for applying FAB to other latent variable models. The practical implementation of the shrinkage acceleration method and the identifiability analysis further strengthen the paper's contribution.

The experimental results robustly support the theoretical claims, showing that FAB/LFMs achieve superior model selection accuracy compared to state-of-the-art methods while being computationally more efficient. The paper's focus on automation (no hand-tunable hyperparameters) is particularly valuable for practitioners.

The paper excels in originality, with the core theoretical insight being genuinely novel and significant. The quality of the theoretical analysis is high, and the experimental validation is thorough. While the paper could benefit from slightly clearer exposition of the theoretical contribution and more detailed computational complexity analysis, these are minor issues that do not detract from the paper's overall significance.

Given my preference for novel theoretical insights that open new directions in the field, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS. The extension of FAB to LFMs represents a significant conceptual advance that will likely inspire future work on applying asymptotic Bayesian methods to other latent variable models. The paper makes a clear and compelling case for the importance and novelty of its contribution.

**Recommendation: Accept**
