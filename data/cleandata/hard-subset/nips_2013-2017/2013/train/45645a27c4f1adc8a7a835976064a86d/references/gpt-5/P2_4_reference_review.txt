Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference—previously developed for mixture models and HMMs—to latent feature models (LFMs). The core theoretical contribution is Lemma 1: despite the lack of block-diagonal Hessian structure that enabled earlier FAB derivations, the authors show that the Hessian determinant for each output dimension contributes an O(∑k log ∑n znk) term, yielding an FIC for LFMs identical in form to that for MMs. This leads to Theorem 2 and Corollary 3: the derived FIC is an asymptotically tight approximation to the marginal log-likelihood. Algorithmically, the paper proposes a tractable lower bound (using a convex linearization of the log ∑n znk term and a mean-field approximation on Z), derives a fixed-point E-step, shows a CCCP interpretation, and proposes a shrinkage acceleration procedure that encourages early elimination of irrelevant features. A further contribution is an identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) showing FAB prefers balanced solutions and motivates a merge post-processing step. Experiments compare FAB/LFM with IBP (sampling and VB), MEIBP, and EM across synthetic, block-structured images, and several real datasets, showing competitive or better model selection and predictive performance at lower or comparable compute.

This work builds on and contrasts with: FAB for MMs/HMMs (Fujimaki et al.), IBP and its scalable variants (Doshi-Velez et al., Reed & Ghahramani), VB LFM, and MAP-asymptotic approaches (MAD-Bayes). The key novelty is extending FAB to a class (LFMs) that violates previous structural assumptions, while preserving the beneficial automatic model complexity term and providing new insights (CCCP, identifiability).

Quality
Strengths:
- Theoretical development is clear at a high level: Lemma 1 explains why the same FIC complexity penalty arises without block-diagonal Hessian assumptions. The asymptotic equivalence to the marginal likelihood (Theorem 2, Corollary 3) mirrors prior FAB results and justifies FAB-asymptotics for LFMs.
- The lower-bound optimization and E/M updates are standard and sound, and the CCCP interpretation is elegant; the connection justifies the shrinkage-acceleration routine (multiple CCCP steps) and explains why early feature elimination reduces runtime.
- The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) addresses a real pathology of LFMs (non-unique ML solutions when features coincide) and motivates a principled merge step.

Concerns:
- Key assumptions for Lemma 1 are deferred to the supplement and are only described as “mild.” Theorem 2 requires bounded joint marginal log-likelihood and ignores O(1) constants; it would help to state conditions (e.g., on Λ, π bounds, W norms, eigenvalue growth) in the main text to delineate the regime where the approximation holds.
- The claim of monotonic increase in the FIC lower bound with alternating updates is standard for coordinate ascent; however, the mean-field restriction changes the nature of the bound (Eq. 1 equality no longer holds). It would be good to state explicitly which steps are guaranteed to increase L after introducing mean-field and after taking multiple CCCP sub-steps and the accelerateShrinkage routine.
- The merge step is heuristic (distance-based) and checked by computing L; acceptable in practice, but the conditions under which merges strictly increase L beyond the exact equality case w·k = w·l could be clarified.
- Fairness of comparisons: competitors’ noise variance is fixed (per released code/prior work), whereas FAB learns Λ. Although the authors try to be fair by picking the true/standard setting, this may advantage FAB on real data; a sensitivity analysis in an appendix would help.
- Complexity: the paper lacks a per-iteration time/memory complexity, and sensitivity to Tshrink is only anecdotally addressed. Reporting wall-clock vs N,D,K and ablations on Tshrink would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- Overall well written and organized. The motivation, derivations, and algorithm are understandable. Some typos/formatting issues exist, but do not impede comprehension.
- Please move a concise statement of Lemma 1’s assumptions and a sketch of its proof idea into the main text (e.g., why log det F(d) decomposes into ∑k log counts), and explicitly list the steps guaranteeing monotonicity under the mean-field/CCCP-accelerated scheme.
- Provide a brief complexity discussion and a small ablation on Tshrink and the merge step threshold.

Originality
- Novel extension of FAB beyond models with block-diagonal complete-data Hessians, with a new asymptotic result (Lemma 1) specific to LFMs.
- The CCCP reinterpretation and shrinkage acceleration are original in this context and practically useful.
- The identifiability theorem and its connection to feature merging add theoretical and algorithmic value.

Significance
- LFMs are widely used; an automated, prior-insensitive, scalable model selection method is valuable. The asymptotic FIC with automatic log ∑n znk regularization has practical impact (smaller K, reduced overfitting).
- Experiments show improved model selection and predictive likelihood on several datasets, with strong shrinkage (smaller K) and competitive runtime. MEIBP is sometimes faster, but FAB tends to give better PLL and more compact models.
- Theoretical insights (asymptotics, CCCP, identifiability) are likely to be used beyond this specific algorithm.

Pros
- Strong theoretical contribution (FIC for LFMs) with practical algorithm.
- Elegant CCCP view; effective shrinkage acceleration.
- Addresses identifiability and offers principled merging.
- Good empirical performance and automation (no delicate hyperparameters).

Cons
- Assumptions and proof details essential to Lemma 1 are not visible in the main text.
- Fairness/rigor of empirical comparisons could be improved (noise variance, complexity reporting, VB omitted due to runtime).
- Limited discussion of applicability beyond Gaussian LFMs with diagonal precision and Bernoulli priors.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The combination of a substantive theoretical extension, practical algorithmic refinements, and solid empirical evidence meets the NIPS criteria. Addressing the clarity and evaluation concerns above would further strengthen the paper.
