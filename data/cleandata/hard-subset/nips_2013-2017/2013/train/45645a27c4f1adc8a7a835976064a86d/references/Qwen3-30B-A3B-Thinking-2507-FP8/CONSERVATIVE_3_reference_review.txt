

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), claiming to provide a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient method for automatic model selection (determining the number of latent features). The authors build on their previous work on FAB for mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models, arguing that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for FAB, they derive a similar FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) for LFMs. The paper claims FAB/LFMs achieve better model selection accuracy with lower computational cost than state-of-the-art methods like IBP, VB, and MEIBP.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which shows that log det |F(d)| for LFMs has the same form as for MMs (with the log ∑_n z_nk term), despite LFMs not satisfying the conditions (A1 and A2) that naturally induce this form for MMs. This leads to Theorem 2, claiming that FIC for LFMs has the same representation as for MMs, and Corollary 3, claiming FIC converges to the marginal log-likelihood. The authors further propose a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation and a merge post-processing step for parameter identifiability.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical analysis of the Hessian for LFMs is detailed, and the connection to CCCP provides an interesting perspective on the optimization.
2. The paper demonstrates a theoretically grounded approach to model selection without hyperparameters.
3. The merge post-processing for identifiability is a useful practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient theoretical validation:** The paper claims FAB/LFMs "converge to a marginal log-likelihood" but provides no guidance on the sample size required for the asymptotic approximation to be valid. The empirical results show improvement only for large N (e.g., Figure 2), but the paper fails to address whether the approximation is meaningful for practical N (e.g., N=1000-10,000) where the asymptotic behavior might not hold. This is a critical gap since the paper claims to offer a "computationally tractable" method.

2. **Incomplete experimental validation:** The experiments are limited to synthetic data and small real-world datasets (yaleB, USPS, EEG, Piano, Libras), with no demonstration on truly large-scale data where the claimed computational advantages would be most valuable. The paper claims "high computational costs restrict [NPB] applications to large-scale data," yet the largest dataset they use (USPS) has only 1000 samples, which is not large-scale by modern standards. The computational speed comparisons are not presented with sufficient detail (e.g., no runtime per sample or scaling with N).

3. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims FAB "achieves as good or even better model selection accuracy" without providing statistical significance tests. Figure 2 shows FAB outperforms MEIBP for large N, but the improvement is marginal (e.g., 1-2% in K estimation accuracy), and the paper fails to address whether this small improvement is practically meaningful. The claim that FAB "consistently achieved good predictive performance" is undermined by the fact that for small N (Libras), FAB performed worse than NPB methods.

4. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art:** The paper omits VB from the main results (stating "we totally omitted VB because of its long computational time"), but this is the most direct competitor to FAB. The authors compare only to IBP, MEIBP, and EM, but not to the most recent state-of-the-art methods for LFMs. The paper cites "state-of-the-art IBP methods" but does not provide a fair comparison.

**Critical concerns:**
The paper's core claim—that FAB can be applied to LFMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition—relies on Lemma 1, which requires "mild assumptions" (see supplementary materials). However, the paper does not discuss whether these assumptions hold in practice for typical LFM applications. The paper also fails to explain why the log ∑_n z_nk term appears in the LFM case despite not satisfying A1 and A2, which is crucial for understanding the method's applicability.

The "shrinkage acceleration" (Algorithm 2) is presented as a key innovation, but the paper does not provide a rigorous analysis of how many iterations are needed for effective shrinkage or how this acceleration affects convergence guarantees. The empirical results (Figure 1) show reduced computational cost, but without runtime breakdowns, it's unclear whether the acceleration is due to fewer iterations or faster per-iteration computation.

**Conclusion:**
While the theoretical extension to LFMs is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of superior performance and computational efficiency. The asymptotic assumptions are not validated for practical sample sizes, the experimental validation is limited to small datasets, and the claims are overstated without proper statistical analysis. The paper makes a modest contribution to the literature on latent feature models but fails to provide compelling evidence that it represents a significant advancement over existing methods.

I recommend rejection because the paper does not sufficiently validate its core claims against established baselines on appropriately scaled datasets. The method may be of interest to a niche community, but without rigorous evidence of its practical advantages, it does not meet NIPS's standard for significant contributions to the field. The authors should provide more thorough experimental validation on larger datasets and clarify the practical conditions under which the asymptotic approximations are valid before the method can be considered for publication.
