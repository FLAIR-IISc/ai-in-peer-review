### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a model selection method that operates by maximizing a computationally tractable lower bound of the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which is an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. Previous work on FAB for models like Mixture Models (MMs) and Hidden Markov Models relied on a critical assumption that the Hessian matrix of the complete-data log-likelihood is block-diagonal. The central challenge addressed in this paper is that LFMs do not satisfy this assumption, as parameters (e.g., the basis matrix W) are shared across all latent features, creating dependencies.

The core technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) showing that despite the lack of a block-diagonal Hessian, the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs decomposes into a sum of terms of the form `log(sum_n z_nk)`. This result is significant because it allows the derivation of an FIC for LFMs that has the same functional form as the one for MMs. Based on this FIC, the paper develops the FAB/LFM algorithm, an iterative procedure that performs model selection by automatically shrinking the number of active latent features via a unique regularization term that arises from the Bayesian marginalization.

The authors further introduce two important refinements. First, by interpreting a key update step as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), they propose an acceleration technique that speeds up the shrinkage of irrelevant features, reducing overall computation time. Second, a theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) shows that the FAB objective resolves ambiguities inherent to maximum likelihood estimation for LFMs, providing a principled motivation for a post-processing step that merges redundant learned features. The FAB/LFM method is presented as a computationally efficient alternative to non-parametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and is empirically validated against IBP variants and a variational EM baseline on various datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The paper's primary contribution—extending the FAB framework to a class of models that violate its original structural assumptions—is highly novel. The theoretical result in Lemma 1, demonstrating that the characteristic FIC penalty term emerges even with shared parameters, is a significant insight that broadens the potential applicability of FAB inference. This work represents a non-trivial theoretical advancement over the foundational FAB papers [5, 6].

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs from the asymptotic analysis of the Hessian is the cornerstone of the work and appears solid. The connection to the CCCP framework is insightful, providing a clear rationale for the proposed shrinkage acceleration algorithm. Furthermore, the analysis of parameter identifiability provides strong theoretical justification for the method's robustness compared to standard Maximum Likelihood Estimation and motivates a practical merging post-processing step.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized. It effectively communicates the motivation, presents the main technical challenge, and logically develops the solution and subsequent algorithmic refinements. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to both prior FAB research and competing methods for LFM model selection, such as IBP.

4.  **Significance and Impact:** The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is a practical and compelling method for learning the structure of LFMs. The experiments show that it is competitive with, and often superior to, state-of-the-art non-parametric methods in terms of predictive accuracy and computational efficiency, while consistently finding more parsimonious models. The ability to perform model selection in an automated fashion, without sensitive hyper-parameter tuning, makes it an attractive tool for practitioners.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

1.  **Reliance on Asymptotic Assumptions:** The entire framework is built upon large-sample (N → ∞) asymptotics. While the empirical results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the practical limitations of this approach. For example, guidance on how large N needs to be relative to the data dimensionality D and the number of features K for the `Op(1)` approximations to hold would be valuable for users. The authors rightly note that performance can degrade in small-N scenarios (e.g., the Libras dataset), and a clearer characterization of this limitation is warranted.

2.  **Details of Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental evaluation, while extensive, could be strengthened in a few areas to ensure a perfectly fair comparison.
    *   The noise variance was learned by the FAB and EM methods but fixed for the IBP, VB, and MEIBP baselines. This gives the proposed method an additional degree of freedom, which might contribute to its better performance. A more controlled comparison would involve either learning this parameter for all methods or fixing it for all.
    *   The choice of hyperparameters for baselines, such as setting `alpha=3` for IBP, is acknowledged by the authors as potentially suboptimal. While justified by following previous work, this could place the baseline methods at a disadvantage.
    *   The paper champions the method's automation, but parameters such as the number of inner shrinkage iterations `T_shrink` and the initial number of features K are introduced. A discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance and convergence speed to these choices would improve the paper's practical value.

3.  **Reproducibility:** Key details, including the precise assumptions for Lemma 1 and the algorithm for the merging post-processing, are deferred to the supplementary materials. While standard for conference submissions, this makes a complete verification of the paper's technical claims from the main text alone challenging.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant theoretical extension of the FAB framework with strong empirical results. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the scope of the discussion and experimental details rather than any fundamental flaws. The work is a solid contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS.
