Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs). The core technical claim is Lemma 1: asymptotically, the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood contributes a complexity term that equals (up to Op(1)) a sum of log feature-usage counts, ∑k log ∑n znk, even though LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition used in prior FAB work. This yields an FIC for LFMs with the same form as for mixtures, enabling automatic feature selection via a shrinkage term. The paper derives a tractable lower bound using linearization of the concave complexity term and a mean-field approximation on Z, leading to an alternating optimization (FAB E/M) algorithm. It further interprets the E-step as a CCCP update and proposes a “shrinkage acceleration” that applies multiple CCCP steps per iteration to eliminate irrelevant features earlier. Finally, it proves an identifiability result that biases toward balanced feature usage and motivates a merge postprocessing. Experiments on synthetic, block data, and several real datasets compare FAB/LFM against IBP (Gibbs), VB, and MEIBP, showing competitive or better model selection and predictive log-likelihood with lower or comparable runtime, especially with the acceleration.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The direction is compelling. The main step is Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 linking the marginal complete-data log-likelihood to an FIC having the same complexity term as in mixtures. However, the paper defers key assumptions and proofs to the supplement. The validity of Lemma 1 (which is nontrivial because the complete-data Hessian couples all features via shared θd and violates earlier block-diagonal conditions) is central; having only “mild assumptions” in the main text is insufficient for readers to assess scope. The results also rely on diagonal noise precision and a linear-Gaussian likelihood; it would help to state explicitly that the FIC result holds in this specific setting and discuss extensions.
- Optimization correctness: The lower bound uses (i) a linearization of −log ∑n znk at π̃k and (ii) replacing the MLE by joint maximization (standard FAB practice). The mean-field restriction on q(Z) makes the bound tractable; the paper acknowledges this departs from the exact FIC but argues asymptotically constant distortion. The fixed-point E-step (Eq. 11) is standard and increases the bound; the CCCP perspective (Eq. 14) is clean. The algorithm claims monotone increase of the bound, but accelerateShrinkage updates π, π̃ within inner loops; please clarify that each inner CCCP step provably increases L, and that updating π along with π̃ preserves monotonicity. Shrink and merge steps are said to increase L; in practice the merge is only applied if L improves, which is sound.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are broadly supportive (automatic model selection, competitive PLL, faster with acceleration). Some fairness concerns remain: baselines do not learn noise variance (fixed to constants); not tuning IBP’s α and other hyperparameters may bias comparisons; VB is omitted on large data due to runtime but no smaller-scale VB comparison is shown; MEIBP is omitted on negative data. Addressing these by (i) including sensitivity analyses or tuned baselines (e.g., grid over noise variance/α), (ii) showing VB results on smaller datasets, and (iii) reporting wall-clock vs. accuracy trade-offs would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The link from Lemma 1 to Theorem 2 is clear, and the CCCP interpretation is illuminating. A few points could be improved:
- State the assumptions of Lemma 1 in the main paper (even briefly) and give a short proof sketch to build intuition for why the “log-count” term emerges despite feature coupling.
- Explicitly state the model scope (linear-Gaussian with diagonal precision) and discuss whether/how the argument would change with full covariance or non-Gaussian likelihoods.
- Clarify monotonicity guarantees when using accelerateShrinkage and simultaneous π, π̃ updates.
- Provide the exact conditions under which shrink/merge provably increase L; for “near 0/1” usage, specify thresholds.

Originality
- Conceptually novel: showing that FIC for LFMs coincides in form with mixtures, despite lack of block-diagonal structure, is a nice and non-obvious asymptotic result.
- Methodologically: the FAB formulation for LFMs and the CCCP-based acceleration are well-motivated, though CCCP itself is standard; the identifiability analysis and the merge heuristic are thoughtful.
- Relation to prior: The positioning vs. IBP/VB and MAD-Bayes is appropriate; related work is well cited.

Significance
Automatic, hyperparameter-free feature selection for LFMs with a principled Bayesian asymptotic justification is valuable. If the theoretical result holds under the claimed conditions, it broadens FAB’s applicability beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians. Practically, the method seems competitive in speed and accuracy, especially with the acceleration, and the identifiability perspective is useful. The impact would be higher with stronger baselines and a clearer account of limitations (small-N regimes, non-Gaussian models).

Pros
- Novel asymptotic result (Lemma 1) leading to an FIC for LFMs identical in form to mixtures.
- Practical, simple algorithm with a clear shrinkage mechanism and an effective CCCP-based acceleration.
- Automatic model selection without sensitive priors/hyperparameters.
- Identifiability analysis and principled merge postprocessing.
- Empirical results generally favorable.

Cons
- Critical assumptions and proof details deferred to the supplement; scope not explicit in main text.
- Some ambiguity about monotonicity guarantees with accelerated updates and merging.
- Baseline comparisons could be fairer (noise variance/hyperparameters not tuned; VB mostly omitted).
- Limited discussion of failure modes (e.g., small N) and generality beyond diagonal Gaussian LFMs.

Questions for authors
1) Please list the exact assumptions of Lemma 1 in the main paper and provide a brief proof sketch. Does the result require diagonal Λ and independent dimensions?
2) Does Theorem 2/FIC carry over to non-Gaussian observation models or full-covariance noise?
3) Can you provide monotonicity guarantees for accelerateShrinkage when updating π, π̃ inside the inner loop?
4) How sensitive is performance to Tshrink and the initialization of µ? Provide an ablation.
5) Could you include tuned IBP (α) and a small-scale VB comparison, possibly with a grid over noise variance for fairness?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important gap, presents a compelling asymptotic result, and delivers a practical algorithm with clear benefits. With stronger presentation of assumptions/proofs and fairer baselines, this would be a solid NeurIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying theoretical conditions and tightening the empirical comparison.
