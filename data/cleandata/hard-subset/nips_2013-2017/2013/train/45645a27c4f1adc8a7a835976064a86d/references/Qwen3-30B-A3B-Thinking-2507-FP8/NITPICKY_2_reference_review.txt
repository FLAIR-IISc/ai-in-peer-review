

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), demonstrating that FAB can be applied to models that do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications. The authors claim that FAB/LFMs achieve automatic model selection (determining the number of latent features), have desirable theoretical properties including convergence to marginal log-likelihood, and offer computational advantages over state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by showing that the FIC for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition. The derivation of Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 is technically sound, and the CCCP interpretation of the FAB algorithm provides valuable theoretical insights. The experimental results demonstrate clear computational advantages over competing methods, particularly with large datasets, and the shrinkage acceleration technique appears effective. The paper also provides valuable theoretical insights about parameter identifiability in LFMs.

## Weaknesses
Despite the promising theoretical contributions, the paper suffers from several significant technical and presentation flaws that undermine its credibility:

1. **Critical Omission of Lemma 1**: The paper states "The following lemma plays the most important role in our derivation" but never actually states Lemma 1. This is a major flaw in the paper's presentation and makes it impossible to verify the core theoretical contribution.

2. **Inconsistent Notation and Formatting**: The paper contains numerous notational inconsistencies. For example, in Equation (2), the notation "det |FZ |1/2" should be "det |F_Z|^{1/2}" for consistency with other notations. In Equation (4), "logNπ̃k" should be "log(Nπ̃_k)" for clarity. These inconsistencies make the paper difficult to follow.

3. **Technical Inaccuracies**: The paper claims "FAB/LFMs have parameter identifiability" but Theorem 4 only provides a sufficient condition for identifiability, not a proof that identifiability holds. The statement "log p(X,Z|M) = J(Z, P̂) +Op(1)" in Theorem 2 is problematic as it's unclear how the log(∑_n z_nk) term behaves asymptotically.

4. **Algorithmic Errors**: In Algorithm 1, step 10 states "if dim(W′) = dim(W) then Converge" but this should be "if dim(W') = dim(W) - 1" or similar to correctly check for convergence.

5. **Experimental Limitations**: The paper claims FAB "consistently achieved good predictive performance (higher PLL) with low computational cost" but provides no statistical significance testing for these claims. The statement "We also investigated the effect of merge post-processing, but none was observed in this small example" lacks sufficient detail to be meaningful.

6. **Terminology and Grammar**: The paper contains grammatical errors such as "affect to the approximation" (should be "affect the approximation") and "two analysis" (should be "two analyses").

7. **Incomplete Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims FAB/LFMs "offer several desirable properties" but doesn't clearly establish why these properties are advantageous for practical applications of LFMs.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical extension of FAB inference to LFMs, the numerous technical and presentation flaws significantly undermine its contribution. The omission of Lemma 1 is particularly concerning as it's central to the paper's main claim. The paper's technical errors, inconsistent notation, and grammatical issues make it difficult to assess the validity of the theoretical claims.

The paper's experimental section is also underdeveloped, lacking statistical significance testing and sufficient detail about the observed effects. While the computational advantages of the proposed method appear promising, the theoretical foundation is too weak to support the claims made.

Given the stringent standards for NIPS, the paper would need substantial revision to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication. The minor methodological details and technical inconsistencies identified here are significant enough to warrant rejection, as they prevent the reader from properly evaluating the paper's core contributions.

I recommend rejection. The paper's theoretical contribution is interesting but is overshadowed by the numerous technical and presentation flaws that prevent proper assessment of its validity. The authors should address the omitted Lemma 1, fix all notational inconsistencies, provide a more complete theoretical justification for the identifiability claims, and strengthen the experimental section with appropriate statistical analysis.
