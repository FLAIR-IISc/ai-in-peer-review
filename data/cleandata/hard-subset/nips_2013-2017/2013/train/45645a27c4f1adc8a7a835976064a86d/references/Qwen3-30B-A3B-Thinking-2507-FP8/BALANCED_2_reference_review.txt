

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), which are more general than the mixture models previously considered in FAB work. The authors prove that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications, they still yield the same Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) form as mixture models. The key theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which shows that the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs naturally contains the log(∑n znk) term that enables automatic model selection. The paper develops an FAB/LFM algorithm with shrinkage acceleration based on a Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation, and provides a merge post-processing step to address parameter identifiability.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by showing that LFMs can use the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying the previous conditions. This expands the applicability of FAB inference to a broader class of latent variable models. Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are well-justified and represent a clear advance over previous work.

2. **Practical Algorithmic Improvements**: The shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation (Algorithm 2) is both theoretically grounded and practically effective, as demonstrated by the computational cost reductions in experiments. The paper successfully connects theoretical insights to a practical implementation.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments thoroughly compare FAB/LFM with state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) across artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets. The results consistently show FAB/LFM achieves better model selection accuracy with lower computational cost, particularly for larger datasets.

4. **Identifiability Analysis**: The connection between FAB inference and parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) is insightful and provides a natural basis for the merge post-processing step, which improves model compactness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper lacks a thorough discussion of when FAB/LFM might not work well. While the asymptotic nature of the approach is acknowledged, the authors don't clearly articulate performance degradation for small N scenarios (beyond the brief mention in Section 4 regarding Libras).

2. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper could better distinguish FAB/LFM from other nonparametric Bayesian approaches. For instance, the connection to MAD-Bayes (mentioned in Related Work) isn't fully explored, and the theoretical differences between FAB/LFM and IBP-based methods aren't as clearly articulated as they could be.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) is presented as a black box. More details on how to choose the number of CCCP iterations (Tshrink) would be helpful for practitioners.

4. **Theoretical Connection to Identifiability**: The connection to Watanabe's work on algebraic geometry and nonidentifiable learning machines (mentioned in References) could be more explicit, as this would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs in the supplementary materials. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., asymptotic nature of the approach).

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written with clear mathematical notation. The structure flows logically from problem statement to solution to evaluation. However, some technical details (e.g., the connection between CCCP and the FAB algorithm) might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the underlying concepts.

**Originality**: The extension of FAB to LFMs is novel, as the authors correctly note that FAB was previously limited to models satisfying specific conditions. The insight that LFMs still yield the same FIC form despite not satisfying those conditions is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper properly situates itself within the literature on nonparametric Bayesian methods.

**Significance**: The results are significant as model selection for LFMs is an important problem with applications in link prediction and other areas. The paper demonstrates clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency, particularly for large-scale data. The automatic model selection capability (no hyperparameters) is practically valuable.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution that expands the applicability of FAB inference to a broader class of latent variable models. The practical algorithmic improvements and comprehensive experimental evaluation further strengthen the paper's contribution. While the paper has some weaknesses in articulating limitations and distinguishing from related work, these are relatively minor compared to the paper's significant theoretical and practical contributions.

I recommend acceptance. With minor revisions to address the limitations in the discussion of small-N scenarios and to better clarify the relationship to related work (particularly MAD-Bayes and Watanabe's identifiability work), this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

**Pro arguments**: Significant theoretical insight expanding FAB applicability; practical algorithmic improvements with clear computational benefits; comprehensive experimental validation.

**Con arguments**: Limited discussion of small-N performance; some connections to related theoretical work could be more explicit.
