Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models/hidden Markov models to latent feature models (LFMs) with linear-Gaussian emission and Bernoulli features. The core technical result (Lemma 1) shows that, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian structure assumed in prior FAB work, the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian of the negated complete log-likelihood asymptotically decomposes into a sum of log ∑n znk terms. This yields an FIC (Theorem 2) for LFMs that has the same form as for mixture models: a data-fit term penalized by DK/2 log N plus (D/2)∑k log ∑n znk, leading to an FAB lower bound whose optimization induces automatic shrinkage of unused features. The authors derive an algorithm with a mean-field variational E-step, closed-form M-step for W, b, Λ (as in PPCA), and a shrinkage step that removes features with average activation 0 or 1. They reinterpret the E-step as a CCCP iteration, propose an accelerateShrinkage routine that applies multiple CCCP steps per FAB E-step, and prove a partial identifiability result (Theorem 4) suggesting FAB prefers small-K ML solutions and motivates a merge post-processing of nearly duplicate features. Experiments on synthetic, block patterns, and several real datasets compare FAB to EM, IBP (Gibbs), VB, and MEIBP. FAB typically selects more compact models with competitive or better predictive log-likelihood and reduced compute (further improved by the acceleration).

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: showing the FAB/FIC form carries over to LFMs without block-diagonal Hessians is surprising and useful. The asymptotic term D/2∑k log ∑n znk matches the MM case and provides principled, prior-free shrinkage.
- Algorithmic contribution: clear derivation of a practical lower bound with a mean-field E-step; CCCP interpretation is insightful and motivates an effective acceleration that empirically reduces cost.
- Theoretical support: asymptotic equivalence of FIC to marginal log-likelihood (Op(1) gap), and an identifiability result that clarifies behavior around non-unique ML solutions and justifies a merge step.
- Empirical evidence: across synthetic and real datasets, FAB often attains better predictive log-likelihood with smaller K than baselines; the shrinkage acceleration substantially reduces runtime.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- The key asymptotic Lemma 1 is deferred to the supplement with “mild assumptions.” The main text would benefit from explicitly stating the substantive conditions (e.g., bounded moments, non-degenerate Z’Z growth, conditioning of Λ, behavior of πk and µnk away from 0/1). It is important to know when the O(1) remainder is uniform over K, D, and parameter sequences.
- Fairness of comparisons: competitors’ noise variance is fixed (to 1/0.75), while FAB learns Λ. This likely advantages FAB on TLL/PLL. Consider either fixing Λ in FAB to the same value or enabling learning Λ in the baselines (some VB-IBP implementations allow this).
- The shrinkage/merge procedures need practical details: “∑n µnk/N = 0 or 1” will rarely hold exactly; what thresholds are used? How sensitive are results to these thresholds and to Tshrink?
- The identifiability theorem is quite limited (sufficient condition when w·k = w·l). A broader characterization (e.g., for nearly collinear columns) or robustness bounds for finite N would strengthen the claim that FAB resolves non-identifiability in practice.
- Complexity: per-iteration computational complexity and memory (in N, D, K) are not quantified. Given the inner Tshrink CCCP loop, a complexity vs. accuracy tradeoff analysis is warranted.

Clarity
- Overall organization is good, but some statements are terse. Clarify: (i) which priors are asymptotically ignored (only priors on P, not on Z), (ii) the monotonicity guarantee when calling accelerateShrinkage and merge (you do check L, but say so explicitly), and (iii) how CCCP steps interact with the π, π̃ updates.
- Minor issues: typos and grammar in places; better explain the relationship between (11) and (14) (the CCCP update) inline.

Originality
- Extending FAB to LFMs with a rigorous asymptotic argument and obtaining the same FIC form as MMs is novel and non-trivial. The CCCP interpretation and acceleration are modest but useful contributions. The identifiability angle and merge heuristic are interesting and pragmatic.

Significance
- LFMs are widely used; automatic K selection without hand-tuned hyperparameters is valuable. The FIC regularizer offers a theoretically grounded alternative to IBP/Beta-Bernoulli priors and ARD-type penalties. If the asymptotic conditions commonly hold, the approach could be adopted broadly.
- Empirical results are promising, but more rigorous fairness and additional baselines (e.g., sBIC/WBIC or recent scalable variational IBP with learned noise) would strengthen claims.

Questions for the authors
1) Please state the precise assumptions of Lemma 1 in the main text and discuss when they may fail (e.g., highly sparse Z, rapidly growing K, ill-conditioned Λ). Is the Op(1) term uniform in K and D?
2) How sensitive are results to the mean-field approximation on Z? Any evidence that correlated posteriors (e.g., structured mean-field) materially change FIC/N?
3) Can you report results with FAB using fixed noise variance to match baselines, or enable noise learning in the baselines?
4) What numerical thresholds are used for pruning and merging? Provide an ablation on Tshrink and the merge routine, and report how often merges occur.
5) Provide per-iteration complexity and measured wall-time vs. K evolution to illustrate speedups.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and original asymptotic theory extending FAB to LFMs with a useful penalty form.
- Practical algorithm with monotone lower-bound optimization and effective acceleration.
- Good empirical performance with compact models and competitive prediction.
- Clear connections to CCCP and identifiability.

Cons for acceptance
- Key assumptions and proofs relegated to the supplement; practical conditions not clearly stated.
- Fairness concerns in empirical comparisons due to different treatment of noise variance.
- Limited exploration of sensitivity to thresholds and hyperparameters (Tshrink).
- Identifiability claim is narrow; more thorough analysis would help.

Recommendation
Leaning toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying assumptions for Lemma 1/Theorem 2, tightening the fairness of experiments (noise variance), and adding practical details (thresholds, complexity). The paper advances FAB theory and practice in a meaningful way for an important class of models.
