## Peer Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB offers a computationally efficient, automated approach to model selection (determining latent feature dimensionality) without relying on priors or hand-tuned hyperparameters. The key contribution is demonstrating that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for the original FAB formulation, a similar FIC can be derived.  The authors further present analysis relating the FAB E-step to a Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP), leading to a shrinkage acceleration method, and prove parameter identifiability, providing a theoretical basis for merging redundant latent features. 

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the FAB framework introduced in Fujimaki et al. (2017, 2018) for MMs and connects it to the broader landscape of Bayesian non-parametric (BNP) and variational Bayesian (VB) methods for LFMs (e.g., Miller et al., 2011; Doshi-Velez & Ghahramani, 2012).  It positions FAB as a competitive alternative to these approaches, aiming for a better trade-off between accuracy and computational cost. The work also touches on related concepts like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and MAP estimation within IBP models (Reed & Ghahramani, 2012). Finally, it builds on ideas from Schwarz (1978) concerning model complexity and Watanabe (2009) regarding identifiability.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, showing its equivalence to the MM case despite lacking the initial block-diagonal Hessian condition, is a significant theoretical result. Lemma 1 is crucial and well-motivated.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, leveraging the CCCP interpretation, offers a practical speedup for the FAB algorithm, a known bottleneck in similar approaches. The empirical results demonstrate this improvement.
* **Automated Model Selection:**  FAB's ability to perform model selection without priors or hyperparameter tuning is a major advantage, simplifying the application of the method.
* **Identifiability and Merging:** The proof of parameter identifiability provides a solid theoretical foundation for the practical feature merging post-processing step, leading to more parsimonious models.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments across synthetic and real-world datasets showcase the competitiveness of FAB/LFMs, demonstrating good predictive performance and scalability.  The comparison with established methods like IBP, VB, and EM is thorough.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, and the algorithm is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in the LFM Formulation:** The underlying LFM model (Equation 5) is a standard formulation. The core novelty lies in the *inference* method applied to this model, not the model itself.
* **Dependence on Mean-Field Approximation:** The assumption of a factorized variational distribution (q(zn) = ∏ k q̃(znk|µnk)) is a simplification that could potentially affect performance.  The authors acknowledge this but a more detailed discussion of the impact of this approximation would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Setup:** The parameter settings for the competing methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) could be more systematically justified. For instance, the choice of α=3 for IBP may be influencing the results.
* **Complexity of Algorithm 1:** Algorithm 1, including the nested loops and the accelerateShrinkage subroutine, is somewhat complex. A pseudocode representation could improve readability.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily highlights successes. A more nuanced discussion of scenarios where FAB/LFMs might struggle or be outperformed by other methods would enhance the overall assessment.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical derivations appear rigorous (though a deeper dive would require expertise in the area). Claims are generally well-supported by experiments. This is a complete piece of work, going beyond a simple position paper.
* **Clarity:**  Mostly clear, but Algorithm 1 could be improved for readability.  Some sections (e.g., the discussion of CCCP) assume a certain level of familiarity with the topic.
* **Originality:** The application of FAB to LFMs is novel, and the derived FIC and the acceleration technique represent original contributions. However, the underlying LFM model is not new.
* **Significance:**  The work has the potential to be significant. FAB offers a promising alternative to existing LFM inference methods, particularly for large-scale datasets. The automation and efficiency gains are valuable.




**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant theoretical contribution extending the FAB framework.
* Practical improvements in computational efficiency through the shrinkage acceleration.
* Competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* Strong theoretical justification for feature merging.
* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in latent feature modeling.

**Con:**

* Incremental novelty in terms of the underlying LFM model.
* Reliance on the mean-field approximation.
* Experimental setup could be more robust to hyperparameter choices.
* Algorithm complexity.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent feature models. The theoretical results are compelling, the proposed acceleration technique is effective, and the experimental validation is convincing. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above (clarifying Algorithm 1, justifying parameter settings, and discussing failure cases) will further strengthen the paper.  The work is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on dimensionality reduction, feature learning, and Bayesian model selection.



**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
