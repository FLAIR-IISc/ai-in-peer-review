## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a fully automated Bayesian inference method that avoids explicit priors and hyperparameter tuning, relying on asymptotic analysis to perform model selection (specifically, determining the dimensionality of latent features). The key contribution is demonstrating that the Hessian of the log-likelihood for LFMs exhibits a structure that allows for the application of the FAB framework despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions required for the original FAB method.  The authors also provide theoretical analysis regarding parameter identifiability and propose a shrinkage acceleration technique based on a connection to the Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), along with a merging post-processing step for latent features.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the authors’ prior work on FAB inference for MMs [5, 6]. It positions itself within the broader landscape of Bayesian non-parametric (BNP) methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [10, 11] and variational Bayesian (VB) approaches [3] for LFM dimensionality selection.  The authors clearly differentiate FAB from these methods by emphasizing its fully automated nature and potentially lower computational cost. They also relate their work to "MAD-Bayes" [1], pointing out the difference in the asymptotic limit considered.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is a significant theoretical contribution, extending the applicability of the FAB framework.  The proof sketches provided (detailed proofs in the supplementary materials) give confidence in the validity of the approach.
* **Automated Model Selection:**  FAB's ability to automatically select the dimensionality of latent features without priors or hyperparameter tuning is a major strength, addressing a common challenge in Bayesian modeling.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration technique (based on CCCP) demonstrably reduces computational cost, addressing a known limitation of FAB. The experimental results (Figure 1) are compelling in this regard.
* **Identifiability Analysis:** The theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) is valuable and provides a principled approach to merging redundant latent features.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods in terms of prediction accuracy and model selection.  The comparison against IBP, VB and MEIBP is thorough.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas.  The introduction clearly motivates the work, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Core LFM Formulation:** The underlying LFM model (5) is a standard formulation. The novelty resides primarily in the inference method, rather than the model itself.
* **Dependence on Laplace Approximation:** The FAB framework relies on the Laplace approximation, which can be problematic in high-dimensional settings or with complex posteriors. The paper acknowledges this implicitly, but a more detailed discussion of the approximation’s limitations would be beneficial.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** While not explicitly stated, the performance of EM-based algorithms like FAB can be sensitive to initialization. The paper mentions random initialization, but it would be useful to report the variance of results across different initializations.
* **Merge Post-Processing Heuristic:**  The merging post-processing step relies on a Euclidean distance metric between latent feature vectors. While justified by the theoretical analysis, it feels somewhat heuristic. Exploring other merging criteria might be valuable.
* **Missing Implementation Details:** While code is mentioned to be available, a more detailed description of the implementation choices (e.g., optimization algorithms used, stopping criteria) would improve reproducibility.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of a promising Bayesian inference framework to a new class of models.
* It addresses a practical challenge (automated model selection for LFMs) in a novel and effective manner.
* The computational improvements offered by the shrinkage acceleration technique are significant.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate competitive performance.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core LFM model itself is not novel.
* The reliance on the Laplace approximation is a potential limitation.
* The merge post-processing step is somewhat heuristic.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The theoretical work is solid, the proposed method is well-motivated and practically useful, and the experimental results are compelling.  While there are minor weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions - clarifying initialization sensitivity and discussion of the Laplace approximation).  I would also suggest adding a brief discussion of the potential limitations of the merging heuristic and providing more implementation details for reproducibility.
