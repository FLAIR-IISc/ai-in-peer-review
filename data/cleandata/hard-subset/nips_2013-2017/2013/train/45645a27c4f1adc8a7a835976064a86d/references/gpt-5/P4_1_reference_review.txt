Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and HMM settings [5, 6] to latent feature models (LFMs) with Bernoulli features and linear-Gaussian observations (a standard IBP model [10, 11]). The core technical result (Lemma 1) analyzes the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood with respect to per-observation-dimension parameters, showing that the log-determinant asymptotically decomposes as a sum of log feature-usage counts, log ∑n znk, up to Op(1) terms. This yields a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs (Theorem 2) that has the same functional form as in mixture models [6]: a data-fit term minus a complexity penalty of order (D/2)∑k log ∑n znk and O(KD log N), rather than the BIC-like O(KD log N) that would arise if the Hessian scaled with N per parameter [23]. The paper then constructs a tractable lower bound to this FIC via linearization of the concave −log term (as in [6]) and a mean-field Bernoulli factorization over Z, yielding an alternating FAB algorithm with: (i) a coordinate ascent E-step for µnk = Eq[znk] featuring an FIC-induced shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k); (ii) M-step updates identical to PPCA-style closed forms for W, b, Λ [24]; and (iii) automatic elimination of unused or always-on features and a merge post-processing step motivated by an identifiability analysis (Theorem 4). A CCCP view [29] of the E-step/linearization leads to a shrinkage-acceleration routine that takes multiple CCCP updates to hasten the elimination of irrelevant features.

Experiments compare the proposed FAB-LFM (with and without acceleration) to variational EM (with only prior-induced shrinkage), IBP with accelerated Gibbs sampling [2], variational IBP [3], and MEIBP [22] on synthetic and several real datasets. FAB typically selects more compact models and attains higher predictive log-likelihoods with lower or comparable runtime (MEIBP can be faster but with worse predictive performance). The authors note that FAB can underperform on small N, consistent with the asymptotic nature of FIC.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Derivation of an LFM FIC with the same log-usage penalty as in mixtures, despite lack of block-diagonal Hessian, is nontrivial and insightful. It clarifies a broader applicability of FIC beyond models satisfying earlier FAB assumptions.
- Algorithmic contribution: A practical, monotone-increasing lower-bound optimization with closed-form M-steps, and a CCCP-based acceleration that materially reduces time-to-shrinkage.
- Identifiability analysis: Theorem 4 provides conditions under which FAB prefers balanced solutions and motivates a merging heuristic to reduce redundant features.
- Empirical results: Across synthetic and diverse real datasets, FAB achieves strong model selection and predictive performance with reasonable computational cost. The shrinkage acceleration is clearly beneficial.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions and proofs deferred: Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 rely on “mild assumptions” and Op(1) terms; the main paper lacks sufficient detail to assess regularity conditions (e.g., on usage counts, conditioning of Λ, and dependence structure across features). This limits the ability to verify technical soundness without the supplement.
- Mean-field and linearization gap: The final objective is a lower bound to FIC that itself approximates the log marginal; the paper does not quantify the tightness or provide diagnostics for when linearization and mean-field approximations materially bias feature selection.
- Merge step validity: The proposed merge sets w′k = 2wk and µ′·k = µ·k + µ·l. Since µ′ can exceed 1, this can violate Bernoulli parameter constraints unless additional handling (e.g., clipping/renormalization) is used. The paper states merges are accepted only if the bound increases, but it is unclear how feasibility is guaranteed or how the variational family is respected. More detail is needed.
- Experimental fairness and scope: Competing methods are constrained to fixed noise variance (following released code) while FAB learns Λ; this can confer an advantage. Clarification or additional tuning (e.g., cross-validated noise variance for baselines) would strengthen the comparison. VB-IBP is omitted on large datasets due to runtime; including smaller-scale comparisons where it is competitive would add balance.
- Complexity analysis: There is no explicit per-iteration complexity or wall-clock scaling with N, D, K; given the emphasis on efficiency, such analysis would be useful.

Quality
The derivations are plausible and align with established asymptotic techniques (Laplace approximation and singular learning theory [25, 26]). The CCCP interpretation is correct for the concave/convex decomposition shown. Monotonicity of the lower bound under the proposed updates follows standard arguments. The main technical risk is the reliance on unexplicated conditions in Lemma 1 and the merge step’s feasibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Suggestions:
- State the key assumptions underpinning Lemma 1 in the main text (even briefly), including requirements on feature usage and parameter scaling.
- Provide a short sketch of the proof intuition for Lemma 1 to build trust.
- Clarify the feasibility of the merge operation (how µ′ is kept in [0,1], and how merging affects the variational family).
- Specify thresholds for pruning features (exact µ sums of 0 or 1 are unlikely under sigmoids; practical thresholds should be stated).
- Add a brief complexity discussion and sensitivity to Tshrink and δ.

Originality
Extending FAB to LFMs with a principled FIC and showing it matches the mixture-model penalty is novel relative to [5, 6]. The CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability-based merging are useful additions. While related asymptotic analyses for LFMs exist (e.g., MAD-Bayes [1]), the proposed route and resulting algorithm differ meaningfully.

Significance
Automated, prior-insensitive model selection for LFMs with competitive speed is relevant to practitioners using IBP-style models [10, 3, 22]. The results suggest practical gains in both parsimony and predictive performance. The theoretical insight on model complexity for LFMs may stimulate further applications of FIC to other latent variable models.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and interesting theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs with a well-motivated FIC (Lemma 1, Theorem 2).
- Practical, monotone algorithm with effective shrinkage acceleration and good empirical performance.
- Identifiability analysis and merging heuristic address a common pathology in LFMs.
- Relevance to active areas (IBP, LFMs) with improved automation and reduced tuning.

Cons for acceptance
- Key assumptions and proofs are not presented in the main text, limiting verifiability.
- Merge procedure may violate Bernoulli constraints unless additional steps are taken; details are missing.
- Experimental comparisons may favor FAB due to learned noise variance; stronger fairness controls desirable.
- No explicit complexity analysis; limited discussion of small-N regimes where FIC may be inaccurate.

Questions for the authors
- Please state the precise assumptions required for Lemma 1 (on Z, W, Λ, and N,K,D scaling) and provide a proof sketch.
- How is feasibility ensured in the merge step when µ·k + µ·l can exceed 1? Is clipping or reparameterization used?
- Can you provide a per-iteration complexity and empirical scaling with N, D, K?
- How sensitive are results to Tshrink and δ, and to initialization of K?
- Can baselines be given access to tuned or learned noise variance for a fairer comparison?

Overall recommendation
The paper provides a solid theoretical and algorithmic contribution with strong empirical results. Addressing the noted concerns (especially assumptions, merge feasibility, and fairness) would further strengthen it.
