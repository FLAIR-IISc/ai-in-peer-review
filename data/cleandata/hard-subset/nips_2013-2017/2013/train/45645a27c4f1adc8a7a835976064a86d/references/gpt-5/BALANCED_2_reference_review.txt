Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models/HMMs to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian observations. The core theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, showing that the log-determinant of the Hessian of the complete-data negative log-likelihood contributes a regularization term of the form (D/2)∑k log(∑n znk), yielding a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs that matches the form previously derived for mixture models. This implies automatic shrinkage of unused features and asymptotic equivalence between FIC and the marginal log-likelihood. The authors further:
- derive a practical FAB algorithm using a tractable lower bound with mean-field q(Z);
- interpret the E-step as a CCCP update, which motivates a “shrinkage acceleration” procedure;
- prove a form of parameter identifiability that biases toward balanced feature usage and motivates a post hoc feature-merging step.

Empirically, the method is compared to IBP Gibbs sampling, VB for IBP (finite K), and MEIBP (submodular MAP) on synthetic data, block images, and several real datasets. FAB is reported to be competitive or superior in model selection and predictive log-likelihood, while being computationally efficient, particularly with the acceleration.

This work builds on FAB for mixtures/HMMs (Fujimaki et al.), NPB LFMs via IBP (Griffiths & Ghahramani; Doshi-Velez et al.), submodular MAP (MEIBP; Reed & Ghahramani), and asymptotic MAP derivations like MAD-Bayes (Broderick et al.). The novelty is in showing the same FIC form arises for LFMs despite non-block-diagonal Hessians, and in the CCCP-based acceleration/identifiability analysis.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are interesting and nontrivial, showing FIC’s applicability beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians. The resulting regularizer is intuitive and useful for automated feature pruning.
- Algorithmic design: The FAB lower bound and alternating updates are clean; monotonicity of the bound and the CCCP interpretation provide a principled optimization story. The shrinkage-acceleration is simple and impactful.
- Identifiability insight: The theorem that equal columns of W imply equal average activations and a justified merging step addresses a real issue in LFMs and connects asymptotics to practical post-processing.
- Empirical promise: On several datasets, FAB selects compact models with good predictive performance and often favorable runtime, especially with the acceleration. The block-data case illustrates the automatic removal of spurious features.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Assumptions/generality of Lemma 1: The “mild assumptions” are deferred to the supplement. It is unclear precisely how restrictive they are (e.g., diagonal noise, Gaussianity, boundedness conditions) and whether the result extends to non-Gaussian likelihoods (e.g., logistic/probit, Poisson) or to non-diagonal Λ. The practical scope should be stated more explicitly in the main text.
- Fairness of experiments: Baselines are forced to use fixed noise variance (1.0 or 0.75), while FAB learns Λ. This likely advantages FAB both in fit and in automatic shrinkage. Many IBP/VB implementations support learning noise variance; otherwise, simple cross-validation or EM for variance should be allowed for baselines. MEIBP sensitivity and manual K settings also complicate fairness. More careful hyperparameter tuning and unified stopping criteria are needed.
- Limited breadth: Experiments focus on linear-Gaussian LFMs; no evaluation on typical IBP tasks like link prediction or non-Gaussian observations, where IBP-based methods shine. Comparisons to well-tuned VB-IBP are omitted on real data due to runtime; this weakens the empirical case.
- Theoretical tightness: While FIC is asymptotically accurate up to O(1), the paper does not quantify finite-sample behavior or give error bounds. The CCCP-based acceleration lacks convergence-rate analysis (though standard CCCP guarantees monotonicity).
- Merging heuristic: Although justified by Theorem 4 in the equal-columns case, the practical merging step relies on thresholding Euclidean distances; more detail on thresholds and their effect would help, as would an ablation.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, but some derivations are compressed. Equation (10) mixes constants and terms; carefully listing all terms and their origins would improve readability. A precise statement of the assumptions for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 in the main text would aid readers.
- Minor typos/notation (e.g., vector/matrix subscripts) and occasional grammatical issues.

Originality
- Extending FAB to LFMs with the same FIC form is novel and elegant. The CCCP interpretation is not entirely new for mean-field updates but is applied creatively here to motivate acceleration. The identifiability/merging angle is a useful addition.

Significance
- If generalized beyond linear-Gaussian settings, this could provide a fast, automated alternative to IBP-based methods for model selection in LFMs. In its current form, significance is moderate: solid for Gaussian LFMs, but broader impact depends on extensions and more comprehensive empirical validation.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Theoretical contribution (Lemma 1) broadens FAB’s applicability and yields a clear regularizer.
- Practical algorithm with monotonic lower-bound optimization and effective acceleration.
- Useful identifiability result and principled merging step.
- Promising empirical results showing compact models and good predictive performance.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Assumptions and scope of the main theorem insufficiently explicit; unclear generality beyond Gaussian-diagonal models.
- Experimental fairness concerns (noise variance learned only by FAB; limited hyperparameter tuning and omitted baselines on real data).
- Limited coverage of non-Gaussian LFMs and standard IBP application domains.
- Missing convergence-rate or finite-sample analyses; merging heuristic details sparse.

Suggestions for improvement
- State the precise assumptions for Lemma 1/Theorem 2 in the main paper; discuss extensions (non-diagonal Λ, non-Gaussian likelihoods) or explicitly limit claims.
- Re-run baselines allowing variance learning or hyperparameter tuning (e.g., grid search for noise), and report sensitivity analyses. Align stopping criteria and initialization across methods; release code for reproducibility.
- Add benchmarks on non-Gaussian LFMs (e.g., logistic, Poisson) and IBP link-prediction tasks, or clearly scope the contribution to Gaussian LFMs.
- Provide an ablation on Tshrink, the acceleration, and merging thresholds; report the number of merges and their effect on the bound and PLL.
- Include computational complexity per-iteration and practical scaling with N, D, K.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper contains a neat and potentially impactful extension of FAB to LFMs with appealing algorithmic consequences. However, the current version underspecifies theoretical assumptions and presents experiments that may not be fully fair or sufficiently broad for NeurIPS. With clearer theoretical scope and stronger, better-controlled empirical comparisons, this work could be a solid contribution.

Confidence: 0.7
