

# Peer Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant gap in the applicability of FAB to more complex models. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by previous FAB work (for mixture models), they can still derive an FIC with the same form as for mixture models. This theoretical insight enables the development of an FAB/LFM algorithm that automatically selects model complexity without hyperparameters, with significant computational advantages over existing methods.

**Theoretical Contribution and Methodology**

The paper's key theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which shows that log det |F(d)| for LFMs has the form ∑_k log(∑_n z_nk)/N + O_p(1), despite LFMs not satisfying the assumptions (A1, A2) that enabled the FAB framework for mixture models. This is a significant insight, as it demonstrates that the FIC representation is more widely applicable than previously thought. The derivation of the FAB/LFM algorithm is sound, and the CCCP interpretation of the optimization process provides a valuable theoretical foundation for the shrinkage acceleration technique.

The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) is particularly strong, offering a clear theoretical justification for the merge post-processing step that resolves non-identifiability issues in LFMs. This is an important contribution to the theoretical understanding of latent variable models, as parameter identifiability is crucial for both theoretical guarantees and practical performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Theoretical novelty**: The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to LFMs, demonstrating a previously unknown connection between FIC forms across different latent variable models.

2. **Practical acceleration**: The shrinkage acceleration mechanism (Algorithm 2) based on CCCP interpretation is well-motivated and empirically validated, significantly reducing computational cost while maintaining performance.

3. **Automatic model selection**: The paper clearly demonstrates that FAB/LFM achieves automatic feature selection without hyperparameters, addressing a key limitation of existing methods.

4. **Comprehensive experiments**: The experimental evaluation across artificial, synthetic, and real-world datasets provides strong empirical support for the claims.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Theoretical justification**: The paper states Lemma 1 holds under "mild assumptions" but doesn't sufficiently elaborate on what these assumptions are or why they're reasonable for LFMs. A brief mention of these assumptions in the main text would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

2. **Experimental methodology**: The comparison with MEIBP is problematic as MEIBP is designed for non-negative data, yet the authors applied it to datasets that may contain negative values (e.g., EEG data). This undermines the validity of the comparison. The paper should either restrict comparisons to non-negative data or clarify the data preprocessing steps.

3. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art**: While the paper compares with IBP, VB, and MEIBP, it omits comparison with more recent methods like the submodular optimization approach for IBP or other recent advances in scalable Bayesian inference for LFMs.

4. **Theoretical gap in model complexity**: The paper claims FAB/LFM achieves the same model complexity as the marginal log-likelihood, but doesn't fully explain why the O_p(1) terms in Theorem 2 don't affect the asymptotic behavior for model selection.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that successfully extends the FAB framework to LFMs, with significant practical implications for scalable Bayesian model selection. The paper demonstrates a theoretically sound and practically efficient method for latent feature model selection that outperforms existing approaches in computational efficiency while achieving comparable or better model selection accuracy.

The main weaknesses relate to the theoretical justification of key assumptions and the experimental methodology, which could be strengthened with more detailed explanations and careful comparison with appropriate baselines. However, these are relatively minor issues that don't undermine the paper's core contribution.

**Recommendation**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that broadens the applicability of FAB inference, and the practical advantages demonstrated in the experiments are compelling. The authors should address the theoretical assumptions mentioned in Lemma 1 more thoroughly and clarify the experimental comparison with MEIBP.

The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection for latent variable models by demonstrating a wider applicability of the FAB framework. The automatic feature selection capability without hyperparameters is particularly valuable for practical applications. The connection between FAB and CCCP also provides a useful theoretical lens for understanding the optimization process.

This work is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical insight with practical algorithmic development, addressing a significant gap in scalable Bayesian inference for latent feature models. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental results robustly support the theoretical claims.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
- Pro: Novel theoretical extension of FAB framework with practical algorithmic improvements; strong experimental validation; addresses an important gap in Bayesian model selection.
- Con: Theoretical justification of key assumptions could be strengthened; some experimental comparisons with MEIBP are methodologically questionable.
