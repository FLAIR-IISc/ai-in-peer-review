

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant gap in the applicability of FAB methods. While previous work had established FAB for mixture models (MMs) under specific Hessian conditions, the authors demonstrate through Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 that LFMs, despite not satisfying those conditions, share the same FIC form as MMs. This theoretical insight enables the development of FAB/LFMs with desirable properties: automatic feature selection, monotonic convergence, and parameter identifiability. The paper further introduces a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP and a merge post-processing step to improve practical efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical contribution:** The key insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as MMs despite not satisfying the Hessian conditions is novel and well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The derivation of Lemma 1 is particularly compelling, showing how the log(∑n znk) term naturally appears in the Hessian approximation without relying on the assumptions used for MMs.

2. **Practical contributions:** The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) based on CCCP interpretation significantly reduces computational cost while maintaining model selection accuracy. The empirical results (Figure 1) clearly demonstrate this efficiency gain without sacrificing performance.

3. **Identifiability analysis:** Theorem 4 provides important theoretical insights into parameter identifiability for LFMs, explaining why FAB prefers smaller-K solutions compared to EM. The merge post-processing algorithm that follows from this analysis is a valuable practical contribution.

4. **Comprehensive experiments:** The experimental evaluation across artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets (Table 1) thoroughly demonstrates FAB/LFM's advantages in model selection accuracy, computational efficiency, and predictive performance. The consistent superiority in PLL with lower computational cost across multiple datasets is convincing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical clarity:** The paper could better explain why the Hessian condition was previously a barrier for FAB and how it's overcome in LFMs. The connection between the Hessian condition and the log(∑n znk) term could be more explicitly articulated, especially for readers unfamiliar with the FAB framework.

2. **Limitations discussion:** The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of the approach. For instance, the impact of the assumptions in Lemma 1 (which are relegated to supplementary materials) on practical applications isn't discussed. The performance on very high-dimensional data or datasets where the assumptions might be violated isn't evaluated.

3. **Comparative analysis:** While the paper compares with multiple baselines, it lacks a detailed computational complexity analysis (e.g., big-O comparisons) between FAB/LFM and other methods. The statement that "MEIBP was much faster than FAB" (Figure 2) needs more nuance - MEIBP's speed advantage appears to stem from a carefully chosen initial K, not inherent algorithmic efficiency.

4. **Related work connection:** The relationship between FAB/LFMs and MAD-Bayes (mentioned in related work) isn't clearly articulated. The authors should clarify how their asymptotic approach differs from MAD-Bayes' limiting case of noise variance going to zero.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution with practical value. The paper makes a clear and significant advance in extending FAB to LFMs, with compelling theoretical insights and empirical validation. The main weaknesses are primarily in the presentation of the theoretical contributions and the lack of thorough discussion about limitations.

**Suggestions for improvement:**

1. Clarify the connection between the Hessian condition and the log(∑n znk) term in the main text, not just in supplementary materials.

2. Add a subsection discussing limitations (e.g., sensitivity to assumptions in Lemma 1, performance on extremely high-dimensional data).

3. Provide a more detailed computational complexity analysis comparing FAB/LFM with baselines, including how the shrinkage acceleration affects the asymptotic complexity.

4. Expand the discussion of how FAB/LFMs relate to and differ from MAD-Bayes.

The paper addresses an important gap in the FAB literature and makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
