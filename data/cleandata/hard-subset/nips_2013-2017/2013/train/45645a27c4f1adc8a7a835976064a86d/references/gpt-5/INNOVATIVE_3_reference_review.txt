Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models to latent feature models (LFMs). The core theoretical claim is that, despite LFMs violating the block-diagonal Hessian condition previously used to derive FIC/FAB for MMs, the marginal complete log-likelihood for LFMs admits an asymptotically equivalent FIC with the same key regularization term ∑k log ∑n znk (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2). Building on this, the authors derive a practical FAB lower bound under a mean-field approximation, give an EM-like algorithm, and interpret the E-step as a CCCP iteration, which motivates an acceleration scheme that speeds shrinkage of irrelevant features. They also analyze identifiability and propose a merge post-processing when features coincide. Empirically, they compare against IBP (Gibbs), VB-IBP, and MEIBP on synthetic and real datasets, reporting strong model selection and predictive performance with competitive or better runtime.

Strengths
- Novel and bold theoretical extension: Showing that the LFM FIC recovers the same form as for MMs without the block-diagonal Hessian assumption is a surprising and important conceptual step. If Lemma 1’s conditions are met broadly, this significantly broadens the applicability of FAB.
- Clean, automated model selection: The FAB-induced regularizer drives automatic shrinkage of features without hyperparameters or priors, aligning with recent interest in hyperparameter-free inference.
- CCCP interpretation and acceleration: The CCCP view of alternating updates is insightful and leads to a practically useful acceleration that appears to reduce runtime substantially while preserving objective monotonicity.
- Identifiability analysis and merge heuristic: Theorem 4 provides a principled rationale for merging coincident features; this addresses a common pathology in LFMs.
- Empirical evidence: Across synthetic and multiple real datasets, FAB often yields compact models (smaller K) with good predictive log-likelihood and competitive speed.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions behind Lemma 1 are deferred: The main paper does not state the “mild assumptions” needed for log det|F(d)| ≈ ∑k log(∑n znk)/N + Op(1). Clarity on conditions (e.g., distributional assumptions on Z, boundedness of covariates, scaling in D and K, the role of diagonal Λ) is essential for readers to assess when the result applies and when it may fail.
- Monotonicity and correctness of accelerated steps: Algorithm 2 updates π together with π̃ and applies multiple CCCP steps per E-step. Please clarify whether each inner step provably increases the same lower bound L (with P fixed), and whether any deviations (e.g., due to joint π, π̃ updates) break monotonicity guarantees.
- Mean-field approximation on Z: While common for IBP-VB, it further loosens the FIC lower bound and may bias posterior correlations among features. A brief discussion of the accuracy impact relative to non-factorized alternatives would help.
- Feature elimination conditions: The shrinkage step removes features when ∑n µnk/N ∈ {0,1}. In practice, µ is continuous and rarely exactly attains these values; is there a principled thresholding rule and a bound showing elimination increases L when close to 0 or 1?
- Fairness in experiments: Some competing baselines did not learn noise variance and were fixed to 1 or 0.75, whereas FAB learns Λ; this can advantage FAB in PLL/TLL. It would be more convincing to either (a) equip baselines with variance learning, or (b) fix λ for all methods. Also, for MEIBP, manually setting K=100 for some datasets could affect outcomes; discuss sensitivity.
- Computational complexity: Please provide per-iteration time and memory complexity in N, D, K for FAB and the accelerated CCCP steps, and empirical scaling plots where possible.
- Scope and robustness: Performance degrades at small N (acknowledged). Can the authors suggest a principled small-sample correction (e.g., retaining prior-induced η regularization more strongly, or tempering the FIC penalty)?
- Clarity: Some notation and equations could be clarified (e.g., line 2 of Algorithm 2: the 1/2·1·w>k term reads awkwardly; please check formatting). The claim that the M-step is “exactly the same as PPCA” would benefit from an explicit formula in the main text, since PPCA assumes Gaussian latent variables whereas here Z is Bernoulli and enters via µ, µµ>+diag(µ−µ2).

Quality
Technically promising, with solid ties to asymptotic Bayesian theory. The main theoretical novelty hinges on Lemma 1; moving at least a precise statement of its assumptions into the main text would substantially strengthen perceived rigor. The algorithmic design is coherent; the CCCP interpretation is neat. Empirics are supportive but could be more controlled regarding variance learning and baseline settings.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, though dense. Algorithms are provided; the stopping criterion is explicit. A more explicit complexity analysis and clearer discussion of assumptions would help reproducibility and applicability judgments.

Originality
High. Extending FAB to LFMs without the prior block-diagonal Hessian requirement is a significant conceptual advance. The CCCP perspective and identifiability/merge post-processing are also interesting contributions.

Significance
Potentially high. Automated, hyperparameter-free model selection for LFMs with good empirical performance is valuable. If the theoretical conditions are broad, this could influence how practitioners approach feature selection beyond IBP/VB.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong conceptual novelty (FAB for LFMs with matching FIC form).
- Practical acceleration and identifiability insights.
- Good empirical performance and compact models.
- No hand-tuned hyperparameters; automation is appealing.

Cons for acceptance
- Key assumptions and guarantees are not fully visible in the main text.
- Some fairness concerns in the empirical setup (variance learning).
- Limited analysis of small-sample behavior and sensitivity to initialization/thresholds.

Recommendation
Accept. The conceptual advance is substantial and likely to stimulate follow-up work, and the practical algorithm is attractive. I am willing to overlook the minor empirical and presentation issues given the novelty and promise. Strengthening the exposition of assumptions/guarantees and tightening the experimental fairness in the camera-ready would make this a very strong contribution.

Questions for authors
- Please state the exact assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text and discuss their practical plausibility.
- Do the accelerated CCCP steps with π, π̃ updates preserve monotone increase of the same lower bound? If not, can you provide a safeguard?
- How sensitive is the method to initialization and Tshrink? Any guidance for practitioners?
- Can you report results with (a) fixed λ for all methods, or (b) variance learning for baselines, to control for that factor?
- What is the per-iteration complexity and empirical scaling in N, D, K?

Confidence
Moderate.
