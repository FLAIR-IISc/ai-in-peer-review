**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for model selection that approximates the marginal log-likelihood via a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) in the large sample limit (N → ∞). Previous applications of FAB, such as to Mixture Models (MMs), relied on the assumption that the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. The primary challenge addressed here is that LFMs do not satisfy this condition, as parameters are shared across features.

The core technical contribution is a new asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) showing that the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs decomposes in a way that yields an FIC with the same form of complexity penalty (∑_k log(∑_n z_nk)) as found in MMs. This result provides the theoretical foundation for the FAB/LFM algorithm. This algorithm iteratively maximizes a lower bound on the FIC, leading to a "model-induced regularization" effect that automatically prunes the number of latent features without relying on priors or hand-tuned hyperparameters.

To improve the practical utility of the algorithm, the authors provide two key refinements. First, by interpreting the E-step as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), they propose a shrinkage acceleration method that significantly speeds up the pruning of irrelevant features. Second, they analyze the parameter identifiability of LFMs under FAB, showing that the framework resolves common non-identifiability issues and motivating a principled post-processing step for merging redundant features. Comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that FAB/LFM is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods like Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants and Variational Bayes (VB) in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** The extension of the FAB framework to models without a block-diagonal Hessian is a novel and important contribution. The derivation in Lemma 1, which shows that the FIC penalty term retains its simple form even in the more complex dependency structure of LFMs, is the key insight that makes this work possible.
2.  **Principled and Automated Model Selection:** The paper presents a well-grounded method for automatically determining the number of latent features in LFMs. The connection to the marginal log-likelihood via asymptotic analysis provides a strong theoretical justification, and its ability to operate without user-specified priors on model complexity is a significant practical advantage over many non-parametric Bayesian approaches.
3.  **Excellent Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly articulates the limitations of prior work, the central technical hurdle, and how the proposed theoretical results overcome it. The logical flow from theory to algorithm to practical refinements is easy to follow.
4.  **Practical Refinements and Analysis:** The paper goes beyond the core derivation by providing valuable practical enhancements. The CCCP interpretation leading to the shrinkage acceleration is clever and empirically shown to be effective. The analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) adds theoretical depth and leads to a useful feature-merging procedure.
5.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed. The proposed method is compared against a strong set of relevant baselines (IBP, MEIBP, VB, and variational EM) across multiple criteria, including model selection accuracy, quality of learned features, predictive log-likelihood, and computational time. The results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed FAB/LFM framework. The inclusion of an EM baseline effectively isolates and highlights the contribution of the FAB-derived regularization term.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Asymptotic Assumptions:** The core theoretical results are asymptotic. Lemma 1 is stated to hold "under some mild assumptions" deferred to the supplementary material. While understandable due to space constraints, briefly summarizing these key assumptions in the main text would help the reader better understand the scope and limitations of the theory. This also relates to the observed performance drop on the small-N Libras dataset, and a sentence connecting the theoretical assumptions to this practical limitation would strengthen the discussion.
2.  **Lack of Direct BIC Comparison:** The paper correctly notes that a naive application of Laplace's method would yield a BIC penalty. Given that BIC is a common and simple baseline for model selection, an empirical comparison against an LFM where K is selected by BIC would be a valuable addition. This would more directly quantify the benefit of the more sophisticated FIC penalty term derived in the paper.
3.  **Sensitivity to Algorithmic Parameters:** The paper presents the method as being free of "hand-tunable hyper-parameters." While this is true from a modeling perspective (e.g., no concentration parameter like in IBP), the algorithm itself introduces parameters like the convergence tolerance `δ` and the number of inner shrinkage iterations `T_shrink`. The choice of `T_shrink = 100` seems somewhat arbitrary. A brief justification or sensitivity analysis for this parameter would make the experimental setup more transparent and reproducible.
4.  **Details on Merging Procedure:** The feature merging post-processing step is a nice practical touch motivated by the identifiability analysis. However, the details of its implementation are sparse. For instance, how is the threshold for deciding if two features are "close enough" to attempt a merge determined? Is it a fixed hyperparameter, or is it decided adaptively? Clarifying this would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you provide more intuition in the main text for why the Hessian's log-determinant for LFMs (Lemma 1) simplifies so elegantly? What properties of the model cause the cross-feature terms to become asymptotically negligible?
*   In the real-world data experiments, the noise variance was fixed for the IBP/MEIBP baselines. Was the noise precision matrix `Λ` learned by FAB/LFM and the EM baseline in these experiments? If so, this could be a confounding factor in the comparison. Please clarify the experimental protocol for all methods regarding the noise parameter.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. It successfully extends the promising FAB framework to the important class of latent feature models by overcoming a non-trivial theoretical hurdle. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and supported by a strong set of experiments. The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is both principled and practical, offering a compelling alternative to existing methods. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to requests for additional clarification and experimental comparison, which do not detract from the core strength of the contribution. The paper is of high quality, originality, and significance, and I strongly recommend its acceptance.
