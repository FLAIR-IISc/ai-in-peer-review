**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a recently developed method for approximate Bayesian model selection that relies on a large-sample approximation of the marginal log-likelihood, termed the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC). Previous work had applied FAB to models like mixture models, where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. The main contribution of this paper is to derive the FIC for LFMs, a model class that does not satisfy this structural condition. The authors show, via an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian (Lemma 1), that the resulting FIC for LFMs surprisingly takes the same form as for mixture models. Based on this derivation, they present the FAB/LFM algorithm, which automatically selects the number of latent features (K). The paper further provides a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation of the optimization, which inspires a "shrinkage acceleration" technique, and analyzes the parameter identifiability of the resulting model. The method is evaluated against several established baselines, including Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants and variational EM, on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's primary strength lies in its theoretical contribution. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2), contingent on the asymptotic result of Lemma 1, is a non-trivial extension of the FAB framework. If the analysis holds, it suggests that the "model-induced regularization" term, previously justified under strong structural assumptions, may be more broadly applicable. The proposed algorithm is also fully automated, eschewing the need for hyperparameter tuning, which is an appealing characteristic. The connection drawn to CCCP is insightful and provides a principled basis for the proposed acceleration scheme.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that cast doubt on the overall contribution's soundness and practical significance.

1.  **Over-reliance on Asymptotic Justification:** The entire FAB framework is founded on large-sample (N → ∞) asymptotics. While asymptotic analysis is a powerful theoretical tool, its practical relevance for finite datasets—the setting for virtually all real-world machine learning—is not guaranteed. The authors themselves concede this limitation in their analysis of the Libras dataset, stating that "in small N scenarios... FIC approximation would be not accurate, and FAB would perform worse than NPBs." This is a critical admission. A method that is only reliable in an asymptotic regime that may not be met in practice is of limited utility. The paper does not provide any theoretical or empirical characterization of how large 'N' needs to be for the approximations to be reliable.

2.  **Weaknesses in Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical comparison to established methods is not as rigorous as it should be.
    *   **Baseline Configuration:** The baselines do not appear to have been run under optimal conditions. For instance, the IBP hyperparameter α was set to a fixed value that the authors note "might cause overestimation of K." This potentially hobbles a key competitor. Similarly, the noise variance for baselines was fixed rather than learned or cross-validated. A fair comparison requires running competitors at their strongest, not under potentially disadvantageous settings.
    *   **Omission of Key Baselines:** Variational Bayes (VB) for IBP, a standard and highly relevant baseline, was "totally omitted" from the real-data experiments due to computational time. While VB can be slow, its complete omission weakens the paper's claims of superiority. A thorough evaluation would include these results, even if they take a long time to generate, or would compare against more modern, scalable variational methods.
    *   **Inconsistent Performance Gains:** The reported performance gains over baselines are not consistently overwhelming. On the large sample datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS), the predictive performance is merely "competitive" with a variational EM approach. The main claimed benefit is a more compact model (smaller K). While parsimony is valuable, it is not clear that FAB's complexity penalty is superior to standard, well-understood regularization or Bayesian priors, especially when it does not lead to a clear win in predictive power.

3.  **Lack of Clarity on Core Theoretical Result:** The paper's central claim rests on Lemma 1, which describes the asymptotic behavior of a log-determinant. However, the proof and any significant intuition for this result are relegated to the supplementary materials. The main paper asks the reader to accept this pivotal claim on faith, which is insufficient for a rigorous technical contribution. The conditions under which this lemma holds should be stated and discussed clearly in the main text.

4.  **Philosophical Concerns with "Prior-Free" Inference:** The paper presents the "asymptotic ignorance of priors" as a desirable feature. From a Bayesian perspective, this is questionable. Priors are the mechanism for encoding domain knowledge and providing necessary regularization in finite-data regimes. An approach derived by explicitly nullifying their effect in the limit risks being brittle and performing poorly when data is not abundant. The "model induced regularization" that replaces the prior is justified only by these same asymptotic arguments. It is not convincingly demonstrated that this is a fundamentally better approach than a carefully chosen prior.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel theoretical extension of the FAB inference method to the important class of Latent Feature Models.
*   The proposed algorithm is fully automated and does not require hyperparameter tuning.
*   The CCCP interpretation and resulting acceleration method are elegant algorithmic contributions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The method's justification is purely asymptotic, and its effectiveness on finite, practical datasets is questionable and demonstrably poor in at least one reported case.
*   The experimental comparison with established baselines is not rigorous; key competitors are omitted or potentially misconfigured, weakening the claims of superior performance.
*   The performance gains in predictive accuracy over standard methods like variational EM are marginal in several key experiments.
*   The core theoretical claim (Lemma 1) is not adequately explained or motivated in the main text, hindering a proper assessment of the paper's technical soundness.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical idea, its practical significance is not convincingly demonstrated. The reliance on asymptotic theory is a major limitation, and the empirical evaluation is not strong enough to overcome skepticism about the method's real-world utility compared to well-established, better-understood Bayesian nonparametric and variational methods. The work feels more like a preliminary exploration of an idea rather than a complete, rigorously validated contribution ready for a top-tier venue like NIPS.
