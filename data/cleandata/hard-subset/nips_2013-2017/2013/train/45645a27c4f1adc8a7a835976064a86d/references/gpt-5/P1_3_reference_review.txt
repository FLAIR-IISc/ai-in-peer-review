Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian emissions. The core technical contribution is Lemma 1, showing that for each data dimension the log-determinant of the Hessian of the negative complete-data log-likelihood contributes a term of the form sum_k log sum_n z_nk / N, despite the fact that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian structure leveraged in prior FAB work for MMs. This yields a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs (Theorem 2) whose leading model-complexity terms match those for MMs, and thus a FAB algorithm with automatic feature pruning, asymptotic consistency of the FIC to the marginal log-likelihood, and a monotonic lower-bound optimization scheme.

On the algorithmic side, the authors derive mean-field E-step fixed-point updates with a novel shrinkage term D/(2 N π̃_k) that drives unused features to extinction, standard M-step updates identical to PPCA for W, b, Λ, and π updates that equal the average posterior feature usage. They reinterpret the FAB E-step as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), using multiple inner CCCP steps to accelerate early shrinkage, and propose a merge post-processing motivated by an identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) that favors balanced feature usage and suggests merging identical columns of W to improve the bound. Experiments on synthetic and several real datasets compare against IBP (accelerated Gibbs), VB-IBP, MEIBP, and EM, reporting that FAB achieves competitive or superior model selection and predictive log-likelihood with lower or comparable runtime, especially benefiting from the acceleration.

Strengths
- Novel asymptotic analysis: The Lemma 1 result is surprising and important—it shows the same “effective sample size” regularization log sum_n z_nk emerges for LFMs without the block-diagonal Hessian assumption used for MMs. This broadens the scope of FAB/FIC beyond mixtures.
- Principled selection without priors: The FIC-derived regularizer produces automatic feature pruning and helps resolve non-identifiability, with asymptotic equivalence of FIC and marginal log-likelihood.
- Practical algorithmic contributions: CCCP-based interpretation and the accelerateShrinkage routine are simple and effective, and the merge heuristic is well-motivated by the identifiability discussion.
- Empirical validation: Demonstrations on synthetic, block data, and multiple real datasets show good model selection and predictive performance, typically with compact K and less overfitting (PLL close to TLL). Shrinkage acceleration clearly reduces time-to-solution.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and proofs deferred: Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 hinge on “mild assumptions” with proofs in the supplement. The main paper would benefit from a clear statement of the key assumptions (e.g., scaling regimes, boundedness, conditions on Z, non-degenerate Λ) and a short proof sketch to assess when the result might fail (e.g., heavy-tailed noise, collinearity, extremely sparse/dense features).
- Fairness/clarity in experimental setup: The baselines (IBP/VB/MEIBP) reportedly do not infer noise variance; the paper fixes σ for them but does not clearly state whether FAB continues to estimate Λ. If FAB learns Λ while others do not, this can affect PLL/TLL comparisons. Please clarify and, if needed, include experiments with Λ fixed for FAB to match baselines.
- Sensitivity and hyperparameters: While FAB has no prior hyperparameters, the acceleration uses Tshrink, and initialization chooses K0 = min(N, D). Sensitivity analyses for Tshrink, initialization, and merge thresholds would strengthen the claims of robustness.
- Complexity and scaling: A per-iteration complexity and memory analysis would help contextualize results relative to MEIBP/IBP/VB. When is the acceleration most beneficial? How does runtime scale with N, D, K empirically?
- Merge step: The identifiability theorem motivates merging equal W columns; the practical merging based on Euclidean distances and bound checking is heuristic. More details and ablations on the merge frequency and effect on PLL/TLL would help.
- Limited comparisons: VB was omitted on real datasets due to runtime; MEIBP was omitted on some data for non-negativity or runtime. While understandable, this reduces completeness; at least include a discussion or smaller-scale runs to triangulate trends.

Quality
- The technical development appears sound, with a careful lower-bounding strategy and monotonic optimization. However, because the central asymptotic arguments are in the supplement, the main text makes it hard to fully verify the key steps. Empirical results are solid but could be more comprehensive regarding sensitivity and fairness.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The role of the shrinkage term and its origin from FIC is nicely explained. Suggestions: explicitly state all assumptions for Lemma 1/Theorem 2 in the main text; clarify whether Λ is learned in all experiments; add algorithmic complexity; clean minor typos and notation (e.g., consistency of vectors vs. matrices; clarify constants in J(Z, P)).

Originality
- High. Extending FAB/FIC to LFMs without the MM Hessian condition, the CCCP reinterpretation and acceleration, and the identifiability/merge analysis together constitute a substantial and novel contribution relative to previous FAB for MMs/HMMs, IBP, and MAD-Bayes.

Significance
- High. Model selection for LFMs is important and typically handled via IBP with substantial computational cost. A scalable, hyperparameter-free alternative with theoretical grounding and good empirical performance should interest the NIPS community and could spur further extensions to richer latent feature models.

Questions for the authors
- Please list the exact assumptions required for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 in the main paper, and provide a brief intuition/proof sketch.
- In the real-data experiments, is Λ learned by FAB? If so, can you report results with Λ fixed to match baselines?
- What is the per-iteration complexity of FAB with and without accelerateShrinkage? How does Tshrink affect convergence and pruning accuracy?
- Does accelerateShrinkage preserve monotonic improvement of the lower bound? If not, is there a safeguard?
- How sensitive are results to initialization (K0 and µ initialization)? Any strategies to mitigate bad local optima?

Minor comments
- Some small typos and formatting glitches (e.g., spacing, equation references) should be corrected.
- Please add a short discussion contrasting FIC/FAB with MAD-Bayes beyond the zero-noise limit.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and general theoretical contribution extending FAB to LFMs.
- Practical algorithm with clear benefits (automatic feature selection, acceleration).
- Strong empirical performance with compact models and reduced overfitting.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key theoretical assumptions and proofs are not visible in the main text.
- Some fairness/sensitivity issues in experiments need clarification.
- Merge step and acceleration are partly heuristic; more ablations would help.

Overall recommendation
- Accept (weak to moderate). The paper advances FAB to an important new class of models with both theoretical and practical contributions, and the empirical results are promising. Addressing the clarity and experimental fairness concerns would make it stronger.
