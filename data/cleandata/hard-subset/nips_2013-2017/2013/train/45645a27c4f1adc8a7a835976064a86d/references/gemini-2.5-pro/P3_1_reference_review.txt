### **Summary**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously applied to models like Mixture Models (MMs), to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core challenge is that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption that simplified the original FAB derivation. The authors' key theoretical contribution is showing (Lemma 1) that despite this structural difference, the log determinant of the LFM Hessian matrix asymptotically yields a penalty term of the same form (`sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)`) as in the MM case. This allows the derivation of a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs that is analogous to FIC for MMs, and which converges to the marginal log-likelihood. Based on this, the paper proposes an EM-like algorithm (FAB/LFM) to maximize a lower bound on the FIC, enabling automatic selection of the number of latent features (K) without explicit priors on model complexity. The paper further provides two valuable analyses: 1) an interpretation of the E-step updates as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), which inspires an effective acceleration technique, and 2) a proof of parameter identifiability under the FAB objective, which motivates a principled post-processing step for merging redundant features. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that FAB/LFM is competitive with or superior to non-parametric Bayesian methods (IBP) and variational Bayes in terms of model selection accuracy and predictive performance, often at a reduced computational cost.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is the elegant and non-trivial extension of the FAB framework. Lemma 1 is the crucial insight, demonstrating that the characteristic FAB complexity penalty arises naturally in LFMs even without the convenient block-diagonal Hessian structure. This significantly broadens the applicability of FAB and suggests a deeper principle at play in asymptotic Bayesian inference for singular models.

2.  **Principled Algorithm with Practical Refinements:** The paper goes beyond the core derivation to provide valuable algorithmic enhancements. The CCCP interpretation of the E-step is insightful and leads directly to the `accelerateShrinkage` procedure, which Figure 1 shows to be highly effective. Furthermore, the analysis of identifiability (Theorem 4) is not merely a theoretical curiosity; it provides a strong justification for the proposed feature merging post-processing step, addressing a known practical issue in LFM training.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against a strong and relevant set of baselines, including fast IBP samplers and the MAP-based MEIBP. The results consistently highlight the advantages of FAB/LFM:
    *   It achieves highly accurate model selection on synthetic data (Figure 2), often outperforming NPB methods, especially at larger N.
    *   It demonstrates superior predictive performance (higher PLL) on several real-world datasets while inferring more compact models (smaller K).
    *   The observation of a smaller gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods provides strong evidence that the "model-induced regularization" of FAB is effective at preventing overfitting.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly articulates the problem, presents the key theoretical results, builds the algorithm upon them, and validates it experimentally. The connections drawn between the theory and the practical algorithm design choices (e.g., acceleration, merging) are particularly clear.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The paper relies on "mild assumptions" for its central results (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2), which are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is standard for space constraints, the main text would benefit from at least an intuitive summary of these assumptions. Are they related to the distribution of latent features? The properties of the basis matrix W? Without this, it is difficult for the reader to assess the full scope and potential limitations of the theoretical claims.

2.  **The Asymptotic Regime:** FAB is an asymptotic method, and its validity hinges on a "large sample limit." The paper honestly acknowledges this limitation in the conclusion, noting that performance may degrade in small N scenarios (as suggested by the Libras result). However, the paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of when the asymptotic approximation is expected to hold. For instance, is it the absolute size of N, or the ratio N/D, that matters most? Providing these details for the datasets (especially Libras) would help practitioners understand the method's boundary conditions.

3.  **Potential Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The introduction claims FAB "does not have a hand-tunable hyper-parameter." While it avoids model complexity priors (like the IBP's concentration parameter), the proposed algorithm introduces `δ` (convergence tolerance) and `T_shrink` (number of acceleration steps). While likely not as sensitive as model priors, their impact is not discussed. A brief comment on the sensitivity to `T_shrink` in particular would be useful.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Relationship to Variational EM:** The paper positions the `D/(2N*pi_tilde_k)` term as the key difference from variational EM. This is an excellent point. It would be illuminating to interpret this term as an adaptive, data-dependent penalty on each feature's marginal probability (`pi_k`), which grows stronger (more negative) for features that are rarely used. This perspective might make the "model-induced regularization" concept even more intuitive.

*   **Merging Procedure:** How frequently was the merge post-processing step triggered in the real-world experiments? Providing this information would give a sense of its practical importance for achieving the reported compact models.

*   **Computational Cost:** While the elapsed times are reported, a complexity analysis would be helpful. Is the per-iteration complexity of FAB/LFM comparable to that of variational EM for LFMs? It appears to be, which would make the overall speedup (due to faster feature shrinkage) even more impressive.

*   **Initialization:** The paper states that the initial number of features was set to min(N,D). How sensitive is the final result to this initial K? Does starting with a much larger K significantly increase computation time before the unnecessary features are pruned?

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian approximate inference. It presents a novel and important theoretical extension of the FAB framework to a widely used class of models, LFMs. The theory is sound, and it is complemented by practical algorithmic improvements and a strong set of experiments. The work is original, technically deep, and the resulting method is likely to be of high interest to both researchers and practitioners working with latent feature models. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to clarifying the method's limitations, which can be easily addressed. I strongly recommend acceptance.
