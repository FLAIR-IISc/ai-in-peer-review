## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a key limitation of the original method. The authors convincingly demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that FAB can be successfully applied to LFMs, offering a compelling alternative to existing Bayesian non-parametric and variational Bayesian approaches, particularly in scenarios where computational efficiency is paramount. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a relevant problem in the field. While not revolutionary, the contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper extends the FAB framework, initially developed for mixture models, to the more general class of Latent Feature Models. FAB is a Bayesian inference technique that leverages asymptotic approximations to perform model selection (specifically, determining the dimensionality of latent features) without relying on explicit priors or hyperparameter tuning. A core challenge in applying FAB has been the requirement for a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete log-likelihood. The authors elegantly address this limitation for LFMs, proving that the necessary conditions for FIC derivation still hold despite the lack of a block-diagonal Hessian. This extends the applicability of FAB significantly.

The paper carefully positions itself within the existing landscape of LFM inference. It contrasts FAB with Bayesian non-parametric (BNP) methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and variational Bayesian (VB) approaches, highlighting the computational advantages of FAB, especially for large-scale datasets.  The authors correctly point out that while BNP and VB offer strong performance, their computational complexity can be prohibitive. They also relate their work to "MAD-Bayes," noting a key difference in the asymptotic assumptions made.  The references are comprehensive and well-chosen, demonstrating a solid understanding of the relevant literature.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The core theoretical result – demonstrating the applicability of FAB to LFMs despite the lack of a block-diagonal Hessian – is a valuable contribution. Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are clearly stated and well-motivated.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors convincingly argue, and demonstrate empirically, that FAB offers a significant speed advantage over BNP methods (IBP, MEIBP) and VB, especially for larger datasets. The shrinkage acceleration technique further enhances this efficiency.
* **Automatic Model Selection:**  Like the original FAB framework, this extension maintains the appealing property of automatic model selection – eliminating the need for manual hyperparameter tuning.  This simplifies the application of the method and makes it more accessible.
* **Identifiability Analysis:**  The discussion of parameter identifiability and the proposed merge post-processing step are insightful and demonstrate a careful consideration of the practical implications of the method. This is a crucial element for robust and reliable model selection.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented in a logical manner, and the algorithmic details are sufficiently explained.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments, encompassing both synthetic and real-world datasets, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparisons with established benchmarks are fair and informative.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experimental Validation:**  While the experiments are comprehensive, the performance gains of FAB are most pronounced in scenarios where the sample size (N) is large.  Further investigation into the performance of FAB in low-data regimes would be valuable.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** While the authors mention initialization, a more detailed analysis of the impact of different initialization strategies on convergence and solution quality would be beneficial.
* **Merge Post-processing Heuristic:** The criteria for merging latent features (Euclidean distance of W vectors and resulting increase in L) appears somewhat heuristic.  A more rigorous justification or exploration of alternative merging criteria would strengthen this aspect of the work.
* **VB Comparison:** The comparison with VB is slightly unfair as the VB experiments were run with a limited range of K values (2 to D). Exploring a wider range of K, particularly determined by an appropriate prior, might reveal more competitive results.



**4. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contribution significantly expands the applicability of the promising FAB framework.
* The demonstrated computational efficiency is a key advantage, particularly for large-scale data.
* The automatic model selection property simplifies the application of the method.
* The experimental results are compelling and support the claims made in the paper.
* The clear writing and thorough related work analysis contribute to the overall quality of the paper.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The performance benefits are most apparent in large-sample scenarios, and further investigation is needed for low-data regimes.
* The merge post-processing step relies on a somewhat heuristic criterion.
* The VB comparison could be improved by exploring a wider range of K values.



**5. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

* Briefly address the limitations of the method in low-data regimes and discuss potential avenues for improvement.
* Provide a more detailed discussion of initialization strategies and their impact on convergence.
* Justify the merging criterion more rigorously or explore alternative approaches.
* Expand the range of K values considered in the VB comparison, possibly using a more principled prior-based approach.




Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent feature models. The extension of FAB to LFMs is a significant step forward, and the demonstrated computational advantages make this work highly relevant to the NIPS community.
