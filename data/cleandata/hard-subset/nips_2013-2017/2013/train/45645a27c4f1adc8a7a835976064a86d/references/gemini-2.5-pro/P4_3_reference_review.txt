### Summary

This paper proposes an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for automatic model selection based on maximizing a lower bound of the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which is an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. Previous work applied FAB to models like Mixture Models (MMs), where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. The primary challenge in extending FAB to LFMs is that this block-diagonal condition is not met, as parameters for each data dimension are shared across all latent features.

The core technical contribution of this paper is to show that, despite this structural difference, the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs can be asymptotically decomposed into a sum of terms, one for each latent feature (Lemma 1). This key result allows the authors to derive an FIC for LFMs that has the same form as the FIC for MMs. This FIC includes a complexity penalty term `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)`, which naturally encourages shrinking the number of active features. The paper then develops an iterative algorithm (FAB/LFM) to optimize a tractable lower bound of this FIC.

Furthermore, the paper provides two additional analyses. First, it interprets the E-step update as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), which inspires a "shrinkage acceleration" method to speed up convergence by eliminating irrelevant features early in training. Second, it analyzes the parameter identifiability of the model under the FAB framework (Theorem 4), showing that FAB resolves ambiguities where different features have identical parameters. This analysis motivates a practical post-processing step to merge redundant features. The proposed method is empirically compared against variational EM and several state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian methods based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), showing favorable performance in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational cost.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper appears to be technically sound. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is the central contribution and hinges on Lemma 1, which provides an asymptotic approximation for the log-determinant of the Hessian. While the full proof and assumptions are in the supplementary material, the result itself is plausible and enables the extension of the FAB framework in a principled manner. The subsequent derivation of the lower bound and the alternating optimization algorithm follows established techniques from previous FAB literature and variational inference.

The analyses in Section 3 add significant value. The interpretation of the E-step as a CCCP is insightful and leads to a practical acceleration algorithm that is shown to be effective empirically. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) provides theoretical justification for the model's behavior and informs a sensible merge post-processing step. These additions demonstrate a thorough and careful treatment of the proposed method.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering synthetic and real-world datasets and comparing against strong baselines. The results generally support the paper's claims, showing that FAB/LFM can find more compact models (smaller K) with competitive or superior predictive performance and often at a lower computational cost.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the work, explains the limitations of prior FAB methods, and situates the contribution within the context of related work on LFMs, such as IBP. The core technical challenge and the key result (Lemma 1) are presented clearly in Section 2. The algorithm is well-described, and the connection between the theoretical penalty term and the practical shrinkage effect in the update equations is explained well. The structure, flowing from the core theory to the algorithm and then to further analysis and refinements, is logical and easy to follow.

A minor point for improvement would be to briefly state the nature of the "mild assumptions" for Lemma 1 in the main text, as this lemma is central to the entire contribution. However, relegating full details to the supplement is standard practice.

### Originality and Significance

The work is original. It represents a non-trivial extension of the recently developed FAB framework to a new, important class of models. Overcoming the block-diagonal Hessian limitation is a key step that suggests the FAB framework may have broader applicability than previously shown. This work is distinct from prior IBP-based approaches, offering a deterministic, asymptotic alternative to stochastic sampling or variational methods for model selection in LFMs. It is also different from standard criteria like BIC, as the model complexity term is data-dependent.

The significance of this work is potentially high. LFMs are widely used, and determining the correct number of latent features is a critical and difficult problem. The proposed FAB/LFM method offers an automated, hyperparameter-free solution that appears to be both computationally efficient and effective. The observation that FAB achieves good predictive performance while producing more parsimonious models (smaller K) is particularly compelling, as is the evidence that it is less prone to overfitting (smaller gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods). If these results hold more broadly, FAB/LFM could become a valuable tool for practitioners. The shrinkage acceleration technique is also a useful practical contribution.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The paper successfully extends the FAB inference framework to LFMs by overcoming a significant technical hurdle (the non-block-diagonal Hessian), demonstrating the framework's wider applicability.
2.  **Principled Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm is derived from a clear objective function (the FIC) and is supported by sound asymptotic theory.
3.  **Practical Advantages:** The method is fully automatic (no tunable hyperparameters for model selection) and is shown to be computationally faster than prominent non-parametric Bayesian alternatives.
4.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** Experiments demonstrate that FAB/LFM achieves competitive or superior predictive performance while identifying more compact models, suggesting effective regularization against overfitting.
5.  **Valuable Insights:** The CCCP interpretation and identifiability analysis provide deeper understanding and lead to practical algorithmic improvements (acceleration and merging).

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

1.  **Experimental Comparison Details:** The experimental setup could be slightly improved for fairness. For instance, the noise variance is learned by FAB/LFM but fixed for the baselines. While the authors state they follow protocols from prior work, this difference could impact performance and complicates a direct comparison.
2.  **Asymptotic Limitations:** The method's justification is asymptotic in the number of data points (N). While the paper acknowledges potential performance degradation in small-N scenarios (e.g., the Libras dataset), the limitations of the asymptotic approximation are not explored in depth.

Overall, the strengths of the paper convincingly outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, technically sound, and practically significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection for latent variable models.
