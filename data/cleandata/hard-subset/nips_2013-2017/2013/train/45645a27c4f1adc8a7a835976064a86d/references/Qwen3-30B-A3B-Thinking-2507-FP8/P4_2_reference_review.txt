

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), which represent a significant theoretical and algorithmic advancement. Previous FAB work [5, 6] was limited to models satisfying specific conditions (block diagonal Hessian matrices), but the authors demonstrate through Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 that FAB inference can be applied to LFMs despite their failure to meet these conditions. The paper shows that FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the assumptions A1 and A2 used in previous work. This generalization is significant as it broadens the applicability of FAB inference to a wider class of latent variable models. Related work on LFMs includes Infinite Latent Feature Models [9, 17], IBP [10, 11], and variational Bayesian approaches [3], but none of these provide the automated model selection capability with the computational efficiency demonstrated in this work.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide complete proofs for their main results (Lemma 1, Theorem 2, Theorem 4) in the supplementary materials, establishing the asymptotic equivalence between FIC and marginal log-likelihood for LFMs. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing FAB/LFMs with state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) across artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets. The results consistently show FAB/LFM achieves better model selection accuracy (particularly in estimating the true number of latent features K) with lower computational cost. The authors carefully address the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, including the theoretical justification for the shrinkage effect and the identifiability analysis. The paper is well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from introduction through theoretical development to experimental evaluation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are clear and provide sufficient detail for reproduction. The connection between FAB and CCCP is well-explained, though the paper could benefit from slightly more intuitive explanation of this relationship for readers unfamiliar with the CCCP optimization technique. The experimental setup is adequately described, and the results are presented with appropriate statistical measures (computational time, model selection accuracy, predictive performance).

**Originality:** This work makes a significant original contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs, which was previously thought to be impossible due to the models' failure to satisfy the required conditions. The key insight that LFMs can still produce the same FIC form as MMs despite not meeting conditions A1 and A2 is novel and well-supported by theoretical analysis. The shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation is also novel, as is the identifiability analysis and merge post-processing approach. This work is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on FAB inference [5, 6] and on LFMs [9, 10, 17], representing a meaningful extension of both the FAB framework and latent feature modeling.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. Model selection for LFMs is a challenging problem that has been addressed by previous methods [3, 10, 16] but with high computational costs that limit scalability to large datasets. The paper demonstrates that FAB/LFMs achieve better model selection accuracy than state-of-the-art methods while requiring less computational resources. The method's automation (no hyperparameters to tune) and theoretical guarantees (parameter identifiability) make it particularly valuable for practical applications. The empirical results on real-world datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS) show FAB/LFMs achieve better predictive performance with more compact model representations, suggesting significant potential for broader adoption in the machine learning community.

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical extension of FAB inference to LFMs despite violating previous conditions
- Rigorous proofs of main results with appropriate asymptotic analysis
- Novel shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing superior performance
- Practical implementation details and analysis of computational efficiency

**Weaknesses:**
- The connection to CCCP could be made more intuitive for readers unfamiliar with this optimization technique
- The paper could provide more intuition about why LFMs satisfy the FIC form despite not meeting the previous conditions
- The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) is theoretically interesting but might benefit from more concrete examples

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Significant theoretical contribution extending FAB inference to a new class of models
- Strong empirical results demonstrating better accuracy and efficiency than state-of-the-art methods
- Novel algorithmic improvements (shrinkage acceleration) with practical impact
- Well-structured paper with rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments
- Addresses an important problem (automated model selection for LFMs) with meaningful solution

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Some technical depth in the theoretical analysis might be challenging for general NIPS audience
- Limited discussion of limitations beyond the computational efficiency claims

The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The extension of FAB inference to LFMs is both theoretically novel and practically valuable, with strong empirical support. The work advances the state of the art in automated model selection for latent feature models, offering a more efficient and accurate alternative to current methods. The paper meets all four criteria for NIPS acceptance, with particularly strong contributions in originality and significance.
