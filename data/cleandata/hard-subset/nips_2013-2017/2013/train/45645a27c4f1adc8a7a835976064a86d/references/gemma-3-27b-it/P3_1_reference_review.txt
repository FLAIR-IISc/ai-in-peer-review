## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB offers a computationally efficient, automated approach to model selection (dimensionality of latent features) without requiring prior specification or hyperparameter tuning. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not initially satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for the original FAB formulation, a similar FIC can be derived. They further present theoretical analyses regarding parameter identifiability and develop a shrinkage acceleration technique based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) to improve computational efficiency.  The paper is accompanied by experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrating competitive performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly on the FAB framework introduced in Fujimaki et al. (2016, 2017) for MMs and HMMs. It addresses a significant limitation of the original FAB – its restricted applicability to models with a block-diagonal Hessian. The authors position their work within the broader context of Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and variational Bayesian (VB) approaches for LFM dimensionality selection, acknowledging the computational bottlenecks of these methods. They also relate their work to "MAD-Bayes" but highlight their distinct asymptotic approach, avoiding prior-derived regularization. The connections to CCCP and existing work on identifiability are clearly delineated.

**Strengths:**

* **Extends a Promising Framework:** Successfully extending FAB to LFMs expands the applicability of this computationally efficient and automated Bayesian inference method.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, despite the lack of the original block-diagonal condition, is a significant theoretical contribution.  Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 provide a solid foundation for the proposed approach.
* **Identifiability Analysis:** The analysis of parameter identifiability and the development of a merge post-processing step are valuable additions, addressing potential non-uniqueness issues in the model.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration technique, leveraging a CCCP interpretation, demonstrably improves the computational speed of the FAB algorithm.
* **Empirical Validation:** Comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods in terms of model selection and predictive performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and algorithmic steps. The notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Derivation:**  While theoretically sound, the derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the supporting lemmas might be challenging to fully grasp for readers not deeply familiar with asymptotic Bayesian inference. More intuitive explanations could be beneficial.  Specifically, the jump from Lemma 1 to Theorem 2 feels a little abrupt.
* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Guarantees:**  The assumptions underlying Lemma 1 are mentioned but not thoroughly discussed.  Understanding the limitations of these assumptions is crucial for assessing the broader applicability of the method.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity of Acceleration:** While the acceleration technique shows significant speedups, the sensitivity of the performance to the `Tshrink` parameter (number of CCCP steps) is not sufficiently explored. A more systematic study of this parameter's impact would be useful.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons to IBP, VB, and MEIBP are valuable, more comparisons to state-of-the-art deep learning based approaches for feature learning (e.g., autoencoders, sparse coding) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's strengths and weaknesses, especially in the real-world data experiments.
* **Merge Post-processing Details:** The description of the merge post-processing is brief and lacks quantitative details on the criteria used for merging (Euclidean distance threshold, for example).  More specifics would be helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant limitation of the FAB framework, broadening its applicability.
* Presents novel theoretical results regarding FIC derivation and parameter identifiability.
* Demonstrates compelling empirical results highlighting the method's performance and efficiency.
* Offers a potentially valuable tool for automated model selection in latent feature modeling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical derivations might be too complex for a broad audience.
* The scope of the theoretical guarantees is limited by the assumptions made.
* The sensitivity of the acceleration technique to hyperparameters needs further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent feature models. The authors successfully extend the FAB framework to a more general class of models, provide solid theoretical justification for their approach, and demonstrate its effectiveness through comprehensive experiments. While some aspects of the theoretical derivations and the experimental setup could be further refined, the overall quality of the work is high. 

**Rating:**  **Strong Accept** 

I believe this paper is well-suited for NIPS and will be of interest to researchers working in Bayesian inference, latent variable modeling, and machine learning. I encourage the authors to consider addressing the minor weaknesses identified above in a revised version.
