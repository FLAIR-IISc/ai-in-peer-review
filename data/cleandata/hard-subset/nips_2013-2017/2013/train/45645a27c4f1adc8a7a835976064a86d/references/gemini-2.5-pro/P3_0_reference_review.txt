This paper presents an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a recent method for approximate Bayesian model selection that maximizes a lower bound on a "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC), which converges to the marginal log-likelihood in the large sample limit. Prior work had applied FAB to models like Mixture Models (MMs), which satisfy a convenient block-diagonal Hessian assumption. The core challenge addressed here is that LFMs do not satisfy this assumption due to parameter sharing across features.

The central technical contribution is Lemma 1, which demonstrates that despite the lack of a block-diagonal Hessian, the log-determinant of the Hessian for an LFM's parameters has the same asymptotic form as in the MM case. This key result allows the authors to derive an FIC for LFMs that includes the same characteristic penalty term, `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)`, which drives model selection by shrinking inactive features. Based on this FIC, the paper develops an iterative algorithm (FAB/LFM) that alternates between E-like and M-like steps to optimize a tractable lower bound. Further contributions include a novel interpretation of the E-step updates as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), leading to a practical "shrinkage acceleration" technique, and a theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability, which justifies a post-processing step for merging redundant latent features. The method is evaluated against non-parametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and its variants, showing competitive or superior performance in terms of model selection and prediction with lower computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Extension:** The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to a more complex and widely used class of models (LFMs). This is a non-trivial contribution that broadens the applicability of FAB and demonstrates that the underlying principles are more general than previously shown.
2.  **Elegant Key Technical Result (Lemma 1):** The derivation showing that the crucial `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)` complexity term re-emerges in the LFM setting, despite the violation of the assumptions from the MM case, is the paper's highlight. It provides a principled way to perform model selection in this context, connecting it to the broader theory of singular learning models.
3.  **Practical Algorithmic Refinements:** The CCCP interpretation of the E-step is insightful and leads directly to the `accelerateShrinkage` algorithm. As Figure 1 demonstrates, this is not just a theoretical curiosity but a significant practical improvement that drastically reduces computation time by pruning the model space more aggressively in early iterations.
4.  **Theoretical Grounding for Heuristics:** The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) is a valuable contribution. It provides a solid theoretical justification for the common problem of redundant features (`w_k = w_l`) and motivates a principled `merge` post-processing step, moving it from an ad-hoc heuristic to a theoretically-backed procedure.
5.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough, comparing FAB/LFM against relevant and state-of-the-art baselines (IBP-Gibbs, VB-IBP, MEIBP) on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The results compellingly demonstrate that FAB/LFM often achieves better or comparable predictive performance with a more compact model (smaller K) and significantly reduced runtime compared to sampling or full VB methods. The small gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods for FAB is strong evidence of its effective regularization.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity on Core Assumptions:** The entire theoretical foundation rests on Lemma 1, which holds "under some mild assumptions" that are deferred to the supplementary material. For a result of this importance, the main paper should at least summarize what these assumptions are and provide some intuition for why they are "mild" and likely to hold in practice. Without this, it is difficult for the reader to assess the robustness and generality of the method.
2.  **Limitations of the Asymptotic Regime:** The framework is explicitly asymptotic. While the experiments show good performance on finite datasets, the paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations. How large does N need to be relative to D and K for the `Op(1)` terms to be negligible and for the FIC to be a reliable approximation? The single poorer result on the small-N 'Libras' dataset hints at this limitation, and a more direct exploration of this boundary condition would strengthen the paper.
3.  **"Hyperparameter-Free" Claim:** The paper emphasizes that FAB is automated and avoids hand-tunable hyperparameters. However, the proposed algorithm introduces `T_shrink` (set to 100) for the acceleration step and relies on a `delta` for convergence. More critically, the `merge` post-processing step, as described, likely relies on a distance threshold to identify "overlapping features" in practice. These feel like new, potentially sensitive hyperparameters. The claim of being "fully automated" should be softened, and the sensitivity to these new parameters (especially `T_shrink` and the merge threshold) should be analyzed.

**Questions and Detailed Comments:**

*   In the experimental comparison with IBP, the hyperparameter `alpha` was fixed to 3, with the authors noting this "might cause overestimation of K." This is a critical point, as the performance of IBP is sensitive to this parameter. Was any effort made to tune `alpha` for the IBP baseline, for instance, by placing a hyperprior on it? A fixed, potentially suboptimal setting for the baseline could inflate the perceived advantage of FAB/LFM.
*   The connection between the M-step and PPCA is mentioned. Could you elaborate on this? Since Z is binary, the model is a form of binary factor analysis, not PPCA which assumes Gaussian latent variables. Is it that the conditional expectation `E[ZZ^T]` makes the updates mathematically equivalent? A clarification would be helpful.
*   While MEIBP is much faster, its predictive performance is notably worse in the experiments. Do the authors have an intuition for why this MAP-based approach underperforms so significantly compared to the asymptotic Bayesian approach of FAB?

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. It elegantly extends an interesting theoretical framework (FAB) to an important class of models (LFMs) with solid theoretical justification and compelling practical results. The algorithmic enhancements are clever and impactful. Despite some minor weaknesses regarding the clarity of assumptions and the "hyperparameter-free" claim, the strengths in originality, technical quality, and significance are substantial. The work is likely to be of great interest to researchers in Bayesian machine learning and will inspire further applications of the FAB framework. I recommend acceptance.
