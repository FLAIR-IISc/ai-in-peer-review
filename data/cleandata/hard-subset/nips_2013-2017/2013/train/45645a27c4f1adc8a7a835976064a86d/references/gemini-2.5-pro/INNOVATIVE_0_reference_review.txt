### Summary

This paper proposes an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core contribution is a novel theoretical analysis showing that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs—a model class that violates the block-diagonal Hessian assumption required by previous FAB methods—asymptotically takes the same form as for simpler models like mixture models. This leads to a principled, non-prior-based regularization term that automatically selects the number of latent features. The authors derive a practical algorithm, FAB/LFM, and enhance it with a "shrinkage acceleration" technique inspired by a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation, and a post-processing step to merge redundant features guided by an identifiability analysis. The work is positioned as a computationally efficient and fully automatic alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). Extensive experiments demonstrate that FAB/LFM is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art IBP variants in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational speed.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its bold conceptual contribution. Prior work on FAB inference was confined to a restrictive class of models. This paper breaks that boundary by demonstrating, via Lemma 1, that the elegant FAB regularization term `log(sum_n z_nk)` emerges naturally even in LFMs where parameters are shared across features. This is a non-obvious and significant theoretical insight that greatly expands the applicability and importance of the FAB framework. It suggests that this form of "model induced regularization" may be a more general principle for Bayesian model selection in singular models than previously understood.

2.  **A Compelling Alternative to Non-Parametric Bayes:** The paper presents a compelling and fundamentally different approach to the long-standing problem of determining the dimensionality of latent features. Instead of placing a prior over an infinite number of features like the IBP, it uses an asymptotic argument to derive a penalty term directly from the model structure and the data. This results in a fully automated method without the need to specify or infer hyperparameters like the IBP's concentration parameter `alpha`, which is a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Clever Algorithmic Refinements:** The paper goes beyond the core theoretical derivation and presents thoughtful solutions to practical challenges. The interpretation of the E-step update as a CCCP, leading to the "shrinkage acceleration" algorithm, is a clever way to speed up the elimination of irrelevant features and reduce overall computation time. Furthermore, the identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and the resulting merge post-processing step provide a principled way to handle a common pathology in LFMs, demonstrating a deep understanding of the model class.

4.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed, comparing FAB/LFM against a strong set of baselines including multiple modern IBP implementations. The results convincingly show that the proposed method is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical and powerful tool. It achieves state-of-the-art or better performance in model recovery on synthetic data and predictive log-likelihood on real-world datasets, often with significantly lower computational cost. The finding that FAB/LFM is less prone to overfitting (as indicated by the small gap between training and predictive likelihood) provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of its unique regularization.

### Weaknesses

While the paper is strong, there are a few minor points that could be considered weaknesses, though in my view, they do not detract from the overall contribution.

1.  **Reliance on Asymptotic Approximations:** The entire framework is built on large-sample (N → ∞) asymptotics. While the experiments show strong performance even for moderately sized N, the theoretical guarantees are asymptotic. The authors are commendably honest about this, noting that performance was weaker on one small dataset (Libras). This is an inherent limitation of the approach, but an acceptable one given the benefits.

2.  **Use of Mean-Field Approximation:** The practical algorithm relies on a factorized variational approximation for the posterior over the latent variables `Z`. This is a standard technique used by many competing methods (e.g., VB for IBP) to ensure tractability, but it introduces an additional approximation gap that is not fully characterized. However, given the strong empirical results, this appears to be a reasonable and effective practical choice.

### Justification for Rating

I am recommending a strong accept for this paper. It presents a significant, novel, and bold idea that pushes the boundaries of a promising inference framework. The central theoretical result—that the FIC for LFMs has the same form as for simpler models—is a genuinely surprising and important finding. This work provides a fresh and powerful perspective on automated model selection, offering a compelling alternative to the dominant non-parametric Bayesian paradigm. While the method relies on approximations (asymptotic and mean-field), these are well-justified trade-offs for tractability and automation. The authors back up their novel theory with a robust, efficient algorithm and a comprehensive set of experiments that clearly demonstrate its practical advantages over state-of-the-art methods. This paper is a complete piece of work, combining novel theory, clever algorithm design, and strong empirical validation. It is precisely the kind of innovative and impactful research that NIPS should showcase.
