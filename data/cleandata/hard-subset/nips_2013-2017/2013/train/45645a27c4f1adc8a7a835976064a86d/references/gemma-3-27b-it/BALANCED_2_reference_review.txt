This paper introduces Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference for Latent Feature Models (LFMs), extending a previously developed framework for mixture models. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions required for the original FAB method (block diagonal Hessian), the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) exhibits a similar form, allowing for the application of FAB. The authors further contribute theoretical analysis regarding parameter identifiability and propose a shrinkage acceleration method inspired by a connection to the convex-concave procedure (CCCP). They support their claims with experimental evaluations on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical justification for extending FAB to LFMs, demonstrating the crucial Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, which establish the applicability of the FIC framework. The identifiability result (Theorem 4) is a valuable theoretical contribution.
* **Practical Acceleration:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, based on CCCP, appears effective in reducing computational cost, as evidenced by the experimental results (Figure 1). This addresses a key limitation of the original FAB method.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. Mathematical derivations are presented with reasonable clarity.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments cover both synthetic and real datasets, comparing FAB/LFMs with several relevant baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM). The inclusion of both model selection accuracy and predictive performance metrics provides a thorough evaluation.
* **Addressing Non-Identifiability:**  The analysis of parameter identifiability and the proposed merge post-processing step are important contributions that address a potential issue in LFM inference.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively connects the proposed method to existing literature on Bayesian nonparametrics, variational inference, and asymptotic Bayesian inference, providing necessary context and demonstrating an awareness of related approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** While the extension of FAB to LFMs is a worthwhile contribution, the core idea of adapting an existing inference method to a new model class isn't inherently groundbreaking. The primary novelty resides in the mathematical analysis that justifies this adaptation.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are comprehensive, they mainly focus on demonstrating the effectiveness of the acceleration method and comparing performance with existing approaches. A deeper investigation into the properties of the learned latent features (e.g., interpretability) could further strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the shrinkage acceleration method is shown to improve runtime, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of FAB/LFMs compared to other methods would be beneficial.  What are the scaling properties with respect to N and D?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., δ, Tshrink) isn't thoroughly discussed. How robust are the findings to variations in these settings?
* **MEIBP Comparison:** The comparison with MEIBP seems somewhat hampered by the difficulty in tuning its parameters, especially for the larger datasets. A more fair comparison might require more extensive parameter exploration for MEIBP. The statement that MEIBP is only applicable to non-negative data is incorrect.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution by extending the FAB framework to a new model class (LFMs) with a rigorous mathematical justification.
* The proposed acceleration method significantly improves the computational efficiency of FAB inference.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance compared to established methods.
* The analysis of parameter identifiability and the proposed merging post-processing are valuable theoretical and practical contributions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The core idea is an adaptation of existing techniques, and the novelty mainly lies in the mathematical analysis.
* The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, doesn't provide a deep understanding of the learned latent features.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters and the detailed computational complexity analysis are lacking.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed acceleration method is effective, and the experimental results are promising. While the core idea isn't dramatically novel, the extension of FAB to LFMs is a significant step forward, and the accompanying analysis and improvements make this a worthwhile contribution.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed computational complexity analysis of FAB/LFMs.
* Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters.
* Explore the interpretability of the learned latent features in the experiments.
* Conduct a more thorough parameter tuning for MEIBP to ensure a fair comparison.
* Elaborate on the practical implications of the identifiability analysis and merging post-processing. Consider providing examples of how this could be used in real-world applications.
