

# NIPS Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously developed for mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for FAB in MMs, LFMs share the same FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) form as MMs. The paper introduces a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP (Convex Concave Procedure) and establishes parameter identifiability for FAB/LFMs, leading to a natural merging post-processing step. The authors show FAB/LFMs achieve superior model selection accuracy and computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The key insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as MMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition (Lemma 1) is novel and significant. This fundamentally expands the applicability of FAB beyond the restricted class of models previously considered.

2. **Practical Innovation**: The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) based on CCCP is a valuable practical contribution that significantly reduces computational cost while maintaining accuracy. The empirical results in Figure 1 compellingly demonstrate this improvement.

3. **Theoretical Rigor**: The identifiability result (Theorem 4) provides important theoretical grounding for the method, explaining why FAB naturally avoids overfitting and how to perform post-processing to merge redundant features.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments across synthetic and real-world datasets robustly demonstrate FAB's advantages in model selection accuracy (especially for large N), computational efficiency, and reduced overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL gap).

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Overstated Novelty**: The paper claims FAB "asymptotically ignores priors," but this is not novel—this is a well-known property of marginal likelihood-based methods and asymptotic Bayesian inference. The paper should more clearly distinguish FAB from similar approaches like "MAD-Bayes" [1], which also exploits asymptotic properties without priors.

2. **Insufficient Related Work**: The related work section misses key recent advances in Bayesian nonparametric models for LFMs. For example, works like "Bayesian Nonparametric Latent Feature Models" (Miller et al., 2010) and "Infinite Latent Feature Models" (Griffiths & Ghahramani, 2005) should be more thoroughly compared. The comparison with MEIBP [22] is insufficient—MEIBP is a MAP approach, while FAB is an approximation to marginal likelihood, making the comparison somewhat apples-to-oranges.

3. **Limited Theoretical Depth**: The paper mentions "rigorous proofs in supplementary materials" but fails to present key insights in the main paper. For instance, the critical assumption in Lemma 1 that enables the log det |F(d)| approximation needs more intuitive explanation for non-specialists.

4. **Experimental Limitations**: The comparison with variational methods (VB) is incomplete. The paper states "we totally omitted VB because of its long computational time," but doesn't provide a fair comparison for small-scale problems where VB might be competitive. Also, the real-world experiments omit a direct comparison with recent scalable Bayesian methods for LFMs.

## Critical Evaluation

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, demonstrating that the method can be applied to a broader class of models than previously thought possible. The theoretical insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as MMs despite different structural properties is particularly valuable.

However, the paper overstates the novelty of key aspects (asymptotic prior ignorance, automatic model selection) and doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related asymptotic Bayesian approaches. The related work section needs significant expansion to properly contextualize the contribution.

The experimental validation is strong for the claims made, but the omission of direct comparisons with recent scalable Bayesian methods for LFMs weakens the significance claim. The paper should address whether FAB/LFMs could be extended to more complex LFM variants (e.g., non-Gaussian observations) or compared with recent deep Bayesian models.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound extension of FAB inference to LFMs with solid theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. The key insight about the shared FIC form between MMs and LFMs is novel and significant. However, the paper's claims about novelty and significance are overstated, and the related work section requires substantial revision to properly position the contribution within the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to:
1. Clarify the relationship between FAB and similar asymptotic Bayesian approaches (MAD-Bayes, etc.)
2. Expand the related work section to include recent advances in Bayesian nonparametric LFMs
3. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the "asymptotic prior ignorance" property
4. Include a fairer comparison with recent scalable Bayesian methods for LFMs

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory and practice of model selection for latent variable models, and with these revisions, it would be a strong NIPS paper. The method's computational efficiency and automatic model selection make it particularly relevant for large-scale applications where current state-of-the-art methods are prohibitively expensive.
