Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously developed for mixture models (MMs) and HMMs, to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian emissions. The core technical result (Lemma 1) shows that, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian structure used in FAB for MMs, the log-determinant of the Hessian with respect to per-dimension parameters θd asymptotically decomposes to a sum of log feature-usage counts, yielding an FIC penalty term of the form (D/2)∑k log ∑n znk. Consequently, the asymptotic approximation of the marginal complete log-likelihood has the same FIC structure as for MMs: J(Z,P) = log p(X,Z|P) − |P| − (DK/2) log N − (D/2)∑k log ∑n znk, leading to an FIC objective that asymptotically matches the marginal log-likelihood.

To optimize a tractable lower bound, the paper employs: (i) linearization of the convex −log ∑n znk term around Nπ̃k (as in prior FAB work), (ii) alternating maximization over the variational distribution q(Z), parameters P, and π̃, and (iii) a mean-field approximation q(zn)=∏k Bern(µnk). The E-step yields a closed-form fixed-point update for µnk via a sigmoid with two regularizers: a prior-based η(πk) and an FAB-induced shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k) that encourages automatic feature elimination. The M-step has PPCA-like closed-form updates for W, Λ, b; π and π̃ are set to the average activation, ∑n µnk/N. A shrinkage step removes features with ∑n µnk/N ∈ {0,1}, folding always-on features into the bias.

The paper also interprets the µ/π̃ updates as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), and proposes “accelerateShrinkage,” which performs multiple CCCP inner steps to hasten feature elimination without violating monotonicity of the lower bound. Finally, it analyzes identifiability: FAB’s regularization resolves certain non-identifiabilities (e.g., duplicated columns in W) and motivates a merge post-processing that combines nearly duplicate features if it increases the bound.

Empirically, the method is compared against variational EM (without FAB regularization), IBP with accelerated Gibbs sampling, a finite-K VB method, and MEIBP. On synthetic data, the acceleration reduces runtime while preserving final objective and model selection. FAB estimates K more accurately than competitors for large N. On block data, FAB recovers clean features with fewer spurious components. On several real datasets with missing values, FAB achieves competitive or superior predictive log-likelihoods with smaller K and reasonable runtimes; MEIBP is sometimes faster but yields worse predictive performance. VB is omitted on large datasets due to computational cost.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A nontrivial asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) showing that LFMs inherit the same FIC penalty structure as MMs, despite lacking block-diagonal Hessians. This extends the applicability of FAB to a broader class of latent variable models.
- Algorithmic design: A monotone, tractable lower-bound optimization with closed-form E/M updates and automatic feature shrinkage independent of priors. The CCCP interpretation is insightful and leads to a practical acceleration that demonstrably reduces runtime.
- Identifiability and post-processing: Analysis clarifies how FAB regularization resolves certain non-identifiabilities and justifies a feature-merge operation that can further simplify models.
- Empirical results: Across synthetic and real datasets, FAB generally selects compact models with good predictive performance, often outperforming or matching IBP/MEIBP/EM in accuracy while being computationally efficient. The method requires no sensitive hyperparameters and provides automation of K selection.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Reliance on asymptotics: The main theoretical guarantees (FIC equivalence and constant-gap to marginal likelihood) are asymptotic. Finite-sample behavior is not analyzed, and the practical impact of the Op(1) terms is not quantified.
- Assumptions deferred: The “mild assumptions” underpinning Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are not stated in the main text. Given the centrality of Lemma 1, a concise statement of conditions (e.g., regularity, boundedness, eigenvalue behavior) should appear in the paper body.
- Mean-field approximation: While standard in VB for LFMs, the impact of the mean-field factorization on model selection accuracy and uncertainty quantification is not discussed.
- Experimental fairness: It is unclear whether FAB also used a fixed noise variance in experiments where baselines did not learn Λ. The text suggests baselines used fixed variance (e.g., 1 or 0.75), but does not explicitly state whether FAB learned Λ or was constrained to the same value. If FAB learned Λ, performance gains may partially reflect that advantage.
- Baseline settings: IBP’s α is fixed at 3, which may bias K upward; MEIBP initializations were modified (e.g., K=100) for some datasets due to runtime; VB is omitted on large datasets. A sensitivity analysis to α and initial K, and reporting of VB on at least some moderate-scale settings, would strengthen claims.
- Complexity analysis: The paper lacks explicit time and space complexity per iteration in terms of N, D, K, and a breakdown of where acceleration helps most. A quantitative ablation on the number of CCCP inner steps (Tshrink) and its effect on pruning accuracy would be useful.
- Shrinkage and merging: The safe removal condition is exact (∑n µnk/N ∈ {0,1}); in practice, accelerated updates may drive µ close to but not exactly to 0 or 1. Clear criteria/thresholds and guarantees against premature pruning are not detailed. Similarly, the merge procedure depends on a distance threshold; its effect is anecdotal and could benefit from a controlled analysis.
- Scope: The extension is demonstrated for linear-Gaussian LFMs with diagonal precision. Discussion on extending to non-Gaussian likelihoods or non-diagonal noise would broaden impact.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The derivations are plausible and consistent with prior FAB work; monotonic lower-bound optimization is standard. However, key proofs and assumptions are deferred; clearer statements in the main text would improve rigor. Empirics are supportive but could be more comprehensive and balanced regarding baseline capabilities and settings.
- Clarity: Generally well organized with intuitive explanations. Some notational and grammatical issues exist. Important assumptions and experimental details (noise variance handling, pruning/merge thresholds) should be clarified. Providing complexity and sensitivity analyses would aid readers.
- Originality: The extension of FAB to LFMs via the new asymptotic determinant identity is novel. The CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability-informed merging are also original contributions relative to prior FAB and IBP literature.
- Significance: Automatic, hyperparameter-free model selection for LFMs is valuable. The approach appears competitive with IBP-family methods while being simpler and efficient, which is likely to be of interest to practitioners working with latent feature models.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs with a clear, practically useful algorithm.
- Insightful CCCP connection and effective acceleration.
- Identifiability analysis and principled merge post-processing.
- Empirical results showing good model selection and predictive performance with compact representations.

Cons for acceptance
- Central assumptions and proofs not presented in the main text.
- Incomplete discussion of experimental fairness (noise variance learning, α sensitivity, VB omission) and lack of complexity/sensitivity analyses.
- Finite-sample behavior and robustness of shrinkage/merging not thoroughly examined.

Suggestions for improvement
- State the assumptions of Lemma 1/Theorem 2 in the main paper and briefly sketch the proof idea.
- Clarify whether FAB learned Λ in experiments; if so, include runs with Λ fixed to match baselines, or enable baselines to learn noise variance.
- Add sensitivity analyses for α (IBP), initial K (MEIBP), Tshrink, and merge thresholds.
- Provide per-iteration complexity and an ablation of the acceleration’s effect on runtime and pruning accuracy.
- Include at least one VB comparison on a mid-scale dataset and report results for tuned IBP/MEIBP hyperparameters.
- Release code to improve reproducibility.
