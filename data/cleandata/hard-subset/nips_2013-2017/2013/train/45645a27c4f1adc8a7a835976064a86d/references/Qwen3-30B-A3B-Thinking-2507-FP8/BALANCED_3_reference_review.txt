

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), building on previous work by the authors on FAB for mixture models (MMs) [5, 6]. The key insight is that despite LFMs not satisfying the conditions (A1 and A2) that previously enabled FAB for MMs, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for MMs. This theoretical contribution is significant as it demonstrates the broader applicability of the FIC framework beyond MMs.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are the paper's most important contributions, showing that LFMs can leverage FAB despite not satisfying the conditions for MMs. This is a non-trivial extension that significantly broadens the scope of FAB inference. The identifiability theorem (Theorem 4) is also valuable for understanding model behavior.

2. **Algorithmic Innovation:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method via CCCP interpretation (Section 3.1) is clever and practically useful. The empirical results (Figure 1) clearly demonstrate significant computational savings while maintaining model quality.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper provides thorough experimental validation across artificial, synthetic, and real-world datasets. The comparison with multiple baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) demonstrates FAB's advantages in model selection accuracy and computational efficiency, particularly for larger datasets.

4. **Theoretical Clarity:** The paper carefully establishes the theoretical underpinnings of FAB for LFMs, including rigorous proofs of key properties (FIC convergence, identifiability) in the supplementary materials.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Intuition:** The paper states that "the log ∑n znk term naturally appears in log det |F(d)| without A1 and A2" (Lemma 1), but doesn't provide sufficient intuition for why this happens. A more accessible explanation of this key insight would strengthen the paper.

2. **Experimental Limitations:** While the paper compares with several baselines, the MEIBP comparison is limited to small datasets due to computational constraints. The paper mentions FAB performs worse than NPBs for small N (Libras dataset) but doesn't analyze this limitation or suggest potential remedies. This is particularly important as the theoretical foundation of FAB relies on asymptotic behavior.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites relevant work but doesn't explicitly compare with more recent advances in latent feature models (e.g., recent NIPS papers on scalable Bayesian inference for LFMs). A brief comparison would better position the contribution.

4. **Merging Post-processing:** The paper mentions "a few merging operations" occur in practice but provides little detail about when and why these occur, or how much they improve performance. More empirical analysis of this step would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that successfully extends FAB to a new class of models (LFMs) with significant practical implications. The paper demonstrates both theoretical novelty and practical utility. The main weaknesses are the limited theoretical intuition for the key insight and some experimental limitations regarding small-N performance and comparison with recent work.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the theory and practice of Bayesian model selection. The theoretical insights are well-supported, and the empirical results demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, these do not undermine the core contribution.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Add a brief intuitive explanation for why the log ∑n znk term appears in LFMs without satisfying A1 and A2 (beyond the mathematical derivation).

2. Include a more thorough analysis of the small-N performance limitation, perhaps discussing when FAB is likely to perform worse and how to mitigate this.

3. Briefly compare with at least one recent NIPS paper on scalable latent feature models to better position the contribution.

4. Provide more detail about the merging post-processing step, including how often it occurs and its impact on final performance.

The paper's main contribution - showing FAB can be applied to LFMs despite not satisfying the previous conditions - is both theoretically interesting and practically valuable. The shrinkage acceleration method is particularly useful for large-scale applications. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.
