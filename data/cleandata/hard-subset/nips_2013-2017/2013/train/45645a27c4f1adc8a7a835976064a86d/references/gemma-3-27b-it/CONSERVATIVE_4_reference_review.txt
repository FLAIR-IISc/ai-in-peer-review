This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a limitation of the original method requiring a block-diagonal Hessian. The authors demonstrate that, despite LFMs not satisfying this condition, the resulting Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) retains a similar form to that of Mixture Models (MMs), allowing for the application of FAB. They further present analytical results relating FAB E-steps to a Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP), leading to a shrinkage acceleration method, and establish parameter identifiability, enabling a merging post-processing step for latent features.

The core idea of extending FAB to LFMs is a logical progression, given the success of FAB with MMs. The analytical work demonstrating the equivalence of FIC forms, despite the differing Hessian properties, is the central contribution. However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a substantial advantage over existing methods, and the presentation feels somewhat terse, lacking sufficient motivation for certain analytical steps.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the demonstration of its similarity to the MM case is mathematically sound and a worthwhile extension of the FAB framework. Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are central to this.
*   **Practical Improvements:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration based on CCCP analysis is a welcome addition, potentially addressing a computational bottleneck.
*   **Identifiability Analysis:** The discussion of parameter identifiability and the development of a merging post-processing step are insightful and practically relevant.
*   **Automated Model Selection:** The core strength of FAB – automated model selection without manual hyperparameter tuning – is preserved and extended to LFMs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while present, feels insufficient to justify the claims of superiority. The comparisons are primarily with synthetic data, and the real-world experiments, while including several datasets, lack a detailed ablation study demonstrating the impact of the proposed acceleration and merging techniques. Specifically, a comparison *without* the acceleration step would reveal its true benefit.
*   **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The choice of baselines is somewhat limited. While IBP-based methods are included, a more comprehensive comparison with standard VB methods optimized for performance (beyond the cited VB from 2015) is warranted, particularly on the real-world datasets. The justification for fixing the noise standard deviation for some baselines is weak.
*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The presentation occasionally lacks clarity. The jump from the analytical derivations to the algorithm feels abrupt. More intuition and explanation would benefit the reader. For example, the rationale for the mean-field approximation is stated, but its potential impact on accuracy isn’t discussed.
*   **Overstated Claims:** Statements like "FAB achieved the most accurate estimation of K" are too strong given the limited scope of the experiments. The accuracy advantage on the synthetic data doesn’t necessarily translate to real-world scenarios.
*   **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** While the supplementary materials are mentioned, key assumptions underlying Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 should be more clearly stated and discussed in the main body of the paper.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The theoretical contribution is solid and extends a promising Bayesian inference technique.
*   The proposed acceleration and merging techniques have the potential to improve the practical applicability of FAB.
*   The work addresses an important problem—automated model selection in LFMs—with a novel approach.

**Con:**

*   The empirical validation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   The presentation lacks clarity in certain sections, and the motivation for some steps is weak.
*   The claims of superiority are overstated given the limited scope of the experiments.
*   The paper feels iterative rather than truly groundbreaking. It extends an existing method, but doesn’t radically change the field.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound extension of FAB inference, but falls short of demonstrating significant practical impact. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the limited empirical evaluation and lack of a more thorough comparison with established methods raise concerns. I believe the paper is *borderline* acceptable. If the authors significantly expand the experimental evaluation, including a more comprehensive baseline comparison and a detailed ablation study of the proposed improvements, it would warrant acceptance. As it stands, the work feels incomplete and lacks the compelling evidence necessary for a strong NIPS paper. I recommend rejection, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the aforementioned concerns.
