### Summary of the Paper

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously applied to models like Mixture Models (MMs), to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is an approximate Bayesian method for model selection that maximizes a lower bound on a "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC), which itself approximates the marginal log-likelihood. A key challenge in applying FAB to LFMs is that the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is not block-diagonal, a condition that simplified the derivation for MMs.

The central contribution of this work is showing that despite this structural difference, the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs can be asymptotically decomposed in a way that yields a FIC with the same form of regularization term (`sum_k log sum_n z_nk`) as in the MM case (Lemma 1). This "model-induced regularization" term encourages sparsity and enables automatic selection of the number of latent features, K. Based on this theoretical result, the authors derive a practical alternating maximization algorithm (FAB/LFM) that monotonically increases a lower bound on the FIC.

Furthermore, the paper provides two valuable analyses: 1) an interpretation of the E-step update as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), which inspires a heuristic "shrinkage acceleration" method to speed up convergence by eliminating irrelevant features early; and 2) a proof that the FAB objective resolves the parameter non-identifiability issue inherent in standard LFM likelihoods, which provides a principled basis for a feature-merging post-processing step. The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation comparing FAB/LFM to several state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian methods (IBP-based Gibbs sampling, VB, and MEIBP) and a variational EM baseline, demonstrating competitive or superior performance in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive likelihood, and computational efficiency.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant and Novel Extension:** The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to the important and widely-used class of LFMs. This is a non-trivial contribution, as it required overcoming the limitation of the block-diagonal Hessian assumption present in prior work. The derivation in Lemma 1 is the key insight that makes this possible and suggests the broader applicability of the FAB framework.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Justification:** The work is well-supported by theory. Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 provide the asymptotic justification for the proposed FIC for LFMs. Furthermore, the analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) is a notable strength. It provides a clear theoretical explanation for why the FAB objective prefers more compact models compared to standard maximum likelihood, and it directly motivates the practical merge post-processing step.

3.  **Practical and Well-Motivated Algorithm:** The authors develop a complete and practical algorithm. The connection drawn to CCCP is insightful and provides a principled, if heuristic, justification for the proposed shrinkage acceleration technique. The guarantee of monotonic convergence of the objective function is a desirable property.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare against a strong suite of relevant baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The experiments rightly assess multiple facets of performance: model selection accuracy (estimating K), predictive performance (PLL), and computational cost. The comparison against a standard Variational EM effectively isolates the benefit of the FAB-specific regularization term. The results consistently show that FAB/LFM is a compelling method, often finding more compact models with better or comparable predictive power at a reasonable computational cost.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on Theoretical Assumptions:** The main theoretical results, particularly Lemma 1, rely on "mild assumptions" that are deferred to the supplementary material. While standard for conference papers, this makes it difficult for the reader to fully assess the scope and limitations of the theory from the main text. Briefly summarizing the nature of these assumptions (e.g., conditions on the data distribution or the latent feature matrix Z) in Section 2.1 would significantly improve the paper's self-containedness and clarity.

2.  **Impact of Mean-Field Approximation:** The final algorithm relies on a mean-field assumption on `q(Z)`, which is a stronger restriction than what is required by the original FIC formulation in Eq. (1). This means the optimized objective `L` is a lower bound on the FIC, which is itself an approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. The paper should be more explicit about how this additional approximation gap affects the asymptotic guarantees in Corollary 3. Does `log p(X|M) = L + O(1)` still hold? Clarifying this point would strengthen the theoretical narrative.

3.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:**
    *   The noise variance is learned by FAB/LFM but was fixed for the baseline methods. The authors state this follows the baselines' original implementations, but it represents an experimental confound. The performance of the baselines might improve if they were also allowed to learn the noise variance. Acknowledging this limitation or, ideally, running a control experiment would make the comparison fairer.
    *   The paper notes that the hyperparameter setting for IBP (`alpha=3`) might cause overestimation of K. While using default parameters is a reasonable choice, this slightly weakens the claims of superiority in model selection, as the baselines might not be optimally tuned.
    *   The text states that MEIBP is for "non-negative data," which seems to be an implementation-specific constraint rather than a limitation of the model itself. This should be clarified, as comparing it on datasets containing negative values is not entirely appropriate.

4.  **Properties of the Acceleration Heuristic:** The `accelerateShrinkage` routine (Algorithm 2) is empirically effective but its theoretical properties are not fully discussed. While the main algorithm (Algorithm 1) guarantees a monotonic increase in the objective `L`, it is not immediately clear if embedding multiple CCCP-style updates within each iteration preserves this guarantee for the *overall* objective `L`. A short discussion or proof sketch clarifying this would be beneficial.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that presents a solid contribution. It provides a novel and well-justified extension of the FAB inference framework to LFMs, supported by both sound theoretical analysis and extensive experiments. The work is original, technically sound, and the resulting algorithm is of significant practical interest as an alternative to NPB methods for automatic model selection in LFMs. The weaknesses identified are largely related to clarity of presentation and experimental nuances, and they do not fundamentally undermine the core contributions of the work. The paper is well-written and makes a valuable addition to the literature on Bayesian inference and latent variable modeling. I recommend acceptance.
