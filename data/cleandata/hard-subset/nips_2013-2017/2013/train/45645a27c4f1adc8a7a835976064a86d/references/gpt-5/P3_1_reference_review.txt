Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian observations. The main theoretical claim (Lemma 1) is that, despite the lack of block-diagonal structure in the complete-data Hessian for LFMs, the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian has an asymptotic form that yields the same “log count” regularizer as in FAB for mixture models. This leads to an FIC approximation for LFMs whose lower bound can be optimized via a mean-field EM-like algorithm that (as in FAB/MMs) induces automatic feature shrinkage and asymptotically ignores priors. The authors further interpret the E-step/π̃-updates as a CCCP and use this to accelerate shrinkage. They also analyze identifiability, arguing FAB favors parsimonious solutions and proposing a merge post-processing step. Experiments on synthetic and several real datasets compare FAB-LFM to variational EM, IBP (Gibbs), VB, and MEIBP, showing competitive or superior model selection and predictive performance with lower computational cost than IBP/VB and better accuracy than MEIBP.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level asymptotic argument is plausible and interesting. However, the core result (Lemma 1) is only stated with “mild assumptions” deferred to the supplement, and several equations contain apparent inconsistencies that need clarification. For instance, in J(Z,P) (Eq. 8), the “−|P|” term is dimensionally and conceptually unusual; one would expect a (dim/2) log N term for each parameter block from Laplace, not a raw −|P|. Similarly, Eq. (10) includes “−2D + K log N” constants whose provenance is unclear. These issues make it hard to verify the derivation without the supplement.
- Mean-field approximation: The method departs from the exact FIC by imposing a fully factorized q(Z). While standard in VB for LFMs, the paper should be clearer that the main guarantees (e.g., asymptotic tightness) no longer strictly hold for the optimized lower bound and under what conditions the gap remains small.
- M-step: The claim that the M-step is “exactly the same as PPCA” is doubtful. PPCA assumes Gaussian latent variables (and often isotropic noise), whereas here Z is binary with mean-field sufficient statistics and diagonal noise Λ. The resulting closed-form updates are similar (linear regression with Szz and Sxz) but not “the same as PPCA.” Please state the actual closed forms used and assumptions on Λ (diagonal vs isotropic).
- CCCP acceleration: The CCCP view is sound and the equivalence to using Nπ̃k = Σn µnk is convincing. It would be useful to state whether multiple CCCP inner steps preserve monotonic increase of the overall lower bound (the algorithm claims monotonicity, but the effect of early feature pruning and merge steps should be explicitly handled: do you check L after each pruning/merge and revert if it decreases?).
- Identifiability and merge: Theorem 4’s statement (equal columns imply equal feature usage) is sensible and aligns with the regularization form. The merge heuristic is reasonable, but more detail on the acceptance criterion (ΔL > 0) and sensitivity to the distance threshold would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, but key derivations are compressed and several equations appear inconsistent. Please:
  - Explicitly list assumptions of Lemma 1 in the main text and provide intuition for why the shared θd across features still yields the sum_k log Σn znk term in the Hessian’s log-det.
  - Fix or justify the constants in Eqs. (7)–(10) and reconcile the “−|P|” and “−2D + K log N” terms with standard Laplace approximations.
  - Provide the exact M-step update formulas for W, b, Λ under the mean-field moments.
  - Define all symbols on first use (Θ vs P, DZ, Dk, |P|).
- The accelerateShrinkage pseudocode could note whether Tshrink affects guarantees and how to set it.

Originality
- Extending FAB to LFMs is novel and nontrivial; showing that the same “log-usage” complexity emerges without block-diagonal structure is a meaningful theoretical contribution.
- The CCCP interpretation is not new per se, but its use to accelerate FAB shrinkage in LFMs is a practical contribution.
- The identifiability analysis and resultant merge procedure are useful add-ons.

Significance
- A prior-free, automated model-selection method for LFMs with good empirical performance is valuable. If the asymptotic link to marginal likelihood truly holds under the stated assumptions, this could influence how we do model selection in latent feature models beyond IBP/VB.
- The acceleration looks practical and the shrinkage behavior seems effective on real data. However, broader tasks (e.g., link prediction, matrix completion) would underscore generality.

Empirical evaluation
- Comparisons are helpful but raise fairness questions:
  - Noise variance: You state other baselines do not learn noise and thus you “fixed it to 1/0.75.” Does FAB also fix Λ to the same value, or does it learn Λ? If FAB learns Λ while baselines do not, this can confound PLL comparisons.
  - Hyperparameters: IBP’s α set to 3 may bias K upward; MEIBP sensitivity is acknowledged. A sensitivity analysis over α and initial K would improve fairness.
  - Omitted VB due to runtime: It would be useful to include at least smaller datasets for VB, or report time-accuracy tradeoffs.
  - Report per-iteration cost and overall complexity to contextualize wall-clock times.
  - Provide confidence intervals for PLL/TLL and K across seeds.
- The block-data qualitative results are clear and supportive.

Pros
- Conceptually strong and potentially general extension of FAB to LFMs.
- Clean regularizer that yields automatic feature pruning without priors/hyperparameters.
- Practical CCCP acceleration; sensible merge procedure.
- Empirical evidence of good model selection and prediction with competitive runtimes.

Cons
- Key theoretical step (Lemma 1) and constants in the FIC are insufficiently justified in the main text; some equations appear inconsistent.
- Ambiguities in M-step equivalence to PPCA.
- Potential fairness issues in experimental setup (noise variance learning; hyperparameters).
- Limited analysis of sensitivity and scalability; no strong application beyond reconstruction/prediction.

Suggestions for improvement
- Bring the assumptions and intuition of Lemma 1 into the main paper; correct and reconcile equations.
- Provide explicit M-step formulas and clarify noise model (fixed vs learned) across methods.
- Add sensitivity analyses (α, initial K, Tshrink), and small-scale VB baselines.
- Clarify monotonicity guarantees when using accelerateShrinkage and merge; ensure you always check L.
- Discuss computational complexity and memory.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The core idea is interesting and, if the derivations hold, impactful. With clearer derivations (fixing inconsistencies), stronger fairness in experiments, and more explicit algorithmic details, this would be a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
