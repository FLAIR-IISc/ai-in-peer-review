### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for automated model selection in latent variable models, which works by maximizing a tractable lower bound on a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) that approximates the marginal log-likelihood. Prior work on FAB was limited to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal, such as mixture models. The primary contribution of this work is to show that FAB can be applied to LFMs, a model class where parameters are shared across features and this block-diagonal condition is not met.

The authors' key theoretical result (Lemma 1) demonstrates that the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs decomposes in a way that yields a model complexity term (`sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)`) identical in form to that of mixture models. This allows the derivation of an FIC for LFMs (FICLFM) that retains the desirable properties of FAB, including the automatic shrinkage of irrelevant features. The paper then develops a practical algorithm, FAB/LFM, based on optimizing a lower bound of this FIC using an alternating maximization scheme.

Furthermore, the paper provides two important analyses and refinements. First, by interpreting the E-step update as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), the authors propose a "shrinkage acceleration" method to speed up the removal of redundant features. Second, they analyze the parameter identifiability of the model under the FAB framework (Theorem 4), showing that FAB resolves a non-identifiability issue present in standard maximum likelihood estimation and motivating a post-processing step to merge similar features. The empirical evaluation on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates that FAB/LFM is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods like Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants and variational Bayes, in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant and Novel Extension:** The paper successfully extends the applicability of the FAB framework to a broader and more complex class of models. The primary technical hurdle—dealing with the non-block-diagonal Hessian in LFMs—is overcome with an elegant theoretical result (Lemma 1). This is a non-trivial contribution that makes a promising inference framework available for a widely-used model class.
2.  **Technical Soundness and Insight:** The theoretical development is well-motivated and appears sound. The connection drawn between the FAB E-step and CCCP is insightful, leading directly to a practical and effective algorithmic improvement (shrinkage acceleration). The analysis of parameter identifiability is another valuable theoretical contribution that not only strengthens the justification for the FAB approach but also provides a principled basis for a post-processing merge step.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, comparing FAB/LFM against strong and relevant baselines (IBP-Gibbs, VB, MEIBP). The evaluation covers multiple important aspects: model selection accuracy (K), computational speed, and predictive performance on held-out data. The results compellingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method, which often finds more compact models (smaller K) with better or comparable predictive power and at a lower computational cost, especially on larger datasets. The smaller gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods for FAB/LFM is strong evidence for its effectiveness in mitigating overfitting.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It clearly articulates the limitations of previous FAB methods, presents the core theoretical ideas, derives the algorithm, and discusses its properties in a logical progression. The connection to the mixture model case is used effectively as a reference point throughout the paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on Supplementary Material for Key Assumptions:** The central result of the paper, Lemma 1, is stated to hold under "some mild assumptions" that are detailed only in the supplementary materials. While this is standard practice, including a brief, intuitive summary of these assumptions in the main text would significantly improve the paper's self-containedness and allow the reader to better gauge the scope and limitations of the theoretical claims.
2.  **Limited Discussion of Failure Modes:** The paper presents a very positive view of the proposed method. The single instance where FAB performs worse than a baseline (Libras dataset) is attributed to the potential inaccuracy of the asymptotic approximation in "small N scenarios" without further analysis. A more critical discussion of the method's limitations would be beneficial. For instance, when does the FIC approximation break down? How does the method behave when N is not significantly larger than D, or when the true features are highly correlated?
3.  **Potentially Unfavorable Baseline Settings:** While the authors state they follow protocols from prior work, some choices for the baseline comparisons could be questioned. For example, fixing the IBP hyperparameter `alpha` to a value that "might cause overestimation of K" could be seen as creating a slight advantage for the proposed method. Similarly, fixing the noise variance for baselines while learning it for FAB/EM may not represent a perfectly level playing field, although the justification is provided.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   In Section 2.1, consider adding a sentence or two to briefly characterize the nature of the assumptions required for Lemma 1. For example, do they relate to the distribution of Z, the properties of W, or the `N -> infinity` regime?
*   To strengthen the empirical analysis, it would be valuable to add a more detailed discussion of the Libras result in Section 4. Could this be related to the `N < D` nature of the dataset? This would add nuance and provide readers with a better understanding of when to apply FAB/LFM.
*   The `merge()` post-processing routine is an interesting and well-motivated part of the algorithm. While the full algorithm is in the supplement, the main text could benefit from a slightly more detailed description of how the merge candidates are identified and how the decision criterion (increase in the lower bound L) is checked in practice.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. It presents a significant theoretical extension of the FAB framework, backs it up with insightful analysis, and provides a robust empirical validation. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to clarifying details and providing a more balanced discussion of limitations. The work is novel, technically solid, clearly presented, and likely to be of high interest to the NeurIPS community. I recommend **acceptance**.
