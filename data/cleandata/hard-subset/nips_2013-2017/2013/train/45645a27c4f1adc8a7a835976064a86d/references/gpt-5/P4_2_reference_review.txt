Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and Gaussian likelihoods. The core technical claim is that, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition previously used for FAB in mixtures, an asymptotic analysis shows the same form of factorized information criterion (FIC) emerges. Specifically, Lemma 1 asserts that log det of the per-dimension Hessian contributes a term ∑k log(∑n znk)/N + Op(1), which leads to an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) identical in structure to that for mixtures: a data-fit term minus global complexity penalties −(DK/2) log N − (D/2)∑k log(∑n znk). Optimizing a tractable lower bound yields an FAB algorithm with mean-field q(Z), a closed-form M-step (as in PPCA), and a sigmoid fixed-point E-step that contains the characteristic shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k), enabling automatic feature selection. The authors relate the E-step to a convex–concave procedure (CCCP), motivating a “shrinkage acceleration” routine that takes multiple CCCP steps per iteration to prune features earlier. They also analyze identifiability: FAB’s regularization selects balanced latent features and suggests a merge post-processing that increases the lower bound when two columns of W coincide. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets compare FAB (with acceleration) against IBP Gibbs sampling [Doshi-Velez & Ghahramani], variational IBP [Doshi-Velez et al.], MEIBP [Reed & Ghahramani], and a variational EM with finite K, reporting that FAB achieves accurate K selection and competitive or better predictive likelihood with reduced computation relative to IBP/VB.

The work builds on FAB for mixtures and HMMs [Fujimaki & Morinaga, AISTATS’12; Fujimaki & Hayashi, ICML’12], and on Bayesian nonparametric LFMs with IBP [Griffiths & Ghahramani], VB [Doshi-Velez et al.], and submodular MEIBP [Reed & Ghahramani]. The asymptotic perspective connects to MDL/BIC [Schwarz] and singular model asymptotics [Watanabe], and the CCCP view to optimization literature [Yuille & Rangarajan].

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Deriving an FIC for LFMs that matches the mixture-model form is nontrivial given parameter coupling in LFMs. If Lemma 1 holds under mild conditions, this widens FAB’s applicability beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians.
- Algorithmic contributions: A practical FAB algorithm with closed-form M-step, mean-field E-step, and a principled CCCP interpretation. The shrinkage acceleration is simple and empirically effective.
- Model selection without priors/hyperparameters: The shrinkage term induced by asymptotics enables automatic determination of the number of features, with asymptotic prior-ignorance, aligning with FAB’s “automation” goal.
- Identifiability insight: Theorem 4 clarifies how FAB’s regularizer resolves some non-identifiability and motivates a merge step that can reduce redundancy.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic and real data compare favorably against IBP/VB/MEIBP in K selection and predictive likelihood, with substantial speedups over IBP/VB.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on asymptotic claims: Lemma 1 is critical but deferred to the supplement. The “mild assumptions” leading to log det F(d) ≈ ∑k log(∑n znk)/N merit more explicit statement and discussion in the main text. Since p(X|P) is singular but p(X,Z|P) is regular, clarifying when the Laplace approximation is accurate for finite N is important; the authors note potential degradation on small N (e.g., Libras), but broader guidance is missing.
- Monotonicity guarantees: The lower bound L is claimed to increase monotonically, but the accelerateShrinkage routine updates π and π̃ “in practice” within inner loops. It is unclear whether these interleaved updates strictly preserve monotonicity; a statement of conditions or a proof sketch would strengthen the technical soundness.
- Fairness of empirical comparisons: For IBP/VB/MEIBP, the noise variance is fixed (1 or 0.75), while FAB learns Λ. This may advantage FAB on real datasets. Either enabling noise learning for baselines (where possible), tuning via validation, or fixing Λ across methods would make comparisons more balanced. MEIBP applicability to nonnegative data limits some comparisons; this caveat is noted but narrows conclusions.
- Scope and baselines: The study focuses on Gaussian LFMs. It is unclear whether the FIC derivation and algorithm extend to other likelihoods (e.g., Bernoulli, Poisson) commonly used in LFM applications such as link prediction. Comparisons omit more recent scalable VI/SGVI for IBP/Beta-Bernoulli process factor analysis, and modern finite-Beta-Bernoulli factor models, which would better situate significance.
- Practical details: No formal time/space complexity analysis is provided. The effect of Tshrink and sensitivity to initialization are not explored. The merge step is heuristic (distance thresholding of W) and only provably safe when columns are equal; empirical ablations would help.

Quality
- The derivations are plausible and consistent with prior FAB work, but key parts (Lemma 1 assumptions, Laplace regularity conditions, and monotonicity under acceleration) are deferred. The CCCP interpretation and update equivalence are clear. The identifiability theorem is well-motivated; the practical merge is heuristic.
- Experiments are reasonable and demonstrate benefits, but fairness issues and limited baselines reduce conclusiveness.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clearly written and organized. Important assumptions for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 should be summarized in the main text. The role of the prior term η(πk) versus the FAB shrinkage could be clarified quantitatively. Pseudocode is helpful; adding computational complexity and a convergence criterion discussion would improve clarity.

Originality
- Extending FAB to LFMs with a nontrivial Hessian structure and showing the same FIC form is novel. The CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability-guided merge are incremental but useful.

Significance
- If the asymptotic justification is sound, the contribution is significant: automated, scalable model selection for LFMs without hyperparameters is valuable and can influence practice. Broader impact depends on extending the approach beyond Gaussian LFMs and providing stronger empirical evidence.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- State the precise conditions for Lemma 1 in the main paper and discuss their realism. Are there data regimes where they fail?
- Provide a bound or discussion on how large N must be for the FIC to be accurate in practice.
- Clarify whether accelerateShrinkage preserves monotonicity; if not, characterize when it may violate it and how you handle it.
- Report big-O complexity per iteration and with/without acceleration.
- Revisit empirical fairness: allow baselines to tune or learn noise variance; report sensitivity to Tshrink and initialization; add ablations for merge.
- Discuss applicability to non-Gaussian likelihoods and what would change in the FIC derivation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and elegant asymptotic derivation extending FAB to LFMs.
- Practical algorithm with automatic feature selection and empirical speed/accuracy gains.
- CCCP interpretation provides a principled acceleration.
- Theoretical insight on identifiability and redundancy reduction.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on asymptotic results not fully specified in the main text; finite-sample behavior not well characterized.
- Monotonicity guarantee under acceleration is unclear.
- Empirical comparisons have fairness limitations (noise variance) and omit modern scalable baselines.
- Scope limited to Gaussian LFMs; generality not demonstrated.
