- Extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs) by proving a shared Hessian structure enabling FIC and FAB derivation for LFMs.
- Proposes a CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration that substantially speeds convergence in practice.
- Provides identifiability analysis and a post-processing merge step to remove redundant latent features.
- Empirically evaluates on synthetic, benchmark, and real-world data, showing competitive or superior predictive performance and model selection versus IBP, VB, and MEIBP.
- Theoretical contribution: significant advance by broadening FAB applicability to LFMs via Lemma 1.
- Practical improvement: effective computational speedup from the proposed acceleration.
- Identifiability and merging enhance robustness and interpretability of learned models.
- Presentation is generally clear with consistent notation and organized derivations.
- Experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate the method’s strengths across diverse scenarios.
- Competitive performance: FAB/LFMs often outperform or match strong baselines in accuracy and selected latent dimension.
- Derivations are dense and require substantial mathematical background; Lemma 1 needs a more intuitive explanation.
- Acceleration via multiple updates/CCCP is effective but not particularly novel in optimization literature.
- Baseline comparisons may be unfair in places (e.g., fixing noise std for competitors while learning it in FAB), risking biased evaluations.
- Merge post-processing description is brief; details on overlap criteria and impact on performance should be expanded.
- Overall quality: technically sound and complete, with claims supported by experiments.
- Clarity: mostly good but improved intuitive explanations and merge details would help accessibility.
- Originality: novel extension of FAB to LFMs; acceleration and identifiability analyses add value though acceleration is standard.
- Significance: high—automated model selection and computational efficiency make the approach impactful for latent feature modeling.
- For acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, practical speedups, solid empirical results, and useful identifiability work.
- Against acceptance: accessibility concerns due to mathematical complexity, limited novelty of acceleration, potential unfair baseline settings, and brief merge description.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept, with suggested revisions to clarify Lemma 1, justify baseline settings, and elaborate the merge algorithm.
