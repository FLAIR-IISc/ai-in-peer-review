### **Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper proposes an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core contribution is in overcoming a key limitation of previous FAB methods, which were restricted to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal (e.g., Mixture Models). LFMs violate this condition due to parameter sharing across features.

The authors' key theoretical result (Lemma 1) demonstrates that the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs, despite its non-block-diagonal structure, asymptotically decomposes into a sum of terms of the form `log(sum_n z_nk)`. This is a non-trivial and elegant finding, as it recovers the same form of complexity penalty found in FAB for Mixture Models. This allows the derivation of a Factorized Information Criterion for LFMs (FICLFM) that is analytically analogous to that of Mixture Models, enabling model selection (i.e., determining the number of latent features K) in a principled, prior-free, asymptotic framework.

Building on this theoretical foundation, the paper develops a practical algorithm, FAB/LFM. Further contributions include:
1.  A novel interpretation of the E-step update as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), which inspires a practical "shrinkage acceleration" heuristic to speed up convergence by pruning irrelevant features early.
2.  A theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability, showing that the FAB objective resolves ambiguities inherent to maximum likelihood estimation in LFMs and provides a natural justification for a feature-merging post-processing step.

This work positions itself as an alternative to the dominant paradigm for model selection in LFMs, namely Non-Parametric Bayesian (NPB) methods based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). While IBP and its variants (e.g., variational inference for IBP, MEIBP) use priors to control model complexity, FAB/LFM derives its complexity control from the asymptotic properties of the marginal likelihood, a fundamentally different approach previously seen for simpler models at AISTATS and ICML.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The main technical hurdle—extending FAB to non-block-diagonal Hessians—is overcome with a compelling theoretical result in Lemma 1. This significantly broadens the scope and applicability of the FAB framework beyond its initial instantiations. The finding that the complexity penalty term retains a similar structure is both surprising and powerful.

2.  **Well-Motivated Algorithmic Refinements:** The paper goes beyond a simple derivation of the algorithm. The CCCP interpretation is insightful and directly leads to a practical acceleration technique. Similarly, the identifiability analysis is not merely a theoretical curiosity; it provides a sound basis for the `merge()` post-processing step, addressing a known issue in LFM training. This demonstrates a deep understanding of both the theoretical underpinnings and the practical behavior of the model.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compare FAB/LFM against a strong and relevant set of baselines, including fast IBP samplers, variational Bayes for IBP, and the recent MAP-based MEIBP. The results convincingly demonstrate the method's strengths:
    *   It consistently achieves high predictive log-likelihoods (PLL), often outperforming competitors.
    *   It is computationally efficient, being significantly faster than sampling and VB methods and competitive with the fast MAP-based MEIBP.
    *   The learned models are more compact (smaller K), and the small gap between training and predictive likelihood suggests its "model-induced regularization" is effective at preventing overfitting.
    *   The ablation-like experiment in Figure 1 clearly shows the practical benefit of the proposed shrinkage acceleration.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It clearly states the problem, reviews the necessary background on FAB, isolates the core technical challenge, presents the solution, and then builds upon it with further analysis and refinements. The connections between the theory and the practical algorithm are explicit and convincing.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Assumptions for Lemma 1:** The central result of the paper, Lemma 1, is stated to hold under "some mild assumptions" which are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice, the self-containedness and rigor of the main paper would be improved by at least summarizing the nature of these assumptions. The validity of the entire framework rests on this lemma, and readers should be given a better sense of its conditions without needing to consult the supplement.

2.  **Nuances in Computational Comparison:** While FAB/LFM is generally fast, the claim of superior speed over MEIBP is not universally supported by the results. For instance, on the yaleB and USPS datasets, MEIBP is faster, albeit after the authors manually reduced its initial K. The paper should present a more nuanced discussion, perhaps framing its advantage not as pure speed, but as a combination of speed and robustness (since MEIBP was sensitive on the block data and did not finish on two others with a large initial K).

3.  **Hyperparameters in a "Hyperparameter-Free" Method:** The paper rightly positions FAB as an automated method that avoids the hand-tunable hyperparameters of NPB models (like the concentration parameter `alpha` in IBP). However, the proposed algorithm introduces its own parameters: the convergence tolerance `delta` and, more notably, the number of shrinkage iterations `T_shrink`. While the authors claim these are not sensitive, they are still user-specified values. A brief discussion on their sensitivity, or guidance on setting them, would strengthen the claim of automation.

4.  **Limited Discussion on Scalability:** The experiments are conducted on datasets of moderate size. The computational complexity of the E-step appears to be O(N*K*D) per inner iteration, which is typical for these models. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion on the computational complexity and potential scalability challenges for datasets with very large N or D, and how the shrinkage acceleration might affect the scaling in practice.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. It presents a significant and non-trivial extension of the promising FAB framework to a widely used class of models. The work is technically sound, combining elegant theory with practical algorithmic insights and thorough empirical validation. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to the framing of claims and the completeness of the discussion in the main paper. They do not detract from the core quality of the contribution. The paper is well-written, original, and significant, and I expect it to be of high interest to the NIPS community working on Bayesian methods, latent variable modeling, and scalable inference. I strongly recommend acceptance.
