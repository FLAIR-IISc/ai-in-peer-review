Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM settings to linear-Gaussian latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features. The central theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, showing that the Hessian determinant of the per-dimension likelihood w.r.t. θd induces the same log sum_n z_nk penalty term that arises in FAB for mixtures, even though LFMs lack the block-diagonal Hessian structure used previously. This yields an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) whose form matches the mixture-model FIC, implying asymptotic closeness to the marginal log-likelihood (Corollary 3). On the algorithmic side, the paper derives a tractable lower bound using linearization and mean-field q(Z), leading to an EM-like FAB procedure with a distinctive shrinkage term -D/(2Nπ̃_k) in the E-step that automatically prunes features. The updates are interpreted as a CCCP, motivating an acceleration (multiple CCCP steps) that hastens shrinkage. The paper also analyzes identifiability: FAB’s regularizer encourages balanced feature activations, and a simple merge post-processing can increase the bound when duplicate features are found. Experiments compare FAB to IBP (Gibbs), VB, and MEIBP on synthetic, “block” data, and several real datasets, showing competitive or superior model selection and predictive performance with lower or comparable computation in many cases.

Quality
- Theory: The core theoretical claim (Lemma 1) is the key novelty; it underpins the FAB penalty for LFMs without the block-diagonal assumption. If correct under the stated “mild assumptions,” this is an elegant and non-obvious result. However, the main text defers all assumptions and proofs to the supplement. Given how crucial Lemma 1 is, more intuition and at least a precise statement of assumptions (e.g., conditions on Z, Λ, boundedness, rank/LLN-type arguments) should be included in the main paper. The transition from (7) to the practical lower bound (10) via linearization and mean-field is standard and appears sound; the monotonicity guarantees hinge on alternating maximization and CCCP interpretation. It would help to explicitly state that accelerateShrinkage maintains non-decreasing L (or show an empirical monotonicity plot), since multiple CCCP steps per E-step change the schedule.
- Algorithmic details: The E-step update (11) is clear; the roles of η(πk) and the FAB shrinkage term are well explained. The M-step and π update are simple. The merge heuristic is reasonable and justified by Theorem 4 for exact duplicates; for near-duplicates, the bound-increase check is sensible.
- Experiments: The synthetic and “block” results support the claims on shrinkage and feature recovery. Real-data results show favorable PLL/TLL and compact K. Some fairness issues remain: (i) baselines’ noise variance is fixed (to the truth for synthetic, to 0.75 for real) while FAB learns λ—this could bias either way; a sensitivity/grid for baselines or learning λ in baselines would strengthen comparisons. (ii) VB is omitted on most real data due to runtime; including VB on at least small/medium datasets would help. (iii) MEIBP hyperparameters and initial K are sensitive; clarifying how these were tuned and reporting sensitivity would be useful. (iv) Reporting per-iteration time and convergence curves would clarify the net benefit of acceleration. Overall, the empirical evidence is positive but could be made more rigorous.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The main gap is the lack of explicit assumptions and intuition for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 in the main text. A brief derivation sketch explaining how the determinant picks up ∑k log ∑n z_nk would greatly aid understanding. The CCCP view is clear and practically motivating. Algorithm boxes are helpful; consider clarifying the difference between π and π̃ and explicitly stating the monotonicity guarantee after accelerateShrinkage and merge. Minor typos exist but do not impede comprehension.

Originality
- Theoretical: Extending FAB beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians and showing that LFMs inherit the same FIC penalty is novel and interesting.
- Methodological: The CCCP interpretation and acceleration are new in this context. The identifiability analysis and merge post-processing are also useful contributions.
- Relation to prior work is appropriate (FAB for mixtures/HMMs, IBP/MEIBP/VB, MAD-Bayes). The distinction from MAD-Bayes (noise→0 regime) is clear.

Significance
Automated model selection for LFMs without tuning priors is valuable, and the specific penalty structure leads to practical shrinkage and identifiability benefits. If Lemma 1’s assumptions hold broadly, this work expands FAB’s applicability to an important class of models. The acceleration makes FAB more practical. Researchers working on LFMs and Bayesian model selection are likely to build on these ideas. Stronger empirical breadth (e.g., link prediction/network LFMs) and more rigorous baseline tuning would further increase impact.

Pros
- Novel theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs via a non-trivial Hessian determinant analysis.
- Same clean FIC form as mixtures; asymptotic justification to marginal likelihood.
- Practical algorithm with a clear, interpretable shrinkage term; CCCP-based acceleration.
- Identifiability insight and simple merging heuristic.
- Good empirical results on model selection and prediction with reasonable runtimes.

Cons
- Critical assumptions and proof details deferred; limited intuition for Lemma 1 in main text.
- Some experimental fairness/sensitivity issues (noise variance handling, VB omission on real data, MEIBP tuning).
- Limited evaluation breadth (no network/link-prediction LFMs where IBP is common).
- Merge step lacks theoretical guarantees beyond exact duplicates (but bound check mitigates).

Questions and suggestions for authors
- State the exact assumptions for Lemma 1 and provide high-level intuition in the main paper.
- Verify and plot monotonicity of L with accelerateShrinkage and after merge steps.
- Report per-iteration time and convergence plots; add ablations on Tshrink and initialization.
- Evaluate sensitivity to prior η(πk) and to D/N scaling of the shrinkage term.
- Include at least one network/link-prediction experiment and a small real-data VB comparison.
- Align baseline settings: either learn λ for baselines or sweep fixed λ; discuss impact on PLL/TLL.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical extension is elegant and potentially impactful, and the method is practical with promising results. Clarifying assumptions and tightening experimental comparisons would strengthen the paper further.
