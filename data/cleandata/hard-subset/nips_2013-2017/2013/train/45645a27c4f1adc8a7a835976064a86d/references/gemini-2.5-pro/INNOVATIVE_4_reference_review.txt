### **Summary**

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a method for automated model selection, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). Previous work on FAB was limited to models like Mixture Models, which have a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete log-likelihood. LFMs do not satisfy this condition. The central contribution of this paper is a novel asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) demonstrating that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs surprisingly takes the same form as for Mixture Models. This key theoretical result allows the authors to derive a FAB algorithm for LFMs (FAB/LFM) that automatically selects the number of latent features (K) without priors or tunable hyperparameters. The authors further enhance the proposed algorithm by providing a CCCP interpretation of the E-step, which inspires a shrinkage acceleration technique, and by analyzing the model's identifiability, leading to a principled feature-merging post-processing step. Experiments show that FAB/LFM is competitive with or superior to established non-parametric Bayesian methods (like IBP) and variational methods in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency.

### **Review**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The core theoretical contribution is the asymptotic analysis of the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs. While the full proof is in the supplementary material, the result itself (Lemma 1) is clearly stated and forms a solid foundation for the rest of the work. The derived algorithm is a direct and well-reasoned consequence of optimizing the resulting FIC lower bound. The claims are well-supported by a reasonable set of experiments on both synthetic and real-world data, comparing against relevant and strong baselines. The authors are honest about the method's asymptotic nature and its potential limitations in small-N scenarios.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. It starts by clearly motivating the problem: extending the promising FAB framework beyond its original constraints. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is presented logically, highlighting the crucial role of Lemma 1. The algorithmic details, including the acceleration and merging refinements, are explained well. The connection drawn between the FAB E-step and CCCP is insightful and elegantly justifies the proposed acceleration scheme.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is its primary strength. This is not an incremental application of an existing method. Instead, it makes a significant conceptual leap by showing that the FAB framework is applicable to a much broader class of models than previously thought. The analysis that circumvents the block-diagonal Hessian assumption is a bold and novel contribution. While methods for LFM model selection exist (most notably, the Indian Buffet Process), this paper introduces a fundamentally different approach rooted in asymptotic Bayesian theory. The proposed method offers a compelling deterministic alternative to sampling-based NPB methods.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is high. LFMs are a fundamental tool in machine learning, but determining the correct number of features remains a challenging problem. This work provides a new, practical, and highly automated tool for this task. By avoiding the computational overhead of methods like IBP Gibbs sampling and the need for hyperparameter tuning, FAB/LFM has the potential to be a widely used method, particularly for large-scale datasets. The "model-induced regularization" effect, which arises naturally from the FIC penalty, is a fascinating and important property that distinguishes this approach from standard MAP or variational EM methods that rely solely on prior-based regularization. This work advances the state of the art by providing a method that is not only computationally efficient but also grounded in a distinct and compelling theoretical framework.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Pros:**
*   **High Novelty:** The core theoretical contribution—extending FAB to models without block-diagonal Hessians—is a significant and non-obvious step forward for this line of research. It opens the door to applying FAB to many other complex latent variable models.
*   **Strong Practical Impact:** The paper delivers a fully automated, computationally efficient algorithm for one of the key challenges in using LFMs: selecting the number of features.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The connection to CCCP and the theoretical treatment of parameter identifiability are valuable additions that deepen the understanding of the algorithm and lead to practical improvements (acceleration and merging).
*   **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate clear advantages over strong baselines like IBP and MEIBP in terms of finding compact, high-performing models efficiently. The small gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods is a strong indicator of the method's effective regularization.

**Cons:**
*   **Asymptotic Foundation:** The method's theoretical guarantees are asymptotic in N. While it appears to work well in the presented experiments, its performance in low-data regimes is less certain, as noted by the authors on one dataset. This is an inherent limitation of the framework, though not a critical flaw.
*   **Reliance on Mean-Field:** The algorithm employs a standard mean-field approximation for the variational distribution q(Z), which is a common but potentially restrictive modeling choice.

### **Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that presents a bold and novel theoretical extension of an interesting inference framework. The work successfully bridges the gap between asymptotic theory and practical application, resulting in a highly effective and automated algorithm for model selection in Latent Feature Models. The novelty of the approach and its potential for significant practical impact far outweigh the minor limitations inherent to the asymptotic and variational approximations used. I strongly recommend acceptance.
