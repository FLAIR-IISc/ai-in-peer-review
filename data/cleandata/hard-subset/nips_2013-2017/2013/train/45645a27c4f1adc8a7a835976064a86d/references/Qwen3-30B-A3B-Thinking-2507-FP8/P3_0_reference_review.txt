

# Peer Review: "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a significant theoretical contribution that broadens the applicability of FAB beyond the previously limited class of models. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the Hessian block-diagonal condition required for prior FAB applications (to mixture models), the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models. This insight enables the application of FAB's attractive properties—automatic model selection, computational efficiency, and asymptotic marginal likelihood convergence—to LFMs.

**Theoretical Contributions and Strengths**

The paper's strongest contribution is Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, which establish that the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs asymptotically contains the same log(∑_n z_nk) term as mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the conditions A1 and A2. This theoretical insight is significant because it demonstrates a deeper structural similarity between these seemingly different model classes. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs (Equation 9) and the subsequent algorithm (Algorithm 1) are technically sound, with rigorous proofs provided in the supplementary materials.

The CCCP interpretation (Section 3.1) provides a valuable theoretical foundation for the shrinkage acceleration method, connecting FAB's optimization to a well-established framework. This interpretation not only explains the acceleration's effectiveness but also provides a principled way to understand the shrinkage behavior. The identifiability result (Theorem 4) is also important, as it addresses a critical theoretical concern in latent variable models and leads to a practical merge post-processing step.

**Experimental Evaluation**

The experimental section demonstrates FAB/LFMs' superiority in model selection accuracy (especially for large N) and computational efficiency compared to IBP, VB, and MEIBP. The artificial simulation results (Figure 1) clearly show the acceleration method's effectiveness in reducing computational cost without sacrificing model selection accuracy. The block data experiments (Figure 3) effectively demonstrate FAB's ability to extract true patterns without irrelevant features.

However, the experimental evaluation has some limitations. The real-world data experiments (Table 1) are limited to just four datasets, and the comparison with VB is omitted due to computational cost without justification. The paper claims FAB "consistently achieved good predictive performance" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests to support this claim. Additionally, the comparison with MEIBP is potentially misleading because the authors set different initial K values for different datasets (e.g., K=100 for yaleB and USPS), which could artificially favor MEIBP in those cases.

**Critical Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of Clear Differentiation**: The paper claims "automatic hidden states selection" as a key advantage, but fails to clearly differentiate this from existing methods like IBP and VB, which also automatically select model complexity. The paper should clarify how FAB's automatic selection mechanism differs in theory or practice.

2. **Theoretical Connection to Prior Work**: The paper cites "Nonparametric latent feature models for link prediction" (Miller et al., NIPS 2009) but doesn't adequately explain how their approach differs from existing nonparametric Bayesian methods for LFMs. This is particularly important given the paper's claim of "better model selection accuracy."

3. **Overstated Claims**: The paper states FAB "achieves as good or even better model selection accuracy as state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian (NPB) methods" without providing statistical evidence. The claim that "FAB consistently achieved good predictive performance" is not sufficiently supported by the limited experimental results.

4. **Incomplete Discussion of Trade-offs**: While the paper emphasizes FAB's automation benefits, it doesn't thoroughly discuss the trade-offs between automation and flexibility that are inherent in such methods. For example, how might the asymptotic nature of FAB affect performance on small datasets?

**Overall Assessment**

This is a technically sound paper with significant theoretical contributions that extend the applicability of FAB inference. The core insight that LFMs share the same FIC structure as mixture models despite not satisfying the previous Hessian condition is valuable and well-supported. The proposed algorithm and acceleration method are practical and well-motivated.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough experimental evaluation with statistical significance tests, clearer differentiation from existing methods, and a more nuanced discussion of theoretical and practical trade-offs. The current experimental section is somewhat limited in scope and doesn't fully support the paper's strong claims about performance superiority.

**Recommendation**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution that expands the scope of FAB inference to a broader class of models. The theoretical insights are sound, and the experimental results generally support the claims. With more comprehensive experiments and clearer differentiation from existing approaches, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Key Strengths**: Theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs (Lemma 1, Theorem 2), CCCP interpretation of the acceleration method, and identifiability result with practical post-processing.

**Key Weaknesses**: Limited experimental evaluation, insufficient differentiation from existing methods, and some overstated claims without sufficient statistical support.

The paper represents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection, particularly for latent variable models. With minor revisions addressing the experimental evaluation and theoretical differentiation, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
