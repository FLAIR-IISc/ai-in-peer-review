Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference—previously developed for mixture models and HMMs—to latent feature models (LFMs) with Gaussian likelihoods and binary feature matrix Z. The main theoretical contribution is Lemma 1: an asymptotic analysis of the per-dimension Hessian determinant of the complete-data log-likelihood, showing that log det F(d) contributes a term of the form sum_k log(∑n znk) (up to Op(1)). This yields Theorem 2: an FIC approximation for LFMs, J(Z,P) = log p(X,Z|P) − (|P|−2D+K)/2 log N − (D/2)∑k log ∑n znk (constants aside), and thus an FIC objective (FICLFM) with exactly the same regularization structure as for mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal/independent component assumptions used for MMs. As with prior FAB work, they optimize a tractable lower bound via alternating updates over q(Z) (mean-field Bernoulli), model parameters, and a linearization parameter π̃, guaranteeing monotone improvement of the bound.

Algorithmically, the E-step update for µnk = Eq[znk] includes a shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k), driving automatic removal of unused features. The authors reinterpret the E-step as a CCCP update and propose an inner loop (“accelerateShrinkage”) that speeds up shrinkage by taking multiple CCCP steps per outer iteration. They also prove an identifiability result: at stationary points, equal basis vectors imply equal expected usages, which justifies a merging post-processing step that combines nearly identical features when it increases the bound.

Experiments compare FAB to IBP (fast Gibbs), VB-IBP (finite K), and MEIBP on synthetic and real datasets. FAB reportedly selects K more accurately than IBP/VB, achieves competitive or better predictive log-likelihoods, and is substantially faster than VB and IBP; MEIBP can be faster but often yields worse predictive performance. The CCCP acceleration reduces runtime without affecting final solutions.

Strengths
- Theoretical insight: Establishing that LFMs share the same FIC form as MMs (via Lemma 1) is nontrivial and broadens the applicability of FAB beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians.
- Principled, automated model selection: The shrinkage arises from the marginalization/geometry (singular learning theory), not from hyperpriors; no hand-tuned hyperparameters for model complexity.
- Optimization framing and acceleration: The CCCP perspective is elegant and leads to a practical speedup. The monotone lower-bound framework and simple E/M updates are attractive.
- Identifiability analysis and merging heuristic: The result connecting equal basis vectors to equal usage gives a clear rationale for feature merging, aiding parsimony and stability.
- Empirical results: On several datasets, FAB delivers competitive or superior predictive performance with compact K and lower runtime than VB/IBP; the generalization gaps (PLL vs. TLL) are small, consistent with the intended regularization.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions behind Lemma 1 and Theorem 2: The core result is deferred to the supplement with “mild assumptions.” The main text should state these assumptions precisely (e.g., conditions on Λ, W, moments of X, boundedness/regularity, scaling of K with N), discuss when they may fail (collinearity, near-singularities, heavy tails), and provide at least a proof sketch to build confidence.
- Asymptotics vs. finite-sample behavior: While FIC is asymptotically accurate, many experiments (e.g., small-N settings, block data) test finite-sample regimes. A discussion or empirical study of finite-sample bias (e.g., how large N needs to be for reliable K selection) would be valuable.
- Mean-field approximation: The factorized q(Z) both weakens the bound and departs from the exact FIC optimization. A brief analysis of the induced gap, or an empirical ablation comparing with a stronger (e.g., structured) approximation on smaller problems, would strengthen the case.
- Fairness of baselines: Some baselines do not learn noise variance (fixed to 1 or 0.75), whereas FAB learns Λ. This likely advantages FAB, especially when signal-to-noise varies across datasets. Consider: (a) reporting FAB with fixed λ to match baselines, or (b) using IBP variants that estimate noise (VB-IBP with learned variance, or MAP methods with variance tuning). Also, α was fixed (α=3) for IBP; sensitivity analyses or tuning would be fairer. VB was omitted due to runtime—please report at least one dataset where VB runs to completion.
- Hyperparameters and guarantees for acceleration: The inner CCCP loop introduces Tshrink, which is a tunable parameter, contrary to the “no hand-tuning” narrative. Provide sensitivity plots for Tshrink and confirm that accelerateShrinkage maintains monotone improvement of the same lower bound (or clarify if it optimizes a slightly different surrogate).
- Complexity and scalability: Provide a clear per-iteration time and space complexity in terms of N, D, K; discuss parallelization/vectorization; and show runtime scaling curves vs N and K alongside shrinkage dynamics (how quickly irrelevant features are pruned).
- Merging details: The merging procedure relies on a distance threshold and checking bound improvement. Please specify the thresholding strategy, how often merging is attempted, and demonstrate that it consistently increases the lower bound (include ablation: no-merge vs merge).

Questions for the authors
- What exact conditions guarantee Lemma 1? Does it hold when K grows with N? Are there pathological cases (e.g., nearly duplicate features) where the approximation is poor?
- How sensitive is FAB to initialization of µ and K? Do different random starts converge to similar K and PLL?
- Can the framework extend to non-Gaussian LFMs (e.g., Bernoulli, Poisson) where the Hessian structure differs?
- Does accelerateShrinkage always preserve monotone increase of L? If not, can you add a linesearch or accept/reject step?

Suggestions for improvement
- Bring key assumptions and a proof sketch of Lemma 1 into the main text; add a discussion of finite-sample applicability.
- Add fairer baselines (noise learning or matched constraints), and an ablation isolating the FAB shrinkage term vs prior-only regularization.
- Report sensitivity to Tshrink and initialization; include complexity analysis and scaling plots.
- Provide more details and ablations for merging; include cases where merging materially improves L, K, and PLL.
- Release code to ensure reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and elegant theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs, with a practical algorithm and provable bound optimization.
- Useful acceleration and identifiability insights; clear empirical benefits in model parsimony and predictive performance.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key theoretical assumptions and proofs are not sufficiently transparent in the main text.
- Some experimental comparisons are potentially unfair due to fixed noise and baseline configuration; limited evidence for VB/strong IBP variants.
- Additional clarity on complexity, sensitivity, and acceleration guarantees is needed.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying assumptions/proofs and strengthening the empirical comparisons as noted above.
