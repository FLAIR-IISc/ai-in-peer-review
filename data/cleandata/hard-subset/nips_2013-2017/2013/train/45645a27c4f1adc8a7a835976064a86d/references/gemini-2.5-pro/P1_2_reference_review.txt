This paper proposes an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The primary goal is to perform automatic model selection, specifically determining the number of latent features, in a computationally efficient and fully automated manner. The core challenge is that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption that simplified the derivation of FAB for simpler models like Gaussian Mixture Models (MMs). The authors' main theoretical contribution is showing that despite this structural difference, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs asymptotically takes on the same form as for MMs. This key result (Lemma 1) allows them to derive a practical algorithm, FAB/LFM, which iteratively optimizes a lower bound on the FIC. The paper further enhances the practical utility of the algorithm by introducing a shrinkage acceleration technique inspired by a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation of the optimization, and an analysis of parameter identifiability that motivates a feature-merging post-processing step. The proposed method is empirically compared against state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and its variants, demonstrating superior or competitive performance in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive power, and computational speed.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The extension of the FAB framework to LFMs is a novel and important contribution. The key insight in Lemma 1—that the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs yields the same penalty term `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)` as in MMs, despite the shared parameters—is non-trivial and suggests that the FAB framework may be more broadly applicable than previously thought. This provides a new, principled way to approximate the marginal likelihood for this important class of models.

2.  **Practicality and Automation:** The resulting FAB/LFM algorithm is fully automated. It does not require setting sensitive hyperparameters (like the concentration parameter in IBP), which is a major practical advantage. The method simultaneously estimates the model structure (number of features K) and parameters within a single optimization framework.

3.  **Algorithmic Refinements:** The paper goes beyond a straightforward derivation. The CCCP interpretation of the E-step is insightful and leads directly to the proposed `accelerateShrinkage` procedure. The experimental results (Fig. 1) convincingly show that this drastically reduces computation time by pruning irrelevant features early, making the method more scalable. The analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) is also valuable, providing theoretical justification for the model's behavior and motivating the `merge` post-processing step.

4.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and compare FAB/LFM against strong and relevant baselines (IBP, VB-IBP, MEIBP, Variational EM). The results are compelling: FAB/LFM is shown to be computationally efficient, accurate in recovering the true number of features in synthetic data, and achieves strong predictive performance on real-world datasets. The observation that FAB has a smaller gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods provides good evidence for its effectiveness in preventing overfitting.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Justification:** The paper's central claims rest on Lemma 1 and Theorem 2. However, the proofs and the "mild assumptions" they rely on are entirely deferred to the supplementary material. While this is common due to space constraints, it makes the core contribution difficult to fully assess from the main paper. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a brief proof sketch or at least the key intuition behind the proof of Lemma 1 in the main text. This would make the work more self-contained and convincing.

2.  **Discussion of Asymptotic Limitations:** The entire framework is based on an asymptotic approximation (large N limit). The paper briefly acknowledges in the conclusion that performance might degrade in small N scenarios (as seen in the Libras dataset). A more explicit discussion of the limitations of this asymptotic approach would be welcome. For instance, is there a way to characterize how large N needs to be for the FIC to be a reliable approximation? This would help practitioners understand when the method is most applicable.

3.  **Minor Experimental Details:**
    *   The choice of `Tshrink = 100` for the acceleration routine seems somewhat arbitrary. A brief sensitivity analysis or justification for this choice would be helpful.
    *   For the baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP), the noise variance was fixed. While this was done to ensure a fair comparison with FAB/LFM, it might handicap methods that are capable of learning this parameter. A brief comment on the potential impact of this experimental choice would add nuance.
    *   The omission of MEIBP from the "Block Data" experiment because its "parameter setting was very sensitive" is understandable but slightly weakens the comparison. It would be more transparent to report the (presumably poor) results and briefly explain the sensitivity issue.

### Summary of Review

This is a high-quality paper that presents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. It successfully extends the promising FAB framework to the important and challenging domain of latent feature models. The work is technically sound, combining novel theoretical analysis with practical algorithmic improvements and a thorough empirical evaluation. The resulting algorithm is automated, efficient, and demonstrates excellent performance against state-of-the-art methods. While the clarity could be slightly improved by bringing more of the theoretical intuition from the supplement into the main paper and discussing the limitations of the asymptotic approach more explicitly, these are minor points. The paper is well-written, original, and significant. It advances the state of the art in automated model selection for LFMs and is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and non-trivial theoretical extension of FAB inference to LFMs.
*   Proposes a fully automated, computationally efficient algorithm for LFM model selection.
*   Strong algorithmic contributions (CCCP-based acceleration, merge post-processing) are well-motivated and empirically effective.
*   Comprehensive experiments show a clear advantage over relevant baselines in many cases.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core theoretical results are not justified within the main paper, relying entirely on the supplement.
*   The limitations inherent in an asymptotic approximation could be discussed in more detail.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
