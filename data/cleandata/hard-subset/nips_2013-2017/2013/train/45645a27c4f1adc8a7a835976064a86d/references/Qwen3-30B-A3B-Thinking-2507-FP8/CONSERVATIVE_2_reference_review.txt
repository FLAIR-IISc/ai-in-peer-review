

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper proposes extending Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models to latent feature models (LFMs). The authors claim that FAB/LFMs offer automatic model selection (i.e., determining the number of latent features without hyperparameters), superior computational efficiency compared to existing methods, and improved model selection accuracy.

The main theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which shows that LFMs, despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by previous FAB work, yield the same FIC form as mixture models. This is an interesting theoretical observation that extends the applicability of FAB inference. The paper further develops an acceleration technique based on CCCP interpretation, and provides theoretical justification for parameter identifiability in FAB/LFMs.

The paper's strengths include:
1. Theoretical soundness of the main derivation (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2), which is rigorously proven in the supplementary materials.
2. The CCCP interpretation provides a novel perspective on FAB optimization.
3. The shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) appears theoretically well-motivated.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that undermine its claims of superiority:

First, the paper's claim of "automatic" model selection is misleading. While FAB/LFMs don't require prior hyperparameters (as in IBP), they still require setting two hyperparameters: the convergence threshold δ = 10^-4 and the number of shrinkage iterations Tshrink = 100. The paper fails to provide any systematic analysis of how these hyperparameters affect performance, which contradicts their claim of "no hand-tunable hyper-parameters." This is a critical omission for a method claiming to be "fully automated."

Second, the experimental validation is insufficient. The paper compares with only four baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP, and EM), but fails to compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods for latent feature models. For instance, the paper doesn't compare with methods like the "Bayesian Nonparametric Latent Feature Models" by Miller et al. (2010) or more recent extensions of IBP. The comparison with MEIBP is particularly problematic since the authors note MEIBP's sensitivity to parameter settings, yet they don't provide a fair comparison by tuning MEIBP's parameters appropriately.

Third, the computational speed claims are not thoroughly validated. The paper shows that shrinkage acceleration reduces computation time (Figure 1), but this is only shown on a single artificial dataset. The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive scalability analysis across multiple datasets with varying sizes or dimensions. The claim that "FAB achieves the most accurate estimation of K" (Figure 2) is based on artificial data with small sample sizes (N=200, 500, 1000), where the asymptotic properties claimed by the paper are unlikely to hold. For real-world datasets, the paper's performance claims are only supported by a limited number of small-scale examples (Libras, yaleB, USPS), which are not representative of the full range of problems where latent feature models are applied.

Fourth, the paper overstates the significance of its identifiability result. Theorem 4 claims FAB resolves non-identifiability issues, but the authors don't provide evidence that this actually leads to better performance in practice. The paper mentions that "a few merging operations were likely to occur in real world data sets," but provides no quantitative evidence of how much this improves performance compared to other methods.

The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of rigorous comparison against established baselines on standard benchmarks. The authors claim FAB "consistently achieved good predictive performance with low computational cost," but their Table 1 shows that for large datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS), MEIBP was faster than FAB (though with worse predictive performance), while for smaller datasets (Libras), FAB performed worse than the baselines. This inconsistent performance across different data sizes undermines the paper's claim of general superiority.

Additionally, the paper fails to address a fundamental question: why should the FIC approximation be accurate for LFMs when it was previously only justified for MMs? The authors claim that "LFMs do not satisfy the condition explained above," but they don't provide a convincing theoretical explanation for why the approximation still holds, which is crucial for establishing the method's validity.

The paper also makes an unsubstantiated claim that FAB "achieves as good or even better model selection accuracy as state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian (NPB) methods and variational Bayesian (VB) methods with less computational cost." However, the experimental results (Table 1) show that for most datasets, FAB's computational cost is comparable to or higher than the baselines, while the predictive performance (PLL) is only marginally better. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests to support these claims.

In summary, while the theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is interesting, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of superior performance and automation. The method still requires hyperparameters, the experimental validation is limited in scope, and the comparison with established baselines is inadequate. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive experiments on larger datasets, a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, and a clearer explanation of why the asymptotic properties hold for LFMs despite the lack of block-diagonal Hessian.

Given these limitations, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper. The methodological contribution is modest, and the experimental validation does not sufficiently support the paper's claims. The authors should address these issues before the method can be considered a meaningful advancement over existing approaches.
