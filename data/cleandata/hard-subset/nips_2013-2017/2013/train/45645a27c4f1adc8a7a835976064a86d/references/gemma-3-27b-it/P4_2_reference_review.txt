This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a limitation of prior FAB implementations that required a block diagonal Hessian. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying this condition, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) retains a similar form to that of Mixture Models (MMs) where FAB has already shown success. The authors further refine the FAB algorithm for LFMs with a shrinkage acceleration method based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation and address parameter identifiability through a merging post-processing step.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression of ideas. The background on FAB inference and its limitations is concisely presented, motivating the need for extending it to more general models like LFMs. The mathematical derivations, while dense, appear sound and are supported by references to established results (e.g., Laplace approximation). The explanation of Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, establishing the key result regarding the FIC form, is particularly clear. The inclusion of a CCCP-based acceleration and the discussion of identifiability are valuable additions. The experimental setup is reasonably comprehensive, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, and comparisons to relevant baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM).

The novelty of this work stems from successfully applying the FAB framework beyond models with block diagonal Hessians. This expands the applicability of this promising Bayesian approximation method. The CCCP-based acceleration and the identifiability analysis further enhance the contribution. The results demonstrate that FAB/LFMs achieve competitive or superior performance in terms of model selection and prediction, especially concerning computational efficiency.  The connection to existing work (e.g., MAD-Bayes) is appropriately acknowledged and contrasted.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis appears rigorous, the assumptions required for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are only mentioned briefly and relegated to the supplementary materials. A more detailed discussion of these assumptions within the main body would strengthen the paper.  The impact of the mean-field approximation introduced in the FAB algorithm for LFMs isn’t thoroughly explored. It is mentioned that it doesn’t significantly degrade performance based on the comparison to VB, but a more focused ablation study would be beneficial.  The experimental section could be improved by a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter settings for the baseline methods.  The choice of α = 3 for IBP, for instance, is mentioned as potentially causing overestimation of K, but this isn’t further investigated.  The merge post-processing’s impact is also mentioned as limited in the simulated data, but further analysis might clarify the conditions under which it proves most valuable. While the results are generally positive, the range of datasets is somewhat limited.  Exploring more diverse datasets would further solidify the claims of general applicability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Extends the applicability of FAB inference to a broader class of models (LFMs).
*   Provides a theoretically sound justification for the extension through Lemma 1 and Theorem 2.
*   Introduces practical improvements (CCCP acceleration, identifiability-based merging).
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Assumptions underlying the theoretical results are not fully discussed in the main text.
*   The impact of the mean-field approximation is not thoroughly investigated.
*   Experimental analysis could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding baseline hyperparameters and a wider range of datasets.
*   Limited analysis of the merging post-processing step’s impact.

**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable extension of the FAB inference framework. The theoretical contributions are solid, the proposed algorithms are well-motivated, and the experimental results are encouraging. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and a more in-depth analysis of the mean-field approximation and the merging post-processing.
