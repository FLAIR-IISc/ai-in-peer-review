This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a key limitation of the original method. The core idea – demonstrating the applicability of the FIC framework to LFMs despite not meeting the previously required Hessian block-diagonal condition – is genuinely novel and represents a potentially impactful contribution. The authors provide theoretical justification (Lemma 1 & Theorem 2) and propose practical improvements, notably the shrinkage acceleration via a CCCP interpretation and a merging post-processing step based on parameter identifiability. The connection to existing work (IBP, VB, MEIBP) is well-established, and the paper clearly articulates the advantages of FAB/LFMs – automation, potentially lower computational cost, and automatic model selection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs is a genuinely novel idea, expanding the applicability of a promising Bayesian approximation technique.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide a theoretical basis for their approach, demonstrating the applicability of the FIC framework to LFMs. Lemma 1 is a key result.
*   **Practical Improvements:** The CCCP-inspired shrinkage acceleration and the merging post-processing are valuable practical contributions that address computational efficiency and model refinement.
*   **Clear Advantages:** The paper effectively highlights the benefits of FAB/LFMs, particularly its automated nature and potential for scalability.
*   **Identifiability Analysis:** The discussion of parameter identifiability and its implications for post-processing is insightful and adds theoretical depth.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Validation:** While the experiments are reasonably comprehensive, they primarily focus on synthetic data and relatively small real-world datasets. The impact on truly large-scale problems remains unclear.  The comparison to existing methods, while present, could be more exhaustive, particularly with respect to modern scalable Bayesian methods.
*   **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate speedups with the shrinkage acceleration, a formal computational complexity analysis comparing FAB/LFMs to alternatives would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters beyond those explicitly mentioned (e.g., the tolerance δ for convergence) is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** While the paper acknowledges assumptions, a more detailed discussion of their potential impact on the results would be beneficial. Specifically, the implications of the mild assumptions in Lemma 1 should be further explored.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a fundamentally new application of FAB inference, overcoming a significant limitation and broadening its applicability.
*   The theoretical justification is sound and provides a strong foundation for the proposed method.
*   The practical improvements (shrinkage acceleration, merging) demonstrate a commitment to making the method computationally feasible.
*   The identifiability analysis adds theoretical depth and guides practical model refinement.
*   The potential for automated model selection and scalability is highly attractive.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is somewhat limited in scope and scale.
*   A more rigorous computational complexity analysis would be beneficial.
*   The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters requires further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly novel contribution. While the experimental validation could be more extensive, the theoretical soundness, practical improvements, and potential impact of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. The authors have tackled a challenging problem and presented a compelling solution. I lean strongly towards acceptance.  The paper’s bold extension of FAB inference and its focus on automation align well with the spirit of NIPS. I would encourage the authors to address the limitations in future work, particularly by evaluating the method on larger datasets and providing a more detailed computational complexity analysis.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
