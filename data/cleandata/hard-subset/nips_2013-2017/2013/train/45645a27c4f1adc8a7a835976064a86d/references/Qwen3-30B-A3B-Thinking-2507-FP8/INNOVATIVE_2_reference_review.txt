

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper makes a significant contribution by extending Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a class of models that previously could not be handled by FAB due to the absence of the required Hessian structure. The core novelty lies in proving that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition that was essential for FAB's application to mixture models (MMs), the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the exact same form as for MMs. This theoretical insight demonstrates the broader applicability of FAB beyond the previously established class of models.

The paper's main contributions are threefold: (1) theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs with rigorous asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2), (2) development of the FAB/LFM algorithm with a novel shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2), and (3) proof of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) which enables a natural merge post-processing step. The theoretical results are particularly compelling because they reveal that the key regularization term log(∑n znk) in FAB emerges naturally from the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs without the need for the assumptions (A1, A2) that were required for MMs.

The novelty here is substantial. Previous work on FAB was limited to MMs and HMMs [5,6], and the extension to LFMs represents a meaningful conceptual leap. The fact that the same FIC form emerges for LFMs despite their fundamentally different structure (where parameters are shared across latent features) is both theoretically interesting and practically significant. This insight opens the door for applying FAB to a much broader class of latent variable models.

The paper's empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims. The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) significantly reduces computational cost while maintaining model selection accuracy, as demonstrated in both artificial simulations and real-world datasets. The results show FAB/LFM achieves superior model selection accuracy compared to state-of-the-art methods like IBP, VB, and MEIBP, with substantially lower computational costs. The paper's finding that FAB achieves more compact models (smaller K) with better predictive performance (higher PLL) while mitigating overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL gap) is particularly compelling for practical applications.

The paper's theoretical contributions are well-motivated and rigorously presented. The connection between FAB E-steps and the Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) provides a valuable new perspective that explains the shrinkage behavior and enables the acceleration method. The identifiability proof (Theorem 4) is particularly important as it addresses a key theoretical concern in latent variable models, showing that FAB resolves non-identifiability issues that plague other approaches like EM.

While the paper is strong in novelty and significance, there are a few minor weaknesses. The comparison with VB is omitted due to computational costs, which slightly weakens the empirical evaluation. Additionally, the theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions that are stated but not thoroughly justified in the main text (though they're referenced to supplementary materials). However, these are minor concerns given the paper's substantial contributions.

This work represents a significant step forward in Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. It successfully extends the FAB framework to a new class of models, provides theoretical justification for the extension, and demonstrates practical benefits through both algorithmic improvements and empirical results. The paper's core insight—that the FIC form can be applied to LFMs despite their structural differences from MMs—is both novel and powerful.

The paper's contributions clearly advance the state of the art beyond incremental improvements. It provides a new theoretical perspective on model selection for latent feature models, a computationally efficient algorithm with automatic model selection, and practical benefits for real-world applications. This represents the kind of bold conceptual extension that NIPS values highly.

In summary, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that extends the applicability of FAB inference, provides a novel algorithm with practical advantages, and demonstrates strong empirical results. The novelty of the core insight outweighs the minor methodological shortcomings. The paper is well-written, well-structured, and makes a clear case for its significance.

I recommend acceptance as a strong paper that advances the field with a novel conceptual contribution that has both theoretical depth and practical relevance.
