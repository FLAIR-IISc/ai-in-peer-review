

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), which have not previously been amenable to FAB due to not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for the original FAB framework. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not meeting this condition, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for Mixture Models (MMs), enabling FAB inference to be applied to LFMs with similar desirable properties: automatic feature selection, convergence to marginal log-likelihood in the large sample limit, and monotonic increase in the FIC lower bound.

The paper makes several key theoretical contributions. Lemma 1 shows that the log-determinant term in the Laplace approximation for LFMs naturally contains the log(∑ₙ zₙₖ) term without requiring the block-diagonal conditions (A1 and A2) used for MMs. Theorem 2 proves that the marginal complete log-likelihood for LFMs can be asymptotically approximated as J(Z,P̂) + Oₚ(1), where J(Z,P) matches the FIC form for MMs. Corollary 3 establishes that FICLFM converges to the marginal log-likelihood. The authors further provide a CCCP interpretation of the FAB optimization (Section 3.1), leading to a shrinkage acceleration method that significantly reduces computational cost. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and merge post-processing provide theoretical guarantees for model selection.

The paper compares FAB/LFM with several baselines: IBP (with fast Gibbs sampling), VB (with finite K), MEIBP, and variational EM. Experiments on artificial data, block data, and real-world datasets demonstrate that FAB/LFM achieves superior model selection accuracy (especially for large N), better predictive performance, and lower computational cost than competing methods. The shrinkage acceleration method significantly reduces computation time without sacrificing model selection performance (Figure 1), and the merge post-processing helps achieve more compact model representations.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical contribution is strong and novel - extending FAB to LFMs despite the non-satisfaction of the original block-diagonal Hessian condition is a significant advancement in Bayesian model selection.
2. The CCCP interpretation (Section 3.1) provides a theoretically grounded method for accelerating shrinkage that is both elegant and practically effective.
3. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and merge post-processing offer important theoretical insights that enhance the reliability of the method.
4. The experimental validation is comprehensive, covering artificial simulations, synthetic block data, and real-world datasets with multiple evaluation metrics (computational time, model selection accuracy, predictive performance).
5. The paper is clearly written with well-structured algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) that would enable reproduction.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper references supplementary materials for rigorous proofs, which limits the ability to fully assess the completeness of the theoretical claims in the main paper.
2. The conditions under which Lemma 1 holds (mild assumptions mentioned in supplementary materials) are not sufficiently explained in the main text, potentially limiting the understanding of the scope of the theoretical results.
3. The comparison with VB is limited in the real-world experiments (VB was omitted due to "long computational time"), which weakens the comparative analysis in some settings.
4. The paper does not provide a thorough discussion of the limitations of the FAB/LFM approach for small N scenarios, where the asymptotic approximation may be less accurate.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature.
- The paper demonstrates both theoretical and practical advantages over existing methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM).
- The shrinkage acceleration technique significantly reduces computational cost while maintaining model selection accuracy.
- The identifiability analysis provides valuable theoretical insights that are missing from competing methods.

*Con:*
- The reliance on supplementary materials for key proofs limits the accessibility of the theoretical contributions.
- The paper does not fully address the limitations of the method for small N, where the asymptotic approximation may not hold.
- The comparison with VB is incomplete in the real-world experiments, potentially overstating the relative advantages of FAB/LFM.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs, demonstrating that FAB can be applied to a broader class of models than previously thought possible. The theoretical results are rigorous, the algorithm is well-designed with a practical acceleration technique, and the experimental validation is comprehensive. The paper addresses an important gap in Bayesian model selection for latent feature models, with clear advantages in model selection accuracy and computational efficiency over state-of-the-art methods.

The theoretical contributions are novel and significant for the field, and the practical benefits are demonstrated through thorough experiments. While the paper could be strengthened by more detailed discussion of the theoretical assumptions and limitations, these do not undermine the core contributions. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong scientific contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection.

I recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS.
