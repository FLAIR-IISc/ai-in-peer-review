Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference—previously developed for mixture models and HMMs—to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian observations. The central theoretical result (Lemma 1) shows that, under mild assumptions, the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian scales as a sum of log feature usage counts (log sum_n z_nk), yielding an FIC for LFMs that has the same form as that for mixture models. This leads to an asymptotically accurate approximation of the marginal complete log-likelihood (Theorem 2), and subsequently a tractable FIC lower bound suitable for alternating optimization over q(Z), parameters P, and linearization parameters π̃.

Algorithmically, the paper derives mean-field updates for the variational q(Z) and standard closed-form M-steps for W, b, and Λ. The E-step reveals a shrinkage term −(D/2)/(Nπ̃_k) that encourages automatic removal of unused or redundant features, providing model selection without tuning priors. The authors interpret the E-step/π̃ updates as a convex–concave procedure (CCCP), leading to a “shrinkage acceleration” scheme with multiple inner CCCP steps to eliminate irrelevant features earlier. They also analyze identifiability, proving that FAB favors balanced solutions when features are redundant (Theorem 4), and propose a merging post-processing rule that increases the lower bound when near-duplicate features exist.

Related work is appropriately discussed: FAB for MMs/HMMs [Fujimaki et al.], IBP priors and scalable inference (accelerated sampling, VB) [Griffiths & Ghahramani; Doshi-Velez et al.], MEIBP (MAP via submodular optimization) [Reed & Ghahramani], and asymptotic MAP-style regularization (MAD-Bayes) [Broderick et al.]. The key novelty is that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumptions used in FAB for MMs, yet the same FIC form emerges asymptotically.

Quality
- Theory: The main contribution is Lemma 1 and its use in deriving an FIC for LFMs. If the stated “mild assumptions” (relegated to the supplement) hold broadly (e.g., conditions on Z^T Z, feature overlap, and non-degeneracy of π_k), the derivation appears coherent and yields a penalty D/2 ∑_k log(∑_n z_nk) that matches known FAB penalties. The asymptotic constant treatment and convergence to the marginal log-likelihood follow standard Laplace arguments. However, the paper relies heavily on the supplement for assumptions, proofs, and justification of ignoring log det terms; more intuition in the main text would strengthen confidence.
- Optimization: The lower bound is standard FAB-style with a mean-field q(Z); the fixed-point updates are clear, and the CCCP equivalence is correct and insightful. The monotonic improvement of the lower bound with basic alternating updates is plausible; with acceleration (multiple CCCP steps and simultaneous π/π̃ updates), a brief argument for monotonicity would be helpful.
- Experiments: Synthetic, block data, and several real datasets are considered. The results suggest substantial computational savings from acceleration and good model selection and predictive log-likelihoods. However, there are fairness issues: baselines (IBP/VB/MEIBP) often use fixed noise variance while FAB learns Λ; IBP’s α is default and may bias K; VB is omitted on larger datasets due to runtime; MEIBP has nonnegativity constraints and MAP objectives, making PLL comparisons imperfect. These choices may overstate FAB’s advantages. Ablations (with/without acceleration/merge; learning vs. fixing noise across methods) would strengthen conclusions.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with clear algorithmic descriptions and pseudocode. Some derivations and constants (e.g., Eq. (10) constants like “− 2D + K log N”) appear abrupt and might contain typographical issues; clarifying constants and providing more intuition for Lemma 1 in the main text would improve clarity. The relationship to prior FAB work and differences from MAD-Bayes are adequately explained.

Originality
The extension of FAB to LFMs, establishing that the FIC has the same functional form as for MMs despite lack of block-diagonal Hessian structure, is novel and interesting. The CCCP perspective and associated shrinkage acceleration are also original and practically valuable. The identifiability result and merge heuristic are useful additions.

Significance
Model selection for LFMs is important, and scalable methods beyond IBP/VB are valuable. If the assumptions for Lemma 1 hold in typical LFM regimes, the work broadens the applicability of FAB and provides an automated, prior-insensitive alternative to NPB approaches. The empirical evidence suggests favorable accuracy/efficiency trade-offs, but stronger, more balanced comparisons and larger-scale evaluations would better establish impact.

Pros
- Novel FIC derivation for LFMs with a clear asymptotic link to marginal likelihood.
- Automated model selection via a theoretically grounded shrinkage term; no hand-tuned priors.
- CCCP interpretation and practical acceleration that reduces runtime markedly.
- Identifiability analysis and a principled merge post-processing.
- Monotonic lower-bound optimization and clear algorithmic steps.

Cons
- Critical assumptions and proofs are deferred; the main paper lacks intuition for Lemma 1.
- Experimental fairness concerns (fixed vs. learned noise, default hyperparameters, omission of VB on large datasets, MAP vs. likelihood comparisons).
- Limited scalability analysis (e.g., wall-clock vs. N, K, D; memory/time complexity).
- Minor clarity/typographical issues in equations and constants.
- No formal convergence guarantee for the accelerated inner-loop variant.

Suggestions for improvement
- Summarize key assumptions and give intuition for Lemma 1 in the main text; outline when it might fail.
- Provide ablations: with/without acceleration and merge; learning vs. fixing noise for all methods where possible.
- Tune IBP hyperparameters (e.g., α) and allow baselines to learn noise (or fix FAB’s λ for parity); report sensitivity.
- Include larger-scale results with complexity scaling and standard deviations over multiple runs.
- Clarify constants in Eq. (10) and provide a brief monotonicity argument for the accelerated updates.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical extension and practical algorithm are novel and promising, with solid potential impact, but clearer exposition of assumptions and more balanced empirical comparisons are needed to fully substantiate the claims.
