## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The authors demonstrate that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions for FAB applicability, a key lemma reveals a shared structure in the Hessian of the log-likelihood. This allows them to derive a FIC and FAB algorithm for LFMs, inheriting desirable properties like automatic model selection and monotonic convergence. They further propose a shrinkage acceleration method based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation, and establish parameter identifiability, leading to a post-processing step for merging redundant latent features.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The core contribution – extending FAB to LFMs by showing the equivalence of FIC forms despite differing model assumptions – is a significant theoretical advancement. This broadens the applicability of the FAB framework.
* **Practical Improvements:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration using CCCP analysis is a valuable addition. The empirical results (Figure 1) demonstrate substantial computational speedup.
* **Identifiability Analysis & Merging:** Addressing parameter identifiability and providing a merge post-processing step are important practical considerations that enhance the robustness and interpretability of the method.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper generally presents the mathematical derivations in a clear and organized manner.  The notation is consistent.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments cover synthetic data, a benchmark dataset (block data), and real-world data, providing a good evaluation of the method's performance in diverse scenarios.
* **Competitive Performance:** FAB/LFMs achieve competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods like IBP, VB, and MEIBP in terms of predictive accuracy and model selection (K).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Derivation:**  While the derivations are generally clear, they are dense and require significant mathematical background to fully grasp.  A more intuitive explanation of Lemma 1 and its implications would be beneficial.
* **Limited Novelty of Acceleration:** While the CCCP-based acceleration is effective, the underlying idea of using multiple updates within a single iteration isn't entirely novel within the broader optimization landscape. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight *why* this works particularly well within the FAB framework.
* **Comparison to Strong Baselines:**  The comparison to baselines isn't always entirely fair. For example, fixing the noise standard deviation for IBP, VB, and MEIBP while learning it in FAB could introduce bias. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough justification for the chosen settings would be appreciated.
* **Clarity of Merge Algorithm:** The description of the merge post-processing is somewhat brief. More details on the criteria used to determine feature overlap (Euclidean distance threshold) and how it impacts performance would be helpful.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous (although difficult for me to fully verify without deep expertise in the area). Claims are generally supported by experimental results. It is a complete piece of work with clear limitations acknowledged.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear, but the mathematical derivations are challenging.  A more intuitive explanation of the core theoretical result (Lemma 1) would significantly improve clarity.  The description of the merge algorithm could also be expanded.
* **Originality:** The extension of FAB to LFMs is novel, and the derivation of the FIC for LFMs is a valuable contribution. The application of CCCP for acceleration and the identifiability analysis add to the originality.  However, the acceleration technique itself is a relatively standard optimization strategy.
* **Significance:** The results are significant. FAB/LFMs offer a compelling alternative to existing methods for latent feature model selection, particularly in large-scale settings. The automatic model selection and potential for computational efficiency are valuable contributions.  The paper addresses a difficult problem (latent feature dimensionality selection) in a novel and effective way.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Significant theoretical contribution extending the FAB framework.
*  Practical improvements through the shrinkage acceleration method.
*  Strong empirical results demonstrating competitive or superior performance.
*  Addresses important practical issues like identifiability and merging.
*  Potential for widespread adoption due to automated model selection and efficiency.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Mathematical complexity may limit accessibility.
*  Acceleration technique is not entirely novel.
*  Some unfairness in baseline comparisons.
*  Merge algorithm description could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  The theoretical contribution is significant, the practical improvements are valuable, and the empirical results are compelling.  While there are some minor weaknesses related to clarity and baseline comparisons, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. This paper addresses an important problem in a novel and effective way and has the potential to significantly impact the field of latent feature modeling.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I suggest the authors address the minor concerns regarding clarity and baseline comparisons in a revised version.  Specifically, providing a more intuitive explanation of Lemma 1 and a more detailed description of the merge algorithm would greatly enhance the paper's accessibility and impact.
