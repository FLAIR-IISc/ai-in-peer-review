This paper introduces Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference for Latent Feature Models (LFMs), extending previous work on mixture models. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that the Fisher Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs, despite not initially satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for previous FAB applications, exhibits a similar form to that of MMs. This allows the authors to adapt the FAB framework to LFMs, offering potential benefits in terms of automated model selection (dimensionality of latent features) and computational efficiency. The paper also presents theoretical analysis regarding parameter identifiability and proposes a shrinkage acceleration method inspired by a connection to the Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP). 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods for LFM model selection and positioning FAB as a promising alternative. The theoretical derivation establishing the applicability of FAB to LFMs is a significant contribution, and the authors convincingly argue for the potential advantages of their approach. The inclusion of identifiability analysis and the proposed acceleration method further strengthen the paper. The experimental evaluation, encompassing both synthetic and real-world datasets, provides a solid empirical foundation for the claims made.  The ablation studies regarding the shrinkage acceleration are particularly valuable. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. 

**Quality:** While the theoretical analysis appears sound, a more detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying Lemma 1 is needed. The supplementary material is referenced but its completeness is difficult to assess without direct access. The claim of "automatic shrinkage" and "parameter identifiability" feels somewhat overstated; the authors demonstrate these properties under specific conditions, but the generality of these results could be clarified. The experimental setup, while comprehensive, lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning for competing methods. It is unclear to what extent the performance of IBP and VB was optimized. The performance gains attributed to the shrinkage acceleration method are not always substantial in the provided figures. The connection to CCCP feels a bit tenuous and the benefits of framing the algorithm this way aren’t fully realized beyond the motivation for the acceleration step. Finally, the stopping criterion `(Lt − Lt−1)/N < δ` doesn't seem to account for potential plateaus in the lower bound and could lead to premature convergence.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the mathematical notation could be improved for clarity. The frequent use of subscripted letters (e.g., `wnk`, `µnk`) can be confusing, especially when multiple indices are involved. A clearer visual representation of the FAB algorithm, perhaps as pseudocode, would be beneficial.  The discussion of the merge post-processing step feels a bit abrupt. More details on how the overlap is quantified and the criteria for merging features would improve understanding.

**Originality:**  The application of FAB to LFMs represents a novel extension of existing work. The insight that the FIC for LFMs retains a similar form to that of MMs is a valuable contribution. However, the connection to CCCP and the shrinkage acceleration method appear less novel. CCCP has been widely used in variational inference and related fields. The acceleration idea, while potentially effective, is not fundamentally new. The extension of the shrinkage regularization based on log determinant to LFMs is a worthy contribution, but feels less groundbreaking than the core theoretical result.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is high.  A computationally efficient and automated method for LFM model selection could have a substantial impact on various applications, particularly those involving high-dimensional data. The ability to avoid hand-tuning hyperparameters is a significant advantage. The work demonstrates good performance on real-world datasets, but the extent to which these results generalize to other domains remains to be seen.  The theoretical contributions – showing the applicability of FAB to LFMs – are likely to be of interest to researchers in Bayesian nonparametrics and latent variable modeling.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel extension of FAB inference to LFMs.
* Important theoretical result demonstrating the applicability of FAB despite the lack of block-diagonal Hessian structure.
* Potential for computational efficiency and automated model selection.
* Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* Inclusion of identifiability analysis and a shrinkage acceleration method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of detailed discussion regarding the assumptions underlying key theoretical results.
* Overstated claims regarding "automatic shrinkage" and "parameter identifiability".
* Limited detail in experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning.
* Incremental novelty of the CCCP connection and shrinkage acceleration.
* Potential for premature convergence due to the stopping criterion.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but the weaknesses outlined above detract from its overall quality. Addressing the concerns regarding theoretical rigor, experimental detail, and clarity would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough discussion of the assumptions required for Lemma 1, potentially relaxing some of the conditions.
2. Clarify the generality of the claims regarding shrinkage and identifiability.
3. Provide more detailed information about the experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning for competing methods.
4.  Strengthen the justification for the CCCP connection and demonstrate more substantial benefits from the shrinkage acceleration method.
5.  Reconsider the stopping criterion to avoid premature convergence.
6.  Make the supplementary material readily available for review.
