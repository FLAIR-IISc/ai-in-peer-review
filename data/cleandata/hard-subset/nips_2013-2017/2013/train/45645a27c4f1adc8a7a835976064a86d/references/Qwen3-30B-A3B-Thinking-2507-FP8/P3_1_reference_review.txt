

# Peer Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary and Context
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), demonstrating that despite LFMs not satisfying the Hessian block-diagonal condition required for FAB in Mixture Models (MMs), they yield the same FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) form as MMs. The authors prove this through Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, showing that log det |F(d)| for LFMs contains the same ∑ₙ zₙₖ/N term as MMs, enabling FAB's automatic feature selection. They develop an algorithm with a CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration and prove parameter identifiability for LFMs. The method is compared against IBP, VB, and MEIBP on synthetic and real-world data, showing improved model selection accuracy with lower computational cost.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Insight**: The key contribution is the surprising theoretical result (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) that LFMs, despite violating the conditions for FAB inference in MMs, still yield identical FIC form. This significantly broadens the applicability of FAB inference and demonstrates the generalizability of the FIC framework beyond specific model structures.

2. **Practical Algorithmic Innovation**: The CCCP interpretation of the FAB E-step (Section 3.1) provides a novel perspective leading to the effective shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2). The empirical results (Figure 1) convincingly show this reduces computational cost without compromising accuracy.

3. **Parameter Identifiability**: Theorem 4 provides important theoretical insight into how FAB resolves non-identifiability issues in LFMs, which is a significant theoretical contribution often overlooked in latent variable model literature.

4. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation**: The experiments cover synthetic data (showing FAB's superior model selection for large N), block data (demonstrating feature extraction capability), and real-world datasets (showing better predictive performance and lower computational cost than state-of-the-art methods).

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Clarity**: While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper could better clarify how FAB's "asymptotic ignorance of priors" differs from other Bayesian methods that also ignore priors in large samples. The distinction between "model induced regularization" and standard regularization is not sufficiently explained.

2. **Experimental Limitations**: The artificial simulations use fixed λₖ = 1 and πₖ = 0.5, which may not represent realistic scenarios. The real-world data experiments omit VB due to computational cost without providing comparative complexity analysis, making it difficult to judge the scalability claims.

3. **Related Work**: The connection to prior work on LFMs could be strengthened. The paper mentions IBP and VB but doesn't clearly distinguish how FAB/LFMs differ in theoretical foundations from these methods. For example, the relationship between FAB's automatic feature selection and IBP's infinite feature prior isn't explicitly contrasted.

4. **Merge Post-processing**: The merge step is mentioned but not thoroughly explained. The paper states "we empirically found that a few merging operations were likely to occur," but doesn't provide details on how this affects model selection or when merging is beneficial.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically strong paper with significant theoretical contributions that advance the understanding of FAB inference. The key insight that LFMs yield the same FIC form as MMs despite violating the Hessian condition is novel and well-supported by rigorous proofs. The practical algorithmic improvements, particularly the CCCP-based acceleration, are well-motivated and empirically validated.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper would benefit from clearer explanations of how FAB differs from existing methods in terms of regularization mechanisms and more comprehensive experimental comparisons that address the computational complexity claims.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory and practice of Bayesian model selection. The theoretical insight that FAB inference extends to LFMs despite violating the Hessian condition is significant and broadly applicable. The algorithmic improvements and empirical validation demonstrate practical value. While some aspects of the presentation could be strengthened (particularly the theoretical distinctions from existing methods), the core contributions are substantial and well-supported.

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in model selection for latent variable models and demonstrates clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods in both accuracy and computational efficiency. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, and the experimental validation is comprehensive. This work advances the state of the art in Bayesian inference for latent feature models and is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community.
