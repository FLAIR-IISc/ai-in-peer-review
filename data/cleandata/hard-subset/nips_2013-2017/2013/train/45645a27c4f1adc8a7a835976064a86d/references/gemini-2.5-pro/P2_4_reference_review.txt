**Summary of the Paper**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for approximate Bayesian inference and model selection that maximizes a lower bound on a "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC), an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. Prior work on FAB was limited to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal, such as Mixture Models (MMs). The primary challenge and contribution of this work is to overcome this limitation for LFMs, where parameters are shared across latent features, thus violating the block-diagonal assumption.

The authors' key theoretical result (Lemma 1) demonstrates that the log determinant of the LFM Hessian matrix asymptotically decomposes into a sum of terms of the form `log(sum_n z_nk)`. This allows them to derive an FIC for LFMs that has the same functional form as that for MMs. This FIC includes a data-dependent complexity penalty that encourages the shrinking of irrelevant latent features, enabling automatic model selection (i.e., determining the number of features K). Based on this, they develop an iterative FAB/LFM algorithm. The paper further provides two refinements: 1) a re-interpretation of the update step through a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), which inspires an acceleration technique that significantly reduces computation time by pruning features early, and 2) a theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability, which shows that FAB resolves ambiguities present in standard Maximum Likelihood estimation and motivates a post-processing step for merging redundant features. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data compare FAB/LFM to standard Variational EM, IBP-based Gibbs sampling, Variational Bayes for IBP, and the MAP-based MEIBP, demonstrating competitive or superior performance in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive likelihood, and computational efficiency.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The theoretical derivation is the paper's main strength. Lemma 1 is a non-trivial result that provides a principled way to extend the FAB framework beyond its original, more restrictive assumptions. This significantly broadens the applicability of the method.
    *   The claims are well-supported. The theoretical contributions (Lemma 1, Theorem 2, Theorem 4) are presented clearly, with proofs deferred to supplementary material as is standard. The connection drawn to the CCCP framework provides a solid justification for the proposed acceleration heuristic, moving it beyond a mere ad-hoc trick.
    *   The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare against a strong and relevant set of baselines, including state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian methods (IBP, VB-IBP) and a fast MAP alternative (MEIBP). The use of multiple metrics (runtime, K selection, predictive log-likelihood) provides a comprehensive view of the algorithm's performance. The authors are also honest about limitations, noting a case in a small-N scenario where FAB performs worse.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The "mild assumptions" underpinning Lemma 1 are crucial to the entire paper but are relegated to the supplementary materials. While this is necessary for space, a brief, intuitive description of their nature in the main text would strengthen the reader's confidence in the result.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper follows a clear logical progression, starting with the motivation and problem definition, presenting the core theoretical result, deriving the algorithm, and then offering insightful analysis and refinements.
    *   The introduction does an excellent job of situating FAB within the landscape of Bayesian inference methods and clearly stating the specific challenge addressed in this work. The explicit contrast between the MM and LFM cases is very helpful for understanding the core contribution.
    *   Algorithm 1 provides a clear, high-level overview of the full procedure.

*   **Weaknesses/Suggestions for Improvement:**
    *   The connection between the FIC penalty term `log(sum_n z_nk)` and the final update equation `µnk = g(...)` could be made slightly more explicit. While the term `-D/2Nπ̃k` in Eq. (11) is correctly identified as the source of shrinkage, a sentence reminding the reader that this term arises directly from the linearization of the log-sum penalty would enhance clarity.
    *   The description of the "merge()" post-processing step is very brief. While details are likely in the supplement, a slightly more detailed summary in the main text (e.g., what distance metric is used to find similar features) would make the paper more self-contained.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The primary contribution—extending FAB to a model class that violates its foundational assumptions—is a novel and significant research step. It is not an incremental application of an existing technique.
    *   The analysis of the FAB algorithm through the lens of CCCP and the subsequent development of the shrinkage acceleration technique are original contributions.
    *   The theoretical analysis of identifiability within the FAB framework for LFMs is novel and provides new insights into the behavior of the algorithm compared to standard EM.
    *   The work is clearly differentiated from prior art. It presents a compelling alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods like IBP, offering similar automatic model complexity control but from a different theoretical foundation (asymptotic frequentist Bayesianism) and with potential computational advantages.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are likely to be significant for the community.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   Model selection for latent variable models is a fundamental and challenging problem. This paper provides a new, effective, and computationally efficient tool for tackling this problem for LFMs, a widely used model class.
    *   The successful generalization of FAB suggests that this framework might be applicable to an even wider range of models with shared parameter structures. This work could inspire further research into developing FIC-based criteria for other complex models.
    *   The demonstrated performance, particularly the combination of speed and accuracy, makes FAB/LFM a very attractive practical method for researchers and practitioners using LFMs on large datasets where traditional methods like Gibbs sampling for IBP would be too slow. The observation that FAB mitigates overfitting (smaller gap between TLL and PLL) is a strong practical selling point.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and novel extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian inference framework. The core theoretical contribution is strong, enabling the application of FAB to the important class of Latent Feature Models. This theoretical work is complemented by insightful analysis leading to practical improvements (acceleration, merging) and a robust set of experiments demonstrating the method's effectiveness against strong baselines. The work is well-written, original, and addresses a problem of high importance to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
