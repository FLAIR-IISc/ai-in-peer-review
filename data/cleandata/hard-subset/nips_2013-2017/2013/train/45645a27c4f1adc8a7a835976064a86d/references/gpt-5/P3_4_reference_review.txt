Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs) with linear-Gaussian likelihood and Bernoulli feature activations. The core technical result (Lemma 1) states that the Hessian determinant of the negative complete log-likelihood with respect to per-dimension parameters θd yields, asymptotically, a term ∑k log(∑n znk) up to Op(1). This leads to an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) that has the same functional form as the FIC for mixture models (MMs), despite LFMs not satisfying the usual block-diagonal Hessian condition used in prior FAB work. Building on this, the authors derive a tractable lower bound and an EM-like FAB algorithm with a mean-field q(Z), in which the E-step has a shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k) that automatically prunes features. They further show the E-step can be recast as a CCCP on a convex-concave objective, which motivates a shrinkage acceleration scheme. Finally, they analyze identifiability and propose a merging post-processing step based on overlapping columns of W. Experiments on synthetic, block-pixel data, and several real datasets compare FAB to EM, IBP (Gibbs), VB, and MEIBP, showing good model selection and predictive performance with competitive or better runtime.

Relation to prior work
- FAB for MMs/HMMs is known (Fujimaki et al.). The novelty here is extending FIC/FAB to LFMs where the complete-data Hessian is not block diagonal.
- The Indian Buffet Process and its variational/MAP approximations are the prevailing approaches for latent feature selection; related asymptotic work includes MAD-Bayes.
- The CCCP view connects to broader variational optimization literature, and the identifiability discussion relates to singular learning theory (Watanabe) and non-uniqueness of ML solutions in LFMs.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: showing that the FIC for LFMs recovers the same regularizer log ∑n znk as for MMs is surprising and useful, broadening the applicability of FAB beyond block-diagonal cases.
- Clean algorithm: the FAB E-step with an explicit shrinkage term is simple, interpretable, and automatically selects K without hand-tuned priors/hyperparameters.
- CCCP interpretation is insightful and leads to a practical acceleration that appears to reduce runtime substantially in experiments.
- The identifiability analysis clarifies how FAB prefers parsimonious ML-equivalent solutions and motivates a principled column-merging post-process.
- Empirical results are encouraging: FAB often recovers the true K on synthetic data and attains strong predictive log-likelihood with compact models on real datasets.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality
- Lemma 1 is the linchpin; however, the main paper does not provide intuition or a proof sketch, only referencing “mild assumptions.” Which precise assumptions are required (e.g., conditions on Z’Z/N, separation of feature usage, bounded moments, non-degeneracy of Λ, scaling of K with N)? Are these verifiable in practice? Please add a proof outline and explicitly list assumptions in the main text.
- Monotonicity: While the base FAB iterations provably increase the lower bound, Algorithm 2 takes multiple CCCP steps and updates π together with π̃ inside the inner loop. Is monotonic increase of the same bound guaranteed for each inner step, or only empirically observed? This needs a precise statement and, ideally, a proof or a conservative variant with guarantees.
- Fairness of comparisons: FAB learns Λ while some baselines use fixed noise std (0.75/1). This favors FAB. A stronger evaluation would (i) allow baselines to learn noise where supported (e.g., VB variants) or (ii) constrain FAB to the same fixed noise and report both. Moreover, IBP α fixed at 3 may bias K upwards; sensitivity analyses over α or using adaptive posteriors would be fairer.
- The merging step is heuristic (distance threshold on W) plus a check of the bound. How sensitive are results to the merging criterion and threshold? Provide an ablation on with/without merge and number of merges used.
- Reporting: No runtime complexity analysis is given. Please state per-iteration costs (in N, D, K) and how Tshrink trades off extra E-steps versus earlier pruning.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, but several equations mix constants and dimensionality somewhat loosely. For example, Eq. (8) includes a term “−|P|” (not log-scaled), and Eq. (10) has a standalone “−2D + K log N”. Please standardize constants from Laplace (log(2π), |P|/2 terms) and clearly separate asymptotically negligible constants from N-dependent penalties.
- The precise definition of |P| and parameter grouping per θd should be given in the main text. Also clarify whether Λ is strictly diagonal and required for Lemma 1.
- The CCCP derivation is nice; you could move the key steps (Eq. 13→14) into the main text with a short derivation.

Originality
- The main theoretical contribution (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) and the realization that LFMs inherit the same FIC penalty as MMs are novel.
- The CCCP view and acceleration are incremental but well-motivated.
- The identifiability angle and merge heuristic are useful additions.

Significance
- If the stated assumptions hold widely (linear-Gaussian LFMs with diagonal noise), this provides a compelling alternative to IBP with fewer hyperparameters and strong practical behavior. The automatic K selection and efficiency are attractive for large N.
- The approach currently targets a specific likelihood (Gaussian, diagonal precision) and mean-field q(Z). Extending the analysis (or at least discussing) to non-Gaussian likelihoods, correlated noise, and structured posteriors would broaden impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and nontrivial theoretical extension of FAB to a widely used model class.
- Simple, effective algorithm with principled shrinkage and acceleration.
- Clear empirical gains in model selection and predictive performance with competitive runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Key assumptions and proofs deferred; lack of proof sketch undermines confidence.
- Experimental fairness concerns (noise learning, IBP hyperparameters).
- Guarantees for accelerated CCCP steps are not rigorously established.
- Limited exploration beyond the Gaussian/diagonal setting.

Questions for authors
1) State the exact assumptions required for Lemma 1 and provide a proof outline. How does K scale with N for the asymptotics to hold?
2) Does CCCP-based accelerateShrinkage guarantee monotonic improvement of the same lower bound? If so, under what conditions?
3) How sensitive is FAB to initialization and Tshrink? Please include sensitivity plots or statistics.
4) Can you report results with fixed noise for FAB to match baselines, and/or allow baselines to learn noise?
5) What failure modes occur when features are highly collinear or when ∑n µnk approaches 0 or 1? Is removal sufficient to avoid ill-conditioning in practice?

Minor comments
- Define all symbols at first use (e.g., |P|, θd). Clarify whether π̃=π always holds at stationary points or only during specific updates.
- Consider comparing to a recent variational IBP that learns noise variance, if available, or cite limitations.

Overall assessment
A strong paper with a noteworthy theoretical insight and a practical algorithm that appears to work well. I recommend acceptance provided the authors move key assumptions and a proof sketch of Lemma 1 into the main paper, clarify monotonicity of the accelerated steps, and address experimental fairness (noise variance and IBP hyperparameters).
