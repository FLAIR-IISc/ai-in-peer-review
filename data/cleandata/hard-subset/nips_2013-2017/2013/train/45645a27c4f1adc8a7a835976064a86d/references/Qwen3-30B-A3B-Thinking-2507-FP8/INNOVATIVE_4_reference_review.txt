

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), a significant theoretical contribution that expands the applicability of FAB beyond the specific models (like Mixture Models) for which it was originally designed. The key insight is that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for FAB, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for Mixture Models, enabling FAB to be applied to LFMs with comparable theoretical guarantees.

The paper makes several noteworthy contributions. First, Lemma 1 establishes that the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs contains the log(∑n znk) term naturally, which is the critical component enabling FAB's automatic model selection. This is a significant theoretical insight that reveals the broader applicability of FIC beyond the initially considered models. Second, the paper proposes a practical shrinkage acceleration method based on interpreting FAB as a Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP), which dramatically reduces computational costs by eliminating irrelevant features earlier in the optimization process. Third, the identifiability analysis and merge post-processing provide important theoretical and practical guidance for handling latent feature models.

The paper's most compelling strength is its theoretical novelty in extending FAB to LFMs. Previous work on FAB [5,6] was limited to models with a specific block-diagonal Hessian structure, and this paper successfully demonstrates that the same theoretical framework applies to LFMs without this condition. This is a bold theoretical extension that opens up FAB to a much broader class of models. The connection to CCCP also provides a new perspective on FAB optimization that could inspire future work.

The empirical results support the theoretical claims, showing that FAB/LFMs achieve better model selection accuracy than state-of-the-art methods (including IBP, VB, and MEIBP) while being significantly faster. The shrinkage acceleration mechanism (Algorithm 2) is particularly impressive in reducing computational costs without sacrificing accuracy. The paper's claim that FAB achieves "automatic" model selection (without hyperparameters) is well-supported by the experiments.

While the paper is strong on novelty and theoretical insight, it has some minor weaknesses. The proofs of key theoretical results are relegated to supplementary materials, which is common practice but leaves some gaps in the main text. The experimental section could benefit from more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity. The paper also doesn't fully explain why the Hessian analysis works for LFMs when the original condition isn't satisfied, though this is a minor point given the empirical validation.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by extending FAB to a broader class of models, which is exactly the kind of bold theoretical contribution I value highly. The methodological extension is not merely incremental but represents a fundamental advancement in understanding the conditions under which FAB can be applied.

Compared to related work, this paper distinguishes itself from other model selection approaches for LFMs [3, 10, 16, 22] by offering a theoretically grounded, automated method without hand-tuned hyperparameters. While MAD-Bayes [1] and other asymptotic approaches exist, this paper provides a more practical and theoretically sound framework for LFMs.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending FAB to LFMs with a theoretically rigorous foundation and practical implementation. The paper's novelty in theoretical extension, the practical shrinkage acceleration method, and the empirical validation all support its significance. While the paper has minor weaknesses in completeness of theoretical exposition, these are outweighed by the substantial novelty of the main contribution.

**Strengths:**
- Significant theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs
- Novel insight that FIC takes the same form for LFMs as for MMs
- Practical shrinkage acceleration method that dramatically reduces computational cost
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets
- Theoretical analysis of identifiability and merge post-processing

**Weaknesses:**
- Some theoretical proofs in supplementary materials
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity
- Minor gaps in explaining why the Hessian analysis works for LFMs

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that significantly extends the applicability of FAB inference, with practical implications for model selection in latent feature models. The novelty and theoretical insight outweigh the minor methodological shortcomings, and the paper addresses an important gap in the literature. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in Bayesian model selection for latent variable models.
