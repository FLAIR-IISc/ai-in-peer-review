

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a key limitation of previous FAB work that required models to have a block-diagonal Hessian structure. While FAB has previously been successfully applied to Mixture Models (MMs) [5,6], the authors demonstrate through rigorous theoretical analysis (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) that LFMs, despite not satisfying the block-diagonal condition, produce FICs with the same form as MMs. This is a significant theoretical extension of the FAB framework. The paper also introduces a shrinkage acceleration method based on the Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) and provides identifiability analysis with a merge post-processing step. This work builds upon prior FAB research for MMs [5,6] and connects to related Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [10,11] and variational Bayesian approaches [3].

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical contributions (Lemma 1, Theorem 2, Theorem 4) are rigorously proven with appropriate assumptions, and the authors carefully address the relationship between the FIC and marginal log-likelihood. The experimental section provides comprehensive evaluations across artificial simulations, synthetic block data, and real-world datasets, with thorough comparisons against state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP). The computational cost reduction from the shrinkage acceleration (Figures 1-2) is particularly compelling, showing a significant speedup while maintaining model selection accuracy. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that the FIC approximation may be less accurate for small N, and provide empirical evidence of this (Libras dataset results).

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from motivation to theoretical development to experiments. The mathematical derivations are presented carefully, though some aspects could be clarified. The connection between FAB and CCCP (Section 3) is well-explained, but the intuition behind Lemma 1 (why the log determinant of the Hessian has that specific form for LFMs) could be strengthened with more intuitive explanation. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are clear, though the "merge()" subroutine is referenced to supplementary materials rather than being detailed in the main text. Overall, the paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the experiments, though some deeper theoretical intuition would enhance accessibility.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs despite the non-block-diagonal Hessian condition, which was previously thought to be a fundamental requirement. The key insight that LFMs produce the same FIC form as MMs (Theorem 2) is novel and theoretically important. The shrinkage acceleration via CCCP interpretation (Section 3) and the identifiability analysis with merge post-processing (Theorem 4) are also original contributions. While FAB for MMs has been previously developed, this extension to LFMs represents a meaningful advancement that broadens the applicability of the FAB framework. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, citing relevant work on IBP, variational Bayesian methods, and related nonparametric approaches.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. LFMs are widely used in applications like link prediction [16], but model selection (determining the number of latent features) has been computationally prohibitive for large-scale data. FAB/LFMs offers a scalable solution that automatically selects the number of features without hyperparameters, with better model selection accuracy than state-of-the-art methods. The computational efficiency gains from the shrinkage acceleration are particularly valuable for real-world applications. The paper's experimental results consistently show FAB/LFMs achieving better predictive performance (higher PLL) with lower computational cost compared to alternatives. The ability to automatically select model complexity while avoiding overfitting (evidenced by smaller TLL-PLL differences) addresses a critical challenge in practical latent variable modeling.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs despite non-block-diagonal Hessian condition
- Rigorous proofs of key theoretical results (Lemma 1, Theorem 2, Theorem 4)
- Practical shrinkage acceleration method that significantly reduces computational cost
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets
- Clear demonstration of better model selection accuracy and computational efficiency

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited intuition provided for why Lemma 1 holds (why log det |F(d)| has that specific form)
- Some details (merge() subroutine) relegated to supplementary materials
- Could better specify when the FIC approximation may fail (small N)

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending the FAB framework to LFMs, demonstrating that the FIC can be applied to a broader class of models than previously thought possible. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the practical implications are substantial for real-world applications of latent feature models. The paper advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection for latent variable models, offering a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically efficient.

The paper meets all NIPS review criteria: it's technically sound with strong theoretical and experimental support, clearly written with a logical structure, makes a meaningful original contribution, and addresses a significant problem with practical implications. I believe this paper would be of interest to the NIPS community and represents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models.

**Recommendation: Accept**
