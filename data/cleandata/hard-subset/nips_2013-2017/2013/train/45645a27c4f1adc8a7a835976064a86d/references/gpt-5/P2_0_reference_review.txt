Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference—previously developed for mixture models and HMMs—to latent feature models (LFMs) with Bernoulli feature activations and linear-Gaussian likelihood. The core theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, showing that, asymptotically, the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian of the complete log-likelihood decomposes into the same “usage-count” penalty that appears in FAB for mixtures: (D/2)∑k log ∑n znk. This yields an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) that has exactly the same form as for MMs, despite LFMs violating the block-diagonal Hessian structure previously required. Consequently, the FAB lower bound can be optimized with an EM-like procedure: a mean-field E-step with a sigmoid update that includes an automatic shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k), a closed-form M-step (PPCA-like), and a shrinkage step that prunes always-on or never-on features. The authors re-interpret the E-step via CCCP, which motivates an “accelerateShrinkage” routine (multiple CCCP updates per E-step) to remove irrelevant features earlier. They also prove a form of identifiability: when two columns of W coincide, their expected usages match at stationary points, motivating a practical merge post-processing. Experiments on synthetic, block data, and several real datasets compare FAB against EM, IBP (Gibbs and VB), and MEIBP; FAB typically selects more compact models, has competitive or better predictive log-likelihoods, and is substantially faster than IBP/VB while somewhat slower than MEIBP but with better predictive performance.

Quality
- Theory: The main asymptotic claim (Lemma 1) is the lynchpin; if correct under the stated “mild assumptions,” it convincingly justifies the FIC penalty for LFMs and supports Theorem 2 (FIC ≈ marginal log-likelihood up to O(1)). The paper cleanly connects to BIC as a contrast. However, the assumptions and proof are deferred to the supplement. As they are central, a brief statement in the main text of needed conditions (e.g., regularity, boundedness, growth rates of K vs N, avoidance of degenerate usage counts) would help assess rigor. The monotonicity claim for the overall algorithm relies on alternating maximization of the lower bound. The base E- and M-steps are standard and appear sound; the accelerateShrinkage routine uses multiple CCCP steps and simultaneous updates of π and π̃—this is motivated and plausible, but a brief argument that these inner steps maintain non-decreasing L would strengthen the guarantee (or an explicit note that the monotonicity guarantee applies to the outer loop but not necessarily to each inner step).
- Algorithmic design: The E-step update is derived clearly and the shrinkage term is the distinctive FAB regularizer (model-induced, prior-agnostic). The merge heuristic is justified by Theorem 4 and checked against L, which is sensible.
- Experiments: The synthetic and block data convincingly demonstrate shrinkage acceleration and feature recovery. Real-data comparisons indicate good predictive performance and compact K. However, fairness has caveats: (i) FAB learns noise variance while some baselines’ software fixes it; the authors fix it for baselines and follow prior settings, but this could favor or disfavor methods—an ablation where FAB also fixes variance would clarify. (ii) VB is omitted on large data due to time; that is understandable, but results for at least one medium-scale case would better position the speed-accuracy trade-off. (iii) The IBP hyperparameter α is set to the default (3), which can bias K; sensitivity analysis would help. Overall, the empirical evidence supports the claims, especially on automation and compactness, but additional controlled ablations would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The motivation for FAB in LFMs is clear, the algorithm is specified, and the CCCP viewpoint is insightful. A few points would improve clarity and reproducibility:
- State the key assumptions for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 in the main text, at least informally, and indicate how K may scale with N.
- Explicitly specify computational complexity per iteration and the impact of Tshrink on wall-clock time.
- Clarify the status of monotonicity with accelerateShrinkage (does L always increase after the inner loop?).
- Provide more detail on the merge procedure (search criterion, complexity), even if a full pseudocode is in the supplement.
- Clean minor typos and notational slips.

Originality
Conceptually, extending FAB beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians to LFMs is novel and nontrivial. Showing that the same FIC penalty emerges for LFMs via a new Hessian determinant analysis is a neat and unexpected result. The CCCP reinterpretation and the identifiability/merging perspective are also original and practically useful. The work is clearly differentiated from IBP (nonparametric priors), variational IBP, and MEIBP, as well as from MAD-Bayes and BIC. Related work is adequately cited.

Significance
The contribution is significant on two fronts:
- Theory: It broadens the applicability of FAB and suggests a path to similar FIC-style criteria for other latent variable models that do not satisfy earlier structural assumptions.
- Practice: It yields a scalable, fully automated method for selecting the number of latent features, with competitive predictive performance and strong shrinkage, filling a gap between flexible but costly nonparametric Bayes and faster but less automated EM/VB. The identifiability insight and merge step are practical contributions likely to be adopted.

Pros
- Elegant theoretical extension: same FIC penalty for LFMs despite structural differences.
- Automated, prior-agnostic model selection with clear shrinkage mechanism.
- CCCP-based acceleration that materially reduces runtime.
- Identifiability analysis leading to a principled merge post-processing.
- Solid empirical results showing good predictive performance and compact models.

Cons and questions
- Key assumptions and proofs are in the supplement; summarize them in the main text and discuss when they may fail (e.g., near-degenerate feature usage, small N).
- Clarify guarantees for accelerateShrinkage regarding monotonic improvement of L.
- Fairness of baselines: effect of fixed noise variance; sensitivity of IBP to α; omission of VB on medium-scale cases.
- Scope limitations: diagonal noise only; mean-field assumption on Z; Gaussian likelihood; how do results extend to non-Gaussian LFMs?
- Behavior when K scales with N or when features are extremely sparse/dense; any failure modes?

Suggestions
- Add a brief proof sketch or at least the main assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main paper.
- Include an ablation showing the effect of the −D/(2Nπ̃k) term (turn it off and compare) and the effect of Tshrink.
- Report time-vs-accuracy curves and complexity per iteration to quantify acceleration benefits.
- Provide a sensitivity study for IBP α and for FAB initialization.
- Compare against ARD-style dimensionality selection baselines (e.g., Bayesian PCA/FA) where applicable.

Overall assessment
A strong submission: technically sound (pending clarity on assumptions), original, and practically useful. With minor clarifications and a couple of additional ablations, it would be a solid accept.
