## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The key challenge addressed is that LFMs don't naturally satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by the original FAB method. The authors demonstrate through asymptotic analysis that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same form as for MMs despite this difference, enabling the application of FAB.  They also provide a theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability and propose a shrinkage acceleration method based on a connection to the Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), and demonstrate the empirical benefits of the method.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds directly on the FAB framework introduced in Fujimaki et al. (2017, 2018) for MMs and HMMs. It positions itself within the broader landscape of Bayesian non-parametric (BNP) methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) (Griffiths & Ghahramani, 2005) and variational Bayesian (VB) approaches (Doshi-Velez et al., 2012) for LFM dimensionality selection.  The paper acknowledges the computational limitations of these existing methods as motivation for exploring FAB's potential efficiency gains.  The comparison to MEIBP (Reed & Ghahramani, 2012) is relevant, highlighting the different asymptotic assumptions (zero noise variance in MAD-Bayes vs. large sample size in FAB). The connection drawn to CCCP (Yuille & Rangarajan, 1995) is a clever insight.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The core result demonstrating the equivalence of FIC forms for MMs and LFMs despite the differing Hessian structures is a significant theoretical advance.  This expands the applicability of the FAB framework beyond its initial scope. The analysis is clearly presented and the Lemma 1 is well-motivated.
* **Practical Algorithm Enhancements:** The introduction of the shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP is a strong practical contribution, demonstrably reducing computational cost.  The connection between the CCCP interpretation and the FAB regularization term is insightful.
* **Identifiability Analysis:** The discussion of parameter identifiability in LFMs and the proposed merging post-processing step address a crucial issue often overlooked in latent feature models. This contributes to a more robust and interpretable algorithm.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are comprehensive, demonstrating the method’s performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.  The comparison with competing methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) is fair and highlights FAB’s strengths in model selection accuracy and computational efficiency.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Core FAB Idea:** While the extension to LFMs is valuable, the underlying FAB concept isn't new. The paper’s contribution lies primarily in *applying* FAB to a new domain and addressing the technical challenges this entails.  This isn’t necessarily a negative, but it’s important to recognize.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of log-priors being constant with respect to N, while common in asymptotic analysis, is a strong one. The paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss the implications of relaxing this assumption.
* **Implementation Details:** The experimental section could benefit from more details about the implementation. For example, specific hyperparameter settings (beyond δ and Tshrink) and computational infrastructure used would enhance reproducibility. The discussion around the noise standard deviation fixed to 1 for artificial simulations and 0.75 for real data feels slightly ad-hoc and could use more justification.
* **Merge Post-Processing:** The description of the merge post-processing is brief. A more detailed explanation of the criteria for merging features and the impact on performance would be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper predominantly highlights successes.  It would be beneficial to discuss scenarios where FAB/LFMs might struggle or underperform compared to other methods, and potential remedies.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical justification for extending FAB to LFMs.
* Novel and effective algorithm enhancements (shrinkage acceleration).
* Comprehensive empirical validation demonstrating competitive performance.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Addresses an important practical problem: efficient model selection for LFMs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited core novelty beyond applying an existing framework.
* Reliance on strong asymptotic assumptions.
* Some lack of detail in the experimental setup.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian non-parametric modeling. The theoretical insights and practical enhancements presented significantly extend the applicability and efficiency of the FAB framework. While the core idea isn’t entirely novel, the extension to LFMs and the accompanying algorithmic improvements are substantial. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* Provide a more thorough discussion of the assumptions and their potential impact on results.
* Expand the description of the merge post-processing step.
* Include more details about the experimental setup, including hyperparameter settings and computational infrastructure.
* Discuss potential failure cases and limitations of the method.
* Consider addressing the sensitivity of performance to the fixed noise standard deviation in the experiments.
