Summary
This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian likelihoods. The core theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, showing that the Hessian determinant for each output dimension induces the same log-sum activation term log ∑n znk that appears in FAB for mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian structure used in earlier FAB derivations. This leads to an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) with the same form as for mixtures, implying asymptotic equivalence to the marginal log-likelihood (Corollary 3). On the algorithmic side, the authors derive a tractable lower bound using mean-field q(Z), obtain a fixed-point E-step with a FAB-induced shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k), and standard M-step updates. They further interpret the E/π̃ updates as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), propose a shrinkage-acceleration routine (multiple CCCP steps per E-step) to eliminate irrelevant features early, and analyze identifiability, proving that FAB favors balanced activations when W columns coincide, which motivates a post-hoc “merge” step.

Experiments on synthetic data, block images, and real datasets compare against IBP Gibbs sampling, VB for IBP, MEIBP, and a variational EM baseline. FAB achieves competitive or superior model selection and predictive log-likelihood with lower computational cost than IBP/VB and typically smaller K than EM; MEIBP can be faster but often yields worse predictive performance.

Quality
- Theory: The main asymptotic result (Lemma 1) is pivotal and interesting. If the stated “mild assumptions” (deferred to the supplement) hold broadly, showing that LFMs inherit the same FIC penalty as mixtures is a notable finding. The resulting FICLFM and its lower bound are consistent, and the monotonic ascent of the bound under the basic alternating scheme is standard. The CCCP interpretation is sound and clarifies the role of the shrinkage term.
- That said, several claims rely on supplemental proofs/assumptions: (i) precise conditions for Lemma 1 (e.g., regularity, boundedness, scaling of Z with N, conditions on Λ) and when O_p(1) terms are negligible; (ii) the validity of dropping log-determinant terms in Laplace factors; (iii) the monotonicity guarantee when using accelerateShrinkage (multiple CCCP steps and simultaneous π/π̃ updates) and during feature pruning/merging. Please make explicit which steps retain the lower-bound ascent guarantee and which are heuristic.
- Identifiability: Theorem 4 provides a useful bias toward balanced activations for duplicated columns. A short intuitive explanation and precise conditions (e.g., excluding degenerate ∑n µnk/N ∈ {0,1}) are appreciated; a pointer to how often the merge increases the bound in practice would help.
- Empirics: The comparisons are illuminating but raise fairness concerns. FAB learns noise precision, while other baselines often use fixed noise (1 for synthetic, 0.75 for real) following their original code. This likely advantages FAB in predictive likelihood. Likewise, IBP’s α is fixed at 3, potentially biasing K upward, and MEIBP’s initialization is sometimes constrained (e.g., K=100) for runtime reasons. Please provide sensitivity analyses: (a) allow noise learning (when supported) or match noise across methods; (b) vary α or use posterior-inference on α; (c) ablate shrinkage acceleration and merge steps; (d) report wall-clock vs. iterations and per-iteration complexity; (e) evaluate on larger-scale data where MEIBP’s speed advantage may be more pronounced.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, but several important assumptions are deferred. Summarize the key assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text and give high-level intuition (e.g., how the shared θd still yields additive log ∑n znk contributions). Provide per-iteration complexity in terms of N, D, K for the baseline and accelerated versions.
- Minor issues: “mean-filed” should be “mean-field”; duplicated words (“this this”); clarify the footnote about regular vs. singular models and when FZ, Fk inverses exist. Formalize the conditions under which eliminating features with ∑n µnk/N ∈ {0,1} preserves/increases the bound.
- The merge procedure is only sketched; a brief criterion in the main text (e.g., threshold on ||w·k − w·l|| and proof sketch that the lower bound increases) would help reproducibility.

Originality
- Extending FAB to LFMs that violate the block-diagonal Hessian assumption is novel, as is the specific asymptotic Hessian determinant calculation that recovers the log ∑n znk term. The CCCP perspective on the E-step and the resulting acceleration are incremental but useful. The identifiability analysis and merge post-processing are thoughtful and practically relevant. The work is clearly differentiated from IBP (Gibbs, VB), MEIBP, and “MAD-Bayes.”

Significance
- LFMs are widely used, and automatic model selection without priors/hyperparameters is attractive. The FIC result suggests broader applicability of FAB to latent feature models beyond mixtures/HMMs. If the assumptions hold in common settings, this could influence future work on scalable, automated model selection for latent variable models. The empirical results are promising, though a more balanced experimental protocol would strengthen the claims.

Pros
- Conceptually clean extension of FAB to LFMs; elegant asymptotic result recovering the key FAB penalty.
- Practical algorithm with monotonic bound ascent (for the basic updates), automatic feature pruning, and a useful CCCP-based acceleration.
- Identifiability-aware analysis and merge heuristic; good empirical performance with compact models.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on assumptions/proofs in the supplement; main text lacks explicit conditions and intuition for Lemma 1.
- Some steps in the accelerated procedure and post-processing appear heuristic; monotonicity guarantees are unclear.
- Experimental fairness concerns (noise learning, α settings, initialization constraints) and limited ablations.

Suggestions for the authors
- State the precise assumptions of Lemma 1 in the main text, add intuition, and discuss scope/limitations (e.g., non-diagonal Λ, non-binary features).
- Clarify which steps preserve lower-bound monotonicity; if accelerateShrinkage or merge can break it, report how often and how you guard against it (e.g., accept only if L increases).
- Provide per-iteration complexity and a runtime vs. K shrinkage trajectory; include ablations for acceleration and merge.
- Improve fairness: allow noise learning for baselines where possible or fix noise for all methods; tune α; include sensitivity plots.
- Expand comparisons to related factor models (e.g., BPFA/beta-process factor analysis, ARD-based sparse factor models) if feasible.

Recommendation: Weak accept
A solid theoretical extension with a practical algorithm and promising results. Addressing the clarity and experimental fairness issues would significantly strengthen the paper.
