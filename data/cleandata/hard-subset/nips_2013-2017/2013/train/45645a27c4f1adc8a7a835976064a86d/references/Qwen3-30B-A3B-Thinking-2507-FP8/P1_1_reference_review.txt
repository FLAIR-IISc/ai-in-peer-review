

# Peer Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary
This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), a significant advancement that addresses a key limitation of previous FAB work. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the specific conditions (block-diagonal Hessian) required by prior FAB work for mixture models, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models. This allows FAB inference to be applied to LFMs, enabling fully automated model selection (determining the number of latent features) without hyperparameters. The paper introduces a novel CCCP interpretation of the FAB optimization process, leading to a shrinkage acceleration method that drastically reduces computational cost. It also provides theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability, which informs a merge post-processing step for latent features. The authors validate their approach through comprehensive experiments showing superior computational efficiency and model selection accuracy compared to state-of-the-art methods like IBP, VB, and MEIBP.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. The key Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 provide compelling theoretical justification for extending FAB to LFMs, which is particularly impressive given that LFMs violate the assumptions that enabled previous FAB work. The authors carefully address the theoretical challenges of applying Laplace's method to LFMs, where parameters are shared across latent features. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with comparisons across artificial data, block data, and real-world datasets, and includes a careful analysis of computational cost, model selection accuracy, and predictive performance. The paper also acknowledges limitations (e.g., FIC approximation may not be accurate for small N).

**Originality:** This work makes several novel contributions. The extension of FAB to LFMs is significant because LFMs are a fundamental class of models that don't satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by prior FAB work. The CCCP interpretation of the FAB optimization provides a fresh perspective and leads to the practical shrinkage acceleration method. The identifiability analysis and merge post-processing are also novel theoretical insights specific to LFMs. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, contrasting with related work on IBP, VB, and MEIBP.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to algorithmic implementation to experimental validation. The key theoretical results are clearly presented, and the connection between the theoretical analysis and practical algorithmic improvements is well-maintained. The supplementary materials (mentioned but not reviewed here) appear to contain rigorous proofs that support the main claims.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important practical challenge in latent variable modeling: automated model selection without hyperparameters. LFMs have broad applications (e.g., link prediction), but existing methods like VB are computationally expensive for large-scale data. FAB/LFMs offer a promising solution that achieves state-of-the-art model selection accuracy with significantly lower computational cost. The theoretical insights about identifiability and the shrinkage behavior also contribute to our fundamental understanding of Bayesian model selection for singular models.

## Weaknesses

**Minor Theoretical Clarification:** While the paper establishes that FIC for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models, the connection between the Hessian analysis (Lemma 1) and the FIC derivation could be made more explicit. The paper states that "the log ∑n znk term naturally appears in log det |F(d)| without A1 and A2," but a brief explanation of why this occurs would strengthen the theoretical narrative.

**Experimental Details:** The paper mentions that FAB consistently achieved good predictive performance with low computational cost, but could provide more specific details on the magnitude of the computational speedup (e.g., "FAB was X times faster than VB on dataset Y"). The comparison with MEIBP on real-world data is limited by the fact that MEIBP was unable to complete on some datasets within reasonable time.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the theory and practice of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and well-supported, the shrinkage acceleration method is both theoretically grounded and practically valuable, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with practical significance.

The main weakness is a minor theoretical clarification that could be addressed in revision, but this does not undermine the paper's substantial contributions. The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing theoretical and algorithmic advances in Bayesian inference with applications to machine learning.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel extension of FAB to LFMs, addressing a key limitation of previous work
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with compelling proofs
- Practical algorithm with significant computational improvements
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing superiority over state-of-the-art methods
- Theoretical insights on identifiability and model shrinkage that advance fundamental understanding

**Con:**
- Minor theoretical clarification needed in the Hessian analysis section
- Limited comparison with MEIBP on some real-world datasets due to computational constraints

## Final Recommendation
I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models, with practical implications for large-scale data analysis. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The minor weaknesses identified are easily addressable in revision, but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution to the field. This work advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection and has the potential to influence both theoretical and applied research in machine learning.
