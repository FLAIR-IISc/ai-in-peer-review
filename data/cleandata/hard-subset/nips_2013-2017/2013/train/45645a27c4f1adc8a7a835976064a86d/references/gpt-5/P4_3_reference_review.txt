Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM settings [5,6] to latent feature models (LFMs) with linear-Gaussian likelihood and Bernoulli features [9,17]. The core technical contribution is Lemma 1, which asserts that the log-determinant of the Hessian of the negative complete-data log-likelihood with respect to per-dimension parameters θd yields, asymptotically, a sum over features of log counts of feature activations, ∑k log ∑n znk, up to Op(1). This yields an asymptotic approximation (Theorem 2) to the joint marginal log-likelihood log p(X,Z|M) of the form J(Z, P̂) = log p(X,Z|P̂) − |P| − (DK/2) log N − (D/2) ∑k log ∑n znk, mirroring the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for mixtures [6]. Substituting this into the standard variational representation of the marginal likelihood produces an FIC for LFMs with the same structure as FIC for mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian conditions previously required.

The authors propose a practical FAB algorithm that optimizes a tractable lower bound using: (i) linearization of the concave −log ∑n znk term at π̃k, (ii) replacing the MLE with current parameters P, and (iii) a mean-field factorization q(zn)=∏k Bern(µnk). The resulting E-step updates µnk via a sigmoid with a shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k), which enforces automatic pruning of irrelevant features. M-steps are closed-form as in PPCA [24], and πk = π̃k = (1/N)∑n µnk. The paper also interprets the µ/π̃ updates as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) [29] and proposes a shrinkage acceleration that takes multiple CCCP steps per iteration. An identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) shows FAB resolves a class of non-identifiabilities (e.g., duplicated columns of W) by equalizing feature usages and motivates a merge post-processing that increases the FAB objective.

Experiments on synthetic data, block patterns [10], and several real datasets compare FAB to variational EM, IBP Gibbs sampling [2], VB for IBP [3], and MEIBP [22]. FAB typically selects more compact models (smaller K), achieves competitive or better predictive log-likelihood (PLL), and reduces the gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods. MEIBP is often faster but yields worse PLL. The CCCP-based acceleration substantially reduces FAB’s runtime by early pruning.

Quality
- Theory: The main asymptotic result (Lemma 1 → Theorem 2) is central and, if the stated “mild assumptions” hold, technically significant. However, the paper defers all conditions and proofs to the supplement. In particular, the validity of Laplace approximation in the presence of binary latent variables and the precise conditions under which log det|F(d)| ≈ ∑k log ∑n znk + Op(1) hold (e.g., handling features with ∑n znk ∈ {0,1}, behavior near the boundary of πk, and regularity/nonsingularity of the Fisher information) should be clearly stated in the main text. The CCCP interpretation is correct and useful; the algorithmic steps preserve monotonicity of the displayed lower bound provided each substep is solved exactly, and the merge step checks the bound before accepting changes.
- Algorithm: The lower bound uses a mean-field q(Z), which loosens the FIC; the paper claims monotone improvement of this bound but should clarify whether the CCCP inner loop and accelerateShrinkage preserve monotonicity per outer iteration. The shrinkage and merge steps are sensible and consistent with the regularizer.
- Empirics: Experiments are informative and include synthetic, structured synthetic (block data), and real datasets. However, baseline comparability has caveats: FAB learns λ while some baselines fix noise variance; IBP hyperparameter α is fixed at 3; VB was omitted for real data due to runtime; MEIBP requires non-negativity and was omitted or initialized specially in some cases. A sensitivity analysis (e.g., learning λ in baselines, varying α, alternative initial K, and reporting confidence intervals) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with clear algorithm boxes and links to prior FAB work. However, key assumptions for Lemma 1/Theorem 2 should be summarized in the main text, as they are crucial to the applicability beyond block-diagonal Hessians. It would help to:
- Provide a brief sketch explaining why log det|F(d)| decomposes into ∑k log counts under the stated model.
- State what happens and how the algorithm handles degenerate counts (∑n µnk ≈ 0 or 1) beyond pruning.
- Discuss per-iteration computational complexity and memory cost explicitly.
- Clarify that the lower bound is distinct from the exact FIC and what guarantees hold under the mean-field restriction.

Originality
Extending FAB to LFMs that violate prior block-diagonal Hessian assumptions is novel and interesting. Showing that the FIC for LFMs retains the same form as for mixtures is a notable theoretical insight. The CCCP interpretation and the associated shrinkage acceleration are also original within this context, as is the identifiability analysis and merge heuristic.

Significance
LFMs are widely used, and automatic feature-number selection without hyperparameters is valuable. The asymptotic connection and the practical algorithm could see adoption, particularly when large N makes IBP inference costly. While MEIBP can be faster, FAB’s predictive advantages and compactness are meaningful. The contribution advances FAB methodology and broadens its applicability.

Pros
- Clear theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs with a principled asymptotic penalty.
- Practical, hyperparameter-free algorithm with guaranteed bound improvement and automatic feature pruning.
- CCCP-based acceleration that materially reduces runtime.
- Identifiability analysis and merge post-processing grounded in the objective.
- Empirical results show competitive or better PLL and more compact models.

Cons
- Key assumptions and proof details are relegated to the supplement; some must be surfaced in the main text for evaluation.
- Baseline comparisons may be biased by differing treatment of noise variance and hyperparameters; VB results are incomplete.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and convergence behavior under accelerateShrinkage.
- Sensitivity analyses (initial K, α, noise variance) and statistical variability (error bars) are largely absent.

Suggestions for improvement
- Summarize the assumptions for Lemma 1/Theorem 2 in the main paper, including discussion of degenerate feature counts and Fisher regularity.
- Report per-iteration complexity and empirical scaling with N, D, K; show convergence plots for the bound with and without acceleration.
- Strengthen empirical fairness: allow baselines to learn noise variance where possible; vary α; include more VB results or a justified rationale; provide sensitivity to initial K and runtime-accuracy trade-offs; report variability across seeds.
- Include an ablation isolating the impact of the CCCP acceleration and the merge step on runtime and PLL.
- Clarify the relationship between the mean-field lower bound and the asymptotic FIC; state which guarantees survive the factorization.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a technically meaningful and practically useful extension of FAB to LFMs, with a compelling asymptotic argument, a sound bound-optimizing algorithm, and effective accelerations. With clearer presentation of the main assumptions and strengthened empirical comparisons, this work would be a solid contribution to NeurIPS.
