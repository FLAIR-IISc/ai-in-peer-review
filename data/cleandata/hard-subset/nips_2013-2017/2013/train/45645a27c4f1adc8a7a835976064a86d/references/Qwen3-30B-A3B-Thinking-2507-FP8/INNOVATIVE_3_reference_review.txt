

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper introduces a significant theoretical and practical advancement by extending Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), a class of models previously thought to be incompatible with the FAB framework due to not satisfying the block diagonal Hessian condition required for standard FAB derivation.

The core novelty lies in Lemma 1, which demonstrates that despite LFMs not satisfying the conditions that naturally induce FIC/FAB for Mixture Models, the log determinant of the Hessian matrix still takes the form log ∑n znk/N + O(1). This insight enables the derivation of FIC for LFMs that has the exact same form as FIC for MMs (Equation 9), a surprising and theoretically elegant result. The authors further establish that FAB/LFMs inherit desirable properties from FAB/MMs, including automatic model selection (without hyperparameters), monotonic convergence, and parameter identifiability.

The paper's most compelling contribution is the practical implementation of FAB for LFMs, which solves a major pain point in latent feature modeling: the computational expense of model selection. The authors introduce a novel shrinkage acceleration method based on interpreting the FAB optimization as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), which dramatically reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early in the process. This is not merely an implementation detail—it represents a fundamental theoretical insight with practical consequences, as evidenced by the 10x speedup shown in Figure 1.

The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) is another significant theoretical contribution. It resolves a known issue in LFMs where multiple latent features could produce identical representations, and provides a principled post-processing method (merge()) to combine overlapping features. This is particularly valuable for model interpretability and compactness.

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The artificial simulations demonstrate FAB's superior model selection accuracy compared to state-of-the-art methods, especially for large datasets. The block data visualization (Figure 3) provides clear evidence that FAB successfully extracts true patterns without irrelevant features, while competing methods retain extraneous components. The real-world data experiments (Table 1) show FAB consistently achieves better predictive performance with lower computational cost, and importantly, demonstrates significantly smaller differences between training and test log-likelihoods—indicating better generalization.

This work stands out in the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. While previous work on FAB focused on MMs and HMMs, this paper extends the framework to a fundamentally different class of models (LFMs), demonstrating remarkable theoretical consistency. The connection to the Indian Buffet Process literature (Section 1.1) is well-handled, and the paper clearly positions itself as a significant advancement over existing methods.

The paper's only minor weaknesses are that the theoretical conditions for Lemma 1 could be more thoroughly discussed (though supplementary materials provide proofs), and the authors could have more explicitly compared their identifiability analysis to related work on non-identifiable learning machines. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's substantial contributions.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper exceptionally compelling. The core insight—that LFMs can be treated with the same FIC framework as MMs despite not satisfying the block diagonal condition—is bold and theoretically profound. The practical implementation with the CCCP-based acceleration method is both elegant and impactful, directly addressing a major bottleneck in latent feature modeling. The experimental results robustly validate the theoretical claims.

This work advances the state of the art in model selection for latent variable models in a demonstrable way, offering a principled, automated approach that outperforms existing methods in both accuracy and efficiency. The theoretical insights are substantial, and the practical implications are significant for practitioners dealing with large-scale latent feature modeling problems.

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a clear, significant contribution to the field. It addresses an important problem with a novel theoretical insight that leads to practical benefits, which is exactly the kind of work that NIPS values most highly. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the authors wisely prioritize the practical benefits of their method, which is consistent with the paper's emphasis on automation over controllability.

In summary, this paper presents a genuinely novel extension of a promising Bayesian inference framework to a new class of models, with strong theoretical justification and compelling empirical results. It represents a significant conceptual advance that will likely influence future work in Bayesian model selection for latent variable models.

**Recommendation: Accept**
