This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core contribution lies in demonstrating that despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions required for standard FAB application (block diagonal Hessian), a similar FIC formulation and advantageous properties can be achieved through asymptotic analysis. The authors provide theoretical justification (Lemma 1, Theorem 2), algorithmic enhancements (shrinkage acceleration via a CCCP interpretation), and empirical validation showcasing computational speedup, model selection accuracy, and predictive performance.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, building upon Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, is a strong technical contribution. Demonstrating the applicability of FAB to a broader class of models beyond those satisfying the initial block diagonal Hessian condition is valuable.
* **Algorithmic Innovation:** The connection to the Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) and the resulting shrinkage acceleration method represent a practical improvement, demonstrably reducing computational cost in experiments.
* **Identifiability Analysis:** Addressing the parameter identifiability issue in LFMs and proposing a merge post-processing step based on this analysis adds depth to the work and provides a potential solution to a common problem in latent variable modeling.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, including both synthetic and real-world datasets. The comparison with existing methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) is thorough and highlights the strengths of the proposed FAB/LFM approach.
* **Clear Writing (mostly):**  The paper is generally well-organized and clearly explains the key concepts. The notation is consistently used.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity in Motivation:** While the introduction highlights the limitations of existing methods, the *practical* motivation for extending FAB to LFMs beyond theoretical interest isn't strongly articulated.  What specific, large-scale applications are enabled by the improved computational efficiency? A more concrete use case would strengthen the impact.
* **Complexity of Presentation:** The theoretical sections, while rigorous, are dense and potentially challenging for readers unfamiliar with the intricacies of asymptotic analysis and FIC. While detail is appreciated, some streamlining or more intuitive explanations could broaden accessibility.
* **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** Lemma 1 relies on “some mild assumptions” mentioned in the supplementary materials. The significance and potential limitations of these assumptions should be briefly discussed within the main paper.  What happens if these assumptions are violated?
* **Comparison Depth:** While comparisons are extensive, the discussion of *why* FAB/LFM outperforms (or underperforms) other methods could be more insightful. For example, the role of the regularization term in preventing overfitting is mentioned but not fully explored.
* **Merge Post-processing Details:** The description of the merge post-processing is somewhat vague. A more detailed explanation of the distance metric used for feature overlap and the criteria for merging would be beneficial. The observation that “a few merging operations were likely to occur” doesn’t convey much information.

**Originality:**

The extension of FAB inference to LFMs is novel and represents a significant contribution. The connection to CCCP and the identifiability analysis are also original ideas. The paper builds upon existing work in FAB, IBP, and VB, but clearly differentiates itself by addressing a limitation of the original FAB framework and providing a practical solution.

**Significance:**

This work has the potential to be highly significant. FAB/LFM offers a computationally efficient and theoretically grounded approach to latent feature modeling, which is relevant to a wide range of applications in machine learning, including dimensionality reduction, feature extraction, and unsupervised learning.  The automation of model selection (determining latent feature dimensionality) is a valuable feature, particularly for large-scale datasets.  The shrinking acceleration and identifiability-driven merge process further enhance its practicality.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound extension of FAB inference.
* Demonstrable computational advantages over existing methods.
* Comprehensive empirical validation on both synthetic and real data.
* Theoretical insights into parameter identifiability and model selection.
* Potential for broad applicability in latent variable modeling.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The theoretical sections may be too dense for some readers.
* The practical motivation could be better articulated.
* Limited discussion of the assumptions underlying the theoretical results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  The paper presents a strong technical contribution with significant potential impact. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above—specifically, improving the clarity of the motivation, discussing the assumptions, and providing more detail on the merge post-processing—would further strengthen the manuscript. The authors have made a compelling case for the advantages of FAB/LFM and have provided convincing evidence of its effectiveness.
