This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), a challenging task given the limitations of the original FAB framework. The authors convincingly demonstrate that despite LFMs not meeting the initial block-diagonal Hessian condition required by FAB, a similar FIC representation can be derived. The introduction of a shrinkage acceleration method via a connection to the Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) and the proof of parameter identifiability are strong contributions. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the technical analysis appears rigorous (detailed proofs in the supplementary material are appreciated).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs is a genuinely novel contribution. The original FAB method had clear limitations, and this work overcomes a major one, broadening the applicability of the approach.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the Lemma 1 establishing the connection to MMs appear solid. The identifiability result is a welcome addition.
* **Practical Improvement:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration based on CCCP is a valuable optimization, demonstrably reducing computational cost in experiments.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and the explanations are generally clear, even for complex theoretical concepts.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets, and comparing FAB/LFM to relevant baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experimental Validation:** While the experimental results are positive, they primarily focus on demonstrating the speedup and model selection accuracy. A deeper dive into the *quality* of the learned latent features, beyond predictive performance, would be beneficial. For example, visualizing the learned basis vectors (W) for the real-world datasets.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (Implicit):** The paper states parameter settings were software default, but does not deeply explore the impact of these settings. It’s not clear how robust the performance is to changes in hyperparameters like the shrinkage parameter or the convergence tolerance.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons to IBP and VB are reasonable, more recent and potentially stronger LFM inference methods might exist that weren’t considered (although the authors do cite relevant work). A more exhaustive benchmarking against state-of-the-art approaches would strengthen the claims.
* **Merge Post-Processing:** The merge post-processing based on identifiability seems insightful, but the experimental evidence presented is weak. The authors state it was not observed in the small example, but more details about its impact (or lack thereof) in the larger datasets would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of the FAB framework, opening up its applicability to a broader class of models.
* The theoretical analysis is convincing, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
* The shrinkage acceleration is a valuable practical contribution.
* The experiments provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The experimental evaluation, while thorough, could be deepened to provide more insight into the quality of the learned representations.
* A more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art LFM inference methods would strengthen the paper.
* The merge post-processing requires further investigation to demonstrate its practical benefit.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. While not perfect, the paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent feature models. The authors have addressed a fundamental limitation of the FAB framework and demonstrated a practical improvement in computational efficiency. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results are promising. The minor weaknesses I have identified do not outweigh the overall strengths of the paper. The boldness of tackling this extension of FAB, and succeeding, is highly commendable. This work opens up exciting new avenues for research in Bayesian latent variable modeling.
