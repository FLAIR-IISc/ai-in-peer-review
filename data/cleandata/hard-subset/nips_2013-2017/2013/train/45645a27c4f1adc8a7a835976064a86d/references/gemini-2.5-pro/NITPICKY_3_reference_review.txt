**Summary**

This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The primary contribution is a theoretical derivation showing that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same functional form as that for Mixture Models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian assumption required by previous FAB work. This is achieved by an asymptotic analysis of the log-determinant of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood (Lemma 1). Based on this result, the authors derive the FAB/LFM algorithm, which performs model selection (i.e., determines the number of latent features, K) automatically without requiring tunable hyperparameters associated with priors. The paper further provides two analyses: 1) it interprets the E-step optimization as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), which motivates a practical "shrinkage acceleration" method to speed up computation, and 2) it analyzes the parameter identifiability of the model under the FAB objective, which informs a post-processing step for merging redundant features. Experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets show that FAB/LFM is competitive with or outperforms other methods like Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants and variational Bayes (VB) in terms of model selection accuracy and predictive performance, often with reduced computational cost.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Extension:** The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to the important class of LFMs. The core technical contribution, Lemma 1, is non-trivial as it circumvents the block-diagonal Hessian assumption that limited previous FAB applications, thus broadening the applicability of the framework.

2.  **Principled Algorithm Development:** The derivation of the algorithm from the asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood is well-motivated. The analyses connecting the optimization to CCCP and addressing parameter identifiability are valuable additions that lead to practical improvements (acceleration) and a more robust procedure (feature merging).

3.  **Automation and Practicality:** A key advantage of the proposed FAB/LFM is its ability to automatically select model complexity (K) without user-specified hyperparameters, a desirable property for a model selection tool. The proposed acceleration technique appears to significantly reduce computation time in practice, making the method more scalable.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments compare FAB/LFM against a relevant set of state-of-the-art baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) and demonstrate its strengths in model selection, predictive accuracy, and computational efficiency across several datasets.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are promising, the paper is marred by a substantial number of issues in its presentation, methodological details, and experimental setup that undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the polish expected of a top-tier publication.
    *   **Inconsistent Notation:** The notation is frequently inconsistent and confusing. `P` is used for the parameter set, but `Θ` also appears without definition (Eq. 10). The transpose operator is non-standardly denoted by `>` (e.g., `1b>`), which hinders readability. The `|P|` term in Eq. (8) is not defined, though one can infer it means the dimensionality of the parameter space. A footnote on page 2 contains a typo, `P (X,Z|P)`, which should presumably be `p(X,Z|P)`.
    *   **Grammar and Formatting:** There are numerous grammatical errors (e.g., "conduct two analysis") and formatting inconsistencies. The reference list is particularly messy, with inconsistent formatting for conference names, page numbers, and author lists.
    *   **Clarity of Algorithms:** Algorithm 1's convergence logic is opaque. It appears a merge operation (line 12) resets the loop without clarifying how the overall `Convergence` condition is met. The description of the merge post-processing is also confusing: the update rule `w*·k = 2w*·k` for merging two identical features seems incorrect and lacks justification, especially since `z` features are binary.

2.  **Opaque Theoretical Assumptions:** Key results like Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 rely on "mild assumptions" that are deferred entirely to the supplementary material. The main text should provide at least some intuition about these conditions to allow the reader to assess the scope and limitations of the theoretical claims without having to consult external material.

3.  **Methodological Ambiguity:**
    *   The introduction of a mean-field approximation on `Z` is a critical step that makes the algorithm tractable but also introduces an additional layer of approximation on top of the asymptotic one. The paper dismisses this by citing its use in other VB contexts but fails to discuss the potential interaction between the two approximations and its effect on the tightness of the final lower bound `L`.
    *   The choice of `Tshrink = 100` for the acceleration algorithm is presented without justification. The sensitivity of the algorithm's performance and speed to this parameter should be analyzed.

4.  **Flaws in Experimental Comparison:** The empirical evaluation, while extensive, suffers from fairness issues that call the conclusions into question.
    *   The most significant issue is that the noise variance was fixed for all baseline methods because their provided code did not support learning it, whereas the proposed FAB/LFM algorithm learns this parameter. This provides an unfair advantage to FAB/LFM and makes the comparison of likelihoods problematic. A fair comparison would require either fixing the noise for FAB/LFM or modifying the baselines to learn it.
    *   The complete omission of VB results due to "long computational time" is uninformative. Some quantitative data, even on a small dataset, should have been provided to justify this decision.
    *   The justification for IBP's performance (hyperparameter `α=3` "might cause overestimation") sounds speculative. For a fair comparison, this hyperparameter should have been tuned for the baseline.
    *   The initial number of features `K` for MEIBP was manually changed for two datasets, which contradicts the goal of a fully automated comparison.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and non-trivial extension of the FAB framework to LFMs.
*   The theoretical analysis provides new insights and leads to a practical, automated model selection algorithm.
*   The empirical results, despite flaws, suggest the method is highly promising.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper has a large number of clarity issues, including inconsistent notation, typos, and poor formatting, which detract significantly from its quality.
*   The experimental comparison is fundamentally flawed due to the inconsistent handling of the noise parameter, biasing the results in favor of the proposed method.
*   Key methodological and algorithmic details are ambiguous or poorly justified.

**Recommendation:**

Reject.

While the paper addresses an important problem and the core technical contribution is interesting, the manuscript in its current form falls short of the standards for NIPS. The combination of numerous presentation flaws, methodological ambiguities, and a critically unfair experimental comparison makes it difficult to confidently assess the work's contributions. The authors should undertake a major revision to address the clarity issues, provide a more transparent discussion of their assumptions and methods, and conduct a fair and rigorous experimental evaluation.
