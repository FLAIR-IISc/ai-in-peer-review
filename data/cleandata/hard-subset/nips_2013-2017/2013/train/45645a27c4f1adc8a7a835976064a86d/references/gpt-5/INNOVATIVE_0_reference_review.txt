Summary
This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian likelihood. The core theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, showing that the log-determinant of the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood with respect to per-dimension parameters yields, asymptotically, the same “Occam factor” term as in mixture models (MMs): a sum over features of log of their effective sample sizes (Σn znk). This implies that the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs has exactly the same form as for MMs, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian conditions used previously. Building on this, the authors derive an FIC lower bound with a mean-field approximation and propose an FAB algorithm with monotonic increases of the bound. They relate the E-step to a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), leading to a practical “shrinkage acceleration” that quickly prunes unused features. They also analyze identifiability, showing FAB regularization resolves a class of non-identifiability and motivates a merge post-processing. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets compare FAB/LFM against IBP (Gibbs and VB), MEIBP, and EM, showing competitive to superior model selection and predictive performance with reduced computation.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: The main insight—that the same FIC structure arises for LFMs without the block-diagonal condition—is surprising and important. If Lemma 1 holds under mild conditions, it significantly broadens the applicability of FIC/FAB beyond MMs/HMMs and points to a unifying asymptotic model selection principle for discrete latent variable models.
- Clean algorithmic story: The FIC leads to a simple shrinkage term in the mean-field E-step that automatically prunes features with no extra hyperparameters. The CCCP reinterpretation is elegant and leads to a practical acceleration scheme that appears to matter in practice.
- Identifiability analysis: Theorem 4 and the merging post-processing address a real pain point in LFMs (redundant features). This connects asymptotic model selection to pragmatic post-processing in a principled way.
- Empirical evidence: The method typically selects compact models with good predictive likelihood, with solid runtime improvements from the acceleration. The synthetic experiments nicely illustrate early shrinkage. On real data, FAB often achieves better PLL with fewer features than baselines.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions behind Lemma 1 and Theorem 2: The paper claims “mild assumptions,” but these are relegated to the supplement. For such a central claim, more intuition and a clear statement of the conditions (e.g., boundedness, regularity of Z moments, scaling of K with N, identifiability up to permutations, noise precision conditions) should appear in the main text. A brief proof sketch would help readers assess scope. Also, please clarify what happens when features are extremely sparse/dense (πk→0 or 1) finite-N corrections.
- Mean-field approximation and monotonicity: FICLFM is replaced by a tractable lower bound via mean-field and linearization. The paper asserts monotonic increase of the lower bound with alternating updates. Does the accelerateShrinkage routine (multiple CCCP inner steps updating π and π̃) preserve monotonicity at each outer iteration? An explicit statement and, ideally, a plot of the bound vs. iterations would strengthen trust.
- Fairness and rigor of experimental setup:
  - Baseline configurations: IBP α fixed to 3 (possibly overestimates K) and MEIBP’s sensitivity/initialization choices could bias comparisons. FAB learns noise precision while some baselines use fixed noise; despite matching prior works, this may advantage FAB. Please add ablations where all methods share learned/fixed noise settings if feasible, and sensitivity to IBP α and MEIBP initial K.
  - Scale and error bars: Include variability across seeds (std/CI), and report wall-clock vs. iterations with clear stopping criteria. Some baselines were omitted on large data; clarify whether that is due to timeouts and provide partial progress where possible.
  - Tasks: Since LFMs are widely used for link prediction and sparse reconstruction, consider at least one graph/link dataset (even small) to show generality beyond matrix completion/missing data.
- Scope of generality: While linear-Gaussian with diagonal noise is a common LFM, it would help to discuss how the analysis might extend to non-diagonal noise, non-Gaussian likelihoods (e.g., logistic), or other latent feature priors. What are the obstacles for extending Lemma 1 beyond this setting?
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is dense; moving the precise assumptions for Lemma 1/Theorem 2 and the merge procedure into the main text would improve clarity. Some typos and reference formatting issues remain.

Reasons to accept
- Substantial conceptual advance: Extending FIC/FAB to LFMs without the previous structural condition is a significant theoretical and practical step.
- Simple, automated model selection with compelling regularizer; no hand-tuned hyperparameters.
- Useful algorithmic refinements (CCCP-based acceleration) and an identifiability-aware merge that practitioners can adopt immediately.
- Strong empirical trend toward compact models with good predictive likelihood and favorable runtime.

Reasons to reject
- Heavy reliance on supplemental proofs for the key lemma with limited main-text conditions could hinder accessibility and verification.
- Some concerns about fairness/completeness of the empirical comparison setup and absence of uncertainty reporting.
- The current scope is limited to a specific LFM; broader generalization is suggested but not demonstrated.

Requested revisions
- State the precise assumptions for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 in the main text, plus a short proof sketch or intuition.
- Clarify monotonicity guarantees with accelerateShrinkage; include a bound-vs-iterations plot.
- Add ablations for noise variance settings and IBP/MEIBP hyperparameters; report error bars.
- Provide the merge procedure details in the main text and a sensitivity analysis of the merge threshold.
- Minor editing for clarity and typos.

Overall recommendation: Accept (7). The paper proposes a bold and impactful extension of FAB to LFMs, with a theoretically interesting result and practical benefits. While some experimental and expository aspects can be improved, the novelty and potential influence justify acceptance.

Confidence: 3.5/5 (familiar with asymptotic Bayes and latent feature models; did not verify supplemental proofs).
