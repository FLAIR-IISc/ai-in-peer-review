This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for prior FAB applications, a similar FIC representation and convergence properties can be derived. The authors further enhance the method with a shrinkage acceleration technique based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation and propose a merging post-processing step to address parameter identifiability issues.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction of FAB inference and its limitations, followed by a detailed derivation of the FIC for LFMs, algorithm description, analysis of the algorithm (CCCP and identifiability) and extensive experimental validation. The related work section is comprehensive, appropriately citing relevant literature on FAB, IBP, and variational inference methods. The mathematical derivations appear sound, with rigorous proofs relegated to the supplementary material, as appropriate. The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons with several baseline methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs is a valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of this promising Bayesian approximation method. The theoretical justification based on Lemma 1 is well presented.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The CCCP interpretation leading to the shrinkage acceleration is insightful and demonstrably improves computational efficiency. Addressing parameter identifiability and proposing a merging post-processing step adds to the robustness and practicality of the method.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is extensive, covering both synthetic and real-world data. The comparisons against state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) provide a strong validation of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details. The notation is consistent and clearly defined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** While the derivations are presented in a structured manner, the mathematical complexity might be a barrier for readers not deeply familiar with Bayesian inference and asymptotic analysis.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (Empirical):** Although the authors emphasize the automation aspect of FAB, some hyperparameters (e.g., δ for convergence, Tshrink for shrinkage acceleration) are still present and require tuning. The sensitivity of the results to these parameters could be further discussed.
*    **Comparison Limitations:** While the experiments are extensive, the parameter settings for the baseline methods (especially IBP) might be suboptimal, potentially influencing the comparison. The authors acknowledge some of these limitations but a more thorough sensitivity analysis for the baselines might strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Merge Post-Processing:** The merging post-processing is theoretically sound but its practical impact appears limited in the presented experiments (mostly minor merges). A more detailed analysis of scenarios where it significantly improves performance would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing FAB inference methods by extending its applicability to a broader class of models (LFMs).
*   The theoretical contributions (FIC derivation, CCCP analysis, identifiability) are substantial and well-justified.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed method compared to state-of-the-art alternatives.
*   The paper is well-written and thoroughly researched.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical complexity may limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
*   Some hyperparameter tuning is still required, diminishing the fully automated appeal of FAB.
*   The impact of the merging post-processing step could be more pronounced and better demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The extension of FAB to LFMs, coupled with the theoretical insights and experimental validation, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While some aspects could be further refined, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
