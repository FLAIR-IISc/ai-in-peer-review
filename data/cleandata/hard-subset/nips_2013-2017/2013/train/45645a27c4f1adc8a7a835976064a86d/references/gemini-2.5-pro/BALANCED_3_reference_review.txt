This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for model selection in latent variable models that works by optimizing a tractable lower bound on a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which is an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. Prior work on FAB was limited to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal, such as mixture models. LFMs do not satisfy this property due to shared parameters across features. The key theoretical contribution of this paper is Lemma 1, which shows that despite this structural difference, the log-determinant of the relevant Hessian for LFMs yields a complexity term of the same form as for mixture models. This allows the authors to derive an FIC for LFMs and a corresponding EM-like optimization algorithm (FAB/LFM) that automatically determines the number of latent features. The paper further provides two valuable refinements: an interpretation of the E-step update as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), which inspires an effective acceleration method for shrinking the model, and a proof of parameter identifiability, which motivates a post-processing step for merging redundant features. This work positions FAB/LFM as a computationally efficient, deterministic alternative to non-parametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) for model selection in LFMs.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is the successful extension of the FAB framework to LFMs. This is a non-trivial step, as it requires moving beyond the block-diagonal Hessian assumption that underpinned previous FAB applications. Lemma 1 is the crucial result that makes this possible, demonstrating that the underlying principle of FAB may be more broadly applicable than previously thought. This opens the door for applying FAB to other complex latent variable models.

2.  **Practical Algorithmic Improvements:** The paper goes beyond the core derivation to offer practical and well-motivated improvements. The connection drawn between the E-step and CCCP is insightful and leads directly to the `accelerateShrinkage` algorithm. The empirical validation in Figure 1 convincingly demonstrates that this is not just a theoretical curiosity but a technique that provides a significant reduction in computation time in practice. The identifiability analysis and subsequent `merge` post-processing step are also valuable, addressing a known issue in LFM training and providing a principled way to obtain more compact models.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare FAB/LFM against a strong and relevant set of baselines, including fast IBP sampling, variational IBP, and a recent MAP-IBP method (MEIBP). The use of both synthetic and diverse real-world datasets strengthens the conclusions. The results consistently support the paper's claims: FAB/LFM generally achieves superior or competitive predictive performance, finds more parsimonious models (smaller K), and is computationally much more efficient than the NPB alternatives, especially on larger datasets. The inclusion of an ablation study against a standard variational EM highlights the effectiveness of the FAB-specific regularization term.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The motivation is established upfront, the theoretical development is presented logically, and the connections between theory, the algorithm, and the refinements are easy to follow. The algorithm itself is presented clearly, and the experimental setup and results are well-documented.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Intuition for the Core Result (Lemma 1):** While Lemma 1 is the cornerstone of the paper, the main text provides little intuition for *why* this result holds. The proof is deferred to the supplementary materials, which is standard, but the paper would be stronger if it could briefly explain why the log-determinant of the Hessian for shared parameters `θd` decomposes into a sum over features `k`. This would help the reader build a more conceptual understanding of the paper's main technical leap.

2.  **Limitations of the Asymptotic Framework:** The method is, by definition, based on a large-sample (N → ∞) approximation. The authors fairly acknowledge in the conclusion of the experiments that this approximation can be inaccurate in small-N scenarios, leading to worse performance than NPB methods (as seen on the Libras dataset). This discussion could be more prominent and detailed. It would be beneficial to provide more guidance or characterization of the regimes (e.g., in terms of N, D, and the true K) where the FAB approximation is reliable versus where an NPB approach might be preferred.

3.  **Hyperparameter for Acceleration:** The proposed `accelerateShrinkage` routine introduces a new hyperparameter, `T_shrink`, the number of inner-loop iterations. The authors set this to 100 in the experiments but provide no discussion on its sensitivity. How was this value chosen? Does performance degrade gracefully if `T_shrink` is smaller, or is there a critical threshold? A brief sensitivity analysis or at least a discussion of this parameter would improve the practical utility of the proposed algorithm.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   In Eq. (8), the term `|P|` is used to denote the dimensionality of the parameter set but is not explicitly defined. It appears to be `dim(W) + dim(b) + dim(λ) + dim(π) = DK + D + D + K`. This term is then replaced by `2D + K` in the lower bound `L` (Eq. 10), which seems to imply that `DK` is handled by the `log N` term. Clarifying this transition would be helpful for readers tracking the derivation of the objective function.
    *   The "mild assumptions" for Lemma 1 are mentioned but not stated in the main text. Briefly listing the most critical assumptions would help the reader assess the scope of the result without needing to immediately consult the supplement.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   A novel and technically sound extension of an interesting inference framework (FAB) to a new, important model class (LFMs).
*   Strong theoretical results (FIC derivation, identifiability analysis) are complemented by practical algorithmic improvements (CCCP-based acceleration).
*   Comprehensive experiments demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art NPB methods in terms of the trade-off between model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational cost.
*   The work is well-presented, complete, and likely to be of significant interest to researchers working on Bayesian inference, model selection, and latent variable models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The reliance on an asymptotic approximation has limitations that, while acknowledged, could be discussed in more depth.
*   The central technical result (Lemma 1) could be motivated more intuitively in the main text.

Overall, this is a very strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed with small revisions. The proposed FAB/LFM method appears to be a powerful and practical tool for automated model selection in latent feature models. I recommend acceptance.
