This paper presents a significant extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for model selection and parameter estimation that maximizes a lower bound on an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood, known as the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC). Previous work on FAB was limited to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal, such as mixture models. The primary challenge in applying FAB to LFMs is that parameters are shared across features, violating this block-diagonal assumption.

The core theoretical contribution of this paper is Lemma 1, which shows that despite the complex parameter sharing in LFMs, the log determinant of the relevant Hessian matrix asymptotically decomposes into a sum of terms of the form `log(sum_n z_nk)`. This crucial result allows the authors to derive an FIC for LFMs that has the same functional form as the one for mixture models. This insight enables the development of an alternating optimization algorithm (FAB/LFM) that enjoys desirable properties like a monotonic increase in its objective function and automatic selection of the number of latent features, K. The paper further provides two key refinements: 1) a "shrinkage acceleration" method derived from a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation of the E-step, which drastically speeds up convergence by pruning irrelevant features early, and 2) a theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) that motivates a post-processing step to merge redundant features. The authors conduct experiments on synthetic and real-world data, comparing FAB/LFM against variational EM, Indian Buffet Process (IBP) Gibbs sampling, Variational Bayes for IBP, and the MAP-based MEIBP, demonstrating competitive or superior performance in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a highly original and significant contribution by extending the FAB framework to LFMs. This is not a straightforward application of an existing technique; it requires the novel theoretical insight of Lemma 1 to handle the non-block-diagonal Hessian. This work substantially broadens the applicability of FAB, a promising inference paradigm that offers an alternative to traditional non-parametric Bayesian and variational methods. Given the prevalence of LFMs in machine learning, a new, efficient, and theoretically-grounded inference method for them is an important result.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound. The derivation of the LFM's FIC is the paper's centerpiece and appears correct (pending verification of proofs in the supplement). The subsequent analysis is also strong: the connection to CCCP is insightful and leads to a practical and effective acceleration technique. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) adds theoretical depth and provides a principled justification for the merge post-processing step. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against a strong set of relevant baselines on multiple datasets and metrics (model selection, predictive log-likelihood, and training time). The authors are also honest about the limitations, for instance, noting the potential suboptimal performance in small-N regimes.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, explaining the limitations of prior FAB work and the challenge posed by LFMs. The logical flow from the key lemma to the FIC derivation, the algorithm, and the refinements is easy to follow. The connection between the `log(sum_n z_nk)` term and the automatic model shrinkage is explained intuitively and effectively. Algorithm 1 and 2 provide a clear overview of the proposed procedure.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Supplementary Material:** As is common in conference papers, the rigorous proofs for the main theoretical results (Lemma 1, Theorem 2, Theorem 4) are deferred to the supplementary materials. This makes it difficult to fully assess the technical correctness from the main paper alone. Similarly, some algorithmic details (e.g., the M-step updates and the merge() procedure) are also in the supplement, slightly hindering full reproducibility from the 8-page text.

2.  **Experimental Details:** While the experiments are generally strong, a couple of minor points could weaken the comparisons. The authors note that for the baselines, they fixed the noise standard deviation, whereas FAB/LFM learns it. This could potentially give FAB/LFM an advantage. Additionally, they mention the hyperparameter for IBP (`alpha=3`) might lead to overestimation of K. While this is a known challenge with IBP, a more thorough sensitivity analysis or a more careful tuning for the baseline might have presented a stronger comparison.

3.  **Ambiguity in Notation:** There is a minor ambiguity in Equation (8) where `|P|` is used but not explicitly defined. It appears to be the dimensionality of the parameter set {W, b, λ, π}, which would be DK + D + D + K, but a precise definition would improve clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and non-trivial theoretical extension of the FAB inference framework to the important class of Latent Feature Models.
*   The work includes practical and well-motivated algorithmic enhancements (shrinkage acceleration via CCCP, feature merging via identifiability analysis) that demonstrably improve performance.
*   The proposed method achieves strong empirical results, often outperforming or being competitive with state-of-the-art methods like IBP variants in terms of model selection, predictive accuracy, and computational efficiency.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and makes a significant contribution that is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and inspire further research into asymptotic Bayesian methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The key theoretical claims are not proven within the main body of the paper, relying on the supplement for validation.
*   The experimental comparison, while extensive, has minor points (e.g., fixed noise for baselines) that could be construed as not being perfectly equitable.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It tackles a clear problem with a novel theoretical insight, develops a practical and efficient algorithm, and validates it with strong empirical evidence. This is a complete and high-quality piece of research that advances the state of the art in approximate Bayesian inference. I strongly recommend acceptance.
