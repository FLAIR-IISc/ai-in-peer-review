## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions required for standard FAB application, the Hessian of the log-likelihood exhibits a form amenable to FAB. This allows for automated model selection (dimensionality of latent features) without relying on priors or hand-tuned hyperparameters.  The authors further analyze the FAB/LFM algorithm, relating the E-step to a Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) and leveraging this connection to propose a shrinkage acceleration method, and proving parameter identifiability, providing a basis for a merging post-processing step.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Advancement:** The core result – extending FAB to LFMs by demonstrating the relevant Hessian structure – is a significant theoretical contribution. It broadens the applicability of FAB to a wider class of latent variable models.
* **Automated Model Selection:**  The method offers a fully automated approach to latent feature dimensionality selection, a challenging problem often requiring significant hyperparameter tuning in other methods.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration, leveraging the CCCP connection, promises substantial computational speedups, a critical factor for large-scale LFM applications.  Experimental results (Figure 1) support this claim.
* **Identifiability Analysis:** The theoretical analysis addressing parameter identifiability is valuable, providing insights into the behavior of the algorithm and justification for the merging post-processing step.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments, including synthetic data, a block data example, and real-world datasets, demonstrate competitive performance against existing methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) in terms of model selection and predictive accuracy. The consistent performance improvement and model compactness highlighted in the results are compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notation and Presentation:** While the paper is generally clear, the notation is dense, and the presentation could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the mathematical derivations. The jump between equations and explanations sometimes feels abrupt.
* **Limited Comparison Scope:**  While comparisons are made to several baselines, the choice of baselines seems somewhat selective. A comparison with more recent and advanced VB methods, or with scalable IBP implementations, could strengthen the claims of superiority.
* **Assumptions:** The paper relies on certain assumptions regarding the smoothness and structure of the log-likelihood, which are stated but not rigorously justified in all cases. A more detailed discussion of the impact of these assumptions would be helpful.
* **Merging Post-processing Detail:** While the concept of merging latent features is interesting, the description of the algorithm in the paper and supplementary materials is brief and lacks details on the merging criteria beyond the increase in the lower bound.

**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and demonstrates a solid understanding of Bayesian inference and latent variable models.  Claims are generally well-supported by analysis and experiments. It is a complete piece of work, offering both theoretical contributions and practical improvements. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations and potential challenges.
* **Clarity:** The paper is reasonably clear, but the density of notation and the occasional lack of intuitive explanation hinder full comprehension. Improved explanations and more illustrative examples would enhance clarity.
* **Originality:** The extension of FAB to LFMs is a novel contribution. While building on existing work, the authors present a unique approach and derive new theoretical insights.  The connection to CCCP and the resulting acceleration are also original. The related work section is adequate, but could be more expansive in surveying recent advancements in LFM inference.
* **Significance:** The results are significant. Automated model selection and computational efficiency are crucial for applying LFMs to large-scale datasets. The identifiability analysis and merging post-processing provide valuable theoretical insights and practical guidelines.  The paper has the potential to impact research in latent variable modeling and related fields.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound extension of a promising inference framework.
* Offers a compelling solution to automated model selection in LFMs.
* Demonstrates significant computational speedups.
* Strong empirical validation against relevant baselines.
* Addresses important theoretical concerns like parameter identifiability.

**Con Acceptance:**

* Notation and presentation could be improved for better clarity.
* Limited scope of baseline comparisons.
* Assumptions require further justification.
* Details of merging post-processing could be elaborated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The theoretical contributions are compelling, the proposed algorithm offers practical advantages, and the empirical results are promising.  While the clarity could be improved and the scope of comparisons broadened, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and comparison scope concerns.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Provide more intuitive explanations for the mathematical derivations, particularly around the Hessian analysis.
2.  Include a comparison against more recent and scalable IBP implementations, if feasible.
3.  Elaborate on the details of the merging post-processing algorithm and provide justification for the merging criteria.
