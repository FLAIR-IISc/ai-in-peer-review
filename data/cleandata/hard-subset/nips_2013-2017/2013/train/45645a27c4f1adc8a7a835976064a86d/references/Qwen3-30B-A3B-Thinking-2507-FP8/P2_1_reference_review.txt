

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously applicable only to mixture models (MMs), to latent feature models (LFMs). The key insight is that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for prior FAB applications, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same form as for MMs. The authors derive theoretical guarantees (including convergence to marginal log-likelihood), propose a CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration method, and demonstrate parameter identifiability with a merge post-processing step. Extensive experiments show FAB/LFM achieves better model selection accuracy than state-of-the-art methods with lower computational cost.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors rigorously prove Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, establishing that LFMs share the same FIC structure as MMs despite lacking the block-diagonal Hessian condition. The theoretical analysis is sound, with appropriate assumptions (detailed in supplementary materials) and clear connections to previous work. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering artificial simulations, synthetic block data, and real-world datasets with multiple metrics (computational time, model selection accuracy, predictive performance). The comparison with multiple baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) is fair and well-executed. The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) is particularly well-justified through the CCCP interpretation.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that FAB's FIC approximation may be less accurate for small N, which aligns with their observed results on the Libras dataset. The authors are careful to distinguish between the theoretical properties of FAB (asymptotic behavior) and practical performance.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, and the key contributions (FIC derivation, shrinkage acceleration, identifiability analysis) are clearly separated. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and actionable. The connection between FAB and CCCP is particularly well-explained, making a complex optimization technique accessible.

However, the paper could improve by providing more intuitive explanations for why the Hessian analysis works for LFMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal condition. The technical depth is appropriate for the NIPS audience, but some readers may struggle with the density of mathematical notation without more concrete examples to illustrate the key insights.

## Originality
This work makes significant original contributions. The extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and addresses an important gap in the literature (previous FAB methods were limited to MMs). The key insight that LFMs share the same FIC structure as MMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal condition is both surprising and theoretically important. The CCCP interpretation of FAB optimization and the resulting shrinkage acceleration method represent a novel practical contribution. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and merge post-processing step are also original and address a critical theoretical issue in latent feature models.

This work is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers on FAB (e.g., Fujimaki & Morinaga, 2014; Fujimaki & Hayashi, 2015) which were restricted to mixture models. It also advances beyond related work on IBP (Griffiths & Ghahramani, 2011) and MEIBP (Reed & Ghahramani, 2015) by providing a more efficient, automated model selection method.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Model selection for LFMs is a critical challenge, particularly for large-scale applications where computational efficiency is paramount. FAB/LFM offers a fully automated method that selects the number of latent features without hyperparameters, achieving better accuracy than state-of-the-art methods with lower computational cost. The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) significantly reduces computational time by eliminating irrelevant features early in the optimization process, making the approach practical for large datasets.

The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages: FAB consistently achieves better predictive performance (higher PLL) with lower computational cost across multiple datasets. The smaller differences between training and predictive likelihood (TLL and PLL) suggest FAB's regularization effectively mitigates overfitting. The theoretical insights about identifiability and parameter regularization provide deeper understanding of latent feature model behavior.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper could better highlight the specific scenarios where FAB/LFM would be preferred over existing methods (e.g., when computational resources are constrained or when automatic model selection is critical).
2. While the authors acknowledge the FIC's asymptotic nature, the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the practical threshold for N where the approximation becomes accurate.
3. The comparison with more recent methods for LFMs (e.g., recent scalable VB approaches) could be expanded.

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. The extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and well-justified, with both theoretical rigor and empirical validation. The shrinkage acceleration method is particularly valuable for practical applications. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Strengths:** Theoretical novelty in extending FAB to LFMs; practical computational improvements; comprehensive experimental validation; clear theoretical connections to existing work.

**Weaknesses:** Could better contextualize the practical N threshold for FIC accuracy; minor opportunity to expand comparison with very recent methods.

**Overall:** This paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It addresses an important problem with both theoretical depth and practical utility. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Final Recommendation:** Accept
