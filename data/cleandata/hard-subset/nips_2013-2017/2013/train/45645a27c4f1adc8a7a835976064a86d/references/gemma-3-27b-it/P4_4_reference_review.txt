The paper proposes a factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework extended to latent feature models (LFMs). It addresses the limitation of prior FAB methods requiring a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete log-likelihood, a condition not met by LFMs. The core contribution is demonstrating that despite this difference, the FICs for LFMs retain a similar form to those for mixture models, enabling the application of FAB. The authors also present an acceleration method based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and prove parameter identifiability, leading to a merging post-processing step for latent features.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented with reasonable detail, and the connection to existing work, particularly prior FAB methods and related techniques like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), is well-established. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is clearly described. The inclusion of a CCCP-based acceleration and identifiability analysis adds depth to the contribution. The experimental validation, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The authors diligently acknowledge limitations and related work.

Strengths:

* **Novel Extension:** Successfully extends FAB inference to LFMs, overcoming a significant limitation of previous work.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The derivation of FICs for LFMs and the accompanying proofs (detailed in the supplementary material) provide a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Practical Improvements:** The CCCP-based acceleration demonstrably improves computational efficiency.
* **Identifiability Analysis:** The identifiability result provides valuable theoretical insight and informs a practical merging post-processing step.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of FAB/LFMs in terms of both accuracy and computational cost.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with FAB inference.

Weaknesses:

* **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** While the paper states assumptions exist, the details are relegated to supplementary material. A more concise discussion of the key assumptions within the main body would be helpful.
* **Dependency on Supplementary Material:**  Critical details, such as proofs and some implementation specifics, are only in the supplementary material. While acceptable, it can hinder quick assessment.
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** While comparisons are made, a more detailed analysis of *why* FAB/LFMs outperform or underperform specific methods in certain scenarios would be valuable.  For instance, the conditions under which MEIBP might be preferred (despite requiring careful parameter tuning) are not explored.
* **Merging Heuristic:** The merging post-processing feels somewhat heuristic. A more principled justification or analysis of the distance threshold used for merging would strengthen this aspect.



Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a significant extension of FAB inference to a broader class of models (LFMs).
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach.
* The practical improvements (acceleration and identifiability-based merging) enhance the utility of FAB inference.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of FAB/LFMs.
* The work addresses a relevant and challenging problem in Bayesian model selection.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The reliance on supplementary material for key details can make it difficult to fully assess the contribution.
* The merging post-processing step lacks a strong theoretical justification.
* The discussion of the assumptions underlying the derivations is limited.



Overall Assessment:

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The extension of FAB to LFMs is a significant advancement, and the accompanying theoretical and practical improvements are well-motivated and effectively demonstrated. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically expanding the discussion of assumptions in the main text and providing a more detailed justification for the merging heuristic.
