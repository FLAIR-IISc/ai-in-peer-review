Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) framework from mixture/HMM settings to linear Gaussian latent feature models (LFMs) with Bernoulli features. The central theoretical claim (Lemma 1) is that, despite the lack of block-diagonality of the complete-data Hessian in LFMs, the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian −∇∇ log p(x·d|Z, θd) contributes, asymptotically, a model complexity term of the form (D/2)∑k log(∑n znk). This yields an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) that matches the well-known FIC for mixtures, implying automatic shrinkage of poorly supported features and asymptotic equivalence to the marginal log-likelihood up to Op(1) (Corollary 3). The authors then develop a practical FAB algorithm using (i) a mean-field variational q(Z), (ii) a linearization bound for the concave term −∑k (Dk/2) Eq[log ∑n znk], and (iii) alternating maximization over q, parameters, and linearization variables π̃. They interpret the E-step as a CCCP iteration and propose a shrinkage-acceleration routine that performs multiple CCCP steps to eliminate weak features early. Finally, they prove an identifiability property (Theorem 4) implying that equal columns w·k = w·l yield balanced feature usages and motivate a merge post-processing that increases the bound.

This work connects to: FAB for MMs/HMMs [5,6], IBP-based nonparametrics and its variants [10,2,3,22], singular learning theory (Watanabe) and asymptotic approximations, and CCCP [29]. It advances the claim that FIC-like penalties extend beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Showing that the FIC penalty form emerges in LFMs without block-diagonality is surprising and useful, broadening FAB’s applicability.
- Practical algorithm: A simple, closed-form mean-field E-step, M-step akin to PPCA, and a CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration that demonstrably reduces runtime.
- Automation: Automatic feature selection without priors or tuned hyperparameters is appealing in practice and distinct from IBP-based approaches.
- Identifiability insight: Theorem 4 clarifies how FAB prefers solutions resolving non-identifiability and motivates a principled merge operation.
- Empirical evidence: Across synthetic and several real datasets, FAB attains competitive or superior predictive log-likelihoods with compact K and reasonable runtimes; shrinkage acceleration materially helps convergence.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical conditions under-specified in main text: Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 hinge on “mild assumptions,” but these are deferred to the supplement. It is difficult to assess the scope of validity (e.g., bounds on πk away from 0/1, boundedness of moments, feature collinearity).
- Fairness and completeness of empirical comparisons: Baseline codes fix the noise variance whereas FAB learns it; MEIBP initializations and time caps may bias results; VB is omitted on real data for time; sensitivity analyses are limited.
- Scope: The model is linear Gaussian with diagonal noise; it is unclear how readily the theory or algorithm extends to non-Gaussian likelihoods or correlated feature priors.
- Complexity analysis: No explicit per-iteration computational/space complexity or scaling with N, D, K and Tshrink; guidance on choosing Tshrink and its overall effect is minimal.
- Clarity gaps: Some constants in the lower bound (e.g., “−2D + K log N” in Eq. (10)) appear without derivation; the merge routine is only sketched; the relationship between η(πk) prior-induced shrinkage and FAB regularization could be more quantitatively analyzed.

Quality
The main claims are plausible and well-motivated, and the algorithmic development is careful with a monotone lower bound. However, critical proofs and conditions are relegated to the supplement; bringing at least a proof sketch and explicit assumptions into the main text would strengthen confidence. Empirical comparisons are suggestive but leave room for concerns about fairness (noise variance handling, dataset-specific parameter choices, initialization of MEIBP). The CCCP-based acceleration is sensible and supported by experiments.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with a clear connection to prior FAB work. Some notation and constants could be explained more fully (Eq. (10)), and the implications and precise conditions of Lemma 1 deserve a main-text summary. Minor typos aside, the exposition of updates and algorithms is clear.

Originality
Moderately high. Extending FIC/FAB to LFMs without block-diagonal Hessians is nontrivial. The CCCP reinterpretation and shrinkage acceleration are neat and practically valuable. The identifiability perspective and merge are also interesting, though incremental.

Significance
If the assumptions hold broadly, this provides a practical, automated alternative to IBP/VB for LFMs with good scaling and model selection. The impact could be notable for practitioners needing compact feature representations without heavy hyperparameter tuning. Extensions to broader likelihoods would further increase significance.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Please state explicitly in the main text the assumptions behind Lemma 1 (e.g., bounds on πk, properties of W, Λ, and data moments), and provide a proof sketch to build intuition.
- Does accelerateShrinkage preserve monotonic ascent of the overall lower bound, or is monotonicity only guaranteed for complete FAB iterations? Empirically, how often does it cause any regressions?
- Provide per-iteration complexity and memory costs, and empirical scaling plots in N, D, K, with and without acceleration.
- How sensitive is FAB to initialization of {µn}, and to Tshrink? Include an ablation.
- Can the baselines be rerun with learned noise variance to ensure fairness, or alternatively lock FAB’s noise to the same value and compare?
- Does the identifiability result extend beyond exact equality w·k = w·l to near-duplicates? How is the merge threshold chosen and how sensitive are results to it?

Suggestions for improvement
- Bring key assumptions and a proof sketch of Lemma 1/Theorem 2 into the main paper.
- Add a complexity section and practical guidance on Tshrink and stopping.
- Strengthen empirical fairness: match noise variance treatment across methods, add sensitivity to MEIBP initialization, and include at least one stronger VB/IBP baseline on a medium-scale dataset.
- Report additional metrics (e.g., feature recovery precision/recall on synthetic) and robustness to noise levels and Ktrue.
- Discuss applicability to non-Gaussian likelihoods and structured priors; outline what parts of the theory carry over.

Arguments for acceptance
- Extends FAB theory to an important model class with a surprising and elegant result (FIC form retained).
- Practical algorithm with clear benefits (automatic K selection, acceleration).
- Useful identifiability insight and post-processing.
- Solid empirical performance.

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on supplemental proofs/assumptions limits verifiability from the main text.
- Empirical comparisons have fairness caveats; some strong baselines are omitted on real data.
- Scope limited to linear Gaussian LFMs; generality of the theory not yet demonstrated.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical insight and practical algorithm are valuable, but the paper would benefit from clearer exposition of assumptions and stronger, fairer empirical validation.
