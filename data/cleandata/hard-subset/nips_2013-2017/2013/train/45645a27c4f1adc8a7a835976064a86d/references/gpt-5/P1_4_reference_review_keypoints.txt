- Extends FAB inference to linear-Gaussian LFMs and derives Lemma 1 showing log-det Hessian term behaves as sum_k log sum_n z_nk, yielding an FIC matching the MM case.  
- Proposes an algorithm that optimizes a mean-field lower bound with a closed-form M-step, monotone guarantees, a CCCP interpretation of the E-step, and a shrinkage-acceleration routine.  
- Proves an identifiability result and proposes a merge post-processing when columns of W coincide.  
- Empirical evaluation on synthetic, block-structure, and real datasets shows faster convergence than VB, competitive/better model selection than IBP/MEIBP, and strong predictive log-likelihood with compact K.  
- Situates work relative to FAB for mixtures/HMMs, IBP-based nonparametrics (Gibbs/VB/MEIBP), MAD-Bayes, and CCCP literature.  
- Theoretical novelty: deriving an FIC for LFMs despite lack of block-diagonal Hessian is nontrivial and important.  
- Algorithmic strength: clean design, closed-form updates, CCCP-based E-step, and effective acceleration that reduces runtime.  
- Identifiability analysis is useful and motivates the merge heuristic.  
- Broad empirical coverage demonstrating reduced overfitting (smaller PLL–TLL gaps) and compact models.  
- Major concern: Lemma 1 is relegated to supplement with only “mild assumptions”; the main text needs precise assumptions and a proof sketch.  
- Unclear robustness of Lemma 1 beyond diagonal Λ or under correlated/collinear features.  
- Mean-field factorization q(zn)=∏k q̃(znk) breaks the exact FIC interpretation; impact on model selection consistency is not analyzed.  
- Baseline comparisons may be unfair because FAB/EM learn Λ while IBP/VB/MEIBP often have fixed noise; this could advantage FAB in PLL.  
- MEIBP omitted on negative-valued datasets and required manual K on some datasets, raising comparison concerns.  
- Request to show sensitivity of FAB to Λ estimation and provide baselines that also learn noise (or ablate FAB to fixed Λ).  
- accelerateShrinkage uses many inner iterations (Tshrink=100) without complexity analysis; need per-iteration cost and scaling w.r.t. N, D, K.  
- Merge heuristic relies on Euclidean distance thresholds for near-duplicates; needs guidance for threshold choice and guarantees that merging increases the bound.  
- Scope limitation: method targets linear-Gaussian LFMs with diagonal Λ; extension to non-Gaussian likelihoods or non-diagonal Λ is unclear.  
- Overall quality: technically sound, monotonicity and CCCP equivalence convincing, but main theoretical claims under-detailed in main text.  
- Clarity: generally clear and organized, but must state assumptions for Lemma 1/Theorem 2 and add a proof sketch in the main paper.  
- Implementation clarity: provide computational complexity and implementation details for accelerateShrinkage and merge (thresholds, stopping conditions).  
- Clarify the role of π estimation (MLE) and reconcile with claims that the method “asymptotically ignores priors.”  
- Originality: high — showing FIC for LFMs matches MM case without block-diagonal Hessian is novel; CCCP interpretation and identifiability-guided merging also novel.  
- Significance: meaningful advance for automated model selection in LFMs and potentially broader impact if theoretical claims generalize.  
- Concrete questions for authors: specify Lemma 1 assumptions and sketch proof; sensitivity to rare features; provide experiments where baselines learn noise; report time/space complexity of acceleration; comment on non-Gaussian likelihoods.  
- Pros summary: elegant theory, automatic K selection, monotone bound optimization, practical acceleration, and strong empirical results.  
- Cons summary: key lemma assumptions/proof not in main text, some comparisons may favor FAB, limited discussion of non-diagonal/non-Gaussian cases and algorithmic complexity.  
- Recommendation: lean accept conditioned on clarifying Lemma 1 assumptions/proof sketch and improving experimental fairness and complexity reporting.  
- Reviewer confidence: high on algorithmic/empirical assessment; medium on full generality of the asymptotic theory.
