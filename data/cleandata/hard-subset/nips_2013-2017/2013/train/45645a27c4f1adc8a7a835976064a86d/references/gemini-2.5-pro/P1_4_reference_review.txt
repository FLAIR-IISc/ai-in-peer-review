### Summary of the paper

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for approximate Bayesian model selection that maximizes a lower bound on the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which converges to the marginal log-likelihood in the large sample limit. A key advantage of FAB is its ability to automatically select model complexity (e.g., the number of mixture components) without relying on priors or user-tuned hyperparameters.

The central challenge addressed is that previous applications of FAB, such as to Mixture Models (MMs), relied on the assumption that the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. LFMs violate this assumption because model parameters are shared across all latent features. The paper's main theoretical contribution (Lemma 1) is to show, through an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian, that the resulting FIC for LFMs surprisingly takes the same functional form as for MMs. This form includes the critical `log(sum_n z_nk)` complexity penalty term that drives the automatic model selection.

Building on this theoretical foundation, the paper derives a practical FAB/LFM algorithm based on optimizing a tractable lower bound of the FIC using a mean-field approximation. The authors make two further contributions:
1.  They interpret the E-step update as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), which inspires a "shrinkage acceleration" method to more rapidly prune irrelevant features and reduce computation time.
2.  They analyze the parameter identifiability of LFMs under the FAB objective, showing that FAB avoids the non-identifiability issues of maximum likelihood estimation and provides a principled basis for a post-processing step to merge redundant features.

The proposed FAB/LFM is compared experimentally against several state-of-the-art methods for LFM model selection, including Indian Buffet Process (IBP) samplers, variational Bayes for IBP (VB), and a MAP-based approach (MEIBP). The results on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that FAB/LFM achieves superior or competitive model selection accuracy and predictive performance, often with significantly lower computational cost and resulting in more compact models.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant and Novel Extension of the FAB Framework (Originality & Significance):** The paper successfully extends the applicability of the promising FAB framework to a broad and important class of models (LFMs) that do not fit the framework's original structural assumptions. The core theoretical insight—that the critical FIC penalty term emerges even without a block-diagonal Hessian—is non-obvious and constitutes a strong technical contribution. This opens the door for applying FAB to other complex, shared-parameter latent variable models.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding and Analysis (Quality):** The paper is well-supported by theoretical analysis. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is the centerpiece, but the additional analyses are also valuable. The CCCP interpretation provides a deeper understanding of the optimization dynamics and leads directly to a practical algorithmic improvement. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) is also important, as it explains why FAB is better behaved than standard EM for this model class and justifies the proposed merging procedure.

3.  **Practical and Efficient Algorithm (Significance):** The paper delivers not just a theoretical result but a complete and practical algorithm. The proposed shrinkage acceleration is shown to be highly effective, drastically reducing computation time. The final method is demonstrated to be significantly faster than competing nonparametric Bayesian methods like IBP Gibbs sampling and VB, making it applicable to larger datasets.

4.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments (Quality):** The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare against a well-chosen set of strong baselines on both synthetic and real-world data. The results consistently highlight the strengths of FAB/LFM: accurate estimation of the true number of features, strong predictive performance (high PLL), and a notable resistance to overfitting (small TLL-PLL gap). The experiments clearly demonstrate that the proposed method advances the state of the art.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Intuition for the Main Theoretical Result (Clarity):** The paper's central claim rests on Lemma 1, but the proof is deferred entirely to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice, the main text would be significantly strengthened by providing a short proof sketch or at least some high-level intuition for *why* the determinant of the non-block-diagonal Hessian `F(d)` factorizes into the `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)` form. This result is surprising and is the foundation for the entire paper, so helping the reader understand its origin would be very valuable.

2.  **Limitation to Diagonal Noise Covariance (Quality):** The LFM formulation assumes a diagonal precision matrix `Lambda`. This simplifies the derivations but limits the model's ability to capture correlations in the noise. It is unclear how the Hessian analysis and the derivation of the FIC would be affected by a full covariance matrix. A brief discussion of the potential challenges or directions for this extension would be welcome.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of the Merging Procedure (Clarity):** While the identifiability analysis provides a strong justification for merging features, the practical implementation described feels somewhat heuristic. The paper states that it searches for overlapping features based on Euclidean distance and merges them if the lower bound increases. More specific details on the search strategy (e.g., threshold for distance, how many pairs are considered) would make this part of the algorithm more reproducible.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian approximate inference and model selection. It elegantly extends the FAB framework to a wider class of models, provides novel theoretical insights (CCCP interpretation, identifiability), and delivers a practical, efficient algorithm with compelling empirical results. The work is well-executed, clearly written, and advances the state of the art in automated model selection for latent feature models. The weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to areas where clarity could be improved; they do not detract from the core value of the contributions. This paper would be an excellent addition to the NIPS program.
