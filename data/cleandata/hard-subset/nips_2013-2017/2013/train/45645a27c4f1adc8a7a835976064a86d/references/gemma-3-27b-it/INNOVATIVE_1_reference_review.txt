This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), a setting where previous work was limited by restrictive model constraints. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that despite these constraints not holding for LFMs, the underlying structure of the Fisher Information Criterion (FIC) remains remarkably similar to that of Mixture Models, allowing FAB to be successfully applied. The authors further bolster their work with insightful analysis connecting FAB E-steps to a Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP), leading to practical acceleration techniques, and demonstrate parameter identifiability, offering guidance for model merging.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The extension of FAB to LFMs is a non-trivial and conceptually important step. The theoretical analysis establishing the FIC form for LFMs, despite lacking the block diagonal Hessian condition, is particularly impressive. The connection to CCCP provides a clear path for optimization improvement – a practically valuable contribution – and the identifiability results are crucial for understanding and interpreting the learned models. The empirical evaluation, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, supports the claims, and the ablation study (acceleration) is well-executed. The presentation is generally clear, and the authors appropriately relate their work to existing literature in Bayesian nonparametrics and variational inference. The demonstration of automatic feature selection and the avoidance of hyperparameter tuning – hallmarks of the FAB approach – are compelling.

However, there are some areas that could benefit from further clarification or elaboration. While the experimental results are promising, the comparisons with state-of-the-art methods aren't consistently decisive. The performance gains of the shrinkage acceleration are clearly demonstrated, but the overall runtime remains a potential barrier for extremely large datasets, and a more detailed comparison of scalability with methods like MEIBP would be valuable. The discussion of identifiability and the merging post-processing, while insightful, feels somewhat disconnected from the main flow of the paper. A more concrete example of the merging procedure in action, perhaps visualized, would be beneficial. The reliance on fixing the noise standard deviation for some comparisons seems a bit ad-hoc; an attempt to learn this parameter within the FAB framework or a more nuanced discussion of its impact would be appreciated. The supplementary material is referenced heavily, which is not inherently negative, but ensuring key theoretical details are accessible within the main body of the paper could improve readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of FAB inference to LFMs is a genuinely novel contribution, opening up the FAB framework to a wider class of models.
* **Theoretical Insight:** The analysis demonstrating the FIC equivalence despite the relaxed Hessian condition is a significant theoretical result.
* **Practical Improvement:** The CCCP-inspired acceleration technique demonstrably improves computational efficiency.
* **Automated Model Selection:** The automatic feature selection capability is a valuable advantage.
* **Potential Impact:**  The work has the potential to influence future research in automated Bayesian model selection.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Inconclusive Comparisons:** While the results are generally positive, the comparisons with SOTA methods aren’t overwhelmingly convincing across all datasets.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:**  The scalability of FAB to very large datasets remains somewhat unclear.
* **Identifiability/Merging Integration:** The connection between identifiability, merging, and the core FAB algorithm could be more tightly integrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and technically sound contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. While some aspects could be further refined, the core ideas are compelling, the analysis is rigorous, and the potential impact is significant. I highly value the boldness of extending the FAB framework to a more complex model and the insightful theoretical work that underpins this extension. The authors have successfully demonstrated that FAB is not merely a method applicable to a narrow class of models, but a more general principle for automated Bayesian inference. 

**Recommendation:** Accept. While not perfect, the novelty and potential of this work outweigh its minor weaknesses. I am confident that this paper will stimulate further research in this area.
