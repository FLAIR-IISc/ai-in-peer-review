

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper proposes extending Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously developed for mixture models, to latent feature models (LFMs). The authors claim that FAB/LFMs offer automatic model selection (determining the number of latent features), computational efficiency, and theoretical guarantees including convergence to marginal log-likelihood. The key theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which establishes that LFMs, despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for the original FAB framework, yield the same FIC form as mixture models.

The paper makes several strong claims: FAB/LFMs achieve better model selection accuracy than state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian (NPB) methods with less computational cost, automatically select the number of latent features without hyperparameters, and provide parameter identifiability. The authors support these claims with theoretical analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) and limited experimental results on artificial and small-scale real datasets.

**Quality Assessment**

The theoretical foundations appear sound, with reasonable assumptions in the supplementary materials. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is mathematically consistent, though the paper lacks rigorous justification for the asymptotic assumptions made in Lemma 1 and Theorem 2. The most significant quality issue is the insufficient empirical validation. The paper compares FAB/LFMs only with IBP, VB, and MEIBP on small datasets (N ≤ 2,000), omitting the most recent state-of-the-art methods for LFMs. The computational cost claims are unsubstantiated for large-scale scenarios, which is particularly concerning given the paper's emphasis on computational efficiency. The paper also fails to demonstrate how FAB/LFMs handle the most challenging aspects of LFMs, such as non-identifiability in practice.

**Clarity Assessment**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with logical flow from theoretical development to algorithm design to experimental validation. The mathematical derivations are presented carefully, and the supplementary materials provide additional theoretical rigor. However, the paper does not sufficiently clarify how FAB/LFMs differ from existing variational approaches for LFMs, which creates confusion about the actual contribution. The comparison with related work (Section 1.1) is inadequate, particularly regarding the relationship between FAB and established variational methods.

**Originality Assessment**

The main theoretical contribution is extending FAB to LFMs, but this appears to be a relatively straightforward application of the existing framework rather than a significant conceptual advance. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the extension to LFMs is non-trivial or that the resulting method offers meaningful advantages over existing approaches. The shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) is presented as a key innovation, but it appears to be a minor technical refinement rather than a substantial new contribution. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from recent work on latent feature models, such as the variational inference methods for LFMs presented by Doshi-Velez et al. (2009) or Miller et al. (2010).

**Significance Assessment**

The significance of the work is questionable given the limited empirical validation. While the paper claims "superiority" over existing methods, the experiments are confined to small artificial datasets and a few small real-world datasets. The paper does not demonstrate how FAB/LFMs would perform on large-scale applications, which is where computational efficiency claims would be most valuable. The claim that FAB "achieves as good or even better model selection accuracy" is not supported by comprehensive comparisons with the most recent baselines. The paper's contribution appears to be marginal rather than transformative for the field.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

*Strengths:*
- The theoretical derivation of FIC for LFMs is mathematically sound
- The paper provides a clear algorithm for FAB/LFMs
- The shrinkage acceleration technique shows promise in the limited experiments presented
- The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) offers useful theoretical insights

*Weaknesses:*
- Inadequate empirical validation against established baselines
- Limited scope of experiments (small datasets only)
- Insufficient comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods for LFMs
- Overstated claims about performance and efficiency without sufficient evidence
- Failure to address how FAB/LFMs handle practical challenges like non-identifiability in real-world data
- Lack of scalability analysis for large N, D, or K

**Recommendation**

This paper makes a modest theoretical contribution to extending the FAB framework, but the claims of superiority over existing methods are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence. The paper fails to meet NIPS' high standards for empirical validation, particularly for a method that claims to offer computational advantages. The theoretical extension appears to be a straightforward application rather than a significant innovation, and the experimental validation is too limited to justify the paper's claims.

I recommend rejection. To strengthen this work, the authors would need to conduct comprehensive experiments on large-scale datasets, compare with the most recent state-of-the-art methods for LFMs, and provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach. The paper's current empirical evidence is insufficient to support the claims of "superiority" over existing methods, which is a critical requirement for acceptance at NIPS.
