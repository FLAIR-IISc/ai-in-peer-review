**Summary**

This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a framework for automated model selection that approximates the marginal log-likelihood without explicit priors. The key challenge is that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian assumption required by previous FAB applications like Mixture Models (MMs). The authors' main theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which shows that the determinant of the Hessian for LFMs decomposes in a way that yields a penalty term (`log sum(z_nk)`) analogous to that for MMs. This leads to the Factorized Information Criterion for LFMs (FIC-LFM). Based on this, they derive an alternating optimization algorithm (FAB/LFM). The paper further provides two analyses: an interpretation of the E-step as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), which inspires a shrinkage acceleration technique, and a theorem on parameter identifiability that motivates a feature-merging post-processing step. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data suggest FAB/LFM is competitive with or superior to non-parametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) in terms of model selection accuracy and computational cost.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important problem of model selection for LFMs. Extending the FAB framework beyond its original, more restrictive assumptions is a novel and valuable contribution. It presents a compelling alternative to computationally expensive non-parametric Bayesian methods.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation showing that the FIC for LFMs takes a similar form to that of MMs, despite the shared parameters, is non-trivial. Lemma 1 is the cornerstone of this work and appears to be a solid technical result.
3.  **Practical Enhancements:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, motivated by a CCCP analysis, is a clever practical improvement. The analysis of parameter identifiability and the resulting merge step also demonstrate a thoughtful approach to the practical challenges of fitting LFMs.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws in clarity, technical rigor, and presentation that undermine its overall quality.

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Core Derivations:**
    *   **Equation (1):** This equation is confusingly written. It presents two separate definitions for `p(X,Z|M)` within the same line, one as part of a variational lower bound on `log p(X|M)` and the other as the marginal complete likelihood. This lack of precision at the outset is concerning.
    *   **Derivation of FIC-LFM:** The derivation is opaque. Key results like Lemma 1 are stated without any proof sketch or intuition, with the reader referred to the supplement. For the central result of the paper, this is insufficient. The jump from Lemma 1 to Theorem 2 and Equation (8) is abrupt. The term `|P|` in Eq. (8) is not defined, and its connection to the final lower bound L in Eq. (10) is not explained.
    *   **Derivation of Lower Bound L (Eq. 10):** The final objective function `L` seems to appear without a clear step-by-step derivation from the FIC in Eq. (9). The penalty term `-(2D+K)/2 logN` is not justified from the preceding equations. A clear derivation tracing how the parameter counts for W, b, λ, and π contribute to the final objective is missing, which is a major methodological gap.

2.  **Organizational and Writing Issues:**
    *   The paper's structure is suboptimal. Algorithm 1 refers to sub-routines `accelerateShrinkage()` and `merge()` which are only explained later in Section 3, forcing the reader to jump back and forth.
    *   The writing contains grammatical errors ("Further we conduct two analysis", "does not affect to the approximation") and awkward phrasing ("in terms of that LFMs approximate X by...").
    *   Footnotes are used to dismiss important experimental details (e.g., Footnote 3: "We totally omitted VB because of its long computational time," which sounds unprofessional) or provide vague statements (Footnote 2: "none was observed in this small example").
    *   The notation is sometimes non-standard (e.g., `X̄ = X − 1b>`). Footnote 1 contains a likely typo ("P (X,Z|P) is a regular model" should probably be `p(X,Z|P)`).

3.  **Overstated Claims and Lack of Rigor:**
    *   **Identifiability (Theorem 4):** The theorem shows that if `w*·k = w*·l`, then the feature usage rates must be equal at a stationary point. The paper claims this "resolves such non-identifiability," which is an overstatement. It characterizes the nature of the stationary points under non-identifiability but does not eliminate the underlying issue that multiple parameter settings can yield the same likelihood. The argument relies on the regularizer's preference for smaller K, which is intuitive but not rigorously proven by the theorem itself.
    *   **CCCP Interpretation:** While interesting, the connection is presented loosely. The procedure in Algorithm 2 seems more like a coordinate ascent with multiple inner steps rather than a standard CCCP implementation, which would typically involve solving a fully concave (or convex) subproblem after linearization. The explanation could be more precise.

4.  **Experimental Concerns:**
    *   **Fairness of Comparison:** The experimental setup has several confounding factors. Baselines had their noise variance fixed, while the proposed method learned it. The IBP hyperparameter `α` was fixed to a value the authors admit "might cause overestimation of K." These choices may unfairly favor the proposed method.
    *   **Handling of Baselines:** MEIBP was omitted from one experiment because its parameters were "very sensitive," which is a weak justification for excluding a key competitor. For other experiments, its initial K was reduced to meet a time limit, which is not a fair comparison. These issues cast doubt on the reliability of the empirical conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful extension of the FAB framework to a wider and more complex class of models (LFMs).
*   The core technical idea (Lemma 1) appears sound and enables a principled, non-Bayesian approach to model selection in LFMs.
*   The empirical results, despite some concerns, are broadly positive and suggest the method is a promising alternative to existing techniques.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is marred by a lack of clarity in its core technical derivations, making it difficult to verify the method's correctness without significant effort and trust in the supplementary material.
*   The writing and organization are below the standard expected for NIPS, with numerous minor errors and structural issues that impede understanding.
*   Claims about resolving non-identifiability are overstated.
*   The experimental evaluation contains several choices that undermine the fairness of the comparison and weaken the empirical claims.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a valuable and original research direction, the execution is not yet at the level required for publication at NIPS. The numerous issues in clarity, rigor, organization, and experimental design collectively constitute a significant set of flaws. The paper requires a major revision to clarify the theoretical derivations, improve the overall presentation, and conduct a more rigorous and fair empirical evaluation.
