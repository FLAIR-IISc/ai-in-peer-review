## Peer Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), a challenging problem due to the lack of block-diagonal Hessian structure present in simpler models like Mixture Models. The authors convincingly demonstrate that the core FIC framework can be adapted to LFMs, retaining desirable properties like automatic model selection and asymptotic marginal likelihood convergence. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the inclusion of a shrinkage acceleration method and identifiability analysis further strengthens the contribution. While the experimental section is reasonably thorough, it could benefit from more detailed ablation studies and a deeper comparison to recent, highly optimized IBP methods. Despite these minor concerns, I believe this paper represents a solid and valuable contribution to the field, and I **recommend acceptance**.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of model selection in Latent Feature Models (LFMs), specifically determining the optimal dimensionality of the latent feature space.  FAB inference, previously successful in Mixture Models (MMs), offers a computationally efficient Bayesian approximation that avoids explicit priors and hyperparameter tuning.  The key challenge is that LFMs don’t naturally satisfy the Hessian block-diagonal condition required for the original FAB framework. The authors overcome this by demonstrating that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs *effectively* behaves as if it does, allowing for a seamless extension of FAB. They further enhance the practical utility of FAB/LFMs by proposing a shrinkage acceleration method based on a connection to the Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) and proving parameter identifiability, leading to a natural feature merging post-processing step. 

This work builds directly upon the foundations laid by Fujimaki et al. (2016, 2017) for FAB inference in MMs. It connects to the broader field of Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) methods for LFMs, such as those based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) (Griffiths & Ghahramani, 2010), and recent MAP estimation approaches like MEIBP (Reed & Ghahramani, 2012).  Crucially, the authors position FAB as a competitive alternative, particularly for large-scale datasets where BNP methods struggle with computational cost. They also differentiate their approach from "MAD-Bayes" (Broderick et al., 2014) by focusing on asymptotic behavior without relying on limiting noise variance assumptions.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's central contribution – demonstrating the applicability of FAB to LFMs despite the lack of a block-diagonal Hessian – is supported by a solid mathematical argument (Lemma 1 & Theorem 2).
* **Practical Enhancements:**  The proposed shrinkage acceleration significantly improves computational efficiency, making FAB more viable for large datasets.  The identifiability analysis and associated feature merging step provide a valuable refinement to the algorithm.
* **Automated Model Selection:** The absence of hyperparameters and priors is a significant advantage, simplifying the application of FAB compared to many other Bayesian methods.
* **Strong Comparison to Existing Methods:** The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance against established BNP and VB approaches, highlighting FAB’s strengths in terms of both accuracy and computational speed.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the theoretical concepts and algorithmic steps.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Depth:** While the experiments are comprehensive, the ablation studies could be more detailed. Specifically, quantifying the speedup gained by the shrinkage acceleration method in different scenarios would be beneficial.  
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art IBPs:** The comparison to IBP methods could be more rigorous. The authors mention using fast Gibbs sampling, but don’t fully detail the specific implementation or parameter settings. More recent, highly optimized IBP implementations (beyond those cited) should be considered for a more thorough benchmark.
* **Limited Real-World Data:** While a variety of datasets are used, the experiments could benefit from a more in-depth analysis on a smaller number of carefully selected, highly relevant real-world datasets.
* **Assumptions:** The paper relies on certain assumptions (detailed in the supplementary materials) regarding the Hessian matrix.  A clearer discussion of the practical implications of these assumptions and their potential limitations would be helpful.




**4. Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The technical contributions are sound and well-supported. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results validate the claims. The paper feels complete, although the experiments could benefit from greater depth.
* **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The mathematical notation is standard, and the explanations are clear.  However, some sections (particularly the CCCP interpretation) could be made more accessible to readers unfamiliar with that technique.
* **Originality (8/10):** Extending FAB to LFMs is a novel and significant contribution. The shrinkage acceleration and identifiability analysis further enhance the originality of the work.
* **Significance (9/10):** The work addresses a challenging problem in Bayesian machine learning and offers a practical solution with potential for wide applicability. The automated model selection and computational efficiency of FAB/LFMs are particularly compelling.  

**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous mathematical analysis.
* Novel extension of FAB to a challenging problem (LFMs).
* Practical improvements (shrinkage acceleration, identifiability) that enhance the usability of the method.
* Competitive performance against existing methods.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Experimental depth could be improved with more detailed ablation studies.
* Comparison to state-of-the-art IBP methods could be more thorough.
* Some reliance on assumptions that could be further discussed.




**6. Final Recommendation:**

**Accept**.  I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian machine learning. The theoretical and practical advancements presented in this work are significant, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized. While some minor improvements could be made, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance to NIPS.
