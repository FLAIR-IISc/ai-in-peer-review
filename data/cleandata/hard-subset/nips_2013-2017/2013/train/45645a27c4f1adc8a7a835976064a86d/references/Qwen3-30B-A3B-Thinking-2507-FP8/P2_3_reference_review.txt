

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, previously applicable only to models with a block diagonal Hessian matrix (e.g., mixture models), to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The authors prove that despite LFMs not satisfying the block diagonal condition, they still yield the same Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) form as mixture models, enabling the application of FAB inference. They develop an FAB/LFM algorithm with a novel shrinkage acceleration technique based on Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation, and prove parameter identifiability for LFMs. Experimental results demonstrate that FAB/LFM achieves better model selection accuracy with significantly lower computational cost than state-of-the-art methods like IBP, VB, and MEIBP.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs (referenced to supplementary materials) extending FAB inference to LFMs. The key insight—that LFMs yield the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying the block diagonal condition—is well-supported by Lemma 1 and Theorem 2. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical foundation, including identifiability analysis and a CCCP interpretation that leads to a practical acceleration technique.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing FAB/LFM with multiple state-of-the-art methods across synthetic, block, and real-world datasets. The results consistently show that FAB/LFM achieves superior model selection accuracy with lower computational cost. The paper appropriately addresses the trade-offs between model complexity and accuracy, and provides detailed analysis of the shrinkage effect and convergence behavior.

A minor limitation is the limited discussion of the asymptotic assumptions' validity for smaller datasets. While the authors note FIC approximation may be less accurate for small N in the experiments, a more detailed theoretical discussion of the regime where the asymptotic approximation holds would strengthen the paper.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from theoretical foundations to algorithm development and experimental validation. Mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The key contributions—FIC derivation, FAB algorithm, CCCP interpretation, and identifiability analysis—are clearly articulated.

The paper's technical depth is appropriate for NIPS, though some readers may find the dense mathematical notation challenging without prior knowledge of FAB inference. The supplementary materials appear to contain the necessary detailed proofs, which is acceptable for a conference paper. The experimental setup and results are clearly described, with appropriate comparisons to baselines.

## Originality
This paper makes several significant novel contributions. First, it extends the theoretical foundation of FAB inference to LFMs, demonstrating that they share the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying the block diagonal Hessian condition. Second, it provides a CCCP interpretation of the FAB optimization, leading to a novel shrinkage acceleration technique. Third, it proves parameter identifiability for LFMs and proposes a merge post-processing technique.

The paper properly cites related work, including the original FAB work [5,6], latent feature models [10,17], and related Bayesian inference methods [1,3,29]. It clearly distinguishes its contributions from previous work, particularly noting that while previous methods like IBP and VB require manual tuning or have high computational costs, FAB/LFM offers fully automated model selection with lower computational cost.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important practical problem in model selection for latent feature models, which is computationally expensive with existing methods. The proposed FAB/LFM approach achieves better model selection accuracy with significantly lower computational cost, making it more applicable to large-scale data. The theoretical insights (FIC form for LFMs, identifiability analysis) advance our understanding of Bayesian inference for latent variable models and have potential for broader application.

The experimental results are compelling, showing consistent advantages of FAB/LFM across multiple datasets. The paper demonstrates practical significance through real-world applications (e.g., link prediction, missing value prediction), showing that FAB/LFM can be used in real-world scenarios. The paper also provides a natural guide for post-processing of latent features through the identifiability analysis, which is valuable for practitioners.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical extension of FAB inference to LFMs
- Rigorous proofs of key theoretical results (FIC form, identifiability)
- Practical algorithm with shrinkage acceleration technique
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing clear advantages over baselines
- Strong connection between theory and practice

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of asymptotic assumptions' limitations for smaller datasets
- No comparison with some recent methods for latent feature models (e.g., [23], [24] if relevant)
- The shrinkage acceleration technique's effect on convergence could be analyzed more deeply

## Recommendation
I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a significant theoretical contribution that extends the applicability of FAB inference, provides practical benefits through a novel algorithm with acceleration techniques, and demonstrates clear experimental advantages over existing methods. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models.

The contribution is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant publication at NIPS. It advances the state of the art in model selection for latent feature models and provides both theoretical insights and practical algorithms that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners in the field.

The paper's theoretical insights about the FIC form for LFMs and the identifiability analysis are particularly noteworthy, as they provide deeper understanding of Bayesian inference for latent variable models beyond just the specific application to LFMs. The practical benefits demonstrated in the experiments further strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.

This paper represents a substantial contribution to the NIPS community, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical application of Bayesian inference for latent feature models.
