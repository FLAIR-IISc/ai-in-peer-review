

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

## Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a key limitation of previous FAB work that required models to satisfy a specific Hessian block-diagonal condition. The authors prove that LFMs, despite not meeting this condition, yield FICs (Factorized Information Criteria) with the same form as Mixture Models (MMs). They derive a new FAB algorithm for LFMs that automatically selects feature dimensionality without priors or hyperparameters, introduce a CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration method, and prove parameter identifiability with a merge post-processing step. The paper demonstrates FAB/LFMs' superiority in computational efficiency and model selection accuracy over state-of-the-art methods like IBP, VB, and MEIBP.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Soundness and Novelty:** The paper's main theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which demonstrates that the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs naturally contains the log(∑ₙ zₙₖ) term without requiring the block-diagonal Hessian condition that previously limited FAB applicability. This insight is both surprising and significant—it shows FAB's applicability is broader than previously thought. Theorem 2 and Corollary 3 establish that FAB/LFMs converge to marginal log-likelihoods, which is a strong theoretical justification for the method.

**Algorithmic Innovation:** The paper introduces two key algorithmic refinements: (1) a CCCP interpretation of FAB updates that leads to an effective shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2), and (2) a merge post-processing step based on Theorem 4's identifiability result. These are not just minor tweaks but substantial improvements that address practical concerns about computational efficiency and model interpretability.

**Rigorous Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive, covering artificial simulations, synthetic block data, and real-world datasets. The results consistently show FAB/LFMs outperforming competitors in model selection accuracy (particularly for large N) and computational efficiency. The empirical demonstration of the shrinkage acceleration's impact (Figure 1) is particularly convincing.

**Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear notation, logical flow, and well-organized sections. The theoretical contributions are presented in a way that makes them accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian inference and latent variable models. The pseudocode for the FAB algorithm is precise and well-motivated.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods:** While the paper compares FAB/LFMs with IBP, VB, and MEIBP, it omits comparison with other recent methods for latent feature models, such as the work by Miller et al. (2009) on nonparametric latent feature models or the more recent approaches to scalable IBP inference. The authors acknowledge that "we totally omitted VB because of its long computational time," but this omission weakens the claim of "superiority" since VB is a standard baseline.

**Assumptions and Practical Constraints:** The theoretical results rely on "mild assumptions" stated in the supplementary materials, but the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss how restrictive these assumptions are in practice. For instance, the identifiability theorem (Theorem 4) requires 0 < ∑ₙ μₙₖ/N < 1, which might be problematic for real-world data where features could be fully activated or inactive.

**Overemphasis on Model Selection Accuracy:** The paper focuses primarily on model selection accuracy (i.e., determining K), but doesn't thoroughly evaluate how FAB/LFMs perform on the primary task of LFMs—predictive performance. While the real-world experiments include predictive log-likelihoods, the paper could better connect the automatic model selection to improved predictive performance.

## Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results supported by experiments. The authors carefully address both strengths (automatic model selection, computational efficiency) and weaknesses (theoretical assumptions, potential limitations of the identifiability condition). The supplementary materials appear to contain the necessary theoretical details.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The key insight—that LFMs yield the same FIC form as MMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal condition—is presented logically and supported by careful derivations.

**Originality:** The extension of FAB to LFMs is genuinely novel, as previous work explicitly restricted FAB to models satisfying the Hessian block-diagonal condition. The theoretical insight that Lemma 1 makes this condition unnecessary for LFMs is a significant contribution. The CCCP-based acceleration and merge post-processing are also novel refinements.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in latent variable modeling—automated model selection without hyperparameters or priors. For large-scale applications where computational efficiency is critical, FAB/LFMs offer a compelling alternative to existing methods. The method's ability to automatically select model complexity without prior tuning is particularly valuable for practitioners.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. The theoretical insight that FAB applies to LFMs despite not meeting the previously required Hessian condition is significant and broadens the applicability of this inference method. The algorithmic improvements and empirical validation are convincing.

The main limitations—omission of some recent baselines and insufficient discussion of practical constraints—do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The paper is clearly written, theoretically sound, and experimentally validated. It advances the state of the art in Bayesian model selection for latent feature models and provides a valuable tool for practitioners dealing with large-scale data.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria exceptionally well. It is of high quality, clearly written, original, and significant. The work is technically sound, and the results are well-supported. The authors have made a meaningful contribution that will likely be of interest to both theoretical and applied machine learning researchers.
