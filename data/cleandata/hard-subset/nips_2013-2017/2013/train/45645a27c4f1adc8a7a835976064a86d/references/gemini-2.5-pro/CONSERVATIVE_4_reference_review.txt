This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a recently developed method for model selection, to the case of Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The main goal is to provide a computationally efficient and automatic method for determining the number of latent features, K. The authors' core technical contribution is to show that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes on a form similar to that of Mixture Models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian assumption previously required for FAB. This is achieved via an asymptotic analysis of the log-determinant of the Hessian (Lemma 1). Based on this result, the paper develops an alternating optimization algorithm (FAB/LFM) that includes a regularization term to automatically shrink the number of active features. The authors also provide a CCCP interpretation of the E-step, which inspires a shrinkage acceleration technique, and analyze the parameter identifiability of their method. The proposed FAB/LFM is evaluated against several baselines, including variants of the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Contribution:** The paper successfully extends the FAB framework to a more complex and widely used model class. The derivation showing that the FIC for LFMs retains the desirable `log(sum(z_nk))` regularization term, despite the shared parameters, is a non-trivial and interesting theoretical result.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, the derivation of the FIC for LFMs is presented step-by-step, and the resulting algorithm is described in sufficient detail.

3.  **Practical Enhancements:** The analysis of the E-step as a CCCP procedure, leading to the proposed "shrinkage acceleration," is a thoughtful addition that addresses a practical concern (computational cost) and appears effective in the experiments (Figure 1). The analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) and the resulting merge post-processing step also add to the completeness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficiently Rigorous Experimental Comparison:** The main weakness of this work lies in the experimental validation. For a paper proposing a new inference scheme intended to replace established methods, the comparison to baselines must be scrupulously fair and thorough. The current evaluation falls short in several aspects:
    *   **Baseline Parameter Settings:** The authors state that for IBP, the hyperparameter `alpha` was set to 3, which "might cause overestimation of K." This raises a red flag. Established methods like IBP should be given a fair chance to perform well, which may involve some hyperparameter tuning. Using a single default value that is acknowledged to be potentially suboptimal weakens the claim of FAB's superiority.
    *   **Inconsistent Model Assumptions:** The proposed FAB/LFM learns the noise variance, while for the IBP, VB, and MEIBP baselines, it was fixed. This is a significant discrepancy. To claim superior predictive performance (PLL), all models should operate under the same assumptions. The justification that the baseline software did not support learning this parameter is not sufficient; a fair comparison would require either implementing this for the baselines or fixing the parameter for FAB/LFM as well.
    *   **Omission of a Key Baseline:** Variational Bayes (VB) for IBP is a standard and highly relevant baseline. Omitting it from the main results table entirely "because of its long computational time" is not acceptable. While its computational cost is a valid point of comparison, its model selection and predictive accuracy are crucial for a complete picture. At a minimum, results on smaller datasets where it could finish in a reasonable time should have been included.

2.  **Reliance on Asymptotic Arguments:** The entire FAB framework is built on asymptotic approximations (N -> infinity). While the theoretical convergence to the marginal log-likelihood is an elegant property, its practical relevance for finite, real-world datasets is not self-evident. The paper acknowledges that "in small N scenarios... FAB would perform worse than NPBs (while we observed such case only in Libras)." This admission is important and suggests that the limitations of the asymptotic approach need to be investigated more thoroughly. The current experiments are not designed to systematically explore the breakdown point of the approximation as N decreases.

3.  **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims that FAB "does not have a hand-tunable hyper-parameter." However, the algorithm requires setting a convergence tolerance `delta` and the number of shrinkage iterations `Tshrink`. While these may be less sensitive than the concentration parameters of NPB models, they are still user-defined parameters. This claim of being fully automatic should be softened.

4.  **Clarity of "Variational EM" Baseline:** The paper compares against a "variational EM algorithm" which appears to be the FAB algorithm with the key regularization term `D/2Nπ̃k` removed. It is not clear if this is a standard, published baseline or an ablation of the proposed method. If it is the latter, it should be presented as such, as it primarily demonstrates the importance of the new regularization term rather than superiority over an existing, independent method.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and technically sound extension of a novel inference framework. The theoretical analysis is the strongest part of the work. However, the claims of empirical superiority over established, state-of-the-art methods are not yet convincingly supported due to several issues in the experimental design that disadvantage the baselines. While the FAB framework shows promise, particularly in terms of computational speed and model compactness, its endorsement requires a more rigorous and unbiased empirical validation against well-tuned standard methods like IBP and VB across a wider range of data regimes.
