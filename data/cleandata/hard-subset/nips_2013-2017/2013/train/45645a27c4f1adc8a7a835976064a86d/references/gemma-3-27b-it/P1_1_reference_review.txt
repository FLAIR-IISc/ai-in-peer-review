## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary:**

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously successful with mixture models, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB offers a computationally efficient, automated approach to model selection (specifically, latent dimensionality) without requiring manual hyperparameter tuning or priors. The authors demonstrate, through asymptotic analysis, that LFMs share the same underlying mathematical structure concerning the Fisher Information Criterion (FIC) as MMs, despite not initially satisfying the necessary block-diagonal Hessian condition. They further refine the FAB algorithm for LFMs with a shrinkage acceleration technique based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and prove parameter identifiability, offering guidance for post-processing via feature merging. The paper supports these claims with both theoretical analysis and experiments.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly on the FAB framework introduced in Fujimaki et al. (2016, 2017) for MMs and HMMs. It connects to the broader field of Bayesian Nonparametric (BNP) methods for LFMs, like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) (Griffiths & Ghahramani, 2005), variational approaches (Doshi-Velez et al., 2011), and MAP estimation techniques (Reed & Ghahramani, 2012).  It differentiates itself by prioritizing automation and computational efficiency, aiming to overcome the scaling limitations of existing BNP and VB methods.  The comparison to MAD-Bayes (Broderick et al.) highlights a different asymptotic regime and regularization approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:**  The key strength lies in demonstrating the applicability of the FIC and FAB framework to LFMs despite the lack of a block-diagonal Hessian, a significant extension of the original method. The lemma and theorem establishing the asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood are well-motivated and crucial to the paper’s core argument.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration using CCCP offers a practical improvement, demonstrably reducing computational cost in experiments.  This is a valuable contribution, addressing a key limitation of many Bayesian inference methods.
* **Identifiability Analysis:**  Proving parameter identifiability is a strong theoretical result, addressing a common concern in latent variable models and providing a principled basis for the feature merging post-processing step.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing FAB/LFM to several strong baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) across synthetic, block data, and real-world datasets.  The results consistently demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in terms of model selection and prediction accuracy.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is standard, and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Derivation:**  While clear, the mathematical derivations require significant background knowledge to fully appreciate. Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs would enhance accessibility.
* **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** The paper mentions "mild assumptions" in Lemma 1 but relegates the details to supplementary material. Briefly outlining these assumptions within the main text would increase clarity and allow readers to assess their applicability to different LFM scenarios.
* **Parameter Sensitivity in Comparisons:** The experimental setup mentions sensitivity to parameter settings in comparison methods (e.g., IBP's α).  While acknowledging this is good, more details on how these parameters were tuned (or chosen) for fair comparisons would be beneficial.
* **MEIBP limitations:** The paper states MEIBP is not applicable to negative values, but doesn't discuss how this constraint influences the fair comparison in the Real World Data section.
* **Merge post-processing detail:** The “merge” post-processing sounds like a useful addition. However, the description is brief and the explanation that it is only happening to a few features does not give a good understanding of why it is beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical extension of the FAB framework.
* Practical acceleration technique for improved computational efficiency.
* Strong experimental validation demonstrating competitive performance.
* Addresses an important problem (automated model selection for LFMs).
* Clear writing and logical organization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Mathematical complexity may limit accessibility.
* Detailed discussion of assumptions would improve rigor and clarity.
* Minor areas for improvement in experimental details.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. It successfully extends the FAB inference framework to LFMs, addressing a critical need for efficient and automated model selection in this domain. While the mathematical complexity and some minor experimental details could be improved, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with minor revisions.**

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical derivations.
2.  Briefly summarizing the key assumptions underlying Lemma 1 in the main text.
3.  Elaborating on the parameter tuning process for the comparison methods.
4. Providing a more detailed explanation of the merge post-processing step.
