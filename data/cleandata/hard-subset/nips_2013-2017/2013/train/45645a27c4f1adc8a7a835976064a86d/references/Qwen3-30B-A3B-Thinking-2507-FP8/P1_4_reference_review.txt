

# Peer Review: "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework, previously developed for mixture models (MMs) and hidden Markov models (HMMs) [5, 6], to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The key theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which demonstrates that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by previous FAB work, their Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) takes the same form as for MMs. This allows the authors to derive an FAB algorithm for LFMs that automatically selects the number of latent features without requiring hyperparameters or priors. The paper also introduces a shrinkage acceleration method based on the Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) and provides theoretical analysis on parameter identifiability with a merge post-processing step.

This work builds upon Fujimaki et al. (2014) [5, 6] who introduced FAB for MMs, and extends it to a more general class of latent variable models. It relates to the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) literature [9, 10, 11, 17] for nonparametric latent feature modeling, but differs significantly in its approach to model selection and computational efficiency.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The key insight that LFMs satisfy the FIC form despite not meeting the previous block-diagonal Hessian condition (Lemma 1) is theoretically significant. The proof of Lemma 1 and its implications (Theorem 2, Corollary 3) are well-motivated and rigorous, providing a compelling theoretical foundation.

2. **Algorithmic Innovation**: The FAB/LFM algorithm is well-designed, with a clear derivation from the FIC. The introduction of the shrinkage acceleration via CCCP (Algorithm 2) is particularly clever and effectively addresses the computational bottleneck in model selection.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments demonstrate clear advantages of FAB/LFM over competing methods in terms of computational efficiency (especially with the acceleration) and model selection accuracy. The comparison with IBP, VB, and MEIBP across artificial, synthetic, and real-world datasets provides strong empirical support.

4. **Theoretical Depth**: The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and the merge post-processing step provide valuable theoretical insights that directly inform practical implementation, demonstrating the paper's strong theoretical-practical connection.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Intuition**: While Lemma 1 is well-supported, the paper could provide more intuition for why the log det |F(d)| term in LFMs ends up with the same form as in MMs. The assumptions behind Lemma 1 (mentioned as "see supplementary materials") should be more clearly stated in the main text.

2. **Comparison with MEIBP**: The comparison with MEIBP [22] is somewhat limited. MEIBP is a MAP-based method, while FAB is a Bayesian approximation, so the comparison should clarify how these fundamentally different approaches should be evaluated.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper claims "drastically reduced computational cost" with the acceleration, but doesn't provide a formal complexity analysis. A comparison of how complexity scales with N, D, and K would strengthen the claims.

4. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions "we set Tshrink = 100 (FAB only)" without justification. A sensitivity analysis of this parameter would strengthen the empirical claims.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs in the supplementary materials appear rigorous, and the experimental results provide strong empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the "small N" case in Libras where FAB underperforms). The paper is a complete contribution to the field.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The main contributions are clearly articulated, and the technical content is presented logically. The algorithm descriptions are clear, and the figures effectively illustrate key results. Some parts of the theoretical discussion (e.g., the Hessian analysis) could be made more accessible to readers without a strong background in asymptotic statistics.

**Originality**: This work makes a significant contribution to the literature on latent variable models. Extending FAB to LFMs is novel and non-trivial, with the key insight (Lemma 1) being genuinely new. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work on IBP and VB methods, and the theoretical analysis provides a fresh perspective on model selection for latent feature models.

**Significance**: The significance of this work is high. Model selection for latent feature models is a challenging problem, and FAB/LFM provides a computationally efficient solution that automatically selects model complexity. The practical advantages demonstrated in the experiments (better model selection accuracy, faster computation) make this work highly relevant for practitioners. The theoretical insights on identifiability and the merge post-processing step further enhance the significance.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a significant extension of the FAB framework to a broader class of latent variable models with strong theoretical justification and empirical validation. The theoretical contribution is novel, the algorithm is well-designed, and the practical advantages are clearly demonstrated. While there are some minor weaknesses in the presentation and analysis, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Novel theoretical insight (Lemma 1) extending FAB to LFMs
- Well-designed algorithm with effective shrinkage acceleration
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets
- Theoretical analysis of identifiability and practical post-processing
- Clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods in both speed and accuracy

**Arguments for Rejection**:
- Limited discussion of the theoretical assumptions behind Lemma 1
- Some comparison details with MEIBP could be more thorough
- No formal computational complexity analysis
- Minor clarity issues in theoretical presentation

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models, advancing both theory and practice in an important area of machine learning.
