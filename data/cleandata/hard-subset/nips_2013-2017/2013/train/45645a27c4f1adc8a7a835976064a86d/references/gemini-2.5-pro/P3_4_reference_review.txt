### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a method for model selection in latent variable models, to the domain of Latent Feature Models (LFMs). Previous FAB work (e.g., for Mixture Models and HMMs) relied on a key structural assumption: that the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. This paper's main contribution is to show, via an asymptotic analysis, that a similar Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) can be derived for LFMs, even though they violate this assumption. The resulting FIC objective function for LFMs surprisingly recovers the same characteristic penalty term, `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)`, which drives the model selection properties of FAB.

Based on this theoretical result, the authors derive a practical algorithm, FAB/LFM, which is an EM-like procedure for jointly inferring model parameters and the number of latent features, K. The paper further provides two key algorithmic refinements: 1) A connection to the Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) is established, which inspires a "shrinkage acceleration" method to speed up convergence by pruning irrelevant features early. 2) An analysis of parameter identifiability is presented, which motivates a principled post-processing step for merging redundant features.

This work sits at the intersection of asymptotic Bayesian inference and non-parametric Bayesian (NPB) methods for LFMs. The canonical NPB approach for inferring the number of latent features is the Indian Buffet Process (IBP). This paper positions FAB/LFM as an alternative to IBP and its variants, such as those based on Gibbs sampling, Variational Bayes (VB), or MAP estimation (e.g., MEIBP). Unlike IBP, which uses a stochastic process prior to control model complexity, FAB relies on an asymptotic approximation of the marginal likelihood, which introduces a data-dependent complexity penalty that is asymptotically prior-free. This work significantly broadens the applicability of the FAB framework to a widely used and more complex class of models.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The core result of the paper, encapsulated in Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, is both elegant and surprising. Demonstrating that the log determinant of the LFM Hessian asymptotically decomposes into the `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)` form, despite the shared parameters across features, is a non-trivial insight. This finding suggests that the underlying principles of FAB are more general than previously shown and opens the door to applying this framework to other models with shared parameter structures.

2.  **Principled Algorithmic Development:** The paper goes beyond simply deriving an algorithm from the new objective. The CCCP interpretation of the E-step is insightful and provides a clear justification for the proposed acceleration scheme. Similarly, the identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) is not just a theoretical curiosity; it directly informs a practical and necessary `merge` post-processing step, addressing a well-known issue in LFM inference.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed. The comparison against strong baselines from the IBP literature (fast Gibbs sampling, VB, MEIBP) is convincing. FAB/LFM demonstrates superior or competitive performance in terms of predictive log-likelihood and, crucially, model selection accuracy (estimating K). The results convincingly show that FAB's "model-induced regularization" is effective at preventing overfitting, as evidenced by the small gap between training and predictive likelihoods. The ablation study showing the effectiveness of the shrinkage acceleration is also a valuable addition.

4.  **Practical Appeal:** The resulting FAB/LFM algorithm is computationally attractive. It avoids the complexities of MCMC sampling (IBP) and the potentially slow convergence and high cost of variational methods over a range of K. The EM-like structure is familiar and relatively easy to implement. Its ability to automatically and efficiently prune a large initial K to a compact representation is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on Assumptions for Lemma 1:** The entire theoretical foundation rests on Lemma 1, yet its underlying "mild assumptions" are deferred to the supplementary materials. For a result of this importance, the main text should at least summarize the key conditions required for the asymptotic approximation to hold. For instance, what are the constraints on the data distribution, the feature matrix W, or the relationship between N, D, and K? This omission makes it difficult for the reader to fully assess the scope and limitations of the method from the main paper alone.

2.  **Nuance on "Hyperparameter-Free" Claim:** The paper emphasizes that FAB is appealing because it "does not have a hand-tunable hyper-parameter." While it is true that it avoids model-level hyperparameters like the IBP's concentration parameter `alpha`, the proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) does introduce algorithmic parameters, namely the convergence tolerance `delta` and the number of inner-loop shrinkage iterations `T_shrink`. The paper should be more precise, stating that the method is free of *model-defining* hyperparameters that directly influence the prior over complexity, while acknowledging the existence of these algorithmic tuning parameters. A brief sensitivity analysis or discussion of how to set `T_shrink` would be beneficial.

3.  **Discussion of Asymptotic Limitations:** As an asymptotic method, FAB's approximation quality depends on having a sufficiently large sample size N. The experimental results are strong, but the paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of the regime where the approximation might break down (e.g., very small N, or high D/N ratio). The single worse result on the "Libras" dataset is noted, which is good, but connecting this observation more directly to the potential limitations of the asymptotic theory would provide a more complete picture.

4.  **Details of the Merge Procedure:** The `merge()` procedure is an important practical component motivated by the identifiability analysis. However, its details are also relegated to the supplement. A brief algorithmic sketch or a more concrete description in the main text (e.g., how the distance threshold for merging is determined) would improve the paper's self-containedness and reproducibility.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and important, and the resulting algorithm is both practical and empirically powerful. The connections drawn to CCCP and identifiability theory demonstrate a deep understanding of the problem. While the presentation could be improved by including more detail on the key lemma's assumptions and algorithmic parameters in the main text, these are minor weaknesses in an otherwise excellent submission. The work advances the state of the art in automated model selection for latent feature models and is likely to be influential.

**Recommendation: Accept**
