

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), claiming it offers fully automated model selection (i.e., determining the number of latent features) without hyperparameters or priors, with lower computational cost than existing methods. The authors build on their prior work on FAB for Mixture Models, showing that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for their original FAB framework, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) takes the same form as for MMs.

The paper makes three main contributions: (1) theoretical justification for FAB's applicability to LFMs via Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, (2) an FAB algorithm for LFMs with a shrinkage acceleration technique based on CCCP interpretation, and (3) a merge post-processing step for identifiability. The authors provide experimental comparisons against several baselines including IBP, VB, and MEIBP on artificial, block, and real-world data.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical connection between FAB for MMs and LFMs is interesting and potentially valuable for understanding model complexity in latent variable models.
- The shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) is well-motivated and appears to offer computational benefits.
- The paper makes a clear theoretical argument about the asymptotic behavior of the method.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper falls short on several critical aspects of rigorous scientific validation:

1. **Insufficient experimental validation:** While the paper claims "drastically reduced computational cost," Figure 1 only shows a modest improvement (about 30-40% reduction in runtime) for the artificial simulation. The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the shrinkage acceleration technique versus other algorithmic components. The claim of "drastically reduced" computational cost is unsubstantiated.

2. **Asymptotic focus without finite-sample analysis:** The entire theoretical development is based on asymptotic behavior, but the paper provides no analysis of finite-sample performance. The claim that FAB "automatically selects feature dimensionality" is not validated with sufficient evidence on real-world data where asymptotic approximations may not hold.

3. **Superficial experimental comparison:** The real-world data results in Table 1 are limited to a few datasets. For instance, the paper states "FAB consistently achieved good predictive performance," but the results show only modest improvements over baselines (e.g., 0.1-0.3 log-likelihood points). The paper also omits VB entirely due to "long computational time" without providing any quantitative comparison of the time differences.

4. **Lack of failure cases:** The paper presents only positive results without demonstrating scenarios where FAB fails or where existing methods would be preferable. For example, the authors claim FAB "achieves the most accurate estimation of K," but don't show cases where it fails to do so.

5. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims FAB "places more importance on automation" than other methods, but doesn't provide evidence that this automation is actually beneficial in practice. The paper also claims FAB "mitigates over-fitting" based on the smaller difference between TLL and PLL, but doesn't provide statistical significance tests or deeper analysis of this phenomenon.

**Quality assessment:**
The paper is technically sound in its theoretical development but lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its key claims. The theoretical analysis is confined to asymptotic behavior without addressing the finite-sample performance that matters in practice. The experimental section is inadequate to support the paper's claims of superior performance and computational efficiency.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written with clear mathematical notation. However, the connection between the theoretical analysis and practical implementation could be strengthened, particularly regarding the conditions under which the asymptotic approximation holds.

**Originality:**
The extension of FAB to LFMs is somewhat novel, but the key insight that the FIC takes the same form as for MMs is not sufficiently differentiated from related work. The CCCP interpretation and shrinkage acceleration are reasonable extensions of existing techniques rather than major innovations.

**Significance:**
The paper does not demonstrate significant advances over existing methods. The claimed computational advantages are not convincingly supported, and the performance gains in real-world applications are marginal. The paper fails to provide compelling evidence that this approach would be adopted by practitioners over established methods.

**Recommendation:**
While the theoretical connection between FAB for MMs and LFMs is interesting, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its key claims. The experimental validation is inadequate, and the paper overstates its contributions without thorough comparison to established baselines. The computational cost claims are particularly unsubstantiated. I recommend rejection due to insufficient empirical validation of the method's advantages.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is non-trivial
- The shrinkage acceleration technique is well-motivated
- The paper makes a clear connection between FAB and model complexity

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Claims of "drastically reduced computational cost" are not supported by evidence
- Theoretical development is limited to asymptotic behavior without finite-sample analysis
- Experimental validation is superficial and lacks ablation studies
- Performance gains over baselines are marginal in real-world settings
- No demonstration of failure cases or limitations of the method

The paper would benefit from significantly more comprehensive experimental validation, including ablation studies, failure case analysis, and a thorough comparison of computational cost across a wider range of problem sizes. Without this, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to justify the claims of superior performance and efficiency.
