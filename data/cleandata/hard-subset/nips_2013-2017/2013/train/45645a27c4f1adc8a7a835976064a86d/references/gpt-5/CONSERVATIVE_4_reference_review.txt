Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM settings to linear-Gaussian latent feature models (LFMs) with Bernoulli features. The key technical claim is Lemma 1, asserting that the Hessian term in the Laplace approximation yields a ∑k log ∑n znk penalty even though the standard block-diagonal Hessian assumptions used for mixtures do not hold. This leads to a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs (Theorem 2) with the same functional form as for mixtures. Building on this, the authors derive a practical FAB algorithm with a mean-field approximation over Z and a CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration. They also present an identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) suggesting FAB favors balanced, lower-K solutions and propose a merge post-processing heuristic. Experiments compare to IBP (Gibbs), finite-K VB (limited), and MEIBP, and report improved model selection and competitive predictive performance with reduced runtime, aided by the acceleration.

Quality
- Theory: The main asymptotic result (Lemma 1) underpins everything, but its conditions and proof are deferred to the supplement and are summarized as “mild assumptions.” Given the non-regular nature of LFMs and the delicacy of Laplace approximations in singular models, a clearer statement (precise regularity, growth rates, identifiability conditions) in the main text is necessary. The transition from the asymptotic marginal likelihood (Theorem 2) to the tractable bound L adds two relaxations (linearization of the concave term and mean-field factorization), so the link between optimizing L and model selection consistency is not established.
- Algorithmic soundness: The E-step fixed-point (Eq. 11) and CCCP view are reasonable; however, the accelerateShrinkage procedure updates π and π̃ in an inner loop. It is not entirely clear that these steps preserve the global monotonic increase of the same lower bound L across all updates. The merge heuristic is motivated by Theorem 4 but uses a distance threshold; its effect on L is only checked post hoc.
- Experiments: There are important fairness issues. FAB/EM learn noise precision, whereas IBP/VB/MEIBP are run with fixed noise levels (1.0 synthetic; 0.75 real), which advantages FAB in likelihood-based metrics. IBP hyperparameters (e.g., α) are fixed rather than inferred/sampled, which is known to bias K. VB is largely omitted because of runtime; this weakens the empirical case against established baselines, given more scalable VB/IBP variants exist. The MEIBP omission on “block” due to sensitivity also weakens the comparison. Variability across runs, sensitivity to initialization/Tshrink, and ablations (e.g., FAB without learning λ; with/without merge) are only partially addressed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with clear notation. However:
- Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 should be stated with full conditions in the main text (even if proofs are in the supplement), because they are the central contribution.
- The precise lower bound being optimized after mean-field and linearization should be clearly named and related to the original FIC; the monotonicity guarantees should be spelled out per step, especially when accelerateShrinkage modifies π and π̃ within the E-step.
- Complexity per iteration (in N, D, K) for vanilla FAB vs accelerated FAB would help interpret scalability claims.

Originality
Extending FAB beyond block-diagonal Hessian models to LFMs and showing the FIC retains the same structure is interesting and, if correct, broadens applicability. The CCCP-based acceleration is a reasonable re-interpretation rather than a fundamentally novel technique. The identifiability angle is a useful addition, though limited to certain stationary points.

Significance
Model selection in LFMs is an important problem with strong established baselines (IBP and variants). If the asymptotics hold and the method is competitive, this could be impactful. However, the current empirical validation does not convincingly demonstrate superiority under fair, controlled conditions, and the theoretical link from the proposed bound to consistent model selection remains informal.

Strengths
- Clear and potentially general asymptotic argument leading to an FIC for LFMs.
- Practical algorithm with an acceleration that empirically reduces runtime.
- Insightful CCCP interpretation and an identifiability discussion leading to a merge heuristic.
- Broad set of datasets, including larger-scale cases.

Weaknesses
- Central theoretical claim (Lemma 1) lacks explicit conditions/proof sketch in the main text; the non-regular context warrants more care.
- The bound optimization includes mean-field and linearization; no guarantees on model selection consistency and only partial monotonicity arguments.
- Experimental comparisons are not fully fair (noise variance learned vs fixed; IBP hyperparameters not inferred; VB largely omitted).
- MEIBP results are omitted on some datasets; sensitivity and multi-run variability are underreported.
- No time/space complexity analysis; limited ablations.

Questions for authors
1. Please state the exact assumptions for Lemma 1 in the main text and provide a proof sketch. How robust is the result to non-diagonal Λ or non-Gaussian noise?
2. Does accelerateShrinkage (with simultaneous π and π̃ updates) provably increase the same L at every inner iteration?
3. Can you provide selection consistency results (even under simplifying assumptions), or at least conditions under which FAB recovers the true K?
4. How sensitive are results to Tshrink, initialization, and merge thresholds? Please report variance across runs.
5. Can you rerun baselines with learned noise variance (or fix λ for FAB/EM) and allow IBP to infer α (e.g., Gamma prior) to ensure fairness?

Suggestions
- Move the precise statement of Lemma 1/Theorem 2 into the main text; add a complexity analysis.
- Provide ablations: FAB with fixed λ; without accelerateShrinkage; without merge.
- Include a more competitive VB/IBP baseline (e.g., with hyperparameter learning or scalable VB/SVI), and report multiple seeds.
- Report calibration/diagnostics (e.g., PLL vs TLL gaps) while controlling noise variance consistently across methods.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The direction is promising, but the theoretical exposition needs to be more rigorous in the main paper, and the empirical comparisons need to be made fairer and more comprehensive to justify the claims.

Confidence: High (familiar with Bayesian asymptotics, IBP, and variational methods).
