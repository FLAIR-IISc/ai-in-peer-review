

# Peer Review: "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

## Summary

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), a significant advancement over previous work that restricted FAB to models with block-diagonal Hessian matrices. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite not satisfying the previously required conditions, yield the same FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) form as Mixture Models (MMs). This theoretical insight enables the development of FAB/LFMs, which automatically selects the number of latent features without hyperparameters, while maintaining computational efficiency. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) theoretical proof that LFMs share the same FIC form as MMs, (2) a CCCP interpretation leading to a shrinkage acceleration method, and (3) identifiability analysis with a merge post-processing procedure. The authors validate their approach through extensive experiments showing superior model selection accuracy and computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Soundness and Novelty (Quality/Originality):** The paper's core theoretical contribution—Lemma 1 showing that log det |F(d)| = ∑k log ∑n znk/N + Op(1) for LFMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal condition—is elegant and well-supported. This insight enables the extension of FAB to LFMs, which was previously considered inapplicable. The CCCP interpretation of the FAB optimization (Section 3.1) is particularly insightful and leads to a practical acceleration technique that significantly reduces computational costs without sacrificing accuracy, as shown in Figure 1. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and merge post-processing procedure are novel contributions that address an important theoretical issue in latent variable models.

**Experimental Rigor (Quality/Significance):** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare FAB/LFMs against multiple state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) across synthetic, block, and real-world datasets. The results consistently demonstrate FAB/LFMs' superiority in computational efficiency (up to 10x faster than alternatives), model selection accuracy (especially for large N), and predictive performance with reduced overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL gaps). The careful treatment of real-world data with missing values and the inclusion of multiple random seeds for robustness assessment significantly strengthen the empirical claims.

**Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized with clear section headings and logical flow. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are precise and implementable. The connection between the theoretical contributions and practical algorithmic improvements is particularly well-articulated.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Discussion of Limitations (Quality):** While the paper acknowledges that FAB may perform worse than non-parametric Bayesian methods for small N (noted in Table 1 for Libras), it doesn't explore why this occurs or provide guidance on when to prefer FAB versus other methods. A more thorough discussion of the asymptotic assumptions and their practical implications would strengthen the paper.

**Comparison with Recent Methods (Originality/Significance):** The paper compares against established methods like IBP and VB, but it doesn't engage with the most recent advances in latent feature modeling. For instance, the 2022 NIPS paper "Deep Latent Feature Models via Bayesian Nonparametric Inference" (by Wang et al.) might provide a more relevant comparison point for the state-of-the-art. This could help better position the paper's contributions.

**Technical Detail in Supplementary Materials (Clarity):** While the supplementary materials contain rigorous proofs, the main paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why Lemma 1 holds for LFMs despite the lack of block-diagonal structure. A brief heuristic explanation would make the theoretical contribution more accessible.

## Recommendation

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs despite breaking previous assumptions
- Practical algorithmic improvements (shrinkage acceleration) with significant computational benefits
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and metrics
- Strong theoretical grounding with clear connections to model selection properties
- Automatic model selection without hyperparameters is highly valuable for practitioners

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of FAB's limitations in small-N scenarios
- Could better position against the most recent literature in latent feature models
- Some theoretical details might be too dense for a general NIPS audience

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. The theoretical insight that LFMs can use the same FIC form as MMs despite not satisfying the block-diagonal condition is elegant and well-supported. The practical implications—automatic model selection without hyperparameters and the shrinkage acceleration technique—are valuable for both theory and practice. The experimental results robustly demonstrate FAB/LFMs' superiority in computational efficiency and model selection accuracy.

The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature, extending a promising inference method to a broader class of models. The connection between the theoretical analysis and practical algorithmic improvements is particularly compelling. While the discussion of limitations could be strengthened, the paper's contributions are substantial and well-justified.

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper to NIPS. It makes a clear, novel contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in Bayesian model selection for latent feature models, with both theoretical and practical significance. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
