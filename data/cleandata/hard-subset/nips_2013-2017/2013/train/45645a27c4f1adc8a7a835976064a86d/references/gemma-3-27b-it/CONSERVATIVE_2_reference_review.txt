This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core contribution lies in demonstrating that, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by previous FAB work, a similar FIC can be derived, enabling automated model selection (dimensionality reduction) without explicit priors. The authors further propose a shrinkage acceleration method based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation and analyze parameter identifiability, offering a post-processing step to merge redundant latent features.

The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations appear sound, particularly regarding the asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix (Lemma 1) and the derivation of the FIC for LFMs. The authors appropriately position their work within the context of existing Bayesian non-parametric (BNP) and variational Bayesian (VB) methods for LFM dimensionality selection. The proposed acceleration scheme, rooted in the CCCP interpretation, is a potentially valuable practical contribution. The discussion of parameter identifiability and the proposed merge post-processing are sensible extensions of the core method.

However, despite the theoretical soundness and clear presentation, I find the paper lacking in substantial empirical validation. The experiments, while including both synthetic and real-world datasets, feel somewhat limited in scope. The comparison against established methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) is not entirely convincing. While FAB/LFMs perform competitively on some datasets, the improvements aren’t always dramatic, and the performance gains appear contingent on specific parameter settings and dataset characteristics. The claim of superiority feels overstated given the available evidence. 

Furthermore, the ablation study comparing FAB with and without shrinkage acceleration, while demonstrating a computational speedup, doesn’t fully justify the added complexity. The performance improvements are minor, and the practical benefit of the acceleration needs further scrutiny, particularly for higher-dimensional datasets. 

The reliance on fixing the noise standard deviation for the IBP and VB comparisons is a weakness. While the authors acknowledge this and cite prior work, it introduces a potential bias and limits the fairness of the comparison. The assertion that FAB consistently achieves “good predictive performance” is supported by the tables, but the magnitude of those improvements, relative to the complexity added, is questionable.

The presentation of the experimental results lacks sufficient detail. While PLL and TLL are reported, the variance across multiple runs is not presented, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the observed differences. Furthermore, the experimental setup, particularly concerning hyperparameter tuning for each compared method, requires greater clarity. The statement that the software default values were used for most methods is insufficient; a more detailed justification of parameter choices is necessary.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound derivation of FAB for LFMs, extending the applicability of this promising inference method.
*   Clear mathematical exposition and logical flow.
*   Potentially valuable shrinkage acceleration technique.
*   Addresses the important problem of automatic dimensionality selection in LFMs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient empirical validation to convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods.
*   Limited scope of experiments and potential bias in the comparison setup (noise standard deviation).
*   The claimed performance gains are not always substantial or statistically significant.
*   Lack of detail in the experimental setup, particularly regarding hyperparameter tuning.
*   The gains from the shrinkage acceleration are not overwhelmingly significant.



**Overall assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper in its current form. While the theoretical contributions are solid and the paper is well-written, the lack of strong empirical evidence and the limitations in the experimental validation raise significant concerns. The paper feels like a promising start, but it requires considerably more thorough experimentation and analysis to justify its claims of superiority and establish its value to the NIPS community. The authors should address the concerns raised above by expanding the experiments, providing more detailed results, and rigorously comparing their method to a broader range of baselines under fair and well-documented conditions. A stronger focus on demonstrating practical benefits and statistical significance is crucial for acceptance.
