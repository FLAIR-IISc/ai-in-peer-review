

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), claiming to achieve automatic model selection (determining the number of latent features) without priors or hand-tunable hyperparameters. The authors build upon previous FAB work for Mixture Models (MMs) and Hidden Markov Models, asserting that FAB inference can be applied to LFMs despite the fact that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by prior FAB work.

The main theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which claims that for LFMs, log det |F(d)| = ∑k log(∑n z_nk)/N + Op(1), allowing the authors to derive an FIC for LFMs that has the same form as for MMs. This leads to the claim that FAB/LFMs inherit desirable properties like automatic shrinkage of irrelevant latent features and parameter identifiability. The paper also introduces an "accelerateShrinkage" algorithm based on a CCCP interpretation of the FAB updates.

The experimental evaluation compares FAB/LFMs against IBP, VB, and MEIBP on artificial data, block data, and real-world datasets. The authors claim FAB achieves better model selection accuracy (particularly for large N), faster computational speed with their acceleration method, and better predictive performance with less overfitting.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a theoretically interesting claim about the applicability of FAB to LFMs, which is not immediately obvious given the different model structure.
2. The CCCP interpretation of the FAB updates provides a useful theoretical perspective and motivates the acceleration method.
3. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) offers a meaningful theoretical contribution to understanding latent feature models.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient theoretical justification:** The authors claim that LFMs don't satisfy the conditions for FAB but still derive FIC for LFMs. However, they don't convincingly demonstrate why the block-diagonal Hessian condition is not required for LFMs. The paper relies heavily on asymptotic arguments (Op(1) terms), but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these approximations hold in practical settings with finite N. The supplementary materials are mentioned for rigorous proofs, but the main paper lacks the necessary details to verify the asymptotic arguments.

2. **Inadequate experimental validation:** The paper's computational claims (e.g., "drastically reduced computational cost") are not sufficiently supported. Figure 1 shows a comparison of FAB with and without acceleration, but doesn't compare the accelerated method against the best existing baselines (MEIBP is mentioned as faster, but FAB achieves better accuracy). The real-world data experiments are limited to a few datasets, and the paper omits VB entirely due to "long computational time" without providing meaningful comparisons of computational efficiency across methods.

3. **Unjustified novelty claim:** The paper claims this is the first extension of FAB to LFMs, but doesn't adequately distinguish this from existing methods. The authors cite VB and MEIBP as alternatives, but don't convincingly demonstrate why FAB should be preferred over these established methods. The paper fails to show how FAB/LFMs provide a significant advantage over these methods in terms of theoretical guarantees or practical performance.

4. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art:** The paper compares with IBP, VB, and MEIBP, but MEIBP is a relatively recent method that has already been shown to be effective for large-scale problems. The authors don't provide a thorough comparison of FAB with other recent advances in latent feature models, such as the more recent work on scalable IBP approximations.

5. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper claims the shrinkage acceleration "drastically" reduces computational cost, but doesn't provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component of the acceleration method. It's unclear whether the observed speedup is due to the acceleration method or simply due to better model selection.

**Overall assessment:**
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs, the claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous theoretical justification or comprehensive experimental validation. The paper is largely a straightforward extension of previous work without demonstrating significant advantages over established methods. The theoretical arguments rely on asymptotic approximations that may not hold in practice, and the experimental validation is limited and not sufficiently thorough.

The paper would benefit from a more rigorous treatment of the asymptotic assumptions, a more comprehensive experimental comparison with state-of-the-art baselines, and a clearer demonstration of why FAB should be preferred over existing methods. The computational claims need stronger empirical support, and the paper should provide a more convincing argument for why this extension is necessary given the availability of other well-established methods for latent feature model selection.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical idea, the lack of sufficient theoretical justification and experimental validation against appropriate baselines makes it difficult to assess the true contribution. The authors make claims about significant advantages (computational efficiency, model selection accuracy) without providing the necessary evidence to support these claims. This is a typical case of a novel method being presented without the thorough validation that would be expected for NIPS.
