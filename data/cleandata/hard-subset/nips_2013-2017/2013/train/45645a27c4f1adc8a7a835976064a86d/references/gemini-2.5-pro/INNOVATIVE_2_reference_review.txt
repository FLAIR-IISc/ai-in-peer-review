### Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

**Summary**

This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a recently developed framework for automated model selection, to the domain of Latent Feature Models (LFMs). Previously, FAB was restricted to models like Mixture Models (MMs) that satisfy a strong condition on their Hessian matrix (block-diagonality). The central and most impressive contribution of this work is to show, via asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1), that this condition is not necessary. The authors demonstrate that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs—which do not have a block-diagonal Hessian—surprisingly takes on the exact same functional form as the FIC for MMs. This key theoretical insight allows them to derive the FAB/LFM algorithm, which can automatically determine the number of latent features without resorting to priors or hand-tuned hyperparameters, a common challenge addressed by Non-parametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

Beyond this core theoretical extension, the paper provides several practical and analytical contributions. It offers a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation of the E-step, which inspires a novel and effective "shrinkage acceleration" method to speed up computation. Furthermore, it analyzes the parameter identifiability of the model under the FAB framework (Theorem 4), demonstrating that the method's induced regularization resolves common identifiability issues in LFMs and provides a principled foundation for a feature-merging post-processing step. The empirical evaluation shows that FAB/LFM is competitive with, and often superior to, established methods like IBP, Variational Bayes (VB), and Maximum-Expectation IBP (MEIBP) in terms of computational speed, model selection accuracy, and predictive performance, especially in finding more compact models.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its bold and impactful theoretical contribution. Extending an inference framework by showing that its core underlying assumption is overly restrictive is a significant step forward. The result that the crucial `log(sum(z_nk))` penalty term re-emerges in LFMs, despite their more complex parameter-sharing structure, is elegant and non-obvious. This finding fundamentally broadens the scope of FAB inference and suggests that the principles of "model induced regularization" may be far more general than previously understood. This work does not merely apply an existing method but expands its theoretical foundations, opening a new direction for applying asymptotic Bayesian methods to a wider and more complex class of latent variable models.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The proposed algorithm is not a collection of heuristics but is well-grounded in asymptotic theory. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is the paper's cornerstone. The subsequent analysis of parameter identifiability is also a major plus. It provides a compelling theoretical argument for why FAB can succeed where standard Maximum Likelihood Estimation might fail (i.e., by becoming lost in a non-identifiable solution space), and it gives a clear motivation for the proposed merging procedure. This level of analysis adds considerable depth to the work.

3.  **Excellent Practical Contributions:** The theoretical insights are successfully translated into a practical and effective algorithm. The CCCP interpretation leading to the shrinkage acceleration method is particularly clever. It addresses a potential bottleneck of the algorithm—slowly pruning irrelevant features—and the experiments show it works extremely well. This demonstrates a thoughtful approach to making the method not just theoretically sound but also computationally viable.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their method against a strong set of established baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) and an appropriate ablative baseline (variational EM). The results consistently demonstrate the benefits of FAB/LFM: it is often faster, finds more compact and accurate models on synthetic data, and achieves strong predictive performance on real-world datasets, while showing less evidence of overfitting (smaller gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods).

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Inherent Limitations of the Asymptotic Approach:** The framework is, by definition, asymptotic, and its theoretical guarantees hold in the large-N limit. The authors are commendably honest about this, noting that performance may degrade in small-N scenarios, as observed in one of the real-world datasets. While this is an inherent trade-off of the approach rather than a flaw in the paper's execution, it is the primary limitation on the method's applicability when compared to fully Bayesian nonparametric methods which do not rely on such asymptotics.

2.  **Clarity on Core Assumptions:** The paper's most critical result, Lemma 1, is stated to hold under "some mild assumptions" which are detailed in the supplementary material. While this is common practice, the entire paper hinges on the validity and non-restrictiveness of these assumptions. Including a brief, high-level description or characterization of these assumptions in the main body of the paper would have strengthened the central claim and given the reader more confidence without needing to consult the supplement immediately.

3.  **Local Optima:** The proposed alternating optimization algorithm is only guaranteed to converge to a local maximum of the objective function. The effect of initialization on the final solution is not discussed in detail. While this is a common issue for nearly all methods in this domain, a brief discussion of sensitivity to initialization would have made the evaluation more complete.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work presents a novel, significant, and bold extension of an important line of research in approximate Bayesian inference. The theoretical contribution—showing that FAB can be applied to models that violate its original core assumptions—is a genuine breakthrough that could inspire considerable follow-up work. This theoretical novelty is backed by strong practical contributions in the form of an efficient algorithm and a compelling set of experiments that demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The paper's weaknesses are minor and largely represent inherent trade-offs of the asymptotic paradigm itself. This is precisely the kind of thought-provoking and direction-setting research that NIPS should showcase.
