Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features. The key theoretical claim is Lemma 1: despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition previously required by FAB, the log-determinant of the per-dimension Hessian asymptotically decomposes into a sum of log feature-usage counts, yielding an FIC for LFMs that has the same form as for mixture models. From this, Theorem 2 gives an asymptotic approximation to the marginal complete log-likelihood with a complexity term proportional to sum_k log sum_n z_nk, and Corollary 3 asserts asymptotic equivalence between FIC and the marginal log-likelihood up to Op(1). A practical FAB algorithm is proposed using a mean-field approximation, a CCCP interpretation of the E-step, and a “shrinkage acceleration” routine to remove irrelevant features early; a heuristic merge post-processing step is motivated by an identifiability argument (Theorem 4). Experiments on synthetic, block data, and several real datasets compare FAB to IBP (Gibbs), finite-K VB, MEIBP, and EM.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on prior FAB for mixtures/HMMs and connects to nonparametric Bayesian LFMs (beta-Bernoulli/IBP), VB for IBP, and MEIBP. The main novelty is extending FAB to a setting that lacks the factorized Hessian structure assumed before, and showing the same FIC form emerges. The CCCP interpretation is a nice unification with known optimization machinery. The paper also situates its penalty relative to BIC scaling.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Deriving an FIC for LFMs that matches the MM case despite violated assumptions is interesting and, if correct under mild conditions, widens FAB’s applicability.
- Algorithmic practicality: The FAB updates are simple (closed-form M-step; logistic fixed-point E-step) and the CCCP-based shrinkage acceleration is sensible.
- Automatic model selection: The regularizer depending on log feature usage offers data-driven elimination of redundant features without hyperparameters.
- Identifiability discussion: Theorem 4 clarifies a common degeneracy in LFMs and motivates a concrete merge post-processing strategy.
- Some promising empirical results: On certain datasets, FAB selects more compact models with competitive or better predictive log-likelihood and substantially fewer features.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy reliance on unverified assumptions: The central Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are deferred to the supplement with only “mild” conditions stated in the main paper. Given the entire contribution rests on these asymptotics, the paper should clearly state the assumptions (e.g., bounded moments, identifiability, non-degenerate feature usage rates, scaling of K with N, misspecification tolerance). Without this, it is difficult to assess validity and scope.
- Asymptotic claims vs finite-sample performance: While the authors acknowledge small-N inaccuracies, the empirical study does not thoroughly characterize regimes (N, D, K, noise) where FAB underperforms NPB/VB, nor does it stress-test the method under model misspecification or highly correlated features.
- Fairness of comparisons: Several choices may bias results toward FAB:
  - FAB learns Λ and b; for competing methods the noise variance is fixed a priori (1 or 0.75), which directly affects log-likelihoods and can penalize baselines. A fair comparison should allow all methods to learn noise parameters (or at least cross-validate them).
  - IBP’s α is fixed (not tuned), which can strongly affect K. MEIBP is reported as non-negative-only and omitted on certain data; clarification and consistent constraints across methods are needed.
  - VB is omitted on real data due to runtime; at minimum include smaller datasets or modern scalable VI baselines for IBP/BPFA that learn variances.
- Monotonicity and pruning/merging: The lower-bound monotonicity of FAB holds for the alternating updates, but the shrinkage elimination and the merge post-processing are only heuristically justified. Removing/merging features changes the parameterization; the paper claims L increases, but a rigorous argument for both operations (with the approximate bound in use) is missing.
- Computational complexity and scalability: The paper does not provide clear per-iteration complexity or memory footprints. With K initialized at min(N, D) and multiple CCCP inner loops (Tshrink=100), worst-case costs could be high. A systematic runtime vs N, D, K plot and sensitivity to Tshrink are lacking.
- Identifiability theorem applicability: Theorem 4 requires exact equality w_k = w_l and activation rates strictly between 0 and 1—conditions that are measure-zero in practice. The practical merge relies on a thresholded Euclidean criterion; its sensitivity and effect on performance are not analyzed.
- Clarity and completeness: The main text omits crucial assumptions and many algorithmic details (e.g., stopping conditions during CCCP inner loops, selection of the merge threshold). Some notational choices and typos may hinder reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. State explicitly in the main paper the assumptions under which Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 hold. Do they allow K to grow with N? What happens under misspecification (e.g., nonbinary latent structure or correlated features)?
2. Can you provide a proof sketch for Lemma 1 to convey the key idea driving the log sum z_nk term without A1/A2?
3. Do the bound-increase guarantees rigorously cover the accelerateShrinkage and merge operations? If not, can you bound the potential degradation?
4. Rerun baselines with learned noise variance (or cross-validated fixed values) and tuned IBP α; report sensitivity analyses for all methods.
5. Provide per-iteration complexity and ablations on Tshrink, initialization K, and the merge threshold; include runtime vs (N,D,K) scaling plots.
6. Compare against additional established baselines (e.g., ARD-based Bayesian FA/BPFA variants) that also perform automatic feature shrinkage.

Evaluation
- Quality: Mixed. The theoretical direction is compelling, but crucial assumptions/proofs are not sufficiently articulated in the main text, and some experimental comparisons are arguably unfair or incomplete.
- Clarity: Moderate. The presentation is dense; key assumptions and algorithmic details should be surfaced in the main paper.
- Originality: Reasonably high. Extending FAB to LFMs and deriving the same FIC form is novel; the CCCP view is neat though not surprising.
- Significance: Potentially meaningful if the theory holds broadly and the empirical advantages persist under fair comparisons. As it stands, evidence is not fully convincing.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). I am sympathetic to the idea, but the paper needs stronger, explicit theoretical conditions, more rigorous handling of pruning/merging steps, and fairer, more comprehensive empirical validation, especially regarding noise variance learning and baseline tuning.

Confidence: High.
