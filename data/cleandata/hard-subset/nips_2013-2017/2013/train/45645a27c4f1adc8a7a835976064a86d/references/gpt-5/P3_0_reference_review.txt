Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian likelihood. The core theoretical claim (Lemma 1) is that the log-determinant of the complete-data Hessian with respect to per-dimension parameters θd decomposes asymptotically into a sum of log feature-usage counts, yielding an FIC of the same functional form as for mixtures: a data fit term penalized by −(DK/2) log N − (D/2)∑k log(∑n znk). This leads to a tractable lower bound optimized via an EM-like algorithm with mean-field q(Z), where the E-step fixed-point updates include an automatic shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k) that eliminates unused features. The authors reinterpret the E-step as a convex–concave procedure (CCCP), propose a shrinkage acceleration routine, and analyze identifiability, arguing FAB prefers identifiable, small-K ML solutions and motivates a post-hoc feature merging step. Experiments on synthetic, block, and several real datasets compare FAB against IBP (Gibbs), IBP-VB, MEIBP, and a variational EM baseline, showing strong model selection and predictive performance with competitive or better runtimes (especially with the shrinkage acceleration).

Strengths
- Novel and potentially impactful theoretical extension: Showing that LFMs (which do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian structure used in FAB for mixtures) nonetheless yield the same FIC form is a neat and non-obvious result, broadening the applicability of FAB beyond canonical models.
- Automation without hyperparameters: FAB selects K and parameters without tuning priors, which is attractive in practice; the shrinkage term arises from asymptotics rather than hand-crafted regularization.
- Algorithmic contributions: The CCCP interpretation and the accelerateShrinkage routine are well-motivated and empirically useful. The merge post-processing guided by an identifiability analysis is sensible and preserves monotonicity by checking the lower bound.
- Empirical evidence: Experiments show clear advantages in model selection accuracy and predictive log-likelihood, and the acceleration substantially reduces wall-clock time. The block-data qualitative results convincingly illustrate the shrinkage effect.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Central theoretical step deferred: Lemma 1 under “mild assumptions” is the linchpin. In the main text, no proof sketch is provided, and the assumptions are unspecified. Given that Z⊤Z is generally dense in LFMs (due to co-activations), it is not obvious that log det F(d) asymptotically splits into −(K/2) log N plus (1/2)∑k log ∑n znk + Op(1) without additional incoherence or bounded-correlation-type conditions. A brief derivation or clear statement of required conditions is necessary for readers to trust the equivalence of FIC forms.
- Bound structure and notational clarity: Some equations have apparent typos or confusing constants (e.g., Eq. (10) contains “−2D + K log N,” which does not align cleanly with Theorem 2’s complexity terms). The presentation would benefit from explicitly showing how the −(DK/2) log N and −(D/2)∑k log ∑n znk arise from det F(d) across the (K+2)-dimensional θd, and how constants are handled.
- Monotonicity guarantees under accelerations: While the base FAB lower bound is increased by alternating updates, the accelerateShrinkage routine and the elimination of features “in early steps” need a formal argument that L never decreases (or an explicit safeguard). For merge, the paper does check L, but for acceleration the monotonicity is not rigorously demonstrated.
- Experimental fairness and setup details:
  - Noise precision: The paper states IBP/VB/MEIBP implementations do not learn noise and thus a fixed σ is used (0.75 on real data). It appears FAB learns λ; if so, this comparison is unfair, as FAB can adapt noise while competitors cannot. Either fix λ for FAB or use variants that learn noise across methods (or ablate FAB with fixed λ).
  - Hyperparameter tuning: IBP’s α is fixed to 3, which can bias K upward; some tuning or cross-validation would be fairer. MEIBP requires careful initialization; reporting sensitivity analyses would help.
  - Omitted baselines: ARD-type Bayesian PCA/FA and spike-and-slab ARD IBP variants are relevant for automatic dimension selection. While not strictly identical, they address the same problem and would contextualize the benefits of FAB’s “prior-free” selection.
  - Reporting: More comprehensive runtime breakdowns and sensitivity to Tshrink, initialization, and δ would strengthen the empirical case. Some datasets omit methods due to runtime; providing partial results or early-stop performance curves would be informative.
- Identifiability and merging: Theorem 4 treats the exact equality w·k = w·l. In practice, near-duplicates are more common; a principled criterion (e.g., bound on L increase under merge as a function of ||w·k−w·l||) would be stronger than a heuristic Euclidean threshold. Also, describe the computational complexity of the merge step for large K.

Quality
- The technical direction is sound and grounded in asymptotic Bayesian analysis for singular models (Watanabe), but the paper relies too heavily on the supplement for critical details. Tightening assumptions and including a proof sketch of Lemma 1 would materially improve rigor.
- The algorithmic developments are reasonable; however, guarantees under acceleration are not fully established in the main text.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but marred by typos/notational inconsistencies and missing definitions (e.g., dimensionalities DZ, Dk, |P|, and constants in Eq. (10)). The paper would benefit from a careful pass to standardize notation and to explicitly connect each term in the bound to its origin.
- Provide a concise summary table of the objective, updates, and guarantees.

Originality
- Extending FAB to LFMs and showing the FIC form carries over despite non-block-diagonal Hessians is original and interesting. The CCCP view for acceleration is a nice twist, albeit CCCP is known; the identifiability angle is a useful contribution.

Significance
- If the theoretical claim holds under broad conditions, this offers a practical, hyperparameter-light alternative to IBP variants for feature selection, with clear advantages in speed and automation. The work is likely to be used and extended.

Questions for authors
1. Precisely state the assumptions for Lemma 1 (on Z, Λ, W, and growth of K with N) and include a brief proof sketch in the main paper. Does the result require limited overlap between features or bounded condition number of Z⊤Z?
2. Do you learn λ in FAB while fixing it for other methods? If so, please either fix λ for FAB or enable noise learning in baselines; include an ablation.
3. Can you provide a monotonicity argument for accelerateShrinkage, or else always perform it in a way that guarantees non-decrease of L?
4. How sensitive are results to Tshrink, initialization of µ, and the merging threshold? Any failure cases?
5. How does FAB behave when N is small relative to D and K? Can you quantify when the FIC approximation becomes unreliable?

Minor comments
- Fix typos/equation inconsistencies (Eq. (6), (8), (10)); define |P| and all dimensionalities clearly.
- Clarify computational complexity per iteration and the cost saved by early shrinkage.
- Cite ARD/variational Bayesian FA/PPCA dimensions selection baselines more explicitly.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying Lemma 1 assumptions/proof and addressing fairness in the experimental setup. Confidence: High.
