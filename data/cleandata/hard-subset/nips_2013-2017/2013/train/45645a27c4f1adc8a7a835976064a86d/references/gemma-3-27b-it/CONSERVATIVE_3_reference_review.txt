This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The authors address a limitation of previous FAB work – its applicability only to models with a block-diagonal Hessian – and demonstrate that LFMs, despite not directly satisfying this condition, can be handled within the FAB framework. They provide theoretical justification via Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, propose a shrinkage acceleration method based on a connection to the CCCP, and discuss parameter identifiability. 

The core idea of extending FAB to LFMs is a reasonable one, given the success of FAB with mixture models. However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the theoretical justifications *appear* sound (though a deeper dive by an expert in asymptotic analysis would be needed to fully confirm), the presentation relies heavily on stating results (Lemmas, Theorems) without sufficient intuition for *why* these results hold, especially for readers unfamiliar with the intricacies of the initial FAB formulation. The leap from block-diagonal Hessian requirements in MMs to applicability to LFMs feels hand-waved; while Lemma 1 establishes a mathematical equivalence, a more intuitive explanation of *why* this equivalence arises would be beneficial.

A significant weakness lies in the experimental validation. The experiments, while numerous, lack a sufficiently rigorous comparison to well-established methods beyond simply stating performance numbers. The comparison to IBP, VB, and MEIBP feels superficial. While the authors note the sensitivity of MEIBP parameter settings, the justification for the chosen parameters (e.g., α=3 for IBP) is weak.  Furthermore, the claim of superior performance for FAB/LFM rests largely on the artificial simulation results, which, while demonstrating computational speedup with the shrinkage acceleration, do not convincingly show a substantial advantage in model selection accuracy *compared to properly tuned baselines*. The "block data" experiment is visually compelling, but doesn’t quantify the performance improvement beyond subjective observation. The real-world data experiments are hampered by the arbitrary stopping criterion of 50 hours for some methods, creating an uneven playing field.  The observed advantages in PLL for FAB on the real datasets should be interpreted cautiously, given this uneven comparison. 

The proposed shrinkage acceleration is a potentially valuable contribution, and the connection to CCCP is interesting. However, the benefits seem primarily computational, with limited discussion of its impact on the quality of the resulting model. The identifiability discussion and merge post-processing are welcome additions but feel somewhat tacked on and lack substantial experimental validation. The claim that FAB inherently mitigates overfitting is asserted but not definitively demonstrated.

**Strengths:**

*   Extends the FAB framework to a new class of models (LFMs).
*   Proposes a computational acceleration method (shrinkage) based on a connection to CCCP.
*   Addresses the issue of parameter identifiability.
*   Demonstrates computational speedup in simulations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Theoretical justification lacks sufficient intuition.
*   Experimental validation is insufficient and lacks a fair comparison to established baselines.
*   Performance gains are not convincingly demonstrated, especially on real-world datasets.
*   Presentation is dense and relies heavily on mathematical statements without sufficient explanation.
*   The “why” behind the extension to LFMs isn’t clearly articulated.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound extension of a promising inference method.
*   Potential for computational efficiency gains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient experimental validation to support claims of superiority.
*   Lack of clarity in the theoretical exposition.
*   The paper feels incomplete; more rigorous comparisons and analysis are needed.
*   The significance of the extension is not fully demonstrated.



**Overall Recommendation:** **Reject**. 

While the paper presents a potentially interesting extension of FAB inference, the lack of rigorous experimental validation and the unclear presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The authors need to provide a more convincing demonstration of the benefits of FAB/LFM compared to established methods and significantly improve the clarity and accessibility of the theoretical exposition.  A substantial revision, with a focus on comprehensive and fair experimental evaluation, is required.
