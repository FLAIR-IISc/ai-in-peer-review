

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

## Summary
This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), claiming that FAB/LFMs maintain the same theoretical properties as FAB for Mixture Models despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for FAB in previous work. The authors derive a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs that has the same form as for MMs, propose an algorithm with shrinkage acceleration, and provide theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability. The experimental section compares FAB/LFMs with IBP, VB, MEIBP, and variational EM on artificial, block, and real-world data.

## Strengths
1. The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is an interesting contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature.
2. The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) appears to be a practical contribution that significantly reduces computational cost.
3. The experimental results generally support the claims of the paper, particularly regarding computational efficiency and model selection accuracy.
4. The paper provides a good comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including recent IBP approaches.

## Weaknesses
The paper contains several technical and presentation issues that undermine its credibility and reproducibility:

1. **Critical Theoretical Omissions**: Lemma 1 states "log det |F(d)| = ∑k log ∑n znk/N + Op(1)" without defining F(d) explicitly. The paper mentions "under some mild assumptions" but fails to state these assumptions in the main text, making it impossible to assess the scope of the result. The term "bounded" in Theorem 2 is undefined (bounded in probability? almost surely?).

2. **Missing Algorithm Details**: Algorithm 1 states "Update P" in step 3 but provides no details on how P is updated. The paper claims the FAB M-step is equivalent to the EM M-step for LFMs but doesn't provide the explicit update equations for W, Λ, and b, which are essential for reproducibility.

3. **Typographical and Formatting Errors**: The paper contains multiple typos ("mean-filed" instead of "mean-field") and inconsistent notation (e.g., "logN" vs "log N"). The paper also incorrectly states "FAB inference does not have a hand-tunable hyper-parameter" despite introducing π̃k as a linearization parameter and Tshrink as a hyperparameter.

4. **Inaccurate Claims**: The paper claims FAB/LFMs "have parameter identifiability" but Theorem 4 only provides a sufficient condition for identifiability, not a proof that the model is identifiable. The statement about MAD-Bayes being "based on a limiting case that the noise variance goes to zero, which yields a prior-derived regularization term" is misleading—the regularization term is not prior-derived.

5. **Experimental Issues**: The paper states "α, a hyperparameter of IBP, was set to 3, which might cause overestimation of K" but fails to discuss the sensitivity of results to this choice. Figure 1 claims "our shrinkage acceleration significantly reduced computational cost" without providing numerical evidence of the speedup.

6. **Theoretical Ambiguities**: The paper states "FAB inference maximizes a computationally tractable lower bound of a 'factorized information criterion' (FIC) which converges to a marginal log-likelihood for a large sample limit" but doesn't specify the sense of convergence (e.g., in probability, almost surely, or in expectation).

7. **Incomplete Justification**: In the FAB M-step, the paper states "For π̃ and π, we obtain the following solutions: πk = π̃k = ∑n µnk/N" without providing any derivation or justification for this solution.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical extension of FAB inference to LFMs, the numerous technical omissions, ambiguities, and presentation errors significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The theoretical claims are not sufficiently precise, and the algorithm description lacks critical details needed for reproducibility. The paper's claims about "automatic" model selection and "no hand-tunable hyper-parameters" are misleading given the introduction of π̃k and Tshrink.

The paper would be much stronger if it addressed these issues, particularly by:
1. Precisely defining all technical terms and assumptions
2. Providing complete algorithm details
3. Correcting the misleading claims about hyper-parameters
4. Providing numerical evidence for computational speedup claims

Given the stringent standards of NeurIPS, these issues are substantial enough to recommend rejection, even though the core idea has potential value. The paper appears to be a promising but incomplete contribution that requires significant revision before it can be considered for publication. The minor flaws accumulate to the point where the paper's technical contribution is difficult to evaluate and reproduce, which is a critical requirement for NeurIPS submissions.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains multiple critical technical omissions and presentation errors that prevent a fair assessment of its contribution. While the core idea is interesting, the lack of precision in theoretical claims and algorithmic details significantly undermines the paper's credibility.
