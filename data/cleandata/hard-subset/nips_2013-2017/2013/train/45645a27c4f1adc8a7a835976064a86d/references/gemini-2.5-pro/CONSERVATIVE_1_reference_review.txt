This paper proposes an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a recent method for approximate Bayesian model selection that relies on maximizing a lower bound of a "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC), which asymptotically converges to the marginal log-likelihood. Prior work demonstrated its utility for mixture models, but was limited to models where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. The primary contribution of this work is to generalize FAB to LFMs, a model class that does not satisfy this condition. The authors achieve this through an asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) showing that the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs yields a penalty term of the same form as that for mixture models. This allows the derivation of a similar FIC and a corresponding optimization algorithm, FAB/LFM. The paper further analyzes the algorithm through a CCCP interpretation, leading to a shrinkage acceleration technique, and discusses parameter identifiability, which motivates a feature-merging post-processing step. The method is evaluated against several established non-parametric Bayesian methods for LFMs, such as those based on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP).

**Strengths:**
1.  The paper addresses a clear and important problem: extending a computationally efficient model selection framework to a broader and more complex class of models (LFMs). The motivation is well-grounded in the high computational cost of existing non-parametric Bayesian methods.
2.  The central theoretical result (Lemma 1), which shows that the FIC penalty term for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models despite structural differences, is elegant and non-trivial. If correct, it suggests a potentially broader applicability of the FAB framework.
3.  The work demonstrates good methodological rigor. The analysis connecting the FAB E-step to a CCCP procedure provides a solid justification for the proposed shrinkage acceleration. Furthermore, the discussion of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) and the resulting merge procedure shows careful consideration of a classic problem in latent variable modeling.
4.  The empirical evaluation is reasonably thorough. The authors compare FAB/LFM against several appropriate and state-of-the-art baselines, including Gibbs sampling for IBP, a variational IBP variant, and the more recent MEIBP. The experiments cover model selection accuracy, feature quality, predictive performance, and computational cost across multiple datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The primary concern with this work is its heavy reliance on asymptotic theory. The entire framework is justified in the large-sample limit (N → ∞). While asymptotic analysis is a powerful tool, its practical validity for finite datasets, especially those of moderate or small size, is not guaranteed. The paper itself notes that FAB may perform worse than non-parametric Bayesian methods in small-N scenarios (e.g., the Libras dataset), which significantly caveats the claims of superiority. The method's robustness in non-asymptotic regimes remains an open question.
2.  The central theoretical claim, Lemma 1, is presented without its underlying assumptions or a proof sketch in the main paper; these are deferred to the supplementary material. A result that so conveniently recovers the desired penalty term warrants careful scrutiny. Without its justification being readily available, it is difficult to fully assess the technical soundness and the conditions under which this critical approximation holds.
3.  While the experiments are generally well-designed, the comparison to baseline methods could be more rigorous. For instance, the authors fix the noise variance for all baselines while it is learned by FAB/LFM. This may confer an unfair advantage to the proposed method. Similarly, hyperparameters for the IBP-based methods (e.g., α) are set to default values, with the admission that this might lead to suboptimal performance (overestimation of K). A more comprehensive comparison would involve tuning the baselines to ensure they are performing optimally.
4.  The core FAB methodology is itself a "recently-developed" approach. While the paper successfully extends this framework, it is promoting an approach that has not been as extensively vetted by the community as the established MCMC and variational inference techniques it is compared against. Endorsing a novel extension of a novel method requires an exceptionally high bar of evidence, which is only partially met here due to the aforementioned concerns about asymptotics and experimental fairness.

**Summary and Recommendation:**
This paper presents a well-executed and interesting piece of research. It extends a novel inference framework to an important class of models, supported by non-trivial theoretical analysis and promising empirical results. The proposed algorithm appears to be computationally efficient and effective at model selection, particularly on large datasets.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the method's fundamental reliance on large-sample asymptotics, which may limit its applicability and reliability in practice. The experimental comparison, while good, could be strengthened to more convincingly demonstrate superiority over well-established and tuned baselines. Given that the core FAB framework is itself not yet a standard, established method, a cautious and critical perspective is warranted.

Overall, the paper makes a solid contribution to the literature on approximate Bayesian inference. The ideas are novel and significant enough to be of interest to the NIPS community. While I harbor reservations about the practical scope of the asymptotic claims, the methodological development and initial results are strong enough to merit acceptance, contingent on the soundness of the proofs provided in the supplementary material.
