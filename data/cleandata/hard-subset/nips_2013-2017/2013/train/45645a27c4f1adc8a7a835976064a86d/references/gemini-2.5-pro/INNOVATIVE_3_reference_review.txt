**Summary:**
This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference, a method for automated model selection, to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core problem is that the original derivation of FAB for models like Mixture Models (MMs) relied on a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete log-likelihood, an assumption violated by LFMs where parameters are shared across features. The central and most significant contribution of this paper is a novel asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) which demonstrates that, despite this structural difference, the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs decomposes into a form analogous to that of MMs. This key theoretical result allows the authors to derive a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs that includes the same `log(sum_n z_nk)` complexity penalty term responsible for FAB's automatic model shrinkage.

Building on this foundation, the paper develops a practical FAB/LFM algorithm. It further provides two insightful analyses: 1) an interpretation of the E-step updates as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), which inspires a shrinkage acceleration technique to speed up computation, and 2) a proof that FAB resolves the parameter identifiability problem common in LFMs, which naturally suggests a feature merging post-processing step. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that FAB/LFM is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods like IBP and MEIBP in terms of model selection accuracy and predictive performance, often at a reduced computational cost.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution is the theoretical derivation that extends the FAB framework beyond its original, restrictive assumptions. The result in Lemma 1 is elegant, non-obvious, and conceptually important. It suggests that the "model-induced regularization" of FAB might be a more general principle for discrete latent variable models than previously understood. This is not an incremental application of an existing method but a significant theoretical step that broadens its scope. This kind of bold, foundational work is precisely what NIPS should encourage.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The paper is well-supported by theory beyond the main derivation. The CCCP interpretation is an insightful connection that not only deepens understanding but also leads directly to a practical algorithmic improvement (shrinkage acceleration). Furthermore, the analysis of parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) is a very strong point. It addresses a known theoretical weakness of LFMs and shows how the FAB objective function inherently favors identifiable solutions, providing a principled justification for the proposed merging step.

3.  **Excellent Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. The comparison against strong and relevant baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP) is thorough. The results convincingly show the practical benefits of the proposed method: accurate recovery of the true number of features, strong predictive performance, and clear evidence of mitigating overfitting (the small gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods). The demonstration of the shrinkage acceleration's effectiveness is also a nice practical result.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the technical challenge is explicitly stated, and the key theoretical result is highlighted before being used to derive the final algorithm. The structure, moving from theory to algorithm to analysis and refinement, is logical and effective.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature:** The method is, by definition, based on an asymptotic approximation. The authors are commendably honest about this limitation, noting in the experiments that performance can degrade in small-N scenarios (e.g., the Libras dataset). While this is an inherent property of the framework, it does define the regime where the method is most applicable. This is an acceptable trade-off for the benefits gained, but it is a limitation nonetheless.

2.  **Reliance on Supplementary Material:** The proofs for the cornerstone results (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) are relegated to the supplementary materials. While this is standard practice, the entire paper's validity hinges on their correctness. Assuming the proofs are sound, this is not a major issue, but it's worth noting.

3.  **Introduction of a New Hyperparameter:** The shrinkage acceleration method introduces a new parameter, `Tshrink`. While the paper claims it was not sensitive, the introduction of any new tunable parameter slightly undermines the "fully automated" narrative of the FAB framework. This is a minor point, as the benefits of acceleration likely outweigh this small inconvenience.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a significant and non-trivial theoretical extension of the FAB inference framework, a novel contribution in its own right.
*   The approach solves a key problem in LFMs—automatic selection of feature dimensionality—without resorting to computationally expensive sampling or complex priors.
*   The theoretical analysis is deep, providing a new CCCP interpretation and a solution to the long-standing parameter identifiability problem.
*   The proposed algorithm is practically effective, demonstrating strong performance against state-of-the-art methods on a variety of datasets.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The method's guarantees are asymptotic, and its performance may be suboptimal compared to non-parametric Bayesian methods on smaller datasets.
*   The core theoretical proofs are not in the main body of the paper.

**Recommendation:**
This paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. Its primary strength lies in its bold and elegant theoretical contribution, which significantly expands the applicability of the FAB framework. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a conceptual advance. The authors back up their theory with insightful analysis and a robust set of experiments that demonstrate clear practical benefits. The weaknesses are minor and largely inherent to the asymptotic approach chosen. This work is novel, significant, and likely to inspire follow-up research into the broader applicability of asymptotic Bayesian methods. I recommend **Accept**.
