This paper introduces an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core idea of FAB is to perform model selection by maximizing a tractable lower bound on the "Factorized Information Criterion" (FIC), an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. While previous work applied FAB to models like mixture models where the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood is block-diagonal, LFMs violate this assumption due to shared parameters (the basis matrix W).

The main technical contribution of this paper is to show that despite this structural difference, the log-determinant of the relevant Hessian for LFMs can be asymptotically approximated by a term of the form `sum_k log(N_k)`, where `N_k` is the number of data points using feature `k`. This is a non-trivial and elegant result (Lemma 1) that allows the authors to derive an FIC for LFMs that has the same penalization structure as the one for mixture models. Based on this FIC, the paper derives an iterative algorithm (FAB/LFM) that alternates between an E-step (updating feature assignments) and an M-step (updating model parameters).

Furthermore, the paper provides two valuable analyses and refinements: 1) It interprets the E-step updates as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), which inspires a "shrinkage acceleration" method to more quickly prune irrelevant features. 2) It analyzes the parameter identifiability of the model under the FAB objective (Theorem 4), showing that the FAB regularizer pushes the model towards a unique solution, and this insight justifies a post-processing step to merge redundant features. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that FAB/LFM is competitive with or outperforms state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian methods (like those based on the Indian Buffet Process) in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—extending the FAB framework to models with shared parameters like LFMs—is significant. The derivation in Lemma 1 is the key insight and is not obvious. It potentially unlocks the application of this efficient, prior-free (asymptotically) model selection framework to a much broader class of latent variable models. This work provides a valuable bridge between the theoretical literature on singular learning theory/asymptotic Bayes and practical machine learning algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development appears solid. The asymptotic analysis of the Hessian is the paper's cornerstone. The subsequent connection to CCCP is insightful and leads to a practical algorithmic improvement. The identifiability analysis is another strong point, providing theoretical grounding for a common problem in LFMs and motivating a principled merge procedure, which is often done heuristically.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts by clearly stating the problem (the limitation of existing FAB methods), presents the core theoretical result, derives the algorithm, analyzes its properties, and validates it experimentally. The narrative is easy to follow.

4.  **Empirical Soundness:** The experiments are comprehensive, comparing FAB/LFM against relevant and strong baselines (IBP Gibbs sampling, VB-IBP, MEIBP). The results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method, particularly in its ability to learn compact and accurate models with lower computational cost and without overfitting, as evidenced by the small gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods. The shrinkage acceleration is also shown to be highly effective.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Reliance on Supplementary Material:** The main paper's claims hinge critically on Lemma 1, but its assumptions and proof are entirely deferred to the supplement. While this is common due to page limits, stating the "mild assumptions" in the main text is crucial for the reader to assess the result's scope and validity. A brief sketch of the proof idea would also significantly strengthen the paper's self-containedness.

2.  **Experimental Details:**
    *   The comparison to baselines could be made fairer. The authors state that the noise variance was fixed for IBP, VB, and MEIBP (following their original papers) but learned within FAB/LFM. Learning the noise can provide a significant advantage in fitting the data. It would be more convincing if all methods were allowed to learn the noise variance or if a justification for this discrepancy was provided.
    *   The choice of hyperparameters for baselines (e.g., IBP alpha=3) can heavily influence their performance. The authors note this might cause overestimation of K. While exhaustive hyperparameter search is costly, acknowledging this and perhaps showing results for another alpha value would add robustness to the claims.

3.  **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper claims FAB is "without priors (i.e., it asymptotically ignores priors)". However, the update equation (11) includes the prior term `η(πk)`. While the FAB regularization term `−D/2Nπ̃k` dominates for large N, the prior is still present and influential, especially in the small N regime. This "prior-free" aspect should be stated with more nuance. The single case on the Libras dataset where FAB performed worse is mentioned briefly at the very end; a more thorough discussion of the regimes where the asymptotic approximation might be less accurate and NPB methods might be superior would be valuable.

4.  **Clarity of Merging Procedure:** The merge post-processing step is an interesting idea motivated by the identifiability analysis. However, its details are relegated to the supplement. Given its importance in the final algorithm (Algorithm 1), a more detailed description in the main text of how features are determined to be "overlapping" and how the decision to merge is made (e.g., based on the increase in `L`) would improve reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. It successfully extends an interesting and practical inference framework (FAB) to a widely used class of models (LFMs). The work is theoretically sound, methodologically novel, and backed by solid empirical evidence. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to presentation and experimental details that could be clarified or improved in a final version. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to be of high interest to researchers working on Bayesian methods, latent variable models, and automated model selection.
