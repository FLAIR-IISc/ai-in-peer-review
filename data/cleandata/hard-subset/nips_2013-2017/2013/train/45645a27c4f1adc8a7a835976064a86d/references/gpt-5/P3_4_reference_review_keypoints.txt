- Extends FAB to latent feature models (LFMs) with linear-Gaussian likelihood and Bernoulli activations; Lemma 1 yields an asymptotic Hessian term leading to an FIC identical in form to mixture models.
- Derives a tractable lower bound and an EM-like FAB algorithm with mean-field q(Z); E-step includes an explicit shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k) that prunes features automatically.
- Recasts the E-step as CCCP on a convex-concave objective and proposes a shrinkage-acceleration scheme; adds a merging post-process based on overlapping W columns for identifiability.
- Experiments on synthetic, block-pixel, and real datasets show FAB often recovers true K, attains strong predictive log-likelihood, and has competitive or better runtime versus EM, IBP (Gibbs), VB, and MEIBP.
- Novelty: extending FIC/FAB to LFMs where the complete-data Hessian is not block-diagonal is a nontrivial theoretical contribution.
- Relates to prior work on IBP, MAD-Bayes, CCCP/variational optimization, and singular learning theory (Watanabe).
- Strength: surprising theoretical insight that LFMs inherit the same FIC penalty as MMs, broadening FAB’s applicability.
- Strength: clean, interpretable algorithm that selects K without hand-tuned priors/hyperparameters.
- Strength: CCCP interpretation is insightful and the acceleration yields practical runtime improvements.
- Strength: identifiability analysis clarifies preference for parsimonious ML-equivalent solutions and motivates the merge heuristic.
- Strength: empirical results show good model selection and compact models with strong predictive performance.
- Weakness: Lemma 1 is presented without intuition or proof sketch and lacks explicit assumptions (e.g., conditions on Z'Z/N, feature usage, Λ non-degeneracy, K scaling).
- Weakness: Monotonicity concerns—Algorithm 2’s inner CCCP steps and simultaneous π/π̃ updates may not provably increase the same bound; need precise statement or proof.
- Weakness: Potentially unfair comparisons since FAB learns noise Λ while some baselines fix noise and IBP α is fixed at 3; need sensitivity analyses and matched baselines.
- Weakness: Merge step is heuristic; sensitivity to threshold and impact on results not analyzed—require ablation with/without merge and counts of merges.
- Weakness: No runtime complexity analysis; must state per-iteration costs in N, D, K and clarify how Tshrink affects extra E-steps vs earlier pruning.
- Clarity: Equations mix constants and dimensionality loosely (e.g., “−|P|”, “−2D + K log N”); standardize Laplace constants and separate asymptotically negligible terms.
- Clarity: Precisely define |P|, parameter grouping per θd, and whether Λ is strictly diagonal and required for Lemma 1 in the main text.
- Clarity: Move key CCCP derivation steps (e.g., Eq.13→14) into the main text with a short derivation for readability.
- Originality: Main theoretical results (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) are novel; CCCP view and acceleration are incremental but useful; identifiability/merge are practical additions.
- Significance: If assumptions hold broadly, method is a compelling IBP alternative with automatic K selection and efficiency; scope currently limited to Gaussian/diagonal noise and mean-field q(Z).
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical extension, simple effective algorithm with principled shrinkage and acceleration, clear empirical gains.
- Cons for acceptance: deferred assumptions/proofs, experimental fairness issues, lack of rigorous guarantees for accelerated CCCP, limited scope beyond Gaussian/diagonal models.
- Key questions for authors: state exact assumptions for Lemma 1 and K-N scaling; prove or bound monotonicity of accelerateShrinkage; report sensitivity to initialization and Tshrink; provide results with fixed noise or let baselines learn noise; discuss failure modes with collinearity or extreme ∑n µnk.
- Minor comments: define all symbols at first use (|P|, θd), clarify π̃=π conditions, and consider comparing to variational IBP variants that learn noise variance.
