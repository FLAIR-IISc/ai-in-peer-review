Summary
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture models and HMMs to latent feature models (LFMs) with binary features and linear-Gaussian likelihoods. The key theoretical contribution (Lemma 1) shows that, asymptotically, the log-determinant of the complete-data Hessian for each observed dimension contributes a term of the form sum_k log sum_n z_nk, even though LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition used previously for FAB in mixtures. This yields an FIC for LFMs (Theorem 2) that has the same functional form as for mixtures, leading to an automated feature selection penalty that scales as O(∑_k log ∑_n z_nk). A practical lower bound is optimized with mean-field q(Z), linearization of the concave penalty, and standard M-steps for W, Λ, b; the resulting E-step update for µ_nk contains an FAB-induced shrinkage term D/(2Nπ̃_k) that prunes irrelevant features without priors. The authors reinterpret the E-step updates as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), propose “accelerateShrinkage” with multiple CCCP steps per iteration, and analyze identifiability, showing FAB resolves non-identifiability among duplicate features and motivates a merge post-processing. Experiments on synthetic data, block images, and several real datasets compare FAB to IBP (fast Gibbs), VB, and MEIBP, showing stronger model selection and predictive performance for FAB with competitive or lower runtime (vs IBP/VB; MEIBP is sometimes faster but less accurate).

Relation to prior work
The work is grounded in prior FAB for mixtures/HMMs [Fujimaki et al.], IBP and its accelerations (Doshi-Velez & Ghahramani), variational IBP [Doshi-Velez et al.], and MEIBP [Reed & Ghahramani]. It also connects to singular learning theory (Watanabe) and CCCP. A related asymptotic perspective (MAD-Bayes) is discussed, but FAB operates under large-sample asymptotics rather than vanishing-noise limits.

Strengths
- Quality: The asymptotic analysis is novel and insightful: despite LFMs’ shared parameters violating previous conditions, the FIC retains the crucial log-activation penalty that enables automatic model selection and regularization. The CCCP interpretation is technically sound and leads to a practical speedup. The identifiability result is useful and links to a simple merge heuristic. The algorithm monotonically increases a tractable lower bound, and pruning/merging is carefully justified by bound increases.
- Originality: Extending FAB to LFMs with a rigorous asymptotic argument is new. The connection to CCCP for accelerating shrinkage and the identifiability analysis for LFMs in the FAB framework are also novel.
- Significance: Automated, prior-free model selection for LFMs is valuable, offering a deterministic alternative to IBP with fewer hyperparameters. The penalty structure provides principled regularization and tends to select compact models. Results indicate improved model selection and predictive performance over strong baselines.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, with algorithms and update equations clearly stated; proofs are deferred to the supplement.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions of Lemma 1: The main result hinges on “mild assumptions” relegated to the supplement. For reproducibility and to assess scope, please state in the main text the essential conditions (e.g., moments, scaling of K with N, regularity/non-degeneracy of Λ, boundedness conditions) under which log det F(d) admits the stated decomposition. How sensitive is the result to correlated noise (non-diagonal Λ) or non-Gaussian likelihoods?
- Bound monotonicity with acceleration: While coordinate ascent updates for µ increase L and M-steps are standard, accelerateShrinkage takes multiple CCCP steps with updates to π and π̃ inside. Please clarify whether each step provably increases L, or whether you accept occasional non-monotonic steps for speed. If the latter, clarify the global monotonicity guarantee across iterations.
- Mean-field approximation: The additional mean-field restriction on q(Z) breaks the tightness in Eq.(1). Some discussion of the empirical impact on the quality of the FIC approximation (e.g., comparisons with structured q on small problems) would strengthen the claims.
- Merging operation: The description “w*_·k = 2w*_·k and µ*_·k = µ*_·k + µ*_·l” appears inconsistent with preserving the expected reconstruction w(µ_k+µ_l). Please clarify the exact merge rule and how the bound is recomputed to ensure improvement.
- Experimental fairness and reporting:
  - Noise variance: For IBP/VB/MEIBP, the noise SD is fixed. Is FAB allowed to learn λ while competitors are fixed? If so, this likely favors FAB on PLL. Consider fixing λ across all methods or reporting both variants.
  - Initialization: Starting with K = min(N, D) may heavily burden some baselines; please justify or test sensitivity to initial K for FAB and MEIBP.
  - VB baseline: You report finite-K VB; the IBP variational method [3] supports infinite features—please clarify which variant you used and, if finite, why.
  - Runtime scaling: Provide per-iteration complexity and wall-clock scaling with N, D, and K, and an ablation of Tshrink to quantify the CCCP speedup vs accuracy.
- Scope limits: FAB’s asymptotics may be less accurate for small N; you note worse performance on Libras. It would help to characterize when FAB is preferable over NPB (e.g., N/D regimes, signal-to-noise).

Pros
- Strong, novel asymptotic analysis yielding an FIC for LFMs with the same beneficial structure as for mixtures
- Deterministic, hyperparameter-free model selection with effective automatic shrinkage
- CCCP-based acceleration with substantial empirical speedups
- Identifiability analysis and practical merge heuristic
- Solid empirical results on model selection and predictive performance

Cons
- Key assumptions for the main lemma are not explicit in the main text; limited discussion of scope/limitations
- Some ambiguities in merging formulation and in monotonicity guarantees under acceleration
- Potential fairness issues in experiments (noise variance, initialization), and missing complexity analysis
- Limited evidence on small-N regimes and on non-Gaussian LFMs

Suggestions for improvement
- Move the core assumptions of Lemma 1/Theorem 2 to the main text and add a brief intuition/proof sketch.
- Clarify the merge operation and provide a bound-change calculation.
- Add complexity analysis and sensitivity studies for Tshrink, initial K, and λ treatment across methods.
- If feasible, add a small-N experiment and a non-Gaussian LFM case (e.g., Bernoulli/Poisson observations).
- Release code to aid reproducibility.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a technically solid and original contribution with promising practical impact. Despite some clarifications needed (assumptions, merging, fairness), I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the above points.
