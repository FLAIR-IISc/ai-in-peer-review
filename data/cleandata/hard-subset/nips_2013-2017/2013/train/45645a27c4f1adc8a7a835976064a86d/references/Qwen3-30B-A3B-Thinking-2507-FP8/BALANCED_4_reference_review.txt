

# Review for "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), demonstrating that the FAB framework can be applied to LFMs despite their failure to satisfy the specific Hessian condition required by previous FAB work for mixture models. The authors prove that LFMs have the same FIC (Factorized Information Criterion) form as mixture models, enabling automatic model selection without hyperparameters or priors. They develop an efficient FAB/LFM algorithm with a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP interpretation, and provide theoretical insights into parameter identifiability and post-processing.

## Quality
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by showing that LFMs, despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by previous FAB work, still yield the same FIC form as mixture models (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2). The theoretical analysis is sound, with rigorous proofs provided in supplementary materials. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing FAB/LFMs with multiple relevant baselines (IBP, VB, MEIBP, EM) across artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets. The results consistently demonstrate FAB's advantages in computational efficiency (with acceleration), model selection accuracy, and predictive performance. A notable strength is the empirical validation of the theoretical insights about shrinkage acceleration and identifiability.

However, the theoretical justification for the key assumption in Lemma 1 could be strengthened. The paper states "under some mild assumptions" but doesn't clearly articulate what these are or why they're reasonable for LFMs. The connection between the FIC approximation and the marginal likelihood (Corollary 3) is important but could be better motivated.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to algorithmic development to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The connection between FAB and CCCP (Section 3.1) is particularly well-explained, making the theoretical insights accessible. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are clear and precise. The supplementary materials appear to contain necessary technical details for reproducibility, though the main paper could benefit from more concise presentation of key assumptions.

## Originality
This work makes a meaningful contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, a class of models that was previously outside FAB's applicability. The key insight that LFMs yield the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition is novel and significant. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work: it's not just a combination of existing techniques but demonstrates a new theoretical connection that enables FAB to be applied to a broader class of models. The shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP is also a novel contribution to the practical implementation of FAB.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important problem in model selection for latent variable models, where automatic selection of latent dimensionality is crucial for practical applications. The FAB/LFM approach offers a compelling alternative to existing methods (VB, IBP) by providing fully automated model selection with lower computational cost. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements in both computational efficiency and model selection accuracy across multiple datasets. The theoretical insights about identifiability and the merge post-processing step provide valuable guidance for practitioners working with LFMs.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, with strong empirical validation of the approach. The theoretical insights about the FIC form for LFMs and the connection to CCCP are particularly noteworthy. While the paper could strengthen its theoretical justification for key assumptions, the overall contribution is significant and well-supported by experiments.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs with rigorous proofs
- Practical algorithm with significant computational advantages
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets
- Theoretical insights into identifiability and model shrinkage

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Some theoretical assumptions require more justification
- The paper could better address why FAB is preferable to MEIBP in cases where MEIBP is faster
- Limited discussion of limitations (e.g., sensitivity to initial conditions)

## Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is significant, and the practical algorithm with shrinkage acceleration offers clear advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written, well-structured, and thoroughly evaluated. While the theoretical justification for some assumptions could be strengthened, the contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Expand the discussion of the assumptions in Lemma 1 and provide more intuition for why they're reasonable for LFMs.
2. Include a brief analysis of FAB's sensitivity to initialization (e.g., how the initial number of latent features affects convergence).
3. Clarify the trade-offs between FAB and MEIBP more thoroughly, particularly why FAB's model selection accuracy is superior despite MEIBP's speed advantage in some cases.
4. Consider adding a short discussion of how the approach might extend to other latent variable models beyond LFMs.

This paper represents a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian model selection, with both theoretical and practical significance. The authors have made a meaningful advance in understanding and applying FAB inference to a broader class of models.
