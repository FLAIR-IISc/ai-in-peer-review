This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a model selection framework, previously applied to models like Mixture Models (Fujimaki et al., 2011), that relies on a specific block-diagonal structure of the complete log-likelihood's Hessian matrix. The core contribution of this work is to demonstrate, through asymptotic analysis, that even though LFMs do not satisfy this structural condition, the resulting Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) takes on the same functional form as for mixture models. This insight allows the authors to derive a FAB algorithm for LFMs that performs automatic model selection (i.e., determines the number of features) without relying on priors, similar to how Indian Buffet Process (IBP) models do. The paper also provides valuable analysis, interpreting the E-step as a convex-concave procedure to develop a practical acceleration technique and proving a parameter identifiability result that motivates a feature-merging post-processing step.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main theoretical result (Lemma 1) is novel and impactful. By showing that the FIC framework extends beyond its original, more restrictive assumptions, the authors substantially broaden the applicability of FAB inference to a widely used class of models. This provides a compelling, computationally efficient alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods for LFMs. The derived algorithm is fully automated and does not require hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-motivated and the algorithmic refinements, such as the shrinkage acceleration and merging post-processing, are grounded in solid analysis. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed FAB/LFM against several state-of-the-art baselines (IBP, VB-IBP, MEIBP) on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The results convincingly demonstrate that FAB/LFM achieves an excellent balance of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational speed.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It effectively communicates the limitations of prior FAB methods, presents its theoretical and algorithmic contributions logically, and provides a clear interpretation of the results.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**
*   **Clarity of Assumptions:** The assumptions underlying the crucial Lemma 1 are deferred entirely to the supplementary materials. While common, a brief characterization of these "mild assumptions" in the main text would improve the paper's self-containedness and allow for a more thorough evaluation of the central claim's robustness.
*   **Computational Speed:** While the method is generally fast, the experiments show that MEIBP can be faster in some cases. The primary advantage of FAB/LFM over the fastest baseline appears to be superior predictive accuracy and model compactness, rather than a universal speed-up.
