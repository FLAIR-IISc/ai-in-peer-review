This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a limitation of the original FAB framework that required a block diagonal Hessian matrix. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite not initially satisfying this condition, exhibit a similar FIC structure to Mixture Models, enabling the application of FAB. They provide theoretical analysis, including identifiability results, and introduce a shrinkage acceleration method to improve computational efficiency. The experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods like IBP and VB, particularly in terms of model selection and computational speed.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous theoretical justification for extending FAB to LFMs. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the proof of identifiability are significant contributions. The connection to the Convex Concave Procedure (CCCP) and the proposed shrinkage acceleration are well-motivated and appear effective. The empirical evaluation is thorough, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, and comparisons with relevant baselines. However, the discussion around the limitations of the Laplace approximation and the conditions under which the derived FIC is valid could be more detailed. The complexity of the mathematical derivations may require careful reading, and the supplementary material is crucial for a complete understanding. Overall, it's a complete piece of work, carefully evaluating both strengths and weaknesses in the experimental setup.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and the proposed solution. The theoretical derivations are presented in a logical order, though as mentioned above, they can be dense. The notation is mostly consistent, but some clarification could be helpful (e.g., explicitly defining all variables in equations before use). The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented clearly. The inclusion of the algorithm descriptions in the main text is helpful. However, a more intuitive explanation of the “FIC” and its relationship to the marginal log-likelihood might benefit readers less familiar with the field.

**Originality:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs is a novel contribution, as it addresses a known limitation of the framework. The analysis demonstrating the shared FIC structure between LFMs and MMs is also original. The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, inspired by the CCCP interpretation, adds to the novelty of the work. The authors adequately cite related work, including comparisons to IBP, VB, and MEIBP. A scan of NIPS proceedings from 2013-2017 doesn't reveal direct overlap with this work; while there’s a body of work on IBP and VB for LFMs, this paper provides a unique perspective through the lens of FAB inference.

**Significance:** The results are significant, as they provide a more computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian non-parametric methods for LFM model selection. The automatic hidden state selection and parameter identifiability properties of FAB/LFMs are valuable. The ability to address large-scale data, as demonstrated in the experiments, is a notable advantage. The theoretical insights regarding FIC and identifiability contribute to a deeper understanding of Bayesian inference in latent variable models. The paper addresses a difficult problem (model selection in LFMs) in a potentially better way than previous research, and the unique theoretical and pragmatic approach has the potential to be adopted by other researchers in the field.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Extends FAB inference to a broader class of models (LFMs).
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Rigorous derivation of FIC for LFMs and identifiability results.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Shrinkage acceleration improves performance compared to existing methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Competitive results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   **Significance:** Provides a potentially more scalable and automated approach to LFM model selection.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** Theoretical derivations can be dense and challenging to follow.
*   **Laplace Approximation:** Discussion of the limitations of the Laplace approximation could be more detailed.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of parameter settings (e.g., δ, Tshrink) on performance could be explored further.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed method is novel and efficient, and the experimental results are compelling. While the paper has some minor areas for improvement, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and detail concerns raised above.
