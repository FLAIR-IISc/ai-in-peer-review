This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The main goal is to perform automatic model selection, i.e., determining the number of latent features, in a computationally efficient manner. The FAB framework, previously applied to models like Mixture Models (MMs), relies on a specific block-diagonal structure of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood, which LFMs do not possess. The core contribution of this work is a novel asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1) showing that despite this structural difference, the log-determinant of the Hessian for LFMs yields a model complexity term of the form `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)`. This is the same form as in MMs and is crucial for deriving a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs. This criterion approximates the marginal log-likelihood and includes a regularization term that naturally encourages shrinkage of irrelevant features.

Based on this theoretical result, the authors derive an EM-like algorithm to maximize a tractable lower bound of the FIC. The update equations for the latent variable responsibilities include a unique regularization term that is responsible for the automatic model selection. The paper further provides two valuable refinements: an interpretation of the update step as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), which inspires a practical acceleration technique to speed up feature pruning, and a theoretical analysis of parameter identifiability, which motivates a post-processing step to merge redundant features. This work positions itself as an alternative to non-parametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), aiming to achieve similar model selection capabilities with greater computational efficiency and fewer tunable hyperparameters.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant and non-trivial extension of the FAB framework. Generalizing FAB to models that do not satisfy the original block-diagonal Hessian assumption is a strong contribution that broadens the applicability of this inference paradigm. The key insight in Lemma 1, demonstrating that the crucial `log(sum z_nk)` penalty term emerges naturally in LFMs, is both elegant and impactful. This work provides a compelling frequentist-flavored Bayesian alternative to the dominant NPB methods for model selection in LFMs.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The asymptotic analysis appears solid, and the derivation of the FIC and the corresponding optimization algorithm is clear and well-motivated. The analysis in Section 3, connecting the algorithm to CCCP and addressing the important issue of parameter identifiability, adds depth and practical value to the work. Theorem 4 provides a strong theoretical justification for the model's behavior and the proposed merging post-processing step.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly state the problem, outline the limitations of prior work, and logically build their solution from the key theoretical result (Lemma 1) to the final algorithm and its refinements. The connection between the theoretical derivation and the practical algorithm's feature-shrinking behavior is explained effectively.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against relevant and state-of-the-art baselines from the IBP literature (fast Gibbs sampling, VB, MEIBP). The experiments on both synthetic and a wide range of real-world datasets demonstrate that FAB/LFM is not only computationally faster but also often achieves better or comparable predictive performance while discovering more parsimonious models (smaller K). The observation that FAB/LFM exhibits a smaller gap between training and predictive log-likelihood strongly supports the claim that its "model-induced regularization" is effective at preventing overfitting.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Asymptotic Assumptions:** The entire framework is predicated on asymptotic behavior (N → ∞). While the empirical results are strong even on datasets of moderate size, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations when N is small. The single case mentioned (Libras dataset) where NPB methods performed better hints at this, but a deeper analysis of when the FIC approximation might break down would be valuable.

2.  **Clarity on Theoretical Assumptions:** Lemma 1 is the cornerstone of the paper, but its "mild assumptions" are deferred to the supplementary material. For such a critical result, it would significantly improve the self-containedness and credibility of the main paper to briefly state the nature of these assumptions (e.g., related to the distribution of Z, properties of W, etc.).

3.  **Baseline Comparison:** The paper rightly positions itself against IBP. However, a comparison against a simpler and widely used baseline, such as training a standard LFM with EM and selecting K via the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), would be very informative. The paper notes that without Lemma 1, the criterion would resemble BIC. A direct empirical comparison would have explicitly demonstrated the practical advantage of the more refined `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)` penalty over BIC's cruder `K log(N)` penalty.

4.  **Details of Algorithm Components:** The `accelerateShrinkage` routine introduces a new parameter, `Tshrink`, which is set to 100. There is no discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance or runtime to this choice. Similarly, the `merge()` post-processing step is an important practical component, but its implementation details are relegated to the supplement. A brief description in the main text of how features are determined to be "overlapping" would improve clarity and reproducibility.

**Arguments Pro/Con Acceptance**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Significant and novel theoretical contribution in extending FAB to LFMs.
    *   Provides a principled, computationally efficient, and effective method for automatic model selection in a popular model class.
    *   Strong and comprehensive empirical results demonstrating superiority over state-of-the-art NPB methods in many scenarios.
    *   The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and of high quality.

*   **Con:**
    *   The reliance on asymptotic theory may limit applicability in small-data regimes, a point that could be discussed more thoroughly.
    *   Some key details (assumptions for Lemma 1, implementation of merging) are in the supplement, slightly reducing the self-containedness of the main paper.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. It successfully bridges a gap by extending the FAB framework to the important class of latent feature models. The theoretical insights are strong, the resulting algorithm is practical and efficient, and the empirical evidence is compelling. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to clarifying details that do not undermine the core contribution. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance, and is very likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.
