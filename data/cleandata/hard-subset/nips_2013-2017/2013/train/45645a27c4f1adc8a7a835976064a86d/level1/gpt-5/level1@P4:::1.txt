Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs). The central technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood for LFMs, yielding a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) that has the same functional form as in mixture models: a complexity penalty with terms of the form (D/2)∑k log(∑n znk) plus model-size terms. This is nontrivial because LFMs violate prior assumptions used for mixtures, namely block-diagonal structure and per-component sample counting. The authors formalize this in Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 and show that the resulting FIC is asymptotically equivalent to the marginal log-likelihood up to Op(1).

They derive a practical FAB algorithm for LFMs by lower-bounding the FIC via linearization of the concave log-count term and a mean-field q(Z)=∏n∏k Bern(µnk). This leads to E-step fixed-point updates with a characteristic shrinkage term −D/(2Nπ̃k) that drives automatic feature pruning, and closed-form M-step updates akin to PPCA. They reinterpret the E-step as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), use multiple CCCP inner iterations to accelerate shrinkage, and propose a post-hoc merge step motivated by an identifiability analysis (Theorem 4), which shows FAB favors balanced solutions when duplicated columns in W occur.

Empirically, the method is compared with IBP (Gibbs), variational IBP, MEIBP, and a variational EM baseline, on synthetic, “block” images, and several real datasets, evaluating model selection, predictive log-likelihood, and runtime. FAB generally achieves competitive or better prediction with more compact K and favorable computational cost; MEIBP can be faster but often yields worse prediction.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The asymptotic derivation is the core: Lemma 1 asserts that log det|F(d)| decomposes into ∑k log(∑n znk) + Op(1), restoring the key log-count term despite parameter sharing in LFMs. This result is plausible and aligns with the intuition that effective Fisher information for each feature scales with its activation count; however, the main paper defers assumptions and proofs to the supplement. Bringing at least the main conditions (e.g., regularity around the MLE, scaling of counts, boundedness) into the paper would strengthen confidence.
- The lower-bound optimization and updates are standard and appear correct. The linearization yielding Eq. (4) and the coordinate ascent in µ ensure monotonic improvement of the bound L; the CCCP interpretation is consistent with the form in Eq. (13)-(14).
- The shrinkage acceleration executes multiple CCCP steps per outer iteration. While each µ update increases the linearized objective, a formal monotonicity guarantee of the original lower bound across the accelerateShrinkage routine would be useful, especially when jointly updating π and π̃ inside the loop.
- The identifiability result (Theorem 4) addresses a real issue in LFMs; the conditions and conclusion are reasonable. The merge heuristic is coherent with the bound but depends on practical thresholds; guarantees that merging increases L are only asserted when W columns are equal, which is rare in practice.
- Experiments are solid but contain confounding factors: baselines that do not learn noise variance are fixed (to true or recommended values), whereas FAB learns λ; IBP hyperparameters (e.g., α=3) may bias K; VB is omitted on larger data due to runtime; MEIBP omitted on negative data. These choices are understandable but should be discussed as limitations.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The derivation flow from FIC to the practical FAB algorithm is easy to follow. The CCCP perspective and algorithms are described succinctly. However:
- The “mild assumptions” for Lemma 1 and the boundedness requirement in Theorem 2 should be summarized in the main text.
- A brief per-iteration complexity analysis (in N, D, K) and memory requirements would improve clarity on scalability.
- The merge post-processing could benefit from a more explicit description of the distance criterion and its effect on the bound.

Originality:
Extending FAB to LFMs and showing that the FIC retains the same structure as in mixtures, despite parameter sharing, is a novel and insightful contribution. The CCCP-based acceleration for shrinkage and the identifiability-driven merge step add practical value. The work is distinct from MAD-Bayes (asymptotics in small-noise limit) and from MEIBP (submodular MAP), and complements prior FAB work on mixtures and HMMs.

Significance:
Automated model selection for LFMs with asymptotic justification and practical scalability is important. The results suggest that FAB/LFM offers a compelling alternative to IBP-family methods with competitive predictive performance, compact representations, and reduced overfitting. The general message that FIC-like penalties extend beyond mixtures could influence future work on broader latent variable models.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical contribution: asymptotic FIC for LFMs with the key log-count penalty recovered.
- Practical, principled algorithm with automatic feature selection and monotonic bound optimization.
- CCCP interpretation and acceleration leading to tangible runtime gains.
- Identifiability analysis and merge heuristic addressing redundancy.
- Empirical results showing good model selection and prediction with competitive or better runtimes.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Critical assumptions and proofs for Lemma 1/Theorem 2 are deferred; lack of a concise statement in the main text weakens accessibility and verification.
- Experimental comparisons have fairness caveats (noise variance treatment, hyperparameter choices, omissions for some baselines); more controlled ablations would strengthen claims.
- Monotonicity guarantees for the accelerated routine are not fully formalized.
- Merge post-processing lacks a formal criterion guaranteeing improvement except in degenerate equal-W cases.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Summarize the precise assumptions underlying Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 in the main paper, including rates and regularity conditions.
- Provide a per-iteration complexity analysis and wall-clock scaling plots vs N, D, K.
- Add an experiment where all methods learn or estimate noise variance (or fix FAB’s λ to the same value) to control for that factor.
- Report sensitivity to IBP hyperparameters (e.g., α) and to initial K for MEIBP; include a runtime vs performance trade-off curve.
- Include an ablation of the accelerateShrinkage and merge steps to quantify their contributions and, if possible, a statement/lemma about monotonicity for the accelerated scheme.
- Clarify the merge criterion and show the change in the bound before/after merges on real datasets.

Overall, this is a technically solid and potentially impactful contribution that extends the FAB framework to an important class of models with both theoretical and practical advances.
