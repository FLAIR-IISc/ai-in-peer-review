- Extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), enabling automatic selection of latent features.
- Key theoretical insight (Lemma 1): asymptotic form of log determinant of the Hessian yields an FIC for LFMs with the same functional form as for Mixture Models.
- Derives a practical EM-like FAB algorithm for LFMs that optimizes this FIC and performs model selection automatically.
- Overcomes the previous obstacle of Hessian block-diagonality not holding in LFMs by a different asymptotic analysis.
- Interprets the E-step as a CCCP procedure and introduces a "shrinkage acceleration" technique for faster feature pruning.
- Provides parameter identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) and a merge post-processing step to address feature redundancy.
- Empirical evaluation compares FAB/LFM to IBP variants (Gibbs, VB, MEIBP) on synthetic and real data, showing competitive or superior model selection, predictive performance, and speed.
- Presents FAB/LFM as a computationally efficient, fully automated alternative to nonparametric Bayesian methods like the IBP, avoiding tuning of IBP hyperparameters (e.g., alpha).
- Theoretical derivation is sound and plausible; Lemma 1 is the central technical hurdle with full proof in supplement.
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with effective figures, tables, and explanations of algorithm components.
- Contributions are highly original: extends FAB to a previously intractable model class and yields a novel data-dependent regularizer from first principles.
- The complexity term sum_k log(sum_n z_nk) arising despite parameter sharing is a potentially generalizable insight to other latent models.
- Limitation: method is asymptotic and can degrade in small-N scenarios; authors acknowledge and demonstrate this in experiments.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theory, strong empirical results, practical automation, high quality and clarity.
- Argument against acceptance: asymptotic guarantees may be less favorable on very small datasets compared to carefully tuned nonparametric methods.
