Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture and HMM models to latent feature models (LFMs). The core technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood that yields Lemma 1: log det|F(d)| ≈ ∑k log(∑n znk) up to Op(1), despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian assumptions used in prior FAB for mixture models. This leads to a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs (Theorem 2) whose penalty has exactly the same form as for mixtures: a global O(DK log N) term and a feature-wise penalty of (D/2)∑k log(∑n znk). Consequently, FAB/LFM inherits automatic feature-shrinkage, asymptotic prior-ignorance, and a monotone lower-bound optimization procedure. The authors further show a CCCP interpretation that motivates a shrinkage-acceleration routine, and provide an identifiability result (Theorem 4) that justifies merging nearly duplicate features post hoc. Experiments compare FAB/LFM to IBP (Gibbs), variational IBP, MEIBP, and a variational EM baseline, showing improved model selection, competitive or better prediction, and substantial computational savings versus IBP/VB, with MEIBP sometimes faster but typically less accurate.

This work connects to a line of asymptotic Bayesian approximations for model selection (BIC; Watanabe’s singular learning theory) and builds on previous FAB for mixtures/HMMs. On the nonparametric side, it targets the same use case as IBP and MEIBP for automated discovery of latent features, but without priors/hyperparameters.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation of an LFM FIC with the same form as in mixtures is nontrivial and hinges on Lemma 1 and Theorem 2. The main paper clearly states the result, while proofs and assumptions are deferred to the supplement. The use of Op(1) approximations and the boundedness condition in Theorem 2 look reasonable in the large-N regime commonly assumed for FIC-style criteria. However, the main text calls the assumptions “mild” without stating them; it would strengthen the contribution to list key assumptions (e.g., conditions ensuring nonsingularity near the MLE and behavior when features are rare) in the main paper and discuss their implications when some ∑n znk are small.
- Algorithm: The lower-bound optimization, mean-field factorization over Z, and alternation with M-steps are standard and yield a tractable procedure. The CCCP-based acceleration is well motivated: linearizing the convex penalty in ∑n µnk explains the iterative shrinkage behavior and enables earlier pruning.
- Guarantees: Monotone increase of the lower bound is argued by standard variational reasoning and coordinate ascent (and consistent with FAB/MM). The identifiability theorem is useful: FAB selects balanced solutions when duplicate features exist, and motivates a merge step that provably improves the bound when features coincide.
- Empirics: The experimental suite includes synthetic recovery, the “blocks” benchmark, and several real datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS, YaleB, Libras). FAB/LFM consistently finds compact K and strong predictive log-likelihoods, and the acceleration reduces runtime markedly. That said, some choices may bias comparisons: (i) competitors’ software did not learn noise variance; the paper says “we fixed it to 1 (or 0.75)”, but it is unclear whether FAB also fixed λ or learned it. Please clarify and, ideally, match this choice across methods (either fix λ for all, or enable noise learning for all). (ii) IBP’s α was left at default (3), which can overestimate K; a sensitivity analysis over α would be more convincing. (iii) MEIBP is sometimes omitted or given tuned initial K; a more systematic setup would help. The authors do note that FAB can underperform NPB methods for small N (e.g., Libras), which is fair.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with helpful algorithm boxes and a clear narrative from theory to algorithm to acceleration to experiments. Notation is consistent. Areas to improve:
- State the key assumptions of Lemma 1 in the main text and discuss their practical scope (e.g., feature sparsity/degeneracy and the need to prune features with ∑n µnk near 0 or N).
- Provide computational complexity per iteration and memory costs in terms of N, D, K, and the number of CCCP steps.
- Briefly specify the merge() routine and the criterion used to accept merges (currently deferred to the supplement).

Originality
The central theoretical insight—that the FIC penalty for LFMs matches mixtures despite non-block-diagonal Hessians—is novel and interesting. The CCCP interpretation of the E-step and the resulting acceleration are neat and practically relevant. The identifiability analysis and merge heuristic are also useful additions.

Significance
LFMs are widely used; automatic model selection without priors/hyperparameters is attractive. A principled, fast alternative to IBP/VB with strong empirical performance will be of interest to the NIPS community. The work advances both the theoretical understanding of asymptotic Bayes for singular models and provides a practical algorithm that appears competitive on real datasets.

Pros
- Clear, principled extension of FAB to LFMs with a novel determinant asymptotics result (Lemma 1).
- Same FIC form as mixtures leads to automatic feature shrinkage and asymptotic prior-ignorance.
- CCCP-based acceleration substantially reduces runtime; monotone lower-bound optimization.
- Identifiability analysis and merge post-processing improve practical solutions.
- Solid empirical results: accurate K selection, strong prediction, and good efficiency vs IBP/VB.

Cons
- Assumptions underlying Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are not stated in the main paper; practical conditions for validity need clearer discussion.
- Experimental fairness needs clarification: noise variance handling, IBP hyperparameter sensitivity, and MEIBP setup.
- Limited analysis of finite-sample behavior; FIC may be less accurate for small N, as observed.

Suggestions
- Include a concise statement of Lemma 1’s assumptions in the main text and a short discussion of edge cases (very sparse or ubiquitous features).
- Clarify whether FAB fixed or learned noise variance in each experiment; consider harmonizing with baselines or running ablations.
- Add runtime and space complexity and a brief description of merge() in the main paper.
- Provide sensitivity plots for IBP’s α and for Tshrink.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a strong paper with a substantive theoretical contribution and a practical algorithm that compares favorably to established NPB methods. Despite minor clarity and fairness issues, I lean toward acceptance.
