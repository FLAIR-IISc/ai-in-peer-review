

# Peer Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), which previously couldn't be handled by FAB due to a requirement for a block-diagonal Hessian matrix. The authors make a significant theoretical contribution by showing that LFMs satisfy the asymptotic conditions for FIC derivation despite not meeting the standard block-diagonal condition. Their key insight is that the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs naturally contains the log(∑_n z_nk) term that appears in mixture models, leading to an identical FIC form. This theoretical extension enables FAB/LFMs to automatically select feature dimensionality without hyperparameters, offering advantages over state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods.

The paper builds on prior FAB work for mixture models (Fujimaki & Morinaga, NIPS 2013) and extends it to LFMs, which have been primarily handled by non-parametric Bayesian methods like IBP (Griffiths & Ghahramani, NIPS 2005) and variational inference (Doshi-Velez et al., AISTATS 2009). The authors also connect to related work on identifiability (Watanabe, Neural Computation 2009) and CCCP (Yuille & Rangarajan, Neural Computation 2001), though these connections could be strengthened.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical contribution is substantial and well-executed. The derivation of Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 is rigorous, and the asymptotic analysis demonstrates how LFMs can be handled by FAB despite not meeting the standard conditions.
2. The practical contribution of shrinkage acceleration via CCCP interpretation (Algorithm 2) is novel and significantly reduces computational cost without sacrificing performance (Figure 1).
3. The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) provides valuable theoretical insights and a practical merge post-processing step that improves model compactness.
4. The experimental evaluation comprehensively compares FAB/LFMs with IBP, VB, MEIBP, and EM across artificial data, block data, and real-world datasets. FAB consistently achieves better model selection (smaller K), better predictive performance (higher PLL), and lower computational cost.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of the asymptotic approximation for smaller datasets. The authors note that "for large N, both [FAB and EM] ideally achieve the maximum likelihood" but provide no analysis of when the approximation breaks down (e.g., for N < 1000). This is critical for practical applications.
2. The comparison with variational methods is limited. The authors omit VB due to computational cost, but this is a significant omission given that VB is a standard baseline for LFMs. The paper claims FAB outperforms "state-of-the-art IBP methods," but doesn't clearly position itself against the full spectrum of VB approaches.
3. The connection to singular model theory (Watanabe's work) is underdeveloped. The paper mentions "model induced regularization" but doesn't fully integrate with the broader literature on singular models, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
4. The paper doesn't adequately discuss the trade-offs between FAB and other methods. While FAB achieves better model selection, it's not clear if it's universally better at prediction (e.g., Libras dataset shows FAB underperforms for small N).

**Significance and Originality:**
The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, which is a non-trivial extension given the different model structure. The key insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as mixture models despite different Hessian properties is novel and valuable. The practical contributions (shrinkage acceleration, merge post-processing) provide real-world applicability. The results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with practical implications for latent feature modeling. While the asymptotic assumptions limit applicability to small datasets, the theoretical insights and practical improvements are significant. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and the experiments are thorough.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs
- Rigorous asymptotic analysis showing identical FIC form to mixture models
- Practical shrinkage acceleration method with significant computational benefits
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating clear advantages

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Limited analysis of asymptotic approximation limitations for small N
- Incomplete comparison with variational inference baselines
- Underdeveloped connections to singular model theory
- Insufficient discussion of trade-offs with other methods

The paper represents a meaningful contribution to Bayesian inference for latent variable models. While the asymptotic assumptions present some limitations, the theoretical insights and practical improvements are substantial. The paper should be accepted as it advances the state of the art in model selection for latent feature models with both theoretical rigor and practical benefits.
