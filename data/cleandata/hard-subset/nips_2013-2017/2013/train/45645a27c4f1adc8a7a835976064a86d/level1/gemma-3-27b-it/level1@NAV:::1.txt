## Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a limitation of the original method which required a block-diagonal Hessian of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite not initially satisfying this condition, exhibit a similar factorized information criterion (FIC) structure to Mixture Models (MMs). They then develop a FAB algorithm for LFMs, including an acceleration step leveraging a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and prove parameter identifiability.  The work builds upon recent advances in asymptotic Bayesian inference [5, 6] and aims to offer a computationally efficient alternative to existing methods like Indian Buffet Processes (IBPs) [10, 11] for model selection in LFMs [9, 17].

The paper's strength lies in its theoretical contribution – successfully extending the FAB framework to a broader class of models. The analysis of the Hessian matrix (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is well-motivated and crucial for establishing the applicability of FAB to LFMs. The inclusion of identifiability proofs (Theorem 4) and the derivation of a practical acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) further strengthens the work. The experimental evaluation, while limited, provides initial evidence of FAB/LFM’s competitive performance against established methods across synthetic and real-world datasets. The clarity of writing is good, with a logical flow and clear explanations of the mathematical derivations.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The experimental section, while thorough in comparing to relevant baselines, lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning for all methods. The impact of the shrinkage acceleration is demonstrated effectively, but a more in-depth ablation study could clarify its specific benefits. Moreover, the comparison with MEIBP [22] feels somewhat constrained by the difficulties encountered with its parameter sensitivity and computational cost, potentially downplaying its relative advantages in certain scenarios. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the FIC approximation for small N, the implications of this on the performance of FAB in such regimes are not fully explored.  The discussion on the merging post-processing is brief and lacks concrete examples illustrating its practical effect.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of FAB inference to LFMs, expanding the applicability of this promising technique.
*   Solid theoretical grounding with clear mathematical derivations and identifiability guarantees.
*   Demonstrated performance competitive with state-of-the-art methods.
*   Practical acceleration technique significantly reduces computational cost.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and include a more detailed hyperparameter analysis.
*   Limited discussion of the FIC approximation's validity in low-sample regimes.
*   Insufficient details regarding the merging post-processing step.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, demonstrating a theoretically sound and practically useful extension of FAB inference. While some aspects of the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the paper meets the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
