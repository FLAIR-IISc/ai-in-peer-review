This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a method for approximate Bayesian inference and model selection that optimizes a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which is an asymptotic approximation of the marginal log-likelihood. Previous work on FAB (e.g., for Mixture Models) relied on a specific block-diagonal structure of the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood, a condition not met by LFMs due to parameter sharing across features. The core contribution of this paper is a theoretical analysis showing that, despite this structural difference, the FIC for LFMs takes on the same functional form as that for Mixture Models. This key insight allows the authors to derive a practical EM-like algorithm for LFMs that automatically selects the number of latent features. The work positions FAB/LFM as a computationally efficient alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), which have been the dominant approach for model selection in LFMs.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The central theoretical claim rests on Lemma 1, which provides an asymptotic form for the log determinant of the Hessian matrix. While the full proof is in the supplementary material, the result is plausible and its derivation appears to be the paper's key technical hurdle. The subsequent derivation of the FIC and the FAB algorithm is sound and follows logically from this lemma.

The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors don't just present their algorithm; they provide deeper analysis in Section 3. The interpretation of the E-step as a CCCP procedure is insightful and leads to a practical "shrinkage acceleration" method. Furthermore, the discussion on parameter identifiability (Theorem 4) and the resulting merge post-processing step demonstrates a thorough treatment of the problem, addressing a known issue in latent variable models. The empirical evaluation is strong, comparing against multiple state-of-the-art IBP variants (Gibbs sampling, VB, and MAP-based MEIBP) across synthetic and real-world datasets. The results consistently show that FAB/LFM is competitive or superior in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational speed. The authors are also honest about the method's limitations, noting that as an asymptotic method, its performance may degrade in small-N scenarios, which they observed in one dataset.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is established clearly in the introduction, framing the work as an important extension of the FAB framework. Section 2 systematically builds from the key theoretical result (Lemma 1) to the final algorithm, making the logic easy to follow. The connection to the FIC for Mixture Models is used effectively to ground the new derivation in familiar territory. The update equations and the overall algorithm are presented clearly. The purpose of each component of the algorithm, particularly the "model induced regularization" term, is explained well. The figures and tables are informative and effectively summarize the experimental results.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While FAB inference and LFMs existed previously, the application of FAB to LFMs was considered an open problem due to the unmet Hessian condition. The paper's main contribution—showing that an analogous FIC can be derived for LFMs via a different asymptotic analysis—is a novel and significant theoretical insight. This broadens the scope of the FAB framework substantially. The proposed method provides a fundamentally different approach to model selection in LFMs compared to the prevailing IBP-based methods. While the final algorithm resembles variational EM, the objective function it optimizes is novel and derived from first principles of asymptotic Bayesian inference, leading to a different, data-dependent regularization term. The CCCP-based acceleration and the identifiability analysis are also original contributions that improve the practicality and theoretical understanding of the method.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant for several reasons.
1.  It provides a new, practical, and highly effective tool for learning the structure of LFMs. LFMs are a fundamental class of models used in numerous applications, and determining the number of features is a critical and difficult problem.
2.  It presents a compelling and computationally efficient alternative to non-parametric Bayesian methods like the IBP. The experiments show that FAB/LFM can achieve better model selection and predictive performance in a fraction of the time required by IBP samplers or VB approximations.
3.  The method has desirable practical properties: it is fully automated, avoiding the need to set and tune hyperparameters like the IBP's concentration parameter (`alpha`), which can be difficult in practice.
4.  The underlying theoretical insight—that the `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)` complexity term arises even in models with shared parameters—may be generalizable to other latent variable models beyond LFMs, paving the way for future work in this direction. Researchers and practitioners working with latent feature models are very likely to find this method useful and build upon it.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a significant and non-obvious theoretical result (Lemma 1) that extends the applicability of the FAB framework to the important class of Latent Feature Models.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is shown to outperform or be highly competitive with state-of-the-art methods (IBP variants) in terms of model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.
*   **Practicality and Automation:** The method is fully automated and avoids sensitive hyperparameters, making it a practical tool for real-world applications.
*   **High Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound, clearly written, well-organized, and presents a complete piece of work including theoretical analysis, algorithm development, and thorough experimentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method's guarantees are asymptotic, and its performance relative to carefully tuned NPB methods might be less favorable on datasets with very small sample sizes (N). However, the authors acknowledge this limitation, and the experiments show strong performance across a range of dataset sizes.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and latent variable modeling. It solves an open problem with a novel theoretical insight and delivers a practical, high-performing algorithm. It clearly meets the standards for publication at NIPS.
