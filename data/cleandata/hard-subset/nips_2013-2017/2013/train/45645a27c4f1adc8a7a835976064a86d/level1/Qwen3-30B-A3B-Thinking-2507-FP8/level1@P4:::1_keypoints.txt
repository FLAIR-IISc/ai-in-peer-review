- Extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs) despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition.
- Proves via Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 that LFMs yield the same factorized information criterion (FIC) form as mixture models.
- Shows FIC converges to the marginal log-likelihood (Corollary 3) and establishes parameter identifiability (Theorem 4).
- Develops a practical FAB/LFM algorithm (Algorithm 1) with a shrinkage-acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) based on CCCP.
- Shrinkage acceleration eliminates irrelevant features early, greatly reducing computational cost while preserving objective value.
- Empirical evaluation across synthetic, block, and real datasets shows FAB/LFM outperforms IBP, MEIBP, and other baselines in model selection, predictive performance, and efficiency.
- FAB’s regularization mitigates overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL gaps) and tends to prefer smaller-K solutions compared to EM.
- Identifiability analysis informs a practical merge post-processing step and explains method behavior.
- Key theoretical proofs and technical details are relegated to the supplementary material, making the main text less self-contained.
- Variational Bayes (VB) is omitted from comparisons due to computational cost, weakening completeness of empirical evaluation.
- The paper does not fully analyze boundary conditions where Lemma 1’s assumptions may fail (e.g., extreme sparsity or non-Gaussian noise).
- Pro: Broadens FAB applicability to LFMs with rigorous theoretical support and automated model selection without hyperparameters.
- Pro: Introduces an efficient, practically accelerated algorithm that maintains performance while lowering computation.
- Pro: Comprehensive empirical results demonstrate consistent advantages over state-of-the-art methods.
- Con: Reliance on supplementary proofs and omission of VB comparisons limit the paper’s self-containment and empirical completeness.
- Overall: Strong, novel contribution with solid theory, practical algorithmic advances, and convincing empirical support—suitable for NIPS.
