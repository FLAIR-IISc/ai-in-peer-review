This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a key limitation of the original method – its applicability only to models satisfying a specific Hessian matrix condition. The authors demonstrate that LFMs, despite not satisfying this condition, exhibit a similar factorized information criterion (FIC) structure as mixture models, enabling the application of FAB. They further enhance the practical utility of FAB/LFMs through a shrinkage acceleration method based on a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) analysis and prove parameter identifiability, offering a basis for merging latent features.

The core contribution – extending FAB to LFMs – is compelling and addresses a notable gap in the literature. The theoretical analysis establishing the FIC form for LFMs is well-motivated and rigorously presented (with details relegated to the supplementary material, as appropriate). The connection to CCCP is insightful, leading to a practical algorithmic improvement that demonstrably speeds up computation. The identifiability proof is a valuable addition, offering theoretical grounding for the merging post-processing step. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets, and demonstrates the effectiveness of FAB/LFMs compared to several baselines, including IBP-based methods and VB.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. While the paper highlights the advantages of FAB/LFMs, a more thorough discussion of the limitations is needed. For example, the scalability to extremely high-dimensional data (where the Hessian computation itself becomes a bottleneck) isn’t fully addressed. The statement that the priors are “asymptotically ignored” is somewhat imprecise; acknowledging the role of the prior in influencing initial stages of learning, even if diminished with larger datasets, would be beneficial. 

The comparison to existing methods feels somewhat uneven. While the authors acknowledge the sensitivity of MEIBP to initial parameter settings, more discussion of the parameter tuning process for *all* methods (including FAB's hyperparameters, even if minimal) would enhance reproducibility and comparability. The choice of the noise standard deviation for the real-world data (0.75) feels somewhat arbitrary; a justification or sensitivity analysis would be valuable. The focus on PLL (predictive log-likelihood) is good, but including metrics like AUC or error rate, particularly for the classification tasks, would provide a more complete picture of performance. The authors state that VB was omitted due to long computational time, but a brief justification for *why* it's significantly slower than FAB/LFMs (beyond just stating it is) would be helpful.

The presentation, while generally clear, could benefit from streamlining. The repetition of ideas across sections (e.g., the description of the FIC) feels redundant. The notation is mostly consistent, but occasionally a bit dense.  The merge post-processing algorithm, while theoretically sound, lacks detailed explanation of how the Euclidean distance threshold is chosen. A visual illustration of the feature merging process would be helpful.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extends FAB inference to a broader class of models (LFMs).
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  Provides a rigorous theoretical justification for applying FAB to LFMs.
* **Practical Improvement:** Introduces a shrinkage acceleration method based on CCCP analysis.
* **Identifiability:** Proves parameter identifiability and leverages it for feature merging.
* **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates competitive performance on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Scalability:** Doesn’t fully address scalability to very high dimensions.
* **Imprecise Language:**  “Asymptotically ignoring priors” could be clarified.
* **Uneven Comparison:** Parameter tuning details for all methods are lacking.
* **Limited Metrics:** Primarily relies on PLL; additional metrics would be informative.
* **Presentation:** Some redundancy and density in the presentation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of FAB inference and extends its applicability to LFMs, a practically relevant class of models.
* The theoretical analysis is sound and provides valuable insights into the properties of FAB/LFMs.
* The practical improvements (shrinkage acceleration, feature merging) enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of the method.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines.
* The work is likely to be influential in the field of Bayesian nonparametrics and latent variable modeling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The discussion of limitations is limited, and the scalability concerns are not fully addressed.
* The comparison to existing methods could be more thorough and transparent.
* The presentation could be streamlined and clarified.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The extension of FAB inference to LFMs is a valuable advancement, and the theoretical and practical improvements presented are well-motivated and supported by empirical evidence. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly concerning a more detailed discussion of limitations and scalability, a more thorough comparison to baselines, and streamlining the presentation.
