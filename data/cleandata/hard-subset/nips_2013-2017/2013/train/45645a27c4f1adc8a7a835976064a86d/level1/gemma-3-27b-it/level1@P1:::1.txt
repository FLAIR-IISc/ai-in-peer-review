This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), overcoming a previously known limitation of FAB requiring a block-diagonal Hessian matrix. The authors demonstrate, through asymptotic analysis, that LFMs possess a similar FIC structure to mixture models, enabling the application of FAB. They further contribute analytical results regarding parameter identifiability and propose a shrinkage acceleration method based on a connection to the convex-concave procedure (CCCP). Experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets showcases the effectiveness of FAB/LFMs in terms of computational efficiency, model selection, and prediction accuracy, outperforming existing methods like IBP and VB in several scenarios.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper correctly positions FAB within the landscape of Bayesian inference methods, referencing key works on mixture models [5, 6], infinite latent feature models and the Indian Buffet Process [9, 10, 11, 16, 17, 19, 22], and variational Bayesian approaches [3]. The discussion of MAD-Bayes [1] provides appropriate context for asymptotic analyses. The connection to Laplace’s method and BIC [23, 28] is well-explained.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs and the demonstration that it has the same form as that for MMs (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) are rigorous and well-motivated. The analysis connecting the FAB E-step to CCCP is insightful, and the subsequent shrinkage acceleration is a valuable contribution.  The identifiability argument (Theorem 4) is also well-presented. The experimental results are comprehensive, including comparisons to several strong baselines on both artificial and real-world datasets. The authors acknowledge the limitations of the BIC approximation for large N. The paper presents a complete piece of work with a clear problem statement, theoretical justification, algorithmic development, and empirical validation.  The evaluation is relatively thorough, including ablation studies (acceleration) and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods. The handling of missing data in the real-world experiments is sensible.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is mostly clear, although some equations could benefit from additional explanation or intuitive justification. The flow of ideas is logical, moving from the introduction of FAB and its limitations to the extension to LFMs and the proposed improvements. The algorithm descriptions are clear enough to be implemented, although providing pseudocode would be beneficial.  The discussion of the merge post-processing is slightly less detailed, and expanding on this aspect would improve clarity.

**Originality:** The extension of FAB to LFMs, a class of models previously inaccessible to this framework, is a novel contribution. The demonstration of the shared FIC structure between LFMs and MMs is an interesting theoretical result.  The connection between the FAB E-step and the CCCP is also a novel observation, leading to a practical acceleration technique. While the use of shrinkage and merging are not entirely new concepts in model selection, their integration within the FAB framework and theoretical justification are original. The authors appropriately reference related work and clearly delineate their contribution.

**Significance:** The results are significant. FAB/LFMs offer a compelling alternative to existing methods for latent feature modeling, particularly in scenarios where computational efficiency is critical. The automatic model selection capabilities (dimensionality of latent features) and the identifiability guarantees are important advantages. The empirical results demonstrate that FAB/LFMs can achieve comparable or superior performance to state-of-the-art methods on a range of datasets. The acceleration technique further enhances the practical applicability of the method. This work has the potential to be influential in areas such as dimensionality reduction, feature learning, and unsupervised representation learning.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Extends FAB to a new class of models, overcoming a significant limitation.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Rigorous mathematical derivations and insightful analysis.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Comprehensive experiments demonstrate effectiveness.
*   **Practical Impact:** The proposed acceleration technique improves computational efficiency.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a challenging problem in latent feature modeling with promising results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The mathematical details can be dense and challenging for readers unfamiliar with the FAB framework.
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on successes; a more detailed discussion of scenarios where FAB/LFMs might struggle would be valuable.
*   **Merge Post-processing Details:** The explanation of the merging process could be more elaborate.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and latent feature modeling. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed method is novel and practical, and the experimental results are compelling. While the paper could benefit from some minor improvements in clarity and discussion of limitations, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider adding pseudocode for the algorithm, expanding on the explanation of the merge post-processing, and including a brief discussion of scenarios where FAB/LFMs might be less effective.
