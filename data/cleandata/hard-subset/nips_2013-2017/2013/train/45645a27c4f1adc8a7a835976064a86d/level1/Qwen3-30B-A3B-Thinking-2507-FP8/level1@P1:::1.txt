

# Peer Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), which previously were not amenable to FAB due to not satisfying the required block-diagonal Hessian condition. The authors demonstrate that despite this apparent limitation, LFMs share the same FIC form as mixture models through careful asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix (Lemma 1). This theoretical insight enables them to develop FAB/LFMs, which offer automatic model selection (dimensionality), parameter identifiability, and computational efficiency. The work builds upon previous FAB applications to mixture models [5, 6] and extends the framework to a broader class of latent variable models. Related to LFMs, the paper contrasts with nonparametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [10, 11], variational Bayesian methods [3], and MEIBP [22], which have higher computational costs for large-scale applications.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 providing solid foundations for extending FAB to LFMs. The authors carefully address the Hessian condition that previously limited FAB applicability. The empirical validation across artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets is comprehensive, showing FAB/LFMs outperforming state-of-the-art methods in model selection accuracy, prediction performance, and computational efficiency. The shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) based on CCCP interpretation is particularly well-motivated and effective. The paper acknowledges limitations, noting that FIC approximation may be less accurate for small N, and appropriately compares with relevant baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The theoretical derivations are logically presented, with sufficient detail for reproduction (though some technical aspects of the Hessian analysis could be clarified). The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are precise and actionable. The experimental section clearly presents results across multiple datasets and metrics. The connection between the CCCP interpretation and the shrinkage acceleration is particularly well-explained. Minor improvements could be made to clarify the relationship between the FAB regularization term and the prior-based regularization, and to better explain the identifiability theorem (Theorem 4) in the main text.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant original contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs, a major class of models that previously couldn't be handled by this framework. The key insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying the Hessian condition is novel and theoretically interesting. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly from IBP-based approaches [10, 11] and variational methods [3]. The shrinkage acceleration technique and merge post-processing for identifiability represent valuable practical innovations that build upon the theoretical foundation.

**Significance:** FAB/LFMs address an important practical problem: efficient model selection for latent feature models without requiring prior knowledge or hyperparameter tuning. The computational efficiency gains (Figures 1-2) make the approach applicable to large-scale datasets where current state-of-the-art methods become prohibitively expensive. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages in model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational cost. The automatic feature selection property is particularly valuable for real-world applications where determining the optimal number of latent features is challenging. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient model selection method for LFMs.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
1. The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and well-supported by rigorous analysis
2. The practical contributions (shrinkage acceleration, merge post-processing) significantly improve computational efficiency
3. Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets demonstrates clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods
4. The work addresses an important practical problem in latent feature modeling with significant implications for large-scale applications

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
1. Some aspects of the theoretical development (particularly the Hessian analysis) could be made more accessible to readers
2. The paper could better articulate how FAB/LFMs relate to recent advances in latent feature modeling beyond IBP
3. The experimental section could include more detailed comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. It successfully extends the FAB framework to a broader class of models while maintaining the desirable properties of automatic model selection and computational efficiency. The theoretical insights are significant, and the practical implementation is well-justified and effective. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission, with strong theoretical foundations, clear empirical validation, and significant practical implications. I recommend acceptance with confidence. The work advances the state of the art in a meaningful way and provides valuable tools for practitioners working with latent feature models.
