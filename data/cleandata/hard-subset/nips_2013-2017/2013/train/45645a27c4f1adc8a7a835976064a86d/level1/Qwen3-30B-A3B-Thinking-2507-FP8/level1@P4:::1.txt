

# Review of "Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models"

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), which were previously outside the scope of FAB due to the requirement of a block-diagonal Hessian matrix. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying this condition, their factorized information criterion (FIC) has the same form as for mixture models through Lemma 1 and Theorem 2. This theoretical insight enables the development of FAB/LFMs, which offer automatic hidden states selection (without hyperparameters), parameter identifiability, and computational efficiency. The paper builds on prior FAB work for mixture models [5,6] and addresses limitations of existing approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [10,11,17] and variational Bayesian methods [3], which suffer from high computational costs for large-scale data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The key theoretical insight (Lemma 1) that LFMs yield the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition is significant. This broadens the applicability of FIC beyond previously considered models. The paper provides rigorous proofs (in supplementary materials) that establish FIC convergence to marginal log-likelihood (Corollary 3) and parameter identifiability (Theorem 4), which are important theoretical contributions to latent variable modeling.

2. **Practical Algorithm:** The authors develop a novel FAB/LFM algorithm (Algorithm 1) with a shrinkage acceleration technique (Algorithm 2) based on CCCP interpretation. This acceleration dramatically reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features early, while maintaining performance. The paper demonstrates this through empirical results (Figure 1), showing significant computational savings without sacrificing objective value.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental section provides thorough comparisons across artificial, block, and real-world datasets. FAB/LFMs consistently outperform state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) in model selection accuracy (Figure 2), predictive performance (Table 1), and computational efficiency. The paper correctly identifies that FAB's unique regularization mitigates overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL differences), which is particularly valuable for practitioners.

4. **Theoretical Insights:** The identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) offers a natural guide for post-processing latent features (merge step) and explains why FAB prefers smaller-K solutions compared to EM. This provides deeper theoretical understanding of the method's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Supplementary Reliance:** While the main paper presents key results, the rigorous proofs and some technical details are relegated to supplementary materials. This makes the main text less self-contained, though common in NIPS submissions.

2. **Limited Comparisons:** The paper omits VB due to computational constraints, which weakens the comparative evaluation. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a clear explanation for why VB was excluded from the comparison (other than computational cost).

3. **Boundary Condition Analysis:** The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the boundary conditions where the assumptions in Lemma 1 might not hold (e.g., for extremely sparse data or non-Gaussian noise), which would strengthen the theoretical claims.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

Pro:
- The extension of FAB to LFMs is a significant theoretical contribution that broadens the applicability of FAB inference.
- The paper provides a novel algorithm with practical acceleration that significantly reduces computational cost while maintaining performance.
- The empirical results are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent advantages over state-of-the-art methods in multiple metrics.
- The method offers fully automated model selection without hyperparameters, addressing a key limitation of existing approaches.
- The identifiability analysis provides valuable theoretical insights with practical implications for post-processing.

Con:
- Some key theoretical details are deferred to supplementary materials, reducing the main text's self-containment.
- The comparison with VB is omitted due to computational constraints, which limits the completeness of the empirical evaluation.
- The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of the method under specific conditions where the assumptions might fail.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The theoretical extension of FAB to LFMs is novel and well-supported, with rigorous proofs in the supplementary materials. The practical algorithm with shrinkage acceleration addresses a meaningful computational challenge in the field. The empirical evaluation across multiple datasets demonstrates clear advantages in both performance and efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with good organization, significantly extends previous work (beyond what's in NIPS 2013-2017 proceedings), and offers important advances in model selection for latent feature models with clear practical implications.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a significant problem in machine learning with a novel solution that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The theoretical insights and practical contributions provide a strong foundation for future research in Bayesian inference for latent variable models.
