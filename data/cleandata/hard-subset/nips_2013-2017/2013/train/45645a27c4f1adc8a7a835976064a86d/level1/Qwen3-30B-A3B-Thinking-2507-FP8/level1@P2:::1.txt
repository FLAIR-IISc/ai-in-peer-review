

# Review: Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian Inference for Latent Feature Models

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a significant contribution as previous FAB work was limited to models satisfying specific conditions on the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying these conditions (specifically, the Hessian not being block diagonal), the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models. This insight enables the application of FAB inference to LFMs, which previously relied on computationally expensive non-parametric Bayesian (NPB) or variational Bayesian (VB) methods. The paper builds on Fujimaki's previous work on FAB for mixture models [5,6] and connects to related work on the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) [9,10,11,17] and latent feature models [3,10,16].

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis with rigorous proofs provided in the supplementary materials. The key Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 are well-justified and demonstrate that LFMs can be incorporated into the FAB framework despite not satisfying the initial Hessian condition. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses, noting that FAB's performance is particularly strong for large N, which aligns with the asymptotic nature of the method. The experimental section is comprehensive, with comparisons across artificial data, block data, and real-world datasets (EEG, Piano, yaleB, USPS, Libras), and the authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach for small N cases. The FAB/LFM algorithm is well-designed, with the shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) and merge post-processing providing practical improvements.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The theoretical development is presented logically, with clear transitions between sections. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including pseudocode for the FAB algorithm (Algorithm 1) and the shrinkage acceleration procedure (Algorithm 2). The explanation of the CCCP interpretation (Section 3.1) is particularly clear and helps contextualize the FAB optimization process. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, including implementation details, parameter settings, and preprocessing steps.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant original contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs, a class of models that previously couldn't benefit from this approach. The key insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying the initial Hessian condition is novel and theoretically interesting. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by showing how the log ∑n znk term appears naturally in LFMs through Lemma 1, which was not previously known. The shrinkage acceleration method and merge post-processing are also original contributions that improve computational efficiency. Compared to recent NIPS papers on latent feature models (e.g., [3,10,16,22]), this work provides a fundamentally different approach to model selection that is both theoretically grounded and computationally efficient.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a critical challenge in latent feature modeling: automatic model selection without manual tuning. The proposed FAB/LFM method achieves better model selection accuracy, prediction performance, and computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art IBP methods, as demonstrated in extensive experiments. The automatic hidden states selection property is particularly valuable for practical applications where determining the optimal number of features is challenging. The paper's contribution advances the state of the art in Bayesian inference for latent variable models by providing a theoretically sound, computationally efficient alternative to existing methods. The empirical results on real-world datasets (Table 1) demonstrate that FAB/LFMs consistently achieve better predictive performance (higher PLL) with lower computational cost, while also producing more compact model representations (smaller K).

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical insight that LFMs can use the same FIC form as mixture models despite not satisfying initial Hessian conditions
- Rigorous proofs provided in supplementary materials
- Novel shrinkage acceleration method that significantly reduces computational cost
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and comparison methods
- Practical implementation details that enable reproducibility
- Clear connection to related work and theoretical grounding

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper acknowledges that FAB's performance may be less accurate for small N, but could provide more insight into the exact conditions under which this occurs
- The merge post-processing step is described but not deeply analyzed in terms of how often it's needed in practice (only "a few merging operations" were observed)
- The paper doesn't fully explore the theoretical limits of the FAB approach for LFMs compared to other asymptotic methods

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending FAB to LFMs, a major advancement in Bayesian model selection
- The proposed method is both theoretically grounded and empirically superior to state-of-the-art approaches
- The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility
- The experimental results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach

*Con:*
- The paper's asymptotic nature may limit its applicability to small datasets, though the authors appropriately acknowledge this
- The paper doesn't fully explore how the FAB approach compares to other asymptotic methods for LFMs

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models. The theoretical insight is novel, the implementation is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria at a high level, with particularly strong contributions in originality and significance. The authors have successfully extended FAB inference to a new class of models with practical benefits, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical applicability of Bayesian model selection. I recommend acceptance.
