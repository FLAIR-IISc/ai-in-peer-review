- Extends the FAB framework to Latent Feature Models by asymptotically analyzing the LFM Hessian (Lemma 1) and deriving an FIC with complexity term sum_k log sum_n z_nk.  
- Proposes FAB/LFM algorithm that maximizes a tractable lower bound of the FIC via alternating optimization and performs automatic feature selection through a prior-independent model-induced regularizer.  
- Interprets the E-step as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and introduces a shrinkage-acceleration heuristic based on this view to speed convergence.  
- Proves the FAB objective resolves parameter non-identifiability in LFMs (Theorem 4) and motivates a principled feature-merging post-processing step.  
- Empirical results: FAB/LFM is competitive or superior to IBP (Gibbs), VB-IBP, and MEIBP on synthetic and real data for model selection accuracy, predictive log-likelihood, and runtime.  
- Theoretical development is technically sound and non-trivial; key achievement is deriving FIC for LFMs despite shared-parameter Hessian structure.  
- The CCCP connection and identifiability analysis provide solid theoretical foundations for algorithmic choices.  
- Main theoretical proofs (Lemma 1, Theorem 2) are deferred to supplementary material, hindering full assessment of assumptions and validity from the main paper.  
- Paper is well-written and structured; derivations, algorithm, and links to variational EM are clearly presented with supportive figures and tables.  
- Description of the accelerateShrinkage subroutine lacks detail on its interaction with E/M steps and precise acceleration mechanism, affecting reproducibility.  
- Novelty: extending FAB to LFMs and the specific shrinkage/identifiability contributions are original and meaningful.  
- Weakness: many algorithmic components (mean-field updates, linear-Gaussian M-step) are standard; novelty centers on the FIC derivation rather than routine updates.  
- Significance: addresses important model-selection challenge in LFMs, offering a fast, deterministic, automated alternative to IBP-based methods with reduced overfitting.  
- Limitation: method is asymptotic and may perform poorly in small-N regimes (e.g., Libras dataset), limiting applicability in data-scarce settings.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel theoretical extension, strong empirical performance, practical utility (fast/deterministic/automated), and principled identifiability solution.  
- Rejection arguments: reliance on supplementary proofs for core claims, limited small-data applicability, and minor clarity issues around the acceleration step.
