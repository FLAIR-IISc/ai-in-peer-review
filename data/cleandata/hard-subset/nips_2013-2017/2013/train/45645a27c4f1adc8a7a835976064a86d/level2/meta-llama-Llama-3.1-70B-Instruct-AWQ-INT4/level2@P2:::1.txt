This paper proposes an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The authors derive a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs, which has the same form as that for Mixture Models (MMs), despite LFMs not satisfying the conditions required for FAB inference. The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is shown to have desirable properties, including automatic hidden state selection, parameter identifiability, and a monotonic increase in the lower FIC bound through iterative optimization.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the FAB inference framework and its limitations. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is rigorous and well-explained, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's properties. The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of FAB/LFM over existing methods, including state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a unified framework for FAB inference that can be applied to both MMs and LFMs. The authors demonstrate that the FIC representation of approximated marginal log-likelihoods is feasible not only for MMs but also for more general (discrete) latent variable models. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it provides a more general and flexible framework for Bayesian inference.

The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the algorithm's properties, including its ability to automatically select feature dimensionality and its robustness to over-fitting. The authors demonstrate that the FAB regularization term induces a model-induced regularization effect, which is independent of the prior and is known as "model-induced regularization" caused by Bayesian marginalization in singular models.

The experimental results are also well-presented, and the authors provide a clear comparison of FAB/LFM with existing methods. The results demonstrate that FAB/LFM achieves good predictive performance with low computational cost and is able to automatically select feature dimensionality.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it assumes a specific form for the prior distribution on the latent variables. While the authors demonstrate that the FAB inference framework can be applied to more general (discrete) latent variable models, it is not clear whether the framework can be extended to more complex prior distributions.

Another potential weakness is that the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the FAB/LFM algorithm. While the authors demonstrate that the algorithm is computationally efficient, it is not clear how the algorithm scales to large datasets.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference. The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is a valuable addition to the toolkit of Bayesian inference methods, and the paper provides a thorough analysis of its properties and performance.
