This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The core challenge is that LFMs do not satisfy the block-diagonal Hessian condition required by previous FAB methods developed for mixture models. The authors overcome this by performing an asymptotic analysis of the LFM's complete-data log-likelihood Hessian. The key result (Lemma 1) shows that the log determinant of the Hessian decomposes into a sum of terms, each dependent on the number of data points associated with a specific latent feature. This allows the derivation of a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs that has the same form as the one for mixture models, thus enabling the application of the FAB framework.

Based on this FIC, the paper develops an efficient iterative algorithm that automatically determines the number of latent features by shrinking irrelevant ones. The authors provide further theoretical analysis, interpreting the optimization as a convex-concave procedure (CCCP), which inspires a practical acceleration technique. They also prove an identifiability result for their method, showing that it avoids redundant features, and use this to motivate a feature-merging post-processing step. The proposed FAB/LFM method is empirically compared against several state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) based methods on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating competitive or superior performance in terms of model selection, predictive accuracy, and computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The main theoretical contribution, the asymptotic analysis of the Hessian for LFMs in Lemma 1, is novel and provides a solid foundation for extending the FAB framework. The derivation of the FIC and the resulting algorithm follows logically from this result. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on multiple datasets, comparing against relevant and strong baselines (IBP Gibbs sampling, VB-IBP, MEIBP). The analysis of identifiability and the CCCP interpretation add significant depth to the work.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on prior work on FAB inference, the extension to LFMs is a significant and non-trivial step that required a new theoretical insight. This work provides a valuable alternative to the dominant non-parametric Bayesian paradigm (i.e., IBP) for model selection in LFMs. The proposed shrinkage acceleration technique and the merging post-processing step are also novel contributions.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of model selection for latent feature models. The proposed FAB/LFM method offers a compelling combination of automation (no sensitive hyperparameters for model selection), computational efficiency, and strong empirical performance. It is likely that researchers and practitioners working with LFMs would be interested in using this method as a practical and scalable alternative to IBP-based approaches. The underlying idea of "model induced regularization" arising from the asymptotic marginal likelihood is an important concept that could inspire further research.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Assumptions for Asymptotic Analysis:** The central result of the paper, Lemma 1, is stated to hold "under some mild assumptions" which are detailed in the supplementary material. While space is limited, it would strengthen the main paper to briefly state the nature of these assumptions, as the validity of the entire framework rests upon them. Are there practical situations where these assumptions might be violated?

2.  **Impact of Approximations:** The final algorithm relies on a sequence of approximations: the Laplace approximation for the marginal likelihood, the linearization of the concave `log-sum` term, and the mean-field factorization of the variational distribution `q(Z)`. While the experiments show strong performance, a brief discussion on the potential cumulative effect of these approximations would be helpful. The paper notes that performance may degrade in small N scenarios; could the authors elaborate on the breakdown point or provide more intuition on when the FIC approximation is expected to be accurate?

3.  **Initialization and Hyperparameters:** The algorithm requires an initial number of latent features, `K`, which is set to `min(N, D)`. How sensitive is the convergence speed and final result to this choice? Starting with a very large `K` could make the initial iterations computationally expensive. Furthermore, the proposed acceleration scheme introduces a new parameter, `T_shrink`. How was this value chosen, and is the performance sensitive to it?

4.  **Clarity on Shrinkage Acceleration:** The shrinkage acceleration is an interesting practical contribution. While Algorithm 2 presents the procedure, the intuition could be slightly clearer. Is the primary benefit that by iterating the `µ` and `π` updates multiple times (the inner loop), the feature usage counts (`sum_n µ_nk`) are driven towards zero more rapidly, allowing the main algorithm to prune features in earlier iterations? A more explicit statement of this intuition would be helpful.

**Summary of evaluation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to Bayesian approximation methods and latent feature modeling. It is well-written, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The extension of FAB inference to LFMs is a novel and important result. The minor weaknesses identified above are mainly requests for clarification and do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and non-trivial theoretical extension of the FAB framework.
*   Provides a practical, efficient, and automated algorithm for model selection in LFMs.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating advantages over state-of-the-art methods.
*   Interesting theoretical insights into identifiability and connections to CCCP.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The exposition could be improved with more discussion on the key assumptions and the potential impact of the multiple approximations involved.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It represents a complete and valuable piece of research that would be of great interest to the NIPS community.
