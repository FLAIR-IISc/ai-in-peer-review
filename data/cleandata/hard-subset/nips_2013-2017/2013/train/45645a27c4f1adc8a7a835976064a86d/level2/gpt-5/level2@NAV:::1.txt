Summary and relation to prior work: The paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference from mixture/HMM settings to latent feature models (LFMs). The key theoretical claim is that, despite LFMs violating the block-diagonal Hessian condition used in prior FAB work, the asymptotic behavior of the Hessian yields the same FIC penalty as in mixture models: a ∑k log(∑n znk) term (Lemma 1, Theorem 2). Building on this, the authors derive a practical FAB algorithm with a mean-field q(Z), provide a CCCP interpretation that motivates a shrinkage-acceleration routine, and show an identifiability property that justifies a merge post-processing step. Empirically, FAB/LFM is compared against IBP (Gibbs, VB) and MEIBP on synthetic and real datasets, reporting improved model selection and predictive performance, and competitive or better computational efficiency (though MEIBP is sometimes faster).

Strengths:
- Novel and insightful asymptotic analysis that broadens FAB’s applicability beyond block-diagonal cases; the resulting penalty clarifies LFM complexity as O(∑k log Nk).
- Monotone lower-bound optimization with automatic feature selection; CCCP-based shrinkage is principled and effective.
- Identifiability analysis is valuable and leads to a sensible merge heuristic.
- Experiments show good predictive performance with compact models and reduced overfitting (PLL vs TLL gaps), plus clear ablations on shrinkage acceleration.

Weaknesses:
- Key assumptions behind Lemma 1 are deferred to the supplement and should be stated prominently (e.g., linear-Gaussian likelihood with diagonal precision, boundedness, regularity). The generality beyond Gaussian LFMs is unclear.
- The mean-field factorization may loosen the bound; no comparison to richer variational families or tempered/structured inference.
- Fairness of comparisons: baselines use fixed noise variance and default hyperparameters (e.g., α=3), which may bias results; limited hyperparameter tuning and variance across runs are reported.
- Scalability evidence is mostly Matlab-based; per-iteration complexity and memory costs are not analyzed; MEIBP can be faster in wall-clock.
- Limited evaluation on non-Gaussian likelihoods and canonical IBP applications (e.g., link prediction).

Constructive suggestions:
- State all assumptions for Lemma 1/Theorem 2 in the main text; discuss finite-sample behavior.
- Include sensitivity analyses (initial K, Tshrink, merging thresholds), report mean±std over multiple runs, and tune baselines (including learning noise variance).
- Provide complexity analysis and larger-scale runs; release code.
- Clarify scope to non-Gaussian likelihoods or add experiments/discussion; compare against MAD-Bayes under matched noise settings.

Quality: Technically sound overall, with some proofs deferred and fairness caveats. Clarity: Good, but assumptions and complexity should be foregrounded. Originality: Strong. Significance: High for automated model selection in LFMs.

Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, practical algorithm, good empirical results, impactful scope. Arguments against: assumptions buried, fairness/scalability concerns. Overall: lean accept.
