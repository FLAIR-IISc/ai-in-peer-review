Summary
The paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs). The central technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood showing that log det F(d) decomposes as sum_k log sum_n z_nk + O_p(1) (Lemma 1). This leads to a factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs with exactly the same model-complexity structure as in mixture models (MMs), despite the lack of block-diagonal Hessian structure. Building on this, the paper derives a tractable FAB lower bound with a mean-field variational form for q(Z), yielding E-step fixed-point updates that include a data-driven shrinkage term proportional to D/(2Nπ̃_k). A convex–concave procedure (CCCP) view of the E-step motivates a shrinkage-acceleration routine that removes irrelevant features earlier and reduces runtime. The work also analyzes identifiability, proving that FAB prefers balanced allocations among duplicate features and motivating a post-hoc merge operation. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets compare FAB/LFM to IBP (Gibbs), VB-IBP, and MEIBP, showing improved model selection (K), competitive or better predictive likelihood, and favorable computational efficiency, especially with the acceleration.

Relation to prior work
FAB was previously developed for MMs and HMMs [Fujimaki & Morinaga; Fujimaki & Hayashi], leveraging Laplace approximations and the FIC. LFMs are typically handled by IBP priors [Griffiths & Ghahramani] and inference via Gibbs [Doshi-Velez & Ghahramani], VB [Doshi-Velez et al.], or MAP/MEIBP via submodular optimization [Reed & Ghahramani]. MAD-Bayes [Broderick et al.] derives MAP-type regularizers in small-noise limits. The present work differs by delivering a FAB/FIC derivation for LFMs under large-sample asymptotics, showing the same FIC form as MMs without the MM block-Hessian assumption, and by introducing a CCCP-based acceleration and an identifiability-based merging heuristic. The theoretical framing connects to singular learning theory and asymptotics (Watanabe; Wong) and contrasts with BIC [Schwarz], which would over-penalize θ_d by O(K log N) in this setting.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation hinges on Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, which are plausible given the model structure and are stated with conditions deferred to the supplement. The resulting FIC matches MM FIC and yields the characteristic log sum_n z_nk regularizer known to drive automatic component/feature shrinkage. The CCCP interpretation is sound and aligns with linearization of the convex term. The identifiability result (Theorem 4) is valuable for understanding why FAB avoids degenerate duplications and justifies the merge step.
- Algorithms: The lower bound and updates are clearly specified. The acceleration routine is straightforward and monotone under the described coordinate updates. The merge heuristic is principled by the theorem and practical.
- Empirics: Comparisons include IBP (accelerated Gibbs), VB-IBP, MEIBP across synthetic, block data, and several real datasets. Results support claims on model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and runtime improvements with acceleration. The observation that FAB and EM converge in large-N regimes but FAB prefers smaller K is consistent with the regularizer.

Clarity
The exposition is clear and well organized. Key ideas, algorithmic steps, and intuitions are provided. However, essential technical assumptions are deferred to the supplement; a brief statement of the most important regularity conditions for Lemma 1 in the main text would improve readability. The fairness of some experimental choices warrants clearer justification.

Originality
- New asymptotic result enabling FAB for LFMs with an FIC identical in form to that of MMs despite non-block-diagonal Hessians.
- CCCP reinterpretation and corresponding shrinkage-acceleration strategy.
- Identifiability analysis and a principled merge post-processing.
These constitute a substantive and novel extension of FAB to an important model class.

Significance
Automated model selection for LFMs without priors and with competitive accuracy and runtime is useful for applications where IBP is computationally heavy. The theoretical insight about FIC structure suggests broader applicability of FAB beyond MMs/HMMs, which is of interest to the community.

Main strengths
- Solid asymptotic analysis leading to a practically useful FIC for LFMs.
- Automatic feature selection via model-induced regularization, independent of priors.
- Monotone lower-bound optimization with a simple and effective acceleration.
- Identifiability analysis with practical merge procedure.
- Empirical evidence of better model selection and competitive predictive performance.

Main weaknesses and suggestions
Essential
- Assumptions for Lemma 1 and Theorem 2: Include a succinct statement of the key conditions (e.g., bounded moments, eigenvalue scaling, regularity around the MLE) and brief proof sketch in the main paper to help readers assess applicability without consulting the supplement.
- Experimental fairness: Several baselines fix the noise variance while FAB learns it. Provide a variant where FAB uses the same fixed noise level, or allow baselines to learn noise if available, to assess sensitivity. Clarify hyperparameters and initialization for all methods and discuss their impact.
- Complexity and scaling: Add a complexity analysis per iteration and an empirical scaling plot in N and D comparing wall-clock convergence across methods. Report the number of features pruned per iteration to quantify acceleration.

Optional but beneficial
- Sensitivity analyses: Investigate sensitivity to Tshrink, initialization of µ, and the tolerance δ. Provide ablations of acceleration and merge.
- Convergence details: State explicitly that the CCCP-accelerated inner loop preserves monotonicity of the same lower bound and indicate any conditions required.
- Robustness: Explore datasets where N is small relative to D to delineate regimes where the FIC approximation is less accurate, as briefly noted (e.g., Libras).
- Code: Release code to facilitate reproducibility.

Questions for clarification
- What are the precise conditions under which log det F(d) admits the stated decomposition, and how do these conditions depend on π_k approaching the boundaries (0 or 1)?
- Does accelerateShrinkage ever decrease the lower bound, or is monotonicity guaranteed for any Tshrink?
- How sensitive is performance to the merge threshold, and is the lower bound always increased after a merge as asserted?
- How are π priors set in practice, and what is their influence relative to the FAB regularizer in finite N?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear theoretical advance enabling FAB for LFMs with principled model selection.
- Effective, simple algorithm with practical acceleration and identifiability handling.
- Strong empirical performance across diverse datasets.

Cons:
- Dependence on supplemental assumptions not summarized in the main text.
- Some concerns about experimental fairness and sensitivity not fully addressed.
- Limited analysis of failure modes when N is small.

Overall, this is a strong and consequential contribution that advances FAB methodology to an important model class and demonstrates practical benefits.
