- Extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), which violate previous block-diagonal Hessian assumptions.  
- Proves that the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs has the same functional form as for mixture models via an asymptotic log-determinant Hessian analysis (Lemma 1, Theorem 2).  
- Derives an EM-like FAB/LFM algorithm that maximizes a tractable lower bound of the FIC.  
- Introduces a model-induced regularization term that automatically prunes irrelevant features, enabling built-in model selection.  
- Interprets the variational E-step as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) and proposes a CCCP-based acceleration (accelerateShrinkage) that substantially reduces computation time.  
- Provides an identifiability analysis showing the FAB objective resolves ML ambiguities and justifies a feature-merging post-processing step.  
- Empirically compares against state-of-the-art nonparametric Bayesian methods (IBP Gibbs, VB-IBP, MEIBP) on synthetic and real data, showing competitive or superior model selection, predictive performance, and speed.  
- Theoretical derivations and algorithmic claims (monotonic bound increase, automatic shrinkage) are technically sound and well-justified.  
- Experimental evaluation is extensive and supports the paper’s claims, while acknowledging asymptotic limitations on very small datasets.  
- Paper is well-written and logically structured, with clear motivation, theory, algorithm, and experiments.  
- Suggests briefly summarizing the “mild assumptions” of Lemma 1 in the main text to improve reader confidence.  
- Recommends a slightly more intuitive presentation of FAB background for readers unfamiliar with prior FAB literature.  
- Highly original contribution: extends FAB beyond previously assumed structural requirements and links to CCCP for practical gains.  
- Significant impact: provides a principled, automatic, hyperparameter-free, and computationally efficient tool for latent feature dimensionality selection and may enable broader FAB applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical extension to LFMs; efficient and empirically strong FAB/LFM algorithm; useful algorithmic refinements and identifiability analysis; clear, complete presentation.  
- Argument against acceptance: reliance on asymptotic theory may reduce advantages on very small datasets compared to nonparametric Bayesian methods.
