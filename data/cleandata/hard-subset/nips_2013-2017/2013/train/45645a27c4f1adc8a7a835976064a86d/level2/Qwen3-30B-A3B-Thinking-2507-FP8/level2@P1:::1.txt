

This paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), addressing a significant gap in the FAB framework. The authors show that despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition required for FAB inference in previous work, LFMs still yield the same FIC form as mixture models. This theoretical insight enables the development of FAB/LFMs, which offer automatic model selection, parameter identifiability, and computational efficiency advantages over existing methods like Indian Buffet Processes (IBP) and variational Bayesian approaches.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 provide a rigorous theoretical foundation showing that FIC for LFMs has the same form as for mixture models, despite LFMs not satisfying the block-diagonal Hessian condition. This is a significant theoretical advance that broadens the applicability of FAB inference. Second, the paper introduces a novel shrinkage acceleration method based on a CCCP interpretation, which drastically reduces computational cost by eliminating irrelevant features earlier in the optimization process. Third, the identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) provides a theoretical basis for a merge post-processing procedure that improves model compactness. The experimental results demonstrate that FAB/LFMs achieve better model selection accuracy (particularly for large N), competitive predictive performance, and significantly reduced computational time compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to algorithmic development to empirical validation. The notation is consistently defined, and the algorithms are clearly presented. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with supplementary materials providing detailed proofs. The experiments are well-designed, including artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets, with appropriate baselines and metrics. The comparison with IBP, variational EM, and MEIBP provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

The main strengths of the paper include its theoretical novelty in extending FAB to LFMs, the practical computational improvements through shrinkage acceleration, and the empirical demonstration of superior performance. The paper addresses an important problem in model selection for latent feature models, which has been a challenge for previous approaches due to high computational costs. The automatic model selection capability without hyperparameters is particularly valuable for practitioners.

Potential weaknesses include:
1) The paper claims FAB/LFMs have "automatic hidden states selection" but doesn't sufficiently differentiate this from other methods that also select model complexity (like IBP or VB with K selection). A clearer explanation of how FAB's automatic selection mechanism differs from existing approaches would strengthen the contribution.
2) The experimental section mentions VB was omitted due to computational time, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of why FAB is more efficient than VB in the context of LFMs. A brief complexity analysis would help clarify this.
3) The identifiability analysis is theoretically sound but could be strengthened with more discussion of how this impacts practical performance beyond the theoretical guarantees.

The significance of this work is substantial. By enabling FAB inference for LFMs, the paper opens up a new approach to model selection for latent feature models that is both theoretically grounded and computationally efficient. The automatic model selection capability without hyperparameters is particularly valuable for practitioners dealing with large-scale datasets. The empirical results across multiple datasets demonstrate clear advantages in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, extending the applicability of FAB inference while providing theoretical insights and practical improvements for latent feature models. The connection between the theoretical analysis and practical algorithmic improvements is particularly compelling. The authors have clearly demonstrated that FAB/LFMs achieve better performance than state-of-the-art methods while being more computationally efficient.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution with practical implications. The theoretical insights about the form of FIC for LFMs are novel and important, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for latent variable models.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel theoretical approach that has been successfully validated through comprehensive experiments. The contributions to both theory and practice are substantial, and the paper represents a meaningful advance in the field.
