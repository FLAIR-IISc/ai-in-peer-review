

This paper extends factorized asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs), a significant advancement since previous FAB approaches required models with a block-diagonal Hessian matrix, which LFMs do not satisfy. The authors demonstrate that despite this difference, the factorized information criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as for mixture models through rigorous asymptotic analysis (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2). This theoretical insight enables the development of FAB/LFMs, which automatically select the number of latent features without hyperparameters, offering parameter identifiability and computational efficiency.

The paper's main strengths include strong theoretical contributions that successfully bridge FAB inference to LFMs, a novel shrinkage acceleration method that dramatically reduces computational cost (Algorithm 2), and comprehensive empirical validation across artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets. The experiments demonstrate that FAB/LFMs outperform state-of-the-art methods (including IBP, VB, and MEIBP) in model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency, particularly for larger datasets.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical details for reproducibility. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental design effectively compares against relevant baselines. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that FAB's asymptotic approximation may be less accurate for small sample sizes.

The work represents a significant advancement in model selection for latent feature models, with clear practical value for practitioners facing computational constraints. The automatic feature selection capability without hyperparameters is particularly valuable. While the paper is strong, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of when FAB/LFMs might not be suitable (e.g., for extremely small datasets) and a comparison with more recent LFM methods beyond those cited.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical insights extend the applicability of FAB inference to a broader class of models, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate practical advantages. The paper meets all four criteria: it is technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), presents a novel extension of FAB to LFMs (originality), and offers a useful method for practitioners (significance). I recommend acceptance.
