This paper extends the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is an approximation method for Bayesian model selection that optimizes a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC), which converges to the marginal log-likelihood in the large sample limit. Prior work on FAB was restricted to models, such as mixture models, where the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood is block-diagonal. LFMs violate this condition due to parameters being shared across latent features.

The central contribution of this work is a theoretical analysis showing that despite this structural difference, the FIC for LFMs has the same functional form as that for mixture models. This is established by an asymptotic analysis of the log determinant of the Hessian matrix. Based on this result, the paper derives the FAB/LFM algorithm, an EM-like procedure that maximizes a tractable lower bound of the FIC. A key property of the resulting objective function is a "model-induced regularization" term that automatically prunes irrelevant features, thus performing model selection. The paper further provides an interpretation of the variational E-step as a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP), which inspires a novel acceleration method that significantly reduces computation time. Finally, an analysis of parameter identifiability demonstrates that the FAB objective resolves ambiguities present in standard maximum likelihood estimation and provides a principled motivation for a feature-merging post-processing step. The proposed method is evaluated against state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian methods, such as the Indian Buffet Process (IBP), demonstrating competitive or superior performance in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational efficiency.

**Quality**

The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The theoretical derivations, centered around Lemma 1 and Theorem 2, appear correct and provide a solid foundation for extending the FAB framework. Claims regarding the algorithm's properties, such as the monotonic increase of the lower bound and the automatic shrinkage of features, are well-justified. The empirical evaluation is extensive, comparing the proposed method against several relevant and strong baselines (IBP Gibbs sampling, Variational Bayes for IBP, MEIBP) on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The experiments convincingly support the claims of improved model selection accuracy, predictive performance, and computational speed. The paper is also forthright about the asymptotic nature of the framework and notes a case in the experiments where its performance on a small dataset is not superior to non-parametric methods.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear motivation, explains the limitations of prior work, presents the core theoretical result, develops the algorithm, and provides further analysis before concluding with a thorough experimental section. The main ideas are communicated effectively.

To further improve clarity, the following minor points could be considered:
*   The key theoretical result, Lemma 1, depends on "mild assumptions" that are deferred to the supplementary material. While standard practice, briefly summarizing the nature of these assumptions in the main text would strengthen the reader's confidence in the result without requiring an immediate detour to the supplement.
*   The explanation of the FAB framework for mixture models in Section 1.1 is quite dense. While necessary background, a slightly more intuitive walk-through could make the paper more accessible to readers not already deeply familiar with prior FAB literature.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. The primary contribution—successfully applying the FAB framework to a class of models that violates the previously understood structural requirements—is a novel and non-trivial extension. The insight that the resulting FIC objective retains the same form is a significant finding. The connection drawn to CCCP and the resulting `accelerateShrinkage` algorithm is a clever and practical contribution that enhances the usability of the method. The analysis of parameter identifiability adds further theoretical depth and distinguishes the FAB approach from standard maximum likelihood estimation for LFMs. This work is clearly distinct from previous contributions on both FAB inference and LFM model selection.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are significant. Determining the dimensionality of latent features is a fundamental and challenging problem in unsupervised learning. This work provides a new tool that is principled, automatic (lacking tunable hyperparameters), computationally efficient, and empirically effective. The experiments suggest that FAB/LFM can outperform state-of-the-art IBP-based methods in terms of finding more parsimonious models with better predictive power, often in a fraction of the computational time. This makes the method highly attractive for practitioners. Furthermore, the theoretical finding that the FIC structure is more general than previously assumed may open the door for applying FAB to an even wider range of latent variable models, stimulating further research in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and significant theoretical extension of the FAB inference framework to the important class of Latent Feature Models.
2.  The proposed FAB/LFM algorithm is computationally efficient and demonstrates strong empirical performance against established state-of-the-art methods on multiple benchmarks.
3.  The work includes valuable algorithmic refinements (CCCP-based acceleration) and theoretical analysis (identifiability) that enhance its practical utility and conceptual soundness.
4.  The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a complete contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The reliance on asymptotic theory means the method's advantages are most pronounced for large datasets, and it may be less suitable than non-parametric Bayesian methods for very small sample sizes, as acknowledged in the text. This is a characteristic of the approach rather than a flaw, but it defines the scope of its applicability.

Overall, this is a strong paper with solid contributions on the theoretical, algorithmic, and empirical fronts. It solves a well-motivated problem with a novel approach and convincingly demonstrates the value of the proposed method.
