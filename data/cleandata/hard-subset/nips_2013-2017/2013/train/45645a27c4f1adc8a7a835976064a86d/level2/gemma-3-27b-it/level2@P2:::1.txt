This paper presents an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). The key contribution lies in showing that, despite LFMs not initially satisfying the conditions required for standard FAB inference, the Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as that for Mixture Models. The authors then derive a FAB algorithm for LFMs, including a shrinkage acceleration method inspired by a convex-concave procedure (CCCP) and demonstrate parameter identifiability. The paper concludes with empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods like IBP and VB.

The paper is well-written and generally clear, with a logical flow of ideas. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is a significant technical contribution, overcoming a limitation of the original FAB framework. The connection to CCCP and the resulting shrinkage acceleration are insightful and contribute to the practical applicability of the method. The empirical results are comprehensive, covering different datasets and comparing against strong baselines. The discussion of parameter identifiability is also valuable.

However, there are several areas for improvement.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the FIC for LFMs, based on the asymptotic analysis of the Hessian matrix, appears mathematically sound and well-motivated. Lemma 1 is a crucial result and well-presented.
*   **Novelty:** Extending FAB inference to LFMs is a novel contribution, addressing a limitation of the original method. The analysis reveals a surprising similarity in the FIC form between LFMs and MMs.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed shrinkage acceleration method, based on CCCP, demonstrably improves computational efficiency, making the algorithm more practical for larger datasets.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough, with comparisons to relevant baselines on both synthetic and real-world data. The inclusion of different datasets and missing value prediction tasks strengthens the results.
*   **Identifiability Analysis:** The discussion of parameter identifiability and the proposed merge post-processing step are valuable contributions to the theoretical understanding and practical implementation of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Detailed Explanation of Assumptions:** While the paper mentions “some mild assumptions” in Lemma 1, these are only detailed in the supplementary materials. Important assumptions should be explicitly stated in the main paper for clarity and reproducibility.
*   **Limited Discussion of Prior Work on FIC and FAB:** While the authors cite relevant work, the discussion of existing FAB applications and the nuances of FIC derivation could be more thorough. A more detailed comparison with existing techniques beyond just performance numbers would be helpful. The role of the prior and how it is asymptotically ignored could be expanded.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper mentions computational speed improvements but lacks a more detailed analysis of the time complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to the baselines. Providing theoretical or empirical complexity analysis would strengthen the claim of efficiency.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors claim the method is less sensitive to hyperparameters, the impact of parameter choices (e.g., the stopping criterion delta, Tshrink) on the results is not thoroughly explored. A sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Merge Post-processing Detail:** The description of the merge post-processing is somewhat brief. More details on the criteria used for merging features and its effect on performance would be appreciated. The reliance on Euclidean distance could be limiting.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant limitation of the FAB framework, extending its applicability to a wider class of models.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the derivation of the FIC for LFMs, are novel and well-supported.
*   The proposed acceleration method and the identifiability analysis enhance the practical value of the method.
*   The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed discussion of assumptions and related work weakens the paper’s clarity and context.
*   The limited computational cost analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis leave some questions unanswered.
*   The brevity of the explanation of the merge post-processing step hampers full understanding.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of Bayesian inference for latent feature models. While there are some areas for improvement, the technical soundness, novelty, and comprehensive evaluation make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by explicitly stating key assumptions in the main paper and providing a more detailed discussion of related work and computational complexity.
