Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to latent feature models (LFMs) with Bernoulli features and linear-Gaussian observations. The central technical contribution is an asymptotic analysis of the complete-data Hessian that yields a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs with the same functional form as for mixture models (MMs), despite the lack of block-diagonal structure in the Hessian. Specifically, Lemma 1 shows that log det of per-dimension Hessians contributes a ∑k log(∑n znk) penalty, leading to an FIC that drives automatic feature shrinkage. Building on this, the authors derive a tractable lower bound amenable to an EM-like FAB algorithm with mean-field q(Z), a CCCP interpretation of the E-step updates, a shrinkage acceleration scheme, and a merge post-processing justified by an identifiability analysis. Experiments compare FAB to IBP sampling, variational IBP, and MEIBP, reporting better model selection and predictive performance with competitive or improved computational cost.

This work builds on earlier FAB for mixtures and HMMs (Fujimaki et al.) and connects to nonparametric latent feature modeling via IBP (Griffiths & Ghahramani; Doshi-Velez et al.) and efficient MAP/MEIBP (Reed & Ghahramani). It also contrasts with MAD-Bayes asymptotics (Broderick et al.) which rely on vanishing-noise limits rather than large-N asymptotics.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Deriving an LFM FIC with the same regularization as for mixtures is nontrivial and broadens the scope of FAB beyond models with block-diagonal Hessians.
- Algorithmic clarity: The FAB lower bound and updates are explicit; the CCCP perspective neatly explains the shrinkage term and motivates acceleration.
- Automated model selection: The −(D/2)∑k log(∑n znk) regularizer provides data-driven shrinkage that does not require tuning hyperparameters.
- Identifiability analysis: Theorem 4 is insightful; it explains how FAB prefers balanced representations and motivates a principled merge post-processing.
- Empirical results: Across synthetic and real datasets, FAB selects compact models and often achieves better predictive log-likelihoods than IBP variants, with substantial speedups from shrinkage acceleration.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Assumptions behind Lemma 1: The main text repeatedly refers to “mild assumptions” with proofs in the supplement. The validity and scope of Lemma 1 are pivotal. Key conditions (e.g., on Λ being diagonal, boundedness/regularity of W, π, and feature usage patterns, rates at which K may grow with N) should be summarized in the main paper.
- Mean-field factorization on Z: While standard in VB IBP, the additional approximation loosens the FIC link to the marginal likelihood. A discussion of the induced gap (or at least empirical sensitivity to dependencies among features) would strengthen the contribution.
- Fairness of comparisons: Competing methods often use fixed noise variance (set by the authors) and default priors (e.g., α=3 for IBP), while FAB learns noise precision. This may disadvantage IBP/VB; experiments where noise variance and α are cross-validated (or learned) would make the comparison more convincing.
- Scope of the model: The results are shown only for linear-Gaussian LFMs with diagonal noise. It would be helpful to discuss whether the FIC derivation extends to non-diagonal Λ, other likelihoods (e.g., Bernoulli/Poisson), or more general latent feature models.
- Computational complexity and convergence: Provide explicit per-iteration cost and empirical iteration counts with and without acceleration, and sensitivity to Tshrink. Does the acceleration preserve monotonicity of the lower bound throughout, or only between outer iterations? The merge step is said to increase the lower bound, but its practical criteria (distance thresholds, search strategy) are deferred; a brief summary would aid reproducibility.
- Large-N vs small-N behavior: The paper notes that FAB may be less accurate when N is small. A controlled study (varying N) would help quantify the regime where FIC becomes reliable.

Quality
- The technical claims are plausible and coherent, and the algorithm is well specified. However, the core asymptotic result (Lemma 1/Theorem 2) is central; summarizing the key steps/conditions in the main text would improve confidence in correctness. Empirical comparisons are broad but could be more carefully controlled regarding hyperparameters and noise variance.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. The role of π and π̃ could be emphasized early, and the relationship between η(πk) and the FAB-induced shrinkage clarified. Including the key assumptions of Lemma 1 and a brief outline of the proof would help. Minor notation issues are minimal.

Originality
- Extending FAB/FIC to LFMs without the block-diagonal Hessian condition, and showing the emergence of the same log-count regularizer, is novel. The CCCP interpretation for accelerated shrinkage and the identifiability-driven merge are also interesting contributions.

Significance
- Model selection for LFMs is important, and methods that are automated and computationally efficient are valuable. If the assumptions of Lemma 1 hold broadly, the work could have meaningful impact. The empirical improvements in model compactness and predictive performance are promising.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical extension of FAB to an important model class.
- Practical algorithm with automatic model selection and effective acceleration.
- Identifiability analysis and merge strategy add robustness.
- Empirical results show advantages in prediction and compactness across several datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on supplementary proofs; key assumptions not stated in main text.
- Comparison fairness concerns (fixed noise/prior settings for baselines).
- Limited discussion of generality beyond linear-Gaussian LFMs and diagonal noise.
- Mean-field approximation’s effect on the FIC gap not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- State the main assumptions for Lemma 1 in the paper, and briefly sketch the derivation.
- Provide results where IBP baselines learn or tune noise variance and α; include sensitivity analyses.
- Report per-iteration complexity, iteration counts, and sensitivity to Tshrink; show bound monotonicity plots.
- Summarize the merge procedure and criteria; quantify how often merges occur and their effect on the bound.
- Discuss extensions to non-diagonal Λ and non-Gaussian likelihoods; clarify limitations.

Overall evaluation
A strong and thoughtful submission with a compelling extension of FAB to LFMs, clear algorithmic contributions, and promising empirical performance. With strengthened presentation of the key assumptions and fairer baselines, this could be a solid accept. My recommendation is borderline accept leaning to accept.
