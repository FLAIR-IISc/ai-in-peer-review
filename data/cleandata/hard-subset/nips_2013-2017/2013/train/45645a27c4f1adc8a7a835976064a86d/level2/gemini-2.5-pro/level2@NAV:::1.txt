This paper presents a significant extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). Prior work on FAB, such as for mixture models, relied on a block-diagonal structure of the Hessian of the complete log-likelihood, a condition LFMs do not satisfy. The authors' primary contribution is a novel asymptotic analysis demonstrating that the resulting Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) for LFMs takes the same form as that for mixture models. This theoretical insight enables the development of an efficient FAB/LFM algorithm for simultaneous parameter estimation and model selection (i.e., determining the number of latent features). The paper also introduces a practical shrinkage acceleration technique derived from a Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) interpretation and proves that the method resolves parameter identifiability issues inherent in LFMs.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The work meaningfully broadens the applicability of the FAB framework to an important class of models. The core theoretical result (Lemma 1), showing that the key model complexity term `log Σ_n z_nk` naturally arises for LFMs, is a non-trivial and elegant contribution that advances the understanding of asymptotic Bayesian methods.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical claims are well-motivated and appear sound. They are supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on both synthetic and real-world data. The proposed FAB/LFM method is shown to consistently outperform state-of-the-art non-parametric Bayesian approaches like the Indian Buffet Process (IBP) and its variants in terms of model selection accuracy, predictive performance on missing data, and computational efficiency. The method demonstrates less overfitting compared to baselines, as indicated by the smaller gap between training and predictive log-likelihoods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation for the work is strong, and the algorithmic contributions, such as the acceleration and merging steps, are well-explained and justified.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Completeness in Main Text:** The paper defers the "mild assumptions" required for the central Lemma 1, as well as rigorous proofs, to the supplementary materials. While understandable for a conference paper, briefly stating the nature of these assumptions in the main text would improve its self-containedness and aid in evaluating the technical soundness of the core claims.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper acknowledges that the asymptotic nature of FIC can be less accurate in small-sample (small N) regimes, as observed in the Libras dataset. This is a critical trade-off against full Bayesian methods and could be discussed more prominently as a limitation of the proposed approach.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a strong combination of novel theory, practical algorithmic improvements, and convincing empirical results. It makes a valuable contribution to the field of approximate Bayesian inference and model selection.
