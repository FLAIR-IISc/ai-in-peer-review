

The paper extends Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a key limitation of prior FAB work that required a block-diagonal Hessian matrix for the complete log-likelihood. The core technical contribution is Lemma 1, which proves that LFMs asymptotically yield the same FIC form as mixture models despite violating the block-diagonal condition. This enables FAB/LFMs to inherit properties like automatic dimensionality selection and parameter identifiability. The paper further develops a shrinkage acceleration method via convex-concave procedure (CCCP) interpretation and provides a merge post-processing step for identifiability. Empirical results demonstrate superior model selection accuracy and computational efficiency compared to IBP, VB, and MEIBP across synthetic and real-world datasets.

Strengths include rigorous asymptotic analysis that resolves a fundamental gap in FAB applicability (Lemma 1, Theorem 2). The derivation of FIC for LFMs is technically sound, with clear connections to prior work on mixture models (e.g., FICMM in Equation 3). The shrinkage acceleration (Algorithm 2) is well-motivated and empirically validated, reducing computational cost without sacrificing objective value (Figure 1). The identifiability proof (Theorem 4) offers a theoretically grounded post-processing strategy for latent feature merging, addressing a known challenge in LFMs. Empirical results are comprehensive: model selection accuracy (Figure 2), feature extraction (Figure 3), and predictive performance (Table 1) consistently show FAB/LFMs outperforming state-of-the-art methods, particularly in selecting compact models with minimal overfitting (smaller TLL-PLL gaps). The paper also carefully contextualizes contributions against related work (e.g., MAD-Bayes, MEIBP), avoiding unsupported novelty claims.

Weaknesses include limited experimental scope for the merge post-processing. The paper notes merge operations occur rarely in real data but provides no ablation study quantifying their impact on final performance (e.g., how much L increases after merging). While the shrinkage acceleration is validated (Figure 1), the CCCP interpretation (Section 3.1) could be clarified; the connection between Equation (14) and the FAB update (11) is somewhat implicit, and a brief example would aid understanding. The real-world experiments (Table 1) omit VB due to "long computational time," but without comparison to the actual runtime, the claim about FAB’s computational efficiency lacks full context. The supplementary materials are referenced for proofs, but the main text could more explicitly state which results depend on them (e.g., "Theorem 2 requires assumptions in Supplementary Material Section S.2").

The work excels in technical quality and significance. The asymptotic analysis is thorough and directly addresses a gap in FAB’s applicability, advancing the state of Bayesian model selection for latent variable models. The practical benefits—automatic dimensionality selection, computational efficiency, and robust model selection—are demonstrated convincingly. The paper’s theoretical insights (FIC form similarity, identifiability) also contribute to broader understanding of singular models. While the experimental comparisons are strong, adding a merge ablation study would strengthen the practical impact. The writing is clear, though the CCCP section would benefit from a concise illustrative step.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution by extending FAB to LFMs through novel asymptotic analysis, with strong empirical validation. The technical depth, theoretical insights, and practical improvements over existing methods (e.g., IBP, MEIBP) meet NIPS standards for quality and significance. Minor clarifications would enhance reproducibility and impact, but the core contribution is robust and well-justified. The work is clearly within scope for NIPS, advancing Bayesian inference for latent variable models with both theoretical and practical value.
