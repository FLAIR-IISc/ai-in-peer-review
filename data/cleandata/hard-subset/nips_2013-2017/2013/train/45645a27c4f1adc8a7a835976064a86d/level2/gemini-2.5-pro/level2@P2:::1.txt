This paper presents an extension of the Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference framework to Latent Feature Models (LFMs). FAB is a model selection method that approximates the marginal log-likelihood via a Factorized Information Criterion (FIC). Prior work on FAB was limited to models like mixture models, where the Hessian of the complete-data log-likelihood is block-diagonal. The main contribution of this paper is to show, via an asymptotic analysis of the Hessian, that a similar FIC can be derived for LFMs despite their shared parameter structure, which violates the block-diagonal assumption. This leads to an efficient algorithm, FAB/LFM, which can automatically determine the number of latent features. The authors further provide a CCCP interpretation of their update rule, leading to an effective acceleration technique, and an analysis of parameter identifiability, which motivates a feature-merging post-processing step. The method is compared empirically to state-of-the-art Indian Buffet Process (IBP) variants and a variational EM baseline, demonstrating superior or competitive performance in model selection, prediction, and computational efficiency.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant and Novel Contribution:** The paper successfully extends the applicability of the FAB framework to a new, important, and more complex class of models (LFMs). Moving from models with independent parameter blocks (like mixture models) to models with shared parameters (like LFMs) is a non-trivial step. The core technical result (Lemma 1) that recovers the crucial `sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)` complexity term is elegant and constitutes a strong theoretical contribution. This work significantly broadens the potential impact of asymptotic Bayesian methods for model selection.

2.  **Sound Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is well-supported by theoretical analysis. The derivation of the FIC for LFMs is the central result and appears solid, pending verification of the proofs in the supplementary material. The connection drawn between the FAB E-step and the Convex-Concave Procedure (CCCP) is insightful, providing a principled justification for the proposed and empirically effective shrinkage acceleration technique. Furthermore, the identifiability analysis (Theorem 4) is a valuable addition, as it addresses a known issue in LFM learning and provides a natural motivation for the proposed feature merging post-processing.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare their method against a well-chosen set of strong and relevant baselines, including fast Gibbs sampling and variational Bayes for the IBP, as well as the recent MEIBP. The evaluation is multifaceted, assessing computational speed, accuracy in recovering the true number of features, predictive log-likelihood on held-out data, and the quality of the learned features. The results consistently demonstrate that FAB/LFM is highly competitive, often achieving better predictive performance and more compact models (smaller K) in less time than the nonparametric Bayesian alternatives. The experiment explicitly demonstrating the effectiveness of the shrinkage acceleration is also a nice touch.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. It begins by precisely stating the limitations of prior work and its main goals. The derivation of the new FIC and the resulting algorithm are presented in a logical flow. The key differences from the standard variational EM updates (i.e., the FAB regularization term) are clearly highlighted and their role in model selection is well explained.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Reliance on Supplementary Material for Key Assumptions:** The paper's central result, Lemma 1, is stated to hold "under some mild assumptions" which are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is necessary for space, it would significantly improve the self-containedness of the main paper if the authors could briefly state the nature of these assumptions (e.g., "assuming the feature vectors W are non-degenerate and data points are sufficiently diverse..."). This would give the reader a better immediate sense of the scope and limitations of the theoretical claims without having to consult the supplement.

2.  **Comparison to BIC:** The paper rightly notes that a naive application of Laplace's method would result in a standard Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), and that their derived FIC provides a more appropriate penalty. This is a crucial point that highlights the novelty of the work. This argument could be strengthened by explicitly writing down the BIC penalty for this model (`(DK/2)logN`) and contrasting it with the FIC penalty (`(D/2) sum_k log(sum_n z_nk)`). A brief discussion of why the FIC penalty is more suitable (e.g., it correctly penalizes features based on usage, not just their existence) would further underscore the significance of the paper's theoretical contribution.

3.  **Experimental Setup Details:** The handling of the noise parameter `lambda` could be clarified. The authors state that `lambda` is learned by FAB/LFM but fixed for the baseline methods. While this may follow the standard implementation of those baselines, it complicates a direct comparison. It would be valuable to discuss the potential impact of this choice. For instance, did the fixed value give the baselines an advantage (by being set to the true value in simulations) or a disadvantage (by being sub-optimal on real data)? A more direct comparison might involve running FAB/LFM with a fixed `lambda` or attempting to have the baselines learn it. Additionally, the choice of `alpha=3` for IBP is noted as potentially causing overestimation; this suggests the baselines might not be optimally tuned, which could weaken the comparative claims. A brief comment on hyperparameter sensitivity for the baselines would be helpful.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

*   **Pro:** A novel and significant theoretical extension of the FAB framework to LFMs.
*   **Pro:** Strong theoretical support, including an insightful CCCP interpretation and an identifiability analysis.
*   **Pro:** Comprehensive and convincing empirical results against state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Pro:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Con:** Key theoretical assumptions are deferred entirely to the supplement.
*   **Con:** Potential for a slightly fairer comparison in the experimental setup regarding noise parameter estimation and hyperparameter tuning for baselines.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong, novel contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and model selection. The theoretical results are significant, and they are backed by a well-executed empirical study. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to presentation and experimental details that could be addressed in a final version. The work is likely to be of high interest to researchers working on latent variable models, Bayesian nonparametrics, and large-scale machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
