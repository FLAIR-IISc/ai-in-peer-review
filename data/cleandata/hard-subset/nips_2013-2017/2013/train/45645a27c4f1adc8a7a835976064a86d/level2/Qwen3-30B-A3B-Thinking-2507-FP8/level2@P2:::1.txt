

This paper proposes an extension of Factorized Asymptotic Bayesian (FAB) inference to Latent Feature Models (LFMs), addressing a key limitation in the existing FAB framework that previously required a specific condition on the Hessian matrix of the complete log-likelihood. The authors demonstrate that despite LFMs not satisfying the conditions required for Mixture Models (MMs), their Factorized Information Criterion (FIC) has the same form as for MMs, enabling the application of FAB to LFMs. The paper makes several theoretical contributions, including Lemma 1 showing the log determinant of the Hessian for LFMs contains the key log ∑n znk term, and Theorem 4 establishing parameter identifiability for FAB/LFMs. The authors also propose a shrinkage acceleration method based on the CCCP interpretation that significantly reduces computational cost.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical contributions and empirical validation. The derivation of FIC for LFMs is rigorous, with proofs provided in supplementary materials. The theoretical insight that LFMs share the same FIC form as MMs despite not satisfying the conditions for MMs is novel and important. The empirical results consistently show FAB/LFMs outperforming state-of-the-art methods (IBP, VB, MEIBP) in terms of model selection accuracy, prediction performance, and computational efficiency across artificial simulations, block data, and real-world datasets. The shrinkage acceleration method (Algorithm 2) is particularly well-motivated and effective, reducing computational cost without sacrificing performance.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. First, the claim that FAB/LFMs "empirically perform better than state-of-the-art Indian Buffet processes" is under-supported by the experiments. The comparison is limited to five datasets (EEG, Piano, USPS, yaleB, Libras), with the authors omitting VB entirely due to computational cost, which weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance. A more comprehensive comparison with additional datasets and methods would strengthen the empirical claims.

Second, the paper's theoretical contributions are somewhat opaque without clearer explanations of why LFMs share the same FIC form as MMs despite not satisfying the conditions for MMs. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of this key insight, which would help readers better understand the theoretical contribution.

Third, the paper claims "automatic hidden states selection" but provides limited analysis of how the algorithm selects the correct number of features across different scenarios. The paper should provide more detailed analysis of the model selection behavior, including how the algorithm handles cases where the true dimensionality is near the boundary of the model's capabilities.

The paper's relation to prior work could be strengthened. While the authors cite relevant work, they don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from MAD-Bayes [1] and MEIBP [22], which also address similar problems. A clearer discussion of how FAB/LFMs differ from these methods would enhance the paper's contribution.

The paper's clarity could be improved by providing more intuitive explanations of the theoretical results, particularly the derivation of the FIC for LFMs. The density of the theoretical sections may make the paper inaccessible to many readers, despite the soundness of the proofs.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in model selection for LFMs, and the proposed method has clear practical advantages. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the specialized nature of LFMs and the relatively narrow scope of empirical validation.

Overall, the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending FAB inference to LFMs, and the empirical results are convincing for the datasets tested. The main weaknesses are the limited scope of empirical validation, the lack of intuitive explanations for key theoretical insights, and the insufficient differentiation from related work. With additional experiments, clearer theoretical explanations, and a more thorough comparison with related methods, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

The paper should be accepted with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above. Specifically, the authors should expand the empirical evaluation to include more datasets, provide clearer explanations of the key theoretical insights, and more thoroughly compare with related methods like MAD-Bayes and MEIBP.
