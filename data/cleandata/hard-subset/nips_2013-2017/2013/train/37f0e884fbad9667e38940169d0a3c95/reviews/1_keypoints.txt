- Proposal: EMGD hybrid algorithm that uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls by replacing each full-gradient epoch with O(k^2) mixed steps and averaging.  
- Main idea: computing one full gradient per epoch plus many cheap stochastic steps yields epoch-count independent of condition number.  
- Novelty: mixing full and stochastic gradients for constrained smooth strongly-convex optimization; simple, self-contained convergence proof.  
- Theoretical strength: achieves linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee (stronger than SAG’s in-expectation result).  
- Advantage vs SAG: EMGD works for constrained problems and has a much simpler proof; potential memory/parallelization advantages noted but unclear.  
- Complexity comparison (sum-of-n-functions): EMGD beats Nesterov if k ≤ n^(2/3); SAG outperforms EMGD in many regimes and matches EMGD when k ≈ n^(1/2).  
- Practical concern: no experiments or concrete examples to demonstrate empirical relevance.  
- Missing discussion: paper lacks limitations/drawbacks and detailed comparison to existing methods (section 3.4 needs expansion).  
- Practical drawback: epoch length must be fixed a priori (needs knowledge of k), unlike SCDA and SAG which are more adaptive.  
- Step-size and adaptivity: EMGD lacks automatic/adaptive step-size mechanisms present in SCDA and practical heuristics used for SAG.  
- Practical regime note: in ML with regularization ~ C/n (k ≈ n/C'), EMGD may not outperform SAG or Nesterov; authors should highlight realistic settings where EMGD helps.  
- Related work: should cite and contrast Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) hybrid deterministic-stochastic method.  
- Clarity improvements: add high-level intuition for proof choices, clarify Table 1 citations and assumptions (e.g., SCDA applicability, gradient expectation identity).  
- Manuscript fixes: address specific line edits and typos, enforce consistent notation for domain, norms, and log/ln usage.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound and theoretically significant with a novel simple proof; authors should add experiments and the recommended discussions/edits (rebuttal acceptable if changes are implemented).
