- Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that combines full and stochastic gradients to reduce dependence on condition number κ.  
- Achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient accesses and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, with linear convergence w.h.p.  
- Shows computational advantage over Nesterov when κ ≤ n^{2/3} for finite-sum problems F(w) = (1/n)∑fᵢ(w).  
- Provides a rigorous convergence proof (Theorem 1) using martingale inequalities.  
- Clearly explains the mixed gradient mechanism (∇F(ŵᵏ) + ∇fₖₜ(wₖₜ) − ∇fₖₜ(ŵᵏ)).  
- Extends to constrained problems (unlike SAG/SDCA) and requires only O(d) storage versus O(n).  
- Offers high-probability convergence guarantees, stronger than expectation-based results for SAG/SDCA.  
- Situates the work well relative to Nesterov, SAG, SDCA, and related literature, and identifies full/stochastic cost trade-offs.  
- Requires prior knowledge of κ, with only vague suggestions for estimation and no concrete strategies.  
- Assumes an unbiased stochastic gradient oracle Of without discussing practical implementation for non-sum-of-functions problems.  
- The claim of advantage over Nesterov assumes full gradients cost n× stochastic ones, which may not hold (e.g., with parallel/distributed full-gradient computation).  
- Table 2 comparison oversimplifies by ignoring communication costs in distributed settings where SAG/SDCA could be preferable.  
- Technical analysis is sound, but the bound T ≥ 1152L²/λ² log(1/δ) seems conservative and potentially tighten-able.  
- Clarity is good overall, but Section 3.2 should more explicitly contrast EMGD with semi-stochastic methods to highlight novelty.  
- Scope is limited to smooth, strongly convex problems; extending to non-smooth or non-strongly convex cases would increase impact.  
- Suggested minor revisions: discuss practical κ estimation, clarify Of oracle feasibility beyond finite-sum problems, and refine Table 2 to include communication costs.  
- Overall assessment: a valuable, technically rigorous contribution that reduces full-gradient dependence and is suitable for acceptance for ill-conditioned, large-scale regularized problems.
