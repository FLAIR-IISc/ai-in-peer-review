- Summary: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), combining occasional full gradients with frequent stochastic gradients to get linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(poly(κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.
- Relation to prior work: Related to semi-stochastic and variance-reduction methods (SAG, SDCA) but claims differences: handles constrained problems, provides high-probability guarantees, and controls variance via epoch centers and shrinking trust regions.
- Main theoretical claim: Linear convergence with full-gradient count independent of condition number κ and a per-epoch martingale-based error contraction proof using smoothness and strong convexity.
- Strength — goal: Clear, well-motivated objective to decouple full-gradient calls from κ while keeping linear convergence.
- Strength — theory: Solid, careful analysis giving a high-probability convergence theorem with explicit dependence on L, λ, T, and δ.
- Strength — implementation: Conceptually simple mixed-gradient update, applicable to constrained settings and low memory footprint.
- Strength — perspective: Useful comparison to finite-sum methods, highlighting scenarios where EMGD may be preferable (constrained, low-memory, distributed full gradients).
- Weakness — complexity inconsistency: Abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, but the theorem and per-epoch requirements imply O(κ^2 log(1/ε)); needs reconciliation.
- Weakness — experiments: No empirical validation or comparisons to strong baselines (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) on ill-conditioned, constrained, or distributed problems.
- Weakness — practicality: Requires knowing κ (and δ) to set parameters; shrinking trust region implies solving constrained subproblems each iteration, raising overhead concerns.
- Weakness — novelty clarity: Relation to existing variance-reduction/control-variate techniques is insufficiently clarified; mixed gradient appears closely related to known methods.
- Quality: Analysis appears technically sound but must fix the κ vs κ^2 complexity issue.
- Clarity: Generally clear but some dense notation; would benefit from an explicit parameter schedule summarizing how to reach ε.
- Originality: Moderate — main novelty is κ-independent full-gradient count plus constrained/high-probability treatment.
- Significance: Potentially useful for constrained problems, but practical impact unclear without experiments.
- Key suggestions: Correct/clarify stochastic gradient complexity, add empirical comparisons on ill-conditioned and constrained tasks (including distributed settings), introduce adaptivity for L and λ, and discuss alternatives to shrinking ball (e.g., proximal regularization).
- Pros: Strong theory, constrained applicability, low memory use, high-probability guarantees.
- Cons: Complexity inconsistency, lack of experiments, dependence on κ for parameter tuning, and per-iteration projection overhead.
