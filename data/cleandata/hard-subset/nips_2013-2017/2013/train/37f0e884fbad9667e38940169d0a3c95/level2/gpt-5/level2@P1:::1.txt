Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization using first-order information, with access to both full gradients and stochastic gradients. The goal is to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations so that it no longer scales with the condition number κ = L/λ. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes one full gradient at a “snapshot” point w̄k. Within the epoch, it performs T iterations using a mixed gradient estimator
g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k),
and projects onto the intersection of W with a shrinking Euclidean ball centered at w̄k. The analysis shows linear convergence with high probability: after m epochs, the suboptimality decreases geometrically, using m full gradients and mT stochastic gradients, with T chosen as T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)). Hence, to achieve ε accuracy, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

The mixed gradient estimator is a classic control variate and is identical to what later became widely known as the SVRG estimator for finite-sum problems F(w) = (1/n)∑ fi(w). EMGD’s use of a snapshot gradient per epoch is similar in spirit to semi-stochastic methods that interleave full and stochastic updates (e.g., Marti 1973; Friedlander & Schmidt 2012). The paper provides a high-probability analysis under general stochastic oracle assumptions (not only finite sums) and highlights differences with SAG and SDCA, which achieve linear rates for finite sums but require more storage (SAG) or rely on duality (SDCA). Compared to Nesterov’s accelerated methods on full gradients, EMGD reduces the number of full gradient calls from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by trading in stochastic gradients.

Strengths:
- Clear objective and motivation: minimize expensive full gradient evaluations while retaining linear convergence on smooth strongly convex problems.
- Conceptually simple algorithm using a well-motivated control variate estimator and an epoch structure.
- High-probability linear convergence guarantee with explicit constants; analysis is careful and self-contained.
- Works with constrained domains (projection onto W ∩ ball), uses only O(d) memory, and requires no dual formulation.
- Useful discussion of trade-offs vs SAG/SDCA and of distributed computation for full gradients.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Apparent inconsistency in complexity claims: the abstract states O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, while Theorem 1 and the body of the paper indicate O(κ^2 log(1/ε)). Please reconcile and, if O(κ^2) is correct, update the abstract and discussion accordingly.
- Compared to known variance-reduction methods for finite sums, the κ^2 dependence is suboptimal. With the same estimator g̃, SVRG-type analyses yield O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients and O(log(1/ε)) full gradients without shrinking trust regions. It would be important to (i) position EMGD relative to these methods, (ii) explain whether the κ^2 dependence is intrinsic to the projection/shrinking approach or an artifact of analysis, and (iii) discuss whether the proof can be tightened to O(κ).
- The shrinking ball constraint is pivotal to bounding the stochastic part of g̃, but it introduces extra projections and may be impractical for some W. Can the method/analysis be adapted to avoid the shrinking ball (as in SVRG), or to use an adaptive trust region?
- The finite-sum setting is central in machine learning. While the paper discusses SAG/SDCA, it does not cover closely related mixed-gradient/variance-reduction methods (e.g., SVRG). A thorough empirical and theoretical comparison would strengthen the case for EMGD.
- The method requires knowledge of κ to set T. The paper acknowledges this and sketches ideas for estimation; more concrete guidance or a doubling/linesearch heuristic would improve practicality.
- Minor clarity issues: a stray footnote about SDCA assumptions appears within the algorithm section; a careful edit would help. Also, check the dependence on n in the complexity table and ensure consistent assumptions.

Quality:
The analysis is technically sound, with a clear inductive structure, use of smoothness/strong convexity, and a martingale concentration bound to control the stochastic term. Constants are explicit. The main concern is the κ^2 dependence and the inconsistency between abstract and main body; otherwise the claims are supported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable. Algorithm and theorem statements are clear. Please correct the κ vs κ^2 discrepancy, clean up minor typographical issues, and better separate main assumptions from remarks. Adding a concise intuition for why the control variate plus shrinking ball leads to κ^2 would aid readers.

Originality:
The idea of mixing a snapshot full gradient with per-iteration stochastic corrections is, by now, closely related to concurrent work on variance-reduced SGD for finite sums. EMGD’s use of a shrinking projection set and its high-probability analysis in a general oracle model are novel facets. However, in the finite-sum regime, the estimator is the same as in SVRG; the paper should acknowledge and differentiate from this line of work.

Significance:
Reducing the number of full gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)) is meaningful, especially when full gradients are expensive and stochastic gradients are cheap. The constrained-setting applicability and low memory are practical advantages. That said, if the total stochastic gradient complexity is O(κ^2 log(1/ε)), the method may be less competitive than best-known finite-sum methods with O((n + κ) log(1/ε)). The paper’s impact will be higher if the κ^2 factor can be improved, or if the general oracle/high-probability aspects address regimes where finite-sum methods do not apply.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Resolve and correct the stochastic gradient complexity claim (κ vs κ^2) throughout.
- Compare and relate to variance-reduced methods using the same estimator (e.g., SVRG; Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), and discuss theoretical and empirical differences, including constrained cases and high-probability guarantees.
- Investigate whether the analysis can be tightened to O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch (e.g., via variance bounds for g̃ that scale with suboptimality rather than the radius), potentially removing the need for a shrinking ball.
- Provide guidance for unknown κ (adaptive epoch length, doubling trick) and discuss projection costs for common constraint sets.
- If space permits, add experiments on standard convex problems (ridge/logistic) comparing EMGD to accelerated GD, SVRG, SAG, and SDCA under matched oracle costs and constraints.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated objective and a simple algorithm with high-probability linear convergence.
- Useful perspective on reducing full gradient usage; applicable to constrained problems with low memory.
- Solid analysis and a potentially impactful idea if κ^2 can be improved.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Suboptimal κ^2 dependence and inconsistency in stated complexity.
- Limited differentiation from concurrent variance-reduction methods using the same gradient estimator; missing comparisons.
- Practicality concerns due to shrinking projections and requirement of κ.

Overall, this is a thoughtful theoretical contribution with a clean analysis and a compelling goal, but it needs to reconcile its complexity claims and situate itself clearly relative to closely related methods. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper.
