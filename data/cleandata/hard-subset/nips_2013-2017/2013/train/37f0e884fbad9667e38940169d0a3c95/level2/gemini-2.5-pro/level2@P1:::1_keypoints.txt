- Problem: optimize smooth, strongly convex functions where full-gradient methods incur O(√κ log(1/ε)) expensive full-gradient evaluations when κ is large.  
- Proposal: Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) — epoch-based method computing one full gradient per epoch and using variance-reduced "mixed" stochastic corrections within inner iterations and a shrinking trust region.  
- Main theoretical result: EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (high-probability guarantee).  
- Relation to prior work: Similar goals to variance-reduced methods (SAG, SDCA) but presented for more general stochastic objectives and convex constraints and with low memory O(d).  
- Strength — Originality: Novel idea to decouple number of full-gradient calls from κ, addressing a key bottleneck for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Strength — Theoretical quality: Rigorous epoch-based analysis using martingale concentration yields strong high-probability convergence guarantees.  
- Strength — Generality and memory: Applies to constrained problems (if projection is efficient) and uses low memory compared to methods like SAG.  
- Strength — Clarity: Paper is well-written, motivation and comparisons are clear.  
- Weakness — High stochastic cost: O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients per epoch may be impractical for large κ and could beat competitors like SDCA which have O(κ).  
- Weakness — No experiments: Entirely theoretical with no empirical validation to assess practical regimes, constants, or comparisons.  
- Weakness — Requires known parameters: Needs κ (or L and λ) a priori to set inner iteration count T, limiting practicality and robustness.  
- Feedback — Add experiments: Include empirical comparisons (Nesterov, SGD, SAG, SDCA) across varying n and κ to demonstrate practical benefits.  
- Feedback — Discuss κ²: More discussion on settings where κ² is acceptable and clearer characterization of practical regimes where EMGD wins.  
- Feedback — Improve dependency: Explore incorporating acceleration or other techniques to reduce κ-dependence (e.g., from κ² toward κ).  
- Overall: Novel and theoretically solid contribution but practical viability is unclear due to O(κ²) stochastic cost and lack of empirical results.
