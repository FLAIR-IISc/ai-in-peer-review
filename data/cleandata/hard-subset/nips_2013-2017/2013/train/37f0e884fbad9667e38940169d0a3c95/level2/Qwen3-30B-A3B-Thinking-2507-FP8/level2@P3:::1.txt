

The paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization that reduces the dependence on the condition number κ by combining full and stochastic gradients. The key contribution is achieving O(log 1/ε) full gradient accesses (vs. O(√κ log 1/ε) for full-gradient methods) while using O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients. Theoretical analysis proves linear convergence with high probability, and comparisons show computational advantages over Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n²/³ for problems decomposable as F(w) = (1/n)∑fᵢ(w).

Strengths include a rigorous convergence proof (Theorem 1) using martingale inequalities, clear exposition of the mixed gradient mechanism (combining ∇F(ŵᵏ) with ∇fₖₜ(wₖₜ) − ∇fₖₜ(ŵᵏ)), and meaningful practical insights. The algorithm extends beyond unconstrained problems (unlike SAG/SDCA) and requires only O(d) storage (vs. O(n)). The high-probability convergence guarantee (vs. expectation-based for SAG/SDCA) is a notable theoretical contribution. The paper effectively contextualizes its work against related literature, including Nesterov's accelerated methods [17], SAG [22], and SDCA [23], and correctly identifies the trade-off between full/stochastic gradient costs.

Weaknesses involve practical assumptions and unaddressed limitations. The requirement that κ must be known beforehand (Section 5) is significant for real-world applications, and while the paper notes this as future work, it lacks concrete estimation strategies beyond vague references to Hessian sampling. The stochastic gradient oracle (Of) is assumed to provide unbiased estimates, but the paper does not discuss implementation challenges (e.g., how to efficiently obtain fₖₜ(·) for general problems beyond sum-of-functions forms). The computational complexity claim (κ ≤ n²/³ for advantage over Nesterov) relies on the assumption that full gradients cost n times more than stochastic ones, which may not hold universally (e.g., for distributed settings where full gradient computation can be parallelized). The comparison in Table 2 oversimplifies by ignoring communication costs in distributed implementations, where SAG/SDCA might have advantages.

The paper is technically sound, with careful handling of strong convexity and smoothness assumptions. The proof of Theorem 1 is thorough, though the condition T ≥ 1152L²/λ² log(1/δ) (Equation 5) appears conservative; tighter bounds might be achievable. Clarity is generally good, but Section 3.2's description of the mixed gradient could be enhanced by explicitly contrasting it with semi-stochastic methods [12,13] to highlight novelty. The significance is strong for ill-conditioned problems where full gradient evaluation dominates computation, but the scope is limited to smooth, strongly convex settings—extending to non-smooth or non-strongly convex cases would broaden impact.

Minor improvements would strengthen the paper: (1) Add a brief discussion on practical κ estimation (e.g., leveraging regularizers as in Section 5), (2) Clarify the Of oracle's feasibility for non-sum-of-functions problems, and (3) Refine Table 2 to include communication costs for distributed settings. These are reasonable revisions that address key limitations without requiring major rework.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by demonstrating condition-number-independent full gradient access. The algorithm provides a practical alternative to full-gradient methods for ill-conditioned problems where stochastic gradients are inexpensive, with clear advantages in storage and convergence guarantees. While the κ-knowledge assumption is a drawback, the work is well-motivated, technically rigorous, and advances the state of the art in first-order optimization. The results are significant for machine learning applications involving large-scale regularized problems (e.g., with λ ≥ τ > Ω(n⁻²/³)), where the conditions hold. The paper meets NIPS quality standards and is suitable for acceptance.
