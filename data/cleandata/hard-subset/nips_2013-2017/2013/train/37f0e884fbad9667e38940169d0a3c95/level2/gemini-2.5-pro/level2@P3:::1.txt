This paper addresses the problem of optimizing smooth and strongly convex functions, where the convergence rate of first-order methods typically depends on the condition number κ. For ill-conditioned problems where κ is large, methods like accelerated gradient descent require a large number of expensive full gradient computations. The main contribution of this work is a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that reduces the number of required full gradient evaluations to be independent of the condition number, achieving this by additionally leveraging access to cheaper stochastic gradients.

The proposed EMGD algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at a reference point. Within the epoch, it performs a fixed number of iterations using a "mixed gradient," which is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient constructed using the full gradient from the start of the epoch and stochastic gradients at the current and reference points. This update is performed over a search domain that shrinks in each successive epoch. The primary theoretical result shows that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate, requiring only O(log 1/ε) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradient evaluations to find an ε-optimal solution. This successfully decouples the number of expensive full gradient computations from the condition number κ.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The goal of making the number of full gradient computations independent of the condition number is highly relevant for large-scale, ill-conditioned optimization problems. The proposed method provides a new and interesting trade-off between full and stochastic gradient calls. The combination of an epoch-based structure, a variance-reduced gradient, and a shrinking optimization domain is a novel approach to achieve this specific goal. The result is significant as it presents a new point in the algorithmic design space that could be highly effective when full gradients are the primary computational bottleneck.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed EMGD algorithm. A key strength of the analysis is that it establishes a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation results common to related stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA. The proof is clearly laid out and appears technically sound.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The algorithm is described precisely, and the main theoretical results are stated formally. The relationship to prior work in both deterministic and stochastic optimization is well-summarized, providing good context for the contribution. The paper also clearly articulates several practical advantages of EMGD over competing methods like SAG and SDCA, including its applicability to constrained optimization problems and its lower memory requirements.

**Areas for Improvement:**

*   **Dependence on Problem Parameters:** A significant practical limitation of EMGD is that setting the number of inner-loop iterations `T` requires knowledge of the smoothness and strong convexity constants, `L` and `λ` (and thus the condition number κ). This assumption is common in theoretical analyses but can be a barrier to practical application. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this limitation in the main body. For instance, addressing the sensitivity of the algorithm to mis-estimation of κ or suggesting potential adaptive strategies for setting `T` would be valuable for practitioners.

*   **Computational Complexity Comparison:** The analysis of computational complexity compared to SAG and SDCA could be more nuanced. The total number of stochastic gradient evaluations for EMGD has a quadratic dependence on the condition number (O(κ²)). While the paper correctly identifies a regime (κ ≤ n²/³) where EMGD is theoretically superior to Nesterov's method, it may be less competitive against SAG or SDCA when κ is large (e.g., κ on the order of `n`). In this case, the O(κ²) term could dominate, making EMGD slower. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs across different regimes of `n` and κ would provide a more complete picture.

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is solid, the work would be substantially more impactful with the inclusion of numerical experiments. An empirical study could serve several purposes: (1) validate the theoretical linear convergence rate in practice, (2) demonstrate how the algorithm's performance scales with the condition number κ, and (3) provide a direct performance comparison against key baselines like accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA on synthetic or real-world datasets. Such experiments would provide valuable insights into the practical utility of the proposed method and the tightness of the derived bounds.

*   **Connection to Concurrent Variance Reduction Methods:** The "mixed gradient" update is a form of variance reduction that is identical to the one used in the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method [Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013], a concurrent development. While the novelty of EMGD lies in its unique epoch structure and shrinking domain to achieve its specific theoretical goal, acknowledging this connection to SVRG would better position the work within the literature on variance-reduced stochastic methods.

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization. The main contribution—achieving a number of full gradient evaluations that is independent of the condition number—is significant and well-supported by a rigorous high-probability convergence analysis. The work is clear and makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field. The primary weaknesses are the reliance on knowing the condition number and the lack of empirical validation. Addressing these points, particularly by adding an experimental section, would greatly enhance the paper's impact. Despite these limitations, the theoretical novelty and importance of the problem being addressed make this a strong contribution.
