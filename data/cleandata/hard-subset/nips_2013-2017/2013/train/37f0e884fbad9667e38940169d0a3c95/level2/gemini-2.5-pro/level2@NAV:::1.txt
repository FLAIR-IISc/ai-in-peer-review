This paper addresses the problem of expensive full gradient computations in smooth and strongly convex optimization, particularly for ill-conditioned problems. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which utilizes both full and stochastic gradients. The core claim is that EMGD can find an ǫ-optimal solution by computing O(log 1/ǫ) full gradients, a number independent of the condition number κ, at the cost of computing O(κ² log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradients. This is achieved by dividing optimization into epochs, where one full gradient is computed at the start of each epoch and then combined with stochastic information for subsequent updates. This work builds on the epoch-based framework of Hazan and Kale [9] but introduces a novel mixed-gradient update rule to achieve a different trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations, distinguishing it from methods like SAG and SDCA which are tailored for finite-sum objectives.

**Strengths (Pros for acceptance):**
1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main contribution is a novel theoretical result that decouples the number of full gradient evaluations from the condition number κ, trading it for an increased number of cheaper stochastic gradient steps. This is a significant conceptual contribution to first-order optimization, addressing a well-known bottleneck in large-scale machine learning.
2.  **Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound, providing a high-probability linear convergence guarantee. The authors clearly state their assumptions and provide a detailed proof sketch. They also offer a thoughtful comparison to related work (SAG, SDCA), highlighting EMGD’s advantages in broader applicability to constrained problems and its lower memory footprint.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and structured. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is presented cleanly, and the main theoretical result is stated upfront.

**Weaknesses (Cons for acceptance):**
1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of experiments. Without empirical results, it is impossible to assess the practical performance of EMGD. A comparison against Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA on datasets with varying condition numbers would be essential to validate the claimed benefits and understand the practical impact of the O(κ²) stochastic gradient complexity.
2.  **Practicality:** The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ to set its parameters (specifically T, the number of iterations per epoch). While the conclusion briefly mentions strategies for estimating κ, this is a major practical limitation that is not addressed in the main body of the paper.
3.  **Comparison to SVRG:** The mixed gradient update is highly reminiscent of the variance-reduced gradient in SVRG. The paper would be strengthened by explicitly discussing the relationship and differences between EMGD and other variance reduction methods.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically interesting and novel idea. However, the lack of any experimental validation makes its practical significance difficult to judge. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, its current form is incomplete for a top-tier conference. I would encourage the authors to add a strong empirical section and resubmit.
