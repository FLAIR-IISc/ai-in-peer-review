Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order algorithm for smooth, strongly-convex optimization that combines occasional full gradients with frequent stochastic gradients. The main claim is that EMGD achieves linear convergence while requiring only O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations, independent of the condition number κ, and O(poly(κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The method resembles semi-stochastic and variance-reduction ideas (e.g., SAG, SDCA), but differs by (i) working for general constrained problems, (ii) using a high-probability analysis, and (iii) controlling variance via an epoch center and shrinking trust region. The analysis uses smoothness, strong convexity, and a martingale concentration argument to show per-epoch error contraction. The paper positions EMGD against Nesterov’s accelerated method and finite-sum methods (SAG, SDCA), arguing favorable trade-offs in memory and distributed implementation.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated goal: decouple the count of full-gradient calls from κ while maintaining linear convergence.
- Solid, careful theory: the main theorem provides high-probability linear convergence with explicit dependence on L, λ, T, and δ.
- Conceptual novelty: a clean mixed-gradient update that is easy to implement and applicable to constrained settings with low memory footprint.
- Useful perspective relative to finite-sum methods: provides conditions under which EMGD may be preferable (e.g., constrained problems, limited memory, distributed full gradients).

Weaknesses:
- Inconsistency in complexity: the abstract and early text state O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, whereas Theorem 1 and the per-epoch requirement imply O(κ^2 log(1/ε)). This must be reconciled.
- Lack of empirical validation: no experiments comparing EMGD to strong baselines (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) on ill-conditioned problems, constrained settings, or distributed implementations.
- Practicality concerns: the method assumes knowledge of κ (and δ) to set T and step sizes; the shrinking trust-region requires solving constrained subproblems each iteration.
- Novelty relative to variance-reduction/control-variate methods should be clarified; the mixed gradient is closely related to known techniques.

Quality: The analysis looks technically sound, though the κ vs κ^2 issue needs correction. Clarity: Generally clear, but some notation is dense; explicitly summarizing the parameter schedule to reach ε would help. Originality: Moderate; contribution is the κ-independent full-gradient count and constrained/high-probability treatment. Significance: Potentially useful, especially for constrained problems, but practical impact is uncertain without experiments.

Suggestions:
- Clarify and correct the stochastic gradient complexity (κ vs κ^2); if κ^2 is required, discuss whether it is improvable.
- Provide empirical comparisons on ill-conditioned tasks, including constrained problems and distributed settings.
- Add adaptivity (e.g., estimating L, λ) and discuss eliminating the shrinking ball via proximal regularization.
- More explicit comparison with SAG/SDCA and related semi-stochastic methods, including memory/runtime trade-offs.

Pros: strong theory; constrained applicability; low memory; high-probability guarantees. Cons: complexity inconsistency; no experiments; dependence on κ; per-iteration projection overhead.
