

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization that aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations required to find an ε-optimal solution. The authors claim EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (independent of condition number κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, compared to O(√κ log(1/ε)) for standard gradient descent.

The paper is well-organized and the theoretical analysis appears technically sound, with a clear proof of linear convergence. The authors provide a reasonable comparison with related work, including full-gradient methods and stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA.

Strengths include the clear motivation for addressing the computational cost of full gradients in ill-conditioned problems, and the thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence properties. The paper makes a reasonable effort to contextualize the work within the optimization literature.

However, several weaknesses significantly limit the paper's contribution. First, the novelty claim is overstated: the paper fails to adequately differentiate EMGD from existing "semi-stochastic" methods (e.g., [12, 13]) that also combine full and stochastic gradients and achieve linear convergence rates. For instance, SAG [22] already achieves linear convergence for finite-sum problems with O(n log(1/ε)) complexity, which is comparable to EMGD's O(κ² log(1/ε)) when κ is small.

Second, the paper lacks empirical validation. Without experiments demonstrating the practical benefits of EMGD (particularly compared to SAG, SDCA, and other methods), the theoretical claims remain unverified for real-world impact. This is particularly problematic given the significant practical assumption that κ must be known beforehand.

Third, the comparison with SAG and SDCA is superficial. The paper claims advantages in storage requirements (Ω(d) vs. Ω(n)) and high-probability convergence, but fails to provide evidence that these theoretical advantages translate to practical performance gains. The claim that "SAG and SDCA only hold in expectation" is inaccurate, as these methods also have high-probability convergence guarantees.

Fourth, the practical significance is limited by the κ knowledge assumption. The authors acknowledge this as a drawback but don't propose a convincing solution (only mentioning "future work" on estimating κ). In most machine learning applications, κ is not known a priori, making this a significant practical constraint.

The significance assessment is limited: while the problem addressed is valid, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. The claimed computational advantage (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) may not hold in many practical settings where κ can be large. The paper would be stronger with more substantial empirical results and a more nuanced discussion of how EMGD differs from existing methods.

The paper's main contribution is a theoretically sound algorithm with a clear convergence analysis, but the novelty is limited and the practical significance is not well-supported. For NIPS, which emphasizes both theoretical and empirical contributions, this paper falls short in the latter aspect. The theoretical analysis is good, but without empirical validation or a clearer demonstration of practical advantages over existing methods, the contribution's significance is diminished.

To improve, the authors should: 1) provide a more thorough comparison with existing semi-stochastic methods, 2) include empirical results demonstrating practical benefits, and 3) address the κ knowledge assumption with a concrete proposal for estimation. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution appears marginal compared to existing work in the field.
