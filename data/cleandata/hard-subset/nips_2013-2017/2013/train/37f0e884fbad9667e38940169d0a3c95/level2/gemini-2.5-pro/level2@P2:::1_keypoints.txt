- Problem: Optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions is costly because optimal first-order methods need O(√κ log(1/ε)) expensive full gradients for ill-conditioned problems (large κ).  
- Proposal: Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) alternates epochs with one full gradient and many updates using a mixed gradient that combines the fixed full gradient with a variance-reduced stochastic term.  
- Core theoretic result: EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, making full-gradient count independent of κ.  
- Analysis strength: Sound induction-based proof and martingale concentration give a high-probability convergence bound stronger than typical in-expectation results.  
- Comparison: Positioned against Nesterov, SAG, SDCA; claims advantages in handling constraints, lower memory, and high-probability guarantees.  
- Strength: Significant and original contribution—novel hybrid approach that circumvents full-gradient lower bounds and advances variance-reduced methods.  
- Strength: Clear presentation with helpful pseudocode, proof sketch, and strong related-work comparison.  
- Weakness: No empirical evaluation—lack of experiments leaves practical performance and hyperparameter sensitivity unvalidated.  
- Weakness: Requires knowing condition number κ (or L and λ) to set inner-loop length T, limiting practical usability.  
- Weakness: Stochastic gradient cost is O(κ²), which may be worse than competitors (e.g., SDCA) for severe ill-conditioning; regime-dependent efficiency unclear.  
- Suggestion: Add experiments (e.g., regularized logistic regression) comparing EMGD to accelerated methods and SDCA/SAG.  
- Suggestion: Discuss or develop adaptive schemes/heuristics to set T without prior knowledge of κ.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly frame the mixed gradient as a variance-reduction technique and relate it to concurrent SVRG work (Johnson & Zhang).  
- Accept argument: Novel, theoretically significant, and clearly presented result making full-gradient complexity κ-independent.  
- Reject argument: Practical impact is uncertain due to absence of experiments and reliance on unknown κ.
