This paper proposes a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), to reduce the computational cost of solving smooth and strongly convex optimization problems, particularly when the condition number is large. The key idea is to combine full gradient and stochastic gradient information, aiming to achieve an iteration complexity of O(log 1/ǫ) with respect to the number of full gradients, independent of the condition number.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical challenge in large-scale optimization where evaluating full gradients can be expensive. The presentation is generally clear, with a structured format and a detailed explanation of the algorithm. The related work section provides a good overview of existing methods and positions the proposed approach within the broader context of optimization. The theoretical analysis appears solid, and the convergence rate result is significant. The comparisons with existing algorithms (SAG, SDCA, Nesterov’s method) are useful and highlight the potential benefits of EMGD under certain conditions.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of combining full and stochastic gradients in an epoch-based manner to achieve condition number independence in the number of full gradient evaluations is a valuable contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The convergence analysis appears rigorous and provides a clear understanding of the algorithm’s performance.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm is well-defined and explained, and the paper is generally easy to follow.
*   **Relevant Comparisons:** The comparisons to existing algorithms provide context and demonstrate the potential advantages of EMGD.
*   **Well-Motivated:** The problem addressed is practically important in many machine learning applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical results are promising, the paper lacks discussion on practical implementation details and potential challenges. For example, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of the step size η, and how can it be tuned effectively?  The assumption of knowing the condition number κ beforehand is a significant limitation not sufficiently addressed. The suggested methods for estimating κ require further elaboration and analysis of their accuracy and computational cost.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any experimental results. While theoretical analysis is important, demonstrating the performance of EMGD on real-world datasets or synthetic problems would significantly strengthen the paper.  Comparisons against state-of-the-art solvers, beyond the theoretical complexity analysis, would be highly valuable.
*   **Notational Overload:** The use of superscripts and subscripts for indexing within Algorithm 1 is somewhat confusing and could be simplified for better readability.
*   **Redundancy:** Some phrases and sentences repeat information across sections (e.g., the discussion of the trade-off between iterations and per-iteration cost).
*   **Specificity of Assumptions:**  The assumption about the function oracle Of is a bit abstract. Providing more concrete examples of functions that satisfy this assumption would improve clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm that addresses a significant practical problem in optimization.
*   The convergence analysis provides a clear understanding of the algorithm’s performance and demonstrates its potential advantages over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical effectiveness of the algorithm.
*   The assumption of knowing the condition number κ beforehand is a significant limitation.
*   The paper lacks discussion of practical implementation details and potential challenges.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical results are strong, and the proposed algorithm is novel and well-motivated. However, the lack of empirical evaluation and the assumption of knowing the condition number beforehand are significant concerns. 

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should prioritize adding experimental results to demonstrate the performance of EMGD on real-world datasets or synthetic problems. They should also address the practical challenges associated with estimating the condition number κ and provide more detailed guidance on choosing appropriate parameters.  Addressing the minor issues regarding notation and redundancy would also improve the clarity of the paper.
