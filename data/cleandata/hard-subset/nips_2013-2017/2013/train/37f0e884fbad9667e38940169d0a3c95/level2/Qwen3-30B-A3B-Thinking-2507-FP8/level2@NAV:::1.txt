

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm that aims to reduce the condition number dependence in gradient-based optimization for smooth and strongly convex problems. The main claim is that by utilizing both full and stochastic gradients, EMGD can achieve an ǫ-optimal solution with only O(log(1/ǫ)) full gradient evaluations (compared to O(√κ log(1/ǫ)) for standard methods) while requiring O(κ² log(1/ǫ)) stochastic gradients. The authors position this work as a significant improvement over both full-gradient methods (like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) and purely stochastic methods (like SAG and SDCA).

The theoretical analysis is strong, with a detailed proof of linear convergence rate and complexity bounds. The paper correctly situates its contribution within the convex optimization literature, referencing key works by Nesterov, SAG, and SDCA. The comparison in Table 2 provides useful insights into computational complexity trade-offs. The algorithm's advantages over existing methods are well-articulated, particularly regarding applicability to constrained problems, lower storage requirements (O(d) vs. Ω(n)), and high-probability convergence guarantees.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses. First, it lacks empirical validation to support the theoretical claims, which is particularly concerning for a conference paper where practical relevance is important. Second, while the authors acknowledge that knowing κ is required, they don't provide a practical solution for estimating κ in real applications, which is a substantial limitation for implementation. The paper also doesn't discuss how the O(κ²) stochastic gradient complexity might impact practical performance.

Strengths: Solid theoretical foundation, clear novelty in combining full and stochastic gradients, comprehensive literature review, and well-motivated problem statement.

Weaknesses: No empirical results to validate the theory, insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges (especially regarding κ), and limited comparison with recent methods like SVRG or SAGA.

Arguments for acceptance: The theoretical contribution is sound and addresses an important computational bottleneck in optimization. The paper makes a clear and novel proposal that advances the state of the art in convergence rates.

Arguments against acceptance: The absence of empirical validation is a major drawback for a conference paper, and the practical implementation challenges are not adequately addressed.

The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach with potential practical value, but the lack of empirical results and insufficient discussion of implementation details significantly weaken its contribution. I recommend rejection unless the authors can provide empirical validation and address the κ estimation problem in a revision.
