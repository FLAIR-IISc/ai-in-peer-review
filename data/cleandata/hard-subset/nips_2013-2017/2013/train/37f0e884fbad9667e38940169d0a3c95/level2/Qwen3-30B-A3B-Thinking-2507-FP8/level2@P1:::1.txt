

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization that reduces dependence on the condition number κ by combining full and stochastic gradients. The authors demonstrate that EMGD achieves an ǫ-optimal solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations (compared to O(√κ log(1/ε)) for standard methods) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations.

The paper is well-motivated and builds upon the rich literature of convex optimization. The related work section effectively summarizes state-of-the-art methods, particularly highlighting recent advances in linear convergence methods like SAG and SDCA. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between full gradient and stochastic gradient evaluations, which is especially important for ill-conditioned problems where κ is large.

The main strength of the paper is the well-designed mixed gradient approach and thorough theoretical analysis. The algorithm is novel in its specific combination of full and stochastic gradients, and the proof of Theorem 1 is clear and rigorous. The comparison with existing methods (Table 2) is insightful, highlighting EMGD's advantages in handling constrained problems and requiring lower storage than SAG and SDCA.

However, the paper has three key weaknesses that need addressing. First, the practical significance of reducing full gradient complexity from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) is not sufficiently established. While the authors claim this trade-off could lead to computational efficiency, they don't provide concrete examples where EMGD would outperform existing methods like SAG (which achieves O(n log(1/ε)) complexity for problems of the form F(w) = (1/n)∑fi(w)). The comparison with SAG and SDCA in Table 2 is misleading because it doesn't account for the fact that SAG and SDCA are specifically designed for sum-of-functions problems, while EMGD is more general.

Second, the paper assumes the condition number κ is known beforehand, which is a significant practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion but don't provide a concrete solution for estimating κ in practice, which is crucial for the algorithm's implementation.

Third, the assumption that stochastic gradients are "efficient" to compute is vague. The paper lacks a clear analysis of the computational cost trade-off between full and stochastic gradients, which is essential for understanding when EMGD would be practically advantageous.

The paper is well-written with clear mathematical notation and logical flow. However, the comparison with SAG and SDCA could be made more precise by explicitly stating the problem settings under which each method is applicable.

The theoretical contribution is sound, with rigorous proofs and careful handling of probabilistic guarantees. The authors correctly reference relevant prior work and acknowledge their approach's limitations.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution to optimization theory that addresses an important practical concern. While the practical significance could be strengthened with more concrete examples and a solution for the κ estimation problem, the paper makes a valuable addition to the literature.

Pro: Novel algorithm design, solid theoretical analysis, clear writing, insightful comparison with existing methods.
Con: Limited practical significance discussion, condition number assumption, comparison with SAG/SDCA could be more precise.
