This paper addresses the problem of optimizing smooth and strongly convex functions, where the number of expensive full gradient computations required by optimal first-order methods, O(sqrt(κ) log(1/ε)), is prohibitively large for ill-conditioned problems (large κ). The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both full and cheaper stochastic gradients to reduce the dependency on the condition number.

The core idea of EMGD is to structure the optimization into epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient," which combines this fixed full gradient with a variance-reduced stochastic term. This approach allows the algorithm to achieve a geometric reduction in error per epoch, leading to an overall linear convergence rate. The main theoretical contribution is showing that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients. This result is significant because it makes the number of expensive full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ.

The paper situates this work relative to classic accelerated methods (Nesterov), stochastic methods, and, importantly, recent methods for finite sums like SAG and SDCA that also achieve linear convergence. The authors provide a compelling comparison, highlighting advantages of EMGD in handling constrained problems, requiring less memory, and providing a high-probability convergence guarantee rather than one in expectation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem in large-scale optimization. The main result—achieving a number of full gradient computations that is independent of the condition number κ—is a novel and significant theoretical contribution. The proposed hybrid approach, combining epoch-based progression with variance-reduced stochastic updates, is an elegant way to bypass the lower bounds for methods that use only full gradients. This work is an early and important contribution to the line of research on variance-reduced stochastic methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and is the core strength of the paper. The proof structure, based on induction over epochs and the use of a martingale concentration inequality to bound the stochastic error term, is clear and well-executed. The resulting high-probability convergence bound is stronger than the in-expectation guarantees common for many stochastic algorithms.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The algorithm is presented clearly in pseudocode, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The analysis section provides a helpful sketch of the main proof idea before diving into the technical details, which aids readability. The comparison with related work in Section 3.4 is particularly strong, as it considers multiple practical dimensions beyond just computational complexity (e.g., memory, constraints, parallelization).

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the absence of any experiments is a significant weakness. Numerical simulations, even on synthetic data, would be invaluable for several reasons: (i) to validate the theoretical rates in practice, (ii) to understand the real-world performance trade-offs against baselines like accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA, and (iii) to investigate the sensitivity of the algorithm to its hyperparameters. Without experiments, the practical impact of the proposed method remains speculative.

2.  **Dependence on Unknown Parameters:** A major practical limitation is that the algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ (or L and λ) to set the number of inner-loop iterations `T`. In most practical applications, these parameters are not known a priori. The authors acknowledge this as a drawback in the conclusion, but this limitation significantly hinders the algorithm's off-the-shelf usability. A more prominent discussion of this issue and potential heuristics for setting `T` would strengthen the paper.

3.  **High Complexity in Stochastic Gradients:** The number of stochastic gradients required, O(κ² log 1/ε), has a quadratic dependence on the condition number. While the goal of reducing full gradients is achieved, the κ² factor could make the algorithm very slow for severely ill-conditioned problems, potentially undermining the overall efficiency gains. This compares unfavorably with methods like SDCA, whose complexity scales linearly with κ. A more detailed discussion of the parameter regimes (in terms of n and κ) where EMGD is expected to outperform its competitors would be beneficial.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

*   I strongly encourage the authors to include an experimental section in a future version of this work. Comparing EMGD against Nesterov's accelerated method and SDCA/SAG on a problem like regularized logistic regression would provide crucial insights into its practical performance.
*   The discussion on the requirement of knowing κ could be expanded. Are there any adaptive schemes that could be used to set `T`? Even a brief exploration of this would increase the paper's practical relevance.
*   The mixed gradient `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is an early instance of a variance reduction technique, similar in spirit to the one used in the concurrent SVRG work (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). Explicitly framing the "mixed gradient" as a variance reduction tool would better place the contribution in the context of this important and growing subfield.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and theoretically significant result on a core optimization problem.
*   The idea of making full gradient evaluations independent of κ is a strong conceptual contribution.
*   The paper is technically sound and clearly written.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The complete lack of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical value and tightness of the theoretical bounds.
*   The algorithm's reliance on knowing the condition number κ is a major practical drawback.

In summary, this is a high-quality theoretical paper with a novel and elegant core idea. The significance of achieving a full-gradient complexity independent of κ is high. However, the work is held back by its purely theoretical nature and the practical issue of needing to know κ. Given the strength and novelty of the theoretical contribution for a 2013 audience, I lean towards acceptance, but I would have rated the paper significantly higher had it included empirical validation.
