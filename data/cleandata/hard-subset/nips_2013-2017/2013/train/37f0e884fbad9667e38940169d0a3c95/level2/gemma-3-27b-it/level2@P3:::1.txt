This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ when solving smooth, strongly convex optimization problems. The core idea is to leverage both full and stochastic gradients, achieving O(log 1/ǫ) full gradients, a significant improvement over the standard O(√κ log 1/ǫ) complexity. The work is clearly motivated by the computational expense of evaluating full gradients in large-scale problems.

The strengths of this work are severalfold. First, the problem addressed – reducing the computational cost of optimization with ill-conditioned problems – is highly relevant and practically important. Second, the proposed algorithm appears to offer a theoretical improvement in the number of full gradient evaluations, a key bottleneck in many applications. Third, the paper provides a reasonably thorough theoretical analysis, including a convergence rate theorem and a comparison to existing algorithms. The inclusion of a discussion on practical considerations and potential future work (estimating κ) adds value. The related work section is also well-organized and provides sufficient context. The comparisons to SAG and SDCA are insightful, highlighting the trade-offs in terms of computational cost and applicability.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the theoretical results are promising, the constant factors hidden within the O(log 1/ǫ) notation are not discussed. These constant factors could be substantial, potentially limiting the practical advantage of EMGD. The algorithm itself (Algorithm 1) is presented without a significant amount of explanation. A more detailed discussion of the implementation details, such as how the domain size ∆k is updated and the selection of the step size η, would be beneficial. The analysis relies heavily on the assumption of smoothness and strong convexity, and the performance under more relaxed conditions (e.g., weaker convexity) is not explored. The discussion regarding estimating the condition number κ feels somewhat superficial. Providing more concrete suggestions, beyond simply mentioning concentration inequalities, would be helpful. While the comparisons to existing methods are good, a more thorough experimental evaluation demonstrating the practical benefits of EMGD would significantly strengthen the paper. The discussion of distributed computing in relation to the gradient oracle call in step 4 is promising but lacks detail; how would this be implemented, and what are the communication costs? The justification for choosing T ≥ 1152L²/λ² ln(δ) seems rather opaque – more explanation of this threshold would be appreciated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to reducing full gradient evaluations.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees, demonstrating a potential improvement over existing methods.
*   Well-motivated problem with practical relevance.
*   Comprehensive related work section.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of experimental validation.
*   Missing discussion of constant factors in the complexity analysis.
*   Limited detail regarding implementation aspects.
*   Superficial discussion of estimating the condition number.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization. However, the lack of experimental results and the absence of detailed implementation guidance limit its impact. Addressing these weaknesses would significantly enhance the paper’s quality and increase its likelihood of influencing future research.

**Recommendations:**

1.  **Essential:** Include experimental results demonstrating the performance of EMGD on a variety of benchmark problems, comparing it to existing algorithms.
2.  **Essential:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the implementation of the algorithm, including practical considerations for selecting parameters and handling numerical issues.
3.  **Major:** Discuss the constant factors hidden within the O(log 1/ǫ) notation and their potential impact on the practical performance of the algorithm.
4.  **Minor:** Elaborate on the techniques for estimating the condition number κ in practice, providing concrete examples and analysis.
5.  **Minor:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of the parameter T and the rationale behind the threshold 1152L²/λ² ln(δ).
