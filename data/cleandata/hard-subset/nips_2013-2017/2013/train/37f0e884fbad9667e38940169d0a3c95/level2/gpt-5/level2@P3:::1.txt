Summary
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization with access to two oracles: a full gradient oracle Og returning ∇F(w) and a stochastic function oracle Of returning f such that F(w) = E[f(w)], with each f L-smooth. It proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, EMGD computes one full gradient at a snapshot point w̄; each inner iteration then uses a mixed gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄), followed by a projected proximal step over a shrinking Euclidean ball centered at w̄. With an epoch length T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)) (κ = L/λ), the analysis shows a constant-factor reduction in suboptimality per epoch, yielding linear convergence: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations to reach ε accuracy with high probability. The paper contrasts EMGD with accelerated full-gradient methods (O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients), and with finite-sum methods such as SAG and SDCA.

Relation to prior work
The mixed-gradient estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is closely related to variance-reduction methods for finite-sum problems, notably SAG (Roux et al., NIPS 2012), SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, JMLR 2013), and SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), where the latter also computes a snapshot full gradient and uses the same control variate to achieve linear convergence. SVRG establishes O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations and O(log(1/ε)) full gradients for finite-sum problems. EMGD differs in its oracle setting (general expectation oracle with access to an exact full gradient) and in the constrained update over a shrinking ball; however, its stochastic complexity scales as O(κ^2 log(1/ε)), which is worse than the O(κ log(1/ε)) dependence achieved by SVRG (finite-sum) and SDCA. The paper does not cite SVRG, which appears highly pertinent given the identical gradient estimator and epoch structure.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: the algorithm is simple and well-motivated as a control-variate scheme that reduces variance using a periodic full gradient.
- Oracle model: the framework does not require a finite-sum structure; it assumes only access to an exact full gradient and a stochastic f with the same smoothness constant. The method applies to constrained problems via projections onto W ∩ B(w̄; Δ).
- High-probability linear convergence: the analysis yields a per-epoch contraction with explicit dependence on δ, not only in expectation.
- Memory efficiency: unlike SAG/SAGA/SDCA, the method uses O(d) memory.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Missing closely related work: the connection to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) is not discussed. EMGD’s mixed gradient is precisely the SVRG estimator; both use snapshot full gradients. The claimed advantages and differences should be positioned against SVRG, including complexity, assumptions (finite sum vs. expectation), constrained settings, and high-probability vs. expectation guarantees.
- Suboptimal κ dependence: the per-epoch length T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)) gives O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients overall, which is weaker than the O(κ log(1/ε)) dependence known for finite-sum variance-reduced methods and also for some later expectation-oracle variants. It would be useful to clarify whether κ^2 is inherent to this oracle setting/analysis or an artifact of proof and the shrinking-ball device.
- Strong convexity inequalities: Equation (4) states ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖, which is not a standard consequence of λ-strong convexity; the standard property is monotonicity: ⟨∇F(w) − ∇F(w′), w − w′⟩ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖^2. Relatedly, in (13) the bound F(wt) − F(w∗) ≤ ⟨∇F(wt), wt − w∗⟩ − λ‖wt − w∗‖^2 appears to miss a factor 1/2; the usual bound is with λ/2. These discrepancies should be corrected, and the constants in the main theorem re-checked.
- Update rule consistency: Algorithm Step 8 and Equation (11) appear inconsistent regarding the quadratic term. In (11) the subsequent analysis produces terms with 1/(2η), while the algorithmic statement shows 1/2‖w − wt‖^2 without 1/η. The strong convexity constant of the subproblem also depends on this scaling. This needs clarification.
- Practicality: the method requires knowledge of L, λ, and hence κ, to set T and η. Although the conclusion section mentions estimating κ, the current analysis is not adaptive. The shrinking domain and projection onto B(w̄; Δk) may introduce additional computational overhead and tuning complexity in practice.
- Empirical evaluation: no experiments are provided. Given the stated computational tradeoffs, even a small empirical study (e.g., comparison to accelerated GD and, when applicable, SAG/SDCA/SVRG) would strengthen the case.

Quality
The high-level idea is sound and the proof strategy (variance control via control variates and a shrinking trust region, with a union-bound high-probability guarantee) is reasonable. However, the technical issues around strong convexity inequalities, scaling in the proximal subproblem, and missing references reduce confidence in the current form.

Clarity
The presentation is generally readable, but several notational and typographical issues hinder precision (e.g., the strong convexity inequality, scaling in the subproblem, factor-of-two constants, and minor typos). The algorithm would benefit from a precise statement of the update with correct scaling and a short discussion of projection costs.

Originality
The estimator and epoch design are very similar to SVRG. Novelty rests mainly in the oracle setting (general Of with exact full gradient access), high-probability analysis with shrinking balls, and applicability to constrained problems. The lack of discussion of SVRG makes it difficult to assess the genuine novelty.

Significance
The goal—reducing full-gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)) while relying on cheaper stochastic gradients—is important. However, the κ^2 stochastic complexity is not competitive with known finite-sum methods; significance would be higher if either (a) the κ dependence were improved, or (b) a compelling application domain required the general Of oracle and constrained setting where finite-sum methods are inapplicable.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Correct the strong convexity statements and constants (replace (4) with the standard inequality and fix the factor 1/2 in (13)).
- Clarify Step 8/Equation (11) scaling and ensure consistency throughout the proof.
- Add and discuss SVRG and related variance-reduction work, carefully contrasting assumptions, complexity (especially κ dependence), memory, and constrained applicability.
- Discuss whether κ^2 can be improved to κ in this oracle setting, or explain why κ^2 appears necessary with the current technique.

Optional but beneficial:
- Provide empirical illustrations on constrained and unconstrained problems, and, when a finite-sum structure exists, compare to accelerated GD, SVRG, SAG, and SDCA.
- Explore adaptivity to unknown L and λ (e.g., backtracking for L, doubling tricks for epoch lengths).
- Explain computational cost of the ball projection and whether it can be replaced by a simpler trust-region or step-size control without shrinking domains.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple algorithm; high-probability linear rate; applicability to constrained problems; O(d) memory; reduction of full-gradient dependence to O(log(1/ε)).

Cons against acceptance
- Overlap with SVRG without explicit discussion; weaker κ dependence; technical inaccuracies; no experiments; reliance on known κ and potentially costly projections.

Overall, the work addresses an important problem with a clean idea, but corrections, positioning with respect to closely related methods, and clarification of technical details are needed to reach the expected standard.
