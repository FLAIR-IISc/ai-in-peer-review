- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) using periodic full gradients and a control-variate mixed gradient with projected proximal steps over shrinking balls.  
- Claims linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations (κ = L/λ) with high probability.  
- Mixed-gradient estimator is essentially the same control variate used in SVRG and finite-sum variance-reduction methods.  
- EMGD differs by assuming access to an exact full-gradient oracle in a general expectation setting and by using shrinking-ball constrained updates.  
- The paper fails to cite or sufficiently compare to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013), despite identical estimator and epoch structure.  
- Conceptually simple and well-motivated as a variance-reduction/control-variate scheme.  
- Oracle model is more general than finite-sum methods and allows constrained problems via projections.  
- Analysis provides high-probability per-epoch contraction (not just expectation) and uses only O(d) memory.  
- Stochastic complexity scales as O(κ^2 log(1/ε)), which is worse than O(κ log(1/ε)) from SVRG/SDCA for finite-sum problems.  
- Unclear whether the κ^2 dependence is inherent to the oracle setting or an artifact of the analysis/shrinking-ball device.  
- Incorrect strong-convexity statements and missing 1/2 factor in key inequalities (e.g., Eq.(4) and Eq.(13)); constants need correction.  
- Inconsistency between algorithm Step 8 and Equation (11) regarding scaling of the quadratic term and subproblem strong convexity.  
- Method requires knowing L and λ (κ) to set parameters (T, η); analysis is not adaptive and shrinking projections add practical overhead.  
- No empirical evaluation; experiments comparing to accelerated GD, SVRG, SAG, SDCA would strengthen the paper.  
- Presentation has notational/typographical issues that reduce precision; update rule and projection cost should be clarified.  
- Novelty is limited given similarity to SVRG; claimed contributions are general oracle setting, high-probability analysis, and constrained applicability.  
- Significance would be higher if κ dependence improved or a compelling application required the general Of oracle and constrained setting.  
- Essential fixes: correct strong-convexity inequalities, fix scaling consistency, add SVRG-related discussion, and address κ^2 vs κ question.  
- Optional improvements: provide experiments, explore adaptivity to unknown L/λ, and discuss projection/trust-region alternatives.  
- Pros for acceptance: simple algorithm, high-probability linear rate, constrained applicability, O(d) memory, reduced full-gradient calls.  
- Cons against acceptance: overlap with SVRG without discussion, suboptimal κ dependence, technical inaccuracies, no experiments, and reliance on known κ.
