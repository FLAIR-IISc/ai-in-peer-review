Summary and contributions:
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm for minimizing smooth and strongly convex objectives that has access to (i) a full gradient oracle ∇F(w) and (ii) a stochastic function oracle f(·) with Ef[f(w)] = F(w) and L-smoothness. EMGD proceeds in epochs; each epoch computes one full gradient at the epoch center w̄k and performs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k), combined with projected gradient steps onto a shrinking ball B(w̄k; Δk). The main result shows a linear convergence rate: after m epochs, F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ λΔ1^2/2^{m+1}, using m full gradients and mT stochastic gradients, where T = O(κ^2 log(1/δ)). Thus, the number of full gradients is O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ, while the number of stochastic gradients is O(κ^2 log(1/ε)). The analysis provides a high-probability guarantee using a martingale concentration bound.

Relation to prior work:
- For smooth, strongly convex optimization with full gradients, Nesterov’s accelerated methods achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) iteration complexity, which motivates reducing the number of full gradient evaluations.
- In the finite-sum setting F = (1/n)∑ fi, methods with linear rates using only stochastic/partial gradients include SAG (O(n log(1/ε)) under n ≥ 8κ) and SDCA (O((n + κ) log(1/ε))). The paper discusses these and claims advantages in constraint handling, memory, and high-probability guarantees.
- Crucially, the proposed mixed gradient estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is identical to the variance-reduced estimator later popularized by SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which also uses one full gradient per epoch and stochastic differences, but achieves O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) total gradients in the finite-sum case and O(κ) stochastic steps per epoch without shrinking domains. This close overlap (both algorithmically and conceptually) is not discussed or cited and materially affects the assessment of originality and significance.
- The paper emphasizes a more general stochastic oracle model (not restricted to finite sums) and constrained domains, which distinguishes it from SAG/SDCA; however, EMGD still requires exact full gradient access, which in practice most naturally corresponds to the finite-sum setting.

Quality:
- The analysis is generally careful, leveraging smoothness, strong convexity, and a martingale difference sequence to control the stochastic error. The projection onto shrinking balls ensures bounded variance of the stochastic part. The induction over epochs is standard and sound given the assumptions.
- There is an inconsistency between the abstract and the main theorem: the abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, whereas Theorem 1 and the discussion clearly yield O(κ^2 log(1/ε)). This should be corrected.
- The requirement T ≥ Ω(L^2/λ^2 log(1/δ)) implies κ^2 dependence in the number of stochastic gradients per epoch. This is suboptimal relative to existing results for related estimators (e.g., SVRG) that obtain O(κ) inner iterations per epoch, and suggests the analysis may be loose (the shrinking-domain technique may be unnecessary).
- The setting assumes access to true full gradients at epoch centers. In the non-finite-sum setting, this may be impractical; the paper should clarify where such an oracle is realistic.
- No experiments are provided to validate practical behavior, sensitivity to κ mis-specification, robustness of the ball-projection step, or comparisons to SAG/SDCA/SVRG on standard tasks.

Clarity:
- The paper is mostly readable, but there are typos and notational glitches (e.g., repeated equation labels, minor mismatches in inequalities, awkward constraints like δ ≤ e^{-1/2}). The algorithm description would benefit from clearer motivation for the shrinking ball and a discussion of the cost of the constrained subproblem. The comparison section’s claims (e.g., “only unconstrained”) should be carefully qualified.

Originality:
- The core mixed-gradient idea aligns very closely with known variance-reduction techniques; the estimator is the same as in SVRG. The novelty here is mainly the general oracle model and the high-probability analysis with shrinking domains for constraints. However, given the overlap, failing to cite or compare to SVRG (NIPS 2013) significantly weakens the originality claim.

Significance:
- Reducing dependence on full gradients to O(log(1/ε)) is useful, especially when full gradients are costly. However, the κ^2 dependence makes EMGD less attractive than competing methods in the finite-sum setting. The constrained version and memory footprint are positives, but without empirical evidence or tighter theory, the impact is limited.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple algorithm with a clear, high-probability linear convergence proof.
- Works with constrained domains and requires O(d) memory.
- Reduces number of full gradient computations to O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ.

Cons against acceptance:
- Core estimator and epoch structure overlap strongly with SVRG; lack of citation and comparison.
- Suboptimal κ^2 dependence vs. O(κ) achievable by related methods; analysis appears conservative.
- Abstract/theorem inconsistency; several clarity and presentation issues.
- No experiments to support practical relevance or to validate claimed advantages.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Cite and compare thoroughly to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and related variance-reduction methods; explain what is new (e.g., constrained setting, high-probability analysis) and where EMGD is preferable.
- Tighten the analysis to aim for O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch; consider removing the shrinking-domain requirement and using standard variance-reduction arguments.
- Fix the abstract to match the theorem, and clean up notation and minor typos. Clarify the practicality of the full gradient oracle in non-finite-sum settings.
- Include empirical results comparing EMGD to Nesterov, SAG, SDCA, and SVRG on convex problems (with and without constraints), reporting both full and stochastic gradient counts and wall-clock time.
- Address sensitivity to the knowledge of κ and propose adaptive strategies.

Overall recommendation: Reject in current form due to overlap with existing variance-reduction methods and suboptimal complexity, coupled with missing comparisons and empirical validation. The work could become competitive if the novelty is better delineated and the κ dependence and presentation issues are addressed.
