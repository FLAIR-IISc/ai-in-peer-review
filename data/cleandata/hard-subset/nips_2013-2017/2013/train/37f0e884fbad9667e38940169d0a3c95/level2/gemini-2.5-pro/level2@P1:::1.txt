This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. For this class of problems, the optimal first-order method using full gradients (e.g., Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) has a complexity of O(sqrt(κ) log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. When κ is large, the required number of expensive full gradient evaluations can be prohibitive. The central idea of this work is to develop a hybrid algorithm that leverages both full and cheaper stochastic gradients to remove the dependence on κ from the number of full gradient calls.

The proposed algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), operates in a series of epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at the current solution. This full gradient is then used as a stable reference point for a sequence of inner iterations. Within each iteration, a "mixed gradient" is formed by correcting the stored full gradient with a variance-reducing term derived from a fresh stochastic gradient. This update is performed within a shrinking trust region. The authors prove that this procedure achieves a linear convergence rate. The main theoretical result is that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This effectively shifts the dependence on the condition number from the expensive full gradient computations to the cheaper stochastic ones. This work is related to the emerging class of variance-reduced stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA, which also achieve linear convergence rates for finite-sum problems, but EMGD is presented in a more general setting that can handle constraints.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—decoupling the number of full gradient evaluations from the condition number κ—is a novel and significant idea. It directly addresses a well-known bottleneck in optimizing ill-conditioned problems over large datasets and offers a new perspective on trading off different types of oracle access.
2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm. The proof, which relies on an epoch-based analysis and a martingale concentration inequality to bound the stochastic terms, appears sound. The resulting high-probability convergence guarantee is stronger than the in-expectation results common for many stochastic algorithms.
3.  **Generality:** The EMGD framework is quite general. Unlike SAG and SDCA which are specifically designed for finite-sum objective functions and unconstrained problems, EMGD can be applied to problems with more general stochastic objectives and convex constraints, provided the projection in step 8 is efficient. It also has a low memory footprint (O(d)), which is an advantage over methods like SAG that require storing past gradients.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the algorithm is presented cleanly, and the theoretical results and their implications are stated precisely. The comparison with related work in Section 3.4 is helpful for positioning the contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **High Complexity in Stochastic Gradients:** A major concern is the O(κ²) dependence in the number of required stochastic gradients per epoch. While shifting the burden from full to stochastic gradients is the goal, a quadratic dependence on κ can be very steep. For problems where κ is large (e.g., 10³ or 10⁴), this would require a very large number of inner-loop iterations, potentially making the algorithm impractical and less efficient than competitors like SDCA, which has only an O(κ) dependence.
2.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. Without any experiments, it is difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed algorithm. An empirical comparison against Nesterov's method, SGD, SAG, and SDCA on benchmark datasets would be crucial to demonstrate in which practical regimes (i.e., for what values of n and κ) EMGD provides a tangible benefit. The constants hidden in the big-O notation can play a significant role in practice.
3.  **Dependence on Known Parameters:** The algorithm requires the condition number κ (or L and λ) to be known in advance to set the number of inner iterations `T`. This is a common requirement in theoretical analyses but a significant practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion and suggest future work, but the paper would be stronger if it included a discussion on the robustness of the algorithm to misspecifications of these parameters or potential heuristics for setting them.

**Constructive Feedback:**
*   The primary suggestion is to include an experimental section. This would tremendously strengthen the paper by validating the theoretical claims and showing where EMGD fits in the landscape of practical optimizers. A study showing the trade-off as κ varies would be particularly illuminating.
*   The O(κ²) dependency deserves more discussion. It would be helpful to more clearly characterize the problem settings where κ² is manageable and the proposed method is expected to outperform alternatives.
*   It may be worth exploring whether techniques from accelerated methods could be incorporated to improve the dependency on κ, perhaps reducing it from κ² to κ.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for an important problem. The idea of making the number of full gradient computations independent of the condition number is a valuable contribution. However, the practical viability of the method is unclear due to the high O(κ²) dependency on stochastic gradients and the complete absence of empirical validation. Despite these concerns, the novelty of the idea and the quality of the theoretical analysis make it a solid contribution to the optimization literature.
