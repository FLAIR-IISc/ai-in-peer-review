

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm that reduces the dependence on condition number κ for smooth strongly convex problems by combining full and stochastic gradients. The authors show EMGD achieves O(log(1/ǫ)) full gradient evaluations (vs. O(√κ log(1/ǫ)) for standard methods) with O(κ² log(1/ǫ)) stochastic gradients, enabling linear convergence. The work builds on key NIPS contributions including Nesterov's accelerated methods (2004, 2005), Rakhlin et al.'s stochastic optimization (2012), and Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang's SDCA (2013), while addressing the practical limitation of high computational cost for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with a rigorous convergence analysis using induction and martingale techniques. The proof is technically sound and properly contextualizes the algorithm's relationship to prior work. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured presentation, clear pseudocode, and consistent notation. The originality is significant: the mixed gradient approach represents a novel combination of techniques that differentiates EMGD from SAG, SDCA, and pure stochastic methods. The significance is substantial, as reducing full gradient evaluations could substantially improve computational efficiency for large-scale problems.

Notable strengths include the careful theoretical analysis, clear differentiation from prior work, and thoughtful discussion of practical limitations (e.g., requiring knowledge of κ). The paper also offers valuable insights into constrained optimization and distributed computing applications.

Key weaknesses include the absence of empirical validation to support the theoretical claims, which would strengthen the quality assessment. The requirement to know κ beforehand is a practical limitation that isn't addressed with a solution. While the computational complexity analysis assumes full gradients are n times more expensive than stochastic, this ratio may vary across applications.

Pro-acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution, clear novelty, significant reduction in full gradient evaluations, well-written with proper contextualization.

Con-acceptance: Missing experimental results, practical limitation of κ knowledge requirement.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that advances optimization methods. While the lack of experiments is a drawback, the theoretical novelty and significance warrant acceptance. The authors should consider adding empirical validation in future work to further strengthen their claims.
