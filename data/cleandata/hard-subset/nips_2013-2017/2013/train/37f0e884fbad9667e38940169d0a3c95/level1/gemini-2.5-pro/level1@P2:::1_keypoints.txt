- Problem: First-order optimization for smooth, strongly convex functions with large condition number κ where standard accelerated methods incur a prohibitive √κ factor.  
- Contribution: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that mixes one full gradient per epoch with variance-reduced stochastic updates.  
- Main theoretical result: EMGD attains linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, decoupling full-gradient count from κ.  
- Relation to prior work: Fits into variance-reduced methods (SAG, SDCA, SVRG); presented in a general stochastic framework rather than finite-sum, but conceptually similar to periodic full-gradient variance reduction.  
- Proof quality: Theoretical analysis is detailed and appears correct, using standard convex optimization tools and concentration inequalities.  
- Missing experiments: No empirical results to validate practical performance, parameter sensitivity, or comparisons to competitors.  
- Stochastic complexity weakness: O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient complexity is worse than the O(κ) (or O(n+κ)) of SVRG/SAG, hurting competitiveness for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Clarity: Well-written and organized; clear motivation, pseudo-code, and theorem statements; analysis is dense but coherent.  
- Originality: Highly original epoch-based mixed-gradient design that independently discovered variance-reduction via periodic full gradients.  
- Practical advantages claimed: Applicable to constrained problems, low memory O(d), and conceptual breakthrough in reducing full-gradient dependence on κ.  
- Practical limitations: Requires knowledge of κ to set inner iterations T and has high stochastic-gradient cost, limiting real-world applicability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel and significant theoretical contribution, originality, generality, and clarity.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Complete lack of experiments, suboptimal O(κ²) stochastic complexity, and dependence on knowing κ.  
- Conclusion: The paper is a valuable and elegant theoretical advance with important ideas, but its practical utility is undermined by absent experiments and quadratic κ-dependence in stochastic cost.
