

# Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that reduces the dependence on the condition number κ by combining full and stochastic gradients. The authors address a well-known limitation of first-order methods: for ill-conditioned problems, standard methods require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, which can be prohibitively expensive for large datasets. EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, effectively removing the condition number dependence from the full gradient count.

The paper builds upon recent advances in convex optimization, particularly the work on stochastic methods like SAG [22] and SDCA [23], and semi-stochastic methods like those in [9, 10, 21]. It extends the epoch gradient descent framework [9] by introducing a "mixed gradient" that combines full and stochastic gradients, leveraging the smoothness property to bound the stochastic component. The authors carefully compare their approach to relevant literature, noting that while SAG and SDCA achieve linear convergence, they require Ω(n) storage and only work for unconstrained problems, whereas EMGD handles constrained optimization with Ω(d) storage.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis with a rigorous proof of convergence (Theorem 1). The authors carefully handle the trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients, providing precise bounds on the number of each type of gradient required. The proof methodology is standard for convex optimization but well-executed, with clear induction steps and careful handling of probabilistic bounds. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the requirement to know κ in advance, and suggests future work to address this. However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims, which is a significant omission for a NIPS paper. Without experimental results demonstrating the practical benefits of EMGD, it's difficult to assess whether the theoretical improvements translate to real-world performance gains.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to analysis. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is detailed and precise, and the notation is consistent throughout. The proof is structured in a way that makes it relatively accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization. However, the paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the mixed gradient approach works, particularly regarding the role of the shrinking domain size and the specific form of the mixed gradient. The comparison with SAG and SDCA in Table 2 is helpful but could be strengthened with a more detailed explanation of the computational cost assumptions.

**Originality:** The paper presents a genuinely novel algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients in a way that reduces the condition number dependence of full gradient evaluations. While the general idea of combining full and stochastic gradients isn't entirely new (see [12] for semi-stochastic methods), the specific implementation and theoretical guarantees are distinct. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, acknowledging related work while clearly differentiating its contributions. The authors make a strong case that their approach differs from previous methods in terms of storage requirements, handling of constrained problems, and convergence guarantees (with high probability rather than in expectation).

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to large-scale machine learning applications where full gradient computations are expensive. The reduction from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations represents a meaningful theoretical improvement for ill-conditioned problems. The paper provides a compelling argument for when EMGD might be preferable to existing methods (κ ≤ n²/³), and highlights practical advantages such as lower storage requirements and applicability to constrained problems. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the lack of empirical results and the practical limitation of requiring knowledge of κ in advance.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel algorithm that effectively removes condition number dependence from full gradient count
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees
- Comprehensive comparison with relevant literature highlighting advantages in storage and constrained optimization
- Addresses a meaningful practical problem in large-scale machine learning

*Con:*
- Lacks empirical validation of theoretical claims (critical omission for NIPS)
- Requires knowledge of condition number κ in advance (practical limitation)
- Does not sufficiently explain why the stochastic gradient count is O(κ²) rather than lower order
- The computational cost comparison assumes full gradient is n times more expensive than stochastic gradient without justification

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the optimization literature, but the lack of empirical results is a significant weakness for a NIPS submission. The theoretical analysis is sound and the approach is novel, but without experimental validation, it's difficult to assess the practical impact of the method. The requirement to know κ in advance is also a notable limitation that could restrict applicability.

In summary, this paper is technically sound and makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to the optimization literature. However, the absence of empirical results and the practical limitation of requiring knowledge of κ make it less compelling as a NIPS submission. While the theoretical results are interesting, they would be significantly strengthened by empirical validation. The paper would be stronger if it included experiments comparing EMGD to existing methods on real optimization problems, demonstrating the practical benefits of the theoretical improvements.

I recommend rejection due to the lack of empirical validation, which is a standard expectation for NIPS papers. The theoretical contribution is interesting but insufficient without experimental support to demonstrate practical utility. The authors should add empirical results before resubmission to a conference like NIPS.
