Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies smooth and strongly convex optimization with access to two oracles: a full-gradient oracle and a stochastic function oracle that yields unbiased stochastic gradients of L-smooth components whose expectation is the objective. The central contribution is Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which proceeds in epochs. Each epoch computes one full gradient at a reference point w̄k and performs T inner iterations using a “mixed gradient” g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k). The inner update is a proximal/gradient-mapping step over a shrinking ball around w̄k. Using smoothness, strong convexity, and a high-probability martingale bound, the analysis shows linear convergence: after m epochs, the suboptimality decreases by 2−m up to constants. Setting T = Ω(κ2 log(1/δ)), the algorithm attains ε accuracy using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients with probability at least 1 − mδ.

The update g̃t is a classical control-variate/variance-reduction form used in semi-stochastic methods. The paper situates EMGD relative to full-gradient accelerated methods [17] and finite-sum methods with linear convergence such as SAG [22] and SDCA [23]. In the finite-sum setting, a full gradient costs n component gradients, so EMGD’s total cost is O((n + κ2) log(1/ε)) component gradients.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea grounded in a well-specified oracle model; clean epoch structure.
- Uses a standard but well-executed high-probability analysis (Hoeffding–Azuma) to establish linear convergence w.h.p., not only in expectation.
- Memory footprint O(d), and the method can handle constrained problems via projected/proximal updates on W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk).
- The proof is self-contained and uses standard smooth/strongly convex inequalities; constants and parameter dependencies are explicit.

Weaknesses
- Inconsistency: the abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, but the main text and Theorem 1 imply O(κ2 log(1/ε)). This must be corrected.
- Complexity is dominated by κ2, which is substantially worse than several contemporary variance-reduced methods (e.g., SAG [22], SDCA [23], and the concurrent SVRG/Prox-SVRG family) that achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) without shrinking domains and with O(d) memory. The paper does not compare to SVRG-style methods, despite using the same gradient estimator.
- Requires prior knowledge of κ to set T and the epoch schedule; this limits practicality. An adaptive procedure is suggested only briefly in the conclusion.
- No empirical evaluation to support practical efficiency claims (e.g., constrained settings, distributed full-gradient step), which are important for a NIPS audience.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The analysis follows standard templates: rewrite with a control-variate decomposition around w̄, bound the variance term via smoothness and the shrinking radius, and use martingale concentration to get high-probability bounds. The per-epoch contraction and union bound yield linear convergence.
- The strong convexity inequality used (‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖) follows from the gradient strong monotonicity and Cauchy–Schwarz, and is consistent with the assumptions. The proximal update objective is 1-strongly convex as written, matching later use.
- The requirement T = Ω(κ2 log(1/δ)) is the bottleneck leading to the κ2 factor; the paper does not argue lower bounds that justify this dependence nor discuss whether tighter analysis could reduce it to κ.
- The comparison section is qualitative and partially outdated: it omits SVRG-type methods that use the identical gradient estimator and achieve better dependence on κ.

Clarity
- Generally well organized with a clear algorithm box and theorem statement. The proof is readable and detailed.
- Notable issues: “epoches” should be “epochs.” The O(κ) vs O(κ2) inconsistency must be fixed throughout (abstract, introduction, and contributions). A concise, apples-to-apples complexity table in the finite-sum setting (total component gradient evaluations and memory) would improve clarity.

Originality
- The mixed gradient estimator and epoch scheme closely mirror variance-reduction methods (SAG/SVRG/SDCA). The most similar is SVRG’s estimator ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄) + ∇F(w̄); EMGD adds a shrinking ball and provides high-probability guarantees. Given the contemporaneous development of SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) with superior κ dependence and O(d) memory, the novelty is limited unless the constrained-setting analysis or high-probability guarantees are emphasized and shown to yield genuinely new advantages.

Significance
- The main theoretical contribution (reducing the number of full gradients to O(log(1/ε)) independent of κ) is conceptually appealing, but total gradient complexity is not state of the art due to the κ2 factor.
- Potential significance in constrained domains and low-memory regimes; however, the same estimator and convergence have been shown for related methods without κ2, which diminishes impact unless new constrained results are demonstrated empirically or theoretically beyond existing work.

Suggestions and questions
- Resolve the O(κ) vs O(κ2) discrepancy; if κ2 is correct, state this upfront and discuss whether analysis can be tightened to κ.
- Compare rigorously to SVRG/Prox-SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and subsequent SAGA (if relevant), including constraints and memory.
- Provide empirical results (including constrained problems and distributed full-gradient computation) to validate practical claims.
- Discuss adaptivity to unknown κ (e.g., doubling tricks for T, automatic radius shrinkage), and whether high-probability guarantees survive.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clean, correct high-probability linear-convergence analysis in a mixed-oracle model.
- Handles constraints with O(d) memory; conceptually simple and implementable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Core estimator and epoch idea overlap strongly with SVRG; missing comparison weakens novelty.
- κ2 dependence is materially worse than the state of the art; no evidence this is necessary.
- Inconsistency in stated complexity and no experiments.

Overall, this is a careful theoretical paper with clear analysis, but its novelty and significance are limited by overlap with known variance-reduction ideas and inferior κ dependence.
