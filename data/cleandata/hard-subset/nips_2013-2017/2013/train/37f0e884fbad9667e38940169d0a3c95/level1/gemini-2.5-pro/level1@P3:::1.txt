### Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Paper Summary**

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors identify a key bottleneck in existing optimal first-order methods, such as Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, whose iteration complexity of `O(sqrt(κ) log(1/ε))` requires a number of expensive full gradient evaluations that scales with the square root of the condition number `κ`. For ill-conditioned problems where `κ` is large, this can be computationally prohibitive.

The central contribution is a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to eliminate this dependency on `κ` for full gradient computations. The key idea is to leverage access to a stochastic gradient oracle in addition to the full gradient oracle. EMGD operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at the current reference point. Within the epoch, it performs multiple updates using a "mixed gradient" that combines this stored full gradient with a variance-reduction term constructed from stochastic gradients. This approach ensures that the variance of the gradient estimate is controlled within a shrinking local region.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves an `ε`-optimal solution by computing `O(log(1/ε))` full gradients and `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))` stochastic gradients. This result is significant because it shifts the main computational burden related to the condition number from expensive full gradient calls to cheaper stochastic gradient calls, achieving a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradient evaluations that is independent of `κ`. The paper compares this complexity to existing deterministic, stochastic, and recent hybrid methods like SAG and SDCA.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of the paper is highly original and significant. It directly confronts the `sqrt(κ)` barrier of optimal deterministic methods by proposing a novel way to combine information from two different oracles. The concept of using a small number of full gradients to "anchor" a sequence of stochastic steps, thereby achieving linear convergence without a `κ`-dependent number of full gradient evaluations, is a powerful and foundational contribution. This work opens a new avenue for designing efficient algorithms for ill-conditioned problems, which are common in machine learning.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous, high-probability convergence proof (Theorem 1). The analysis, which relies on an inductive argument over epochs and uses martingale concentration inequalities to bound the stochastic terms, is technically sound. The derivation clearly shows how the mixed gradient formulation, combined with a shrinking optimization domain, leads to a geometric reduction in error at each epoch. The high-probability guarantee is also stronger than the in-expectation results common for many stochastic algorithms.

3.  **Generality:** A major strength of EMGD is its generality. Unlike recent competing methods such as SAG and SDCA, which are specifically designed for the finite-sum problem structure `F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w)`, the proposed algorithm works for the more general stochastic objective `F(w) = E[f(w)]`. Furthermore, the update step (Step 8) is formulated as a projection onto a convex set, meaning EMGD can naturally handle constrained optimization problems, a feature lacking in many other variance-reduced methods.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is compelling, the algorithm is presented cleanly, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The authors do a good job of placing their work in the context of related literature from deterministic and stochastic optimization.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Complexity Dependence on `κ`:** While the number of full gradients is independent of `κ`, the number of stochastic gradients per epoch (`T`) scales with `κ^2`. The total stochastic gradient complexity of `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))` is quite high. For the important finite-sum setting, this may be less favorable than the `O((n + κ) log(1/ε))` complexity of SDCA or the (concurrently developed) SVRG algorithm. This `κ^2` factor could limit the practical applicability of the algorithm for problems with very large condition numbers. It is possible that the analysis is not tight and this dependency could be improved.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the absence of any experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of EMGD. An empirical comparison against Nesterov's AGD, SAG, and/or SDCA on a canonical machine learning problem (e.g., regularized logistic regression) would significantly strengthen the paper. Such an experiment would help to understand the practical trade-offs involved and in what regimes of `n` and `κ` EMGD is superior.

3.  **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The algorithm's parameters, particularly the number of inner iterations `T`, depend on knowledge of the smoothness `L` and strong convexity `λ` constants (i.e., `κ`). In many real-world applications, these constants are unknown and difficult to estimate. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation in the conclusion, but it remains a practical barrier to deployment.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   The "mixed gradient" `g̃_kt = ∇F(w̄^k) + ∇f_kt(w_kt) - ∇f_kt(w̄^k)` is a key component. This form is an unbiased estimator of the true gradient `∇F(w_kt)` and has reduced variance when `w_kt` is close to `w̄^k`. This is a beautiful construction and is a precursor to what is now known as the SVRG estimator. Highlighting this variance reduction property more explicitly in Section 3.2 could improve the intuition.

*   In the complexity comparison in Section 3.4, the analysis of when EMGD is superior to competitors is insightful. However, the condition `κ <= n^(2/3)` is quite restrictive. It would be helpful to discuss problem classes where this condition is likely to hold.

*   The proof relies on the assumption that `w*` is in the ball `B(w̄; ∆)`. While the induction shows `||w̄ - w*|| <= ∆`, it's worth briefly justifying that this holds for all subsequent iterates `w_t` as well, as they are projected onto this ball.

**Recommendation: Accept**

Despite the weaknesses, primarily the high `κ^2` dependency in the stochastic complexity and the lack of experiments, the novelty and significance of the core idea are substantial. The paper introduces a new algorithmic paradigm for tackling ill-conditioned problems that is both elegant and theoretically sound. It successfully demonstrates that the `sqrt(κ)` dependency on full gradient evaluations is not fundamental if one can leverage a stochastic oracle. This is a foundational result that is likely to inspire significant follow-up work. The paper's generality to handle general stochastic objectives and constraints further elevates its contribution above more specialized methods. While an experimental section would have made the paper much stronger, its theoretical contribution is sufficient for acceptance at NIPS.
