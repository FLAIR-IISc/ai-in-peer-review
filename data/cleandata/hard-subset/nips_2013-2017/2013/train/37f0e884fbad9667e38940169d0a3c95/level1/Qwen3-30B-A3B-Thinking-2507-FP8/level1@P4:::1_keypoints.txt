- Main idea: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that mixes full and stochastic gradients to get linear convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, improving full-gradient dependence for ill-conditioned problems.

- Relation to prior work: Situates EMGD against optimal full-gradient (O(√κ log(1/ε))) and stochastic methods, and contrasts with SAG/SDCA which need Ω(n) storage or are limited to separable/unconstrained settings.

Strengths:
- Technical soundness: Provides rigorous proof of linear convergence using standard convex-analysis tools and clear conditions for correctness.
- Novelty: Mixed full/stochastic gradient approach is new and reduces full-gradient dependence while handling constrained problems with only Ω(d) storage.
- Significance: Addresses a practical bottleneck (expensive full gradients) and is beneficial when κ ≤ n^(2/3) for many learning problems.
- Clarity: Well-written, organized, includes precise pseudocode and helpful comparisons (e.g., Table 2).

Weaknesses:
- No experiments: Completely theoretical with no empirical validation to demonstrate practical performance.
- Requires known κ: Algorithm needs the condition number a priori, which is rarely available and not addressed in practice.
- High stochastic complexity: O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient cost may be large compared to SAG/SDCA and limit practical gains.
- Incomplete comparisons: Does not engage fully with the latest relevant work (e.g., stochastic primal-dual or accelerated stochastic methods).

Overall assessment:
- A technically solid and novel theoretical contribution that meaningfully advances understanding of full vs. stochastic gradient trade-offs, but with practical limitations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important theoretical problem with clear practical motivation.
- Novel algorithm and rigorous analysis that improve full-gradient requirements.
- Low storage and constraint handling make it broadly applicable.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of empirical results weakens practical impact.
- Necessity of knowing κ is a significant practical drawback.
- Stochastic-gradient complexity may be inferior to state-of-the-art methods in practice.

Final recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance based on high-quality, original theoretical contribution despite practical limitations.
