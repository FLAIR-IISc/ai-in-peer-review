

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm that aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations required for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems by combining full and stochastic gradients. The key theoretical contribution is showing that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, rather than the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients required by full gradient methods. The approach builds on epoch gradient descent [9] but introduces a novel "mixed gradient" that combines full and stochastic gradients within each epoch.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work, particularly the semi-stochastic methods [12, 13] and recent linear convergence results for sum-of-functions problems [22, 23]. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between full gradient evaluations (computationally expensive but deterministic) and stochastic gradients (computationally cheaper but slower convergence). The paper also appropriately cites the seminal work on optimal convergence rates [17] that establishes the O(√κ log(1/ε)) bound for full gradient methods.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with a detailed inductive proof demonstrating the linear convergence rate. The authors carefully handle the stochastic component using martingale arguments (Lemma 2), which is a strength of the paper.
2. The paper clearly articulates the novelty: EMGD achieves condition number independent full gradient access without requiring the full problem structure (unlike SAG/SDCA that need to process all n functions).
3. The algorithm is well-motivated and the mixed gradient approach is elegant, effectively balancing the deterministic and stochastic components.
4. The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper provides no experimental results to demonstrate the practical benefits of EMGD. Given the strong theoretical claims, this is a significant omission for a NIPS paper, as empirical validation is crucial for establishing practical relevance.
2. **Confusing computational complexity comparison:** Table 2 claims EMGD has lower computational complexity than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but the analysis assumes full gradients are n times more expensive than stochastic gradients. This assumption needs better justification in the context of the sum-of-functions problem (1), as the paper doesn't clarify whether this is standard or reasonable.
3. **Missing critical assumption:** The paper assumes each f_i is λ-strongly convex (required for the SDCA-like analysis), but this assumption isn't explicitly stated in the problem formulation (1). This is a significant gap that would affect the algorithm's applicability.
4. **Condition number knowledge requirement:** The paper acknowledges that EMGD requires knowing κ beforehand, but doesn't adequately address how this limitation impacts practical use. The authors briefly mention future work on estimating κ, but this is a major practical drawback.
5. **Overstated advantage over SAG/SDCA:** The claim that EMGD's linear convergence holds "with high probability" while SAG/SDCA only have "expectation" convergence needs verification. Both SAG and SDCA can be shown to have high-probability convergence bounds with similar techniques.

**Comparative Analysis:**
The paper's main contribution is the reduction of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), which is theoretically significant. However, the O(κ²) stochastic gradient complexity is higher than SAG (O(n log(1/ε))) and SDCA (O((n+κ) log(1/ε))) when κ is large. The paper's claim that EMGD has "lower computational cost" than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3) is correct under their computational model, but this doesn't necessarily translate to practical advantages without empirical validation.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to reducing full gradient evaluations in smooth strongly convex optimization. The technical contribution is solid, but the lack of empirical results and the practical limitation of requiring known κ significantly weaken the paper. The theoretical novelty is substantial but not transformative compared to recent advances in stochastic optimization (e.g., [22, 23, 26]).

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The problem is important and well-motivated.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and correct.
- The algorithm is novel and addresses a practical limitation in optimization for ill-conditioned problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- No empirical validation to support theoretical claims.
- Critical assumption about strong convexity of individual f_i is not clearly stated.
- Practical limitation (requiring known κ) is not adequately addressed.
- Computational complexity comparison is confusing and lacks practical justification.

**Overall:** The paper makes a valid theoretical contribution but falls short of NIPS standards due to the absence of empirical results and insufficient practical discussion. With empirical validation and better clarification of assumptions, this could be a strong paper. As it stands, it's borderline for acceptance. I recommend rejection, but with strong encouragement to add empirical results and clarify the assumptions in a revised version.
