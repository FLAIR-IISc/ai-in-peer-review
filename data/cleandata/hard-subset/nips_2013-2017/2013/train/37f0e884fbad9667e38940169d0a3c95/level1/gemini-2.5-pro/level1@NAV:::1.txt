This paper proposes a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems. The primary motivation is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, whose optimal complexity O(√κ log 1/ǫ) depends on the condition number κ. EMGD achieves this by combining full and stochastic gradients in an epoch-based framework. In each epoch, the algorithm computes one full gradient and uses it to construct a variance-reduced "mixed gradient" for a series of O(κ²) stochastic updates within a shrinking domain. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD finds an ǫ-optimal solution with O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient and O(κ² log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradient evaluations. This work builds upon ideas from epoch-based stochastic optimization (Hazan & Kale, 2011) and variance reduction techniques like SAG and SVRG.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality & Significance:** The goal of making the number of full gradient computations independent of the condition number is novel and highly significant, especially for ill-conditioned large-scale learning problems where full gradients are a bottleneck. The proposed mixed-gradient approach is an elegant way to tackle this.
*   **Quality:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis, providing a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation results common for similar methods like SAG. The proof structure is clear and appears technically sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The algorithm is described precisely, and its relationship to and advantages over prior work (e.g., applicability to constrained problems, low memory overhead) are well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Practicality:** The algorithm's main drawback is its O(κ²) dependence on the number of stochastic gradients per epoch. For problems with a very large κ, this could make the overall computational cost prohibitive, potentially exceeding that of competing methods like SDCA. The authors' own analysis shows EMGD is only superior in a limited regime.
*   **Quality:** The paper lacks any experimental validation. While the theoretical contribution is solid, the absence of experiments makes it impossible to assess the practical significance of the proposed trade-off. Empirical comparisons against Nesterov acceleration, SAG, and SDCA on ill-conditioned problems are necessary to demonstrate if the theoretical benefits translate into real-world performance gains.
*   **Assumptions:** The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ to set its hyperparameters (specifically T), a common but significant limitation for practical application.

In summary, this paper introduces a theoretically sound and interesting algorithm for a well-motivated problem. However, the steep O(κ²) cost for stochastic gradients and the complete lack of empirical results significantly limit its current impact.
