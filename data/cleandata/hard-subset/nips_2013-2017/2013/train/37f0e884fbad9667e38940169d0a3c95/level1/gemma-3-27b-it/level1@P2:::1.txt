This paper proposes a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for strongly convex and smooth optimization, aiming to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ when using first-order methods. The core idea is to combine full gradient and stochastic gradient information within epochs, achieving a linear convergence rate with O(log 1/ǫ) full gradients – independent of κ.

The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the problem and proposed solution. It appropriately positions the work within the context of existing first-order methods, referencing relevant literature (e.g., Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent, SAG, SDCA, epoch gradient descent). The introduction effectively motivates the need for the algorithm, highlighting the computational cost of full gradients for ill-conditioned problems. The algorithm itself (Algorithm 1) is presented in a straightforward manner. The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, provides a convergence guarantee and demonstrates the desired reduction in full gradient evaluations. The comparison to other methods (Table 2) and discussion of limitations add to the thoroughness of the presentation.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly identifies a significant practical issue in optimization – the cost of full gradient evaluations for ill-conditioned problems – and proposes a solution.
* **Novelty:** The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework seems genuinely novel. While epoch-based methods exist, the specific mixing strategy presented here is a valuable contribution.
* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** Theorem 1 provides a rigorous convergence analysis, demonstrating the algorithm's linear convergence and the reduction in full gradient complexity.
* **Comprehensive Comparison:** The comparison with existing methods like Nesterov, SAG, and SDCA provides a good understanding of EMGD’s trade-offs and potential advantages.
* **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is easy to follow, with clear explanations and a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper doesn't delve deeply into practical implementation challenges. For instance, determining a "good" estimation of the condition number κ *beforehand* (as acknowledged in the conclusion) could be difficult in practice. The paper briefly touches on possible estimation methods but doesn't provide a detailed discussion.
* **Constant Factors:** The theoretical analysis focuses on asymptotic complexity (O() notation). The constant factors hidden within the O() notation could be significant and impact the algorithm’s performance in practice. A more detailed analysis, potentially including numerical experiments, could address this.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks experimental validation. While theoretical guarantees are valuable, demonstrating the algorithm’s performance on real-world datasets would strengthen the paper considerably.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption about the function oracle *Of* (providing a random function *f*) feels slightly abstract. How this translates to specific stochastic gradient estimation techniques in practical applications isn’t fully elaborated.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in optimization.
* The proposed algorithm is novel and offers a potentially significant improvement over existing methods.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and supports the claimed benefits.
* The paper is well-written and presents the material in a clear and organized manner.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation weakens the paper’s impact.
* The practical challenges of estimating the condition number are not adequately addressed.
* The discussion of constant factors and implementation details is limited.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed algorithm is innovative, and the theoretical analysis is compelling. However, the lack of empirical evaluation and the limited discussion of practical considerations are significant weaknesses.

**Rating:** 7/10 –  Strong Accept, but would benefit significantly from empirical results and a more thorough discussion of implementation details. The theoretical contribution is strong enough for acceptance, but addressing the weaknesses would elevate the paper's impact substantially. I recommend accepting the paper with the encouragement for the authors to include experimental results in a follow-up work.
