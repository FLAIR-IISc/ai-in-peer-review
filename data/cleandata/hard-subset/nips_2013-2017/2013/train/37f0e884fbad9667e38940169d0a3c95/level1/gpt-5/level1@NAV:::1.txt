Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth, strongly convex optimization with access to both full and stochastic gradients. Each epoch computes one full gradient at an anchor point and then performs T updates using a “mixed” estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄), with iterates projected into a shrinking ℓ2-ball around the anchor. The analysis shows linear convergence: O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full-gradient oracle and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle, with high-probability guarantees. Conceptually, the mixed gradient is a control-variate/variance-reduction estimator closely related to methods developed at NIPS, notably SAG (NIPS 2012), SVRG (NIPS 2013, Johnson & Zhang), and later SAGA. Unlike SAG/SDCA/SVRG in the finite-sum setting, EMGD is analyzed in a more general oracle model, and it emphasizes condition-number independence in the number of full-gradient evaluations.

Strengths: 
- Clear problem motivation: reducing expensive full-gradient evaluations for ill-conditioned problems. 
- Technically sound high-probability analysis using a shrinking-domain argument; the proof is careful and self-contained.
- The algorithm applies to constrained problems and requires only O(d) memory, addressing limitations of SAG/SDCA.
- The “cost per epoch = 1 full + T stochastic” pattern is simple and amenable to distributed computation for the full gradient.

Weaknesses:
- κ-dependence of stochastic gradients is κ^2, which is suboptimal compared to SVRG/SAGA’s O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) in the finite-sum case and O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic steps per epoch in related variance-reduction analyses.
- Empirical validation is missing; no experiments to substantiate constants, robustness to κ mis-specification, or practical performance against SAG/SDCA/SVRG.
- Related work is incomplete: the close connection to SVRG (NIPS 2013) and subsequent SAGA is not discussed; comparisons focus mainly on SAG/SDCA.
- Practicality concerns: the need to know κ to set T, and the shrinking-ball projection may be unnecessary or costly; adaptive epoch lengths or variance-based stopping could improve practicality.

Quality: Proofs appear correct and precise; assumptions are explicit. Clarity: Generally well written, though some notation and equation numbering could be tightened. Originality: The estimator and epoch structure mirror SVRG-like variance reduction; the main novelty is the κ-independent count of full gradients in a general oracle model with high-probability rates. Significance: Theoretical interest is moderate; practical impact is unclear without experiments and given κ^2 dependence.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, careful theory with high-probability linear convergence in a general oracle setting.
- Memory- and constraint-friendly variant of variance reduction.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Suboptimal κ dependence; lacks empirical evidence.
- Incomplete positioning relative to SVRG/SAGA; incremental novelty.
