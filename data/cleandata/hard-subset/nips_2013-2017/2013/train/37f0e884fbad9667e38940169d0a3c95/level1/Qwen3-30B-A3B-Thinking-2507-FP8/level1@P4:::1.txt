

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that combines full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations required. The key insight is that while standard first-order methods with full gradients require O(√κ log(1/ε)) evaluations (where κ is the condition number), EMGD achieves linear convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This is a significant theoretical improvement for ill-conditioned problems where κ is large.

The paper situates itself within the well-established body of work on convex optimization. It correctly references the optimal O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity for full gradient methods [17] and the O(1/λε) complexity for stochastic gradient methods [9, 10]. It also compares with recent approaches like SAG [22] and SDCA [23], which achieve linear convergence for problems with separable structure (F(w) = (1/n)∑f_i(w)). The paper identifies a gap in the literature: while these methods achieve linear convergence, they require either Ω(n) storage (SAG/SDCA) or only work for unconstrained problems.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of EMGD, proving linear convergence with clear bounds on the number of full and stochastic gradients required. The proof technique is sound, using standard tools from convex optimization (strong convexity properties, martingale inequalities) and carefully tracking the dependencies on κ. The conditions for the algorithm to work (δ ≤ e^(-1/2), T ≥ 1152L²/λ² log(1/δ)) are well-justified.

2. **Novelty**: The mixed gradient approach is genuinely novel. Unlike previous methods that either use only full gradients or only stochastic gradients, EMGD combines both in a way that reduces the dependence on κ for full gradient evaluations. The paper clearly articulates how EMGD differs from related work, particularly in its ability to handle both constrained and unconstrained problems with minimal storage requirements (Ω(d) vs Ω(n) for SAG/SDCA).

3. **Significance**: The problem addressed is important in machine learning, where full gradient computations can be prohibitively expensive for large datasets. The paper demonstrates that EMGD can outperform standard full gradient methods when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is a reasonable condition for many practical problems (e.g., regularized learning with τ > Ω(n^(-2/3))). The theoretical contribution represents a meaningful advance in understanding the tradeoffs between full and stochastic gradients.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm is presented with precise pseudocode, and the key concepts (mixed gradient, shrinking domain) are explained thoroughly. The comparison with related work in Table 2 provides valuable context for the contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical and lacks empirical results to validate the theoretical claims. While theoretical contributions are valuable, the absence of even minimal experiments (e.g., on a synthetic problem) weakens the paper's impact and makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the approach.

2. **Practical Limitation of Known κ**: The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ in advance, which is often unknown in practice. While the paper acknowledges this limitation and suggests future work on estimating κ, this is a significant drawback for practical applications. The paper doesn't provide guidance on how to estimate κ effectively in common machine learning scenarios.

3. **Stochastic Gradient Complexity**: The bound on the number of stochastic gradients (O(κ² log(1/ε))) is relatively large compared to other methods like SAG (O(n log(1/ε))) or SDCA (O((n+κ) log(1/ε))). While the paper shows EMGD can be more efficient than full gradient methods for κ ≤ n^(2/3), the practical advantage might be limited due to this large constant factor.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites recent NIPS work [9, 10, 21, 26] but doesn't engage with the most relevant recent advances in the field. For instance, the paper doesn't discuss how EMGD compares to the more recent work on "stochastic primal-dual" methods or "accelerated" stochastic methods that also achieve linear convergence for composite objectives.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound and theoretically significant contribution to convex optimization. The core idea of combining full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and correctly situates the work within the broader literature.

However, the lack of empirical validation and the requirement of knowing κ in advance are notable limitations. The paper would be significantly stronger with even basic experiments demonstrating the theoretical claims on a small-scale problem. The comparison with more recent methods in the field could also be expanded.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper addresses an important theoretical question in optimization with clear practical relevance.
- The algorithm and analysis are novel, technically sound, and make a meaningful contribution to the literature.
- The paper clearly articulates the advantages of EMGD over existing methods in terms of storage requirements and constraint handling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The absence of empirical results limits the paper's impact and makes it difficult to assess practical utility.
- The requirement of knowing κ in advance is a significant practical limitation that isn't addressed in the current work.
- The bound on stochastic gradient complexity (O(κ² log(1/ε))) is relatively large compared to state-of-the-art methods like SAG and SDCA.

## Final Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of convex optimization. The core idea is novel, the analysis is sound, and the results are significant for the theoretical understanding of optimization algorithms. While the lack of empirical results and the requirement of knowing κ in advance are limitations, these are common in theoretical papers and could be addressed in future work. The paper would be a strong addition to NIPS, particularly for the optimization community.

Given the high quality of the theoretical contribution and the clear significance of the problem addressed, I recommend acceptance. The paper meets all four criteria: it is of high quality, clearly written, original, and significant.
