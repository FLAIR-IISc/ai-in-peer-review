## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), to address the computational expense of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned strongly convex optimization problems. The core idea is to combine full and stochastic gradients within epochs to achieve a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ. This is a significant goal, as the standard iteration complexity for full gradient methods scales with √κ, which can be prohibitive for large κ.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost. The proposed EMGD algorithm builds on the idea of epoch-based methods and introduces a “mixed gradient descent” step. The theoretical analysis claims that EMGD achieves O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradient evaluations, demonstrating the desired independence from κ for full gradient accesses. The authors appropriately connect their work to related areas like deterministic, stochastic, and semi-stochastic optimization, referencing key papers such as Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent [17], SAG [22], and SDCA [23]. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of full and stochastic gradients in a structured epoch-based manner is a relatively novel approach.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating the convergence rate and complexity bounds.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The problem addressed – reducing the cost of full gradient evaluations – is well-defined and relevant to numerous applications.
*   **Connections to Prior Work:** The authors diligently relate their work to existing literature in optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks any experimental evaluation. While the theoretical analysis is valuable, it’s unclear how well EMGD performs in practice compared to existing methods, particularly given the κ² factor in the stochastic gradient complexity. The assumption of knowing κ beforehand could also be a limitation.
*   **Comparison Depth:** While related work is cited, the comparison to existing methods (SAG, SDCA) in Table 2 feels superficial. A more detailed analysis of the constant factors and practical implications of each algorithm would be beneficial.
*   **Proof Complexity:** The detailed proof in the appendix is quite lengthy and could benefit from increased clarity and organization. 
* **Limited Discussion of Applicability:** The paper mentions application to machine learning, but lacks a concrete discussion of scenarios where this algorithm might be particularly advantageous, such as specific model types or data characteristics.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to a significant problem in optimization.
*   The theoretical results are clearly stated and provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between different gradient types.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of experimental validation raises concerns about the practical relevance of the algorithm.
*   The κ² term in the stochastic gradient complexity could be a significant drawback in practice.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a worthwhile theoretical contribution to the field of optimization. However, the lack of experimental results and potential practical limitations raise concerns. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on the authors adding experimental validation and a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of their results, particularly in relation to the κ² term.  A clarification of how to estimate κ in practice would also strengthen the paper.
