Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization that can query two oracles: a full gradient oracle Og and a stochastic function oracle Of (yielding unbiased stochastic gradients). EMGD runs in epochs; at the start of each epoch it computes the full gradient at the epoch center w̄k, and then performs T projected updates with a “mixed” variance-reduced gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k). A shrinking trust region (ball around w̄k with radius Δk) controls the magnitude of the stochastic component via smoothness. The analysis shows linear convergence: after m epochs (m full gradients, mT stochastic gradients), one has F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ O(2−m), provided T = Ω(κ2 log(1/δ)), giving O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach ε-suboptimality with high probability.

This “control variate” mixed gradient is closely related to contemporaneous variance-reduction methods for finite sums, notably SAG (Roux et al., NIPS 2012) and SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013), which achieve linear convergence with complexities O(n log(1/ε)) (SAG, under n ≥ cκ) and O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) (SDCA). EMGD differs in that it does not require storing per-example gradients and explicitly uses an Og call each epoch. It is also very close in spirit to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which also computes a periodic full gradient and uses the same control variate form, achieving O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients. The paper cites SAG/SDCA and semi-stochastic methods (Marti, 1999–2003) but not SVRG; the core idea overlaps substantially.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The high-probability epoch-wise contraction proof is coherent: smoothness bounds the variance of the stochastic part within the shrinking ball; Azuma-Hoeffding controls the martingale term; choosing η = 1/(L√T) and T = Θ(κ2 log(1/δ)) yields a 1/2 reduction per epoch. However, two issues need attention:
  1) Inconsistency in rates: The abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, while Sections 1, 3.3, and Theorem 1 imply O(κ2 log(1/ε)). Please reconcile.
  2) Strong convexity is misstated. Equation (4) asserts ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖, which is generally stronger than λ-strong convexity (which implies (∇F(w) − ∇F(w′))⊤(w − w′) ≥ λ‖w − w′‖2). The proof appears to rely on the standard definition (e.g., Lemma 1), not on (4). Please correct and ensure no step uses the stronger assumption.
- Completeness: The analysis provides a high-probability linear convergence bound and clear parameter scaling, but lacks empirical evaluation. Given the algorithm’s practical motivation (reducing costly full gradients), experiments on standard datasets comparing to Nesterov AGD, SAG/SAGA, SDCA, and SVRG would substantially strengthen the paper.
- Assumption realism: The two-oracle model is reasonable for finite-sum problems (full gradient via a pass over data; stochastic via a sampled fi). The trust-region projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) might be more expensive than a standard projection onto W; the paper acknowledges this and assumes it can be done efficiently.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and the intuition behind the mixed gradient and shrinking domain is clear. There are several typos (duplicate equation numbering for (2), minor grammar), and the rate inconsistency noted above. The comparison section is helpful but could be tightened with explicit, consistent complexity counts in a common unit. It would help to clarify that the algorithm naturally extends to composite optimization with proximal steps (common in ML).

Originality:
The main idea—a periodic full gradient as a control variate to reduce variance of stochastic updates—is now well known via SAG/SDCA and, most closely, SVRG. Relative to SAG/SDCA, EMGD does not require O(n) memory and can accommodate constraints; relative to SVRG, EMGD uses a shrinking trust region and achieves a weaker κ2 dependence. If positioned as a general two-oracle formulation beyond finite-sum, that novelty should be emphasized; however, the full-gradient oracle presumes exactly the structure that finite-sum methods exploit in practice. The lack of citation/discussion of SVRG is a significant omission given the near-identical update form.

Significance:
The condition-number independence in the count of full gradients is attractive, and the high-probability linear convergence is stronger than the in-expectation guarantees in some related work. However, the overall stochastic complexity O(κ2 log(1/ε)) is not state of the art compared to O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) methods, and the practical impact is unclear without experiments. The constrained setting and low memory are potential advantages if demonstrated.

Pros:
- Clear and simple algorithm with an intuitive mixed-gradient update.
- Linear convergence with high probability; number of full gradients O(log(1/ε)) independent of κ.
- Low memory footprint; accommodates constraints in principle; easy distributed parallelism for the full-gradient step.

Cons:
- Stochastic complexity scales as κ2, substantially worse than SAG/SDCA/SVRG.
- Inconsistency between abstract and theorem (κ vs κ2).
- Misstatement of strong convexity; several minor presentation issues.
- No empirical results; unclear practical advantage.
- Overlaps heavily with SVRG; missing citation and comparison.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix the rate inconsistency and the strong convexity definition; audit the proof to ensure only standard strong convexity is used.
- Discuss and compare to SVRG explicitly, highlighting similarities/differences. If possible, strengthen the analysis to improve κ2 to κ (e.g., variance bound that scales with suboptimality rather than a worst-case Δ bound), which would align with SVRG/SAGA rates.
- Provide experimental evaluation on finite-sum problems, including constrained cases, comparing wall-clock and gradient counts to AGD, SAG/SAGA, SDCA, and SVRG.
- Discuss composite objectives (prox-EMGD) and adaptivity to unknown κ (e.g., doubling trick or adaptive choices of T).
- Clarify computational cost of the trust-region projection and when it is cheap.

Overall, the paper has a clean idea and a solid high-probability analysis, but given the overlap with existing/concurrent variance-reduction methods and the inferior κ dependence, I am not convinced of its novelty or impact in its current form. Empirical evidence and a tighter rate would substantially strengthen the case.
