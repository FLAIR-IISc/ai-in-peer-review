This paper proposes a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aimed at reducing the computational cost of optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions, particularly when the condition number is large. The core idea is to strategically combine full gradient evaluations with cheaper stochastic gradient evaluations to achieve a logarithmic iteration complexity with respect to accuracy, independent of the condition number – a significant improvement over standard full-gradient methods.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying a practical bottleneck in large-scale optimization (the cost of full gradient computations) and proposing a plausible solution. The authors correctly situate their work within the existing landscape of optimization algorithms, referencing key papers on deterministic, stochastic, and semi-stochastic methods (e.g., Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent, SAG, SDCA). The table summarizing iteration complexities (Table 1) is helpful in framing the problem and demonstrating the potential advantages of EMGD. The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, appears rigorous and establishes the desired convergence properties. The comparisons to related work (SAG, SDCA, Nesterov’s method) are thoughtful, highlighting the tradeoffs of EMGD and its potential applicability under specific conditions.

However, the paper also suffers from some weaknesses. First, while the high-level idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients is intuitive, the specific implementation in Algorithm 1 feels somewhat opaque. The choice of updating the solution via a constrained optimization problem (Step 8) adds complexity and isn’t fully justified.  Why is this particular constrained update crucial for achieving the claimed convergence rate? A more detailed explanation of the intuition behind this step would be valuable. 

Second, the analysis, while technically sound, is dense and challenging to follow.  The proof of Theorem 1 is sketched rather than fully detailed, leaving the reader to fill in many gaps. The connection between the algorithm's steps and the theoretical bounds isn't always clear. The reliance on the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality (Lemma 2) is appropriate, but a brief explanation of why this inequality is applicable in this context would enhance readability.

Third, the discussion of practical considerations is limited. The paper acknowledges the need to estimate the condition number κ, but only offers vague suggestions (sampling, leveraging regularizers).  A more concrete discussion of methods for estimating κ, along with their computational costs and potential impact on performance, would be beneficial.  Similarly, the impact of the step size η on performance isn't thoroughly investigated.

Fourth, the numerical experiments are entirely absent. While the paper focuses on theoretical analysis, the lack of empirical validation is a significant shortcoming.  Demonstrating the practical performance of EMGD on benchmark problems, comparing it to existing algorithms, and illustrating the benefits of using stochastic gradients would greatly strengthen the paper.  Even a simple experiment would be better than none.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of full and stochastic gradients with the epoch-based approach is a novel contribution to the field.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing a logarithmic convergence rate independent of the condition number.
*   **Problem Importance:** Addressing the computational cost of full gradient evaluations is a significant challenge in large-scale optimization.
*   **Clear Presentation of Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the existing literature and accurately position their work within it.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of numerical experiments is a major weakness.
*   **Opaque Algorithm Implementation:** The constrained optimization step in Algorithm 1 is not well-motivated.
*   **Dense and Challenging Proof:** The theoretical analysis is difficult to follow and lacks sufficient detail.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper does not address practical issues such as condition number estimation and step size selection adequately.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The idea of leveraging stochastic gradients to overcome the curse of ill-conditioning is promising. However, the lack of empirical validation and the challenging theoretical analysis significantly detract from the paper’s impact. While the theoretical results are intriguing, without experimental confirmation, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of EMGD.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit.** The authors should prioritize adding numerical experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithm. They should also clarify the implementation details of Algorithm 1 and provide a more accessible and detailed explanation of the theoretical analysis. Addressing the practical considerations discussed above will further strengthen the paper.
