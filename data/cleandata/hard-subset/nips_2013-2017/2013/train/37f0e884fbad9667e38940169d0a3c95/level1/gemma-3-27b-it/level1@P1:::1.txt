This paper introduces the Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm, aiming to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ in strongly convex optimization by leveraging both full and stochastic gradients. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient steps within epochs, achieving a linear convergence rate with O(log 1/ǫ) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradients. This is a direct attempt to address the computational bottleneck of full gradient evaluations when κ is large, a common scenario in machine learning.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, algorithm, theoretical analysis, and comparisons with existing methods. It correctly identifies the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost in optimization and positions EMGD as a method to exploit cheap stochastic gradients to reduce the number of expensive full gradient evaluations. The connection to related work, particularly epoch gradient descent (EGD), stochastic average gradient (SAG), and stochastic dual coordinate ascent (SDCA), is well-established. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the need to know κ beforehand, and suggest directions for future work to address this.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of high computational cost due to large condition numbers in strongly convex optimization and positions EMGD as a solution.
*   **Novelty:**  The combined use of full and stochastic gradients within epochs, and the resulting convergence guarantees, offer a contribution. While not a revolutionary change, it's a logical and well-justified extension of existing techniques.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:**  The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with a convergence theorem and accompanying proof sketch (details in the appendix, presumably). The stated convergence rates are a significant achievement.
*   **Comprehensive Comparison:** The authors thoughtfully compare EMGD with related algorithms (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) in terms of computational complexity, highlighting its advantages and disadvantages in different scenarios.
*   **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is easy to follow, with clear notation and logical flow. The algorithm is presented concisely and effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality of κ Estimation:** The authors acknowledge the need to know κ beforehand as a limitation, and mention future work on estimation, but this remains a substantial practical hurdle. The suggested estimation methods (sampling, leveraging regularization) may themselves be computationally expensive or require strong assumptions. This makes the immediate applicability of the algorithm questionable.
*   **κ² Dependence in Stochastic Gradient Count:**  While reducing the full gradient count to O(log 1/ǫ) is a win, the O(κ² log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradient count could still be quite large for highly ill-conditioned problems.  It's not immediately clear if the trade-off is always beneficial, especially if stochastic gradient computation is also expensive.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is purely theoretical. The absence of empirical results weakens the claims.  Demonstrating the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets with varying condition numbers would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Proof Sketch:** While a full proof isn’t always necessary, the proof sketch provided feels *too* skeletal. The critical steps involving bounding terms (AT and BT) are given, but the justification feels hand-wavy and relies heavily on invoking results without sufficient detail. More insight into these bounding arguments is needed.
*   **Connection to Existing Semi-Stochastic Methods:** The paper mentions semi-stochastic approximation methods but doesn’t fully explore the relationship between EMGD and these approaches. A more detailed discussion of how EMGD differs and improves upon existing semi-stochastic methods would be valuable. Specifically, referencing [12, 13] in more detail would be helpful as they also aim to combine full and stochastic gradients.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in optimization – the computational cost of full gradient evaluations with ill-conditioned problems.
*   The proposed algorithm, EMGD, is theoretically sound and achieves a desirable convergence rate.
*   The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.
*   The presentation is clear, concise, and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on knowing the condition number κ beforehand limits practical applicability.
*   The potentially large number of stochastic gradient evaluations (O(κ² log 1/ǫ)) may negate the benefits for highly ill-conditioned problems.
*   The lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm’s performance in practice.
*   The proof sketch is insufficient to fully evaluate the correctness and tightness of the analysis.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid theoretical contribution with the potential to be valuable to the optimization community. However, the lack of empirical validation and the practical concerns surrounding κ estimation weigh against acceptance in its current form. I would recommend **weak accept** with the following suggestions:

1.  **Include preliminary empirical results:** Even a small-scale evaluation on synthetic datasets with varying condition numbers would significantly strengthen the paper.
2.  **Expand the proof sketch:** Provide more detail on the key bounding arguments in the proof.
3.  **Discuss κ estimation methods more thoroughly:** Elaborate on the proposed estimation techniques and their associated costs.
4.  **Clarify the relationship to semi-stochastic methods:** Provide a more detailed comparison and highlight the unique contributions of EMGD.
