- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): each epoch computes one full gradient at an anchor and T mixed-gradient updates with iterates projected into a shrinking ℓ2-ball.  
- Proves high-probability linear convergence: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.  
- Mixed gradient is a control-variate/variance-reduction estimator closely related to SAG, SVRG, and SAGA.  
- Emphasizes analysis in a general oracle model and condition-number independence for the number of full-gradient evaluations.  
- Clear motivation: reduce expensive full-gradient computations for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Technical strength: careful, self-contained high-probability analysis using a shrinking-domain argument.  
- Practical strengths: handles constrained problems, uses only O(d) memory, and has a simple epoch structure amenable to distributed full-gradient computation.  
- Main weakness: stochastic-gradient complexity scales as κ^2, which is suboptimal versus SVRG/SAGA results (≈κ or (n+κ) dependence).  
- Lacks empirical validation: no experiments to confirm constants, robustness to κ mis-specification, or comparisons with SAG/SDCA/SVRG.  
- Incomplete related work discussion: close connections to SVRG (2013) and SAGA are not adequately addressed.  
- Practicality concerns: requires knowing κ to set T and uses shrinking-ball projections that may be unnecessary or costly.  
- Quality: proofs appear correct and assumptions are explicit.  
- Clarity: generally well written but some notation and equation numbering need tightening.  
- Originality/significance: method mirrors SVRG-like variance reduction; main novelty is κ-independent full-gradient count in a general oracle model with high-probability rates, yielding moderate theoretical interest but unclear practical impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid, careful theory with high-probability linear rates and a memory-/constraint-friendly variance-reduction variant.  
- Arguments against acceptance: suboptimal κ dependence, missing experiments, and incremental novelty due to close similarity to existing variance-reduction methods.
