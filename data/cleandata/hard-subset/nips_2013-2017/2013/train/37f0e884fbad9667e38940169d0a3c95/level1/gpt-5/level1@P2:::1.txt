Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization when both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic function/gradient oracle are available. It proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which organizes iterations into epochs. At the start of each epoch, the method computes one full gradient at an “anchor” point w̄k; within the epoch it runs T steps using a “mixed” gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k). Iterates are constrained to a shrinking ball around w̄k. The analysis shows linear convergence: after m epochs (and m full gradients, plus mT stochastic gradients) the suboptimality shrinks by 2−m, yielding O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach ε accuracy (with high probability), where κ = L/λ.

The mixed-gradient estimator is a variance-reduction device that cancels most of the stochastic noise by anchoring at w̄k. This is closely related to contemporaneous “semi-stochastic”/variance-reduced methods such as SAG (RouX–Schmidt–Bach, NIPS 2012), SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz–Zhang, JMLR 2013), and especially SVRG (Johnson–Zhang, NIPS 2013), which uses the same estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f_i(w) − ∇f_i(w̄) without the shrinking-ball constraint. Compared to deterministic accelerated methods (Nesterov), EMGD trades most full-gradient computations for cheaper stochastic ones.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The high-probability linear-rate proof is careful and uses standard tools (strong convexity, smoothness, martingale concentration). The intuition that the stochastic component’s norm is bounded by L‖w − w̄‖, combined with a shrinking domain, is clear and correctly exploited.
- Assumptions/parameters: The algorithm requires knowledge of L and λ to set step size and T, and uses a shrinking-ball constraint with Δ1 required to upper bound the distance to w* (via an expression involving F(0) − F(w*)). These are nontrivial requirements in practice. The paper acknowledges the need to know κ and briefly suggests future estimation strategies.
- Complexity claims: There is an inconsistency: the abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients overall, while Theorem 1 implies O(κ2 log(1/ε)) (since T = Θ(L2/λ2 log(1/δ))). The body text (Section 1) states O(κ2 log(1/ε)). This discrepancy should be corrected.
- Scope of the oracle model: In general expectation settings, a full-gradient oracle may be unavailable or as costly as the data pass required in finite-sum problems. In finite-sum settings, a full gradient is n times a stochastic gradient, which the paper discusses in its comparative table.

Clarity:
- The algorithm and proof are presented clearly, and the role of the mixed gradient and domain shrinking is well explained. However:
  - Fix the κ vs κ2 discrepancy across abstract, introduction, and theorem.
  - “Epoches” should be “epochs”; minor typos (e.g., “interstage” → “investigate”) recur.
  - Explicitly discuss how to select Δ1 in practice and what happens if the bound is loose.
  - The comparison section would benefit from a cleaner, more precise table and tighter statements about storage and distribution assumptions.
- Reproducibility: The paper provides enough detail to implement the method given L, λ, Δ1.

Originality:
- The estimator and epoch structure closely parallel SVRG’s variance reduction, but with a shrinking-ball constraint and a high-probability analysis. The use of a ball constraint to control the variance is a distinct design choice; the analysis in terms of mixed-gradient descent over shrinking domains is an original proof technique in this context.
- Relative to SAG/SDCA/SVRG, the main new angle is “condition-number–independent access to full gradients” (O(log(1/ε)) full gradients). However, similar variance-reduced methods already achieve linear convergence with one (or a few) full gradients per outer loop and O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch in the finite-sum case, which is asymptotically stronger than the κ2 dependence here.

Significance:
- The idea of minimizing the use of full gradients while keeping linear convergence is practically appealing in large-scale settings. The high-probability guarantee is also attractive.
- That said, the κ2 dependence for stochastic gradients limits impact relative to state-of-the-art variance-reduced algorithms, which achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) total gradient computations and do not require a shrinking-ball projection or knowledge of κ. The paper’s broader oracle model (general expectation with a full-gradient oracle) is less compelling without practical examples where such an oracle exists and is cheaper than a data pass.
- No experiments are provided; empirical comparisons to SAG/SDCA/SVRG and to accelerated full-gradient methods would strengthen the case and test the cost tradeoffs and sensitivity to mis-specified κ, L, and Δ1.

Pros:
- Clear algorithm with intuitive variance reduction and a clean high-probability linear-rate proof.
- Reduces the number of full gradients to O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ.
- Handles constrained problems; uses only O(d) memory.
- Provides a thoughtful comparison to related methods and discusses distributed computation of the full gradient.

Cons:
- Discrepancy between abstract and theorem (κ vs κ2) and overall suboptimal κ2 dependence compared to contemporaneous variance-reduced methods.
- Requires knowledge of L, λ, and an initial radius Δ1; practicality of these requirements is unclear.
- Relies on access to an exact full-gradient oracle in the general expectation setting.
- No experimental validation.
- Some presentation issues (typos; minor inconsistencies).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Resolve the κ vs κ2 inconsistency; if possible, refine the analysis to achieve O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch (as in SVRG-style proofs) without the shrinking-ball constraint.
- Add experiments on standard finite-sum benchmarks comparing wall-clock and gradient counts with SAG/SDCA/SVRG and Nesterov, including constrained cases to highlight EMGD’s advantages.
- Discuss practical strategies to estimate L, λ, and Δ1 adaptively; consider restarts or line searches to reduce parameter dependence.
- Clarify when the full-gradient oracle is realistically available outside finite-sum problems.

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses a relevant problem and offers a neat algorithm with a solid linear-rate analysis and an attractive reduction in full-gradient calls. However, given existing variance-reduced methods with better κ dependence and practical performance, the current contribution feels incremental unless the κ2 issue is resolved and empirical evidence is provided.
