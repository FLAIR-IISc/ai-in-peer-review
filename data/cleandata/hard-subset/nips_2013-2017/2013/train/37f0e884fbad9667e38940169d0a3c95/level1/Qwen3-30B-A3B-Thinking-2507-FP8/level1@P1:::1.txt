

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in convex optimization: reducing the computational burden of ill-conditioned problems where the condition number κ is large. The authors propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm that combines full gradients with stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with condition number-independent access to full gradients. The main theoretical result shows that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, improving upon the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient complexity. The work builds on epoch gradient descent [9] and extends the understanding of how to leverage both full and stochastic gradients in optimization.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work, particularly the stochastic optimization literature (e.g., [9, 10, 21, 22, 23] on SAG and SDCA), while providing a novel perspective by mixing gradient types. It correctly identifies that standard full gradient methods suffer from poor performance on ill-conditioned problems, while pure stochastic methods have slower convergence rates.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proof is detailed and carefully constructed using induction, with appropriate lemmas and bounds. The authors correctly identify and address the key challenge of bounding the stochastic gradient component. The assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity) are standard and well-justified.

The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the requirement to know κ in advance and the computational trade-off that makes EMGD advantageous only when κ ≤ n^(2/3). This demonstrates the authors' careful consideration of the algorithm's practical constraints.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to analysis. The pseudocode for EMGD (Algorithm 1) is clear and well-explained. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The proof is technically complex but presented with sufficient detail for experts to follow the reasoning.

One minor improvement could be providing more intuition about why the mixed gradient approach works, particularly the role of the shrinking domain constraint. The authors do a good job of explaining the intuition in the main text, but a brief visual or simple example might help readers grasp the core idea more quickly.

## Originality
The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by proposing to mix full and stochastic gradients in a way that removes the condition number dependence in full gradient access. This is distinct from previous approaches that either use only full gradients (e.g., Nesterov's method [17]) or only stochastic gradients (e.g., SAG [22], SDCA [23]). The authors properly contextualize their work against related NIPS papers, demonstrating awareness of the field.

The combination of epoch-based methods with mixed gradient updates represents a meaningful advance over existing techniques. While epoch gradient descent [9] is referenced, the mixed gradient approach is novel and well-justified.

## Significance
The paper addresses a practical problem in machine learning and optimization: the high cost of computing full gradients for ill-conditioned problems. The significance is clear in the context of large-scale machine learning where full gradient computation is expensive. The theoretical guarantee of condition number-independent full gradient access is a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature.

The paper provides a compelling argument for when EMGD is advantageous (when κ ≤ n^(2/3)), and identifies important practical advantages: applicability to constrained problems, lower storage requirements (Ω(d) vs. Ω(n)), and high-probability convergence (vs. expectation for SAG/SDCA). These points strengthen the significance of the work.

## Strengths
- Novel mixed gradient approach that achieves condition number-independent full gradient access
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with complete convergence proof
- Clear comparison with state-of-the-art methods (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA)
- Identification of practical advantages over existing methods (storage, constrained problems, high-probability convergence)
- Careful acknowledgment of limitations and conditions for applicability

## Weaknesses
- The requirement to know κ in advance is a significant practical limitation not fully addressed
- The computational advantage (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) may not hold in many real-world applications
- No empirical validation of the theoretical claims (the paper is purely theoretical)
- The proof is quite technical and might be challenging for non-experts to follow
- The paper doesn't discuss how to handle non-smooth objectives or non-strongly convex cases

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical contribution that solves a well-identified problem
- Strong technical quality with complete proof
- Clear significance for large-scale machine learning applications
- Well-written with good contextualization of related work
- Identifies specific practical advantages over existing methods

**Against Acceptance:**
- The requirement to know κ is a significant practical limitation
- The computational advantage is restricted to a narrow regime (κ ≤ n^(2/3))
- Lack of empirical results to validate the theoretical claims
- The technical complexity might limit accessibility to a broader audience

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. While the paper has some practical limitations (notably the need for knowledge of κ), the theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed. The paper makes a meaningful advance in understanding how to combine full and stochastic gradients for optimization, with clear advantages in certain scenarios that are well-articulated. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the comparison with existing methods is thorough.

The lack of empirical results is a minor weakness, but the paper is clearly a theoretical contribution, and the authors acknowledge this limitation. The paper's significance for understanding optimization algorithms and its practical advantages in specific settings justify acceptance. This work would be valuable to the NIPS community as it contributes to the growing understanding of how to optimize large-scale machine learning problems efficiently.

The paper is technically sound, addresses a meaningful problem, and advances the state of the art in optimization algorithms. It meets all four criteria for NIPS acceptance with strong technical quality, clear significance, and novel theoretical insights.
