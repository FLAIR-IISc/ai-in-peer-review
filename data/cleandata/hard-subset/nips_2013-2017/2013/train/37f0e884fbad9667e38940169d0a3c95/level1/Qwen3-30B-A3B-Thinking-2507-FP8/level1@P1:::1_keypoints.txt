- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that mixes full and stochastic gradients to reduce full-gradient dependence in ill-conditioned convex optimization.
- Shows EMGD achieves ε-optimality with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, improving full-gradient access over standard methods.
- Builds on epoch-based methods and places the approach within existing stochastic optimization literature (e.g., SAG, SDCA).
- Strong theoretical rigor: detailed convergence proof using induction with appropriate lemmas and bounds.
- Assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity) are standard and well-justified.
- Authors acknowledge limitations, including needing prior knowledge of κ and a constrained regime of advantage (κ ≤ n^(2/3)).
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written; pseudocode and notation are clear and consistent.
- Proof is complex but sufficiently detailed for experts; a simple visual or example could improve intuition about the shrinking-domain idea.
- Novel contribution: mixing full and stochastic gradients to remove condition-number dependence in full-gradient access.
- The mixed epoch-based approach is a meaningful extension beyond pure full-gradient or pure stochastic methods.
- Significant for large-scale ML because it reduces expensive full-gradient computations in some regimes.
- Identifies practical benefits: applicability to constrained problems, lower storage (Ω(d) vs Ω(n)), and high-probability convergence.
- Strength: clear comparison to state-of-the-art (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) and careful discussion of applicability.
- Weakness: requires knowing κ in advance, which is a practical limitation.
- Weakness: computational advantage only in a narrow regime (κ ≤ n^(2/3)), limiting real-world impact.
- Weakness: no empirical validation; results are purely theoretical.
- Weakness: technical complexity of the proof may limit accessibility to non-experts.
- Weakness: does not address non-smooth objectives or non-strongly convex cases.
- For acceptance: novel theoretical contribution, strong technical quality, clear significance, and good contextualization.
- Against acceptance: practical limitations (κ knowledge, narrow regime), lack of experiments, and potential accessibility issues due to technical complexity.
- Recommendation: accept — significant and rigorous theoretical advance despite practical limitations and lack of empirical results.
