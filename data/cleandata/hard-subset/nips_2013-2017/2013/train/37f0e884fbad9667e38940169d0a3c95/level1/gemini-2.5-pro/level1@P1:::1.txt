### Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors focus on the setting where the problem is ill-conditioned (i.e., has a large condition number κ). Standard accelerated first-order methods, such as Nesterov's, achieve an optimal complexity of O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations. For large κ, this can be computationally prohibitive.

The key contribution of this paper is a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that reduces the number of expensive full gradient computations to O(log(1/ε)), making it independent of the condition number κ. This is achieved by assuming access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. EMGD operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at a reference point. Then, for a number of inner iterations, it performs updates using a "mixed gradient," which combines the fixed full gradient with a stochastic term that corrects for the difference between the reference point and the current iterate. This mixed gradient is an unbiased, low-variance estimator of the true gradient near the reference point. The algorithm guarantees a constant factor reduction in error per epoch, leading to a linear convergence rate. The overall complexity is O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient calls.

The paper situates this result in the context of deterministic and stochastic optimization methods. It provides a detailed comparison with very recent work on linear-rate stochastic methods for finite-sum problems, such as Stochastic Average Gradient (SAG) and Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent (SDCA), highlighting tradeoffs in computational complexity, memory requirements, and applicability to constrained optimization. The proposed mixed-gradient technique is an early and elegant example of the variance reduction paradigm that was emerging at the time.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The central goal of making the number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number is highly significant and addresses a major bottleneck for first-order methods. The proposed method for achieving this by combining full and stochastic gradients is novel and insightful. It presents a new tradeoff in optimization complexity that was not previously explored and bypasses the established lower bounds for methods using only a full gradient oracle.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of EMGD, proving a linear convergence rate with high probability. This is a strong type of guarantee, often more desirable than the in-expectation guarantees common for stochastic algorithms. The analysis appears sound and is well-presented.

3.  **Generality of the Framework:** A key advantage of EMGD over some contemporary methods like SAG and SDCA is its generality. The framework applies to general stochastic optimization problems of the form F(w) = E[f(w)], not just finite sums. Furthermore, the algorithm naturally handles constrained optimization problems (as long as the projection in step 8 is efficient), which is a significant advantage over methods designed only for unconstrained problems.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very clearly written and well-structured. The algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner, the main theorem is stated upfront, and the proof sketch provides good intuition for the analysis. The comparison with related work in Section 3.4 is particularly excellent; it is honest and detailed, clearly articulating the pros and cons of EMGD in terms of computational complexity, storage, parallelization potential, and the nature of its convergence guarantee.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **No Experimental Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the lack of any experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical implications of the proposed complexity tradeoff. It is unclear in what real-world regimes the O(log(1/ε)) full + O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient cost of EMGD would translate to a wall-clock time advantage over, for example, Nesterov's O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient method or the methods in [22, 23]. Empirical validation on a synthetic or real dataset, especially in a high-κ scenario, would have greatly strengthened the paper's claims of computational efficiency.

2.  **Strong Dependence on κ for Stochastic Gradients:** The number of inner iterations per epoch (T) is O(κ²), leading to a total stochastic gradient complexity of O(κ² log(1/ε)). This quadratic dependence on the condition number is quite severe and may make the algorithm impractical for very ill-conditioned problems, where the cost of the stochastic steps could dominate any savings from fewer full gradient calls. While the authors are transparent about this complexity, it is a significant limitation.

3.  **Dependence on Known Problem Parameters:** The algorithm's implementation requires setting the number of iterations T, which depends on the smoothness (L) and strong convexity (λ) parameters, and thus on the condition number κ. In many practical applications, these parameters are unknown and difficult to estimate. The authors acknowledge this as a drawback in the conclusion and suggest it as future work, but it remains a practical barrier to applying the proposed method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and significant theoretical idea: decoupling full gradient complexity from the condition number.
*   Provides a strong, high-probability linear convergence guarantee.
*   The proposed framework is general, applying to constrained and general stochastic problems.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and provides an insightful comparison to related state-of-the-art methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The lack of any experimental results leaves the practical utility of the algorithm unevaluated.
*   The O(κ²) dependence in the stochastic gradient complexity is a major limitation that may restrict its applicability.
*   The requirement to know problem parameters (L, λ) is a common but important practical hurdle.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical paper that introduces a novel and significant idea for accelerating optimization on ill-conditioned problems. The strengths—originality, theoretical rigor, generality, and clarity—are substantial. The main weaknesses are the lack of experiments and the algorithm's strong (quadratic) dependence on κ for its stochastic complexity. Despite these limitations, the paper makes a valuable contribution by presenting a new perspective and a new algorithmic tool for convex optimization. The ideas presented are likely to inspire follow-up work aiming to improve the κ-dependence and validate the approach empirically. I recommend acceptance.
