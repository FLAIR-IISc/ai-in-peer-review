- Paper studies reducing full-gradient evaluations in smooth λ-strongly-convex optimization using stochastic gradients via Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD).
- EMGD computes a full gradient at each epoch and uses the mixed estimator ∇F(w̄k)+∇f(wt)−∇f(w̄k) with projected steps in shrinking balls.
- Theoretical claim: linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and overall O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (but abstract inconsistently states O(κ log(1/ε))).
- The mixed estimator and epoch structure closely match prior semi-stochastic variance-reduced methods (notably SVRG), but SVRG is not cited or compared.
- Strength: clear motivation and simple estimator that intuitively reduces variance as iterates stay near the reference point.
- Strength: provides a clean, high-probability linear-rate analysis and handles constrained problems without per-example gradient storage.
- Major weakness: limited novelty since the core estimator and epoch scheme are essentially identical to SVRG and related methods.
- Major weakness: stochastic gradient complexity is suboptimal (κ2 vs κ in SVRG/SDCA), likely due to a crude Azuma-Hoeffding based analysis with shrinking balls.
- Major weakness: inconsistency between abstract and theorem regarding stochastic complexity must be resolved.
- Major weakness: no empirical evaluation to support practical efficiency or claimed advantages over SAG/SDCA/SVRG/accelerated methods.
- Practical concern: requires knowledge of κ to set T and epoch schedule; projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) may be computationally nontrivial.
- Comparisons to finite-sum methods are outdated/weak and omit memoryless alternatives like SVRG that avoid storage costs.
- Technical quality: proofs are careful and readable but conservative; analysis choices obscure potentially sharper κ dependence achievable via standard variance-reduction arguments.
- Clarity: algorithm and proofs are clear, but notational issues and minor typos exist; needs clearer positioning vs variance-reduced methods and corrected complexity claims.
- Originality: judged moderate-to-low — two-oracle viewpoint and high-probability bound are incremental without sharper complexity or new insights.
- Significance: limited as the main qualitative message is already established by existing variance-reduced methods; inferior stochastic complexity and lack of experiments reduce impact.
- Pros for acceptance: self-contained analysis in a general oracle model, applicability to constrained problems with low memory, and highlighting decoupling full-gradient cost from κ.
- Cons for acceptance: strong overlap with SVRG (uncited), suboptimal κ2 complexity, abstract/theorem inconsistency, no experiments, reliance on κ, and potentially expensive projections.
- Key suggestions: cite and compare to SVRG and related work; tighten analysis to O(κ) stochastic gradients or correct abstract; add experiments versus SVRG/SAG/SDCA/accelerated GD.
- Additional suggestions: remove shrinking-ball constraint or use standard variance-reduction lemmas, provide adaptive parameter schedules that do not require κ, and clarify projection cost.
- Overall assessment: overlaps substantially with known methods and presents weaker complexity and inconsistent claims; not recommended for acceptance without a κ-tight analysis, proper positioning, and empirical validation.
