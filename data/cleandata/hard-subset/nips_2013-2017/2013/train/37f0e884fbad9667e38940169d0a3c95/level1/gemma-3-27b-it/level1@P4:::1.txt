This paper proposes a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations needed for strongly convex and smooth optimization problems, particularly when the condition number is large. The core idea is to interleave full gradient and stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, aiming for a complexity of O(log 1/ǫ) full gradients, independent of the condition number κ, while requiring O(κ log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradients.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction outlining the motivation and contribution, followed by a related work section contextualizing the proposed approach. The algorithm itself is presented in a concise and understandable manner, and the theoretical analysis appears rigorous, culminating in a convergence rate theorem. The paper also includes comparisons with existing methods and discusses potential future work.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of high computational cost associated with full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned optimization problems.
* **Novel Approach:** The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework (EMGD) represents a novel approach to address the stated problem.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a theoretical convergence rate, demonstrating the algorithm’s ability to achieve an ǫ-optimal solution with a specified number of full and stochastic gradient evaluations.
* **Comparative Analysis:** The comparison with other algorithms (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) provides valuable context and highlights the potential benefits of EMGD.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and follows a logical structure, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper lacks detailed discussion of practical considerations such as hyperparameter tuning (e.g., step size selection) and the sensitivity of the algorithm to noise in the stochastic gradients.  The assumption of knowing the condition number κ beforehand is a significant limitation in practice. The suggestions for estimating it in future work are brief and may require substantial further development.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical; a lack of experimental validation limits the assessment of the algorithm’s performance in real-world scenarios. Even simple synthetic experiments demonstrating the advantages of EMGD over existing methods would substantially strengthen the paper.
* **Incremental Improvements:** The comparison section acknowledges that EMGD may not be the *best* in terms of computational complexity in all cases, suggesting it represents an incremental improvement rather than a breakthrough.  The advantage of the algorithm is less compelling in such cases.
* **Redundancy in Theorem Proof:** The proof in Section 4 is very detailed, but includes intermediate steps whose justification is fairly straightforward. A more concise proof focusing on the core ideas would improve readability.
* **Oracle Definitions:** The definition of the function oracle `Of` could be clarified. It’s stated that `Ef[f(w)] = F(w)`, but it's not immediately obvious why this implies `f(·)` is L-smooth.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in optimization.
* The proposed algorithm (EMGD) is novel and theoretically well-supported.
* The paper provides a rigorous analysis and demonstrates a clear convergence rate.
* The comparative analysis places the work in the context of existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation hinders a thorough evaluation of the algorithm’s practical performance.
* The reliance on knowing the condition number κ limits the algorithm’s applicability in real-world scenarios.
* The improvements over existing methods are incremental, and the paper doesn’t strongly demonstrate substantial advantages in all cases.
* The detailed proof, while rigorous, is somewhat verbose.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to reducing the computational cost of full gradient evaluations in strongly convex and smooth optimization. However, the lack of empirical evaluation and the practical limitation regarding the condition number raise concerns.  While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the absence of experimental results makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's real-world impact.  I would rate this as a **weak accept**.  Acceptance would be significantly strengthened with the inclusion of even a limited set of experimental results demonstrating the algorithm’s performance on synthetic datasets.
