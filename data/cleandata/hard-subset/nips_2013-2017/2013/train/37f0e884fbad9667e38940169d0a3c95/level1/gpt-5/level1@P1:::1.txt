Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization and asks whether one can reduce the number of full gradient evaluations, which in accelerated methods scales as O(√κ log(1/ε)), by leveraging access to stochastic gradients. The authors propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch, the algorithm computes the full gradient at a reference point w̄k. Within the epoch, it uses a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), and performs projected gradient steps over a shrinking ball around w̄k. The analysis shows linear convergence: after m epochs, the error decreases as 2−m, implying O(log(1/ε)) epochs and thus O(log(1/ε)) full gradients. Each epoch uses T stochastic gradients; with the given choice T = Ω((L/λ)2 log(1/δ)) the total number of stochastic gradients is O(κ2 log(1/ε)).

The mixed-gradient estimator and epoch structure are closely related to semi-stochastic variance-reduced methods developed around the same time. In particular, Johnson and Zhang (NIPS 2013) introduced SVRG, which uses the same estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇fi(w) − ∇fi(w̄) and achieves linear convergence with O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch (and one full gradient per epoch), for a total of O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) component-gradient evaluations in the finite-sum setting; see also SAG (NIPS 2012) and SDCA (JMLR 2013). Earlier “hybrid” approaches (Friedlander & Schmidt, 2011) and epoch-based SGD (Hazan & Kale, 2011) are also relevant. The present paper formulates a two-oracle model (exact full gradient + sampling oracle) and gives a high-probability analysis with shrinking trust regions.

Strengths:
- Clear problem motivation: reducing expensive full gradient computations for ill-conditioned problems.
- Simple algorithmic idea with a clean estimator that intuitively reduces variance as wt stays near w̄k.
- Theoretical linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, independent of κ, and high-probability guarantees.
- Handles constrained problems (via projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk)) without storing per-example gradients (unlike SAG/SDCA).

Weaknesses:
- Novelty relative to SVRG is limited: the core estimator is identical, and the epoch structure is standard. The paper does not cite SVRG (NIPS 2013), nor compare to it. This is a major omission.
- Complexity is suboptimal: the analysis yields O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, worse than O(κ log(1/ε)) in SVRG/SDCA. The proof uses a crude Azuma-Hoeffding bound with shrinking balls, which appears to be the source of the extra κ factor.
- Inconsistency: the abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, whereas Theorem 1 and Section 3.3 state O(κ2 log(1/ε)). This discrepancy must be resolved.
- No empirical evaluation to support practical efficiency or the claimed advantages over SAG/SDCA/accelerated methods.
- Assumes knowledge of κ to set T and the epoch schedule; practical guidance is limited. The projection onto intersected sets may be nontrivial for general W.
- Some comparisons with finite-sum methods are weak or outdated (e.g., stating that SAG/SDCA linear rates hold only in expectation, storage trade-offs, and distributed aspects) and do not consider SVRG’s memoryless, variance-reduced alternative.

Quality:
The technical development is mostly careful and readable, with a complete proof of a linear-rate bound. However, the bound is conservative (κ2 factor) and the analysis choices (shrinking radii and Azuma) seem to obscure a sharper κ dependence achievable by standard variance-reduction arguments. The inconsistency between abstract and theorem needs correction. Missing critical related work (SVRG) undermines the positioning.

Clarity:
The algorithm is clearly described. The proof is detailed. Notational issues (e.g., “epoches”) and minor typos occur. The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning versus variance-reduced methods and from fixing the stated complexities. A brief discussion of the cost of the projection onto W ∩ B(·) would help.

Originality:
Moderate to low. The mixed gradient estimator and epoch framework closely match existing semi-stochastic variance-reduced techniques. The two-oracle viewpoint and high-probability bound are incremental contributions, but not sufficient without sharper complexity or new insights.

Significance:
As written, limited. The main qualitative message—that one can achieve O(log(1/ε)) full gradients by mixing in stochastic gradients—is important but already established by SVRG/SDCA/SAG in various settings. The inferior κ2 stochastic complexity and lack of experiments lessen impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean, self-contained analysis in a general oracle model with high-probability guarantees.
- Applicability to constrained problems with low memory.
- Highlights the practical objective of decoupling the cost of full gradients from κ.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overlaps strongly with SVRG; missing citation and comparison.
- Suboptimal stochastic complexity (κ2 vs κ).
- Abstract/theorem inconsistency; no empirical validation.
- Requires κ and a potentially expensive projection onto an intersection.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Cite and compare thoroughly with SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and related variance-reduction methods; clarify what is new.
- Tighten the analysis to achieve O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch, possibly removing the shrinking-ball constraint and using standard variance-reduction lemmas bounding Var(g̃t) by function suboptimality.
- Correct the complexity statement in the abstract or improve the bound to match it.
- Provide experiments on finite-sum problems comparing against accelerated GD, SVRG, SAG, SDCA, highlighting constrained cases and memory trade-offs.
- Discuss how to set T, η, and Δk without knowing κ; consider adaptive schedules.
- Clarify the computational cost of projecting onto W ∩ B(·).

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses a timely goal but substantially overlaps with known variance-reduced methods and presents a weaker complexity result with inconsistent claims. Without a sharper analysis, proper positioning, and empirical support, I cannot recommend acceptance. I encourage the authors to revise with a κ-tight bound and comprehensive comparisons.
