- Problem & contribution: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm mixing rare full gradients and many cheap stochastic gradients for smooth strongly convex constrained optimization.  
- Complexity claim: EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, making full-gradient dependence independent of condition number k.  
- Epoch design: Each epoch computes one full gradient and O(k^2) mixed steps, averaging iterates to get a constant suboptimality reduction independent of k.  
- Theoretical novelty: The mixing idea and the simple, self-contained convergence proof are novel and interesting.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on efforts to leverage cheap stochastic oracles while keeping linear rates (e.g., SAG, SCDA).  
- Comparison to Nesterov: EMGD can be faster than Nesterov when k is not too large (roughly k ≤ n^{2/3} in the sum-of-n-functions setting).  
- Comparison to SAG: EMGD is generally slower than SAG across regimes, matching SAG’s complexity only when k ≤ n^{1/2}; SAG outperforms EMGD for many practical k ranges.  
- Advantages over SAG: EMGD handles constrained optimization (given efficient gradient mapping) and has a significantly simpler convergence proof; its result holds with high probability rather than only in expectation.  
- Parallelization/memory claims: Suggested memory/parallelization advantages over SAG are unclear, since SAG can use mini-batches and be parallelized.  
- Practical relevance: The practical impact is unproven due to no experimental evaluation.  
- Limitations: The paper insufficiently discusses algorithmic limitations and trade-offs (section 3.4 needs expansion).  
- Requirement to know k: A major practical drawback is that epoch length must be fixed in advance (requires knowledge of k), unlike SCDA or SAG which do not require predefining steps.  
- Step-size/adaptivity: SCDA offers automatic step-size selection and SAG uses effective adaptive heuristics; EMGD lacks these practical conveniences.  
- Missing citation: Should cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods.  
- Practical regime note: In common ML settings where regularization implies k ≈ n/C, EMGD is not favorable; authors should point out regimes where k < n^{2/3} occurs in practice.  
- Proof presentation: The proof is rigorous and self-contained but would benefit from high-level motivating comments to clarify key constructions.  
- Suggested clarifications: Expand discussion of the sum-of-n-functions example and compare threshold regimes between EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov in detail.  
- Requested edits: Improve section 3.4, add experiments or practical examples, and incorporate rebuttal clarifications.  
- Minor corrections: Fix multiple typos, notation inconsistencies (D vs Ω), explicit statements about assumptions (e.g., ∇F(w)=E[∇f(w)]), and several line-specific clarifications indicated by reviewer.  
- Reviewer stance after rebuttal: Reviewer is satisfied with authors’ responses and expects the suggested changes to be implemented; theoretical contribution is strong despite lack of experiments.
