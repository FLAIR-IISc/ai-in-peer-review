This paper proposes a novel hybrid optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex objective functions. The goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, which can be a bottleneck for ill-conditioned problems. Standard accelerated methods require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, where κ is the condition number. By incorporating stochastic gradients, EMGD aims to make the number of full gradient evaluations independent of κ. The algorithm proceeds in epochs, using one full gradient and a series of mixed stochastic/full gradient updates within each epoch to achieve a constant reduction in error. The main theoretical result is that EMGD finds an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations.

**Strengths**

*   The paper tackles an important and relevant problem: mitigating the high computational cost of gradient-based methods on ill-conditioned problems, which are common in machine learning.
*   The proposed hybrid strategy, which combines a single full gradient with variance-reduced stochastic updates within each epoch, is novel. The idea of using a mixed gradient to achieve linear convergence is interesting.
*   The theoretical analysis appears to be sound and establishes a strong result: a linear convergence rate (logarithmic dependence on 1/ε) that holds with high probability, with a number of full gradient computations that is independent of the condition number κ.

**Weaknesses**

My main concern is about the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm in the large-scale machine learning settings that are central to the NIPS community. The paper's primary claim of superiority rests on a comparison of computational complexities, but the conditions under which EMGD is advantageous are not convincingly shown to be applicable to common ML problems.

The authors state that EMGD has a lower computational cost than Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent when κ ≤ n²/³, where n is the number of samples. However, the paper does not provide sufficient discussion or evidence to demonstrate that this is a practical or interesting regime. For the algorithm to be compelling, the authors need to characterize the types of machine learning problems (e.g., in terms of data properties, model choice, or regularization strength) where this condition is likely to hold. Without this context, the theoretical improvement risks being disconnected from practical application.

Furthermore, the convergence guarantee for EMGD is a high-probability bound, whereas the guarantees for the batch gradient methods it is compared against are deterministic. This represents a trade-off that should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed when evaluating the algorithm's overall advantages.

To strengthen the paper and establish its significance for the machine learning community, this point must be addressed carefully. The authors should provide a more thorough discussion that contextualizes their results and clearly demonstrates a practical advantage over standard batch gradient descent in relevant scenarios.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions**

1.  While it is straightforward to deduce, it would improve clarity to explicitly state that L ≥ λ early in the preliminaries (Section 3.1).
2.  The derivations in the analysis section, particularly the chain of inequalities from (13) to (17), are quite dense. While the steps seem correct, breaking them down with more intermediate explanation could significantly improve readability.
3.  Please perform a careful proofread of the entire manuscript, especially the equations in the analysis section, to check for any minor typos or notational inconsistencies.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a theoretically novel and interesting hybrid gradient algorithm with a strong high-probability convergence guarantee. The mathematical contribution, particularly the proof of linear convergence, appears solid. However, the paper's main weakness is the insufficient justification of its practical relevance. The claims of outperforming standard methods hinge on assumptions about the condition number and the number of samples, and it is not clear whether these assumptions hold in regimes of interest to machine learning practitioners. While the theoretical ideas are valuable, the paper would be significantly stronger if it included a more convincing discussion of its practical utility for the NIPS audience.
