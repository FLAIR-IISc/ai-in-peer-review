The paper presents a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that combines deterministic and stochastic gradient steps to achieve a linear convergence rate for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors show that EMGD can reduce the number of full gradients needed from O( √ κ log 1/ǫ ) to O(log 1 ǫ ), where κ is the condition number.

The theoretical analysis is well-presented and provides a clear understanding of the algorithm's behavior. However, there are several concerns with the current state of the paper. Firstly, the analysis only covers the case where the condition number is perfectly known beforehand, and the behavior of the algorithm when this hyper-parameter is misspecified is not discussed. This is a significant limitation, as in practice, the condition number is often unknown or difficult to estimate.

Another major concern is the lack of experimental evaluation. Since the main contribution of the paper is a new algorithm, it would be beneficial to include experimental results to assess the theoretical findings and study the algorithm's behavior on real-world data. This would provide valuable insights into the algorithm's performance and help to establish its practical relevance.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing algorithms, such as stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and stochastic average gradient (SAG). The authors mention that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate, but it is unclear how this compares to other algorithms in terms of computational efficiency and accuracy.

Furthermore, the optimization setting considered in the paper is not clearly stated. The authors seem to be solving a deterministic optimization problem using a stochastic algorithm, but this is not explicitly stated. Clarifying the optimization setting and providing a clear comparison with other algorithms would strengthen the paper.

In terms of minor concerns, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the algorithm's parameters and how they are set. The authors mention that the step size η needs to be set, but it is unclear how this is done in practice. Providing more guidance on parameter tuning would make the algorithm more accessible to practitioners.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and novel algorithm, but it requires additional work to address the concerns mentioned above. With experimental evaluation, a more detailed comparison with existing algorithms, and clarification of the optimization setting, the paper could be significantly strengthened.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel algorithm that combines deterministic and stochastic gradient steps to achieve a linear convergence rate.
* The theoretical analysis is well-presented and provides a clear understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
* The algorithm has the potential to be more efficient than existing algorithms, such as SGD and SAG, for certain types of optimization problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of experimental evaluation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's performance on real-world data.
