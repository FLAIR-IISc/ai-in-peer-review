SUMMARY: The paper studies optimization of a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, assuming the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. Nesterov’s optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence for this setting, but its constant depends on the square root of the condition number k. The authors consider a hybrid oracle model with access to both an expensive full gradient and a cheaper stochastic gradient. They propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which makes O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle. When k is not too large, this can improve theoretical runtime.

The key idea is to organize iterations into epochs. At the start of each epoch, the algorithm computes one full gradient. It then performs a fixed number O(k^2) of mixed-gradient steps within the epoch, each combining the stored full gradient with freshly sampled stochastic gradients. Averaging the O(k^2) iterates within an epoch yields a constant reduction in suboptimality that does not depend on k, which in turn implies that the number of epochs (and thus full-gradient evaluations) is independent of the condition number. The paper provides a simple, self-contained proof of the convergence rate, but no experiments.

I enjoyed reading the paper and find the idea of mixing a small number of full-gradient computations with many cheap stochastic-gradient steps novel and interesting. This work fits into a recent line of research that leverages stochastic oracles while retaining linear convergence. A notable theoretical and practical advance in this direction is the SAG algorithm [16], which applies to smooth, strongly convex objectives that are finite sums of n component functions (e.g., regularized empirical risk minimization). In such settings, a reasonable assumption is that computing a full gradient is n times more expensive than computing a stochastic gradient. Under this cost model, SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov’s method whenever k ≤ n/8. By contrast, in the same setting EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov’s method when k ≤ n^(2/3), but it is slower than SAG in all regimes (with the same big-O complexity as SAG when k ≤ n^(1/2)). For illustration: if k = n^(1/2), then both SAG and EMGD require O(n log(1/ε)) operations, whereas Nesterov’s method requires O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)). EMGD’s two main advantages over SAG are: (1) as the authors note, EMGD handles constrained optimization (assuming the gradient mapping can be computed efficiently), whereas SAG has so far been developed for unconstrained problems; and (2) EMGD’s convergence proof is much simpler than SAG’s and yields high-probability guarantees rather than the in-expectation guarantees of SAG. The claimed memory/parallelization advantages are less clear, since SAG can also use mini-batches, which both parallelize computation and reduce memory.

EVALUATION SUMMARY:
Pros:
- A novel and interesting algorithmic idea, supported by clean, simple, and solid theory.
- Like SAG, provides linear convergence for regularized empirical loss minimization without multiplying the number of samples n by the condition number k in the leading constant; EMGD is more general (handles constrained problems) and the proof is simpler.
- Clear exposition with a self-contained proof.

Cons:
- No experiments to demonstrate practical impact or to illustrate where the theoretical advantages matter in practice.
- Limited discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and drawbacks, especially relative to existing methods (Section 3.4 should be expanded; concrete suggestions are provided below).
- The proof would benefit from more high-level commentary to motivate the key constructions and insights.

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. While experiments would be valuable, the theoretical contribution can stand on its own. The authors should significantly expand Section 3.4 to discuss limitations and drawbacks. They should also include a more concrete discussion of the “sum of n functions” setting to highlight the differences with SAG and Nesterov’s method. In particular: EMGD is worse than Nesterov’s method for (roughly) k > n^(2/3); SAG matches EMGD for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2); SAG is better than both EMGD and Nesterov for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8; and SAG is worse than Nesterov for k > n/8.

A major practical drawback of EMGD is that the number of inner steps per epoch must be fixed in advance based on knowledge of k. In contrast, both SCDA [17] and SAG do not require fixing the number of steps a priori and can benefit from faster empirical convergence than worst-case bounds (or from better local condition numbers). Moreover, SCDA has automatic step-size selection, whereas SAG employs an adaptive step-size heuristic that works well in practice. The paper should discuss these issues explicitly.

ADDENDUM (after discussion with other reviewers):
a) As another reviewer noted, in many ML applications the regularization parameter is C/n, so the condition number is approximately n/C′. In this regime SAG still improves over Nesterov’s method, but EMGD does not. This should be pointed out, along with at least one practical setting where k < n^(2/3).
b) Please cite: M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt, “Hybrid Deterministic–Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting,” SISC, 2012. That work also proposes a hybrid deterministic–stochastic method with a linear rate, but the rate still depends on k, so it does not ultimately beat standard gradient descent (it only speeds up the initial phase via cheaper steps). The present submission can still offer theoretical improvements when k is not too large relative to n.

CLARITY: The paper is generally clear. The summary in Table 1 is especially helpful and unusually clear relative to the literature. The proof is easy to follow, but would benefit from a few high-level explanations to motivate the definitions and how they fit together, which currently feels a bit “magical.”

ORIGINALITY: A novel combination of known techniques.

SIGNIFICANCE: Practical relevance remains to be demonstrated. Nonetheless, the theoretical contribution is potentially impactful. Given the difficulty of SAG’s proof (versus the simpler proof of SDCA that applies in a more restricted setting), EMGD’s simple proof is a major contribution.
