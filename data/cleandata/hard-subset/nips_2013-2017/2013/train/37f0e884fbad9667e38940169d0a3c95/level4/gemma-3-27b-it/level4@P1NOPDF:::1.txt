SUMMARY: The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence for such problems, its convergence rate constant depends on the square root of the condition number, *k*. The authors consider a scenario where both a computationally expensive full gradient of the objective function and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle are available. They propose a hybrid algorithm, called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires O(log 1/ε) calls to the full gradient oracle – independent of the condition number – and O(*k*<sup>2</sup> log(1/ε)) calls to the cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. This could be faster in theory than existing methods, provided the condition number is not excessively large. The core idea of EMGD is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with a fixed number, O(*k*<sup>2</sup>), of mixed gradient steps. These mixed steps combine the full gradient (computed once per epoch) with stochastic gradients (which vary within the epoch). By averaging the O(*k*<sup>2</sup>) iterates within each epoch, they demonstrate a constant decrease in suboptimality that is independent of the condition number, leading to a number of full gradient computations (epochs) independent of *k*. They provide a simple and complete self-contained proof of their convergence rate, but do not include any experimental validation. 

I found this paper enjoyable and believe the idea of combining a limited number of full gradient computations with a larger number of inexpensive stochastic gradient steps is novel and interesting. This work builds on a growing body of research focused on leveraging cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence rates. A recent theoretical and practical breakthrough was achieved with the SAG algorithm [16], which is designed for smooth, strongly convex objectives that can be expressed as the sum of *n* simpler functions (common in regularized empirical loss minimization, where *n* represents the number of training examples).  In this context, a reasonable assumption is that computing the full gradient requires *n* times more computation than computing a stochastic gradient. SAG is faster than Nesterov’s algorithm in theory when the condition number *k* ≤ *n*/8. In contrast, EMGD is faster than Nesterov’s algorithm in theory when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>2/3</sup>, but slower than SAG across all regimes (having the same big-O complexity when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>). To illustrate these speedups, if *k* = *n*<sup>1/2</sup>, both SAG and EMGD have a complexity of O(*n* log(1/ε)), while Nesterov’s algorithm has a complexity of O(*n*<sup>5/4</sup> log(1/ε)). The two primary advantages of EMGD over SAG that I see are: 1) as the authors mention, EMGD works for constrained optimization problems (provided the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently), whereas SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems; and 2) the convergence proof for EMGD is significantly simpler than that for SAG, potentially yielding more insights and making modifications to EMGD more amenable to provable guarantees. Their result also holds with high probability, compared to SAG’s in-expectation guarantee. The authors also suggest potential memory and parallelization advantages, though this is less clear since SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which also reduces the memory requirement. 

EVALUATION SUMMARY: 

Pros: 
- Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theoretical foundation.
- Like SAG, achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization, avoiding a dependence of the condition number *k* on the number of training examples *n* in the constant factor; however, their algorithm is more general (handling constrained optimization) and has a simpler proof.
- The paper is clearly written and the proof is self-contained. 

Cons: 
- Lacks an experimental section to demonstrate the practical relevance of the algorithm (and does not provide concrete examples illustrating why its suggested theoretical advantages might be relevant in practice).
- Does not adequately discuss the limitations and drawbacks of the algorithm, especially in comparison to existing approaches (Section 3.4 should be improved! See suggestions in this review).
- The proof would benefit from higher-level comments justifying the key insights underlying its construction. 

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. While experiments would be valuable, I believe the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to stand on its own. The authors should definitively expand Section 3.4 to address the limitations and drawbacks of their algorithm. They should also provide a more concrete discussion of the sum of *n* functions example, highlighting the differences between EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov (as I mentioned above – e.g., EMGD performs worse than Nesterov for (roughly) *k* > *n*<sup>2/3</sup>; SAG performs the same as EMGD for 1 ≤ *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>; better than EMGD (and Nesterov) for *n*<sup>1/2</sup> < *k* ≤ *n*/8; and worse than Nesterov for *k* > *n*/8). A practical drawback of EMGD is that the number of steps within an epoch must be fixed in advance, based on knowledge of *k*. In contrast, SCDA [17] and SAG do not require this fixed step count and can therefore potentially achieve faster practical convergence than predicted by the theoretical bound, or benefit from better local condition numbers. Furthermore, SCDA includes automatic step-size selection, while SAG employs an adaptive step-size heuristic that appears to work well in practice. The authors should discuss these points in the paper. 

[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: a) As another reviewer noted, in practice, the regularization parameter is often set to *C*/ *n*, resulting in a condition number of approximately *n*/ *C*. This is a regime where SAG still outperforms Nesterov, but where EMGD does not. This should be pointed out, and perhaps another practical setting where *k* < *n*<sup>2/3</sup> could be mentioned. b) The authors should cite [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012], which also presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear rate of convergence. This algorithm still has the condition number appearing in the rate, so the overall rate is not faster than standard gradient descent (though the initial steps are faster due to cheaper computations) – thus, the current submission can still offer a theoretical improvement when *k* is not too large relative to *n*.]

CLARITY: The paper is generally clear. I appreciated the summary in Table 1, which I have not encountered presented so clearly elsewhere. The proof is rigorous and can be followed closely, but would be more helpful if the authors provided a few high-level comments motivating some of the defined quantities, which currently seem to fit together somewhat magically. 

ORIGINALITY: This is a novel combination of established techniques. 

SIGNIFICANCE: The practical relevance of the algorithm has not yet been demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution has the potential to be impactful. Given the difficulty of proving the convergence of SAG (and the simpler proof of SDCA, which only applies to a restricted setting), the simple proof of EMGD is a significant contribution.
