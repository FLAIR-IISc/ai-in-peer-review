

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel first-order optimization algorithm that combines full gradients and stochastic gradients for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors claim that by using a "mixed gradient" approach that blends deterministic and stochastic gradient information, their algorithm achieves an O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate in terms of full gradient evaluations, removing the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) dependence on the condition number κ. This represents a significant theoretical advancement in optimization theory, as it demonstrates how hybrid access to both gradient types can circumvent the well-known lower bounds for pure first-order methods.

The paper makes a theoretically compelling contribution. The authors establish that under the assumption that the condition number κ is known in advance (i.e., both the strong convexity parameter λ and smoothness parameter L are known), their algorithm can achieve high-probability linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This is a meaningful improvement over standard methods that require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients. The paper's theoretical analysis is technically sound, with a clear inductive proof showing the convergence rate, and the authors properly contextualize their results within the broader literature on convex optimization.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that substantially undermine its contribution. First, the theoretical analysis is limited to the case where the condition number is perfectly known beforehand, which is a severe restriction in practice. The paper provides no guidance on how to estimate κ, nor does it analyze the algorithm's behavior when κ is misspecified. This is a critical gap since the condition number is typically unknown in real-world applications. The authors should either provide a method for estimating κ or analyze the algorithm's performance under different estimation scenarios, including both overestimation and underestimation of κ.

Second, the paper lacks any experimental validation of the theoretical results. Given that the main contribution is a new algorithm, empirical evaluation is essential to demonstrate its practical relevance. The authors should compare EMGD with standard methods (SGD, SAG, SDCA, Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) on both synthetic and real-world datasets to validate the claimed theoretical improvements in computational efficiency. Without experiments, it's impossible to assess how the algorithm performs in practice or whether the theoretical O(log(1/ε)) rate translates to meaningful computational savings.

Third, the paper fails to adequately contextualize its work within the existing literature on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization. The authors do not discuss related work such as Friedlander and Schmidt's "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" (SISC 2012), which presents a similar framework for combining deterministic and stochastic gradients. The paper should compare their approach with these existing methods, particularly regarding convergence rates and computational complexity. The related work section is insufficiently comprehensive.

Fourth, the optimization setting is ambiguously stated. The paper claims to solve a deterministic optimization problem (min_w F(w) = 1/n ∑_{i=1}^n f_i(w)) using a "stochastic" algorithm, but the theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is misleading. The authors should clearly specify whether they are addressing a deterministic optimization problem with a randomized algorithm (like SAG and SDCA) or a stochastic approximation problem. This distinction is critical for properly evaluating the contribution.

Regarding the four review criteria:

Quality: The theoretical analysis is technically sound but limited to the unrealistic assumption that κ is known. The paper lacks experimental validation, which is essential for a claims of practical significance.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but the ambiguous optimization setting and lack of experimental results reduce its clarity. The algorithm description is clear, but the connection between theory and practice is weak.

Originality: The paper presents an interesting theoretical result, but the approach of mixing full and stochastic gradients is not entirely novel. The key contribution is the theoretical analysis showing how to remove κ dependence, but this is limited to the known-κ case.

Significance: The significance is limited by the unrealistic assumption that κ is known and the lack of experimental validation. While the theoretical result is interesting, its practical relevance remains unproven without empirical demonstration.

The paper's main contribution is the theoretical insight that condition number dependence can be removed when both full and stochastic gradients are available, but this insight is significantly weakened by the missing analysis of the practical case where κ must be estimated. The authors should address these limitations before the paper can be considered for publication.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution but has significant weaknesses that prevent it from making a substantial impact on the field. The most critical improvements needed are: (1) analysis of the algorithm's behavior when κ is unknown or misspecified, (2) comprehensive experimental validation, and (3) better contextualization within the existing literature on hybrid optimization methods. Without these additions, the paper's contribution remains limited to a narrow theoretical setting with questionable practical relevance.
