The paper introduces a novel "hybrid" deterministic-stochastic approach for first-order optimization, combining deterministic and stochastic oracle queries to achieve a more efficient convergence rate. This method allows the authors to eliminate the dependency on the condition number in the rate of convergence. 

The authors present a remarkable theoretical finding, demonstrating that with a known condition number (i.e., known strong convexity and strong smoothness constants of the objective) and a first-order hybrid oracle, a suitably chosen mix of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps can achieve a high-probability O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization. 

However, there are several concerns with the paper's current state. Firstly, the theoretical analysis is limited to the case where the condition number is perfectly known beforehand, without discussing the algorithm's behavior when this hyper-parameter is misspecified. The current result implies that the dependence on the condition number can be removed from the convergence rate when the condition number is known beforehand. Although the theoretical analysis for other cases (where the condition number is unknown) may be challenging, experimental analysis could have been conducted to address this issue. Unfortunately, the paper lacks an experiments section, which is a major concern. Given that the paper's primary contribution is a new algorithm, it would be beneficial to include experiments to assess the theoretical results and study their relevance to the algorithm's actual behavior on empirical data.

Detailed comments:

The proposed EGD algorithm relies on the update rule defined by Eq. 8 (page 4), utilizing the "mixed gradient" defined in Eq. 7. Consequently, EGD requires the hyper-parameter η to be set (or estimated), which boils down to knowing the condition number κ (both the strong convexity modulus λ and the strong smoothness modulus L). However, the authors do not provide any guidelines or theoretical arguments for setting κ beforehand. 

There are two possibilities: either this parameter needs to be estimated, in which case the corresponding estimation procedure is missing (even a heuristic procedure supported by numerical experiments would be acceptable), or this parameter is assumed known, as the paper's primary focus is theoretical. In the latter case, the theoretical analysis/experimental section should cover cases where this hyper-parameter is misspecified, studying the convergence rate when the hyper-parameter is set too large or too small.

Although the smooth and strongly convex case is popular in theoretical analysis and realistic in many situations, other settings (non-strongly convex) are also interesting. In particular, the non-strongly convex case is important, as it arises in several situations. See Bach & Moulines (2011) for a theoretical analysis of the different behaviors depending on the cases (convex vs strongly convex).

Other concerns include the fact that blending deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps in a first-order optimization algorithm is not a new idea. The algorithm presented in the paper is not written as an alternation of deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps with different frequencies for each type of steps. Instead, it is written as one "mixed" update per iteration (within an epoch), followed by a gradient-like update step. It would be interesting to discuss how the proposed algorithm relates to and compares with a similar-in-spirit algorithm that interleaves deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps (at least in the "unconstrained" case).

The authors do not review a related line of work, namely hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms; see Friedlander and Schmidt (2012). Discussing and comparing the convergence rates would be valuable here. See also the above reference for a review of older works on this topic.

A thorough experimental study would be a valuable addition to the paper, including a detailed comparison with regular SGD, averaged SGD, and recent proposals for stochastic first-order optimization (SAG, etc.).

A minor concern is that the optimization setting considered in the paper is not clearly stated. The purpose of the paper is to combine the benefits of deterministic optimization (exponential rate of convergence) and stochastic optimization (dependence on the condition number). The authors do not specify clearly what they intend to solve: the deterministic optimization problem [Min w F(w) = 1/n ∑ i=1^n F_i(w)] with a "stochastic" (or more precisely, "randomized") algorithm, or the stochastic approximation problem [Min w E(F(w))]. Note that both SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for solving the deterministic optimization problem [Min w F(w) = 1/n ∑ i=1^n F_i(w)]. The theoretical setup stated in Section 3.1 is misleading in this respect, and none of the claims made later in the paper clarifies which setting is considered. This could easily be fixed.
