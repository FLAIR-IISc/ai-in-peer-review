### Summary

This paper proposes a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems. The central idea is to reduce the number of computationally expensive full gradient evaluations, which in optimal first-order methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, scales with the square root of the condition number `κ`. EMGD achieves this by employing a hybrid approach that accesses both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. The algorithm operates in epochs; at the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" that combines this stored full gradient with a variance-reduced stochastic component.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD achieves an `ε`-optimal solution with `O(log 1/ε)` full gradient computations and `O(κ² log 1/ε)` stochastic gradient computations. This effectively makes the number of full gradient accesses independent of the condition number `κ`, shifting the dependency onto the cheaper stochastic gradient calls. The authors compare this complexity to existing deterministic, stochastic, and specialized finite-sum methods like SAG and SDCA, arguing for advantages in memory efficiency, applicability to constrained problems, and high-probability convergence guarantees.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Result:** The paper's core claim—achieving a linear convergence rate `O(log 1/ε)` in terms of full gradient evaluations, independent of the condition number `κ`—is a strong and interesting theoretical result. It directly addresses a primary bottleneck for first-order methods on ill-conditioned problems.

2.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The construction of the "mixed gradient" and its use within a shrinking-domain epoch-based framework is a novel combination of techniques. It provides a principled way to leverage stochastic gradients to accelerate convergence in a deterministic sense, achieving a linear rate that purely stochastic methods typically cannot.

3.  **Generality:** Unlike recent competitors such as SAG and SDCA, which are designed specifically for unconstrained finite-sum problems, the proposed EMGD framework is more general. It naturally handles constrained optimization problems (provided the projection is efficient) and does not require the objective to be a finite sum.

4.  **Stronger Convergence Guarantee:** The analysis provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation results common for many stochastic algorithms like SAG and SDCA.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Dependence on a Known Condition Number:** The algorithm and its theoretical guarantees depend critically on knowing the condition number `κ` (or the smoothness `L` and strong convexity `λ` constants) beforehand. These values are required to set key hyperparameters like the step size `η` and the number of inner iterations `T`. In practice, `κ` is rarely known. The paper does not analyze the algorithm's performance under a misspecified `κ`, nor does it propose a practical method for estimating it. The brief discussion in the conclusion is insufficient for a parameter that is so central to the method's application.

2.  **Complete Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper's primary contribution is a new algorithm with claims of improved practical performance (by reducing expensive computations). However, there are no experiments to support these claims. An experimental section is essential to:
    *   Validate the theoretical convergence rates on real or synthetic data.
    *   Demonstrate the algorithm's sensitivity to the crucial hyperparameter `κ`.
    *   Provide a practical comparison against the methods it claims to improve upon, such as Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SGD, SAG, and SDCA, in terms of actual wall-clock time.

3.  **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** While the paper reviews standard deterministic and stochastic methods, it overlooks a relevant line of work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithms. For instance, Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012) present methods that interleave full gradient and stochastic steps and provide a review of earlier work on this topic. A comparison of EMGD's approach and convergence rates with this body of work is necessary to properly contextualize its contribution.

4.  **Ambiguity in the Problem Setting:** The problem formulation in Section 3.1, based on an oracle that returns a random function `f(·)` such that `F(w) = E[f(w)]`, suggests a general stochastic approximation setting. However, the comparisons to SAG and SDCA are only meaningful in the finite-sum setting, where `F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w)`. The paper should clarify whether it is designed to solve the finite-sum problem (as a "randomized" algorithm) or the more general stochastic expectation problem. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the exact scope and impact of the work.

### Detailed Comments

*   The algorithm's design, using a fixed full gradient as an anchor within an epoch, is reminiscent of variance reduction techniques. It would be valuable to discuss the connection between the "mixed gradient" `g̃_kt` and the variance-reduced gradients used in methods like SVRG, even if SVRG was developed later.
*   The authors argue that EMGD is superior to SAG/SDCA when `κ` is small relative to `n`. However, the complexity of EMGD has a `κ²` dependency on stochastic gradients, which can be prohibitive if `κ` is large. An empirical study would be the best way to understand the practical trade-offs.
*   The argument that EMGD is more amenable to distributed computing than SAG/SDCA is interesting but speculative. This claim would be stronger if supported by either a more detailed algorithmic description for a distributed environment or experimental evidence.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically elegant and novel algorithm with a striking convergence guarantee. However, the work in its current form is incomplete. The strong assumptions about knowing the condition number and the complete absence of experiments significantly limit its practical relevance and prevent a full assessment of its quality and significance. The paper would be much stronger if it addressed the issue of `κ` estimation and provided a thorough empirical evaluation against relevant baselines.
