This paper proposes a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex functions. The primary goal is to reduce the dependency on the condition number (κ) for the number of full gradient evaluations, which is a bottleneck in ill-conditioned problems. The standard optimal rate for deterministic first-order methods is O(√κ log 1/ε), requiring that many full gradient computations. The proposed EMGD algorithm leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. By constructing a "mixed gradient" from one full gradient and multiple stochastic gradients within each epoch, the algorithm achieves a linear convergence rate. The main theoretical result is that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution by computing only O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients, effectively making the number of expensive full gradient calls independent of the condition number.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its novel and striking theoretical result. The analysis demonstrates that, under the given assumptions, it is possible to achieve a linear convergence rate where the number of expensive full gradient evaluations is independent of the condition number κ. Shifting the dependency on κ from the full gradient complexity to the stochastic gradient complexity is an important conceptual contribution. If the cost of a stochastic gradient is significantly lower than a full gradient, this trade-off could lead to substantial computational savings. The theoretical analysis is detailed and appears to be sound.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

Despite the interesting theoretical contribution, the paper has several significant weaknesses in its current form that limit its impact and completeness.

1.  **Strong Reliance on a Known Condition Number:** The theoretical analysis, and indeed the algorithm's parameterization (e.g., the step size η = 1/[L√T] and the number of inner iterations T), critically depends on prior knowledge of the smoothness constant L and the strong convexity constant λ, and therefore the condition number κ. This is a very strong assumption that is often not met in practice. The paper does not address the algorithm's sensitivity to a mis-specified κ, nor does it provide any procedure for estimating it. The future work section briefly mentions this, but for a practical algorithm, this is a central issue that should be addressed in the main paper. What happens if the provided κ is an under- or over-estimate?

2.  **Complete Absence of Empirical Evaluation:** A major weakness is the lack of an experimental section. For a paper introducing a new algorithm, empirical evaluation is crucial to:
    *   Validate the theoretical convergence rates in practice.
    *   Demonstrate the actual performance benefits over existing methods.
    *   Study the algorithm's sensitivity to its hyperparameters, particularly the condition number κ (addressing the point above).
    *   Compare its computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time) against relevant baselines such as Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SGD, SAG, and SDCA on real-world datasets. The theoretical cost comparison in Table 2 is useful, but it cannot replace an empirical study.

3.  **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** The paper overlooks a relevant line of research on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods. For instance, the work by Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012) explicitly explores combining full gradients with stochastic updates to achieve fast convergence. Their work also provides a review of earlier methods with similar motivations. A thorough discussion and comparison of the proposed approach and convergence rates with this body of work is necessary to properly situate the paper's contribution and originality.

4.  **Ambiguity in the Problem Formulation:** The optimization setting is not clearly defined. Section 3.1 introduces a general stochastic optimization problem where F(w) = E[f(w)], but the comparisons in Section 3.4 are primarily with algorithms like SAG and SDCA, which are designed for the finite-sum setting, F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w). These are distinct settings. If the paper targets the finite-sum problem, the oracles should be defined accordingly (e.g., sampling an f_i). If it targets the general stochastic problem, the comparison to SAG/SDCA is less direct. This ambiguity should be resolved to clarify the paper's scope and claims.

5.  **Clarity of the "Mixed Gradient" Contribution:** The "mixed gradient" update is an interesting construction that serves as a variance-reduced estimator of the true gradient ∇F(w_kt) centered at w̄_k. It would be beneficial to discuss how this specific construction relates to or improves upon a simpler strategy of just interleaving standard full gradient steps with stochastic gradient steps. Is the proposed mixing scheme fundamentally more powerful, and if so, why?

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and theoretically significant result: achieving a linear convergence rate where the number of full gradient computations is independent of the condition number.
    *   The proposed trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations is an interesting direction for optimization research.

*   **Arguments for Rejection:**
    *   The strong and unaddressed assumption that the condition number κ is known beforehand is a major practical limitation.
    *   The complete lack of experiments makes it impossible to assess the practical viability, robustness, and actual performance of the proposed algorithm.
    *   The paper fails to discuss or compare against a key line of related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods.
    *   The problem setting (general stochastic vs. finite-sum) is ambiguous.

**Conclusion:**

The paper presents a theoretically appealing idea. However, the lack of experimental validation, the critical dependence on knowing the condition number without a discussion of robustness, and the omission of key related work significantly weaken the contribution. As it stands, the paper feels more like a preliminary theoretical result than a complete work. I would recommend rejection at this stage but would be open to reconsidering a revised version that thoroughly addresses these major concerns, especially with the addition of a comprehensive experimental study.
