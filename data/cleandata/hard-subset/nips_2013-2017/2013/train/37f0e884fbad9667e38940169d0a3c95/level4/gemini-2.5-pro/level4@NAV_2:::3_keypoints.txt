- Proposal: Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), combining full-gradient and stochastic-gradient updates to reduce full-gradient dependence on κ.  
- Main theoretical claim: EMGD finds ε-accurate solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.  
- Motivation: Aims to bridge deterministic (O(√κ log(1/ε)) full-gradient) and purely stochastic methods by replacing many full-gradient steps with cheaper stochastic ones.  
- Strength: Tackles an important problem of reducing expensive full-gradient computations for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Strength: EMGD algorithm and the mixed-gradient-within-shrinking-domain idea are novel.  
- Strength: Convergence proof appears technically sound and gives a high-probability linear convergence guarantee.  
- Weakness: Practical significance is unclear because the algorithm trades reduced full-gradient calls for an O(κ²) increase in stochastic-gradient calls.  
- Weakness: Total cost O((n + κ²) log(1/ε)) compares unfavorably to accelerated batch methods O(n√κ log(1/ε)), making EMGD preferable only when κ ≲ n^(2/3).  
- Weakness: Many ML settings (e.g., small regularization) produce very large κ, so the favorable regime may be uncommon; authors should discuss realistic scenarios where EMGD helps.  
- Weakness: EMGD’s high-probability guarantee is weaker than the deterministic guarantees of accelerated methods and should be acknowledged in comparisons.  
- Minor: State early that L ≥ λ, so κ = L/λ ≥ 1.  
- Minor: In the derivation of (14), replacing the equality with a simpler inequality could improve clarity.  
- Minor: Clarify notation |Vt| in the bound after (18) — it appears to be an absolute inner product bounded via norms.
