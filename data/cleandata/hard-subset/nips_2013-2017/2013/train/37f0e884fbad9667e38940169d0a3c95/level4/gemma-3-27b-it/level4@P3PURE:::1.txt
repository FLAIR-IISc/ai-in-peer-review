SUMMARY: The authors consider the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. The optimal first-order algorithm of Nesterov achieves linear convergence for such problems, but the constant depends on the square root of the condition number *k*. The authors consider the scenario where access is available to both an expensive full gradient of the objective and a cheap stochastic gradient oracle. They propose a hybrid algorithm, called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires O(log 1/ε) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of the condition number) and O(*k*<sup>2</sup> log(1/ε)) calls to the cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. Under the assumption that the condition number is not excessively large, this could be faster in theory. The main idea behind EMGD is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with a fixed number O(*k*<sup>2</sup>) of mixed gradient steps, utilizing a combination of the full gradient (computed once per epoch) and stochastic gradients (varying within the epoch). By averaging the O(*k*<sup>2</sup>) iterates within an epoch, they demonstrate a constant decrease in suboptimality independent of the condition number, which explains why the number of required full gradient steps (epochs) is independent of *k*. They provide a simple and complete self-contained proof of their convergence rate, but do not include any experiments.

I enjoyed reading this paper and believe the idea of mixing full gradient computations with a large number of cheap stochastic gradient steps is novel and interesting. This work builds upon a growing body of recent research attempting to leverage cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining a linear convergence rate. A recent theoretical and practical breakthrough was achieved with the SAG algorithm [16], which works for smooth strongly convex objectives that are the sum of *n* simple functions (e.g., in regularized empirical loss minimization, where *n* is the number of training examples). In this case, a reasonable assumption is that the full gradient oracle is *n* times more expensive than the stochastic gradient oracle. SAG is then faster than Nesterov’s algorithm in theory when the condition number *k* ≤ *n*/8. In contrast, EMGD is faster than Nesterov’s algorithm in theory when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>2/3</sup>, and is slower than SAG in all regimes (with the same big O complexity when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>). To illustrate these speed-ups, if *k* = *n*<sup>1/2</sup>, both SAG & EMGD are O(*n* log(1/ε)), whereas Nesterov’s algorithm is O(*n*<sup>5/4</sup> log(1/ε)). The two main advantages of EMGD over SAG are: 1) as the authors mention, EMGD works for constrained optimization (assuming the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently), while SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems; and 2) the convergence proof for EMGD is significantly simpler than that of SAG, potentially leading to greater insights and making modifications to EMGD more amenable to provable guarantees. Their result also holds with high probability, versus in expectation for SAG. The authors also suggest potential memory/parallelization advantages, though this is less clear as SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches (which also reduces the memory requirement by the size of the mini-batches).

EVALUATION SUMMARY: 

Pros: 
- Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theoretical foundation.
- Similar to SAG, achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization when the condition number *k* does not scale with the number of training examples *n*; however, their algorithm is more general (works for constrained optimization) and the proof is significantly simpler.
- The paper is clearly written and the proof is self-contained.

Cons: 
- Lacks an experimental evaluation to demonstrate the algorithm's practical impact (and does not provide concrete examples illustrating why its suggested theoretical advantages might be relevant in practice).
- Does not sufficiently discuss the algorithm’s limitations and drawbacks, especially in comparison to existing algorithms (section 3.4 should be improved!).
- The proof lacks high-level comments that could clarify the essential insights driving its construction.

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. Experiments would be valuable, although the theoretical contribution is strong enough to stand on its own. The authors should definitely expand section 3.4 to address the limitations and drawbacks of their algorithm. They should also include a more detailed discussion of the sum of *n* functions example, highlighting the differences with SAG and Nesterov (e.g., EMGD is worse than Nesterov for (roughly) *k* > *n*<sup>2/3</sup>; SAG is comparable to EMGD for 1 ≤ *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>; better than EMGD (and Nesterov) for *n*<sup>1/2</sup> < *k* ≤ *n*/8; and worse than Nesterov for *k* > *n*/8). A major drawback for practitioners is that the number of steps within an epoch must be fixed in advance (requiring knowledge of *k*) – in contrast, SCDA [17] and SAG do not require a pre-defined number of steps and can potentially benefit from faster practical convergence than the theoretical bounds predict (or from better local condition numbers). Furthermore, SCDA includes automatic step-size selection, while SAG employs an adaptive step-size heuristic that appears to work well in practice. The authors should include this discussion in the paper.

[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: a) As another reviewer noted, in many machine learning applications, the regularization parameter is often set as *C*/ *n*, leading to a condition number of approximately *n*/ *C*. This is a regime in which SAG still outperforms Nesterov, but where EMGD does not. This should be pointed out, and another practical setting where *k* < *n*<sup>2/3</sup> should be mentioned. b) The authors should cite [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012], which also presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear rate of convergence. This algorithm still includes the condition number in the rate, so its ultimate rate is not faster than standard gradient descent (though the initial steps are faster due to the use of cheaper steps) – thus, the current submission can still theoretically improve upon this rate when *k* is not too large with respect to *n*.] 

CLARITY: The paper is fairly clear. I appreciated the summary in Table 1, which I haven’t encountered in such a clear format elsewhere. The proof is rigorous, but would be more helpful with a few high-level comments motivating the defined quantities, which currently fit together somewhat magically.

ORIGINALITY: This represents a novel combination of known techniques.

SIGNIFICANCE: The practical relevance of the algorithm remains to be demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution is potentially impactful. Considering the challenging proof of convergence for SAG (and the simpler proof of SDCA, but which applies only to a restricted setting), the simple proof of EMGD is a significant contribution.
