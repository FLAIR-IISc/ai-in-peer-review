The paper introduces a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining full gradient and stochastic gradient queries. The authors demonstrate that this approach can eliminate the dependence on the condition number in the convergence rate.

This work presents a compelling and significant theoretical result: under the assumption of a known condition number (strong convexity and smoothness constants) and access to a first-order hybrid oracle, a carefully balanced combination of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps achieves a high-probability O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization.

However, several issues require attention. The current theoretical analysis is limited to the scenario where the condition number is precisely known *a priori*. The algorithm’s performance under hyperparameter misspecification – specifically, when the condition number is inaccurately estimated – remains unexplored. The result effectively removes condition number dependence from the convergence rate only when the condition number is known. While analyzing the unknown condition number case may be challenging, experimental investigation could provide valuable insights.  Critically, the paper lacks an experimental section, a significant omission given the introduction of a new algorithm. Empirical validation is needed to assess the theoretical findings and their practical relevance.

Specifically, the proposed algorithm (EGD), defined by Eq. 8 (page 4) and utilizing the mixed gradient in Eq. 7, requires setting the hyperparameter η, which is directly tied to the condition number κ. The paper does not offer guidance or theoretical justification for determining κ beforehand. This necessitates either an estimation procedure (even a heuristic supported by experiments) or a clear statement that κ is assumed known, coupled with an analysis of the algorithm’s behavior when κ is misspecified – including convergence rates with overestimated and underestimated values.

Furthermore, while the smooth and strongly convex setting is common in theoretical analysis, considering non-strongly convex cases would broaden the applicability and interest of the work. 

The proposed algorithm isn’t presented as a simple alternation between deterministic and stochastic steps, but rather as a single “mixed” update per iteration. A discussion comparing this approach to algorithms that explicitly interleave deterministic and stochastic steps would be beneficial.  

The paper also omits a review of related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms, such as Friedlander & Schmidt (2012).  Comparing convergence rates with existing methods would strengthen the contribution. A comprehensive experimental study, comparing EGD to SGD, averaged SGD, and recent stochastic optimization methods (SAG, etc.), is highly recommended.

Finally, the optimization setting is not clearly defined. It is unclear whether the authors aim to solve the deterministic optimization problem using a randomized algorithm or the stochastic approximation problem directly. The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is ambiguous in this regard, and subsequent claims do not clarify the intended setting. This ambiguity should be addressed.
