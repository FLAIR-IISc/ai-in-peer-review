

**Review for "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"**

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing smooth and strongly convex functions by reducing the dependence of gradient-based methods on the condition number κ. The authors propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. This contrasts with standard methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, which requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations.

The work builds upon recent advances in first-order optimization, particularly the SAG and SDCA algorithms that achieve linear convergence for sum-of-functions problems (F(w) = (1/n)∑ᵢ=1ⁿ fᵢ(w)). While SAG and SDCA achieve O(n log(1/ε)) complexity for certain condition numbers, EMGD offers a different tradeoff: for the sum-of-functions case, it outperforms Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), though it is slower than SAG when κ > n^(1/2). The paper's key contribution is a simple, high-probability convergence proof for EMGD, which contrasts with the more complex proofs for SAG and SDCA.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality (8/10):** The algorithmic approach of mixing full and stochastic gradients within epochs is novel and effectively leverages the availability of both gradient types. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from related work, particularly SAG and SDCA, by highlighting the constrained optimization capability and simpler proof structure.

2. **Quality (7/10):** The theoretical analysis is sound and well-structured, with a complete proof showing linear convergence with high probability. The paper correctly identifies the trade-offs between different optimization methods and provides a clear mathematical foundation for the algorithm's performance.

3. **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is well-written, with a clear summary of key results in Table 1 and a logically organized presentation. The algorithm description is precise, and the proof, while dense, is self-contained. The authors could enhance clarity by adding brief high-level explanations of key design choices in the proof.

4. **Significance (7/10):** The theoretical contribution is meaningful, particularly for constrained optimization problems where SAG and SDCA do not apply. The simpler proof also offers potential for future extensions. However, the practical relevance needs stronger justification through experiments or concrete examples.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Experiments:** The paper provides no empirical validation of the algorithm's performance, making it difficult to assess its practical utility. Given the computational claims, experiments on standard machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression with varying condition numbers) would significantly strengthen the paper.

2. **Incomplete Comparison with Related Work:** Section 3.4 could be improved by more thoroughly discussing limitations. For example:
   - EMGD requires knowledge of κ to set the number of iterations per epoch, whereas SAG and SDCA do not have this requirement and can adapt to local condition numbers.
   - The memory advantage claimed for EMGD is overstated; SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which reduces memory requirements.
   - The paper should clarify the practical regime where κ ≤ n^(2/3) is relevant (e.g., when regularization is proportional to 1/n, κ ≈ n/C, which is often the case in practice).

3. **Missing Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper should address how EMGD handles cases where κ is unknown or varies. While the authors mention potential estimation methods, this remains speculative without empirical validation.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and novel algorithmic approach for smooth strongly convex optimization. The key strength is the clean, high-probability convergence proof that is simpler than those for SAG and SDCA. However, the lack of experimental validation and incomplete discussion of practical limitations prevent the paper from reaching its full potential.

The authors should address the following points before publication:
1. Add experiments comparing EMGD with SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's method on standard machine learning problems with varying condition numbers.
2. Expand Section 3.4 to provide a more balanced comparison, particularly addressing the practical limitations of requiring knowledge of κ.
3. Clarify the practical regimes where κ ≤ n^(2/3) is meaningful (e.g., when regularization is proportional to 1/n).
4. Correct minor issues noted in the draft review (e.g., clarify the domain notation consistency, properly cite Friedlander and Schmidt's hybrid method).

The theoretical contribution is significant, but the paper would benefit from stronger practical grounding. With the suggested revisions, this work could be a valuable addition to the literature on first-order optimization methods.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper makes a novel theoretical contribution with a clear algorithmic idea and sound analysis. While the lack of experiments and incomplete comparison with related work are notable weaknesses, the theoretical foundation is strong enough to warrant acceptance with minor revisions. The algorithm's applicability to constrained optimization problems is a particularly valuable feature that distinguishes it from existing methods.
