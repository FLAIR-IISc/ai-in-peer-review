This paper introduces a novel hybrid stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm that combines stochastic and batch gradient updates. The authors demonstrate that the algorithm converges to an ε-accurate solution with a complexity of O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(k<sup>2</sup>log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. The convergence proof appears largely correct and represents a valuable contribution, though I noted a few minor technical details needing attention (detailed below).

My primary concern revolves around the practical relevance of this work within the machine learning (ML) context, given the conference’s focus. The analysis relies heavily on a strong convexity constant, *μ*. In typical ML applications, strong convexity is often imposed by regularization, resulting in *μ* being proportional to the number of samples, *N* (i.e., *μ* = *CN*, where *C* is independent of *N*). Under this common assumption, the proposed method only offers a speedup over batch gradient descent when the number of samples is bounded by O(*C*<sup>3</sup>/*L*<sup>3</sup>). This appears to be a rather restrictive regime and its practical implications are unclear.

Furthermore, the convergence rate presented is probabilistic, while batch gradient descent offers deterministic convergence. This distinction is crucial. The paper needs to thoroughly address whether the probabilistic guarantee provides a meaningful advantage over the certainty of batch gradient descent, and convincingly demonstrate the algorithm’s benefits for realistic ML optimization problems. A careful discussion of this trade-off is essential to establish the paper’s relevance to the ML community.

**Minor Comments/Suggested Improvements:**

*   **Equation (6):** The left-hand side should be ||*w*<sup>*</sup> - *ŵ*||<sup>2</sup> to represent the squared norm of the error.
*   **Line 288:** Please clarify which function is being referenced when applying equation (7). Greater specificity would improve readability.
*   **Line 286:** The equality following the statement should be removed. It does not contribute to understanding and potentially distracts from the main point.
*   **Equation (13):** The absolute values should be replaced with norms to accurately represent vector magnitudes.
*   **Relationship between *L* and *λ*:** While likely obvious to experts, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that *L* ≥ *λ*. Clarity is always preferred.
*   **Equation (4):** *x*<sup>*</sup> should be *w*<sup>*</sup>, and *f* should be *F* to maintain consistency with notation. Additionally, Lemma 1 implies the first term in the `max` is always greater than the second; this could be explicitly stated.
*   **Condition Number:** Please provide a precise definition of the condition number in terms of *λ* and *L*. This would aid in understanding the algorithm’s sensitivity to ill-conditioning. 

Overall, the algorithm presents a theoretically interesting contribution, but its practical advantages over established methods, particularly batch gradient descent, are not convincingly demonstrated under commonly encountered ML scenarios. A more in-depth discussion addressing this point is critical for justifying the paper’s inclusion in a machine learning conference.
