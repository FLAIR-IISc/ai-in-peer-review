

The paper introduces a novel "hybrid" deterministic-stochastic optimization method that combines full gradient and stochastic gradient evaluations. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm achieves linear convergence with a number of full gradient evaluations that is logarithmic in 1/ε, independent of the condition number κ. This represents a significant theoretical contribution as it breaks the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity barrier for first-order methods when both gradient types are available.

The paper makes a strong theoretical case under the assumption that the condition number κ is known in advance. The authors demonstrate that EMGD achieves an ǫ-optimal solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. This is a substantial improvement over standard full gradient methods (which require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients) and matches the convergence rate of specialized methods like SAG and SDCA while offering additional advantages.

However, the paper has several significant limitations:

First, the analysis assumes perfect knowledge of κ, which is often unrealistic in practice. The paper fails to discuss how the algorithm behaves when κ is misspecified (either overestimated or underestimated), a critical concern for practical implementation. The authors should either provide a method for estimating κ or analyze the algorithm's robustness to κ misspecification. The current theoretical result only applies when κ is known, and the paper does not address how this limitation affects real-world applications.

Second, the paper lacks any empirical validation. Given that the main contribution is a new algorithm, an experimental section comparing EMGD with standard methods (SGD, SAG, SDCA) would be essential to validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical utility. Without empirical results, it is difficult to assess whether the theoretical improvements translate to real-world performance gains.

Third, the paper does not adequately contextualize its contribution within existing hybrid optimization literature. It should discuss and compare with Friedlander and Schmidt's "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" (SISC 2012), which presents similar approaches. The authors should clarify how EMGD differs from and improves upon this line of work.

Fourth, the theoretical setup is confusingly presented. The paper discusses optimization over a deterministic objective F(w) = (1/n)∑f_i(w), but the theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is framed as if solving a stochastic optimization problem E[F(w)]. This ambiguity needs resolution as it impacts the interpretation of the results.

Fifth, the paper does not address the non-strongly convex case, which is important for many practical applications. While the strongly convex setting is a common theoretical assumption, the authors should at least acknowledge the limitations of their approach for more general optimization problems.

In summary, while the theoretical result is interesting and the algorithm is novel, the paper falls short in addressing practical concerns and lacks empirical validation. The requirement for perfect knowledge of κ is a significant practical limitation, and the absence of experimental results makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's real-world utility. The authors should address these issues to strengthen their contribution, particularly by providing a method for estimating κ or analyzing robustness to κ misspecification, and including empirical validation comparing EMGD with existing methods.
