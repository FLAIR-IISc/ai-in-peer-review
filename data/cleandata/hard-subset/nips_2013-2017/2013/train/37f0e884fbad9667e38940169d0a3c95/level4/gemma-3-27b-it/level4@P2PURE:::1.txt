SUMMARY: The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence for this problem, its convergence rate’s constant depends on the square root of the condition number, k. This work considers scenarios where both an expensive full gradient and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle are available. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires O(log 1/eps) calls to the full gradient oracle—independent of the condition number—and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle. This approach could be faster than existing methods in theory when the condition number is not excessively large. The core idea of EMGD is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with a fixed number, O(k^2), of mixed gradient steps. These steps combine the full gradient (computed once per epoch) with stochastic gradients (varying within the epoch). By averaging the O(k^2) iterates within each epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant decrease in suboptimality independent of the condition number, leading to a number of required full gradient computations—and therefore epochs—that is independent of the condition number. The paper presents a simple and self-contained proof of this convergence rate, but lacks experimental validation.

I found this paper enjoyable to read, and the concept of mixing full gradient computations with a large number of cheaper stochastic gradient steps is both novel and interesting. This work contributes to the growing body of research aimed at leveraging cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence rates. A recent theoretical and practical advancement in this area is the SAG algorithm [16], which is designed for smooth strongly convex objectives expressed as the sum of n simple functions (common in regularized empirical loss minimization, where n is the number of training examples). SAG is more efficient than Nesterov’s algorithm in theory when the condition number k is less than or equal to n/8, assuming the full gradient oracle is n times more expensive than the stochastic gradient oracle. EMGD, in contrast, outperforms Nesterov’s algorithm in theory when k is less than or equal to n^(2/3), but is less efficient than SAG across all regimes (with comparable big O notation when k is less than or equal to n^(1/2)). For illustrative purposes, if k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD have a complexity of O(n log(1/eps)), while Nesterov’s algorithm is O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)). I see two primary advantages of EMGD over SAG: 1) as the authors note, EMGD extends to constrained optimization problems where the gradient mapping update is efficiently computable, whereas SAG is currently limited to unconstrained problems; and 2) EMGD’s convergence proof is significantly simpler than SAG’s, potentially offering greater insight and enabling easier modifications with provable guarantees.  Furthermore, EMGD’s result holds with high probability, while SAG’s is in expectation. The authors also suggest potential memory and parallelization advantages, though these are less conclusive since SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches (which also reduces memory requirements). 

EVALUATION SUMMARY: 
Pros: 
- Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea supported by a clean, simple, and solid theoretical foundation.
- Similar to SAG, achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization without the condition number multiplying the number of training examples, n; however, the algorithm is more general (handles constrained optimization) and the proof is considerably simpler.
- The paper is well-written and the proof is self-contained. 
Cons: 
- The absence of experimental results hinders assessment of the algorithm's practical impact. No concrete examples are provided to illustrate the relevance of its theoretical advantages in practice.
- The discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and drawbacks is insufficient, particularly in comparison to existing methods; section 3.4 requires improvement (as detailed in this review).
- The proof lacks high-level commentary to elucidate the key insights driving its construction.

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. While experiments would be valuable, I believe the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to stand on its own. The authors should definitively expand section 3.4 to address the algorithm's limitations and drawbacks. They should also include a more concrete discussion of the sum of n functions example, clearly illustrating the differences between EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov (as I previously mentioned—for example, EMGD performs worse than Nesterov when k > n^(2/3); SAG is equivalent to EMGD for 1 <= k <= n^(1/2); better than EMGD (and Nesterov) for n^(1/2) < k <= n/8; and worse than Nesterov for k > n/8). A key limitation for practitioners is the need to predefine the number of steps within an epoch, based on knowledge of k—in contrast, SCDA [17] and SAG do not require a fixed number of steps and can thus potentially achieve faster practical convergence or benefit from favorable local condition numbers. Moreover, SCDA features automatic step-size selection, and SAG employs an adaptive step-size heuristic that performs well in practice. The authors should incorporate this discussion into the paper. 
[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: a) As another reviewer pointed out, in many machine learning applications, the regularization parameter is C/n, leading to a condition number of approximately n/C. This is a regime where SAG continues to outperform Nesterov, but where EMGD does not. This should be noted, and another practical scenario where k < n^(2/3) could be mentioned. b) The authors should cite [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012], which also presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear convergence rate. This algorithm still includes the condition number in the rate, resulting in a rate no faster than standard gradient descent (although the initial steps may be faster due to the use of cheaper steps)—allowing the current submission to theoretically improve upon this rate when k is not too large relative to n.] 

CLARITY: The paper is generally clear. I particularly appreciated the clear summary of convergence rates in Table 1, which is a valuable addition not commonly found in the literature. The proof is logically sound but would benefit from additional high-level comments to clarify the motivations behind the defined quantities, which currently appear somewhat arbitrary. 

ORIGINALITY: The work represents a novel combination of established techniques.

SIGNIFICANCE: The practical relevance of the algorithm remains to be demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution is potentially impactful. In light of the challenging convergence proofs for SAG and the simplicity of the SDCA proof (although it applies only to a limited setting), the straightforward proof of EMGD is a significant achievement.
