

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors demonstrate that EMGD requires only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations to reach an ε-optimal solution, improving upon the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient complexity. The approach builds on recent work in stochastic optimization (e.g., SAG, SDCA) but introduces a unique mixed gradient descent technique that achieves condition number-independent full gradient access.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The theoretical analysis is technically sound and appears correct. The convergence proof is well-structured, with careful handling of the mixed gradient properties and appropriate use of martingale inequalities for the stochastic components. The authors properly address the relationship between the condition number κ and the convergence rate.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm description is precise, and the mathematical notation is consistently used. The main theorem is presented with appropriate conditions, and the comparison with existing methods is well-explained in Table 2.

*Originality:* The mixed gradient approach represents a novel combination of techniques. While epoch-based methods exist (e.g., EGD), the specific mechanism of using both full and stochastic gradients in the mixed gradient step is distinct from previous approaches like SAG or SDCA. The authors appropriately contextualize their work against relevant NIPS proceedings.

*Significance:* The reduction in full gradient evaluations could be valuable in settings where full gradient computation is significantly more expensive than stochastic gradients. The algorithm's applicability to constrained optimization problems is a notable advantage over SAG and SDCA.

**Weaknesses:**

*Significance:* The most significant concern is the practical relevance to machine learning problems, which is the primary focus of NIPS. In standard ML settings, strong convexity is typically induced by a regularizer (e.g., λ = Ω(1/n) for ridge regression), making λ proportional to the number of samples n. Under this common assumption, the condition number κ = O(n), and the paper's advantage over batch gradient descent (which requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) = O(√n log(1/ε)) full gradients) only holds when κ ≤ n^(2/3), i.e., when n is bounded by a constant. This represents an uninteresting regime for practical machine learning problems where n is large. The authors should clarify why this regime is meaningful for real-world applications.

*Quality:* The high-probability convergence guarantee (Theorem 1) is weaker than the deterministic convergence of standard batch gradient descent. While the authors acknowledge this, they do not sufficiently address why this probabilistic guarantee is preferable in practical ML contexts where deterministic guarantees are often preferred.

*Clarity:* The paper could better explain the relationship between the condition number and the practical computational advantage. The connection between the theoretical analysis and concrete machine learning applications needs strengthening. The statement that "κ ≤ n^(2/3) is a reasonable scenario" (Section 4.1) requires stronger justification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel mixed gradient approach that achieves condition number-independent full gradient access
- Theoretical proof of linear convergence with rigorous analysis
- Applicability to constrained optimization problems, unlike SAG/SDCA
- Potential computational advantage in specific problem settings

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The practical advantage over batch gradient descent is limited to an uninteresting regime for machine learning (κ = O(n^(2/3)) when λ = Ω(1/n))
- High-probability convergence guarantee is weaker than deterministic alternatives
- Insufficient justification for the practical relevance to machine learning problems
- The computational complexity analysis (Table 2) shows EMGD is outperformed by SAG/SDCA in most practical cases

**Recommendation:**

While the technical contribution is sound and the algorithm is novel, the paper's significance for NIPS is questionable due to the limited practical relevance to machine learning problems. The authors should significantly revise the paper to better justify the practical advantages of their approach, particularly by addressing the strong convexity parameter's relationship to problem size in standard ML applications. They should also provide empirical evidence demonstrating the practical benefits in realistic settings.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of when the proposed method is preferable to existing alternatives, and a clearer explanation of why the high-probability convergence guarantee is sufficient for practical applications. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution to the machine learning community appears marginal.

I recommend rejection, as the paper's main claim of practical significance for machine learning problems is not adequately supported. The theoretical contribution is interesting, but it does not sufficiently advance the state of the art in a way that would be meaningful for the NIPS community, which prioritizes practical relevance in machine learning. The authors should either provide stronger empirical evidence or significantly revise their claims about the practical advantages of EMGD.
