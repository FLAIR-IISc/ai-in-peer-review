The paper proposes a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining calls to a deterministic oracle and a stochastic oracle. The authors demonstrate that this approach can eliminate the dependency on the condition number in the rate of convergence. They present an interesting and striking theoretical result, showing that with a suitably chosen mix of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps, a high-probability O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate can be achieved for batch optimization, assuming the condition number is known.

However, there are several concerns with the current state of the paper. Firstly, the theoretical analysis only covers the case where the condition number is perfectly known beforehand and does not address the behavior of the algorithm when this hyper-parameter is misspecified. The current result only applies when the condition number is known in advance, and it is unclear how the algorithm performs when this parameter is unknown or misspecified. While the theoretical analysis for these cases may be challenging, experimental studies could have been conducted to provide insights into the algorithm's behavior.

The absence of an experiments section is the second major concern. Given that the main contribution of the paper is a new algorithm, it would be beneficial to include experimental results to assess the theoretical findings and study their relevance to the algorithm's actual behavior on empirical data.

Furthermore, the proposed algorithm, EGD, relies on the update rule defined by Eq. 8, which requires the hyper-parameter η to be set. This, in turn, requires knowledge of the condition number κ, which is not provided by the authors. There are two possibilities: either this parameter needs to be estimated, in which case a heuristic procedure supported by numerical experiments is missing, or it is assumed to be known, in which case the theoretical analysis should cover the cases where this hyper-parameter is misspecified.

Additionally, the smooth and strongly convex case may be too restrictive, and other settings, such as non-strongly convex cases, are also important and arise in several situations. The authors could have discussed the extension of their results to these cases.

The idea of blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not new, and it would be interesting to compare the proposed algorithm with similar algorithms that interleave deterministic and stochastic gradient steps. The authors do not review related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms, which would be valuable for discussing and comparing convergence rates.

Finally, a thorough experimental study, including a comparison with regular SGD, averaged SGD, and recent proposals for stochastic first-order optimization, would be a valuable addition to the paper. The optimization setting considered in the paper is not clearly stated, and the authors should specify whether they intend to solve the deterministic optimization problem or the stochastic approximation problem.
