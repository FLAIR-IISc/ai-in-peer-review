### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex set, under the assumption that projections onto the constraint set (or gradient mapping updates) are computationally efficient. The authors propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. The key theoretical result is that EMGD requires only O(log 1/ε) full gradient computations—a number independent of the problem's condition number (κ)—alongside O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradient computations. For problems where the condition number is not excessively large, this complexity offers a theoretical advantage over standard first-order methods.

The core idea of EMGD is to structure the optimization into epochs. Each epoch begins with a single full gradient calculation, followed by a fixed number (O(κ²)) of "mixed" gradient steps that combine the pre-computed full gradient with fresh stochastic gradients. By averaging the iterates generated within an epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant-factor reduction in suboptimality, independent of the condition number. This mechanism explains the logarithmic dependence on ε for full gradient calls. The paper provides a simple, self-contained proof of this convergence rate but does not include any experimental validation.

### **Evaluation**

This is a well-written paper presenting a novel and interesting theoretical contribution. The idea of mixing a few full gradient computations with many cheap stochastic steps to achieve a linear convergence rate, where the dominant cost factor is independent of the condition number, is compelling. The work is well-situated within the recent line of research aiming to combine the benefits of stochastic and full-batch methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and elegant algorithmic idea. A significant contribution is the simplicity and clarity of the convergence proof. Compared to the complex analyses required for related methods like SAG, the proof for EMGD is remarkably straightforward and self-contained, which may facilitate future analysis and extensions. The result is also strong, holding with high probability rather than only in expectation.

2.  **Generality:** EMGD extends some of the benefits seen in algorithms like SAG to the constrained optimization setting, a domain where SAG is not currently defined. This broadens the applicability of fast hybrid methods.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, and the inclusion of Table 1 provides a helpful summary of convergence rates for related algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Experiments:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of empirical validation. Without experiments, it is difficult to assess whether the theoretical speed-ups translate into practical performance gains, especially since the O(κ²) dependence in the stochastic complexity could be substantial. The authors should provide experiments to demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness and to support the practical relevance of the theoretical advantages they claim (e.g., for constrained problems).

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations and Comparisons:** The comparison to existing work in Section 3.4 is underdeveloped and should be significantly expanded to provide a more balanced perspective.
    *   **Comparison with SAG/SCDA/Nesterov:** For the important case of regularized empirical loss minimization (a sum of *n* functions), a more detailed comparison is warranted. The authors should explicitly state the regimes where EMGD is superior or inferior. For example:
        *   EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov's method when κ ≲ n^(2/3).
        *   SAG is faster than EMGD when n^(1/2) ≲ κ ≤ n/8.
        *   In the common machine learning setting where the regularization parameter leads to a condition number κ ≈ n, SAG maintains an advantage over Nesterov's method, while EMGD does not. This is a critical limitation that should be acknowledged.
    *   **Practical Drawbacks:** The paper should address practical drawbacks. EMGD requires knowledge of the condition number κ to set the number of inner iterations, a parameter that is often unknown in practice. In contrast, algorithms like SAG and SCDA can be run without pre-fixing the number of inner steps and can benefit from better local conditioning. Furthermore, methods like SCDA feature automatic step-size selection, and SAG has effective adaptive step-size heuristics—both are practical advantages not addressed by EMGD.
    *   **Memory and Parallelization:** The claim that EMGD has a parallelization advantage over SAG is not entirely convincing. SAG can be implemented with mini-batches, which enables parallelization and reduces memory requirements. This point should be clarified or revised.

3.  **Missing Citation:** The authors should cite and discuss "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" by M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt (SISC, 2012). This paper also presents a hybrid algorithm with a linear convergence rate. While its rate still depends on the condition number (unlike EMGD's full-gradient rate), it is a highly relevant piece of prior art.

4.  **Clarity of Proof:** While the proof is technically sound, its presentation could be improved by adding high-level commentary. The current derivation, while correct, can feel somewhat "magical." Explaining the intuition behind key steps and the purpose of certain defined quantities would greatly enhance the reader's understanding.
