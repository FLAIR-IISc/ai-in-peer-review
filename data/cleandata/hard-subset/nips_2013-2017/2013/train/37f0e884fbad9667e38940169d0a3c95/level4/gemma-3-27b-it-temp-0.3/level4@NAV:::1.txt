## Review

**Quality: 7/10**
**Clarity: 8/10**
**Originality: 8/10**
**Significance: 7/10**

**SUMMARY:** The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence for this problem, its convergence rate depends on the square root of the condition number, *k*. This work considers scenarios where both an expensive full gradient and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle are available. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), requiring O(log 1/ε) full gradient oracle calls (independent of *k*) and O(*k*<sup>2</sup> log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient oracle calls.  This approach offers potential theoretical advantages over Nesterov’s algorithm when the condition number is not excessively large. The core idea of EMGD is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with O(*k*<sup>2</sup>) mixed gradient steps, combining the full gradient (computed once per epoch) with stochastic gradients (varying within the epoch). Averaging the iterates within each epoch yields a constant decrease in suboptimality independent of the condition number, leading to a number of full gradient steps independent of *k*. The authors provide a self-contained and straightforward proof of the convergence rate, but do not include any experimental validation.

I found this paper enjoyable and believe the concept of integrating full gradient computations with numerous inexpensive stochastic gradient steps is both novel and intriguing. This work contributes to the growing body of research focused on leveraging cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence rates. A recent theoretical and practical advancement is the SAG algorithm [16], applicable to smooth, strongly convex objectives expressible as the sum of *n* simpler functions (common in regularized empirical loss minimization, where *n* represents the number of training examples). SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov’s algorithm when *k* ≤ *n*/8, assuming the full gradient oracle is *n* times more expensive than the stochastic one.  In contrast, EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov’s algorithm when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>2/3</sup>, but slower than SAG across all regimes (sharing the same asymptotic complexity when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>).  For example, if *k* = *n*<sup>1/2</sup>, both SAG and EMGD are O(*n* log(1/ε)), while Nesterov’s algorithm is O(*n*<sup>5/4</sup> log(1/ε)).  EMGD’s primary advantages over SAG are its applicability to constrained optimization (given an efficient gradient mapping update) – a feature currently lacking in SAG – and its simpler convergence proof, potentially offering deeper insights and facilitating provable extensions. The authors also suggest potential memory and parallelization benefits, though these are less definitive as SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which also reduces memory requirements.

**EVALUATION SUMMARY:**

**Pros:**
- Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea supported by a clean, simple, and rigorous theoretical analysis.
- Similar to SAG, achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization without the condition number multiplying the number of training examples, *n*; however, the algorithm is more general (handles constrained optimization) and the proof is significantly simpler.
- The paper is well-written and the proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**
- The absence of experimental results hinders the demonstration of the algorithm’s practical impact and relevance.  No concrete examples are provided to illustrate the potential benefits of the proposed theoretical advantages.
- The discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and drawbacks, particularly in comparison to existing methods, is insufficient. Section 3.4 requires improvement, as detailed in my suggestions below.
- The proof lacks high-level commentary that could elucidate the key insights driving its construction.

**QUALITY:** The paper is technically sound. While experiments would be valuable, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to stand independently. The authors should definitively expand section 3.4 to address the limitations and drawbacks of their algorithm. They should also provide a more detailed discussion of the sum of *n* functions example, highlighting the differences with SAG and Nesterov (e.g., EMGD performs worse than Nesterov for approximately *k* > *n*<sup>2/3</sup>; SAG is comparable to EMGD for 1 ≤ *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>; superior to EMGD (and Nesterov) for *n*<sup>1/2</sup> < *k* ≤ *n*/8; and inferior to Nesterov for *k* > *n*/8). A significant practical drawback of EMGD is the need to predefine the number of steps within an epoch based on knowledge of *k* – a limitation not shared by SCDA [17] or SAG, which can benefit from faster practical convergence or adapt to local condition numbers. Furthermore, SCDA incorporates automatic step-size selection, while SAG employs an effective adaptive step-size heuristic. These points should be addressed in the paper.

[**ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers:** a) As another reviewer noted, in many machine learning applications, the regularization parameter is often set as C/*n*, resulting in a condition number approximately equal to *n*/C'. In this regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov, while EMGD does not. This should be acknowledged, and another practical scenario where *k* < *n*<sup>2/3</sup> should be mentioned. b) The authors should cite [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012], which also presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear convergence rate. However, this algorithm still exhibits the condition number in its rate, resulting in a rate no faster than standard gradient descent, whereas the current submission can theoretically improve upon this rate when *k* is not excessively large relative to *n*.]

**CLARITY:** The paper is generally clear. I appreciated the concise summary of convergence rates in Table 1, which is a valuable addition not commonly found in the literature. The proof is rigorous and followable, but would benefit from additional high-level comments to motivate the defined quantities and explain their interplay.

**ORIGINALITY:** This work represents a novel combination of established techniques.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The practical relevance of the algorithm remains to be demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution is potentially impactful, particularly given the complexity of the SAG convergence proof and the simplicity of the EMGD proof, which may facilitate further research and provable extensions.
