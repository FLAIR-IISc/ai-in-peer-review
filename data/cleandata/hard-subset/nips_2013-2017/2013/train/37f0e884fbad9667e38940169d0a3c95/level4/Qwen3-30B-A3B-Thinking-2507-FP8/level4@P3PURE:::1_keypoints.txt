- Ratings: quality 7/10, clarity 8, originality 8, significance 7.  
- Problem: optimize smooth, strongly convex functions with cheap stochastic gradients plus expensive full-gradient oracle.  
- Contribution: proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) using O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic calls for moderate κ.  
- Key idea: replace each full-gradient epoch with O(κ²) mixed steps combining one full gradient and multiple stochastic gradients; average iterates to remove κ-dependence in epoch count.  
- Theory: provides a simple, self-contained convergence proof with high-probability guarantees.  
- No experiments: manuscript omits empirical validation of practical relevance and regimes where theory matters.  
- Comparison to SAG and Nesterov: EMGD is slower than SAG generally but matches asymptotic complexity when κ ≤ n^(1/2); outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3) in sum-of-functions setting.  
- Advantages over SAG: handles constrained problems and has a much simpler proof plus stronger high-probability results (vs. SAG’s expectation-based analysis).  
- Practical caveat: typical ML regularization often yields κ ≈ n/C', a regime where SAG still outperforms EMGD.  
- Limitation: requires fixing epoch size O(κ²) in advance, unlike adaptive methods (e.g., SCDA, SAG) that exploit faster local convergence.  
- Requested expansion: substantially improve Section 3.4 to discuss limitations, adaptive strategies, and concrete comparisons in the sum-of-functions setting.  
- Cite related work: add Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) for hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods with linear convergence (but κ-dependent rates).  
- Proof clarity: add high-level commentary/motivation for key steps (e.g., role of epoch averaging) to avoid "magical" derivations.  
- Distributed/parallelization claim: correct Line 224—both SAG and EMGD can use mini-batches for parallelization and reduced memory.  
- Specific manuscript edits: several line-level clarifications and notation fixes (Table 1 reference, SCDA applicability, universal quantification "for all w", clarify ∇F(w)=E[∇f(w], substitute steps, parentheses around summations, justify δ condition in (4)).
