Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth, strongly convex optimization that blends one full gradient per epoch with multiple stochastic gradients. Each epoch centers at the averaged iterate from the previous epoch, computes the full gradient at that center, and then performs T projected updates using “mixed” gradients that replace the stochastic gradient’s value at the center with the full gradient at the center, thereby reducing variance. The algorithm also shrinks the trust region around the center across epochs. The main theorem establishes linear convergence: to reach ε accuracy, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, where κ = L/λ is the condition number.

This work lies between full-gradient methods (e.g., Nesterov’s accelerated methods with O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients) and semi-/incremental methods such as SAG and SDCA, which achieve linear convergence in finite-sum settings. Compared to SAG/SDCA, EMGD works with constrained domains, uses O(d) memory, and provides high-probability (rather than in-expectation) guarantees.

Quality:
The main convergence proof appears sound and, to my knowledge, novel in its particular mixed-gradient plus shrinking-constraint analysis. The high-probability analysis and the decomposition around the epoch center are interesting and technically careful. I did find several minor mistakes and clarity issues (listed below) that should be fixed.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear, with a well-structured algorithm and proof sketch. Some notation and assumptions could be stated more explicitly (e.g., the precise definition of the condition number, the scaling of L and λ depending on whether F is a sum or an average). A few steps in the proof would benefit from clearer references and corrected notation.

Originality:
While hybrid/semi-stochastic schemes exist, the specific mixed-gradient construction combined with a shrinking ball constraint and the resulting O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient complexity are, to the best of my understanding, original. The high-probability linear-rate statement also strengthens the result relative to in-expectation rates common in related work.

Significance and practical relevance:
My main concern is the practical regime in typical ML problems. In many ML settings, strong convexity comes from the regularizer; depending on whether one optimizes the sum or the average of losses, the effective strong convexity parameter scales differently. Under the assumption (made explicitly) that the strong convexity parameter grows with the number of samples N (e.g., λ ≍ C N when the objective is a sum), the proposed method improves over accelerated full-gradient methods only in a relatively small-N regime (roughly N ≲ O(C^3/L^3) under the stated constants), which may be of limited practical interest. Moreover, the rate for accelerated full-gradient methods is deterministic, whereas EMGD’s is high-probability. The paper should carefully articulate the regimes (sum vs average objective), how L and λ scale with N in those regimes, and provide empirical or analytical evidence that EMGD offers a concrete wall-clock advantage over accelerated full-gradient methods in realistic ML settings.

Pros:
- Reduces full-gradient calls from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ.
- High-probability linear convergence proof with a clear mixed-gradient mechanism.
- Handles constrained optimization and uses low memory.
- Conceptually simple and potentially parallelizable for the full-gradient step.

Cons:
- Requires O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients; the κ^2 factor may be large in ill-conditioned problems.
- Assumes knowledge (or tuning) of κ; practical guidance is limited.
- Advantage over accelerated full-gradient methods in common ML regimes is unclear without precise scaling of L and λ and careful cost modeling.
- High-probability rate vs deterministic rate for full-gradient methods may complicate fair comparisons.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper:
- Clearly specify whether F is a sum or an average and how L and λ scale with N in both cases; then revisit the complexity comparisons under both scalings.
- Provide empirical comparisons (wall-clock and gradient calls) against Nesterov’s accelerated methods, SAG, and SDCA across a range of κ and N, including constrained settings where EMGD has a stated advantage.
- Discuss the κ^2 dependence in the number of stochastic gradients and whether adaptive choices of T or variance-reduction tricks could reduce this to O(κ).
- Explain the practical procedure for estimating κ (or provide a κ-free schedule) and assess robustness to mis-specification.
- Emphasize cases where constrained projections are cheap and SAG/SDCA are less applicable.

Minor comments and corrections:
- Equation (6): should read ||w* − ŵ||^2 (norm squared), not as currently written.
- Line 288: specify explicitly to which function you apply inequality (7).
- Line 286: the displayed equality appears unnecessary and obscures the flow; consider removing it.
- In (13), use norms (||·||) rather than absolute values for vector quantities.
- Please state explicitly that L ≥ λ (implied by smoothness and strong convexity), and define κ = L/λ ≥ 1.
- In (4), x^ should be ŵ (or ŵ), and f should be F. Also, by Lemma 1, the first term in the max dominates the second.
- Define the condition number precisely on first use as κ = L/λ and maintain consistent notation (λ for strong convexity, L for smoothness).
- Proof references: when invoking Jensen’s inequality and Hoeffding–Azuma, add brief justifications for the martingale and bounded-difference conditions.

Overall assessment:
A theoretically interesting hybrid method with a clean reduction in the number of full-gradient evaluations and a solid high-probability analysis. However, the paper should more convincingly establish when EMGD yields practical advantages over accelerated full-gradient methods in common ML regimes and address the κ^2 dependence and parameter knowledge. Pending a clearer discussion and empirical evidence of practical benefits, I am on the fence regarding significance for the NIPS ML audience.
