This paper proposes a hybrid first-order method that combines queries to a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). The central theoretical claim is that, under a known condition number, an appropriate mixture of deterministic and stochastic steps yields a high-probability O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization, effectively removing the dependence on the condition number from the rate. This is an interesting and striking result.

However, the current presentation has several shortcomings.

Major concerns
1) Reliance on a known condition number and lack of robustness analysis. The main theory assumes the condition number kappa is known a priori (i.e., both the strong convexity parameter lambda and the smoothness/Lipschitz gradient constant L are known), and the algorithm EGD depends on a hyperparameter eta that effectively requires knowledge of kappa = L/lambda. The paper provides no guidance on how to set or estimate kappa, nor any sensitivity analysis when kappa is misspecified. At a minimum, the paper should:
- Offer a principled or heuristic procedure to estimate kappa (or eta) and discuss its cost.
- Analyze or empirically evaluate the impact of overestimating or underestimating kappa on convergence (e.g., what happens when eta is too large or too small).
- Clarify whether the claimed kappa-free rate remains stable under moderate misspecification.

2) No experimental validation. Given that the contribution is an algorithm, empirical results are essential. Experiments could:
- Validate the theoretical rates and illustrate practical gains from mixing deterministic and stochastic steps.
- Study robustness to kappa misspecification and the mix ratio between full and stochastic gradients.
- Compare against strong baselines: vanilla SGD, averaged SGD, and recent variance-reduced or randomized finite-sum methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA; SVRG/SAGA would also be appropriate).
- Report both optimization error versus number of stochastic and full-gradient oracle calls and versus wall-clock time on standard finite-sum datasets.

3) Unclear optimization setting. The paper does not clearly state whether it targets:
- A deterministic finite-sum problem: minimize F(w) = (1/n) sum Fi(w), solved with a randomized algorithm; or
- A stochastic approximation problem: minimize E[F(w)] under a sampling oracle.
Section 3.1 is ambiguous, and later claims do not resolve the ambiguity. Since SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for the finite-sum setting, a precise statement of the problem class, oracle model, and performance metrics (e.g., counts of stochastic vs. full gradient queries) is necessary.

4) Relation to prior hybrid methods. The paper omits discussion of hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization, notably Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012), which surveys and develops related methods. The authors should compare assumptions, oracle complexity, and convergence guarantees with this and earlier hybrid approaches, and clarify conceptual differences between their “mixed gradient” step and an explicit alternation of deterministic and stochastic steps, especially in the unconstrained case.

5) Restrictive assumptions. The analysis focuses on smooth, strongly convex objectives. While common in theory and relevant in practice, it is important to discuss extensions to merely convex (non-strongly convex) settings, which arise frequently. Bach and Moulines (2011) analyze differing behaviors in convex vs. strongly convex regimes; a discussion or partial extension would strengthen the work.

Detailed comments
- Algorithm specification and hyperparameters: EGD’s update (Eq. 8) uses a mixed gradient (Eq. 7) and requires setting eta. As written, choosing eta effectively requires knowing kappa (with L the smoothness constant, not “strong convexity modulus,” as the draft states). Please correct the terminology (“condition number,” not “conditional number”) and clearly define L and lambda. Provide either a data-driven procedure to set eta or a theoretical rationale for default choices. If the point is primarily theoretical, include analysis of convergence when eta (or kappa) is misspecified.

- Mixed versus interleaved updates: While the algorithm is expressed as a single mixed update per iteration (within an epoch), a natural baseline is to interleave full-gradient and stochastic-gradient steps at different frequencies. A discussion of the relationship between these two designs—both conceptually and in terms of oracle complexity and rates—would be helpful. If possible, include a comparison or an argument explaining why the mixed update is preferable.

- Clarify the oracle and cost model: Since the algorithm uses both full and stochastic gradients, the rate statement should specify how many of each are required to reach epsilon accuracy. For fair comparison to existing methods, the paper should distinguish between iteration complexity, stochastic gradient calls, and full-gradient calls.

- High-probability guarantees: The abstract and theorems should consistently state whether guarantees are in expectation or with high probability, and under what variance assumptions on the stochastic oracle.

- Broader scope: If extending the analysis to the non-strongly convex case is technically challenging, at least provide a discussion of expected behavior and whether restarting, regularization, or adaptive schedules could retain some of the benefits.

Suggestions for an experimental section
- Benchmarks: Standard finite-sum problems (e.g., logistic regression, ridge regression) across multiple datasets.
- Baselines: SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SDCA, and possibly SVRG/SAGA.
- Metrics: Optimization error vs. number of stochastic and full-gradient queries; wall-clock time; sensitivity to eta and to misestimation of kappa; effect of different mixing schedules.
- Ablations: Vary the frequency of full-gradient steps and the construction of the mixed gradient.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a compelling theoretical idea: using a hybrid oracle to obtain logarithmic convergence without explicit dependence on the condition number, provided kappa is known. To be suitable for publication, it should (i) clarify the problem setting and oracle model, (ii) relate the method to prior hybrid work, (iii) address the dependence on a known kappa—either by providing estimation procedures or by analyzing robustness to misspecification, and (iv) include an empirical evaluation that supports the theory and demonstrates practical benefits.
