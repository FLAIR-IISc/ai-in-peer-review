SUMMARY: The paper studies optimization of a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex set, assuming the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. Nesterov’s optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence here, but its constant depends on the square root of the condition number k. The authors consider a setting with access to both an expensive full gradient and a cheap stochastic gradient oracle. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that uses only O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full gradient (independent of k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle. When k is not too large, this can be faster in theory.

The key idea is to replace each full-gradient step (an epoch) by O(k^2) mixed-gradient steps that combine a single full gradient (computed once per epoch) with varying stochastic gradients within the epoch. Averaging the O(k^2) iterates yields a constant decrease in suboptimality independent of k, so the number of epochs (full gradients) is k-independent. The paper provides a simple, self-contained convergence proof, but no experiments.

I enjoyed the paper. Mixing a few full gradients with many cheap stochastic steps is novel and interesting. This work follows a line of recent methods that exploit cheap stochastic oracles while maintaining linear rates. A notable theoretical and practical breakthrough is SAG [16], which applies when the objective is a sum of n smooth functions (e.g., regularized empirical risk), in which case a full gradient costs roughly n times a stochastic one. SAG is then theoretically faster than Nesterov when k ≤ n/8. In contrast, in that setting EMGD is faster than Nesterov when k ≤ n^(2/3), but EMGD is slower than SAG in all regimes (it matches SAG’s big-O only when k ≤ n^(1/2)). For intuition: if k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD achieve O(n log(1/ε)) while Nesterov requires O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)).

Two main advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) EMGD handles constrained optimization (assuming the gradient mapping update is efficient), whereas SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems; and (2) EMGD’s proof is much simpler and may yield more insight and easier modifications with provable guarantees. The result is also stronger in that it holds with high probability, versus in expectation for SAG. The purported memory/parallelization advantage is less clear, since SAG can be parallelized with mini-batches, which also reduce memory.

EVALUATION SUMMARY:
Pros:
- A novel and appealing algorithmic idea with clean, simple, and solid theory.
- Like SAG, achieves a linear rate for regularized empirical loss without a factor of k multiplying n in the constant; yet it is more general (handles constraints) and the proof is much simpler.
- Clear writing with a self-contained proof.

Cons:
- No experiments to demonstrate practical impact (nor concrete examples illustrating when the theoretical advantages are practically relevant).
- Limited discussion of limitations/drawbacks, especially relative to existing algorithms (Section 3.4 should be strengthened; see suggestions below).
- The proof would benefit from high-level commentary explaining the key insights and how the pieces fit together.

QUALITY: Technically sound. Experiments would help, but the theoretical contribution can stand alone. Section 3.4 should be expanded to discuss limitations and drawbacks. Please also add a concrete discussion of the “sum of n functions” setting to highlight differences with SAG and Nesterov. For example: EMGD is worse than Nesterov for roughly k > n^(2/3); SAG matches EMGD for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2); SAG is better than EMGD (and Nesterov) for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8; and SAG is worse than Nesterov for k > n/8. A major practical drawback of EMGD is that the number of steps per epoch must be fixed in advance (requiring knowledge of k). By contrast, both SDCA [17] and SAG do not fix the number of steps a priori and can benefit from faster practical convergence than the bound predicts (or from a better local condition number). Moreover, SDCA has automatic step-size selection, and SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic that works well in practice. Please add this discussion.

ADDENDUM (after discussion with other reviewers):
a) In many ML applications the regularization parameter is C/n, so the condition number is ≈ n/C′. In this regime SAG still outperforms Nesterov, but EMGD does not; this should be pointed out (and perhaps another practical setting where k < n^(2/3) should be mentioned).
b) Please cite: Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting (Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012), which also proposes a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method with a linear rate. That rate still depends on the condition number, so the ultimate rate is not better than standard gradient descent (only the early phase benefits from cheaper steps). The current submission can still improve on this when k is not too large relative to n.

CLARITY: The paper is fairly clear. I appreciated the summary in Table 1; I have not seen such a clear presentation elsewhere. The proof is tight and readable, but would be more helpful with a few high-level explanations motivating the defined quantities, which otherwise fit together a bit “magically.”

ORIGINALITY: A novel combination of known techniques.

SIGNIFICANCE: Practical relevance is unproven, but the theoretical contribution could be impactful. Given SAG’s difficult proof (and SDCA’s simple proof in a more restricted setting), EMGD’s simple proof is a major contribution.
