## Review of NIPS_2013-2017/2013 Submission

**Quality:** 7/10
**Clarity:** 8/10
**Originality:** 8/10
**Significance:** 7/10

**SUMMARY:** The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where gradient computations are efficient. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence, its convergence rate’s constant depends on the square root of the condition number *k*. This paper proposes a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), leveraging both an expensive full gradient and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. EMGD requires O(log 1/ε) full gradient calls (independent of *k*) and O(*k*<sup>2</sup> log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient calls. The key idea is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with *O*(*k*<sup>2</sup>) mixed gradient steps, combining the full gradient (computed once per epoch) with stochastic gradients. Averaging iterates within an epoch yields a constant decrease in suboptimality independent of *k*, resulting in a full gradient step count independent of the condition number. The authors provide a simple and self-contained convergence proof, but do not include experimental validation.

I found this paper enjoyable to read and believe the idea of mixing full and stochastic gradient computations is novel and interesting. This work fits within a growing body of research aiming to exploit cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence. A recent theoretical and practical breakthrough is the SAG algorithm [16] for smooth, strongly convex functions that are a sum of *n* simpler functions (common in regularized empirical loss minimization). SAG is faster than Nesterov’s algorithm when *k* ≤ *n*/8, assuming the full gradient is *n* times more expensive than the stochastic gradient. EMGD, in contrast, is faster than Nesterov’s algorithm when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>2/3</sup>, but slower than SAG across all regimes (having the same big-O complexity when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>). Considering an example with *k* = *n*<sup>1/2</sup>, both SAG and EMGD achieve O(*n* log(1/ε)), while Nesterov’s algorithm requires O(*n*<sup>5/4</sup> log(1/ε)). EMGD’s strengths over SAG include its applicability to constrained optimization (given an efficient gradient mapping update) and its simpler convergence proof, potentially yielding further insights and facilitating provable modifications. The authors also mention potential memory/parallelization advantages, though SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, reducing memory requirements.

**EVALUATION SUMMARY:**

**Pros:**

*   Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theoretical foundation.
*   Like SAG, achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization where the condition number *k* does not scale with the number of training examples *n*; however, the algorithm is more general (handles constrained optimization) and boasts a significantly simpler proof.
*   The paper is well-written and the proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**

*   The lack of experimental results hinders demonstrating the algorithm's practical relevance. No concrete examples illustrate why the suggested theoretical advantages might be beneficial in practice.
*   Section 3.4 lacks a thorough discussion of the algorithm's limitations and drawbacks, particularly in comparison to existing methods.
*   The proof would benefit from higher-level comments justifying key insights driving its construction.

**QUALITY:** The paper is technically sound. While experiments would be valuable, the theoretical contribution is substantial. However, the authors should expand Section 3.4 to address the algorithm's limitations. A more detailed discussion of the *n*-function example highlighting the differences between EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov is also needed. Specifically, a discussion of the regimes where each algorithm outperforms the others—e.g., EMGD is worse than Nesterov for *k* > *n*<sup>2/3</sup>; SAG is comparable to EMGD for 1 ≤ *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>; SAG outperforms EMGD (and Nesterov) for *n*<sup>1/2</sup> < *k* ≤ *n*/8; and Nesterov surpasses both for *k* > *n*/8. A practical drawback of EMGD is the need to fix the number of steps within an epoch based on prior knowledge of *k*. Algorithms like SCDA [17] and SAG don’t require this fixed step size, potentially leading to faster practical convergence or benefiting from local condition number estimations. Furthermore, SCDA incorporates automatic step-size selection, and SAG utilizes an adaptive step-size heuristic that performs well in practice. This should be discussed.

**ADDENDUM (following discussion with other reviewers):** a) Often in machine learning, the regularization parameter is *C*/n, resulting in a condition number of approximately *n*/C. In this regime, SAG still often outperforms Nesterov, while EMGD may not. This should be highlighted, along with an additional practical scenario where *k* < *n*<sup>2/3</sup>. b) The authors should cite [Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC, 2012] which presents a similar hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm, though its convergence rate still depends on the condition number, unlike the current submission's potential for faster rates when *k* is relatively small.

**CLARITY:** The paper is generally clear. Table 1’s summary of convergence rates is particularly commendable. The proof is rigorously presented but would be more insightful with higher-level commentary motivating the defined quantities and their interactions.

**ORIGINALITY:** The work offers a novel combination of established techniques.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The practical relevance remains unproven. However, the theoretical contribution is impactful, particularly considering the complexity of SAG’s convergence proof and the simplicity of EMGD’s proof, which could lead to further insights and easier-to-prove modifications.
