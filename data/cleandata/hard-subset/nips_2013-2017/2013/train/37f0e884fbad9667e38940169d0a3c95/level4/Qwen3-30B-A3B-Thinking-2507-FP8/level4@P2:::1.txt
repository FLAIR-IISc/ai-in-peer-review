

**Review for "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"**

**Summary:** This paper addresses the problem of optimizing smooth and strongly convex functions where the full gradient is computationally expensive to evaluate. The authors propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve a condition number-independent number of full gradient evaluations. The key insight is that by using a fixed number of stochastic gradient steps per epoch (with one full gradient evaluation per epoch), EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, compared to the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) for full gradient methods. This work builds on recent advances in stochastic optimization (SAG, SDCA) but offers a more general framework applicable to constrained problems and with a simpler convergence proof.

**Strengths:**
- **Originality:** The paper presents a novel hybrid approach that effectively combines full and stochastic gradients. While related to SAG/SDCA, EMGD extends these methods to constrained optimization and offers a significantly simpler convergence proof.
- **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound, complete, and self-contained. The proof of linear convergence with high probability is well-structured and correct.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-written with clear exposition, helpful table summarizing convergence rates, and logical organization. The algorithm description is precise and accessible.
- **Significance:** The theoretical contribution is meaningful, particularly for constrained optimization problems where SAG/SDCA cannot be applied. The simpler proof could facilitate future algorithmic extensions.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Lack of experiments:** The paper lacks empirical validation, which would have strengthened the practical relevance claim. A comparison of actual runtime on real-world datasets would have demonstrated whether the theoretical advantages translate to practice.
- **Incomplete discussion of limitations:** Section 3.4 needs significant expansion to compare EMGD's practical limitations against SAG/SDCA. Specifically:
  - The requirement to know κ in advance is a major practical drawback, as practitioners typically don't know this parameter.
  - The paper should explicitly state that EMGD is theoretically superior to Nesterov's method only when κ ≤ n^(2/3), while SAG is superior when κ ≤ n/8.
  - The claim that SAG cannot be parallelized is incorrect; both SAG and EMGD can use mini-batches for distributed implementation.
- **Missing high-level insights:** The proof would benefit from more intuitive explanations of why certain quantities were chosen (e.g., the specific form of the mixed gradient).

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
- Add a concrete example showing when κ ≤ n^(2/3) in practice (e.g., with specific regularization parameters in machine learning problems).
- Clarify the relationship between EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov's method in a table showing computational complexity under different κ regimes.
- Correct the incorrect statement that SAG cannot use distributed computing (mini-batches enable parallelization for both methods).
- Add a brief discussion of the practical challenge of knowing κ in advance, and suggest potential workarounds (e.g., using a conservative estimate based on early iterations).
- In the proof, explicitly state that the condition number κ = L/λ appears in the convergence rate but is now independent of the number of full gradient evaluations.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with a novel algorithmic approach that extends the applicability of linear convergence methods to constrained optimization problems. While the theoretical results are significant, the lack of empirical validation and incomplete discussion of practical limitations reduce the paper's impact. The simpler convergence proof compared to SAG/SDCA is a notable advantage for future theoretical extensions.

**Rating:**
Quality: 8/10 (Theoretical contribution is strong, but missing experimental validation)
Clarity: 9/10 (Well-written with clear exposition)
Originality: 8/10 (Novel combination of techniques with meaningful extension)
Significance: 7/10 (Theoretical contribution is valuable, but practical relevance not demonstrated)

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of hybrid optimization methods. With the suggested revisions (particularly adding a more complete comparison with existing methods and addressing the practical limitations), this would be a strong contribution to NIPS. The theoretical novelty and clarity of the presentation outweigh the current lack of experimental results.
