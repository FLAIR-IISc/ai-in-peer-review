- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): hybrid using infrequent full gradients + many stochastic gradients achieving O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.  
- EMGD attains linear convergence with the number of full-gradient calls independent of the condition number k.  
- Algorithm naturally handles constrained optimization (with efficient gradient mapping), unlike SAG which is for unconstrained problems.  
- Analysis is simple, self-contained, and provides high-probability guarantees; proof is substantially simpler than SAG’s.  
- No experiments provided; empirical validation of practical benefits is missing.  
- Limited discussion of limitations and trade-offs; Section 3.4 needs expansion.  
- Proof would benefit from higher-level intuition for key constructions and parameter choices (e.g., epoch length O(k^2), importance of averaging, variance stabilization).  
- In the sum-of-n-functions setting: SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov when k ≤ n/8; EMGD improves over Nesterov when k ≤ n^(2/3) but is generally slower than SAG (matches SAG when k ≤ n^(1/2)).  
- Two practical advantages over SAG: handles constraints and has simpler, potentially more extensible analysis with stronger high-probability bounds.  
- Missing citation: Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC, 2012) on hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods.  
- Sensitivity to k: EMGD requires fixing epoch length based on k, unlike SCDA and SAG which are more adaptive.  
- Step-size selection and robustness of EMGD are not addressed; SCDA and SAG offer more practical adaptivity/heuristics.  
- Practical regimes (regularization ≈ C/n implying k ≈ n/C′) often favor SAG; authors should identify realistic regimes where k ≤ n^(2/3) holds.  
- Parallelization/memory claims need clarification; SAG/SCDA can leverage mini-batches and distributed computing—current statement is inaccurate.  
- Paper is technically sound; adding experiments would substantially strengthen practical impact.  
- Exposition is clear and Table 1 is helpful for comparing rates.  
- The algorithmic idea of mixing infrequent full gradients with many stochastic updates is novel and well motivated.  
- Detailed suggested additions: contrast EMGD with SAG/Nesterov, discuss need to know k, step-size/robustness, parallelization/memory trade-offs, and add the Friedlander & Schmidt citation.  
- Numerous minor line edits, notation fixes, and typos are listed for correction.
