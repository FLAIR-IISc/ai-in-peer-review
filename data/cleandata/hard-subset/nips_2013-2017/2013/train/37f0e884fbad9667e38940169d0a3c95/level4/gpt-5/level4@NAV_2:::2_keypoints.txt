- Proposes EMGD, a hybrid deterministic–stochastic first-order method that mixes one full gradient per epoch with many stochastic gradients.  
- Key idea: “mixed” gradient steps eliminate condition-number dependence in the count of full-gradient evaluations while achieving linear O(log(1/ε)) convergence with high probability.  
- Under smoothness and strong convexity, achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and more stochastic-gradient calls, offering a theoretical gain when full gradients are costly.  
- Central theoretical contribution is clear and appealing, complementing prior accelerated full-gradient and finite-sum stochastic methods.  
- Analysis requires exact knowledge of the condition number κ = L/λ (i.e., L and λ must be known) to set the stepsize η.  
- No analysis of robustness to misspecification of κ; convergence when κ is set too large or too small is not characterized.  
- The paper contains no experiments to validate theory or probe practical behavior and sensitivity to κ misspecification.  
- If κ must be estimated, the paper provides no estimation procedure or heuristic and offers no empirical support for such strategies.  
- Restriction to smooth and strongly convex objectives is limiting; extension or discussion for merely smooth convex (non-strongly convex) cases is missing.  
- The mixed-update idea is related to simple alternation between deterministic and stochastic steps; the paper should clarify advantages of the mixed form versus explicit interleaving.  
- Important related work on hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012) is omitted and should be discussed and compared.  
- A thorough experimental section with baselines (SGD, averaged/Polyak–Ruppert SGD, SAG, SDCA) and large-scale finite-sum tasks is strongly recommended.  
- The problem setting is not stated crisply: it’s unclear whether results target finite-sum empirical risk minimization or stochastic expectation formulations; the oracle model should be clarified.  
- Overall: an engaging theoretical result, but weakened by reliance on known κ and lack of experiments; robustness analysis or empirical studies are needed to strengthen the contribution.
