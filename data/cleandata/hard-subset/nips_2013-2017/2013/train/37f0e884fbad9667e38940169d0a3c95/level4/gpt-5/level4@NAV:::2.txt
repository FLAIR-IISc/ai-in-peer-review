The paper proposes a “hybrid” deterministic–stochastic first-order optimization method. The algorithm interleaves access to a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). The authors claim that this yields convergence rates that remove the dependence on the condition number.

The theoretical result is intriguing and notable. In essence, if the condition number is known in advance (i.e., the strong convexity and smoothness constants of the objective are given) and a hybrid first-order oracle (able to return both stochastic and full gradients) is available, then by choosing an appropriate mixture of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps one can obtain, with high probability, an O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization.

That said, there are several issues with the current version. First, the analysis only treats the case where the condition number is exactly known and does not address what happens when this hyper-parameter is misspecified. In effect, the result shows that the dependence on the condition number kappa can be eliminated from the rate only when kappa is provided to the algorithm ahead of time. If extending the analysis to the unknown-kappa setting is difficult, experiments could have filled the gap. However, the paper contains no experimental results, which is the second major concern. Since the main contribution is a new algorithm, at least some empirical evaluation seems essential to validate the theory and to assess how it behaves on real data.

Detailed comments
The proposed algorithm (EGD) uses the update in Eq. 8 (page 4) based on the “mixed gradient” from Eq. 7. Consequently, EGD requires setting the hyper-parameter eta. Choosing eta essentially amounts to knowing the condition number kappa (“conditional number” in the paper), namely both the “strong convexity modulus” lambda and the “smoothness modulus” L. As far as I can tell, the paper gives no guidance or theoretical principle for selecting kappa beforehand.

This leaves two options. Either kappa must be estimated—in which case the estimation procedure is missing (a heuristic would be acceptable if supported by numerical evidence)—or kappa is assumed known because the contribution is primarily theoretical. If the latter, then the theory and/or experiments should address misspecification, analyzing the convergence when this hyper-parameter is set too large or too small.

While smooth and strongly convex assumptions are standard and often realistic, they can be restrictive; other regimes (e.g., non-strongly convex) are important as well. The non-strongly convex case arises frequently and merits discussion. See Bach & Moulines (2011) for a theoretical treatment of the differing behaviors in the convex versus strongly convex settings.

Additional concerns. Combining full-gradient and stochastic-gradient steps in a first-order method is not new. Moreover, the proposed algorithm is not presented as an alternation of deterministic and stochastic updates with separate frequencies; instead, it uses a per-iteration “mixed” update within an epoch followed by a gradient-like step. It would be useful to relate and compare the method to an approach that explicitly interleaves deterministic and stochastic steps (at least in the unconstrained case).

The paper omits a relevant body of work on hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods; see: Hybrid Deterministic–Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting (M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt, SISC, 2012). A discussion and comparison of convergence rates would be valuable, along with the overview of earlier work provided in that reference.

Finally, a comprehensive experimental section would strengthen the paper, including comparisons against standard SGD, averaged SGD, and recent stochastic first-order methods (SAG, etc.).

A minor point: the optimization setting is not clearly specified. The stated goal is to combine benefits from deterministic and stochastic optimization—namely, exponential convergence from the deterministic side and the condition-number behavior from the stochastic side. It is unclear whether the target is the finite-sum deterministic problem [min_w F(w) = (1/n) sum_i F_i(w)] solved by a randomized algorithm, or the stochastic approximation problem [min_w E(F(w))]. Note that SAG and SDCA are randomized methods for the finite-sum setting. The formal setup in Section 3.1 is ambiguous in this respect, and subsequent claims do not resolve the ambiguity. This should be straightforward to clarify.

The authors present an interesting and striking theoretical result. In short, if the condition number is known (i.e., the strong convexity and smoothness constants are available) and a hybrid first-order oracle can be queried, then with an appropriately tuned mixture of deterministic and stochastic steps one attains, with high probability, an O(log(1/epsilon)) rate for batch optimization. However, the current analysis only treats the perfectly known condition number; it does not discuss the algorithm’s behavior under hyper-parameter misspecification. In effect, the result shows that a first-order method’s rate can drop the kappa dependence when kappa is known a priori. If extending the theory to unknown kappa is difficult, experiments should have been used to explore this case. Yet the paper contains no experiments, which is a major shortcoming. Given that the main contribution is a new algorithm, it is important to include empirical studies to corroborate the theory and to assess practical relevance.
