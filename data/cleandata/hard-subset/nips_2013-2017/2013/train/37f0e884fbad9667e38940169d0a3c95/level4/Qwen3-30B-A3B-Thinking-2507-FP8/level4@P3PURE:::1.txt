SUMMARY: The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex constraint set where the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. While Nesterov's optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence with a constant depending on the square root of the condition number κ, the authors consider a setting where an expensive full gradient oracle is available alongside a cheap stochastic gradient oracle. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle, provided κ is not excessively large. The key innovation is replacing each full gradient step (an "epoch") with a fixed number O(κ²) of mixed gradient steps that combine a single full gradient computation (for the epoch) with varying stochastic gradients. By averaging the O(κ²) iterates within an epoch, they achieve a suboptimality decrease independent of κ, enabling the epoch count (full gradient calls) to be κ-independent. The authors provide a simple, self-contained proof of convergence but omit experiments.  

I enjoyed this paper and find the idea of mixing a few full gradient computations with numerous cheap stochastic steps novel and compelling. The work fits within recent efforts to leverage stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence. A notable breakthrough is SAG [16], which achieves linear convergence for smooth, strongly convex objectives that are sums of n simple functions (e.g., regularized empirical loss minimization), assuming the full gradient oracle is n times more expensive than the stochastic one. SAG outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n/8, while EMGD does so when κ ≤ n^(2/3). EMGD is slower than SAG for all regimes but shares the same asymptotic complexity when κ ≤ n^(1/2). For example, when κ = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD achieve O(n log(1/ε)), while Nesterov requires O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)). Two key advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) EMGD handles constrained optimization (assuming efficient gradient mapping updates), whereas SAG is limited to unconstrained problems; (2) EMGD’s proof is significantly simpler than SAG’s, offering clearer insights and stronger high-probability guarantees (vs. SAG’s expectation-based analysis). The authors also note a potential memory/parallelization advantage, though this is less compelling since SAG can also leverage mini-batches for parallelization and reduced memory.  

EVALUATION SUMMARY:  
Pros:  
- Presents a novel, interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and rigorous theoretical foundation.  
- Achieves linear convergence for regularized empirical loss minimization (like SAG) without the condition number κ multiplying the number of examples n in the constant, while being more general (handles constraints) and featuring a simpler proof than SAG.  
- The paper is clearly written, with a self-contained proof and a helpful Table 1 summarizing rates.  

Cons:  
- Lacks experiments to demonstrate practical relevance or illustrate when the theoretical advantages manifest.  
- Omits a thorough discussion of limitations and drawbacks compared to existing methods (Section 3.4 requires significant improvement).  
- The proof lacks high-level commentary to motivate key insights, making some steps appear "magical."  

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. While experiments would strengthen the contribution, the theoretical advance stands on its own. The authors must substantially expand Section 3.4 to address limitations, including:  
- EMGD’s requirement to fix the epoch size (O(κ²)) in advance—unlike SCDA [17] and SAG, which adaptively choose step counts and benefit from faster local convergence or adaptive step sizes.  
- A concrete comparison for the sum-of-functions setting: EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3), matches SAG for 1 ≤ κ ≤ n^(1/2), and is outperformed by SAG for n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8, while being worse than Nesterov for κ > n/8.  
- A practical note: In machine learning, regularization parameters often yield κ ≈ n/C' (where C' is a constant), a regime where SAG still dominates EMGD. The authors should cite a specific practical scenario where κ < n^(2/3).  

[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers:]  
a) Emphasize that in typical ML settings (κ ≈ n/C'), SAG remains superior to EMGD.  
b) Cite Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) for a related hybrid deterministic-stochastic method with linear convergence, though their rate still depends on κ (unlike EMGD’s κ-independent full gradient complexity for moderate κ).  

CLARITY: The paper is well-written. Table 1 effectively summarizes rates, and the proof is logically structured. However, high-level motivations for key definitions (e.g., the role of the epoch average) would enhance readability.  

ORIGINALITY: The algorithm combines known techniques in a novel way.  

SIGNIFICANCE: The theoretical contribution is significant, particularly given the complexity of SAG’s proof. However, the practical relevance remains unproven without experiments or concrete examples.
