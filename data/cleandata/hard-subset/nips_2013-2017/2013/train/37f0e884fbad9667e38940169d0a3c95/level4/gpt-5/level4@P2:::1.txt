Summary:
The paper studies first-order optimization for smooth, strongly convex objectives over a convex set where gradient mappings are efficient. While Nesterov’s accelerated method achieves a linear rate with a constant scaling as O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients, this can be costly when κ is large. The authors assume access to both an expensive full-gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic function (hence stochastic gradient) oracle, and propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD). EMGD computes one full gradient per epoch and then performs a fixed number O(κ^2) of “mixed” updates that combine the epoch’s full gradient with per-iteration stochastic gradients, within a shrinking ball around the epoch point. Averaging the iterates yields a constant reduction in error per epoch, so EMGD reaches ε-accuracy with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The analysis is self-contained and yields high-probability linear convergence. No experiments are provided.

Relation to prior work:
- Full-gradient methods: For smooth, strongly convex problems, accelerated gradient methods achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients (Nesterov). EMGD reduces the number of full gradients to O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ, by replacing most full gradients with stochastic gradients.
- Stochastic methods: Classical SGD has polynomial dependence on 1/ε; recent strongly convex variants achieve O(1/(λ ε)) but still lack linear rates. Semi-stochastic schemes improve constants but typically retain κ in the rate.
- Finite-sum methods: SAG (RouX–Schmidt–Bach) achieves O(n log(1/ε)) under n ≥ 8κ, and SDCA achieves O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) in its setting. Assuming a full gradient costs n times a stochastic gradient, EMGD is faster than Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3), comparable to SAG/SDCA when κ ≤ n^(1/2), and slower otherwise. Unlike SAG/SDCA, EMGD applies to constrained problems and uses O(d) memory, and its rate holds with high probability (vs. in expectation for SAG). Please also cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), which presents a hybrid deterministic–stochastic scheme with linear convergence but rate constants that still depend on κ.

Evaluation summary:
Pros:
- A clear, simple algorithmic idea with a clean and solid analysis.
- Linear convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients; high-probability guarantees.
- Broader applicability than SAG/SDCA: works for constrained problems (given efficient gradient mapping) and requires only O(d) memory.
- Writing is generally clear; the proof is self-contained.

Cons:
- No experiments demonstrating practical gains or illustrating regimes where the theoretical advantages matter.
- Limited discussion of limitations and trade-offs versus existing methods; Section 3.4 should be expanded.
- Requires prior knowledge (or tuning) of κ to set the epoch length (O(κ^2)), whereas SDCA and SAG can adapt better in practice and use automatic/adaptive step sizes.
- Some claims about parallelization/distributed computing are too strong; SAG/SDCA can use mini-batches, which aid both speed and memory.

Quality:
Technically sound, with a transparent and instructive proof. Experiments would strengthen the paper but are not essential if the discussion of applicability and limitations is expanded. Please elaborate on the finite-sum case and provide a more concrete comparison with Nesterov, SAG, and SDCA. For example: EMGD is worse than Nesterov when κ ≳ n^(2/3); EMGD and SAG have the same O(n log(1/ε)) cost when κ ≤ n^(1/2); SAG is better than EMGD (and Nesterov) for n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8; SAG becomes worse than Nesterov for κ > n/8. Also note a practical regime in ML where regularization scales as 1/n, giving κ ≈ n/C′; SAG can still beat Nesterov there, whereas EMGD may not.

Clarity:
Overall clear. The high-level motivation in the proof could be expanded (e.g., why the mixed gradient and shrinking domain control the stochastic part). Table 1 is helpful; please ensure all rates are cited in the Related Work section.

Originality:
A novel and appealing combination of known components (epochs, mixed gradients, shrinking domains) that yields a simpler analysis than SAG/SDCA and extends to constrained problems.

Significance:
Theoretical contribution is meaningful, especially given the simplicity and generality. Practical impact remains to be shown.

Detailed suggestions:
- Expand Section 3.4 to discuss limitations: dependence on κ, fixed epoch length, sensitivity to step size, and when EMGD loses to Nesterov/SAG/SDCA. Clarify memory and parallelization: SAG/SDCA can use mini-batches; EMGD’s full-gradient step can be distributed without affecting the rate.
- Add more concrete guidance for the finite-sum setting (cost model, thresholds on κ vs. n).
- Discuss estimating κ in practice or making EMGD adaptive to unknown κ (e.g., doubling tricks, epoch-wise diagnostics).
- Citations: include Friedlander & Schmidt (2012). Clarify that SDCA requires specific structure, while SAG accommodates general smooth convex f_i.
- Proof presentation: add brief intuition for the mixed gradient decomposition and the role of the shrinking ball; make explicit uses of assumptions and indices.

Minor edits and typos:
- “an convex” → “a convex”; use consistent domain notation throughout; “strong convexity; condition number.”
- Explicitly state that ∇F(w) = E[∇f(w)] and that the assumption holds for all w.
- Clarify that SAG/SDCA can exploit mini-batches (affecting both memory and parallelization).
- In derivations, add parentheses where sums involve F(w_t) − F(w*); use norm symbols consistently; use ln for natural logs for consistency; clarify where the δ ≤ e^(−1/2) condition is needed.
