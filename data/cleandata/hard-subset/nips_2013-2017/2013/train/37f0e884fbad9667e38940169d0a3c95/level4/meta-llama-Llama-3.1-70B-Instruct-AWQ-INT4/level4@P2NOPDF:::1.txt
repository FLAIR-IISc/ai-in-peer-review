**Summary**

The authors present a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set. The algorithm leverages both expensive full gradient and cheap stochastic gradient oracles to achieve a linear convergence rate. EMGD requires O(log 1/eps) calls to the full gradient oracle and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle, where k is the condition number. The algorithm's main idea is to replace a full gradient step with a fixed number of mixed gradient steps, combining the full gradient with stochastic gradients. By averaging the iterates within an epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant decrease in suboptimality, independent of the condition number.

**Evaluation**

I enjoyed reading this paper, and the idea of mixing full gradient computations with cheap stochastic gradient steps is novel and interesting. The work contributes to a string of recent papers attempting to utilize cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining a linear convergence rate. A notable example is the SAG algorithm, which achieves a faster theoretical convergence rate than Nesterov's algorithm for certain condition numbers. EMGD, however, has advantages over SAG, including its applicability to constrained optimization and a simpler convergence proof.

**Pros**

* Novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theory.
* Achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization, with a more general algorithm than SAG.
* Clearly written paper with a self-contained proof.

**Cons**

* Lacks experimental section to demonstrate practical relevance.
* No discussion of limitations or drawbacks of the algorithm.
* Proof lacks high-level comments to justify essential insights.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, but experiments would be appreciated. The authors should extend the discussion of limitations and drawbacks, including a more concrete comparison with SAG and Nesterov. A major drawback of EMGD is the need to fix the number of steps within an epoch in advance, which may not be practical.

**Clarity**

The paper is fairly clear, with a useful summary in Table 1. However, the proof could benefit from high-level comments to motivate the defined quantities.

**Originality**

This is a novel combination of known techniques.

**Significance**

The practical relevance of the algorithm is not demonstrated, but the theoretical contribution could have impact. The simple proof of EMGD is a major contribution, especially in the context of difficult proofs for SAG and SDCA.
