SUMMARY: The authors address smooth strongly convex optimization by proposing Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which reduces full gradient dependency on the condition number κ. EMGD leverages both full and stochastic gradients to achieve O(log 1/ε) full gradient calls (independent of κ) and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradient calls. The core innovation replaces each full gradient step with a fixed number O(κ²) of mixed gradient steps (combining one epoch-wise full gradient and epoch-varying stochastic gradients), enabling constant suboptimality reduction per epoch. Theoretical analysis establishes linear convergence with high probability, contrasting with SAG/SDCA (which require κ-dependent storage and converge in expectation). The paper is technically sound, self-contained, and clearly written but lacks empirical validation.  

The work situates itself within recent advances in hybrid optimization (e.g., SAG [22], SDCA [23]), which achieve linear convergence for sum-of-functions objectives F(w) = (1/n)∑ᵢfᵢ(w) under strong convexity. For such problems, EMGD outperforms Nesterov’s method when κ ≤ n²/³ (vs. SAG’s κ ≤ n/8), though it is dominated by SAG for n¹/² < κ ≤ n/8. Key advantages over SAG/SDCA include: (1) applicability to constrained problems (assuming efficient gradient mapping), (2) simpler convergence proof with high-probability guarantees (vs. expectation), and (3) reduced storage (O(d) vs. Ω(n)).  

EVALUATION SUMMARY:  
Pros:  
- Novel algorithmic approach combining full/stochastic gradients with clean, rigorous theory.  
- Generalizes SAG/SDCA to constrained settings while maintaining linear convergence.  
- Proof is self-contained and significantly simpler than SAG’s analysis.  
- Clear exposition with Table 1 effectively summarizing iteration complexities.  

Cons:  
- Missing experiments to demonstrate practical relevance (e.g., convergence speed on real problems).  
- Section 3.4 inadequately compares limitations vs. SAG/SDCA (e.g., EMGD requires prior κ knowledge, unlike adaptive SAG).  
- Overstates SAG/SDCA’s parallelization limitations (mini-batch variants exist; see Friedlander & Schmidt [5]).  
- Fails to discuss regimes where EMGD is impractical (e.g., κ ≈ n²/³ in ML, where regularization often yields κ = Θ(n/C), favoring SAG).  

QUALITY: The technical contribution is sound. The theory is complete and correctly bounds dependencies on κ. The omission of experiments is a drawback but does not undermine the theoretical novelty. **Critical revision needed:** Expand Section 3.4 to explicitly compare EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov across κ regimes (e.g., EMGD dominates Nesterov only for κ ≤ n²/³; SAG dominates EMGD for n¹/² < κ ≤ n/8) and address the requirement for prior κ knowledge. Cite Friedlander & Schmidt [5] as a related hybrid method with linear convergence (though their rate still depends on κ).  

CLARITY: The paper is well-structured and readable. The proof is logically presented but would benefit from high-level intuition for key steps (e.g., the mixed gradient design in Line 7). Table 1 is particularly effective.  

ORIGINALITY: The combination of epoch-based mixed gradients with condition-number-independent full gradient access represents a novel technical synthesis. It builds on EGD [9] but introduces a critical stochastic mixing step to decouple full gradient cost from κ.  

SIGNIFICANCE: The theoretical advance is meaningful for constrained optimization and simplifies convergence analysis. However, the lack of empirical validation and the κ-dependency requirement limit immediate practical impact. The simplicity of the proof may facilitate future extensions, but the paper should clarify when EMGD is preferable to SAG (e.g., for constrained problems with κ ≤ n²/³).
