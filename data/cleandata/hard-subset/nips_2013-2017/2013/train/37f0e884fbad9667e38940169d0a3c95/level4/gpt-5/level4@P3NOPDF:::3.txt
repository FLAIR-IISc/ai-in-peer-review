Summary
The paper proposes a hybrid optimization method that interleaves stochastic and full-batch gradients for strongly convex and smooth objectives. The algorithm achieves ε-accuracy using O(log(1/ε)) evaluations of the full gradient and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. The convergence analysis appears largely correct and contains novel elements, aside from a few minor issues noted below.

Strengths
- Conceptually interesting hybrid strategy that aims to reduce reliance on full gradients while retaining linear convergence.
- Clear theoretical target: reducing the number of expensive full-gradient computations to O(log(1/ε)).
- The proof techniques seem original and (with minor fixes) sound.

Primary concerns and significance for ML practice
My main concern is the practical advantage over standard batch gradient descent in typical ML formulations.

- Scaling of strong convexity: In many ML problems, strong convexity arises from ℓ2 regularization. Depending on whether the objective is defined as a sum or an average over samples, the strong convexity parameter μ may or may not scale with N. If the objective is the sum over samples plus a regularizer, then μ can be of order N (e.g., μ = C N with C independent of N). If the objective is the average over samples plus a regularizer, μ is typically of order λ, independent of N. The paper should clearly state which convention it adopts and how μ, L, and the condition number κ are defined under that convention.

- Comparative complexity: Under the “sum” convention (μ ≈ C N), the proposed method is faster than standard batch gradient descent only when N is at most on the order of O(C^3/L^3) (as argued in the submission). This does not appear to be a practically interesting regime for large-scale ML, where N is typically large. Under the “average” convention (μ ≈ λ), the claimed advantage may differ; however, this needs to be made explicit by translating the complexity into units meaningful for ML practice, such as:
  - number of passes over data (full gradients),
  - total number of individual stochastic gradient computations, and
  - wall-clock time under reasonable computational models.

- High-probability vs deterministic guarantees: The proposed rate holds with high probability, whereas standard batch gradient descent enjoys deterministic linear convergence for strongly convex smooth objectives. Please clarify the dependence on the confidence parameter (e.g., δ), how large the hidden constants are, and whether the expected convergence rate matches the high-probability one. This comparison matters when trading off reliability versus speed.

- Practical relevance: To establish clear relevance for the ML community, the paper should (i) provide a head-to-head comparison with standard batch gradient descent (and strong baselines like SVRG/SAGA if applicable) in terms of passes over data and wall-clock time, and (ii) identify regimes (in N, κ, and stochastic-to-full gradient cost ratios) where the hybrid method yields a tangible advantage. Without this clarification, it is difficult to conclude that the algorithm improves over widely used batch or variance-reduced stochastic methods in realistic settings.

Clarity and presentation
- Be explicit about the definitions:
  - State whether the objective is a sum or an average over samples.
  - Define μ and L under that convention and formally state the condition number κ as a function of λ and L (and N, if applicable).
  - Explain why L ≥ λ in your setting.

- Separate the roles and costs of full versus stochastic gradients. For example, quantify costs as:
  - one full gradient ≈ N stochastic gradient computations;
  - total cost = O(N log(1/ε)) + O(k^2 log(1/ε)) under your model.
  This will help readers assess practical improvements.

- Discuss constant factors and parameter choices (e.g., how k is set, sensitivity to step sizes, and how these interact with κ and N).

Minor comments and corrections
- Equation (6): should be ||w* − ŵ||^2.
- Line 288: specify on which function you apply inequality (7).
- Line 286: the displayed equality seems unnecessary and detracts from clarity; consider removing it.
- Equation (13): replace absolute values with vector norms.
- State explicitly that L ≥ λ and why, even if it seems obvious in your model.
- Equation (4): x^ should be w^, f should be F; also, by Lemma 1, the first term in the max dominates the second.
- Please provide a precise definition of the condition number as a function of λ and L (and clarify whether there is an N dependence based on sum vs average formulation).

Questions for the authors
- Under which data-scaling convention (sum vs average) are your μ and L defined? How do your complexity bounds translate to the number of passes over the dataset?
- What regimes of N and κ yield a practical advantage over batch gradient descent? Can you provide empirical evidence on common benchmarks?
- How do your guarantees and empirical performance compare with variance-reduced SGD methods (e.g., SVRG/SAGA), which also achieve linear convergence with reduced full-gradient computations?
- What is the dependence on the confidence parameter in your high-probability bounds? Do similar guarantees hold in expectation?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a novel and well-argued hybrid approach with seemingly correct analysis. However, its practical advantage over standard batch gradient descent (and modern variance-reduced methods) in typical ML formulations is not yet convincingly established. With clearer problem scaling, explicit complexity in passes over data, and empirical comparisons highlighting regimes of superiority, the contribution could be impactful. In its current form, I am uncertain about the relevance to large-scale ML optimization.
