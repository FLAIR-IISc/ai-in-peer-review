The paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which combines the benefits of deterministic and stochastic gradient descent methods. The authors demonstrate that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate with high probability, reducing the dependence on the condition number of the objective function. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses the limitation of traditional first-order methods, which often require a large number of full gradient evaluations, leading to high computational costs.

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical analysis. The use of two oracles, a gradient oracle and a function oracle, allows for a flexible and efficient optimization process. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the related work, highlighting the advantages and limitations of existing methods.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement:

1. The paper assumes that the condition number is known beforehand, which might not be the case in practice. It would be beneficial to discuss the behavior of the algorithm when this hyper-parameter is misspecified or unknown. This could be done through theoretical analysis or experimental evaluation.
2. The lack of an experiments section is a significant concern. As the main contribution of the paper is a new algorithm, it would be essential to evaluate its performance on real-world datasets and compare it with existing methods. This would provide valuable insights into the algorithm's practicality and effectiveness.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between EMGD and other hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms. A comparison of convergence rates and experimental evaluation would be valuable in this context.
4. The optimization setting considered in the paper is not clearly stated. The authors should clarify whether they intend to solve the deterministic optimization problem or the stochastic approximation problem.
5. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the choice of hyper-parameters, such as the step size and the number of iterations. Providing guidelines or theoretical arguments for setting these parameters would be helpful.

In summary, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization, but it would benefit from addressing the concerns mentioned above. With additional experimental evaluation and discussion of the algorithm's limitations, the paper could be even more impactful.
