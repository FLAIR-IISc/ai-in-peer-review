- Problem: Optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions over convex constraint sets with efficient gradient mapping updates.  
- Setting: Availability of expensive full gradients and cheap stochastic gradients; goal to leverage both.  
- Contribution: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (for moderate κ).  
- Key insight: Replace each full-gradient epoch with O(κ²) mixed steps combining one full gradient per epoch and multiple stochastic gradients, averaging iterates to get κ-independent epoch reduction.  
- Theoretical result: Linear convergence with number of epochs independent of condition number κ; self-contained, clear convergence proof.  
- Novelty: Interesting and novel hybrid idea that mixes few full gradients with many cheap stochastic steps.  
- Comparison to prior work: EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3), comparable to SAG when κ ≤ n^(1/2), and has different scaling regimes than SAG and Nesterov.  
- Advantages over SAG: Handles constrained optimization (via gradient mapping) and has a significantly simpler convergence proof.  
- Unclear claims: Memory and parallelization advantages over SAG are not convincingly established (SAG can use mini-batches).  
- Missing evaluation: No experiments provided to demonstrate practical relevance or validate theoretical advantages.  
- Limitations: Section 3.4 insufficiently discusses limitations; EMGD requires fixing steps per epoch ahead of time (needs knowledge of κ), unlike adaptive methods like SAG/SDCA.  
- Presentation: Paper is well-written with a helpful summary table, but the proof would benefit from more high-level commentary explaining key choices.  
- Specific corrections: Several textual clarifications and fixes suggested (e.g., citations, explicit statements for smoothness and gradient expectation, distributed computing claim, indexing/notation fixes).  
- Typos: Multiple minor typos and notation inconsistencies listed (e.g., "an convex" → "a convex", domain notation, norm vs absolute value, "log" vs "ln").
