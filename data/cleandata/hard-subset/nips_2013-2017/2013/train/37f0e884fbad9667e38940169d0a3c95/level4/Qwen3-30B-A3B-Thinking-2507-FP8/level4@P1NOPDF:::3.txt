

The paper proposes a novel hybrid stochastic/batch gradient descent algorithm that combines stochastic and batch gradients. It claims to converge to an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(k² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The convergence proof appears correct and novel, aside from minor issues noted below.

My primary concern is the algorithm's practical relevance to machine learning, which is central to NIPS. In standard ML settings, strong convexity arises from regularization, so μ scales as Θ(N), where N is the number of samples (μ = Θ(N)). Under this common assumption, the proposed method only outperforms batch gradient descent when N = O(C³/L³), a regime of limited practical interest. Furthermore, the convergence guarantee is high-probability, whereas batch gradient descent achieves deterministic convergence. This critical point must be thoroughly addressed to demonstrate a meaningful advantage over batch methods and justify the paper's relevance to the ML community.

The paper’s title, "Novel hybrid stochastic/batch gradient descent," raises a fundamental question: does the algorithm offer any practical advantage over standard batch gradient descent in real ML problems? Without a compelling argument for its relevance in typical large-scale ML settings (where N is large and μ = Θ(N)), the contribution appears limited. The authors must explicitly demonstrate that their method provides tangible benefits—such as faster convergence or lower computational cost—under realistic ML conditions, rather than relying on asymptotic regimes that do not align with standard practice. This discussion is essential for the paper to meet NIPS' standards for practical impact.
