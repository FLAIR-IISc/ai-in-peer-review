

The paper introduces a "hybrid" deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithm that combines full gradient (deterministic) and stochastic gradient queries. The key theoretical contribution is a convergence rate of $O(\log(1/\epsilon))$ for batch optimization under the assumption that the condition number $\kappa$ (ratio of strong smoothness to strong convexity constants) is known *a priori*, eliminating its dependence in the rate. This result is theoretically intriguing, as it suggests exponential convergence rates typically associated with deterministic methods can be achieved in a stochastic setting.

However, the paper’s core claim is critically undermined by two major limitations. First, the analysis *only* covers the idealized case where $\kappa$ is perfectly known, with no discussion of the algorithm’s behavior when $\kappa$ is misspecified (e.g., overestimated or underestimated). The authors acknowledge this gap but provide no theoretical exploration or empirical validation of this practical scenario. The claim that "dependence on $\kappa$ can be removed" *only* holds when $\kappa$ is known—this crucial caveat is insufficiently emphasized, making the result appear more general than it is. If theoretical analysis for unknown $\kappa$ is intractable (as suggested), the authors should have conducted experiments to demonstrate robustness to $\kappa$ misspecification. Instead, the paper lacks *any* experimental section, which is unacceptable for a paper proposing a new algorithm. Experiments are essential to validate the theoretical claims and assess practical relevance—e.g., comparing the hybrid method against SGD, SAG, or averaged SGD on standard problems.

Second, the algorithm’s dependence on $\kappa$ creates a significant practical hurdle. The proposed method (EGD) requires $\eta$ (a step size parameter) to be set based on $\kappa$ (via $\eta = c/\kappa$), yet the paper provides *no guidance* for estimating $\kappa$ in practice. The authors offer no heuristic for $\kappa$ estimation, nor do they discuss how to handle its misspecification. This renders the algorithm impractical without a known $\kappa$, contradicting the paper’s stated goal of "combining the best of both worlds." The analysis should explicitly address: (1) whether $\kappa$ can be estimated efficiently (e.g., via adaptive schemes), and (2) how convergence degrades when $\kappa$ is inaccurate. Without this, the contribution remains purely theoretical with unclear empirical utility.

Additional concerns include:
- **Lack of novelty context**: The algorithm is presented as a "mixed gradient" update within each iteration, but the approach resembles existing hybrid methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012). The paper fails to compare convergence rates or discuss how it differs from interleaving deterministic/stochastic steps (e.g., alternating between full and stochastic gradients). A review of related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization (e.g., [1]) is essential.
- **Ambiguous problem setting**: The paper does not clarify whether the goal is solving the *deterministic finite-sum problem* $\min_w \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n F_i(w)$ (using a randomized algorithm) or the *stochastic approximation problem* $\min_w \mathbb{E}[F(w)]$. This is critical, as methods like SAG and SDCA address the former, while SGD addresses the latter. Section 3.1’s setup is misleading, and later claims (e.g., "batch optimization") do not resolve this ambiguity. The authors should explicitly state their setting.
- **Overly restrictive assumptions**: The analysis focuses solely on strongly convex, smooth objectives. The authors neglect to discuss extensions to non-strongly convex settings (e.g., $\mu=0$), which are common in practice (Bach & Moulines, 2011). This limits the paper’s applicability.

The paper’s strongest point—its theoretical result—is compelling *only* under the restrictive assumption of known $\kappa$. Without addressing the practicality of $\kappa$ estimation, validating the method empirically, or clarifying the problem setting, the contribution lacks substantial impact. The absence of experiments is a severe omission; for a paper centered on a new algorithm, empirical evidence is non-negotiable. The authors should either: (i) provide a rigorous analysis of $\kappa$ misspecification (theoretically or via experiments), or (ii) significantly revise the claims to reflect the known-$\kappa$ limitation.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting theoretical insight but fails to deliver a practical algorithm due to its reliance on known $\kappa$ and lack of empirical validation. The work would be significantly strengthened by addressing these gaps through experiments, a clearer problem statement, and a more nuanced discussion of related methods. As it stands, the contribution is limited to a narrow theoretical regime without demonstrable advantages over existing approaches in practice.
