- Paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization with an efficient gradient mapping, noting Nesterov’s rate depends on sqrt(κ).
- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): hybrid using costly full gradients and cheap stochastic gradients.
- EMGD complexity: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic calls.
- EMGD uses epochs: one full gradient per epoch plus O(κ²) mixed stochastic steps and averaging to get κ-independent constant decrease.
- Theoretical proof is simple, self-contained, and yields high-probability linear convergence; no experiments are provided.
- EMGD can outperform Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3) but is theoretically slower than SAG in all regimes.
- For κ = n^(1/2), EMGD and SAG have O(n log(1/ε)) complexity, while Nesterov is slower (O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε))).
- Advantages over SAG: applicable to constrained problems, simpler proof, high-probability guarantees; claimed memory/parallelization benefits are less convincing.
- Major con: no experiments to demonstrate practical advantage or relevant problem instances.
- Paper lacks discussion of limitations/drawbacks and needs a substantially improved Section 3.4.
- Proof is correct but would benefit from high-level intuition and motivation for key quantities.
- Practical drawback: epoch length must be fixed using knowledge of κ, unlike SCDA and SAG which adapt and can use faster local convergence.
- Recommend adding a concrete performance-regime comparison among EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov (specific κ vs. n thresholds).
- Addendum a: in ML practice with regularization κ ≈ O(n), EMGD is not favorable; authors should mention settings where κ < n^(2/3).
- Addendum b: cite and discuss Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods and contrast their condition-number dependence.
- Clarity: generally clear with a helpful Table 1, but proof would be clearer with more explanatory commentary.
- Originality: novel combination of known techniques.
- Significance: theoretical contribution is meaningful due to the simple proof, though practical relevance is unproven.
- Detailed editorial suggestions: several specific line edits and clarifications are recommended throughout the manuscript.
- Typos and notation issues: multiple minor errors (e.g., "an convex" → "a convex", inconsistent domain notation D vs Ω, norm vs absolute-value misuse).
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer is satisfied with authors’ response but expects the suggested revisions to be implemented.
