Summary
The paper proposes a hybrid first-order method that interleaves information from a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). Under strong convexity and smoothness, and assuming the condition number κ is known, the authors show that a suitable mixture of deterministic and stochastic updates yields a high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate for batch optimization. The result is striking in that it removes the dependence on κ from the convergence rate, provided κ is given to the algorithm.

Strengths
- Novel theoretical guarantee: Under the stated assumptions, the method achieves an exponential rate while avoiding explicit κ-dependence in the rate.
- Clear conceptual motivation: Leverages the complementary strengths of deterministic and stochastic methods.

Major concerns
1) Reliance on known condition number: The analysis and algorithm require κ (equivalently, λ and L) to set the step size η and the mixing schedule. The paper does not address how to choose or estimate κ in practice, nor does it analyze robustness when κ is misspecified. As stated, the result “removes the dependence on κ” only when κ is known a priori, which severely limits practical relevance.

2) No empirical evaluation: Given that the main contribution is an algorithm, an experimental section is essential to:
- Assess robustness to misspecification of κ (η too large or too small).
- Validate the predicted rates and constants.
- Compare against strong baselines (e.g., SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SVRG, SDCA) on finite-sum problems.

3) Optimization setting is unclear: It is not explicit whether the goal is finite-sum minimization F(w) = (1/n)∑i Fi(w) using randomized oracles, or stochastic optimization minw E[F(w)]. This ambiguity affects the interpretation of the oracles, the complexity measures (number of full vs. stochastic gradient calls), and the relevance of comparisons.

4) Relation to prior work: The paper does not discuss hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods such as Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012), nor recent variance-reduction methods (e.g., SAG, SVRG, SDCA) that also blend full-gradient information with stochastic updates. Positioning the contribution relative to these methods and comparing convergence rates and oracle complexities are necessary.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Hyper-parameter selection and robustness:
  - The proposed EGD update (Eq. 8) with the “mixed gradient” (Eq. 7) requires step size η that depends on κ, where λ is the strong convexity constant and L is the smoothness constant (the manuscript mistakenly refers to both as “strong convexity modulus”). The paper should:
    - Provide practical guidelines or heuristics for estimating L and λ (e.g., spectral bounds from data, backtracking for L, regularization to induce λ, or adaptive restart schemes).
    - Analyze performance when κ is misspecified. What happens if η is set for κ’ > κ or κ’ < κ? Is the rate degraded to polynomial? Does the algorithm remain stable?
    - Consider adaptive strategies that avoid knowing κ, such as doubling/halving schemes, line-search for L, or restart schedules that depend on observed progress, and provide supporting analysis or experiments.

- Clarify problem setting and oracle model:
  - Explicitly state whether the focus is finite-sum or expectation minimization.
  - Define the oracle cost model and the complexity metric: number of full-gradient evaluations, number of stochastic gradient evaluations, and wall-clock time implications.
  - If the result targets finite-sum problems, discuss memory and pass complexity, and how often full gradients are required relative to n.

- Relation to existing methods:
  - Contrast your estimator and schedule with:
    - Hybrid methods in Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and references therein.
    - Variance-reduced methods (SAG, SVRG, SDCA), which can achieve linear rates under strong convexity and implicitly blend periodic full-gradient information with stochastic updates.
  - Clarify similarities/differences: Is the mixed gradient akin to a control variate estimator? How does the required number of full gradients compare to SVRG’s periodic full-gradient computations? Under what regimes does your method outperform in theory?

- Alternative algorithmic view:
  - Although the method is presented as a “mixed” update, it is conceptually close to interleaving deterministic and stochastic steps at different frequencies. Provide a discussion of this equivalence or key differences, and explain whether an explicit alternation would preserve the rate.

- Beyond strong convexity:
  - Strongly convex and smooth settings are standard but restrictive. Discuss extensions to non-strongly convex problems (e.g., by adding regularization and using a homotopy/restart scheme), and cite relevant theory such as Bach & Moulines (2011) on differences between convex and strongly convex cases. Even a brief theoretical sketch or empirical evidence would broaden applicability.

- Experiments (strongly recommended):
  - Evaluate on standard finite-sum tasks (e.g., logistic regression, least squares) with synthetic and real datasets.
  - Include baselines: SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SVRG, SDCA.
  - Report performance versus ε with clear accounting of:
    - Number of full-gradient calls.
    - Number of stochastic gradient calls.
    - Total runtime.
  - Test sensitivity to misspecification of κ (under- and over-estimation), and ablations that vary the mixing ratio and step size rules.
  - Include both well-conditioned and ill-conditioned settings.

Minor issues
- Fix terminology: L is the smoothness (Lipschitz gradient) constant; λ is the strong convexity constant. Avoid calling both “strong convexity modulus.”
- Clarify “batch optimization” and “hybrid oracle” terminology early, and ensure consistency throughout.
- Provide pseudocode for reproducibility, including the schedule of deterministic vs. stochastic calls and all hyper-parameters.

Overall assessment
The paper contains an appealing theoretical result that highlights the power of combining deterministic and stochastic information. However, its practical impact is limited by the reliance on a known condition number, the lack of robustness analysis when κ is unknown or misspecified, missing context relative to closely related hybrid and variance-reduced methods, and the absence of empirical validation. I encourage the authors to address these issues—especially clarifying the setting, adding robustness analysis, and providing experiments with strong baselines—to make the contribution compelling both theoretically and empirically.
