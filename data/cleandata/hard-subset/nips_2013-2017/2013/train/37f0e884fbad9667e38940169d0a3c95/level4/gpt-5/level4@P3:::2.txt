Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization with access to two oracles: (i) a full-gradient oracle and (ii) a stochastic function oracle that induces unbiased stochastic gradients. The authors propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch, EMGD computes one full gradient at an anchor point w̄k. Within the epoch it performs T iterations of “mixed” gradient steps using g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), followed by a projection onto the intersection of the feasible set and a shrinking ℓ2 ball centered at w̄k. With an appropriate choice of T and step size, the analysis establishes linear convergence: after m epochs, the suboptimality decreases by a factor 2−m, using O(m) full gradients and O(mT) stochastic gradients. Instantiating T yields overall O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (with κ = L/λ).

This work connects to several strands of NIPS-related literature. For full-gradient methods on smooth strongly convex problems, accelerated methods achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations (Nesterov). For stochastic optimization, classical SGD achieves O(1/(λε)) rates; more recent algorithms attain optimal O(1/(λε)) for strongly convex problems. For finite-sum problems, SAG and SDCA achieve linear rates O(n log(1/ε)) or O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) under smoothness/strong convexity. The proposed “mixed” gradient estimator, ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄), is closely related in spirit to semi-stochastic or variance-reduced estimators used in later works on finite-sum optimization.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: EMGD shows that allowing a modest number of full-gradient evaluations can remove the condition-number dependence from the number of full gradients needed for linear convergence, leveraging cheaper stochastic gradients to carry most of the computational burden.
- Theoretical guarantees: The paper provides a high-probability linear convergence result with explicit parameter choices and oracle complexity. The epoch structure and shrinking domain are clearly described, and the proof technique is transparent.
- Scope: Unlike SAG/SDCA, EMGD applies to constrained problems (assuming the projection in step 8 is tractable) and requires only O(d) memory.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on κ and misspecification: The analysis and step-size schedule require knowledge of L and λ (hence κ). The paper does not address how to choose or estimate these constants in practice, nor how sensitive EMGD is to misspecification. This is a major practical limitation.
- Lack of experiments: There are no empirical results validating the theory, assessing robustness to hyperparameter misspecification, or comparing wall-clock performance against full-gradient methods and modern stochastic finite-sum methods (SGD/ASGD, SAG, SDCA).
- Oracle model and problem setting: Section 3.1 presents both a stochastic expectation model and later discusses finite-sum problems. The intended primary setting (finite-sum deterministic objective vs. stochastic expectation) should be stated unambiguously, and claims tied to that setting.
- Rate inconsistencies: The abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, whereas Section 1 and Theorem 1 imply O(κ2 log(1/ε)). This inconsistency must be resolved.
- Technical clarity: The strong convexity condition is misstated as ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖; the standard property is strong monotonicity ⟨∇F(w) − ∇F(w′), w − w′⟩ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖2. There are also notational issues (e.g., “conditional number,” “epoches,” EMGD vs EGD).
- Relation to hybrid/semi-stochastic methods: The paper should more directly position EMGD relative to prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic approaches (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) and variance-reduced estimators that mix full and stochastic gradients. A direct comparison of convergence guarantees and oracle trade-offs would strengthen the contribution.
- Practicality of the shrinking-ball projection: While theoretically convenient, projecting onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) may be costly depending on W. The paper asserts efficiency “provided step 8 can be solved efficiently,” but does not discuss when this is the case.

Quality
The main theorem is carefully proved, but relies on exact knowledge of L, λ, and contains an incorrect formal statement of strong convexity. The rate inconsistency (κ vs κ2) and unclear setting weaken the presentation. The comparisons section is helpful but would benefit from a more nuanced accounting of total gradient cost in finite-sum settings and explicit constants.

Clarity
The algorithm and proof are generally readable, but the manuscript needs editing for consistency and precision:
- Correct the strong convexity definition and unify notation (EMGD, epochs).
- Clarify the primary problem setting (finite-sum vs. expectation) and the meaning of the function oracle in each case.
- Resolve the O(κ) vs. O(κ2) discrepancy in the stochastic gradient complexity.
- Fix typos (“condition number,” “epochs,” etc.).

Originality
The mixed gradient estimator and epoch structure are interesting, though related to earlier hybrid and semi-stochastic ideas. The high-probability linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients is a noteworthy angle. Positioning relative to existing methods needs to be sharpened.

Significance
If made practical (adaptive to unknown κ) and empirically validated, EMGD could be a useful tool when occasional full gradients are affordable (e.g., distributed computation) and constraints are present. As written, the lack of adaptivity and experiments limits immediate impact.

Specific suggestions
- Provide guidance for unknown L and λ: backtracking/line-search for L, doubling or regularization-based lower bounds for λ, or an adaptive epoch length. Analyze robustness if L and/or λ are over/underestimated.
- Add an experiments section on standard finite-sum tasks (e.g., logistic regression, least squares), comparing EMGD to AGD, SGD/ASGD, SAG, and SDCA, reporting both iteration counts and wall-clock time. Include sensitivity to mis-specified κ and to the projection radius schedule.
- Clarify the oracle and problem setting. If the focus is finite-sum, state this upfront and map the function oracle to random component selection; if the focus is expectation minimization, discuss how full gradients are obtained.
- Discuss relations to hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods (Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) and to mixed-gradient/variance-reduction estimators, highlighting similarities and differences in oracle complexity and assumptions.
- Streamline the projection step or discuss alternatives when projecting onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) is expensive.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting theoretical result showing how occasional full gradients can remove condition-number dependence from full-gradient counts, with high-probability linear convergence and applicability to constrained problems.

Arguments against acceptance
- Requires prior knowledge of L and λ; no adaptivity or robustness analysis.
- No empirical validation; unclear practical relevance relative to SAG/SDCA and related methods.
- Clarity issues (incorrect strong convexity statement, κ vs κ2 inconsistency, ambiguous setting) that need revision.

Overall
This is a theoretically appealing contribution, but the manuscript needs clarification, adaptivity/robustness discussion, and experimental validation to meet NIPS standards for significance and practicality. I would lean toward rejection in its current form, but would be positive about a revision addressing the above points.
