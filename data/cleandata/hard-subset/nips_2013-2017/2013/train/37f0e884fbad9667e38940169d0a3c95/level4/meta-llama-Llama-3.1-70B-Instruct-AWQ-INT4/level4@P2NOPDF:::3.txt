The paper introduces a novel hybrid strategy for stochastic gradient descent, combining stochastic and batch gradients. The algorithm is proven to converge to an epsilon-accurate solution, requiring O(log(1/eps)) full gradients and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) stochastic gradients.

The convergence proof appears to be correct and novel, aside from a few minor errors that are detailed below. However, my primary concern lies in the relevance of the proposed algorithm within the machine learning context, which is the primary focus of the conference.

In typical machine learning algorithms, strong convexity is often ensured by the regularizer. Consequently, the value of mu is typically on the order of the number of samples N, i.e., mu = C N, where C does not depend on N. Under this assumption, the proposed method is faster than batch gradient descent only if the number of samples is bounded by O(C^3/L^3), which does not seem to be an interesting regime. Furthermore, the convergence rate for the proposed algorithm holds only in high probability, whereas the convergence rate for batch gradient descent is deterministic.

This point is crucial and warrants a thorough discussion to demonstrate the algorithm's genuine advantage over batch gradient descent and to establish the paper's relevance to the machine learning community.
