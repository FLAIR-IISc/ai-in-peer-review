- Proposal: EMGD mixes infrequent full gradients with frequent stochastic gradients in epochs, using a control-variate estimator and shrinking Euclidean balls.  
- Complexity: Achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls (κ = L/λ).  
- Relation to prior work: Closely related to SVRG/SAG/SDCA-style variance-reduction methods and to Nesterov’s accelerated full-gradient methods.  
- Main proof: Clear per-epoch decrease argument using smoothness, shrinking domain, and Hoeffding–Azuma to control martingale differences.  
- Assumptions: Oracle model and roles of L and λ are stated, but κ and L ≥ λ need explicit definition.  
- Quantitative comparison: Lacks careful, quantitative apples-to-apples comparison to existing variance-reduced methods (SAG/SDCA/SVRG).  
- Readability: Generally readable and well organized, with methodical proofs and clear epoch/mixed-gradient explanations.  
- Presentation issues: Notation, equation references, and typographical errors impede comprehension in places.  
- Originality: Use of mixed estimator + shrinking trust-region in a general oracle model is interesting but conceptually similar to semi-stochastic methods.  
- Distinct contributions: Condition-number–independent full-gradient calls, constraint support with low memory, and high-probability guarantees.  
- Theoretical significance: Reducing full-gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)) and providing high-probability linear rates are attractive for expensive full-gradient settings.  
- Practical significance: Per-epoch stochastic cost T = Θ(κ^2 ln(1/δ)) makes EMGD competitive only when κ^2 ≲ n in finite-sum settings; may be outperformed by O(n + κ) methods.  
- Dependence on κ: Requires knowing or upper-bounding κ to set T and step-sizes, limiting practicality; adaptivity not addressed.  
- Pros: Simple algorithm, clean high-probability linear proof, constraint support, O(d) memory, and potential for distributed full-gradient computation.  
- Cons: Total stochastic complexity dominated by κ^2 factor, weaker than O(n + κ) alternatives; limited comparison to related methods and lack of adaptivity.  
- Suggested comparisons: Provide rigorous cost-model comparisons (n vs 1 per stochastic), clarify regimes n ≫ κ and κ ≫ n, and include wall-clock tradeoffs.  
- Regularization discussion: Clarify regimes where EMGD helps given typical λ (τ) scaling with n and refine conditions beyond τ ≳ n^(−2/3).  
- SVRG comparison: Explicitly compare to SVRG-style methods on memory, constraints, and high-probability vs expectation guarantees.  
- Adaptivity suggestion: Consider adaptive epoch lengths or backtracking to avoid needing κ a priori.  
- Experiments: Add experiments showing wall-clock benefits in theoretically predicted regimes (large n, moderate κ, constrained/distributed settings).  
- Minor corrections: Define κ = L/λ and state L ≥ λ; fix notation errors (e.g., norms, ŵ vs x̂, equation references, ball notation, iteration averaging).  
- Technical clarifications: Remove redundant equalities, specify which inequalities are applied at key steps, and clarify required δ and T ranges (e.g., δ ≤ e^(−1/2), T ≥ 1152 L^2/λ^2 ln(1/δ)).  
- Overall assessment: Solid theoretical contribution with clean analysis and reduced full-gradient usage, but practical impact is unclear due to κ^2 dependence and similarity to existing variance-reduction methods; authors should improve comparisons, adaptivity, and presentation.
