This paper proposes a hybrid gradient method that combines stochastic and full (batch) gradients. The algorithm is shown to reach an epsilon-accurate solution using O(log(1/epsilon)) full-gradient evaluations and O(k^2 log(1/epsilon)) stochastic-gradient evaluations. The convergence analysis appears novel and largely correct, aside from the minor issues listed below.

My primary concern is the relevance to typical machine learning settings, which is the focus of the conference. In most ML problems, strong convexity is induced by the regularizer. Under this common setup, the strong convexity parameter μ typically scales with the sample size N (e.g., μ = C·N with C independent of N). With this assumption, the proposed method is faster than standard batch gradient descent only when N is bounded by O(C^3 / L^3), which does not seem like a practically interesting regime. Moreover, your convergence guarantees hold only with high probability, whereas batch gradient descent enjoys deterministic rates.

This issue is critical for establishing the practical impact of the work. The paper should carefully analyze and discuss the regimes (in terms of N, L, μ/λ, and variance) in which the proposed method provides a clear advantage over batch gradient descent on real ML problems. Without such a discussion or empirical evidence in representative settings, it is hard to assess the algorithm’s benefits for the ML community.

Minor comments:
- Equation (6) should read ||w* − ŵ||^2.
- Please specify at line 288 which function inequality (7) is applied to.
- The equality at line 286 should be removed; it adds no insight and reduces clarity.
- In equation (13), replace absolute values with norms.
- Please explicitly state why L ≥ λ (even if it is standard, a brief justification improves readability).
- In equation (4), x^ should be w^, f should be F, and by Lemma 1 the first term in the max is always larger than the second.
- Please define the condition number precisely as a function of λ and L.

Overall: interesting hybrid stochastic/batch gradient method with a largely sound analysis, but it is unclear whether it offers a practical advantage over standard batch gradient descent in typical ML optimization problems. A more thorough discussion (and ideally empirical evidence) delineating the regimes where the method outperforms batch GD is needed to establish relevance.
