The paper presents a “hybrid” deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization. The algorithm mixes calls to a deterministic oracle (querying full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (querying stochastic gradients). The authors demonstrate that this approach can eliminate the dependence on the condition number in the convergence rate.

The authors present an interesting and significant theoretical result. Specifically, assuming the condition number is known – that is, both the strong convexity and strong smoothness constants of the objective are known – and a first-order hybrid oracle (providing access to both stochastic and deterministic gradients) is available, then a suitably chosen mixture of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps achieves a high-probability O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization. 

However, several concerns warrant attention. The current theoretical analysis is limited to the scenario where the condition number is perfectly known *a priori*. The algorithm’s behavior when this crucial hyperparameter is misspecified remains unexplored. The current result essentially states that the dependence on the condition number (κ) can be removed from the convergence rate of a first-order optimization algorithm *when* this condition number is known to the algorithm beforehand. While extending the analysis to unknown κ may be challenging, this could be partially addressed through experimentation. Crucially, the paper lacks an experimental section, which represents a major weakness. Given the paper’s primary contribution is a novel algorithm, empirical validation to assess the theoretical results and evaluate the algorithm’s practical relevance is essential.

Detailed comments:

The proposed algorithm (EGD) relies on the update rule defined by Eq. 8 (page 4), utilizing the “mixed gradient” defined in Eq. 7. Consequently, EGD requires η as a hyperparameter. Setting η necessitates knowledge of the condition number κ, specifically both the strong convexity modulus λ and the strong smoothness parameter L. The paper does not offer guidance or a theoretical basis for determining κ beforehand. 

This presents two possibilities: either the parameter must be estimated, and a corresponding estimation procedure is missing (even a heuristic supported by numerical experiments would be valuable), or the parameter is assumed known, implying a primarily theoretical focus. If the latter is true, the theoretical analysis and/or an experimental section should address the consequences of hyperparameter misspecification, investigating convergence rates when η is set too large or too small.

While the smooth and strongly convex setting is popular in theoretical analysis and realistic in many scenarios, it can be restrictive. Non-strongly convex settings are also of interest, particularly as they arise in various applications.  See Bach & Moulines (2011) for a discussion of behavior under different convexity assumptions.

Further concerns exist. Blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps in first-order optimization is not novel. The presented algorithm does not follow the common paradigm of alternating deterministic and stochastic steps with varying frequencies. Instead, it employs a single “mixed” update per iteration, followed by a gradient-like update. A discussion of how the proposed approach compares to algorithms that *do* interleave deterministic and stochastic gradient steps (at least in the unconstrained case) would be beneficial. 

The authors do not acknowledge related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms; see Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC, 2012). Comparing convergence rates would add significant value.  See also the aforementioned reference for a review of earlier contributions.

Finally, a thorough experimental study would significantly strengthen the paper. This should include a detailed comparison against standard SGD, averaged SGD, and recent stochastic first-order optimization methods (SAG, etc.).

A minor point: the optimization setting is not clearly defined. The paper aims to leverage the strengths of both deterministic and stochastic optimization – exponential convergence from the former and condition number independence from the latter. However, it’s unclear whether the authors intend to solve the deterministic optimization problem [Min<sub>w</sub> F(w) = 1/n Σ<sub>i=1</sub><sup>n</sup> F<sub>i</sub>(w)] using a “stochastic” algorithm or the stochastic approximation problem [Min<sub>w</sub> E(F(w))]. Note that both SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for the deterministic problem. The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is potentially misleading in this regard, and subsequent claims do not clarify the intended setting. This could be easily addressed with a clearer problem definition.
