- Proposes EMGD, a hybrid algorithm using expensive full gradients and cheap stochastic gradients for smooth strongly convex optimization over convex sets.  
- EMGD requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls (κ-independent) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic calls, replacing each full-gradient epoch with O(κ²) mixed steps and averaging iterates to remove κ-dependence.  
- Provides a clear, self-contained convergence proof but includes no experiments.  

- Novel, well-motivated hybrid approach combining full and stochastic steps.  
- Achieves linear convergence for constrained problems (unlike SAG) with a significantly simpler proof.  
- Insightful theoretical comparison to SAG and Nesterov, characterizing regimes where each method dominates (e.g., EMGD better than Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3)).  

- No experiments to demonstrate practical speedups or validate theoretical claims.  
- Insufficient discussion of limitations (Section 3.4); key drawbacks: fixed epoch length needing prior κ knowledge, and inferior performance to SAG for common regimes (κ ≤ n/8).  
- Proof lacks high-level intuition for key constructions (e.g., why averaging removes κ-dependence).  
- Overstates that SAG cannot leverage distributed computing; mini-batching enables parallelization and memory reduction.  

- Recommend adding a concrete example contrasting EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov across κ regimes (EMGD < Nesterov when κ > n^(2/3); EMGD ≈ SAG when 1 ≤ κ ≤ √n; SAG < EMGD when √n < κ ≤ n/8; Nesterov < SAG when κ > n/8).  
- Fix notation inconsistencies, clarify specific lines (e.g., line 132, line 139 expectation statement, line 224 on SAG parallelization), and correct typos/inconsistent log notation.  
- Cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) as a related hybrid method.  

- Technical contribution is sound and significant: offers a theoretically faster alternative to Nesterov for constrained problems under mild κ assumptions and a simpler proof that may inspire further work.  
- Main remaining gap is lack of practical validation via experiments.  

- Paper is well-structured and readable; proofs would benefit from brief high-level remarks explaining key steps (especially averaging).  
- The hybrid technique is original though building on prior hybrid methods.  

- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution appropriate for acceptance pending expanded limitations discussion, clarification of practical regimes (κ ≤ n^(2/3)), minor fixes, and ideally experiments.
