Summary:
The paper studies optimization of smooth, strongly convex objectives over convex constraint sets where the gradient mapping (projection/proximal) is efficiently computable. Nesterov’s optimal first-order method attains linear convergence, but with constants depending on the square root of the condition number k. The authors consider access to both an expensive full-gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic-gradient oracle and propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid method that uses O(log(1/eps)) full-gradient calls (independent of k) and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) stochastic-gradient calls. Each epoch computes one full gradient and then performs O(k^2) “mixed” steps combining the fixed full gradient with varying stochastic gradients; averaging the epoch’s iterates yields a constant suboptimality reduction independent of k. The paper presents a clean, self-contained proof of convergence but no experiments.

Evaluation summary:
Pros:
- Novel and appealing hybrid algorithm with a simple and solid analysis.
- Achieves linear convergence for regularized empirical risk minimization without multiplying the condition number k by the dataset size n; unlike SAG, it applies to constrained problems (given an efficient gradient-mapping update). The proof is substantially simpler than SAG’s and provides high-probability guarantees (vs. SAG’s expectation bound).
- Clear writing and self-contained theory; Table 1 provides a useful summary of related rates.

Cons:
- No experiments to demonstrate practical benefits or to illustrate scenarios where the hybrid approach provides real-world gains.
- Limited discussion of limitations and trade-offs, especially relative to existing methods; Section 3.4 should be expanded.
- The proof would benefit from higher-level intuition for key constructions and parameter choices.

Comparison to related work:
- In the common “sum of n functions” setting (e.g., regularized ERM), assume a full gradient costs n times a stochastic gradient. SAG achieves linear convergence and is theoretically faster than Nesterov’s method when k ≤ n/8. EMGD improves over Nesterov’s method when k ≤ n^(2/3) but is slower than SAG across all regimes (matching SAG’s big-O when k ≤ n^(1/2)). For example, if k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD cost O(n log(1/eps)), while Nesterov’s method costs O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)).
- Two advantages of EMGD relative to SAG: (1) it naturally handles constrained optimization (assuming efficient gradient mapping), whereas SAG is defined for unconstrained problems; and (2) the analysis is much simpler and may be more readily extendable, with stronger high-probability guarantees.
- The authors should cite Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012), which also studies hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods with linear convergence. That method’s rate still depends on the condition number, so EMGD improves the asymptotic rate when k is not too large relative to n.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound. While experiments are not strictly necessary given the theoretical focus, an empirical section would substantially strengthen the work. Section 3.4 should be expanded to discuss limitations and practical trade-offs, including:
- Sensitivity to k: EMGD requires fixing the number of steps per epoch in advance using k. In contrast, SCDA [17] and SAG do not require fixing iteration counts a priori and can benefit in practice from better local conditioning or faster-than-worst-case behavior.
- Step-size selection: SCDA offers automatic step-size selection; SAG uses an adaptive heuristic that works well in practice. EMGD’s requirements and robustness should be discussed.
- Practical regimes: In many ML applications, the regularization is C/n, so k ≈ n/C′. In this regime, SAG remains better than Nesterov’s method, but EMGD does not improve upon it. The paper should highlight this and, conversely, identify realistic regimes where k ≤ n^(2/3) is expected.

Clarity:
The exposition is clear, and the proof is easy to follow. Table 1 is particularly helpful. The proof would be more instructive with brief high-level commentary on why the epoch length is set to O(k^2), why averaging within an epoch is crucial, and how the mixed gradient stabilizes variance.

Originality:
The algorithmic idea—mixing infrequent full gradients with many cheap stochastic updates to achieve condition-number–independent epoch progress—is novel in this precise form and well motivated by recent trends.

Significance:
The theoretical contribution is potentially impactful, especially given the simplicity of the analysis relative to SAG. Practical relevance is not yet demonstrated.

Detailed suggestions:
- Add a dedicated discussion contrasting EMGD with SAG and Nesterov in the “sum of n functions” setting. For instance: EMGD outperforms Nesterov when k ≤ n^(2/3); SAG matches EMGD for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2), outperforms both EMGD and Nesterov for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8, and Nesterov beats SAG for k > n/8.
- Discuss the need to know k to set the epoch length and the lack of adaptivity relative to SCDA and SAG; comment on practical step-size selection and robustness.
- Clarify the parallelization/memory discussion. The claim that SAG (or SCDA) cannot leverage distributed computing is inaccurate: both can use mini-batches, which also reduce memory.
- Cite: M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt, “Hybrid Deterministic–Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting,” SISC, 2012.
