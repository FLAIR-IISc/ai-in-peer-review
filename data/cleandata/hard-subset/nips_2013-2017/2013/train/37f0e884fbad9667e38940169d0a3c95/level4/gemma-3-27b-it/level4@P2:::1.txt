The authors consider the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where computing the gradient can be done efficiently. The optimal first-order algorithm of Nesterov achieves linear convergence for such problems, but the constant depends on the square root of the condition number κ. The authors address the situation where both expensive full gradients and cheap stochastic gradient oracles are available. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), requiring O(log 1/ε) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the cheaper stochastic gradient oracle—potentially faster in theory if κ is not too large. The core idea is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with a fixed number O(κ²) of mixed gradient steps, combining the full gradient (computed once per epoch) with stochastic gradients. Averaging the iterates within an epoch yields a constant decrease in suboptimality independent of κ, resulting in a number of full gradient computations independent of κ. A self-contained proof of the convergence rate is provided, but no experiments are included.

I found this paper enjoyable to read, and the idea of combining a limited number of full gradient computations with a larger number of inexpensive stochastic gradient steps is novel and interesting. This work builds on a series of recent papers aiming to leverage the cheaper stochastic oracle while maintaining a linear convergence rate. The SAG algorithm [22] is a recent theoretical and practical breakthrough for smooth, strongly convex objectives expressed as a sum of *n* simple functions (common in regularized empirical loss minimization, where *n* is the number of training examples). Assuming the full gradient oracle is *n* times more expensive than the stochastic gradient one, SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov’s algorithm when κ ≤ *n*/8. In contrast, EMGD is faster than Nesterov’s algorithm when κ ≤ *n*<sup>2/3</sup>, but slower than SAG in all regimes. For instance, if κ = *n*<sup>1/2</sup>, both SAG & EMGD are O(*n* log(1/ε)), whereas Nesterov’s algorithm is O(*n*<sup>5/4</sup> log(1/ε)). EMGD's two primary advantages over SAG are its applicability to constrained optimization (assuming an efficient gradient mapping update) and its simpler convergence proof, potentially offering more insights and facilitating provable modifications. The authors also suggest potential memory/parallelization advantages, though this is less clear given SAG's parallelization via mini-batches, which also reduces memory requirements.

**EVALUATION SUMMARY:**

**Pros:**

*   Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theoretical foundation.
*   Like SAG, avoids having the condition number κ multiply the number of training examples *n* in the constant term, offering a more favorable scaling. The algorithm is more general (works for constrained optimization) and the proof is simpler.
*   The paper is clearly written and the proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**

*   Lacks experimental validation to demonstrate practical impact.  It doesn't provide concrete examples illustrating the theoretical advantages.
*   Limited discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and drawbacks compared to existing algorithms (Section 3.4 could be improved).
*   The proof could benefit from high-level comments justifying key insights and choices.

**QUALITY:** The paper is technically sound. While experiments would be valuable, the theoretical contribution is significant. The authors should expand Section 3.4 to discuss limitations and drawbacks. They should also elaborate on the sum of *n* functions example, highlighting the differences with SAG and Nesterov (e.g., EMGD is worse than Nesterov for κ > *n*<sup>2/3</sup>; SAG outperforms EMGD when 1 ≤ κ ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>; and is better than both Nesterov and EMGD when *n*<sup>1/2</sup> < κ ≤ *n*/8; and worse than Nesterov when κ > *n*/8). A practical drawback is the need to pre-fix the number of steps within an epoch (requiring knowledge of κ) – SCDA and SAG don’t have this limitation and can benefit from faster practical convergence or adaptation to local condition numbers. The authors should address this.

**CLARITY:** The paper is generally clear. Table 1 provides a helpful summary of convergence rates not commonly presented in a consolidated manner. The proof is detailed but would be more accessible with additional high-level explanations motivating the defined quantities.

**ORIGINALITY:** This represents a novel combination of established techniques.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The practical relevance remains to be shown. However, the theoretical contribution is potentially impactful, especially considering the complex proofs associated with SAG and the simpler proof offered here.
