The paper introduces a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining full gradient and stochastic gradient queries. The authors demonstrate that this approach can eliminate the dependence on the condition number in the convergence rate. 

The central theoretical result is compelling: under the assumption of a known condition number (strong convexity and smoothness constants) and access to a first-order hybrid oracle, a carefully tuned mixture of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps yields a high-probability O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization. 

However, the paper presents several concerns. The current analysis is limited to scenarios where the condition number is perfectly known *a priori*; the algorithm’s behavior with a misspecified condition number is unexplored. The result effectively removes the condition number (kappa) dependence from the convergence rate only when kappa is known. While analyzing the unknown kappa case may be challenging, experiments could address this limitation. Crucially, the paper lacks an experimental evaluation, which is a significant weakness given that the primary contribution is a novel algorithm. Empirical validation would strengthen the theoretical findings and demonstrate their practical relevance.

Specifically, the proposed algorithm (EGD), defined by Eq. 8 (page 4) and utilizing the “mixed gradient” in Eq. 7, requires careful setting of the hyperparameter η, which is directly tied to the condition number κ. The authors do not provide guidance or theoretical justification for determining κ beforehand. Consequently, either an estimation procedure is needed (even a heuristic supported by experiments) or the analysis and experiments should cover cases of misspecification – specifically, performance with η set too high or too low.

Furthermore, while the smooth and strongly convex setting is common in theoretical analysis, its restrictiveness should be acknowledged. Exploring the non-strongly convex case, as discussed in Bach & Moulines (2011), would broaden the work’s impact. 

Blending deterministic and stochastic gradients is not entirely novel. The algorithm's formulation, as a single "mixed" update per iteration, differs from approaches that alternate deterministic and stochastic steps with varying frequencies. A discussion comparing the proposed method to such interleaved algorithms would be valuable.

The paper also omits a review of related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization, such as Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC, 2012). Comparing convergence rates with existing methods would strengthen the contribution. A thorough experimental study comparing EGD to SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, and other recent stochastic optimization techniques is highly recommended.

Finally, the optimization setting is not clearly defined. The paper aims to combine the benefits of deterministic and stochastic optimization – exponential convergence and reduced condition number sensitivity – but does not explicitly state whether it addresses deterministic optimization with a randomized algorithm [Minw F(w)=1/n \sum{i=1}^n Fi(w)] or stochastic approximation [Minw E(F(w))]. Section 3.1’s theoretical setup is potentially misleading, and subsequent claims do not clarify the chosen setting. This ambiguity should be addressed.
