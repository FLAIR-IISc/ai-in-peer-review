

The paper proposes a hybrid deterministic-stochastic first-order optimization algorithm that combines full gradient and stochastic gradient queries. The key theoretical claim is that, under the assumption that the condition number κ (defined by the strong convexity constant λ and smoothness constant L) is known *a priori*, the algorithm achieves a high-probability convergence rate of O(log(1/ε)) for batch optimization, eliminating the standard κ-dependence in the rate. While the result is theoretically intriguing, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution and credibility.

The primary flaw lies in the unrealistic assumption of perfect knowledge of κ. The algorithm requires κ to set the hyperparameter η (Equation 8), yet the paper provides no guidance for estimating κ in practice or analyzing the algorithm’s behavior when κ is misspecified (e.g., overestimated or underestimated). Theoretical analysis of misspecification is non-trivial, but the absence of even empirical exploration is unacceptable for a paper centered on a new algorithm. Without experiments, the theoretical claim remains unverified, and the practical relevance of the method is unclear. The authors should either: (1) provide a principled estimation procedure for κ supported by experiments, or (2) rigorously analyze the convergence rate under κ misspecification (e.g., for fixed η ≠ κ), which would require extensive numerical validation. The current omission of any empirical study is a critical deficiency.

Furthermore, the paper fails to contextualize its work within the existing literature on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods. The algorithm bears conceptual similarity to approaches like those in Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC 2012), which explicitly blend deterministic and stochastic steps. The authors neglect to cite this seminal work or compare their convergence rates to established hybrid methods. This omission obscures the novelty of their contribution and misrepresents the state of the art. A brief discussion of related work (e.g., [Friedlander and Schmidt, 2012]) would clarify whether the "mixed gradient" update (Equation 7) offers a meaningful advancement over interleaved deterministic-stochastic steps.

The paper also suffers from ambiguity in its optimization setting. The theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) conflates two distinct problems: (1) deterministic minimization of the empirical risk min_w (1/n ∑_{i=1}^n F_i(w)) using a randomized algorithm (e.g., SAG, SDCA), and (2) stochastic approximation min_w E[F(w)]. The claims about "batch optimization" and the oracle model are inconsistent with this distinction. For instance, the algorithm is framed as solving the deterministic problem (as in SAG), yet the convergence rate analysis assumes a stochastic oracle. This confusion must be resolved: the paper should explicitly state whether it targets the empirical risk minimization (ERM) problem or the stochastic approximation problem, as the assumptions and implications differ substantially.

Additional minor concerns include:
- The algorithm’s update rule (Equation 8) is presented as a "mixed gradient" step per iteration, but the paper does not clarify how it compares to simpler alternatives, such as alternating deterministic and stochastic steps at varying frequencies. A discussion of this design choice would strengthen the contribution.
- The analysis is restricted to strongly convex and smooth objectives, overlooking the more common non-strongly convex case (e.g., as in Bach and Moulines, 2011). While the theoretical focus is understandable, the paper should acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential extensions.
- The absence of experiments is particularly egregious given the algorithm’s reliance on κ. Even minimal comparisons against SGD, averaged SGD, or SAG on standard datasets (e.g., logistic regression on MNIST or CIFAR) would validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical utility.

In summary, the paper’s core theoretical insight is interesting but hinges on an unverified assumption (known κ). Without empirical validation or analysis of κ misspecification, the result lacks practical significance. The failure to engage with related work (e.g., Friedlander and Schmidt) and the ambiguous problem setting further weaken the contribution. To be acceptable, the authors must address these issues in a major revision: (1) add experiments demonstrating the algorithm’s behavior under κ misspecification and comparing it to baselines; (2) clarify the optimization setting and cite key related work; and (3) provide a principled approach for κ estimation or analyze the sensitivity to κ. As currently written, the paper’s claims are unsupported, and the contribution is overstated. I recommend rejection pending substantial revision.
