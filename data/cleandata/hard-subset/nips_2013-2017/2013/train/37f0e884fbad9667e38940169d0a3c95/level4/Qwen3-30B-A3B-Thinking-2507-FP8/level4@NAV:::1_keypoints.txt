- Problem/setting: Optimize a smooth strongly convex function over a convex constraint set with efficiently computable gradient-mapping updates.  
- Main contribution: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, making epoch count independent of κ.  
- Core idea: Replace a full-gradient epoch with O(κ²) mixed steps that use one full gradient plus stochastic gradients and average the iterates to get κ-independent suboptimality decrease.  
- Theoretical result: Self-contained, complete proof showing linear convergence; no experiments provided.  
- Novelty/impact: Novel hybrid approach that leverages cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence; aligns with recent research trends.  
- Comparison to SAG: SAG outperforms Nesterov for κ ≤ n/8; EMGD helps when κ ≤ n^(2/3); EMGD is generally slower than SAG across regimes and matches SAG for some κ ranges.  
- Advantages over SAG: Handles constrained optimization (via gradient mapping) and has a much simpler proof with stronger high-probability guarantees versus SAG’s expectation-based proof.  
- Major cons: No experiments to validate practical relevance and insufficient discussion of limitations; Section 3.4 needs substantial expansion.  
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires a fixed, κ-dependent epoch size (needs κ knowledge), unlike adaptive methods such as SCDA/SAG which adjust automatically and offer step-size selection.  
- Clarify regime comparisons: Authors should explicitly state when EMGD is worse/better than Nesterov and SAG across κ vs. n regimes.  
- Requested clarifications: Add high-level motivation in the proof, clarify Table 1 citations, explicit assumptions (e.g., ∇F(w)=E[∇f(w], “for all w”), and several equation/text clarifications noted in-line.  
- Editorial fixes: Correct typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., "an convex"→"a convex", Ω vs D, norm vs absolute, use ln vs log, parentheses in sums).  
- Addendum notes: In typical ML regularization settings SAG usually remains superior; cite Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) for related hybrid methods.  
- Overall assessment: Technically sound, well-written, and theoretically meaningful (quality 7/10, clarity 8, originality 8, significance 7), but needs experiments and revisions to fully establish practical impact.
