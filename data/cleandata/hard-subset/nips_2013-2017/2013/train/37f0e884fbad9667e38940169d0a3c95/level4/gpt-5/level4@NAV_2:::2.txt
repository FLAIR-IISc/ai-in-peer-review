The paper proposes a hybrid deterministic–stochastic first-order method that interleaves access to a full-gradient oracle with access to a stochastic-gradient oracle. The key idea is to use a “mixed” gradient step that combines one full gradient per epoch with many stochastic gradients, thereby eliminating the dependence on the condition number in the count of expensive full-gradient evaluations while attaining a linear (O(log(1/epsilon))) convergence rate with high probability. Under smoothness and strong convexity, the method achieves O(log(1/epsilon)) full-gradient calls and a larger number of stochastic-gradient calls, yielding a compelling theoretical improvement when full gradients are costly.

The central theoretical contribution is appealing and clear: assuming the condition number is available (i.e., both the strong convexity parameter lambda and the smoothness parameter L are known), and given access to both deterministic and stochastic first-order oracles, an appropriate schedule of mixed updates yields a high-probability linear rate in epsilon for batch optimization. This nicely complements prior NIPS and optimization literature on accelerated full-gradient methods and finite-sum stochastic methods by trading expensive full-gradient evaluations for cheaper stochastic ones while preserving linear convergence.

That said, there are several concerns in the current form. First, the theory covers only the regime where the condition number is known exactly; there is no analysis of robustness when this hyperparameter is misspecified. In effect, the result shows that the dependence on kappa can be removed in the convergence rate only when kappa is assumed known to the algorithm. If analyzing the misspecified case is challenging, empirical evidence could provide guidance—but the paper contains no experiments, which is a second major concern. Given that the main contribution is an algorithm, it would be natural to include experiments to assess the theory and probe practical behavior.

Detailed comments
- The proposed algorithm (EMGD) uses the update rule in Eq. 8 (page 4) based on the “mixed gradient” in Eq. 7, and thus requires a stepsize eta to be specified. Choosing eta effectively amounts to knowing the condition number kappa = L/lambda; in other words, one needs both the strong convexity parameter lambda and the smoothness constant L. As far as I can tell, the paper does not offer guidance or a principled procedure to set kappa a priori.
- This leaves two possibilities. Either kappa must be estimated, in which case an estimation procedure (even a heuristic) should be described and supported by experiments; or kappa is assumed known because the contribution is primarily theoretical, in which case the analysis and/or experiments should study sensitivity to misspecification. In particular, it would be valuable to characterize convergence when kappa is set too large or too small relative to the truth.
- While the smooth and strongly convex setting is common and useful, it can be restrictive. The non-strongly convex case is also important in practice. It would strengthen the paper to discuss how the approach might extend (or fail to extend) to merely smooth convex objectives, and to relate expected behaviors across regimes (see, e.g., Bach & Moulines, 2011).
- The idea of combining deterministic and stochastic gradient information is not new. Although EMGD is formulated as a single “mixed” update per iteration within epochs, it is closely related in spirit to alternating deterministic and stochastic steps at different frequencies. It would help to clarify how EMGD compares to a simple interleaving scheme (especially in the unconstrained case) and to articulate any advantages of the mixed form versus explicit alternation.
- The paper omits an important related line of work on hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods; see Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC, 2012) for analysis and a survey of earlier approaches. A discussion and comparison of convergence guarantees and oracle complexities with this literature would be valuable.
- A thorough experimental section would considerably improve the paper. Suggested baselines include standard SGD, averaged/Polyak–Ruppert SGD, and recent finite-sum methods such as SAG and related variants. Empirical comparisons on finite-sum problems and on large-scale datasets would help assess the practical tradeoffs between full and stochastic gradient calls and validate the claimed advantages.
- A more minor clarity issue: the problem setting is not stated crisply. The paper aims to combine the best features of deterministic (linear convergence) and stochastic (cheap iterations) optimization, but it is unclear whether the primary target is empirical risk minimization over a finite sum F(w) = (1/n) sum_i f_i(w) with a randomized algorithm, or stochastic approximation minimizing E[f(w)]. Note that SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for the finite-sum setting. The oracle framework in Section 3.1 blurs this distinction, and later claims do not explicitly resolve it. This can be fixed by clearly specifying which setting each result targets and how the oracle model maps to finite-sum versus expectation formulations.

The authors present an engaging and notable theoretical result: if the condition number is known and both full and stochastic gradients are available, then by appropriately mixing these two sources of information one can obtain, with high probability, a linear (O(log(1/epsilon))) rate for batch optimization while using only O(log(1/epsilon)) full-gradient evaluations. However, the analysis assumes exact knowledge of the condition number and does not explore robustness to misspecification, and the paper lacks experiments. If theoretical treatment of the unknown-kappa case is difficult, empirical studies would still be informative. Including such analyses or experiments would make the contribution significantly stronger.
