- Reviewer scores: quality 7/10, clarity 8/10, originality 8/10, significance 7/10.
- Problem & contribution: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) combining occasional full gradients with many cheap stochastic steps for smooth, strongly convex optimization with constraints.
- Complexity claim: EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of condition number k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.
- Core idea: Each epoch uses one full gradient plus O(k^2) mixed stochastic steps, averaging epoch iterates to get k-independent per-epoch reduction.
- Theory: Provides a simple, self-contained convergence proof that holds with high probability.
- Novelty: Mixing full and stochastic gradients in this way is novel and interesting; proof simplicity is a strength.
- Applicability: EMGD handles constrained problems (via gradient mapping) unlike SAG which is currently unconstrained-only.
- Comparison to SAG/Nesterov: EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov when k ≤ n^(2/3), but slower than SAG in most regimes (matches SAG only when k ≤ n^(1/2)); SAG dominates for many practical parameter ranges.
- Practical drawbacks: No experiments; lack of demonstration of practical impact or regimes where theoretical gains matter.
- Algorithm limitations: Requires fixing number of inner steps per epoch in advance (needs knowledge of k), unlike SDCA and SAG which adapt in practice.
- Step-size/implementation: SDCA has automatic step-size and SAG uses adaptive heuristics; EMGD lacks such practical adaptivity discussion.
- Claims about parallelization/memory: Claimed benefits are unclear because SAG can use mini-batches and be parallelized to reduce memory.
- Related work omission: Should cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods.
- Requested expansions: Expand Section 3.4 to discuss drawbacks/limitations, and add concrete discussion of the "sum of n functions" setting comparing EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov across k vs n regimes.
- Proof suggestions: Add high-level commentary motivating key definitions and clarify some derivation steps to make the proof less “magical.”
- Presentation fixes: Several clarifications and explicit statements requested (e.g., domain notation, gradient expectation equality, conditions on inequalities).
- Typos/edits: List of minor typos and notation consistency fixes (e.g., "an convex" → "a convex", domain symbol consistency, norm vs absolute value, log vs ln).
- Final stance: Reviewer satisfied with authors' rebuttal and believes the theoretical contribution stands if revisions (discussion, clarifications, citations, and optional experiments) are incorporated.
