The paper proposes a hybrid first-order method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that interleaves access to full and stochastic gradients. The method achieves an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) evaluations of the full gradient and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, where κ = L/λ is the condition number. The analysis hinges on a mixed-gradient step that combines a reference full gradient at the epoch anchor with variance-reduced stochastic differences, together with a shrinking trust region per epoch. This design yields a linear (logarithmic in 1/ε) convergence rate in terms of the number of epochs (full-gradient calls). The work relates to and contrasts with accelerated full-gradient methods (O(√κ log(1/ε))) and to finite-sum variance-reduced or semi-stochastic methods (e.g., SAG/SDCA), emphasizing reduced dependence on κ for full-gradient evaluations and high-probability guarantees.

The convergence proof appears sound and, to my knowledge, novel, aside from the minor issues noted below.

My primary concern is the practical relevance in standard machine learning settings, which are the focus of the conference. In typical ML formulations, strong convexity is induced by the regularizer. Under common scalings, this implies the strong convexity parameter μ grows on the order of the sample size N (e.g., μ = C·N with C independent of N). Under this assumption, the proposed approach improves on batch (full-gradient) methods only when N is bounded by O(C^3/L^3), which does not appear to be a practically meaningful regime. Moreover, EMGD’s linear rate is stated with high probability, whereas the convergence of batch gradient methods is deterministic. This issue is critical and should be addressed carefully to demonstrate a concrete advantage over full-gradient methods and to clarify the paper’s significance for the ML community.
