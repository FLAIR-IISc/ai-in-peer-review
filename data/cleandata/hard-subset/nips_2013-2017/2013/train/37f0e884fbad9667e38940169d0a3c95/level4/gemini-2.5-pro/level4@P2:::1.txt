**Summary**

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a setting where the optimal first-order method (Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) achieves a linear convergence rate that depends on the square root of the condition number, κ. The authors propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for scenarios where both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle are available. The goal is to reduce the dependency on κ for the number of expensive full gradient computations.

The core idea of EMGD is to organize the optimization process into epochs. Each epoch consists of one full gradient evaluation, followed by O(κ²) "mixed gradient" steps. These steps use a combination of the single full gradient computed at the start of the epoch and a new stochastic gradient at each iteration. By averaging the iterates within an epoch, the algorithm demonstrates a constant factor of error reduction, leading to a linear convergence rate. The main theoretical result is that EMGD requires only O(log 1/ε) full gradient evaluations, a quantity independent of the condition number κ, alongside O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradient evaluations. The paper provides a complete and self-contained proof of this convergence rate.

**Evaluation**

This work is part of a recent and important line of research aiming to achieve linear convergence rates while leveraging inexpensive stochastic gradient information, notably following the breakthrough of the Stochastic Average Gradient (SAG) algorithm.

**Pros:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and interesting algorithmic idea that combines full and stochastic gradients in a new way. The theory is clean, simple, and appears solid.
*   The algorithm is more general than some competing methods like SDCA, as it can handle constrained optimization problems (provided the projection step is efficient).
*   Compared to the complex analysis of SAG, the proof for EMGD is remarkably simple and self-contained. This clarity is a significant contribution, potentially offering new insights and making the algorithm more amenable to future modifications with provable guarantees.
*   The resulting convergence bound holds with high probability, which is a stronger theoretical guarantee than the in-expectation results for SAG and SDCA.

**Cons:**
*   The paper lacks any experimental validation. Without experiments, it is difficult to assess whether the algorithm is practically useful or if its theoretical advantages translate to real-world performance gains.
*   The discussion of the algorithm's limitations is insufficient. Section 3.4 should be significantly expanded to provide a more balanced comparison with existing methods, acknowledging the drawbacks of EMGD.
*   The proof, while technically clear, could benefit from more high-level intuition to explain why certain steps are taken and how the key components fit together.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

**Quality and Significance:**
The paper is technically sound and the theoretical contribution is potentially significant. However, the authors should provide a more thorough discussion of the algorithm's trade-offs. For the common empirical risk minimization setting (a sum of `n` functions), where a full gradient is `n` times more costly than a stochastic one, the comparison to related work should be sharpened:
*   EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3).
*   SAG is faster than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n/8.
*   In regimes where both methods outperform Nesterov's, EMGD has a worse dependency on κ than SAG and SDCA, except when κ ≤ n^(1/2), where its complexity is on the same order.

A major practical drawback is that EMGD requires knowledge of the condition number κ to set the number of inner iterations per epoch. In contrast, algorithms like SAG and SDCA do not require fixing the number of iterations in advance and can benefit from better local properties of the objective function. This limitation should be explicitly discussed.

Furthermore, as noted in the addendum, a common machine learning scenario involves a regularization parameter proportional to 1/n, leading to κ ≈ O(n). In this important regime, EMGD does not offer a theoretical improvement over Nesterov's method, whereas SAG does. The authors should acknowledge this and perhaps suggest other practical settings where the condition κ < n^(2/3) is met.

Finally, the authors should cite and discuss the work of Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012) on "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting," which also presents a hybrid algorithm with a linear convergence rate. While that work's rate still depends on κ, it is highly relevant prior art.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and the inclusion of Table 1 is helpful. As mentioned, the proof would be more impactful with additional high-level commentary motivating the definitions and key steps. Below are specific suggestions for improvement:
*   Line 50: Suggest rephrasing to "are summarized in Table 1 and elaborated in the related work section" to clarify where the details for the table can be found.
*   Lines 118-119: It would be helpful to specify that SDCA applies to a more restricted functional form than SAG.
*   Line 132: Clarify that the condition F(w) = E[f(w)] holds for all w ∈ W.
*   Line 139: Explicitly state that ∇F(w) = E[∇f(w)].
*   Line 224: The claim that SAG and SDCA cannot benefit from distributed computing is not accurate. Both can be parallelized using mini-batches, which also has the benefit of reducing memory requirements. This point should be corrected.
*   The notation for the domain (W vs. D) should be made consistent throughout the paper.

**Originality:**
The algorithm represents a novel combination of established techniques (epoch-based schemes, variance reduction) to achieve a new theoretical result. The "mixed gradient" update is a creative idea.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents EMGD, a novel algorithm with a clean and elegant theoretical analysis. Its primary contribution is achieving a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradient evaluations that is independent of the condition number. The simplicity of its proof is a notable strength. However, the practical relevance of the algorithm is not demonstrated, and the paper needs a more comprehensive discussion of its limitations and a more nuanced comparison to state-of-the-art methods like SAG and SDCA. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the paper would be much stronger with the inclusion of experiments and a more thorough discussion of the points raised in this review.
