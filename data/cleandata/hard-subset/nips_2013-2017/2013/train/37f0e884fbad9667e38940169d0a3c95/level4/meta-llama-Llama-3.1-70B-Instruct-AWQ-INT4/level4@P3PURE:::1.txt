**Summary**

The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages both expensive full gradient and cheap stochastic gradient oracles. EMGD requires O(log 1/eps) calls to the full gradient oracle, independent of the condition number, and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle, making it theoretically faster than Nesterov's algorithm for certain condition numbers.

The main idea behind EMGD is to replace a full gradient step with a fixed number of O(k^2) mixed gradient steps, combining the full gradient and stochastic gradients. By averaging the iterates within an epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant decrease in suboptimality, independent of the condition number. A self-contained proof of the convergence rate is provided, but no experiments are included.

**Evaluation Summary**

**Pros:**

* The paper presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theory.
* EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization, similar to SAG, but is more general (works for constrained optimization) and has a simpler proof.
* The paper is clearly written, and the proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**

* The lack of experimental results makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the algorithm.
* The discussion of limitations and drawbacks is incomplete, particularly in comparison to existing algorithms.
* The proof could benefit from high-level comments to justify the essential insights used in its construction.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, but the absence of experiments is a notable limitation. The authors should extend Section 3.4 to discuss the limitations and drawbacks of EMGD, including its comparison to other algorithms. A more concrete discussion of the sum of n functions example would also be beneficial in highlighting the differences between EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov.

A significant drawback of EMGD is the need to fix the number of steps within an epoch in advance, which may not be practical. In contrast, SCDA and SAG do not require this, allowing for faster practical convergence. Additionally, SCDA has automatic step-size selection, and SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic, which may be advantageous in practice.

**Clarity**

The paper is fairly clear, with a useful summary in Table 1. However, the proof could be improved with high-level comments to motivate the defined quantities and make the construction more intuitive.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel combination of known techniques, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Significance**

While the practical relevance of the algorithm is not demonstrated, the theoretical contribution has the potential to impact the field. The simple proof of EMGD is a major contribution, particularly in comparison to the complex proof of SAG.
