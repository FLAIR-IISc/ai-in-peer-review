The paper presents a “hybrid” deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization. The algorithm combines calls to a deterministic oracle (querying full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (querying stochastic gradients). The authors demonstrate that this approach can eliminate the dependency on the condition number in the convergence rate.

The authors present an interesting and significant theoretical result. Specifically, assuming the condition number is known (i.e., both strong convexity and strong smoothness constants of the objective are known), and a first-order hybrid oracle (allowing queries of both stochastic and deterministic gradients) is available, a suitably chosen mixture of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps can achieve, with high probability, an O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization. 

However, there are several concerns with the current state of the paper. First, the theoretical analysis only considers the case where the condition number is perfectly known *a priori* and does not address the algorithm’s behavior when this hyperparameter is misspecified. The current result essentially states that the dependence on the condition number (κ) can be removed from the convergence rate of a first-order optimization algorithm, but only when κ is known in advance. While analyzing the case of an unknown κ may be challenging, it could have been investigated empirically. The paper’s second major concern is the complete absence of an experimental section. Given that the main contribution is a novel algorithm, it would be reasonable to include experiments to validate the theoretical results and assess their practical relevance.

Detailed comments:

The proposed algorithm (EGD) relies on the update rule defined by Eq. 8 (page 4), which uses the “mixed gradient” defined in Eq. 7. Consequently, EGD requires η to be set (or estimated). Setting η necessitates knowing the condition number κ, which requires knowing both the strong convexity modulus λ and the strong smoothness parameter L. As far as I understand, the authors do not provide any guidance or theoretical justification for determining κ beforehand.

This leads to two possibilities. Either the parameter must be estimated, in which case an estimation procedure is missing (even a heuristic supported by numerical experiments would be valuable), or the parameter is assumed known, implying the paper’s focus is purely theoretical. If the latter is true, the theoretical analysis or an experimental section should address scenarios where the hyperparameter is misspecified. This includes studying the convergence rate when the hyperparameter is set too high or too low.

While the smooth and strongly convex case is commonly used in theoretical analysis and realistic in many situations, it can be restrictive. Other settings, such as non-strongly convex problems, are also of interest. The non-strongly convex case is particularly important as it arises frequently in practice; see Bach & Moulines (2011) for a discussion of behavior in different convexity regimes.

Further concerns exist. Blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not a novel idea in first-order optimization. Moreover, the presented algorithm isn’t formulated as an alternation of deterministic and stochastic steps with varying frequencies. Instead, it uses a single “mixed” update per iteration (within an epoch), followed by a gradient-like update step. It would be beneficial to discuss how the proposed algorithm relates to algorithms that explicitly interleave deterministic and stochastic gradient steps, particularly in the unconstrained case.

The authors do not review related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms; see [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012]. Discussing and comparing convergence rates would add value. Also, the cited reference provides a review of older work in this area.

Finally, a thorough experimental study would significantly strengthen the paper. This should include a detailed comparison with standard SGD, averaged SGD, and recent stochastic first-order optimization methods like SAG, etc. 

A minor point: the optimization setting is not clearly defined. The paper aims to combine the advantages of deterministic and stochastic optimization—namely, the exponential convergence rate of deterministic methods and the condition number independence of stochastic methods. However, the authors don't clearly specify whether they are solving the deterministic optimization problem [Min<sub>w</sub> F(w) = 1/n Σ<sub>i=1</sub><sup>n</sup> F<sub>i</sub>(w)] with a randomized algorithm, or the stochastic approximation problem [Min<sub>w</sub> E(F(w))]. Note that both SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for solving the deterministic problem. The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is somewhat misleading in this regard, and the subsequent claims do not clarify which setting is being considered. This could be easily addressed.
