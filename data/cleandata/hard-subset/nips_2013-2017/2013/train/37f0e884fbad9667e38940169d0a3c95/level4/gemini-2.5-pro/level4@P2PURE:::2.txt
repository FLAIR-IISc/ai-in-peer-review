This paper introduces a hybrid first-order optimization method that combines deterministic (full gradient) and stochastic oracle calls. The authors present an interesting and significant theoretical result: for smooth and strongly convex objectives, this approach can achieve a convergence rate of O(log(1/epsilon)) that is independent of the problem's condition number.

However, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses in its current form.

First and foremost, the entire theoretical analysis is contingent upon knowing the exact condition number ($\kappa$) of the objective function in advance. The proposed algorithm (EGD) requires a hyperparameter ($\eta$, Eq. 8) that is directly determined by $\kappa$. The paper provides no guidance on how to set this parameter if the strong convexity and smoothness constants are unknown, nor does it analyze the algorithm's performance when this crucial hyperparameter is misspecified. In essence, the result claims that the dependence on the condition number can be eliminated from the convergence rate, but only if the condition number is provided as an input to the algorithm. While a theoretical analysis of the misspecified case may be challenging, its impact should have been investigated empirically.

This leads to the second major concern: the complete absence of an experimental section. As the primary contribution is a new algorithm, empirical validation is essential to support the theoretical claims and understand its practical behavior. An experimental study would be the natural place to investigate the algorithm's sensitivity to the choice of $\kappa$ and compare its performance against established methods. A thorough study should include comparisons with standard baselines like SGD and averaged SGD, as well as more recent algorithms like SAG.

Further detailed comments are as follows:

*   **Problem Setting and Assumptions:** The optimization setting is not clearly defined. It is ambiguous whether the goal is to solve the finite-sum deterministic problem (min 1/n \sum F_i(w)), for which randomized algorithms like SAG and SDCA exist, or the stochastic approximation problem (min E[F(w)]). This distinction is important for context and comparison, and the theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is misleading in this regard. Additionally, the analysis is confined to the smooth and strongly convex setting, which can be restrictive. An analysis of the non-strongly convex case, which is also prevalent in practice (e.g., Bach & Moulines, 2011), would significantly strengthen the paper.

*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The concept of combining deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not new. The paper fails to discuss a significant body of related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods, such as Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC, 2012). A discussion and comparison of convergence rates with this line of work is necessary. Furthermore, the proposed algorithm formulates the combination as a single "mixed" update at each iteration. It would be beneficial to discuss how this approach relates to and compares with simpler strategies that interleave distinct full-gradient and stochastic-gradient steps.
