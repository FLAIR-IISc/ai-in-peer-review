- Problem and contribution: Proposes EMGD, a hybrid algorithm combining occasional full gradients with many cheap stochastic gradients to optimize smooth, strongly convex functions with convex constraints.  
- Main theoretical claim: EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of condition number k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, achieving linear convergence.  
- Core idea: Replace each full-gradient epoch by O(k^2) mixed steps that use one full gradient plus varying stochastic gradients, and average iterates to obtain k-independent epoch progress.  
- Novelty and interest: The hybrid deterministic–stochastic approach is novel, interesting, and builds on recent work leveraging cheap stochastic oracles.  
- Proof quality: Provides a simple, self-contained, and rigorous convergence proof with high-probability guarantees.  
- Generality advantage: EMGD applies to constrained optimization (with efficient gradient mapping), unlike SAG which is currently for unconstrained problems.  
- Simplicity advantage: Convergence proof is significantly simpler than SAG’s, potentially enabling easier modifications and insights.  
- Comparative complexity: EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov when k ≤ n^{2/3}; EMGD and SAG match for k = n^{1/2}; SAG outperforms EMGD in many regimes (especially when k between n^{1/2} and n/8).  
- Practical caveat on regimes: In typical regularization settings (e.g., regularization ~ C/n with k ≈ n/C), EMGD may not outperform SAG or Nesterov—this should be discussed.  
- Missing experiments: The paper lacks experimental validation to demonstrate practical relevance and illustrate theoretical advantages.  
- Insufficient limitations discussion: Section 3.4 should be expanded to address drawbacks and comparisons to existing methods (e.g., SCDA, SAG).  
- Fixed epoch-length drawback: EMGD requires pre-specifying the number of steps per epoch based on known k, which is impractical compared to methods without this requirement.  
- Adaptive/heuristic methods: SCDA and SAG offer adaptive step-size or heuristics that can yield better practical convergence; EMGD lacks such mechanisms and should be compared.  
- Parallelization/memory claims: Claims about EMGD’s parallelization and memory advantages over SAG are unclear—SAG can use mini-batches and be parallelized as well.  
- Missing citation: Should cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods and discuss differences.  
- Proof exposition: Proof would benefit from higher-level comments and motivation for key quantities, which currently appear ad hoc.  
- Presentation and clarity: Paper is generally clear and well-written; Table 1 is commended.  
- Minor corrections: Multiple wording, notation, and typo fixes are suggested (specific line edits provided in review).  
- Overall recommendation: Theoretical contribution is substantial and publishable if experiments are added and limitations/discussion are expanded as suggested.
