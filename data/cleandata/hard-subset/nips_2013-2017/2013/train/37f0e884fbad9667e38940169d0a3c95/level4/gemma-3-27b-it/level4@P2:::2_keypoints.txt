- Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method mixing full and stochastic gradients to remove condition-number dependence in convergence.
- Theoretical result: EMGD attains O(log(1/ε)) convergence assuming known condition number and access to both full and stochastic gradients.
- Core idea of strategically mixing deterministic and stochastic updates is compelling and well motivated for expensive full-gradient scenarios.
- Theoretical analysis is strong and provides a solid foundation for the claimed convergence guarantees.
- Major weakness: no empirical validation—absence of experiments prevents assessment of practical performance and comparisons to SGD, SAG, SDCA.
- Strong assumption that the condition number κ is known a priori; paper does not analyze behavior under estimation or misspecification.
- Lacks discussion or strategies for estimating κ or robustness analysis to inaccurate κ, which limits practical applicability.
- Related work coverage is limited—insufficient comparison to hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012).
- Ambiguity in problem formulation: unclear whether the setting is deterministic optimization using stochastic algorithms or true stochastic approximation.
- Section 3.1’s theoretical setup is somewhat unclear and needs clarification of the precise optimization setting.
- Algorithm depends on step-size η tied to κ; no practical guidance or heuristics for choosing η are provided.
- Paper focuses only on smooth, strongly convex problems; exploring non-strongly convex or less restrictive settings would broaden impact.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty in reducing condition-number dependence, theoretical soundness, and potential significance for ill-conditioned large-scale problems.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing experiments, reliance on known κ, limited related-work discussion, and ambiguous optimization setting.
- Overall recommendation: weak reject, with expectation authors add empirical evaluation, robustness to κ misspecification, clearer problem formulation, and fuller related-work comparison.
