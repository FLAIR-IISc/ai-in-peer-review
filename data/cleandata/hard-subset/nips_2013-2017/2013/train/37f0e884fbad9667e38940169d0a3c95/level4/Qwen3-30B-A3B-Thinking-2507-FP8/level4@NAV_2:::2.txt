

The paper introduces a novel optimization algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors demonstrate that by utilizing stochastic gradients, the number of full gradient evaluations required can be reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. This represents a significant theoretical advance over traditional first-order methods that rely solely on full gradients.

The paper's main strength lies in its elegant theoretical analysis showing how the condition number dependence can be eliminated through a hybrid approach, which is both theoretically sound and potentially valuable for practical optimization. The algorithm design is clever, particularly the mixed gradient update rule that effectively combines deterministic and stochastic components.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need addressing. First, the theoretical analysis assumes perfect knowledge of the condition number κ (i.e., both the strong convexity parameter λ and smoothness parameter L), which is unrealistic in practice. The authors provide no discussion of how to estimate κ or analyze the algorithm's behavior when this parameter is misspecified. This is a critical omission, as the algorithm's performance is highly sensitive to this hyperparameter.

Second, the paper completely lacks experimental validation, which is particularly concerning given that the main contribution is a new algorithm. Without empirical results, it's impossible to assess how well the theoretical guarantees translate to practical performance. The authors should at minimum include experiments comparing EMGD with standard methods like SGD, SAG, and SDCA to validate their theoretical claims.

Third, the paper fails to adequately contextualize its contribution within the existing literature on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization. The authors should discuss and compare their approach with Friedlander and Schmidt's 2012 SISC paper on hybrid methods, which addresses similar problems and might have related algorithms. This would strengthen the paper's positioning in the literature.

Fourth, the paper's theoretical setup is confusingly presented. The authors do not clearly specify whether they are solving a deterministic optimization problem (min_w F(w) = 1/n ∑_i=1^n f_i(w)) using a randomized algorithm or a stochastic approximation problem (min_w E[F(w)]). This ambiguity undermines the paper's theoretical claims, as SAG and SDCA are specifically designed for the former problem setting.

Finally, the paper does not address the more general case of non-strongly convex optimization, which is important as many practical problems fall into this category. The authors should at least acknowledge this limitation and discuss how their approach might be extended to broader settings.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is interesting but its practical relevance is questionable without experimental validation and without addressing the condition number estimation problem. The authors should either provide a thorough experimental evaluation or substantially strengthen the theoretical discussion of parameter sensitivity to make the paper suitable for publication at NIPS.
