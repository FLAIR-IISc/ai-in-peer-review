The paper presents a "hybrid" deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, which combines calls to a deterministic oracle (querying full gradients) and stochastic oracle (querying stochastic gradients). The authors demonstrate that this approach allows for the removal of the dependency on the condition number in the rate of convergence.

The authors present an interesting and striking theoretical result. Assuming the condition number is known (i.e., both strong convexity and strong smoothness constants of the objective are known), and assuming a first-order hybrid oracle (both stochastic and deterministic gradient can be queried) is available, they show that with a suitably chosen mix of deterministic gradient and stochastic gradient steps, one can achieve a O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization with high probability.

However, there are several concerns with the current state of the paper. Firstly, the theoretical analysis only covers the case where the condition number is perfectly known beforehand and does not address the behavior of the algorithm when this hyper-parameter is misspecified. The current result states that the dependence on the condition number (kappa) can be removed from the convergence rate of a first-order optimization algorithm when this condition number is known beforehand to the algorithm. It is possible that the theoretical analysis for the cases where kappa is unknown is challenging, but if so, this analysis could have been conducted through experiments. However, the paper lacks an experiments section, which is the second major concern. Given that the main contribution of the paper is a new algorithm, it would be beneficial to include some experiments to assess the theoretical results presented in the paper and study their relevance to the actual behavior of the algorithm on empirical data.

Detailed comments:

The proposed algorithm (EGD) relies on the update rule defined by Eq. 8 (page 4) using the so-called "mixed gradient" defined in Eq. 7. Therefore, EGD requires the hyper-parameter eta to be set (or estimated). Setting eta boils down to knowing the condition number kappa (referred to as "conditional number" in the paper), which comprises both the "strong convexity modulus" lambda and the "strong smoothness modulus" L. However, the authors do not provide any guidelines or theoretical arguments for setting kappa beforehand.

There are two possibilities: either this parameter has to be estimated, in which case the corresponding estimation procedure is missing (even a heuristic procedure supported by numerical experiments would be acceptable), or this parameter is assumed known because the point of the paper is mainly theoretical. However, if the latter is the case, then the theoretical analysis/experimental section should cover the cases where this hyper-parameter is misspecified, including studying the convergence rate when the hyper-parameter is set too large or too small.

Although the smooth and strongly convex case is popular in theoretical analysis and realistic in many situations, it can be too restrictive, and other settings (non-strongly convex) are also interesting. In particular, the non-strongly convex case is important, as it also arises in several situations. See Bach & Moulines (2011) for a theoretical analysis of the different behaviors depending on the cases (convex vs strongly convex).

There are other concerns. Blending deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps in a first-order optimization algorithm is not a new idea. In fact, the algorithm presented in the paper is not written as an alternation of deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps with different frequencies for each type of steps. Instead, it is written as one "mixed" update per iteration (within an epoch), followed by a gradient-like update step. It would be interesting to discuss how the proposed algorithm relates to and compares with a similar-in-spirit algorithm that interleaves deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps (at least in the "unconstrained" case).

The authors do not review a related line of work, namely hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms; see Friedlander and Schmidt (2012) for an example. Discussing and comparing the convergence rates would be valuable. See also the above reference for a review of older works on this topic.

Finally, a thorough experimental study would be a valuable addition to the paper, including a detailed comparison with regular SGD, averaged SGD, and recent proposals for stochastic first-order optimization (SAG, etc.).

A more minor concern is that the optimization setting considered in the paper is not clearly stated. The purpose of the paper is to combine the benefits of both deterministic optimization and stochastic optimization, namely exponential rate of convergence from the deterministic world and dependence on the condition number from the stochastic world. However, the authors do not specify clearly what they intend to solve: the deterministic optimization problem [Min w F(w) = 1/n \sum{i=1}^n F_i(w)] with a "stochastic" (or more precisely, "randomized") algorithm, or the stochastic approximation problem [Min w E(F(w))]. Note that both SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for solving the deterministic optimization problem [Min w F(w) = 1/n \sum{i=1}^n F_i(w)]. The theoretical setup stated in Section 3.1 is misleading in this respect, and none of the claims made later in the paper clarifies which setting is considered. This could easily be fixed.
