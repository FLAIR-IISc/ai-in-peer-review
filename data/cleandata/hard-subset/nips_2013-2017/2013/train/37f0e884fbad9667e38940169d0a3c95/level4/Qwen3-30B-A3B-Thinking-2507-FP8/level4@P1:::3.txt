

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with reduced dependence on the condition number κ. The authors demonstrate that EMGD requires only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to achieve an ε-optimal solution, significantly improving upon the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity of full gradient methods. The work builds on recent advances in stochastic optimization [9, 10, 22, 23], particularly addressing the limitation that full gradient methods become computationally expensive for ill-conditioned problems.

The paper makes a strong technical contribution with a well-structured theoretical analysis. The convergence proof is rigorous, and the algorithm's design is clearly motivated by the need to balance computational cost per iteration with the number of iterations. The authors properly contextualize their work against existing methods, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent [17] and stochastic methods like SAG [22] and SDCA [23]. The technical arguments are sound, with the main theorem (Theorem 1) correctly establishing the algorithm's convergence properties with high probability.

The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical novelty in achieving condition number-independent access to full gradients while maintaining linear convergence. The algorithm's design is elegant, particularly the mixed gradient descent step that properly balances deterministic and stochastic components. The authors provide thorough comparisons with related work, including computational complexity analysis that demonstrates EMGD's advantages in specific regimes (κ ≤ n^(2/3)).

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's relevance to the machine learning community, which is the focus of NIPS. The authors' claim that EMGD "reduces the number of full gradients required from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε))" is technically correct, but the practical significance requires careful justification for machine learning applications. In standard machine learning problems, strong convexity typically arises from regularization (e.g., λ = τ, where τ is the regularization parameter), making λ independent of the number of samples N. This means κ = L/λ is proportional to the condition number of the data matrix, which in practice is often large (κ = O(N) for typical problems).

Under this standard ML setting, EMGD's computational advantage over batch gradient descent only holds when κ ≤ O(n^(2/3)), which corresponds to the case where the regularization parameter τ is sufficiently large (τ = Ω(n^(-2/3))). This is a very restrictive regime in practical machine learning applications, where τ is typically small to avoid over-regularization. The paper fails to adequately discuss this limitation or provide empirical evidence demonstrating EMGD's practical advantage in realistic ML settings.

Another important concern is that the paper's convergence guarantee holds with high probability, while standard batch gradient descent provides deterministic convergence rates. While high-probability guarantees are theoretically stronger, they may be less practically useful than deterministic guarantees in many ML applications where practitioners prefer predictable behavior.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with clear algorithm presentation and well-organized sections. However, some minor issues need addressing: equation (6) should use ||w* - ŵ||² instead of ||w* - ŵ||; the reference to equation (7) in line 288 should be clarified; the equality in line 286 adds no value; absolute values in equation (13) should be norms; and the relationship between L and λ should be explicitly stated earlier. The condition number definition should be precisely specified as κ = L/λ.

The main weakness of the paper is its limited practical relevance for machine learning problems. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the authors do not sufficiently demonstrate why their approach would be preferred over existing methods in realistic ML scenarios. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical regime where EMGD outperforms standard batch gradient descent, including empirical validation on real datasets.

To strengthen the paper's significance, the authors should:
1. More clearly articulate the practical regimes where EMGD is advantageous
2. Provide empirical results showing computational speedup in realistic ML settings
3. Discuss how their high-probability guarantee compares to the practical requirements of machine learning practitioners

In conclusion, while the paper presents a technically sound algorithm with interesting theoretical properties, its significance for the NIPS community is limited by the narrow practical regime where it outperforms standard methods. The theoretical contribution is novel and correct, but the paper would benefit from stronger justification of its relevance to machine learning applications, which is the primary focus of NIPS. The authors should address these concerns before the paper can be considered a strong contribution to the field.
