- Problem & claim: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) to get linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations (κ-independent) plus O(poly(κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects to finite-sum variance-reduction methods (SAG/SDCA) and classical accelerated methods, highlighting differences in assumptions and costs.  
- Strength — novelty: Introduces a clean mixed-gradient update using anchoring and shrinking feasible balls to reduce variance.  
- Strength — theory: Presents a simple, self-contained proof with high-probability linear convergence and clear oracle assumptions.  
- Strength — scope: Handles constrained problems and uses only O(d) memory without storing per-example gradients.  
- Weakness — experiments: No empirical results to demonstrate practical competitiveness or runtime behavior.  
- Weakness — parameter dependence: Requires knowledge of κ to set epoch length and step size, limiting practicality compared to adaptive methods like SAG/SDCA.  
- Weakness — finite-sum complexity: Asymptotically slower than SAG/SDCA in many regimes (EMGD beneficial roughly up to κ ≲ n^{2/3}; SAG/SDCA often better when κ ≳ n^{1/2}).  
- Weakness — overclaims and inconsistency: Some comparative claims about distributed/memory efficiency are too strong; inconsistency between abstract (O(κ log(1/ε))) and main text (O(κ^2 log(1/ε))) for stochastic-gradient complexity.  
- Technical evaluation: Analysis appears sound and the high-probability guarantee is valuable, but practical issues (unknown κ, projection costs, κ^2 factor) need fuller discussion.  
- Clarity: Well written overall, but proof would benefit from a short high-level intuition and reconciled assumptions (e.g., ∇F(w)=E[∇f(w)] stated explicitly).  
- Originality: Novel hybrid use of full and stochastic gradients, but should cite related hybrids such as Friedlander & Schmidt (2012).  
- Significance: Theoretical interest in constrained, low-memory linear-rate methods; practical impact uncertain without experiments.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Clear novel idea, simple high-probability analysis, broader applicability and low memory usage.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Lacks empirical validation, needs κ knowledge, and is inferior to SAG/SDCA in key finite-sum regimes.  
- Suggestions for improvement: Add quantitative finite-sum comparisons (with n-vs-κ thresholds), propose adaptive/doubling schemes for unknown κ, clarify projection costs, correct κ vs κ^2 claims, add intuition paragraph, and cite related hybrid work.  
- Minor edits: Fix typos, notation inconsistencies, and explicitly state smoothness/expectation assumptions.  
- Overall recommendation & scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 7/10; recommend acceptance conditional on stronger comparisons, resolving κ discrepancy, and adding empirical illustration.
