This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to accelerate convex optimization. The core idea is to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ typically present in the iteration complexity of full-gradient methods. The authors demonstrate that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The theoretical analysis appears sound and the proposed algorithm presents a potentially interesting trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, algorithm details, and theoretical results. The connection to existing work in deterministic and stochastic optimization is adequately established, referencing relevant literature such as Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent [16, 18], stochastic average gradient (SAG) [22], and stochastic dual coordinate ascent (SDCA) [23]. The inclusion of a discussion regarding computational complexity compared to other algorithms (Table 2) is helpful.

However, a significant weakness is the limited discussion of the algorithm’s practical relevance, particularly within the context of machine learning, which is a primary focus of NIPS. The authors acknowledge that strong convexity often arises from regularization in machine learning settings, leading to a condition number dependent on the number of samples (κ ~ O(N)). They rightly point out that EMGD offers an advantage over batch gradient descent only when N is sufficiently small (bounded by O(C³/L³)). This severely restricts the algorithm's applicability to large-scale machine learning problems. The authors need to more thoroughly explore this limitation and discuss potential scenarios where EMGD might still be beneficial – for example, problems with specific data structures or when full gradient computation is exceptionally expensive compared to stochastic gradients.  Furthermore, the probabilistic convergence guarantee of EMGD, while strong, is less desirable than the deterministic convergence of standard batch gradient descent, particularly when the cost of ensuring high probability is substantial. This trade-off requires further justification.

Several minor points could enhance clarity:

*   Equation (6) should read ||w* - ŵ||².
*   Line 288: Clarify which function (7) refers to.
*   Line 286: Removing the equality sign adds to readability.
*   In (13), replace absolute values with norms.
*   Explicitly state the relationship L ≥ λ for completeness.
*   In (4), replace 'x^' with 'w^' and 'f' with 'F'. Note the assertion that the first term in the max is always bigger than the second is incorrect and relies on the specific context of strong convexity, which should be explicitly stated.
*   Clearly define the condition number, κ = L/λ, upfront.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The algorithm is novel and theoretically well-supported.
*   The paper presents a potentially interesting trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations.
*   The analysis is rigorous and the convergence proof appears correct.
*   The paper acknowledges limitations and suggests future research directions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The algorithm’s practical relevance to large-scale machine learning is questionable due to the requirement of a small number of samples for significant performance gains.
*   The probabilistic convergence guarantee is less desirable than the deterministic convergence of batch gradient descent.
*   The limited discussion of scenarios where EMGD would outperform existing methods weakens the argument for its significance.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound algorithm with a rigorous theoretical analysis. However, the limited discussion of its practical relevance and the unfavorable scaling behavior for large datasets raise concerns about its impact on the machine learning community. While the work is not without merit, its current form does not fully justify acceptance to NIPS. I recommend **weak reject**, and suggest the authors significantly expand the discussion of practical implications and potential use cases before resubmission. A more convincing demonstration of EMGD's advantages in specific scenarios is crucial for elevating the paper's significance.
