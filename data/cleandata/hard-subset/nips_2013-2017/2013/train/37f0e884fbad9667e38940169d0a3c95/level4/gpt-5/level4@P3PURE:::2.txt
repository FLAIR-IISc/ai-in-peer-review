Summary
The paper proposes a hybrid deterministic–stochastic first-order optimization method that alternates access to two oracles: a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). The main theoretical claim is striking: if the condition number κ is known (i.e., both the strong convexity parameter λ and the smoothness constant L are known) and one can query both oracles, then with a suitable mixture of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps the method achieves a high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate for batch optimization, while removing the dependence on κ from the convergence rate.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing hybridization that aims to combine exponential convergence from deterministic methods with the favorable data-scaling properties of stochastic methods.
- A clean and surprising theoretical result showing O(log(1/ε)) convergence without explicit κ-dependence in the rate, under the assumption that κ is known.

Major concerns
1) Dependence on known condition number and lack of analysis under misspecification
- The theory assumes κ is known exactly. As far as I can tell, the paper does not provide guidance on how to set κ (or, equivalently, η) in practice, nor does it analyze the algorithm when κ is misspecified.
- The current result effectively says that the dependence on κ can be removed from the rate when κ is available to the algorithm a priori. This is valuable theoretically, but leaves a critical gap for practical use.
- Two possibilities should be addressed:
  a) If κ must be estimated, the paper should provide at least a heuristic estimation procedure, supported by numerical experiments.
  b) If the contribution is primarily theoretical and κ is assumed known, the analysis (and/or experiments) should cover robustness to misspecification, e.g., what happens when κ is set too large or too small. Studying the resulting convergence rate under over- or under-estimation is important for understanding the method’s behavior.

2) No experimental evaluation
- The paper lacks an experiments section. Given that the main contribution is a new algorithm, empirical results are necessary to validate the theory and assess practical relevance.
- A thorough empirical study should compare against standard baselines (SGD, averaged SGD) and recent stochastic first-order methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA), with attention to sensitivity to κ (or η), wall-clock time, and sample complexity.

3) Clarity on the optimization setting (finite-sum vs. stochastic approximation)
- It is not clearly stated whether the target is the finite-sum deterministic problem min_w F(w) = (1/n) ∑_{i=1}^n F_i(w), solved with a randomized algorithm, or the stochastic approximation problem min_w E[F(w)]. Section 3.1 is ambiguous in this respect, and later claims do not unequivocally fix the setting.
- This distinction matters for interpreting the O(log(1/ε)) rate, the oracle models, and comparisons to related methods.
- Note that both SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for the finite-sum setting. The paper should explicitly state which setting it addresses and align assumptions and rates accordingly.

4) Relation to prior hybrid methods and algorithmic form
- Blending deterministic and stochastic gradient information is not new. The paper should discuss its relation to “hybrid deterministic–stochastic” methods, in particular Friedlander and Schmidt, SISC 2012, which also surveys earlier work. A comparison of assumptions and convergence rates would be valuable.
- The proposed algorithm (EGD) is written as a per-iteration “mixed gradient” (within an epoch) followed by a gradient-like update, rather than an explicit alternation between deterministic and stochastic steps with specified frequencies. It would be helpful to relate EGD to an alternation scheme (especially in the unconstrained case), clarify similarities/differences, and, if possible, compare convergence behavior and oracle costs.

5) Restrictive assumptions: strong convexity and smoothness
- While common in theory and sometimes realistic, the smooth and strongly convex setting is restrictive. The non-strongly convex case is also important in practice.
- It would strengthen the paper to discuss (or extend to) the merely convex case and to relate to known behaviors in the convex vs. strongly convex regimes (e.g., Bach & Moulines, 2011).

Detailed comments
- The proposed algorithm (EGD) relies on the update rule in Eq. 8, using the “mixed gradient” of Eq. 7. This requires setting the step-size-like parameter η, which in turn depends on κ. The paper currently provides no guideline or theoretical argument for setting κ (referred to as the “conditional number” in the text) in practice. Moreover, the text twice refers to “strong convexity modulus” for both λ and L; presumably L is the smoothness (L-Lipschitz gradient) constant.
- As noted above, either:
  a) Provide an estimation or tuning procedure for η/κ (even a heuristic), supported by experiments; or
  b) If κ is assumed known, analyze the algorithm’s robustness to misspecification (too large vs. too small) and quantify the impact on convergence.
- The algorithm’s design as a mixed update per iteration (within epochs) invites a comparison to architectures that interleave explicit deterministic and stochastic steps. Clarifying the relationship could also help position the method among hybrid approaches (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012).
- The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 should be revised to unambiguously specify whether the goal is finite-sum minimization or stochastic approximation, especially since the claims aim to combine exponential convergence (from deterministic finite-sum methods) with favorable κ-dependence (often associated with stochastic methods).
- An empirical section would substantially improve the paper. Suggested comparisons include:
  - Regular SGD and averaged SGD
  - Recent stochastic first-order methods for finite sums (SAG, SDCA)
  - Ablations on the frequency or weight of deterministic vs. stochastic information, and sensitivity to η/κ
  - Tests in both strongly convex and merely convex settings

Conclusion
The paper presents an interesting hybrid algorithm with a notable theoretical guarantee: high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence for batch optimization without κ in the convergence rate, provided κ is known and both oracles are available. However, the current version has significant gaps: it assumes perfect knowledge of κ without analyzing misspecification, lacks empirical validation, does not clearly specify the optimization setting, and omits discussion of closely related hybrid methods. Addressing these issues—particularly adding experiments, clarifying the setting, providing guidance or robustness analysis for κ, and situating the work relative to prior hybrid approaches—would substantially strengthen the contribution.
