- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): epochs with one full gradient plus T mixed stochastic updates and a shrinking trust region, claiming high-probability linear convergence.  
- Claims O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and either O(κ log(1/ε)) or O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations (discrepancy needs clarification).  
- Targets strongly convex, smooth problems and positions itself against accelerated full-gradient methods (O(√κ log(1/ε))) and finite-sum variance-reduced methods (SAG/SDCA).  
- EMGD is a hybrid deterministic–stochastic method similar in spirit to prior interleaving approaches but uses a single full gradient per epoch and shrinking-ball constraints.  
- Strength: clear theoretical result—high-probability linear convergence with few full-gradient calls.  
- Strength: conceptually simple mixed-gradient correction that leverages smoothness to control variance.  
- Strength: applicable to constrained problems and has low storage requirements (no per-example gradient memory).  
- Major concern: algorithm and proof require knowing L and λ (hence κ); no procedure or analysis for estimating κ or robustness to misspecification.  
- Major concern: no experiments to validate theory, test parameter sensitivity, or compare wall-clock performance to baselines.  
- Major concern: insufficient comparison to prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods and lack of clarity on novelty vs existing interleaving schemes.  
- Major concern: ambiguity in problem setting—unclear if results target expectation-minimization or finite-sum optimization, which affects comparisons.  
- Major concern: analysis limited to strongly convex objectives; no discussion of extensions or limitations for merely convex cases.  
- Suggestion: provide adaptive or practical rules for setting η, T, and epoch radius without knowing L and λ (e.g., backtracking, doubling/halving, adaptive schemes).  
- Suggestion: analyze or empirically study robustness to κ misspecification (over- and under-estimation).  
- Suggestion: add experiments on synthetic and real data, comparing to accelerated GD, SGD/ASGD, SAG, SDCA, and simple interleaving, reporting iterations and wall-clock time.  
- Suggestion: clarify the optimization model up front (expectation vs finite-sum) and align oracle/comparisons accordingly.  
- Suggestion: expand discussion of related hybrid and variance-reduction methods and explicitly explain EMGD’s differences (single full gradient per epoch, high-probability bounds, constrained setting).  
- Minor: fix inconsistent statements about stochastic-gradient complexity (κ vs κ^2) across abstract and body.  
- Minor: correct terminology/typos (e.g., “condition number” not “conditional number”).  
- Minor: comment on cost of projection onto W ∩ B(·; ·) and whether it can be avoided or approximated for unconstrained problems.  
- Pro for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution with reduced reliance on frequent full gradients and high-probability linear convergence.  
- Pro for acceptance: simple algorithm with potential practical value for constrained problems and low memory use.  
- Con for acceptance: reliance on known κ without practical estimation or robustness analysis.  
- Con for acceptance: lack of experiments to support practical benefits and sensitivity.  
- Con for acceptance: inadequate positioning relative to existing hybrid and finite-sum variance-reduced methods.  
- Con for acceptance: ambiguity about whether the primary problem setting is finite-sum or expectation minimization.
