The paper introduces a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining full-gradient and stochastic-gradient oracles. The authors demonstrate that this approach can eliminate the dependence on the condition number from the convergence rate.

This work presents a compelling theoretical contribution: under the assumption of a known condition number and the availability of a first-order hybrid oracle, a carefully balanced combination of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps yields a high-probability O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization. 

However, the paper faces several significant shortcomings. The theoretical analysis is limited to scenarios where the condition number is perfectly known *a priori*, and fails to address the algorithm’s performance when this parameter is misspecified. The current result demonstrates the removal of condition number dependence only when the number is known. While analyzing the unknown condition number case may be difficult, experimental validation would be valuable.  Crucially, the paper lacks an experimental section altogether. Given the introduction of a novel algorithm, empirical evaluation is essential to assess the theoretical findings and demonstrate their practical relevance.

Specifically, the proposed algorithm (EGD), defined by Eq. 8 and utilizing the “mixed gradient” in Eq. 7, requires careful tuning of the hyperparameter η, which directly relates to the condition number κ (comprising the strong convexity modulus λ and the strong smoothness parameter L). The paper provides no guidance or theoretical basis for setting κ beforehand.  This necessitates either a parameter estimation procedure (which is absent) or a clarification that the analysis focuses solely on the known-κ case. If the latter, the analysis should be extended to consider the impact of parameter misspecification, examining convergence rates with overly large or small values of η.

While the smooth and strongly convex setting is common in theoretical work, exploring other scenarios, such as the non-strongly convex case, would broaden the paper's appeal and applicability.

Furthermore, blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not entirely novel. The current implementation, using a single "mixed" update per iteration, differs from algorithms that explicitly interleave deterministic and stochastic steps. A discussion comparing the proposed approach to such alternatives would be beneficial. 

The paper omits a review of relevant prior work, specifically hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms [Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC, 2012]. Comparing convergence rates with existing methods would strengthen the contribution.  A comprehensive experimental study, comparing the algorithm to standard SGD, averaged SGD, and state-of-the-art methods like SAG, would also be a significant improvement.

Finally, the optimization setting—deterministic optimization with a randomized algorithm versus stochastic approximation—is not clearly defined. This ambiguity is reflected in Section 3.1 and remains unclear throughout the paper. Clarifying this distinction would improve the presentation.
