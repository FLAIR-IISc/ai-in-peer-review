The paper presents a novel first-order optimization method that combines deterministic and stochastic gradient updates to achieve a convergence rate independent of the condition number. Specifically, the authors introduce the Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm and demonstrate, theoretically, that it can achieve an O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate under the assumption of a known condition number and access to both full and stochastic gradients. This is a potentially significant result, as it bypasses the typical √κ dependence in the iteration complexity for strongly convex and smooth optimization problems.

The core idea – strategically mixing full and stochastic gradients – is compelling, and the theoretical analysis provides a strong foundation for the proposed method. The paper clearly articulates the benefits of this approach, particularly in scenarios where evaluating full gradients is computationally expensive. However, the paper currently suffers from a lack of empirical validation and a limited discussion of practical considerations.

A primary concern is the reliance on a known condition number. While the theoretical analysis elegantly handles the case where κ is known *a priori*, it doesn’t address the algorithm’s behavior when this parameter is estimated or misspecified. This is a crucial limitation, as accurate condition number estimation can be challenging in practice. Exploring strategies for condition number estimation, or analyzing the algorithm’s robustness to inaccurate estimates, would significantly strengthen the work. Furthermore, the absence of an experimental section is a significant drawback. Given that the contribution is a new algorithm, empirical validation is essential to assess its practical performance and compare it to existing methods like SGD, SAG, and SDCA.

The paper’s presentation of existing work could also be improved. While the authors acknowledge related work on convex optimization and stochastic gradient methods, the discussion of hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithms, such as those presented in Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), is insufficient. A more thorough comparison of EMGD with these existing approaches, particularly in terms of convergence rates and practical considerations, would provide valuable context. Additionally, the authors briefly mention the distinction between solving a deterministic problem with a stochastic algorithm versus a stochastic approximation problem, but this point requires further clarification to precisely define the optimization setting. The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 feels somewhat ambiguous in this regard.

Several minor points warrant attention. The algorithm relies on a step size parameter η, directly linked to the condition number. Providing guidance on setting this parameter, even if heuristic, would be helpful. Moreover, the paper focuses on the smooth and strongly convex case. While justifiable for the theoretical analysis, exploring the algorithm’s behavior in less restrictive settings, such as non-strongly convex problems, would broaden its applicability and potential impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed algorithm offers a novel approach to reducing the dependence on the condition number in first-order optimization.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis provides a strong guarantee of convergence under specific conditions.
*   **Potential Significance:** The method has the potential to improve the efficiency of optimization algorithms for ill-conditioned problems, particularly in large-scale machine learning applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
*   **Strong Assumption of Known Condition Number:** The reliance on a known condition number limits the algorithm’s immediate applicability.
*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization methods.
*   **Ambiguous Optimization Setting:** The precise optimization setting being addressed (deterministic vs stochastic) is not clearly defined.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially significant contribution, the lack of experimental validation and the strong assumption of a known condition number raise concerns. I recommend **weak reject** with the expectation that the authors will address these issues through empirical evaluation, discussion of robustness to parameter misspecification, and a more thorough comparison with related work. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field.
