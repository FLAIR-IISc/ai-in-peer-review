SUMMARY: This paper addresses the optimization of smooth, strongly convex functions over a convex set, focusing on problems where the gradient mapping update is efficient to compute. While Nesterov's optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence on this problem class, its rate constant is dependent on the square root of the condition number, k. The authors investigate a setting where both a computationally expensive full gradient and an inexpensive stochastic gradient oracle are available. They introduce a hybrid algorithm that necessitates only O(log 1/eps) calls to the full gradient oracle—a quantity independent of the condition number—and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) calls to the stochastic oracle. In theory, this approach can be faster when the condition number is not excessively large. The central idea of their algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), is to structure the optimization into epochs. Each epoch, which would typically be a single full gradient step, is replaced by a fixed O(k^2) number of mixed gradient steps. These mixed steps leverage a single full gradient computed at the epoch's start, combined with numerous stochastic gradients calculated within the epoch. By averaging the O(k^2) iterates from an epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant, condition-number-independent reduction in suboptimality. This is the key reason why the total number of required full gradient computations is independent of k. The paper provides a simple, self-contained proof for its convergence rate but lacks experimental validation.

I found this to be a well-written and enjoyable paper. The concept of blending a few full gradient computations with a large number of inexpensive stochastic steps is both novel and compelling. This research situates itself within a recent line of work aiming to achieve linear convergence rates while primarily using a stochastic oracle. A significant recent advance, both theoretically and practically, was the SAG algorithm [16], designed for smooth, strongly convex objectives that are a sum of n simple functions (e.g., regularized empirical loss minimization, where n is the training set size). In this setting, it is reasonable to assume the full gradient is n times more costly than a stochastic one. SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov's algorithm when the condition number k ≤ n/8. In comparison, EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov's method when k ≤ n^(2/3), but it is slower than SAG in all regimes (having the same big-O complexity when k ≤ n^(1/2)). For context, if k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD have O(n log(1/eps)) complexity, while Nesterov's algorithm is O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)).

I see two primary advantages of EMGD over SAG. First, as the authors note, EMGD is applicable to constrained optimization problems (assuming an efficient gradient mapping update), whereas SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems. Second, EMGD's convergence proof is significantly simpler than SAG's, which could offer greater insight and make modifications to the algorithm more amenable to theoretical guarantees. The authors' result is also stronger, holding with high probability, in contrast to SAG's guarantee in expectation. A potential memory or parallelization benefit is mentioned, but this point is less compelling, as SAG can also be parallelized via mini-batches, which similarly reduces memory requirements.

EVALUATION SUMMARY:
Pros:
- Presents a novel and interesting algorithm with an elegant, simple, and theoretically sound foundation.
- For regularized empirical loss minimization, it achieves a linear convergence rate where the constant is not multiplied by the number of training examples n (similar to SAG), but it is more general (applicable to constrained optimization) and features a much simpler proof.
- The manuscript is clearly written, and the proof is presented in a self-contained manner.

Cons:
- The absence of experiments means the practical impact of the algorithm has not been demonstrated. No concrete examples are provided to illustrate scenarios where its theoretical advantages would be relevant.
- The paper lacks a discussion of the algorithm's limitations and drawbacks, particularly in comparison to existing methods. Section 3.4 requires significant improvement.
- The proof could benefit from high-level commentary to explain the key insights behind its construction.

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. While experiments would have strengthened the submission, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to stand on its own. However, it is essential that the authors expand Section 3.4 to include a thorough discussion of their algorithm's limitations and drawbacks. They should also incorporate a more concrete analysis of the "sum of n functions" example to clearly delineate the performance differences between their method, SAG, and Nesterov's algorithm (e.g., EMGD is worse than Nesterov for k > n^(2/3); SAG is equivalent to EMGD for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2), superior to EMGD for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8, and worse than Nesterov for k > n/8). A significant practical limitation of EMGD is the requirement to pre-specify the number of inner-loop steps, which depends on prior knowledge of the condition number k. In contrast, both SCDA [17] and SAG do not require a fixed number of steps, allowing them to potentially converge faster in practice than their theoretical bounds suggest (e.g., by exploiting better local conditioning). Furthermore, SCDA offers automatic step-size selection, and SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic that performs well in practice. This discussion should be added to the paper.

[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: a) As noted by another reviewer, a common machine learning scenario involves a regularization parameter of C/n, leading to a condition number of approximately n/C'. In this regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov's method, whereas EMGD does not. This should be acknowledged, and perhaps an alternative practical setting where k < n^(2/3) could be proposed. b) A relevant citation to [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012] should be included. That paper also presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear convergence rate. However, its rate still includes the condition number, meaning it does not ultimately improve upon standard gradient descent, whereas the current submission offers a theoretical improvement when k is sufficiently small relative to n.]

CLARITY: The paper is quite clear. I particularly appreciated the summary in Table 1, which is presented with a clarity I have not seen elsewhere in the literature. The proof is followable, but it would be more beneficial to the reader if the authors added high-level comments to motivate some of the defined quantities, which currently seem to fit together somewhat magically.

ORIGINALITY: The work represents a novel synthesis of existing techniques.

SIGNIFICANCE: While the practical utility of the algorithm remains to be demonstrated, its theoretical contributions are potentially impactful. In light of the complex convergence proof for SAG (and the simple proof for SDCA, which applies to a more restricted setting), the simplicity of EMGD's proof is a major contribution.
