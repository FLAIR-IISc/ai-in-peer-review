SUMMARY: The authors address optimizing a smooth strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. While Nesterov's optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence for this problem, its constant depends on the square root of the condition number κ. The authors consider a setting with access to an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. They propose a hybrid algorithm (Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent, EMGD) requiring O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle—provided κ is not too large, this could yield faster convergence in theory. The core idea replaces each full gradient step (an "epoch") with a fixed number O(κ²) of mixed gradient steps. Within an epoch, the algorithm combines a single full gradient computation (for the epoch) with varying stochastic gradients. By averaging the O(κ²) iterates within an epoch, the authors achieve a suboptimality decrease independent of κ, explaining why the number of epochs (full gradient calls) is κ-independent. The paper provides a clear, self-contained convergence proof but lacks experiments.  
I enjoyed reading this paper. The idea of mixing a few full gradient computations with many cheap stochastic steps is novel and interesting. This work fits within recent efforts to leverage cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence. A recent theoretical and practical breakthrough was SAG [16], which applies to smooth strongly convex objectives that are sums of n simple functions (e.g., regularized empirical loss minimization, where n is the number of training examples). Here, the full gradient oracle is typically n times more expensive than the stochastic one. SAG outperforms Nesterov's algorithm in theory when κ ≤ n/8. In contrast, EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3), and is slower than SAG across all regimes (with the same big-O for κ ≤ n^(1/2)). For intuition, when κ = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD achieve O(n log(1/ε)), while Nesterov requires O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)). Two key advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) EMGD handles constrained optimization (assuming efficient gradient mapping updates), whereas SAG is limited to unconstrained problems; and (2) EMGD's convergence proof is significantly simpler than SAG's, offering more insight and facilitating future modifications with provable guarantees (EMGD also holds with high probability, unlike SAG's expectation). The authors note a potential memory/parallelization advantage, though SAG can also use mini-batches to reduce memory and parallelize.  

EVALUATION SUMMARY:  
Pros:  
- Introduces a novel, interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and rigorous theory.  
- Like SAG, achieves linear convergence for regularized empirical loss minimization without the condition number κ multiplying n in the constant. EMGD is more general (handles constraints) and has a simpler proof than SAG.  
- The paper is clearly written, and the proof is self-contained.  

Cons:  
- No experiments to demonstrate practical relevance or illustrate why the theoretical advantages matter.  
- Insufficient discussion of limitations/drawbacks compared to existing algorithms (Section 3.4 needs substantial improvement).  
- The proof lacks high-level comments to justify key insights.  

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. While experiments would strengthen it, the theoretical contribution stands alone. The authors must definitively extend Section 3.4 to address limitations, including a concrete comparison to SAG/Nesterov for the sum-of-n-functions case: EMGD is worse than Nesterov for κ > n^(2/3), comparable to SAG for 1 ≤ κ ≤ n^(1/2), better than SAG (and Nesterov) for n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8, and worse than Nesterov for κ > n/8. A major practical drawback is that EMGD requires fixing the epoch length in advance (depending on κ), unlike SCDA [17] or SAG, which adaptively adjust steps and can leverage better local condition numbers or automatic step-size selection. The authors should add this discussion.  
[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: (a) As another reviewer noted, in ML practice, the regularization parameter is often C/n, making κ ≈ n/C'—a regime where SAG outperforms Nesterov but EMGD does not. This should be highlighted, along with a practical setting where κ < n^(2/3). (b) The authors should cite [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting, Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012], which proposes a hybrid algorithm with linear convergence but still includes κ in the rate (so it does not improve over standard gradient descent theoretically, unlike EMGD for small κ).]  

CLARITY: The paper is clear. Table 1 provides an excellent summary of rates not commonly seen this clearly in the literature. The proof is followable but would benefit from high-level comments motivating key defined quantities.  

ORIGINALITY: The work presents a novel combination of known techniques.  

SIGNIFICANCE: The practical relevance remains unproven, though the theoretical contribution is valuable. The simple proof of EMGD—contrasted with the complex proof for SAG (and the restricted applicability of SDCA's simple proof)—is a major contribution.
