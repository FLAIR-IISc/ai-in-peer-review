The paper presents a novel hybrid stochastic/batch gradient descent algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), guaranteeing convergence to an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients for smooth, strongly convex optimization. The convergence proof appears sound and represents a contribution to the field.

My primary concern relates to the practical relevance of this algorithm within the machine learning context, which is a core focus of NIPS. The analysis heavily relies on the strong convexity parameter (μ) being independent of the dataset size (n). However, in typical machine learning applications, strong convexity often arises from regularization, implying μ is proportional to n (μ = Cn, where C is independent of n). Under this common scenario, the algorithm’s advantage over batch gradient descent is limited to cases where n is bounded by O(C³/L³), a restriction that doesn't appear particularly compelling. Furthermore, the convergence guarantee of EMGD is probabilistic, while standard batch gradient descent offers deterministic convergence. This distinction is crucial and demands thorough discussion to justify the algorithm's benefits and demonstrate its relevance to the ML community. A more detailed comparison addressing this point is essential.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a technically sound algorithm with a novel convergence analysis. It addresses a well-defined problem—reducing the cost of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned optimization—and provides a theoretical solution.

Arguments against acceptance: The practical relevance of the algorithm to standard machine learning problems, where strong convexity stems from regularization, is questionable. The probabilistic convergence guarantee is less desirable than the deterministic guarantee of batch gradient descent in those scenarios. The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion acknowledging these limitations and demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods in realistic settings.

Overall, while the paper demonstrates theoretical novelty, the limited practical applicability and lack of a compelling argument for its advantages in common ML settings raise concerns. Addressing these issues through expanded discussion and analysis would significantly strengthen the contribution.
