- Problem/setup: minimizes a smooth, strongly convex function using an expensive full-gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic-gradient oracle; gradient-mapping updates assumed efficient.  
- Algorithm/idea: Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) mixes O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient epochs with O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient steps, averaging iterates per epoch.  
- Theoretical guarantee: achieves linear convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (independent of k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic calls.  
- Comparison to Nesterov: EMGD can outperform Nesterov when k is not too large (improvement threshold around k ≤ n^(2/3) in the sum-of-n setting).  
- Comparison to SAG: SAG generally outperforms EMGD across regimes; EMGD matches SAG’s big-O only when k ≤ n^(1/2), and is worse elsewhere.  
- Advantages over SAG: EMGD supports constrained problems and has a much simpler proof with high-probability guarantees.  
- Parallelism/memory claim weak: claimed memory/parallelization advantages unclear because SAG can use mini-batches too.  
- Pros: novel and appealing algorithmic idea, clean/simple/self-contained analysis, linear rates for regularized ERM without multiplying constant by n.  
- Cons: no experiments to demonstrate practical impact; limited discussion of limitations and trade-offs versus existing methods.  
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires fixing the number of steps per epoch in advance using knowledge of k, unlike SCDA and SAG which adapt and can exploit better-than-worst-case behavior.  
- Requested analyses: expand Section 3.4 and concretely analyze the sum-of-n-functions case, clarifying regime boundaries relative to SAG and Nesterov.  
- Addendum (a): in ML with regularization ~C/n (k ≈ n/C′), EMGD typically does not help—acknowledge and provide settings where k < n^(2/3).  
- Addendum (b): cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods and contrast rates (their method has k in the rate).  
- Presentation suggestions: add more high-level commentary in the proof, clarify several equations/phrases (multiple line-specific suggestions), and tighten notation consistency.  
- Typos/minor issues: several small wording and notation fixes suggested (e.g., “an convex” → “a convex”, domain notation D vs Ω, norm vs absolute value, log vs ln).  
- Overall verdict: technically sound and interesting theoretical contribution; reviewer satisfied after rebuttal but requests fuller discussion of limitations and ideally experiments.
