**Summary:** The authors consider the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex bounded set, so that the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. Nesterov's best-first algorithm has linear convergence for such problems, but the constant depends on the square root of the condition number k. The authors consider a situation where one has both an expensive complete gradient and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle for the objective. They propose a hybrid algorithm that requires O(log 1/eps) calls for a complete gradient oracle and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) for a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle – theoretically faster unless the condition number is very large. The main idea of their algorithm (known as Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent - EMGD) is to replace a complete gradient step (known as an epoch) with a certain number of mixed gradient steps, using a combination of complete gradients (calculated once for the epoch) and stochastic gradients (which vary within an epoch). Taking the average of O(k^2) iterations within an epoch, they can show a constant reduction in sub-optimality, regardless of the condition number, which is why the number of complete gradient steps required (number of epochs) is independent of the condition number. They provide a simple and completely self-contained proof of their convergence rate, but do not provide any tests.

I enjoyed reading this paper and I think that the idea of combining several complete gradient calculations with a large number of cheap stochastic gradient steps is novel and interesting. This work places itself in a row of recent papers that try to use cheap stochastic oracles while maintaining a linear convergence rate. A recent theoretical and practical success has been achieved with the SAG algorithm [16] which works for a smooth strongly convex objective that is a collection of n simple functions (such as in regularized empirical loss reduction - where n is the number of training examples). In this case, a reasonable assumption is that a complete gradient oracle is n times more expensive to compute than a stochastic gradient. SAG is therefore theoretically faster than Nesterov's algorithm, unless the condition number k < = n/8. Conversely, in this case EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov's algorithm, unless the condition number k < = n^(2/3), and EMGD is slower than SAG in all cases (it has the same big O if k < = n^1/2). To get this speed-enhancing idea, if k = n^1/2, then both SAG and EMGD are O(n log(1/eps)), whereas Nesterov's algorithm is O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)). The two main advantages I see for EMGD over SAG are 1) as mentioned by the authors, EMGD works for bounded optimization [assuming that the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently] whereas SAG is only defined for bounded problems; and 2) the convergence proof for EMGD is much easier than SAG and therefore may provide more insight and make EMGD's changes more easily provable guarantees [their result is more likely than SAG, so is stronger]. The authors also mention a potential memory/parallelization advantage, although this is less clear because SAG can be parallelized using mini-batches (which also reduces memory requirements through the size of the mini-batch).

**Evaluation Summary:**
**Pros:**
- Provides a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clear, simple, and rigorous theory.
- Like SAG, get a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss reduction where the condition number k does not multiply the number of training examples n within the constant; but their algorithm is more general (works for bounded optimization) and the proof is much easier.
- The paper is clearly written and the proof is self-contained.
**Cons:**
- There is no test that can show that this algorithm can make any difference in practice (and also does not give any real examples to show that its proposed theoretical advantages may be relevant in practice).
- There is no discussion of the algorithm's limitations/flaws (especially, compared to existing algorithms - section 3.4 should be improved! I am making several suggestions in this review).
- There are some high-level comments on the proof that may justify the essential insights used in its construction.

**Quality:** The paper is technically correct. Some tests would have been appreciated, although I think that the theoretical contribution can stand on its own. The authors should definitely extend section 3.4 with the limitations and flaws of their algorithm. They should add a more realistic discussion of the n function collection examples that nicely highlight the differences with SAG and Nesterov (as I mentioned above - for example EMGD (roughly) is worse than Nesterov for k > n^(2/3); SAG is like EMGD for 1 < = k < = n^(1/2); EMGD (and Nesterov) is better for n^(1/2) < k < = n/8; and worse than Nesterov for k > n/8). A major drawback of EMGD for practitioners is that the number of steps within an epoch must be predetermined (with knowledge of k) - in contrast, both SCDA [17] and SAG do not have to predetermined the number of steps and therefore may benefit from having a faster practical convergence that can estimate the boundary (or benefit from a better local condition number). Moreover, SCDA has an automatic step-size selection; whereas SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic that seems to work quite well in practice. The authors should add this discussion to the paper.

**Addendum after discussion with other reviewers:** a) As another reviewer noted, regularization parameter C/n is often in ML and therefore the condition number ~ n/C' is a rule where SAG still does better than Nesterov, but where EMGD does not. This should be mentioned (and perhaps another real-world situation where k < n^(2/3) should be mentioned). b) The authors should quote [hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for data fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Smith. SISC, 2012] which also presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear convergence rate. This last algorithm still displays the condition number in the rate, so the final rate is not faster than standard gradient descent (just faster because of using cheaper steps at the beginning) - so the current submission may still be better than this rate theoretically when k is not much larger than n.]

**Clarity:** The paper is reasonably clear. I appreciated the summary from table 1 which I have not seen so clearly in the literature. The proof is strictly followed, but would be more useful for readers if the authors could add a few high-level comments that are somewhat magically combining the defined quantities.

**Originality:** It is a novel combination of known techniques.

**Importance:** The practical relevance of the algorithm is not yet demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution may have an impact. In the context of the rigorous proof of convergence of SAG (and the simple proof of SDCA, but which is only applicable for a bounded setup), the simple proof of EMGD is a major contribution.
