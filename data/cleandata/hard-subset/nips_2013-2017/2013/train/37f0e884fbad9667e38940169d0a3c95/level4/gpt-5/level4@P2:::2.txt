Summary
The paper proposes a hybrid first-order method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that combines access to full (deterministic) gradients and stochastic gradients. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch it queries one full gradient, then performs T iterations of a “mixed” gradient update that adds a variance-reducing correction using stochastic gradients evaluated at the current and anchor points. With a shrinking trust region per epoch, the authors prove a high-probability linear convergence rate: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ log(1/ε)) or O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations (the abstract and body differ; this discrepancy should be clarified). The key claim is that the number of full-gradient evaluations can be made independent of the condition number κ, assuming smoothness and strong convexity and access to both oracles.

Relation to prior work
EMGD targets the strongly convex, smooth setting where accelerated full-gradient methods have iteration complexity O(√κ log(1/ε)) (Nesterov). In contrast, purely stochastic methods have polynomial dependence on 1/ε and generally do not benefit from smoothness in the same way. Recent finite-sum methods such as SAG and SDCA achieve linear rates O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) in expectation by leveraging the finite-sum structure. There is also a body of “hybrid” deterministic–stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander and Schmidt, 2012) that interleave stochastic and deterministic steps. EMGD is in this spirit but uses a mixed-gradient with a single full gradient per epoch, a shrinking ball constraint, and high-probability guarantees.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting theoretical result: linear convergence with high probability while using only O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations.
- Conceptual simplicity: the mixed-gradient correction is intuitive and leverages smoothness to control variance within an epoch.
- Potentially applicable to constrained problems, unlike some finite-sum methods that are tailored to unconstrained settings.
- Storage footprint is modest (no need to maintain per-example gradient memories as in SAG).

Main concerns and weaknesses
- Dependence on known condition number: The algorithm and proof require η and epoch parameters that depend on L and λ (hence κ). The paper does not provide a procedure to estimate these constants, nor does it analyze robustness to misspecification. The central message—removing κ from the number of full-gradient calls—hinges on κ being known. The behavior when κ is over- or under-estimated is not discussed.
- No experiments: Given the algorithmic contribution, empirical results would be valuable to (i) validate the theory, (ii) study sensitivity to parameter misspecification, and (iii) compare wall-clock performance to accelerated GD, SGD/averaged SGD, SAG/SDCA, and simple alternating deterministic/stochastic schedules.
- Positioning with respect to hybrid methods: The paper does not adequately discuss prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic approaches (e.g., Friedlander and Schmidt, 2012) or earlier interleaving schemes. A direct comparison of convergence rates, assumptions, and practical costs would clarify novelty.
- Problem setting clarity: Section 3.1 introduces an expectation oracle, while other parts discuss finite-sum structure and comparisons to SAG/SDCA. It is not clear whether the primary target is the expectation-minimization (stochastic approximation) problem or deterministic finite-sum optimization. This ambiguity affects how results should be interpreted and compared.
- Scope: The analysis is restricted to strongly convex objectives. While standard in theory, a discussion of extensions (or limits) to merely convex settings would broaden relevance (e.g., different regimes in strongly convex vs convex as in Bach & Moulines, 2011).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide guidance for setting η, T, and epoch radius without prior knowledge of L and λ. Possible avenues: adaptive schemes, backtracking/line-search variants, or coarse-to-fine doubling/halving strategies, with corresponding theoretical or empirical support.
- Analyze robustness to misspecified κ: characterize convergence when κ is under/over-estimated, ideally with bounds; at minimum, provide empirical evidence.
- Add an experimental section: synthetic and real datasets; compare to accelerated GD, SGD/ASGD, SAG, SDCA, and a simple interleaving baseline (e.g., periodic full-gradient steps within SGD). Report both iteration counts and wall-clock times, and study the effect of κ and data size.
- Clarify the optimization model up front: explicitly state whether the main theorem addresses expectation minimization, finite-sum minimization, or both; align the oracle model and comparisons accordingly.
- Discuss related hybrid methods and variance-reduction techniques more comprehensively, and explain how EMGD differs (e.g., use of a single full gradient per epoch, high-probability guarantees, constrained problems).

Minor comments
- Ensure consistent statements of stochastic gradient complexity (κ vs κ^2 factor) across abstract, theorem, and comparisons.
- Fix terminology (“condition number,” not “conditional number”) and small typographical issues.
- The algorithm is referred to as EMGD; avoid confusion with EGD. Briefly comment on computational cost of the projection onto W ∩ B(·; ·) and whether it can be avoided or approximated in the unconstrained case.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contribution with high-probability linear convergence and reduced reliance on full gradients.
- Simple algorithmic idea with potential applicability to constrained problems and low memory use.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on known κ with no practical estimation/robustness path.
- Lack of experiments to substantiate practical benefits and sensitivity.
- Insufficient positioning relative to existing hybrid and finite-sum variance-reduced methods.
- Ambiguity about the primary problem setting (finite-sum vs expectation).
