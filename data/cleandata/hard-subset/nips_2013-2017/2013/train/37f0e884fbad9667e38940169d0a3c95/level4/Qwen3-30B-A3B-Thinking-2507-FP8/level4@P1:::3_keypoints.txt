- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) combining full and stochastic gradients for smooth, strongly convex optimization.  
- Claims EMGD needs O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach ε-optimality.  
- Asserts improvement over standard full-gradient methods’ O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity.  
- Builds on recent stochastic optimization work [9,10,22,23] and addresses high cost of full gradients for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Strong technical contribution with rigorous, well-structured convergence proof (Theorem 1) and clear algorithm motivation.  
- Correctly contextualizes against Nesterov’s accelerated methods and stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA.  
- Main strength: theoretical novelty achieving condition-number-independent access to full gradients while maintaining linear convergence.  
- Algorithm design is elegant, balancing deterministic and stochastic components; complexity advantages shown for κ ≤ n^(2/3).  
- Major concern: limited practical relevance for ML because typical strong convexity from regularization makes κ often large (κ = O(N)), so EMGD advantage is restrictive.  
- Under standard ML settings EMGD outperforms batch methods only when τ = Ω(n^(-2/3)), a regime too restrictive in practice.  
- Paper lacks empirical evidence demonstrating practical advantage in realistic ML problems.  
- Convergence guarantee is high-probability rather than deterministic, which may be less appealing for practitioners.  
- Clarity is generally good, but minor issues need fixes: eq.(6) should use ||w* − ŵ||²; clarify eq.(7) reference; remove pointless equality in line 286; use norms instead of absolute values in eq.(13); state L vs. λ relationship earlier and define κ = L/λ precisely.  
- Main weakness: limited practical significance for ML despite sound theory; needs stronger justification for adoption over existing methods.  
- Recommendations: clearly articulate practical regimes where EMGD helps, provide empirical speedups on real datasets, and discuss implications of high-probability vs deterministic guarantees.  
- Conclusion: technically novel and correct but narrow ML relevance; authors should address practical limitations before it’s a strong NIPS contribution.
