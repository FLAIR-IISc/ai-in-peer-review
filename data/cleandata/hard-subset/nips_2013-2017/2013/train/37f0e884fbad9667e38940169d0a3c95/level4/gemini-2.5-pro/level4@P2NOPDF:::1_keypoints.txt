- Paper studies optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions with efficient gradient mapping, proposing Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD).
- EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, making full-gradient complexity independent of κ.
- Algorithm structures optimization into epochs: one full gradient per epoch plus O(κ²) mixed gradient steps, averaging iterates to get κ-independent epoch progress.
- Provides a simple, self-contained proof of linear convergence (high-probability guarantee) but contains no experiments.
- EMGD can be theoretically faster than Nesterov when κ is not too large and is applicable to constrained problems (unlike SAG).
- Compared to SAG: SAG is faster in most regimes; EMGD is slower overall but matches SAG’s big-O when κ ≤ n^(1/2).
- EMGD’s proof is simpler and gives high-probability guarantees, which are stronger than SAG’s in-expectation results.
- Claimed memory and parallelization advantages of EMGD are unclear; SAG can also be parallelized via mini-batches.
- Major weakness: no empirical evaluation to validate practical relevance or demonstrate scenarios where EMGD’s constraints-handling matters.
- Paper lacks sufficient discussion of limitations and should expand Section 3.4 for balance.
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires pre-specifying epoch length based on κ, so it needs knowledge of the condition number.
- EMGD performs poorly in high-condition-number regimes common in ML (e.g., κ ≈ n/C'), where SAG remains preferable.
- Authors should identify practical settings where κ < n^(2/3) to justify EMGD’s applicability.
- Related work omission: should cite and discuss Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods.
- Clarity suggestions: add high-level intuition for the proof and smoother linking of defined quantities.

- Technical clarifications: correct claim about parallelization (l.224), clarify derivation at l.288, add parentheses around expressions (l.304), and justify inequality condition at l.366.
