SUMMARY
This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions over a convex domain. The authors propose a novel hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which utilizes both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. The main contribution is an algorithm that finds an ǫ-optimal solution by performing O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient computations—a number independent of the problem's condition number, κ—and O(κ² log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradient computations. The method operates in epochs, with each epoch initiated by a single full gradient calculation, followed by a series of "mixed gradient" steps that combine this full gradient with stochastic information. The analysis shows that averaging the iterates within an epoch achieves a constant reduction in suboptimality, yielding a linear convergence rate where the number of expensive full gradient calls is decoupled from κ.

The work is well-situated within recent literature aiming to achieve linear convergence rates while primarily using inexpensive stochastic updates, such as the SAG and SDCA algorithms. For the common empirical risk minimization setting (where the objective is a sum of n functions and the full gradient is n times more costly than the stochastic one), EMGD is shown to theoretically outperform Nesterov's accelerated gradient method in regimes where κ ≤ n^(2/3). While the complexity of EMGD appears less favorable than that of SAG in most regimes, the authors point out several potential advantages, including its applicability to constrained optimization problems and its simpler convergence analysis, which holds with high probability rather than in expectation.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and interesting algorithmic framework based on mixing full and stochastic gradients, supported by a solid and clean theoretical analysis.
*   The convergence proof is remarkably simple and self-contained, which is a valuable contribution, especially when contrasted with the more complex analyses required for related methods.
*   The proposed algorithm is more general than some alternatives (e.g., SAG, SDCA) as it naturally handles constrained optimization problems, assuming an efficient projection step.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The practical utility and performance of the algorithm are not demonstrated, as the paper lacks any empirical evaluation or experiments.
*   The paper does not adequately discuss the algorithm's limitations and drawbacks, particularly in a comparative context with existing state-of-the-art methods.
*   The theoretical analysis, while correct, could be improved with more high-level intuition to motivate the key steps and definitions, which currently appear somewhat magical.

**EVALUATION**
**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the theoretical claims are well-supported. Although the theoretical contribution can stand on its own, the absence of experiments makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's practical potential. A significant area for improvement is the discussion of the algorithm's limitations (Section 3.4). This section should be expanded with a more nuanced comparison, explicitly stating the regimes where EMGD is less efficient than Nesterov's method (i.e., when κ > n^(2/3)) or SAG. Crucially, the authors should address the common machine learning scenario where regularization scales as 1/n, leading to a condition number κ proportional to n. In this important regime, EMGD offers no theoretical improvement over Nesterov's method, a point that should be clearly stated. From a practitioner's perspective, EMGD has the disadvantage of requiring prior knowledge of κ to set its parameters, unlike SAG or SDCA which are more adaptive. Finally, the authors should cite and discuss "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" (Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) to better contextualize their theoretical advance.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The inclusion of Table 1 effectively summarizes the complexities of various methods. While the proof is technically complete, it would benefit from additional high-level commentary explaining the purpose of key definitions and steps, which would provide the reader with greater insight into the mechanics of the analysis.

**Originality:** The work represents a novel combination of existing ideas (epoch-based schemes, variance reduction) to create a new hybrid algorithm. The core idea of using a fixed full gradient within an epoch to control the variance of subsequent stochastic steps is an original contribution.

**Significance:** The practical relevance of the algorithm is yet to be demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution has the potential for impact. In the context of the difficult convergence proofs for some related methods, the simple and clean proof provided for EMGD is a notable achievement that could inspire further research and simplify the analysis of other hybrid algorithms.

**Detailed Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   In the introduction, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the complexities summarized in Table 1 are detailed later in the related work section, to avoid initial reader confusion about their sources.
*   When discussing SDCA, clarify that its applicability is limited to a specific structural form of the objective function components, unlike SAG.
*   The description of the function oracle should explicitly state that F(w) = E[f(w)] implies ∇F(w) = E[∇f(w)].
*   The claim that SAG and SDCA cannot leverage distributed computing should be reconsidered or softened, as both can be parallelized using mini-batches, which also has implications for memory usage.
*   The proof derivation would be easier to follow with more explicit guidance. For example, when deriving the bound on the inner product in the analysis, briefly state which properties or lemmas are being applied.
*   To improve readability in the summation inequalities, use parentheses to clarify the scope of the summation, e.g., `sum_{t=1 to T} (F(wt) - F(w*))`.
*   The condition δ ≤ e^(-1/2) is used to simplify a bound in the final steps of the proof; it would be helpful to explicitly mention this when the condition is first introduced.
*   Please ensure consistent notation for the optimization domain throughout the paper.
*   There are minor typographical errors that should be corrected (e.g., "an convex" -> "a convex", inconsistent use of `log` vs `ln`).
