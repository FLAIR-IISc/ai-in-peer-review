This paper presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, which combines calls to a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). The authors demonstrate a striking theoretical result: for smooth and strongly convex functions, their approach can achieve an O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate with high probability, effectively removing the dependency on the condition number from the rate.

However, the paper in its current state has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Reliance on a Known Condition Number:** The core theoretical analysis relies critically on the assumption that the condition number, κ (defined by the strong convexity and smoothness constants), is known beforehand. The algorithm's key hyperparameter (η in Eq. 8) is set using κ. The paper does not address the practical and crucial scenario where this parameter is unknown or misspecified. Essentially, the result shows that dependence on the condition number can be eliminated from the convergence rate only if the algorithm is given this exact value as input. If a theoretical analysis of misspecification is too challenging, this should have been investigated empirically.

2.  **Complete Lack of Experiments:** The paper's primary contribution is a new algorithm, yet it includes no experimental section. This is a major omission. An empirical evaluation is essential to validate the theoretical claims, assess the algorithm's practical performance, and understand its behavior on real-world data, especially its sensitivity to the aforementioned hyperparameter κ. A thorough comparison with standard baselines (e.g., SGD, averaged SGD) and recent proposals (e.g., SAG, SDCA) is necessary to contextualize the algorithm's contribution.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Unclear Problem Setting:** The paper does not clearly state the optimization problem it aims to solve. It is ambiguous whether the objective is the deterministic finite-sum problem [min_w F(w) = 1/n Σ F_i(w)], which is the focus of algorithms like SAG and SDCA, or the general stochastic approximation problem [min_w E(F(w))]. The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is misleading in this respect, and this ambiguity should be clarified to properly position the paper's contribution.

*   **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** The paper fails to review or compare against a relevant line of work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithms (e.g., "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting," Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC, 2012). A discussion of how the proposed method relates to these existing approaches and a comparison of convergence rates would be valuable.

*   **Novelty and Formulation:** The idea of blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not new. The proposed algorithm is formulated using a single "mixed gradient" update per iteration rather than as an alternation between deterministic and stochastic steps. It would be insightful to discuss how this formulation relates to and compares with simpler strategies that interleave the two types of steps with varying frequencies.

*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The analysis is restricted to the smooth and strongly convex case. While this is a common setting for theoretical analysis, the non-strongly convex setting is also highly important in practice. It would strengthen the paper to discuss potential extensions or limitations of the algorithm in this setting, perhaps referencing works that analyze these different cases (e.g., Bach & Moulines, 2011).

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical result, its strong and unaddressed assumption about knowing the condition number, combined with a complete absence of empirical validation and insufficient engagement with related work, significantly diminishes its impact. The paper would be much stronger if it addressed these critical issues.
