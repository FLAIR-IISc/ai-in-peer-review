SUMMARY:
The paper studies minimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex set where gradient-mapping updates can be computed efficiently. Classical first-order methods with full gradients achieve linear convergence, but their complexity scales as O(√k log(1/ε)) with the condition number k, implying many full gradient evaluations when k is large. The authors consider access to both an expensive full-gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic-gradient oracle and propose a hybrid method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD). EMGD makes only O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full-gradient oracle (independent of k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle. The algorithm partitions optimization into epochs: each epoch computes one full gradient and then performs O(k^2) “mixed-gradient” steps that combine this full gradient with stochastic gradients; averaging the iterates yields a constant reduction in suboptimality per epoch, hence linear convergence. The paper provides a clean, self-contained proof of convergence, but no experiments.

The work fits within recent efforts to leverage stochastic oracles while retaining linear rates. In the “finite-sum” setting F(w) = (1/n)∑ fi(w), SAG achieves O(n log(1/ε)) provided n ≥ 8k, and SDCA achieves O((n + k) log(1/ε)). Assuming a full gradient costs n times a stochastic gradient, EMGD is faster than Nesterov’s accelerated method when k ≤ n^(2/3). In contrast, SAG is faster than Nesterov when k ≤ n/8. EMGD is slower than SAG in all regimes, with the same order only when k ≤ n^(1/2). For example, if k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD take O(n log(1/ε)) versus O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)) for Nesterov. EMGD’s main advantages over SAG are: (1) applicability to constrained problems (assuming the gradient mapping can be computed efficiently), whereas SAG is developed for unconstrained settings; and (2) a simpler proof with high-probability guarantees (vs. in expectation for SAG), which may enable easier analysis of variants.

EVALUATION SUMMARY:
Pros:
- A novel and appealing hybrid idea with a clean, simple, and solid analysis.
- Linear convergence without multiplying n by k in the constant for finite-sum problems; broader applicability than SAG (handles constraints); proof is substantially simpler and gives high-probability bounds.
- Clear presentation and self-contained proof.

Cons:
- No experiments or concrete practical scenarios illustrating when the approach provides real benefits.
- Limited discussion of limitations and trade-offs versus existing methods; Section 3.4 should be expanded (see below).
- The proof would benefit from higher-level commentary to motivate key constructions.

QUALITY:
Technically sound. While experiments would strengthen the paper, the theoretical contribution can stand alone. The authors should expand Section 3.4 with a frank discussion of limitations and comparative drawbacks. In the finite-sum case, please add a concrete comparison: EMGD is worse than Nesterov roughly when k > n^(2/3); EMGD matches SAG only for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2); SAG is better than both EMGD and Nesterov for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8; and SAG is worse than Nesterov for k > n/8. A practical drawback is that EMGD requires fixing the number of steps per epoch in advance (and thus knowing k). By contrast, SDCA and SAG do not require fixing the horizon; SDCA has automatic step-size selection, and SAG uses adaptive step-size heuristics that work well in practice. Please discuss these points.

ADDENDUM (after discussion with other reviewers):
a) In many ML applications the regularization scales as C/n, making the condition number ≈ n/C′. In this regime SAG still improves over Nesterov, but EMGD does not; please highlight this, and consider pointing to practical regimes where k < n^(2/3).
b) Please cite: Friedlander and Schmidt, “Hybrid Deterministic–Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting,” SISC 2012. Their hybrid method has a linear rate but still depends on the condition number, so EMGD can be theoretically superior when k is not too large relative to n.

CLARITY:
Generally clear and well organized. Table 1 is a helpful summary of known rates. The proof is tight but could be made more accessible by adding high-level explanations for the key definitions and how they interact (e.g., the role of mixed gradients, the shrinking domain, and why averaging yields constant contraction).

ORIGINALITY:
A novel combination of familiar tools (epochs, mixed gradients, shrinking domains) yielding an appealing theoretical guarantee and high-probability analysis.

SIGNIFICANCE:
The practical impact remains to be demonstrated, but the theoretical idea and especially the simple proof may influence follow-on work (particularly given the complexity of SAG’s proof and the restricted setting of SDCA).
