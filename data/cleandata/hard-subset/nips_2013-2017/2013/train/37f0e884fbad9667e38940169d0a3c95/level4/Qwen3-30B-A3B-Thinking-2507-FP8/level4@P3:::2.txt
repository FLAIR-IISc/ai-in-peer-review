

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradient evaluations to achieve linear convergence for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems without condition number dependence. The authors claim that EMGD requires only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (compared to O(√κ log(1/ε)) for standard methods) when the condition number κ is known, while using O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper builds on the epoch gradient descent framework [9] and relates to recent stochastic optimization works [9, 10, 21, 22, 23].

**Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with a rigorous proof of Theorem 1 showing the claimed convergence rate. The paper clearly distinguishes between full and stochastic gradient access, and the mixed gradient update (Equation 7) is a novel technical contribution. The comparison to SAG [22] and SDCA [23] in Table 2 provides valuable context for the computational complexity.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant limitation is the paper's reliance on knowing κ exactly before algorithm execution. The theoretical analysis only covers the ideal case where κ is perfectly known, without addressing how the algorithm behaves when κ is misspecified (either overestimated or underestimated). This is a major practical concern, as condition numbers are rarely known in advance for real-world optimization problems. The paper lacks any discussion of how to estimate κ in practice, nor does it provide experimental validation of the theoretical claims. Without experiments, it's impossible to assess how the algorithm performs in realistic scenarios where κ is unknown or approximately known.

The paper also fails to properly contextualize its approach within the broader literature on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization. The related work section should have explicitly discussed Friedlander and Schmidt's 2012 SISC paper on "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting," which directly addresses blending deterministic and stochastic steps. This omission makes it difficult to assess the novelty of the proposed approach.

Furthermore, the paper's optimization setting is ambiguously stated. It's unclear whether the authors are addressing the deterministic optimization problem min_w F(w) = (1/n)Σf_i(w) with a randomized algorithm (as in SAG/SDCA) or the stochastic approximation problem min_w E[F(w)]. This distinction matters because SAG and SDCA are designed for the former setting, while the paper's theoretical setup (Section 3.1) is somewhat misleading on this point.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is strong in the known-κ regime but lacks completeness regarding practical implementation considerations. The paper doesn't address how the algorithm would behave when κ is not known, which is a critical gap in the quality assessment.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, but the ambiguity around the optimization setting and the lack of experimental results reduce its clarity. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of the problem being solved (deterministic optimization with randomized algorithm vs. stochastic approximation).

**Originality:** The mixed gradient update is novel, but the core idea of combining deterministic and stochastic steps is not entirely new. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison with existing hybrid methods, particularly Friedlander and Schmidt (2012).

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is interesting but its practical significance is limited by the requirement that κ must be known in advance. Without experiments demonstrating the algorithm's performance in realistic scenarios, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed theoretical improvements translate to practical benefits.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by showing how to remove condition number dependence when κ is known, and the mixed gradient technique is novel.
*Con:* The requirement that κ must be known beforehand severely limits practical applicability, and the lack of experimental validation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's real-world value.

**Recommendation:** The paper should be rejected due to the significant practical limitation (requirement of known κ) and the absence of experimental validation. To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Add an analysis of the algorithm's behavior when κ is misspecified (over- or underestimated)
2. Include an experimental section comparing EMGD with SAG, SDCA, and standard methods on real optimization problems
3. Clarify the optimization setting (deterministic vs. stochastic) and properly contextualize the work within Friedlander and Schmidt (2012)
4. Discuss practical approaches for estimating κ when it's not known in advance

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution remains largely theoretical without sufficient evidence of practical relevance. The authors' claim that "EMGD may not be the best one [in terms of computational cost], but it has advantages in other aspects" is unsubstantiated without empirical results to support these advantages.
