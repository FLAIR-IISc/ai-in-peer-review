- Addresses optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions over convex sets and proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) mixing full and stochastic gradients.  
- EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls (independent of condition number k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient calls.  
- Key idea: replace each full-gradient epoch with O(k^2) mixed-gradient steps, averaging iterates to get k-independent constant decrease.  
- Provides a simple, self-contained convergence proof but no experimental validation.  
- Novel and interesting idea that fits trends exploiting cheaper stochastic oracles while retaining linear convergence.  
- EMGD is more general than SAG (handles constrained optimization) and has a much simpler proof.  
- In some regimes EMGD is faster than Nesterov (e.g., when k ≤ n^(2/3)), but SAG outperforms EMGD across most regimes.  
- Comparative regime summary recommended: EMGD worse than Nesterov for k > n^(2/3); SAG ≈ EMGD for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2); SAG best for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8; Nesterov best for k > n/8.  
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires fixing epoch lengths based on prior knowledge of k, unlike SCDA and SAG which adapt step sizes.  
- Suggest discussing regularization setting (k ≈ n/C) where SAG often outperforms EMGD and providing practical scenarios with k < n^(2/3).  
- Recommend citing Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) for a related hybrid deterministic-stochastic approach and contrasting convergence dependencies on k.  
- Pros: novel algorithmic idea, clean theoretical foundation, linear convergence for practical regimes, well-written with self-contained proof.  
- Cons: no experiments to show practical relevance, Section 3.4 lacks discussion of limitations, and the proof needs higher-level motivation.  
- Quality: technically sound but should expand discussion of limitations and the n-function example comparing EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov.  
- Clarity: generally clear; Table 1 is helpful, but proofs would benefit from explanatory commentary about key quantities.  
- Detailed suggestions and typos: multiple line-specific editorial fixes and clarifications (e.g., fix “an convex,” clarify SCDA applicability, notation consistency, log→ln).
