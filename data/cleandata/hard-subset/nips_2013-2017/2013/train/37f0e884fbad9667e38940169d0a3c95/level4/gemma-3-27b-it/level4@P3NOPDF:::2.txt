This paper presents a novel “hybrid” deterministic-stochastic first-order optimization method. The core idea is to strategically combine queries to both deterministic (full gradient) and stochastic oracles, achieving a convergence rate that, notably, eliminates the dependence on the condition number under specific conditions. The theoretical result is interesting and potentially impactful. However, significant concerns regarding the practical relevance and completeness of the work currently limit its impact.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis. Assuming a known condition number (i.e., strong convexity and smoothness constants are known *a priori*) and access to a first-order hybrid oracle, the authors demonstrate an O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate for batch optimization with high probability. This is a compelling result. However, the analysis is heavily restricted to the case where the condition number is perfectly known. The algorithm’s behavior when this crucial hyperparameter is misspecified remains entirely unexplored. While acknowledging that analyzing the unknown condition number case may be challenging, the paper would benefit substantially from either a theoretical investigation of this scenario or, crucially, an experimental evaluation.

The complete absence of an experimental section is a major weakness. Given the introduction of a new optimization algorithm, empirical validation is essential to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical findings and demonstrate its performance in realistic settings. Experiments could illuminate the sensitivity of the algorithm to condition number misspecification and provide insights into heuristic methods for estimating the optimal mixing parameter η (which is directly tied to the condition number, as described by Equations 7 and 8).

The reliance on a known condition number raises a fundamental practical question: how is this parameter determined in practice? The paper does not offer guidance on estimating the condition number or provide a discussion of the implications of inaccurate estimation. The analysis needs to consider scenarios where η is set too high or too low, and the corresponding convergence behavior needs to be characterized. 

Furthermore, while the strong convexity/smoothness assumption is common in theoretical analyses, restricting the work to this setting limits its applicability. Investigating the algorithm's performance under weaker assumptions, such as non-strongly convex objectives, would broaden its appeal. The authors should consider acknowledging and discussing the implications of this restriction, referencing works such as Bach & Moulines (2011) which detail the differing behaviors in convex vs. strongly convex scenarios.

The algorithm’s approach to blending deterministic and stochastic gradients, as a mixed update within each iteration, differs from more traditional alternating approaches. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how it relates to, and compares with, algorithms that explicitly interleave deterministic and stochastic gradient steps. 

A significant omission is the lack of engagement with relevant prior work. The paper should discuss and compare its approach to hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms, specifically citing Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC, 2012) and related work. A comprehensive review of the existing literature in this area would contextualize the contribution and highlight its novelty.

Finally, the optimization setting under consideration is not clearly defined. Is the algorithm intended for solving the deterministic finite-sum problem with a randomized solver, or the stochastic approximation problem? This ambiguity is evident in Section 3.1 and remains unresolved throughout the paper. Clarifying this point is crucial for a precise understanding of the theoretical results. A thorough experimental study, comparing the proposed method with standard algorithms like SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, and SDCA, would significantly strengthen the paper.



In summary, while the theoretical result presented is promising, the paper suffers from a lack of practical validation and a limited scope of analysis. Addressing the concerns regarding the condition number, experimental evaluation, comparison to related work, and clarity of the optimization setting are crucial for elevating the paper’s impact and establishing the relevance of this algorithm.
