Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization that combines infrequent full gradients with frequent stochastic gradients. EMGD proceeds in epochs: at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at the current averaged iterate, and then performs T inner iterations of “mixed” gradient steps using the control-variate-type estimator ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), together with a gradient mapping on a shrinking Euclidean ball around w̄k. The analysis shows linear convergence: to reach ε accuracy, EMGD needs O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full-gradient oracle and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle, where κ = L/λ is the condition number.

The method is closely related to prior first-order methods. For full-gradient methods, Nesterov’s accelerated gradient achieves O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations with deterministic guarantees. In stochastic/finite-sum settings, SAG and SDCA obtain linear convergence with iteration complexity O(n log(1/ε)) or O((n + κ) log(1/ε)), and employ variance reduction across component gradients. EMGD is also conceptually close to semi-stochastic variance-reduction methods that use a full gradient intermittently with stochastic corrections (e.g., methods in the spirit of SVRG), but EMGD is formulated for a more general oracle model (not only finite sums), supports constraints via projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk), and provides high-probability convergence.

Quality:
- The main proof technique is clear: the algorithm ensures a per-epoch constant factor decrease by bounding the stochastic deviation using smoothness and a shrinking domain, applying Hoeffding–Azuma to control the martingale difference terms, and choosing η and T to balance bias and variance. The high-probability linear rate appears sound, modulo the minor issues listed below.
- The oracle model and assumptions are stated, and the role of smoothness (L) and strong convexity (λ) is explicit. The reduction of full-gradient calls from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) is well-justified.
- However, several details would benefit from clarification: precise definition of κ; explicit statements that L ≥ λ; and a more careful quantitative comparison to existing variance-reduced methods (SAG/SDCA and closely related semi-stochastic methods).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and well organized. The epoch structure and mixed gradient are explained, and the proof is methodical.
- Some notation, equation referencing, and typographical issues impede comprehension (see Minor comments). The comparison section would benefit from a clearer, unified cost model (e.g., wall-clock comparisons under n ≫ κ vs κ ≫ n, and explicit accounting for T = Θ(κ^2 ln(1/δ))).

Originality:
- The specific use of a mixed gradient estimator and a shrinking trust-region-style domain to control variance in a general oracle model is interesting. That said, the mixed gradient estimator is very similar to variance-reduction estimators used in recent semi-stochastic methods for finite sums (e.g., SAG/SDCA/SVRG family). The paper’s distinct contributions are (i) oracle complexity with condition-number–independent full-gradient calls, (ii) support for constraints with low memory, and (iii) high-probability guarantees. A more explicit discussion of similarities/differences with SVRG-style methods would strengthen the novelty claim.

Significance:
- Theoretical significance: Reducing the number of full gradients to O(log(1/ε)) is attractive, especially when full gradients are expensive or computed in parallel. The high-probability linear convergence under general stochastic oracles is also of interest.
- Practical significance: The per-epoch cost requires T = Θ(κ^2 ln(1/δ)) stochastic gradients. In finite-sum problems, if one full gradient costs n stochastic gradients, the per-epoch cost is roughly n + Θ(κ^2); thus EMGD is advantageous only when κ^2 ≲ n in the intended cost model, and may be dominated by SAG/SDCA/SVRG-type methods that achieve O(n + κ) per epoch. The paper partially acknowledges this but should more carefully delineate the regimes where EMGD is preferable and provide empirical or more detailed theoretical guidance.
- The requirement to know (or upper-bound) κ to set T and step sizes limits practicality; adaptive strategies would improve applicability.

Pros:
- Clear and simple algorithm with a clean high-probability linear convergence proof.
- Condition-number–independent number of full gradients, with O(log(1/ε)) dependence.
- Works with constraints and needs only O(d) memory.
- Potentially amenable to distributed computation for the full gradient step.

Cons:
- Total complexity dominated by O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients; less competitive than O(n + κ) methods when κ is large.
- Lacks an explicit and thorough comparison to closely related variance-reduction methods (e.g., SVRG), both theoretically and experimentally.
- Requires knowledge of κ; adaptivity not addressed.
- High-probability (vs deterministic) guarantees are acceptable, but a clearer discussion contrasting to deterministic accelerated methods would help.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a rigorous, apples-to-apples complexity comparison under a common cost model for finite-sum problems (cost per full gradient ≈ n vs per stochastic ≈ 1), including regimes n ≫ κ, κ ≫ n, and practical ranges of τ (regularization).
- Discuss typical ML settings where λ stems from regularization τ. In many applications τ decreases with n (e.g., τ = O(1/n) or O(1/√n)), which increases κ; clarify whether EMGD remains advantageous and under what τ–n relationships (beyond the current τ ≳ n^(-2/3) condition).
- Consider citing and comparing with variance-reduced methods closely matching the mixed-gradient estimator (e.g., SVRG-style approaches), including memory usage, support for constraints, and high-probability vs expectation guarantees.
- Discuss strategies to remove the need for a priori κ (e.g., adaptive epoch lengths or backtracking).
- If possible, add experiments demonstrating wall-clock benefits in regimes predicted by the theory (e.g., large n, moderate κ, constrained problems, distributed full gradients).

Minor comments and corrections:
- Please explicitly define κ = L/λ and state L ≥ λ under the given assumptions.
- Equation (6): the norm should be ‖w* − ŵ‖^2 (as appropriate for the statement being proved).
- In the proof around (286): the equality that restates definitions can be removed to improve readability; it adds little and interrupts the flow.
- Around line 288: specify clearly which function inequality (7) is applied to.
- In (13): absolute values should be replaced by norms where vector magnitudes are intended.
- In (4): replace x̂ with ŵ and f with F; also note that by Lemma 1 the first term in the max dominates the second term.
- Ensure consistent notation for balls B(x; r), gradient operators, and averaging over T vs T + 1 iterates.
- Clarify that the high-probability bounds require δ ≤ e^(−1/2) and T ≥ 1152 L^2/λ^2 ln(1/δ), and explain the implications for setting T in practice.

Overall assessment:
A solid theoretical contribution with a clean analysis and an appealing reduction in full-gradient usage. However, the practical impact for mainstream ML optimization is unclear given the κ^2 dependence and the proximity to existing variance-reduction methods. I encourage the authors to refine the comparisons, clarify the regimes of advantage, address adaptivity to κ, and fix the noted presentation issues.
