Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid first-order method that combines full gradients with stochastic gradients. The algorithm operates in epochs: at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at the averaged iterate from the previous epoch, and within the epoch it performs T projected updates using a “mixed gradient” that adds a stochastic gradient difference to the cached full gradient. With appropriate stepsizes and a shrinking trust-region-style constraint, the method guarantees linear convergence: it achieves an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full-gradient oracle and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle (κ is the condition number). The analysis establishes high-probability bounds and relies on smoothness, strong convexity, and a martingale argument (Hoeffding–Azuma) to control the stochastic error within each epoch.

Relative to prior work: for smooth, strongly convex problems with access only to full gradients, accelerated methods achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations (Nesterov). Purely stochastic methods with strong convexity typically achieve O(1/(λ ε)) rates, i.e., polynomial in 1/ε. Recent finite-sum methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA) obtain linear rates under additional structure. EMGD aims to reduce the number of full-gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)) by offloading most work to cheaper stochastic gradients.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean hybridization that caches a full gradient and uses stochastic differences to correct it within a shrinking domain.
- The convergence proof appears correct and novel to me (apart from minor issues listed below). The high-probability guarantee is stronger than in many related stochastic methods that provide only expected rates.
- The algorithm is simple to implement given access to both oracles and applies to constrained problems (via the projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk)), unlike some finite-sum methods.

Weaknesses and main concerns
- Relevance and advantage in typical ML settings are unclear. In many ML problems, strong convexity comes from an ℓ2-regularizer; denoting the strong convexity parameter by μ (λ in the paper), the paper’s discussion suggests μ scales with the number of samples N (μ ≈ C N with C independent of N). Under this assumption, EMGD is faster than batch accelerated gradient methods only when N is bounded by O(C^3/L^3), which does not seem a practically interesting regime. Moreover, EMGD’s rate holds with high probability, whereas the batch accelerated method’s rate is deterministic.
- The paper should carefully articulate when EMGD provides a real computational advantage over standard full-gradient methods in ML (e.g., regimes of κ, N, and oracle costs), to justify its significance for the community.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The technical development is sound overall, and the high-probability analysis is well-structured. Please address the minor issues below.
- Clarity: The presentation is mostly clear, but several notational and editorial slips hinder readability. More explicit discussion of when EMGD outperforms full-gradient methods would improve clarity and impact.
- Originality: The mixed-gradient epoch scheme and its analysis are, to my knowledge, novel in this precise form.
- Significance: Potentially significant if the regime where full gradients are expensive and stochastic gradients are cheap is common and if the method is shown to outperform accelerated full-gradient methods in realistic ML regimes. As written, this comparative advantage is not convincingly demonstrated.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel hybrid approach with a clean analysis and high-probability linear rate.
- Handles constrained problems and requires only O(d) memory.

Cons for acceptance
- Unclear advantage over standard accelerated full-gradient methods in typical ML regimes where strong convexity stems from regularization.
- Empirical or more detailed theoretical comparison to delineate practical regimes of superiority is missing.

Requests for revision and clarification
- Provide a precise, practical comparison to batch accelerated methods: for which ranges of κ, N, and oracle cost ratio does EMGD reduce wall-clock time?
- Explicitly discuss the ML setting where strong convexity arises from regularization, and justify the assumption μ ≈ C N. Under this assumption, clarify the claim that EMGD is faster than batch gradient only if N ≤ O(C^3/L^3), and explain why this regime is relevant.
- Emphasize that your guarantee is high-probability and contrast it with deterministic guarantees for full-gradient methods.

Minor comments and corrections
- Equation (6): the right-hand side should involve the norm of w* − ŵ, i.e., use ||w* − ŵ||^2 (please check context to ensure the intended variables).
- Please specify in line 288 which function you apply inequality (7) to.
- The equality on line 286 seems unnecessary and hurts readability; consider removing it.
- In (13), replace absolute values with norms where appropriate.
- Please explicitly state that L ≥ λ and briefly justify it (it follows from smoothness and strong convexity definitions).
- In (4), replace x^ with w^ and f with F; moreover, by Lemma 1 the first term in the max is always larger than the second.
- Precisely define the condition number as κ = L/λ.
- Notation: use a single symbol for the strong convexity parameter (λ in the paper) consistently; avoid mixing μ and λ unless you define both.

Overall
A technically solid and potentially useful hybrid method. However, its practical advantage over accelerated full-gradient methods in standard ML regimes is not yet convincingly argued. I encourage the authors to clarify and strengthen the discussion of applicability and to address the minor issues above.
