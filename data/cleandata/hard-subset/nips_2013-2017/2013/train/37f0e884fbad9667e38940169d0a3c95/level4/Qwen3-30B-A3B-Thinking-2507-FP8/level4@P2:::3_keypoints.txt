- Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that mixes full and stochastic gradients to reduce full-gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)).
- Theoretically shows EMGD requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients for ε-optimality.
- Targets the computational bottleneck in ill-conditioned problems where full gradient evaluations are costly.
- Empirically/theoretically compared to Nesterov, SAG, SDCA, highlighting advantages in constrained optimization and storage.
- Strength: technical quality — sound theoretical analysis and rigorous, induction-based convergence proofs.
- Strength: originality — novel epoch-based combination of full and stochastic gradients for strongly convex problems.
- Strength: clarity — well-organized exposition, precise algorithm description, and a helpful comparison table.
- Weakness: ML significance — under standard ML regularization (κ=O(N)), EMGD outperforms batch GD only when N=O(1), which is impractical.
- Weakness: convergence guarantee — EMGD gives high-probability linear convergence versus deterministic guarantees from some batch methods.
- Weakness: technical details need fixes and clarifications (explicit κ=L/λ, eqn/notation errors in eqs.4,6,13 and lines 286/288).
- Argument for acceptance: technically sound, novel approach addressing a genuine optimization challenge with rigorous analysis.
- Argument against acceptance: claimed practical advantage is limited to trivial problem sizes and probabilistic guarantees may be undesirable.
- Recommendation: reject unless revised to demonstrate practical ML relevance or explicitly acknowledge and delimit EMGD’s applicable scenarios.
