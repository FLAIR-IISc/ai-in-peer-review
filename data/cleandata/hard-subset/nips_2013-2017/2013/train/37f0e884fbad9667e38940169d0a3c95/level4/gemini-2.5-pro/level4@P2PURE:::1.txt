**SUMMARY**

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex set, under the assumption that the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. While Nesterov's optimal first-order algorithm achieves a linear convergence rate for this problem class, its convergence constant depends on the square root of the condition number, κ.

The authors consider a setting where both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic gradient oracle are available. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires only O(log 1/ε) calls to the full gradient oracle—a complexity independent of the condition number—and O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle. In theory, this approach can be faster than standard methods, provided the condition number is not excessively large.

The central idea of EMGD is to structure the optimization into 'epochs.' Each epoch begins with a single full gradient computation. This full gradient is then combined with O(κ²) new stochastic gradients in a series of 'mixed gradient' steps. By averaging the iterates within an epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant factor decrease in suboptimality, independent of the condition number. This explains why the number of full gradient computations is independent of κ. The paper provides a simple, complete, and self-contained proof of this convergence rate but includes no experiments.

I enjoyed reading this paper; the idea of interleaving a small number of expensive full gradient computations with a large number of cheap stochastic steps is both novel and interesting. This work joins a recent line of research aiming to achieve linear convergence rates using stochastic oracles. A key point of comparison is the SAG algorithm [16], a recent breakthrough for unconstrained, smooth, strongly convex objectives that are sums of n functions (a common structure in empirical risk minimization).

Assuming the full gradient is n times more expensive than a stochastic one, SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n/8. In contrast, EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3). However, EMGD is theoretically slower than SAG in all regimes, though they share the same O(n log(1/ε)) complexity when κ ≤ n^(1/2). For context, if κ = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD have a complexity of O(n log(1/ε)), while Nesterov's method is slower at O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)).

Despite being theoretically slower in some regimes, EMGD offers significant advantages over SAG. First, as the authors note, EMGD is applicable to constrained optimization problems (provided the gradient mapping update is efficient), whereas SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems. Second, the convergence proof for EMGD is substantially simpler than that for SAG. This simplicity could yield further insights and make it easier to develop modifications to the algorithm with provable guarantees. Furthermore, the convergence result for EMGD holds with high probability, which is a stronger guarantee than the in-expectation result for SAG. The authors also suggest a potential memory or parallelization advantage, but this point is less convincing, as SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which similarly reduces memory requirements.

**EVALUATION SUMMARY**

**Pros:**
*   The paper presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theoretical foundation.
*   Like SAG, it achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization where the complexity does not involve the product of the number of training examples (n) and the condition number (κ).
*   The algorithm is more general than some competitors (e.g., it works for constrained optimization), and its proof is much simpler.
*   The paper is clearly written, and the proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**
*   The paper lacks experiments to demonstrate that the algorithm could make a difference in practice or to provide a concrete example where its theoretical advantages are relevant.
*   There is no discussion of the algorithm's limitations or drawbacks, especially in comparison to existing algorithms. Section 3.4 should be substantially improved.
*   The proof, while correct, lacks high-level comments that would provide intuition for its construction.

**QUALITY:** The paper is technically sound. While experiments would have been appreciated, the theoretical contribution can stand on its own, provided the authors expand Section 3.4 to address the algorithm's limitations and drawbacks.

Specifically, the authors should add a more concrete comparison with SAG and Nesterov's method for the sum-of-n-functions case, highlighting the different performance regimes. For example: EMGD is worse than Nesterov for κ > n^(2/3); SAG and EMGD have similar performance for 1 ≤ κ ≤ n^(1/2); SAG is better than EMGD (and Nesterov) for n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8; and SAG is worse than Nesterov for κ > n/8.

A major practical drawback of EMGD for practitioners is that the number of steps within an epoch must be fixed in advance using knowledge of κ. In contrast, both SCDA [17] and SAG do not require fixing the number of steps and can thus benefit from faster-than-predicted practical convergence or better local condition numbers. Moreover, SCDA features automatic step-size selection, and SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic that works well in practice. This discussion should be added to the paper.

[**ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers**]
a) As another reviewer noted, in machine learning practice, the regularization parameter is often set to C/n, leading to a condition number κ ≈ O(n). In this important regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov's method, whereas EMGD does not. This limitation should be pointed out, and perhaps the authors could mention another practical setting where the condition κ < n^(2/3) is relevant.
b) The authors should cite and discuss "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" by M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt (SISC, 2012), which also presents a hybrid algorithm with a linear rate of convergence. Although the condition number still appears in the rate of the latter algorithm (making it ultimately no faster than standard gradient descent), the current submission offers a theoretical improvement for problems where κ is not too large relative to n.

**CLARITY:** The paper is fairly clear. Table 1 provides an excellent summary of convergence rates, a feature not always presented so clearly in the literature. The proof can be followed, but it would be more useful if the authors added high-level comments to motivate some of the defined quantities, which currently fit together in a way that seems a bit magical.

**ORIGINALITY:** The work represents a novel combination of known techniques.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The practical relevance of the algorithm has not yet been demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution could have an impact. In the context of the difficult convergence proof for SAG (and the simple proof for SDCA, which applies to a more restricted setup), the simple proof of EMGD is a major contribution.
