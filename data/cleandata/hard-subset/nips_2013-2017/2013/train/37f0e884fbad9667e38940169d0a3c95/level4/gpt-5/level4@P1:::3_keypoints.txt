- Proposal: Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid method that caches a full gradient each epoch and uses stochastic gradient differences within epochs.  
- Algorithmic structure: epochs with one full-gradient evaluation at epoch start and T projected updates using a mixed gradient; shrinking trust-region constraint.  
- Convergence guarantee: linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, under smoothness and strong convexity.  
- Analysis: high-probability bounds using a martingale (Hoeffding–Azuma) argument to control within-epoch stochastic error.  
- Comparison to prior work: contrasts with accelerated full-gradient methods (O(√κ log(1/ε))) and purely stochastic or finite-sum methods; aims to reduce costly full-gradient evaluations.  
- Strength: conceptually clean hybridization that corrects cached full gradients with stochastic differences inside a shrinking domain.  
- Strength: convergence proof appears correct and novel, providing stronger high-probability guarantees than many stochastic methods.  
- Strength: simple to implement with both oracles, supports constrained problems via projection, and uses only O(d) memory.  
- Main concern: unclear practical advantage in typical ML settings where strong convexity comes from ℓ2-regularization and the parameter scales with sample size N.  
- Quantitative concern: under μ ≈ C·N, EMGD outperforms batch accelerated methods only when N ≤ O(C^3/L^3), a regime that may be impractical.  
- Needed clarity: paper should delineate regimes of κ, N, and oracle-cost ratios where EMGD reduces wall-clock time compared to full-gradient methods.  
- Needed clarification: explicitly discuss and justify the μ ≈ C·N assumption and the claim about the N ≤ O(C^3/L^3) threshold.  
- Guarantee contrast: emphasize the high-probability nature of EMGD’s guarantee versus deterministic guarantees for accelerated full-gradient methods.  
- Quality: technically sound with well-structured high-probability analysis, though minor issues remain.  
- Clarity: mostly clear but marred by notational and editorial slips; more explicit discussion of practical advantage would help.  
- Originality: the mixed-gradient epoch scheme and analysis are novel in this precise form.  
- Significance: potentially significant if regimes with expensive full gradients and cheap stochastic gradients are common and convincingly demonstrated.  
- Pro-acceptance: novel hybrid with clean analysis, high-probability linear rate, constrained-problem handling, and low memory.  
- Contra-acceptance: unclear advantage over accelerated full-gradient methods in typical ML regimes; lacks empirical or detailed theoretical comparisons to show practical superiority.  
- Revision requests: provide precise practical comparisons (ranges of κ, N, oracle-cost ratios), justify ML assumptions (μ ≈ C·N), and contrast high-probability vs deterministic guarantees.  
- Minor corrections: fix Eq.(6) to use ||w*−ŵ||^2; specify which function inequality (7) applies to (line 288); remove unnecessary equality (line 286); use norms in (13); state L ≥ λ and justify; replace x^→w^ and f→F in (4); define κ = L/λ; use a single symbol for strong convexity (λ) consistently.  
- Overall: technically solid and potentially useful, but needs clearer demonstration of practical advantage and fixes to minor notational/editorial issues.
