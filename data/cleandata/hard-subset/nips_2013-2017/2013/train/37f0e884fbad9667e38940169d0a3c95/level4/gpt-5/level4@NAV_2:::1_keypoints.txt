- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid using occasional full gradients plus many cheap stochastic gradients to optimize smooth strongly convex objectives.  
- EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of κ) and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, and epochs combine one full gradient with O(κ^2) mixed steps.  
- Averaging iterates within an epoch yields a constant, κ-independent reduction in suboptimality, making the number of epochs (and full-gradient calls) κ-independent.  
- Analysis is straightforward, self-contained, and provides high-probability linear convergence guarantees; the paper contains no experiments.  
- EMGD is appealing as a fresh synthesis that preserves linear convergence while leveraging stochastic oracles.  
- Compared to SAG, EMGD handles constrained problems and has a substantially simpler proof with high-probability (vs. in-expectation) guarantees.  
- In finite-sum settings, EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3) but is asymptotically slower than SAG except when κ ≤ n^(1/2); regime comparisons are detailed.  
- Claimed memory and parallelization benefits are unclear because SAG can use mini-batching, which aids both.  
- Major cons: no empirical results, limited discussion of limitations/trade-offs (Section 3.4 needs expansion), and proofs lack high-level intuition.  
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires fixing the number of inner iterations using knowledge of κ, unlike SDCA/SAG which adapt and often benefit from faster-than-worst-case behavior.  
- Addendum: in common ML regimes with regularization scaling like 1/n (κ ≈ n/C) SAG still improves over EMGD; authors should cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid methods.  
- Clarity: overall clear with helpful Table 1, but proofs would benefit from more intuition and several concrete textual/notation fixes.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound and potentially influential theoretically, but needs experiments and a fuller discussion of practical properties and limitations.
