**Summary**  
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm for optimizing smooth strongly convex functions over convex sets. EMGD leverages both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic oracle. It achieves linear convergence with \(O(\log(1/\epsilon))\) full gradient calls (independent of the condition number \(\kappa\)) and \(O(\kappa^2 \log(1/\epsilon))\) stochastic calls, provided \(\kappa\) is bounded. The key insight replaces each full gradient step (an "epoch") with \(O(\kappa^2)\) mixed gradient steps using a fixed full gradient and adaptive stochastic gradients. Averaging iterates within an epoch yields suboptimality reduction independent of \(\kappa\), enabling the epoch count to be \(\kappa\)-independent. The authors provide a concise, self-contained convergence proof but omit experiments.  

**Evaluation**  
The work presents a novel algorithmic approach that blends deterministic and stochastic steps, offering a theoretically compelling alternative to Nesterov’s method. Its primary strengths are:  
1. **Theoretical novelty**: EMGD achieves linear convergence without \(\kappa\) in the full gradient complexity, unlike Nesterov’s \(O(\sqrt{\kappa})\) dependence.  
2. **Generality**: Unlike SAG (which requires unconstrained problems and separable objectives), EMGD handles constrained optimization with efficient gradient mapping.  
3. **Simplicity**: The proof is significantly cleaner than SAG’s, offering clearer insights and potential for extension.  
4. **Practical relevance**: The algorithm’s structure may facilitate memory-efficient or parallel implementations (though this requires further elaboration).  

**Comparison to SAG**  
In the common setting where the objective is a sum of \(n\) functions (e.g., regularized empirical risk), SAG achieves linear convergence when \(\kappa \leq n/8\) with \(O(n \log(1/\epsilon))\) complexity. EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov only when \(\kappa \leq n^{2/3}\), but:  
- For \(\kappa \leq n^{1/2}\), EMGD matches SAG’s \(O(n \log(1/\epsilon))\) rate.  
- For \(n^{1/2} < \kappa \leq n/8\), SAG outperforms EMGD.  
- For \(\kappa > n/8\), both SAG and EMGD are slower than Nesterov.  
Critically, EMGD’s requirement to fix the epoch length \(O(\kappa^2)\) *a priori* (vs. SAG’s adaptive step size) is a practical drawback, as it prevents leveraging local condition number improvements or automatic step-size tuning.  

**Major Concerns**  
1. **Lack of experiments**: No empirical validation of the algorithm’s practical impact or comparison to baselines (SAG, Nesterov). This is especially problematic given the theoretical regime (\(\kappa \leq n^{2/3}\)) where EMGD could outperform SAG—a regime common in machine learning (e.g., with regularization \(\sim C/n\), \(\kappa \sim n/C\)). The authors should demonstrate a concrete scenario where EMGD’s theoretical advantages manifest in practice.  
2. **Incomplete comparison**: Section 3.4 requires expansion to address limitations:  
   - EMGD is strictly dominated by SAG for \(\kappa > n^{1/2}\), a regime where SAG is empirically effective.  
   - The algorithm’s *a priori* epoch length requirement contrasts with SAG’s adaptive step size, which often yields faster practical convergence.  
   - A brief discussion of the Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) hybrid method (which uses cheaper steps but retains \(\kappa\)-dependence in the rate) would contextualize EMGD’s advancement.  
3. **Proof clarity**: The proof lacks high-level intuition for key steps (e.g., why averaging within epochs decouples from \(\kappa\)). Adding a sentence or two to motivate these choices would greatly enhance accessibility.

**Conclusion**  
EMGD offers a theoretically sound, elegant solution to a well-motivated problem, with a proof that is notably simpler than SAG’s. Its generality for constrained optimization and \(\kappa\)-independent full gradient complexity are significant contributions. However, the absence of experiments and incomplete discussion of practical limitations (especially the epoch-length constraint and comparison to SAG) prevent the paper from fully demonstrating its value. If the authors address these points—particularly by adding a concrete example of \(\kappa \leq n^{2/3}\) and clarifying EMGD’s practical trade-offs—the paper would be a strong contribution. As it stands, the theoretical novelty is compelling, but the significance hinges on future empirical validation.  

**Recommendation**: Accept (with major revisions addressing the above concerns). The core theoretical contribution is robust and novel, but the paper requires substantial expansion of its practical discussion and comparison.
