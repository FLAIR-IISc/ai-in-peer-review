This paper introduces a novel hybrid algorithm for stochastic gradient descent that integrates both stochastic and full-batch gradients. The theoretical analysis shows convergence to an epsilon-accurate solution with a complexity of O(log(1/eps)) full gradient computations and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) stochastic gradient computations. The convergence proof appears to be sound and original, despite a few minor errors detailed below.

My primary concern is the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm within the machine learning context, which is the central focus of this conference. In typical regularized machine learning models, the strong convexity constant (μ) is dictated by the regularizer and thus scales with the number of samples, N (i.e., μ ≈ CN for a constant C). Under this common assumption, the proposed method is only more efficient than batch gradient descent when the number of samples is bounded by O(C^3/L^3). This seems to be a very restrictive regime with limited practical interest. Furthermore, the convergence guarantee for the proposed algorithm is probabilistic (holding with high probability), whereas the guarantee for batch gradient descent is deterministic.

This point is critical and requires a thorough discussion. To demonstrate the paper's significance to the machine learning community, the authors must clearly establish the conditions under which their algorithm offers a tangible advantage over the standard batch gradient descent baseline.
