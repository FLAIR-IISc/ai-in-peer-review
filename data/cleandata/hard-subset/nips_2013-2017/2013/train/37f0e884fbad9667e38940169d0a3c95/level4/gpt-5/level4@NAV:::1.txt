SUMMARY: The paper studies minimizing a smooth, strongly convex objective over a convex constraint set where gradient-mapping updates are efficient. Nesterov’s optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence, but the prefactor depends on the square root of the condition number k. The authors consider access to both an expensive full-gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic-gradient oracle, and introduce a hybrid method that uses only O(log(1/eps)) full-gradient evaluations (independent of k) and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) stochastic-gradient calls. When k is moderate, this can be advantageous in theory. The core idea (Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent, EMGD) is to replace each full-gradient step (an epoch) by a fixed budget of O(k^2) mixed steps that combine the single epoch full gradient with per-iteration stochastic gradients. Averaging the O(k^2) iterates within each epoch yields a constant contraction in suboptimality that does not depend on k, so the number of epochs (full-gradient computations) is k-independent. The authors give a clear, self-contained convergence proof, but provide no experiments.

I found the paper enjoyable and the hybridization of a few full-gradient computations with many low-cost stochastic steps both novel and appealing. This work aligns with recent efforts to leverage stochastic oracles while retaining linear rates. The SAG algorithm [16] is a recent theoretical and practical advance for smooth, strongly convex objectives that are sums of n component functions (e.g., regularized ERM), where a full gradient typically costs n times a stochastic gradient. In that setting, SAG outperforms Nesterov when k <= n/8. By contrast, EMGD beats Nesterov theoretically when k <= n^(2/3), yet EMGD is slower than SAG across all regimes (and matches SAG only in big-O when k <= n^(1/2)). For illustration, when k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD cost O(n log(1/eps)), while Nesterov requires O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)). Two notable advantages of EMGD over SAG are: 1) EMGD handles constrained problems (assuming efficient gradient-mapping updates), whereas SAG has so far been developed for unconstrained settings; and 2) EMGD’s analysis is far simpler than SAG’s, potentially offering more insight and easier extensions, with the added benefit that EMGD’s guarantee holds with high probability rather than only in expectation. The paper mentions possible memory/parallelization benefits, though this is less convincing since SAG can also use mini-batches (which can reduce memory and enable parallelism).

EVALUATION SUMMARY:
Pros:
- Presents an interesting hybrid algorithm with clean, simple, and rigorous analysis.
- Like SAG, achieves linear rates for regularized ERM without a multiplicative k factor on n in the constant; it is also more general (supports constraints) and the proof is considerably simpler.
- Clear exposition and a self-contained proof.

Cons:
- No experiments to demonstrate practical impact (nor concrete case studies explaining when the theoretical advantages would matter in practice).
- Insufficient discussion of limitations and trade-offs relative to existing methods (section 3.4 should be expanded; see suggestions below).
- The proof would benefit from more high-level intuition explaining the key constructs.

QUALITY: The technical content is solid. An empirical section would help, but the theory is strong enough to stand alone. Section 3.4 should be expanded to cover EMGD’s limitations and drawbacks. Please also add a concrete treatment of the sum-of-n-functions case to clarify differences with SAG and Nesterov (e.g., EMGD is worse than Nesterov for roughly k > n^(2/3); SAG matches EMGD for 1 <= k <= n^(1/2); SAG is better than both EMGD and Nesterov for n^(1/2) < k <= n/8; and SAG is worse than Nesterov for k > n/8). A practical drawback is that EMGD requires fixing the number of inner steps per epoch in advance, using knowledge of k. In contrast, SCDA [17] and SAG do not require a pre-fixed inner length and can capitalize on faster-than-worst-case behavior or better local conditioning. Moreover, SCDA selects step sizes automatically, and SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic that works well in practice. This comparison should be included.

[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: a) As another reviewer noted, in ML practice the regularization is often C/n, giving k ≈ n/C′. In that regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov, but EMGD does not; this should be acknowledged, and perhaps another realistic scenario with k < n^(2/3) should be highlighted. b) Please cite: Hybrid Deterministic–Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting (M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt, SISC, 2012), which also proposes a hybrid method with a linear rate. Their rate retains dependence on the condition number, so it is not ultimately faster than standard gradient descent (only the initial phase benefits from cheaper steps). The present submission still offers a theoretical improvement when k is not too large relative to n.]

CLARITY: The paper is quite clear. I appreciated the concise summary in Table 1, which I have not seen presented so clearly elsewhere. The proof is traceable but would be more helpful with brief high-level explanations that motivate the chosen definitions, which currently feel a bit ad hoc.

ORIGINALITY: A fresh combination of established techniques.

SIGNIFICANCE: Practical relevance remains to be shown, but the theoretical contribution could be impactful. Given the difficulty of SAG’s analysis (and SDCA’s simplicity but narrower scope), EMGD’s simple proof is a notable contribution.
