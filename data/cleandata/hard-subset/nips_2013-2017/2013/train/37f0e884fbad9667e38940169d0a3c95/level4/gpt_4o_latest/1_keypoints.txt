- Paper studies optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions with both expensive full-gradient and cheaper stochastic-gradient oracles.  
- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of condition number k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.  
- EMGD replaces each full-gradient epoch with O(k^2) mixed stochastic steps and averages iterates to get k-independent full-gradient complexity.  
- Theoretical proof is simple, self-contained, and yields high-probability linear convergence.  
- EMGD could outperform Nesterov when k is not too large (specifically when k ≤ n^{2/3} in the n-function setting).  
- Compared to SAG, EMGD is slower in most regimes but matches SAG when k ≤ n^{1/2}; EMGD outperforms Nesterov for k ≤ n^{2/3}.  
- EMGD's advantages over SAG: applicable to constrained problems and has a simpler proof with high-probability guarantees.  
- Potential practical benefits claimed (memory/parallelism) are less clear since SAG can use mini-batches for similar effects.  
- Main pros: novel algorithmic idea, clean theoretical guarantees, generality to constrained optimization, and clearer proof than SAG.  
- Main cons: no experimental validation to demonstrate practical relevance.  
- Main cons: insufficient discussion of limitations and comparisons to existing methods (expand Section 3.4).  
- Main cons: proof would benefit from higher-level explanations to clarify key insights.  
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires pre-specifying number of steps per epoch based on knowledge of k (non-adaptive), unlike SCDA and SAG.  
- SCDA and SAG have adaptive/automatic step-size or heuristics in practice; EMGD lacks dynamic adaptation discussion.  
- In common ML regularization (regularizer ~ C/n → k ≈ n/C'), EMGD does not outperform SAG; this regime should be explicitly discussed.  
- Related work: should cite Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods and contrast condition-number dependence.  
- Paper is technically sound but would be strengthened by experiments and an expanded discussion of n-function examples and regime boundaries (k > n^{2/3}, k ranges vs. SAG/Nesterov).  
- Clarity: overall clear and well-written, Table 1 is helpful, but some quantities and proof steps need more intuitive motivation.  
- Detailed editorial suggestions: clarify several lines/statements (lines 50, 118–119, 132, 139, 224, 288, 304, 366) and fix multiple typos and notation inconsistencies.  
- After rebuttal: reviewer is generally satisfied but expects the revised paper to incorporate the suggested clarifications, expanded limitations discussion, and corrections.
