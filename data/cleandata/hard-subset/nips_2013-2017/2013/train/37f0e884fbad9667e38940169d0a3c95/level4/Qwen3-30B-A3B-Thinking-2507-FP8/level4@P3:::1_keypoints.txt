- Problem & goal: Reduce dependence on condition number κ for optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions by mixing full and stochastic gradients.

- Algorithm & claim: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) achieving linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (κ-independent) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

- Comparison to classics: Improves over Nesterov which needs O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients.

- Sum-of-functions case: EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3) but is slower than SAG when κ > n^(1/2).

- Relation to prior work: Builds on SAG and SDCA literature, offering a different tradeoff and applicability to constrained problems where SAG/SDCA don't apply.

- Proof contribution: Provides a simpler, high-probability linear-convergence proof compared to more complex analyses for SAG/SDCA.

- Originality (8/10): Novel epoch-wise mixing of full and stochastic gradients and clear differentiation from related methods.

- Quality (7/10): Sound, well-structured theoretical analysis with a complete proof and correct identification of method trade-offs.

- Clarity (8/10): Well-written and organized; algorithm description and table are clear though high-level proof motivations could be added.

- Significance (7/10): Theoretical relevance, especially for constrained optimization, but practical impact needs stronger justification.

- Major weakness — experiments: No empirical validation provided, making practical utility and runtime behavior uncertain.

- Major weakness — related work comparison: Section 3.4 incompletely discusses limitations (requires κ knowledge, overstated memory advantage, unclear practical regimes for κ ≤ n^(2/3)).

- Major weakness — practical considerations: Lacks discussion and empirical treatment for unknown or varying κ and speculative remarks on estimation methods.

- Actionable revisions requested: Add experiments comparing EMGD to SAG/SDCA/Nesterov across condition numbers; expand Section 3.4 on limitations; clarify regimes where κ ≤ n^(2/3) is relevant; fix minor notation/citation issues.

- Recommendation & overall assessment: Theoretically strong and novel with simpler proof; recommend acceptance with minor revisions contingent on stronger practical grounding and clearer comparisons.
