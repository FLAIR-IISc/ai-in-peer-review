SUMMARY: The paper studies minimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex set where the gradient mapping update is efficient. Nesterov’s optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence, but with a constant that scales with the square root of the condition number k. The authors assume access to both an expensive full-gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic-gradient oracle, and propose a hybrid method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD). EMGD uses only O(log(1/eps)) full-gradient evaluations (independent of k) and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) stochastic-gradient calls. When k is not too large, this can be faster in theory. The core idea is to replace each full-gradient “epoch” with O(k^2) mixed-gradient steps that combine a single full gradient (fixed within the epoch) with varying stochastic gradients; averaging the iterates within the epoch yields a constant reduction in suboptimality independent of k. The analysis is simple and self-contained, but the paper includes no experiments.

I found the paper enjoyable and the mix of a few full gradients with many cheap stochastic steps novel and interesting. This work fits into a line of recent methods that aim to leverage cheap stochastic oracles while preserving linear rates. A recent theoretical and practical advance is the SAG algorithm [16], for smooth strongly convex objectives that are sums of n simple terms (e.g., regularized ERM). Under the standard assumption that a full gradient costs n times a stochastic gradient, SAG outperforms Nesterov when k ≤ n/8. In contrast, EMGD improves over Nesterov when k ≤ n^(2/3), but is slower than SAG across all regimes (it matches SAG’s big-O only when k ≤ n^(1/2)). For illustration, if k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD achieve O(n log(1/eps)), while Nesterov requires O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)). Two advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) EMGD handles constrained problems (assuming efficient gradient-mapping updates), whereas SAG has so far been developed for unconstrained settings; and (2) EMGD’s convergence proof is much simpler and yields high-probability guarantees, potentially making extensions easier. The claimed memory/parallelization advantage is less clear, since SAG can also use mini-batches (which help both parallelism and memory).

EVALUATION SUMMARY
Pros:
- A novel, appealing algorithmic idea with clean, simple, and solid theory.
- Like SAG, achieves a linear rate for regularized ERM without multiplying the constant by n, but is more general (supports constraints) with a much simpler proof.
- Clear writing and a self-contained analysis.

Cons:
- No experiments demonstrating practical impact or illustrating when the theoretical advantages matter in practice.
- Limited discussion of limitations and trade-offs compared to existing methods (Section 3.4 should be expanded; see suggestions below).
- The proof could benefit from more high-level commentary that explains its key insights.

QUALITY: The results appear technically sound. Experiments would strengthen the paper, but the theoretical contribution can stand on its own. The authors should expand Section 3.4 to discuss limitations and drawbacks. They should also more concretely analyze the sum-of-n-functions case, highlighting the regimes relative to SAG and Nesterov: EMGD is worse than Nesterov when roughly k > n^(2/3); SAG and EMGD coincide (big-O) for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2); SAG is better than EMGD (and Nesterov) for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8; SAG is worse than Nesterov for k > n/8. A key practical drawback of EMGD is the need to fix the number of steps per epoch in advance, using knowledge of k. In contrast, SCDA [17] and SAG do not require fixing the number of steps and can benefit from faster-than-worst-case behavior (e.g., better local conditioning). SCDA also has automatic step-size selection; SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic that works well in practice. Please add this discussion.

ADDENDUM (after discussion with other reviewers):
a) In many ML settings, regularization is C/n, giving condition number ≈ n/C′. This is a regime where SAG still outperforms Nesterov, but EMGD does not. This should be acknowledged, and perhaps provide another realistic setting where k < n^(2/3).
b) Please cite: M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt, “Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting,” SISC, 2012. They also propose a hybrid method with a linear rate, but with k appearing in the rate; thus the present submission can improve on that when k is not too large relative to n.

CLARITY: The paper is generally clear. The summary in Table 1 is particularly helpful and not commonly presented so clearly elsewhere. The proof is tight and readable, but would benefit from high-level motivation for some of the key definitions and how they fit together.

ORIGINALITY: A novel combination of known techniques.

SIGNIFICANCE: Practical relevance remains to be demonstrated, but the theoretical contribution is potentially impactful. Given the difficulty of SAG’s proof (and the simplicity of SDCA, which applies in a narrower setting), EMGD’s simple proof is a notable contribution.
