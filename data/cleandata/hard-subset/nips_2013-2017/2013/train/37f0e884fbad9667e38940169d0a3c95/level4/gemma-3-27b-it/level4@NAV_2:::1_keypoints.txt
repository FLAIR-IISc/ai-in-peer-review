- Problem and contribution: proposes EMGD, a hybrid algorithm using few full gradients and many cheap stochastic gradients to get O(log(1/eps)) full-gradient and O(κ^2 log(1/eps)) stochastic-gradient calls for smooth strongly convex constrained problems.

- Algorithmic idea: replace each full-gradient epoch by O(κ^2) mixed-gradient steps (one full gradient per epoch + stochastic steps) and average iterates to obtain an epoch decrease independent of κ.

- Theoretical result: simple, self-contained proof of linear convergence holding with high probability.

- Novelty: mixing full and stochastic gradients for linear rates is novel and situates well among recent work (e.g., SAG, SCDA).

- Comparison to SAG/Nesterov: EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ <= n^(2/3) in the sum-of-n-functions setting, while SAG outperforms Nesterov when κ <= n/8; EMGD is generally slower than SAG and matches SAG only when κ ≈ n^(1/2).

- Advantages over SAG: EMGD applies to constrained problems and has a much simpler/high-probability convergence proof.

- Practical/implementation issues: no experiments to support practical relevance or identify problem regimes where EMGD helps.

- Missing discussion: paper lacks a clear section on limitations/drawbacks and direct comparisons to existing methods (section 3.4 should be expanded).

- Practical drawback: EMGD requires fixing the number of steps per epoch in advance (depends on knowing κ), unlike SCDA and SAG which do not require this.

- Step-size and adaptivity: EMGD lacks adaptive step-size or practical heuristics that SCDA (auto step-size) and SAG (adaptive heuristic) provide.

- Parallelization/memory claim: claimed memory/parallelization advantages are unclear since SAG can use mini-batches and parallelism to reduce memory and exploit distributed computing.

- Requested additions: add concrete discussion of the sum-of-n-functions example delineating regimes where EMGD/SAG/Nesterov are better (reviewer gives threshold comparisons).

- Literature: cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods and clarify how EMGD improves on that work.

- Proof exposition: include higher-level motivation for key quantities and steps to make the proof less “magical” and more insightful.

- Clarity: paper is fairly clear and well written; Table 1 is particularly helpful.

- Minor technical clarifications: several suggested clarifications (e.g., specify “for all w”, note E[∇f(w)] = ∇F(w), correct derivation comments at specific lines).

- Typos and notation fixes: reviewer lists multiple editorial corrections (e.g., “an convex” → “a convex”, consistent domain notation D vs. Ω, use norm symbols, ln vs log).
