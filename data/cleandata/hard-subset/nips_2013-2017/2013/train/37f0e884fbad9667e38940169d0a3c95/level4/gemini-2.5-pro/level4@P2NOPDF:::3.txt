This paper presents a novel hybrid strategy for stochastic gradient descent that combines full-batch and stochastic gradient updates. The authors provide a convergence proof guaranteeing that the algorithm achieves an epsilon-accurate solution using O(log(1/eps)) full gradient computations and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) stochastic gradient computations. The convergence analysis appears to be sound and novel, notwithstanding a few minor issues detailed below.

My primary concern regards the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm within the context of machine learning, the main focus of this conference. In many common ML applications, such as regularized empirical risk minimization, the strong convexity constant (μ) is determined by the regularization parameter. Consequently, μ is often proportional to the number of training samples N (i.e., μ ≈ C*N for some constant C that is independent of N).

Under this realistic assumption, the proposed method's theoretical guarantees suggest it would outperform standard batch gradient descent only when the number of samples N is relatively small, specifically when N is bounded by a term on the order of O(C^3/L^3). This appears to be a restrictive and potentially uninteresting regime for large-scale machine learning, where N is typically very large. Furthermore, it is worth noting that the convergence guarantee for the proposed algorithm is probabilistic (holding in high probability), whereas the guarantee for standard batch gradient descent is deterministic.

This point is critical and requires careful discussion. To establish the paper's relevance to the ML community, the authors must more clearly articulate the scenarios where their algorithm offers a tangible advantage over standard batch gradient descent.
