The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update is efficiently computable. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence, its convergence rate is dependent on the square root of the condition number, k. This paper proposes a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), leveraging both expensive full gradients and cheaper stochastic gradient oracles. EMGD requires O(log 1/ε) full gradient evaluations (independent of k) and O(k² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. This approach offers potential speedups over Nesterov’s algorithm when the condition number is sufficiently small. The core idea involves replacing full gradient steps (epochs) with a fixed number, O(k²), of mixed gradient steps—a combination of the full gradient (computed once per epoch) and stochastic gradients. By averaging the iterates within each epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant decrease in suboptimality, independent of the condition number, leading to a number of required full gradient steps independent of k. The paper provides a self-contained and straightforward proof of the convergence rate, but lacks experimental validation.

I found this paper enjoyable and believe the concept of integrating full gradient computations with numerous stochastic gradient steps is both novel and interesting. The work contributes to the growing body of research aimed at utilizing cheaper stochastic oracles while preserving linear convergence rates. A significant recent advancement in this area is the SAG algorithm [16], which applies to smooth, strongly convex objectives expressed as the sum of n simple functions (e.g., regularized empirical loss minimization). SAG demonstrates theoretical speedups over Nesterov’s algorithm when k ≤ n/8, assuming the full gradient is n times more expensive than the stochastic gradient. In contrast, EMGD theoretically outperforms Nesterov’s algorithm when k ≤ n^(2/3), but is consistently slower than SAG across all regimes (sharing the same big O notation when k ≤ n^(1/2)). To illustrate this, when k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD exhibit O(n log(1/ε)) complexity, while Nesterov’s algorithm is O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)). The key advantages of EMGD over SAG appear to be: 1) its applicability to constrained optimization (given an efficient gradient mapping update), unlike the unconstrained nature of SAG; and 2) its considerably simpler convergence proof, which may facilitate deeper insights and easier modifications with provable guarantees (its result also holds with high probability versus SAG’s in expectation guarantee). The authors also suggest potential memory/parallelization advantages, although SAG's parallelization via mini-batches can also reduce memory requirements.

**EVALUATION SUMMARY:**

**Pros:**
- Presents a novel algorithmic idea with a clear, simple, and sound theoretical foundation.
-  Similar to SAG, achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization, independent of the condition number multiplied by the number of training examples (n); however, the algorithm is more general (applicable to constrained optimization) and benefits from a significantly simpler proof.
- The paper is well-written and its proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**
- Lacks experimental results to demonstrate the practical impact of the algorithm.  It also doesn’t provide a concrete example illustrating the relevance of its theoretical advantages.
- The discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and drawbacks, especially in comparison to existing algorithms, is insufficient and requires improvement (particularly Section 3.4).
- The proof would benefit from higher-level comments to clarify the rationale behind essential insights driving its construction.

**QUALITY:** The paper is technically sound. While experiments would be valuable, I believe the theoretical contribution is significant enough to stand on its own. The authors should definitively expand Section 3.4 to address the limitations and drawbacks of their algorithm. They should also provide a more thorough discussion of the sum of n functions example, highlighting the differences with SAG and Nesterov (e.g., EMGD is worse than Nesterov for roughly k > n^(2/3); SAG performs similarly to EMGD for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2); better than EMGD and Nesterov for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8; and worse than Nesterov for k > n/8). A practical limitation of EMGD is the need to pre-define the number of steps within an epoch based on knowledge of k, whereas SCDA [17] and SAG dynamically adapt, potentially achieving faster practical convergence or benefiting from better local condition numbers. Additionally, SCDA offers automatic step-size selection, while SAG employs an adaptive step-size heuristic that performs well in practice. These points should be discussed within the paper.

[**ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers:** a) As noted by another reviewer, in typical machine learning settings, the regularization parameter is often C/n, resulting in a condition number of approximately n/C', a regime where SAG still outperforms Nesterov, but EMGD does not. This should be pointed out, and another practical scenario where k < n^(2/3) should be included. b) The authors should cite [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012], which presents a similar hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear convergence rate. However, this algorithm still includes the condition number in the rate, resulting in an ultimate rate no faster than standard gradient descent—the current submission can theoretically improve upon this rate when k is not excessively large relative to n.]

**CLARITY:** The paper is generally clear, and the summary in Table 1 is particularly well-presented and novel. The proof is logically sound but could be enhanced with high-level comments justifying the key quantities and their interrelationships.

**ORIGINALITY:** This work represents a novel combination of established techniques.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** While the practical relevance remains undemonstrated, the theoretical contribution has the potential to be impactful. In light of the complex convergence proof for SAG and the relatively simple proof for SDCA (albeit limited to a specific setup), the streamlined proof of EMGD is a notable achievement.
