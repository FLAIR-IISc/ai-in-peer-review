

This paper proposes a hybrid stochastic/batch gradient descent algorithm combining stochastic and batch gradients, claiming convergence to an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(k² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The convergence proof appears technically sound and novel, with minor notational errors addressed below. However, the paper's core contribution lacks clear relevance to machine learning (ML) optimization, which is central to NIPS.

The primary concern is the impracticality of the proposed method's advantage over batch gradient descent (BGD) in standard ML settings. In ML, strong convexity typically arises from regularization (e.g., L2), making the strong convexity parameter μ scale linearly with the sample size N (μ = Θ(N), specifically μ = CN for constant C independent of N). Under this standard assumption, the hybrid method outperforms BGD only when N = O(C³/L³). This bound represents an uninteresting regime for practical ML, where N is typically large (e.g., millions of samples). For instance, with typical values C = 10⁻³, L = 10, this requires N < 10, which is irrelevant for real-world datasets. Consequently, the proposed algorithm offers no meaningful speedup over BGD in settings where regularization dominates convexity.

Furthermore, the hybrid method's convergence guarantee holds only with high probability, whereas BGD's convergence is deterministic. This probabilistic nature introduces additional complexity in practical deployment and reproducibility, without compensating gains in the relevant regime. The paper must explicitly address these points to justify its relevance to the ML community. Without demonstrating a clear advantage—such as significantly faster convergence on large-scale problems with standard regularization—the contribution appears theoretical rather than practically impactful for NIPS.

**Summary:** While the algorithmic approach and convergence analysis are technically valid, the paper fails to establish a meaningful advantage over BGD in practical ML optimization. The claimed speedup is confined to a trivial regime (N = O(1)), and the high-probability convergence does not offset the lack of deterministic guarantees. The authors must rigorously demonstrate relevance to large-scale ML—e.g., by showing empirical gains on standard datasets with regularization, or by identifying non-standard settings where μ = Θ(1) (e.g., small N or no regularization). Without this, the contribution is unlikely to interest the NIPS community. The minor corrections above are straightforward to address.
