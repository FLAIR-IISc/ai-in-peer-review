- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid using expensive full gradients and cheaper stochastic gradients for smooth strongly convex optimization over convex sets.  
- Claims linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of condition number κ) and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic calls when κ is bounded.  
- Key insight: replace each full-gradient epoch by O(κ^2) mixed-gradient steps using a fixed full gradient plus adaptive stochastic gradients, with intra-epoch averaging reducing suboptimality independent of κ.  
- Provides a concise, self-contained convergence proof but omits empirical experiments.  
- Theoretical novelty: achieves linear convergence without κ in full-gradient complexity, contrasting with Nesterov’s O(√κ) dependence.  
- Generality: handles constrained problems and efficient gradient mapping, unlike SAG which requires unconstrained, separable objectives.  
- Simplicity: proof is cleaner and offers clearer insights and extension potential compared to SAG.  
- Practical relevance: algorithm structure could enable memory-efficient or parallel implementations, though details are not fleshed out.  
- In sum-objective settings, EMGD is faster than Nesterov only when κ ≤ n^(2/3); matches SAG when κ ≤ n^(1/2); SAG outperforms EMGD for n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8; both are slower than Nesterov for κ > n/8.  
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires fixing epoch length O(κ^2) a priori, preventing adaptation to local condition-number improvements and automatic step-size tuning.  
- Major concern 1: No experiments to validate practical impact or demonstrate regimes where EMGD outperforms baselines (e.g., κ ≤ n^(2/3)).  
- Major concern 2: Incomplete comparison—needs expansion on EMGD being dominated by SAG for κ > n^(1/2), the epoch-length limitation versus SAG’s adaptive steps, and relation to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012).  
- Major concern 3: Proof clarity—lack of high-level intuition for why averaging decouples dependence on κ; add motivating sentences for key steps.  
- Minor suggestions: clarify Table 1 reference, specify SCDA restrictions, state conditions hold for all w, add ∇F(w)=E[∇f(w)], correct distributed computing claim, clarify derivation around lines 288, and fix typos/notation inconsistencies.  
- Conclusion: EMGD is a novel, theoretically solid contribution with simpler proofs and useful generality, but its practical significance is unproven without experiments and a fuller discussion of limitations.  
- Recommendation: Accept with major revisions to add experiments, expand practical comparisons, and improve exposition.
