

The paper introduces a "hybrid" deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining queries to a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). The authors demonstrate that this approach eliminates the dependence on the condition number in the convergence rate. Specifically, assuming the condition number κ (i.e., known strong convexity constant λ and strong smoothness constant L) and access to a first-order hybrid oracle (supporting both gradient types), a suitably mixed sequence of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps achieves a high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate for finite-sum optimization. This represents an interesting and theoretically striking result.

However, the paper has several significant concerns. First, the theoretical analysis exclusively addresses the case where κ is perfectly known beforehand, with no discussion of algorithm behavior under κ misspecification (e.g., hyper-parameter set too large or too small). The current result establishes that κ dependence can be removed *only* when κ is known in advance. If theoretical analysis for unknown κ is intractable—which the authors do not address—this should be validated through experiments. The absence of any experimental section is a major deficiency, especially given the paper's focus on a novel algorithm; empirical validation is essential to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical claims.

Detailed concerns:
1.  **κ as a required hyper-parameter**: The proposed algorithm (EGD, Eq. 8) relies on η, which depends on κ (defined as the ratio of L to λ). The paper provides no guidance for setting κ (e.g., estimation procedure or theoretical justification), leaving it unclear how κ would be determined in practice. Two possibilities exist: (a) κ must be estimated, but no estimation method or supporting experiments are provided; or (b) κ is assumed known, in which case the theoretical analysis must address misspecification, which it does not.
2.  **Restrictive smooth/strongly convex assumption**: While theoretically popular, the smooth and strongly convex setting is overly restrictive. The non-strongly convex case (which arises frequently, as discussed in Bach & Moulines, 2011) merits analysis, as it exhibits different convergence behaviors.
3.  **Novelty and related work**: Blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not new. The paper presents a single "mixed" update per iteration (within an epoch), rather than interleaving distinct deterministic/stochastic steps at varying frequencies. A comparison with such a simpler interleaving approach (e.g., in the unconstrained case) would be valuable. Crucially, the authors omit discussion of related hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012), including a comparison of convergence rates.
4.  **Ambiguous optimization setting**: The paper does not clearly specify whether the goal is solving a *deterministic finite-sum problem* (min_w (1/n)∑_{i=1}^n F_i(w)) using a randomized algorithm (like SAG or SDCA), or a *stochastic approximation problem* (min_w E[F(w)]). Section 3.1's theoretical setup is misleading, and no subsequent claim clarifies this. This ambiguity must be resolved.
5.  **Experimental necessity**: A thorough experimental section comparing the proposed algorithm against standard baselines (SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, etc.) is essential to validate the theoretical claims and assess practical performance. This is especially critical given the missing analysis of κ misspecification.

The core theoretical contribution is compelling under the stated assumptions, but the lack of analysis for unknown κ, absence of experiments, omission of related work, and ambiguous problem setting significantly undermine the paper's impact and credibility. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the submission.
