- Addresses optimization of smooth strongly convex functions over convex sets with efficient gradient mapping updates; proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) using expensive full and cheaper stochastic gradient oracles.  
- EMGD requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic calls, potentially faster when κ is not too large.  
- Core idea: replace each full-gradient epoch with O(κ²) mixed steps combining one full gradient and many stochastic gradients, averaging iterates to make epoch progress κ-independent.  
- Provides a clear, self-contained convergence proof with high-probability guarantees; proof is simpler than SAG’s.  
- Novel and interesting hybrid approach that fits recent work leveraging cheap stochastic oracles while keeping linear convergence.  
- EMGD handles constrained problems (assuming efficient gradient mapping), unlike SAG which is limited to unconstrained settings.  
- Compared to SAG and Nesterov: EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3), is comparable to SAG for 1 ≤ κ ≤ n^(1/2), and is slower than SAG across most regimes; detailed κ vs. n regime comparisons are required.  
- Major cons: no experiments to show practical relevance, insufficient discussion of limitations (Section 3.4), and the proof lacks high-level motivation for key quantities.  
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires fixing epoch length in advance (depends on κ), unlike adaptive methods (e.g., SCDA, SAG) that can exploit local condition numbers or automatic step-size selection.  
- Reviewer requests a concrete comparison to SAG/Nesterov for sum-of-n-functions and a discussion of practical regimes (e.g., regularization making κ ≈ n/C' where SAG may outperform EMGD).  
- Addendum requests: highlight practical regime where κ < n^(2/3) and cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods.  
- Clarity praise: paper is well written, Table 1 is excellent; suggest more high-level comments in the proof and several minor clarifications (specific line edits given).  
- Technical quality is sound; theoretical contribution stands alone but experiments and expanded limitations/comparisons are required to strengthen practical impact.
