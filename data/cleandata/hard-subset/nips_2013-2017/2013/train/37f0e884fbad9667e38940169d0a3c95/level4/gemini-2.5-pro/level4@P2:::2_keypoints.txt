- Proposes EMGD, a first-order algorithm that mixes one full gradient with multiple stochastic gradients per epoch to reduce dependence on the condition number κ.  
- Main theoretical claim: EMGD achieves linear convergence using O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients, making full-gradient calls independent of κ.  
- Strength: novel and striking theoretical result shifting κ-dependence from expensive full gradients to cheaper stochastic gradients, with detailed sound analysis.  
- Requires prior knowledge of L and λ (hence κ) for step-size and inner-loop tuning, with no procedure or analysis for mis-specified κ.  
- Completely lacks empirical evaluation to validate convergence, measure practical speedups, assess sensitivity to hyperparameters (especially κ), and compare wall-clock costs to baselines.  
- Insufficient discussion of related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012) and missing comparisons to that literature.  
- Ambiguous problem formulation: paper mixes general stochastic setting F(w)=E[f(w)] with comparisons to finite-sum methods (F(w)=(1/n)Σf_i), leaving scope unclear.  
- Unclear whether the “mixed gradient” construction materially outperforms simpler interleaving of full and stochastic steps; needs justification and comparison.  
- Argument for acceptance: presents a meaningful theoretical trade-off and a convergence rate where full-gradient complexity is κ-independent.  
- Arguments for rejection: reliance on knowing κ, absence of experiments, omitted related work, and ambiguous problem setting undermine practical impact.  
- Conclusion: theoretically promising but incomplete; recommend rejection until robustness to κ, empirical validation, related-work comparisons, and problem-clarity are addressed.
