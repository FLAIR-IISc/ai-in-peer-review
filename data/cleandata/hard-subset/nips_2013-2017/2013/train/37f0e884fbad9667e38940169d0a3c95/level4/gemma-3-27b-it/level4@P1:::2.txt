The paper presents a novel first-order optimization method that combines deterministic and stochastic gradient steps. The algorithm, named Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aims to mitigate the dependence on the condition number κ typically observed in the convergence rate of gradient-based methods for smooth and strongly convex optimization. The authors demonstrate that, assuming knowledge of κ and access to both full and stochastic gradients, EMGD achieves a convergence rate of O(log(1/ε)).

The theoretical result presented is interesting and potentially impactful. The core contribution lies in showing that, under specific conditions—namely, a known condition number and the availability of both gradient oracles—the dependence on κ can be removed from the iteration complexity, leading to an O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate. This is a significant theoretical advance.

However, several concerns limit the practical impact of the current work. A primary issue is the reliance on perfect knowledge of the condition number κ. The analysis does not address the algorithm’s behavior when κ is misspecified, which is often the case in real-world applications. While analyzing the misspecified case theoretically might be challenging, exploring its impact through experiments would be highly valuable.  Currently, the result primarily applies to scenarios where κ is known *a priori*. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks an experimental evaluation. As the main contribution is a new algorithm, empirical validation is crucial to assess the theoretical findings and demonstrate their practical relevance. Experiments comparing EMGD with existing algorithms (e.g., SGD, SAG, SDCA) on benchmark datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.

Detailed comments:

The proposed algorithm (EMGD) relies on an update rule (Eq. 8, page 4) employing a "mixed gradient" (Eq. 7). Consequently, the step size η, and thus the condition number κ (comprising the strong convexity modulus λ and smoothness constant L), must be known. The paper does not provide guidance on estimating κ, leaving open the question of how to handle scenarios where it is unknown.  

This leads to two possibilities: either κ must be estimated, requiring an estimation procedure absent from the current manuscript (even a heuristic supported by experiments would be beneficial), or it is assumed known for theoretical purposes. If the latter is true, the analysis should address the implications of a misspecified κ—analyzing convergence rates when κ is overestimated or underestimated.

The assumption of strong convexity, while common in theoretical analyses, may be restrictive. Considering non-strongly convex cases, as discussed by Bach & Moulines (2011), would broaden the applicability and relevance of the work.

Blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not entirely novel. The current algorithm, defined by a single "mixed" update per iteration within each epoch, differs from approaches that interleave deterministic and stochastic steps with varying frequencies. Discussing the relationship between EMGD and such algorithms would be valuable, particularly in the unconstrained setting.

The paper does not adequately review related work in hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization.  Specifically, it would benefit from discussing and comparing convergence rates with algorithms such as the hybrid deterministic-stochastic method proposed by Friedlander and Schmidt (2012).

A thorough experimental evaluation is a crucial missing component. This should include comparisons with standard SGD, averaged SGD, and recent advancements in stochastic first-order optimization like SAG and SDCA.

A minor point concerns the problem setting. The paper aims to combine the benefits of deterministic and stochastic optimization—achieving an exponential convergence rate while mitigating the condition number dependence. However, the intended problem is not clearly defined. Is the goal to solve a deterministic problem (Min<sub>w</sub> F(w) = 1/n ∑<sub>i=1</sub><sup>n</sup> F<sub>i</sub>(w)) using a stochastic algorithm, or to solve a stochastic approximation problem (Min<sub>w</sub> E[F(w)])?  SAG and SDCA, for example, are randomized algorithms designed for the deterministic setting. Clarifying this distinction would improve the clarity of the paper. The current theoretical setup (Section 3.1) is somewhat ambiguous in this regard.



In summary, while the theoretical result is promising, the lack of experimental validation and the strong reliance on known condition number limit the immediate practical impact of the work. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field.
