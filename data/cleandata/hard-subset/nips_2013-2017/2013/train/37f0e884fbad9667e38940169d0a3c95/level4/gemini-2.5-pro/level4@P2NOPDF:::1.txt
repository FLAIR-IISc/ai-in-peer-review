### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex set, under the assumption that the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. While Nesterov's optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence for such problems, its rate depends on the square root of the condition number, κ.

The authors consider a setting where one has access to both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic gradient oracle. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires only O(log 1/ε) calls to the full gradient oracle—a complexity independent of the condition number. This is complemented by O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle. For problems where the condition number is not excessively large, this approach can be theoretically faster than standard methods.

The core idea of EMGD is to structure the optimization into "epochs." At the start of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. This fixed gradient is then used in a series of O(κ²) mixed gradient steps, where each step combines the stored full gradient with a new stochastic gradient. The authors prove that by averaging the iterates within an epoch, they can achieve a constant decrease in suboptimality, independent of κ. This key result explains why the number of required full gradient computations (i.e., epochs) is independent of the condition number. The paper provides a simple, self-contained proof of this convergence rate but does not include experimental results.

### Evaluation

This is an interesting paper that introduces a novel algorithmic idea, supported by clean and solid theory. The concept of mixing a few expensive full gradient computations with many cheap stochastic steps to achieve a linear convergence rate is compelling. This work joins a recent line of research aiming to leverage stochastic oracles while retaining the fast convergence of deterministic methods.

A key point of comparison is the recently proposed SAG algorithm [16], which has achieved both theoretical and practical breakthroughs for unconstrained, smooth, strongly convex objectives that are a sum of *n* functions (e.g., regularized empirical loss minimization). In this setting, the full gradient is typically *n* times more expensive than a stochastic one.
*   SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n/8.
*   In contrast, EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3).
*   Compared to each other, EMGD is slower than SAG in all regimes, although they share the same big-O complexity when κ ≤ n^(1/2).
To put these complexities into perspective, if κ = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD have a complexity of O(n log(1/ε)), which is a significant improvement over Nesterov's algorithm at O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)).

Despite being slower than SAG in this common setting, EMGD offers two significant advantages:
1.  **Constrained Optimization:** As the authors note, EMGD is applicable to constrained optimization problems (assuming an efficient projection), whereas SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems.
2.  **Simpler Proof and Stronger Guarantee:** The convergence proof for EMGD is considerably simpler than that for SAG. This simplicity could yield further insights and make modifications to the algorithm more amenable to theoretical guarantees. Furthermore, EMGD’s convergence result holds with high probability, which is a stronger guarantee than SAG’s convergence in expectation.

The authors also mention potential memory and parallelization advantages, but this point is less clear. SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which similarly reduces its memory footprint.

Despite its theoretical merits, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed:
*   **Lack of Experiments:** The absence of any experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the algorithm. It is unclear if the theoretical advantages translate into real-world performance gains, or if there are concrete examples where its unique properties (like handling constraints) are particularly relevant.
*   **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the algorithm's drawbacks, especially in comparison to existing methods. Section 3.4 should be significantly expanded to provide a more balanced view.
*   **Dependence on Condition Number:** A major practical drawback of EMGD is that the number of steps within an epoch must be fixed in advance, requiring knowledge of the condition number κ. In contrast, methods like SAG and SDCA [17] do not require this, allowing them to benefit from faster practical convergence than their worst-case bounds might predict, or to adapt to local problem structure. Furthermore, SAG has effective adaptive step-size heuristics, and SDCA features automatic step-size selection.

A crucial point, raised by another reviewer, is that in many machine learning applications, the regularization parameter is chosen as C/n, leading to a condition number κ ≈ n/C'. In this high-condition-number regime, SAG remains faster than Nesterov's method, but EMGD does not. This is a significant limitation that should be explicitly discussed, and the authors should suggest other practical settings where the condition κ < n^(2/3) might hold.

Finally, the authors should cite and discuss "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" (Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012), which also presents a hybrid algorithm with a linear convergence rate. While that algorithm's rate still depends on the condition number, its hybrid nature makes it relevant prior work.

### Detailed Suggestions

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, and Table 1 provides a helpful summary of convergence rates. However, the proof, while technically sound, would benefit from more high-level commentary to explain the intuition behind the construction. Some of the defined quantities fit together a bit too "magically" in the current presentation.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel synthesis of existing first-order techniques.

**Significance:** The practical significance is not yet demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution is noteworthy. In light of the complex convergence proof for SAG, EMGD's simple and elegant proof is a valuable contribution that could inspire further research.

**Specific Points:**
*   **l. 50:** Suggestion: Rephrase to "are summarized in Table 1 and detailed in the related work section" to clarify where the citations for the rates can be found.
*   **l. 118-119:** It would be helpful to specify that SDCA is only defined for a specific functional form of *f_i*, unlike SAG, which handles any smooth convex *f_i*.
*   **l. 132:** The condition on the stochastic gradient should be explicitly stated to hold "for all *w*." The current phrasing is slightly ambiguous.
*   **l. 139:** Mention explicitly that we also have ∇F(w) = E[∇f(w)].
*   **l. 224:** The claim that SAG and SDCA cannot leverage distributed computing is incorrect. Both can be parallelized using mini-batches, which also reduces memory requirements.
*   **l. 288:** To improve clarity, I suggest writing "(7) on (10) with x=w (feasible by (5)) and x=w_{t+1}". It is not immediately obvious how this line is derived.
*   **l. 304 (and elsewhere):** Add parentheses around (F(w_t) - F(w*)) to clarify that F(w*) is also part of the sum over *T*.
*   **l. 366:** Note that the second inequality is valid for all T ≥ 1 only if δ ≤ exp(-1/2). This also explains a condition in (4) that was not explicitly justified.

**Typos:**
*   **l. 42:** 'an convex' -> 'a convex'
*   **l. 56:** Clarify "strong convexity, condition number."
*   **l. 88 (and elsewhere):** The notation for the domain is inconsistent (D vs. Ω). Please choose one and use it throughout the paper.
*   **l. 307:** Use the norm symbol ||·|| instead of absolute value |·|.
