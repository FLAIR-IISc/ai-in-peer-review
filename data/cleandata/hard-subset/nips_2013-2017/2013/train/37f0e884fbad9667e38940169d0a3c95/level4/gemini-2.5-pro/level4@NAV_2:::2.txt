This paper proposes a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems. The method operates in epochs, utilizing a "mixed gradient" that combines a single full gradient (computed at the start of an epoch) with a series of stochastic gradients. The central contribution is a theoretical result demonstrating that EMGD can achieve an ε-optimal solution with O(log 1/ε) full gradient computations, a number independent of the problem's condition number, κ. This is achieved by performing O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradient computations. This work relates to the extensive literature on first-order methods, particularly those that seek to accelerate convergence for ill-conditioned problems, such as Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, and recent stochastic methods with linear convergence rates like SAG and SDCA.

**Strengths:**

The paper's primary strength lies in its interesting and significant theoretical finding. The ability to reduce the number of expensive full gradient evaluations to be independent of the condition number κ, by leveraging cheaper stochastic gradients, is a compelling result. If the underlying assumptions hold, this approach could offer substantial computational savings for ill-conditioned problems where full gradient computations are the bottleneck. The analysis leading to the high-probability linear convergence bound is also technically sound.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising theoretical result, the paper has two major weaknesses in its current form:

1.  **Dependence on a Known Condition Number:** The theoretical analysis and the algorithm's parameterization (specifically, the number of iterations T per epoch) critically depend on prior knowledge of the condition number κ. This is a strong assumption that limits the practical applicability of the proposed method. The paper does not address the algorithm's behavior if κ is unknown, misspecified, or must be estimated. Essentially, the work demonstrates that the dependence on κ can be removed from the full gradient complexity, but only if κ is provided to the algorithm upfront.

2.  **Absence of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks an experimental section. For a work that introduces a new algorithm, empirical validation is essential. Experiments would serve to (a) corroborate the theoretical convergence rates on synthetic or real-world datasets, (b) demonstrate the practical benefits compared to existing methods, and (c) investigate the algorithm's sensitivity to its hyperparameters, particularly the assumed value of κ.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **Problem Formulation Clarity:** The optimization setting is not precisely defined. Section 3.1 introduces a general stochastic optimization framework where F(w) = E[f(w)]. However, the comparisons in Section 3.4 are made with algorithms like SAG and SDCA, which are designed for the finite-sum (or "batch") setting, F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w). Clarifying whether the target is the general stochastic approximation problem or the finite-sum problem is crucial for properly positioning the paper's contribution.

*   **Missing Related Work:** The literature review should be expanded to include the line of work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms. A key reference here is "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" by M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt (SISC, 2012), which also explores combining full and stochastic gradients. A discussion comparing EMGD's approach and convergence guarantees to this and other related methods would strengthen the paper.

*   **Practicality and Hyperparameter Selection:** The step size η and the number of inner iterations T are critical hyperparameters that depend on L and κ. The paper would be more impactful if it included a discussion on how to set these in practice. Even a heuristic procedure for estimating κ, possibly supported by experiments, would significantly enhance the work's practical value.

*   **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** The mixed gradient update is an interesting construction. However, it would be valuable to discuss how EMGD compares, at least conceptually, to a simpler algorithm that explicitly interleaves a small number of full gradient steps with a larger number of stochastic gradient steps.

*   **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the strongly convex case. While this is a standard setting for proving linear convergence, many practical problems are not strongly convex. A brief discussion on potential extensions or the challenges of applying this method to the merely convex case would be welcome.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically elegant idea with a striking result. However, its significant reliance on a known condition number and the complete lack of empirical validation are major concerns that prevent a recommendation for acceptance in its current state. Addressing these issues, particularly by adding a thorough experimental section that investigates the practical behavior and robustness of the algorithm, would substantially improve the manuscript.
