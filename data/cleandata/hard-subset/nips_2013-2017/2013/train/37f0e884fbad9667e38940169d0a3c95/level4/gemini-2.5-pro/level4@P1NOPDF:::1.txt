**SUMMARY:**
The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex set, assuming the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. Nesterov's optimal first-order algorithm achieves a linear convergence rate for this problem, but the constant in the rate depends on the square root of the condition number, k. This work considers a setting where one has access to both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic gradient oracle. They propose a hybrid algorithm that requires only O(log 1/eps) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of the condition number) and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle. In theory, this approach can be faster than Nesterov's method, provided the condition number is not too large.

The main idea behind their algorithm, Epoch-wise Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), is to replace a full gradient step (which they term an "epoch") with a fixed number, O(k^2), of mixed-gradient steps. These steps use a combination of the full gradient (computed once per epoch) and stochastic gradients (computed at each step within the epoch). By averaging the O(k^2) iterates within an epoch, the authors show a constant decrease in suboptimality that is independent of the condition number. This result explains why the total number of full gradient computations (i.e., the number of epochs) is also independent of k. The paper provides a simple, complete, and self-contained proof of its convergence rate, but includes no experiments.

I enjoyed reading this paper; the core idea of mixing a few expensive full gradient computations with a large number of cheap stochastic gradient steps is both novel and interesting. This work is situated among a string of recent papers that aim to achieve linear convergence rates while leveraging cheaper stochastic gradient oracles. A recent theoretical and practical breakthrough in this area is the SAG algorithm [16], which applies to smooth, strongly convex objectives that are a sum of n functions (a common setting in machine learning, such as regularized empirical risk minimization, where n is the number of training examples). In this setting, it is reasonable to assume that the full gradient is n times more expensive to compute than a single stochastic gradient. Under this assumption, SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov's algorithm as long as the condition number k <= n/8. In contrast, EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov's algorithm when k <= n^(2/3). However, EMGD is slower than SAG in all regimes (though it achieves the same big-O complexity when k <= n^(1/2)). To provide a concrete sense of these speed-ups, if k = n^(1/2), the complexity for both SAG and EMGD is O(n log(1/eps)), whereas for Nesterov's algorithm it is O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)).

I see two main advantages of EMGD over SAG. First, as the authors mention, EMGD applies to constrained optimization problems (provided the gradient mapping is efficient), whereas SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems. Second, the convergence proof for EMGD is much simpler than that for SAG. This simplicity could yield new insights and make it easier to develop modified versions of EMGD with provable guarantees. Furthermore, the convergence result for EMGD holds with high probability, which is a stronger guarantee than the in-expectation result for SAG. The authors also suggest a potential advantage in terms of memory and parallelization, though this claim is less convincing. SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which similarly reduces its memory footprint.

**EVALUATION SUMMARY:**

**Pros:**
*   Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea supported by a clean, simple, and solid theoretical analysis.
*   Like SAG, the proposed algorithm achieves a linear convergence rate for problems like regularized empirical risk minimization where the dependency on the condition number k is decoupled from the dependency on the number of examples n. However, EMGD is more general (it handles constrained optimization) and has a much simpler proof.
*   The paper is clearly written, and the proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**
*   The paper lacks experiments to demonstrate that the algorithm is effective in practice. It also fails to provide concrete examples where its theoretical advantages (e.g., handling constraints) would be practically relevant.
*   There is no discussion of the algorithm's limitations or drawbacks, particularly in comparison to existing methods. Section 3.4, which covers this, should be significantly improved based on the suggestions in this review.
*   The proof lacks high-level commentary that could help the reader understand the key insights behind its construction.

**QUALITY:**
The paper is technically sound. While experiments would have strengthened the submission, the theoretical contribution can stand on its own. However, the authors must extend Section 3.4 to include a thorough discussion of the algorithm's limitations and drawbacks. They should also add a more concrete analysis of the sum-of-n-functions case to clearly delineate the performance regimes of EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov's method. For example, they should state that EMGD is slower than Nesterov's method for k > n^(2/3); SAG has the same complexity as EMGD for 1 <= k <= n^(1/2); SAG is faster than both EMGD and Nesterov's for n^(1/2) < k <= n/8; and Nesterov's is better than SAG for k > n/8.

A major practical drawback of EMGD is that the number of steps within an epoch must be fixed in advance, requiring knowledge of the condition number k. In contrast, neither SCDA [17] nor SAG requires this, allowing them to potentially converge faster in practice than their theoretical bounds suggest, for instance by adapting to local properties of the objective function. Furthermore, SCDA features automatic step-size selection, and SAG employs an adaptive step-size heuristic that works well in practice. The authors should include a discussion of these practical considerations in the paper.

**[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers]**
a) As another reviewer noted, a common setting in machine learning involves a regularization parameter of C/n, leading to a condition number k ≈ n/C'. In this regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov's method, but EMGD does not. This limitation should be explicitly stated. The authors should also suggest an alternative practical setting where the condition k < n^(2/3) might hold.
b) The authors should cite "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" by Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012), which also presents a hybrid algorithm with a linear convergence rate. However, the rate in that work still depends on the condition number, meaning its asymptotic rate is no better than standard gradient descent (though it may be faster initially). The algorithm in the current submission offers a clear theoretical improvement over this prior work when k is not too large relative to n.

**CLARITY:**
The paper is quite clear. I particularly appreciated the summary in Table 1, which presents the related work more clearly than I have seen elsewhere. The proof is rigorous and can be followed step-by-step, but it would be more accessible if the authors provided some high-level intuition. In its current form, some of the defined quantities seem to fit together almost magically, and commentary explaining their motivation would be beneficial.

**ORIGINALITY:**
The work represents a novel combination of existing techniques.

**SIGNIFICANCE:**
The practical relevance of the algorithm has not yet been demonstrated, but the theoretical contribution could be impactful. In light of the notoriously difficult convergence proof for SAG, the simple proof for EMGD is a major contribution. While SDCA also has a simple proof, it applies to a more restricted setting, making the accessible analysis of the more general EMGD algorithm particularly valuable.
