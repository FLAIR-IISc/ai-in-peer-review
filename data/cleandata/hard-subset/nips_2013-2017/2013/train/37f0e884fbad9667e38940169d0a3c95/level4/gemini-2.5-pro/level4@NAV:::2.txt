This paper introduces a hybrid optimization algorithm that combines deterministic and stochastic first-order methods. The proposed method interleaves the use of a deterministic oracle for full gradient computations with a stochastic oracle for individual gradient estimates. The primary theoretical claim is that this hybrid strategy achieves a convergence rate independent of the problem's condition number.

The paper's central contribution is a compelling theoretical finding: under the assumption that a hybrid first-order oracle is available and the problem's condition number (i.e., its strong convexity and smoothness constants) is known, the algorithm can attain an O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate with high probability for batch optimization.

Despite this, the paper has significant weaknesses in its current form. Firstly, the theoretical guarantees are contingent on prior knowledge of the exact condition number. The analysis does not address the algorithm's performance when this crucial hyperparameter is incorrectly specified. The central claim—that the dependence on the condition number (kappa) can be eliminated—is thus predicated on knowing kappa in advance. While a theoretical treatment of the misspecified case may be difficult, this limitation could have been explored empirically.

This leads to the second major issue: the complete absence of an experimental section. For a contribution centered on a novel algorithm, empirical validation is essential to corroborate the theoretical claims and investigate the practical relevance of the proposed method on real-world data.

**Detailed comments**

*   The EGD algorithm's update rule (Eq. 8) is based on a "mixed gradient" (Eq. 7) that depends on a hyperparameter `η`. The setting of `η` requires knowledge of the condition number `κ` (and thus the strong convexity modulus `λ` and smoothness modulus `L`). The paper provides no guidance on how to set this value in practice. Either an estimation procedure for `κ` is required (even a heuristic one, if validated by experiments), or, if `κ` is assumed to be known, the analysis must be extended to investigate the algorithm's robustness to its misspecification (i.e., when the chosen value is too high or too low).

*   The analysis is confined to the smooth and strongly convex setting. While this is a common assumption in theoretical work, it can be restrictive. The non-strongly convex case, which arises frequently in practice, is also of significant interest and its omission limits the paper's scope (see, for example, the analysis in Bach & Moulines, 2011).

*   The concept of combining deterministic and stochastic gradient updates is not novel. The proposed algorithm formulates this as a single "mixed" update per iteration rather than as an explicit alternation between full and stochastic gradient steps. A discussion is needed to position this approach relative to simpler, similarly-motivated strategies that interleave full gradient steps with stochastic ones, particularly in the unconstrained setting.

*   The paper fails to acknowledge or discuss a relevant body of literature on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization. For instance, the work by Friedlander and Schmidt ("Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting," SISC, 2012) and the references therein should be reviewed, and the convergence rates should be compared.

*   An empirical evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper. Such a study should include rigorous comparisons against standard and state-of-the-art baselines, such as SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, and other modern stochastic first-order methods.

*   The optimization problem being addressed is not clearly defined. It is ambiguous whether the goal is to solve the finite-sum deterministic problem (Min F(w) = 1/n Σ F_i(w)) using a randomized algorithm—as methods like SAG and SDCA do—or the general stochastic approximation problem (Min E[F(w)]). The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is particularly unclear in this regard, and this ambiguity persists throughout the paper. Clarifying the precise problem setting is crucial and should be a straightforward correction.
