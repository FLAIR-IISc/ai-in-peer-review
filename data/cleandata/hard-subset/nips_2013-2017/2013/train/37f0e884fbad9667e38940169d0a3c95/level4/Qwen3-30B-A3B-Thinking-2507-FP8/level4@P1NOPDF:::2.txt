

The paper introduces a "hybrid" deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining queries to a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). The authors claim that this approach eliminates the dependence on the condition number in the convergence rate. Their key theoretical result demonstrates that, assuming the condition number κ (i.e., known strong convexity constant λ and strong smoothness constant L) is provided to the algorithm, and given access to a hybrid first-order oracle, a suitably mixed update rule achieves a high-probability convergence rate of O(log(1/ε)) for finite-sum optimization.  

However, the paper has significant shortcomings. First, the theoretical analysis is limited to the case where κ is known exactly. The behavior when κ is misspecified—either underestimated or overestimated—is entirely unaddressed. The current result only states that κ dependence vanishes *if* κ is known beforehand. If analyzing misspecification is theoretically intractable (as the authors imply), this should be acknowledged, and the paper should supplement this with empirical validation. Yet, the paper lacks any experimental section—a major omission given that the algorithm is the paper’s central contribution. Experiments would be essential to verify the theoretical claims and assess practical performance on real data.  

The proposed algorithm (EGD, Eq. 8) relies on the "mixed gradient" (Eq. 7), requiring the hyperparameter η, which is directly tied to κ. The paper provides no guidance for setting η or estimating κ. Two possibilities arise: (1) η must be estimated, but no estimation procedure is proposed (even a heuristic supported by experiments would suffice), or (2) κ is assumed known, making the paper primarily theoretical. In the latter case, the analysis should explicitly cover misspecification scenarios (e.g., η set too large/small) to demonstrate robustness. The current treatment of κ is inadequate.  

Furthermore, the focus on strongly convex and smooth objectives is overly restrictive. As noted in Bach & Moulines (2011), non-strongly convex settings are equally important and arise frequently. The paper should address this gap or clarify the scope of applicability.  

The algorithm’s design also warrants deeper discussion. While the authors frame it as a "hybrid" method, it is implemented as a single "mixed" update per iteration (within an epoch), rather than interleaving deterministic and stochastic steps at varying frequencies. A comparison with a simpler interleaved approach (e.g., alternating deterministic and stochastic steps) would clarify the novelty and practical implications.  

Critically, the paper omits key related work. The 2012 Friedlander & Schmidt SISC paper ("Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting") presents a closely related framework for hybrid optimization, including convergence rate comparisons. The authors should engage with this work and contextualize their contribution relative to existing hybrid methods.  

Finally, the optimization setting is ambiguously defined. The paper claims to bridge deterministic and stochastic optimization, but it fails to specify whether the problem is (a) finite-sum minimization (min_w (1/n)∑F_i(w))—solved by algorithms like SAG/SDCA—or (b) stochastic approximation (min_w E[F(w)]). Section 3.1’s setup is misleading, and subsequent claims do not resolve this ambiguity. Clarifying the intended setting is essential for interpreting the results.  

In summary, while the theoretical result is intriguing under the restrictive assumption of known κ, the paper’s lack of analysis for misspecified κ, absence of experiments, omission of key related work, and unclear problem setting significantly weaken its contribution. The algorithm’s practical relevance cannot be assessed without empirical validation, and its theoretical scope requires broader justification. The authors should address these gaps before the work can be considered for publication.
