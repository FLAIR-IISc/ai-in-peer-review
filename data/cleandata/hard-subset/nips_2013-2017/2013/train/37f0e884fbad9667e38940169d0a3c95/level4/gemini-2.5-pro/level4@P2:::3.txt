### Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of smooth and strongly convex optimization, where the optimal complexity for first-order methods using full gradients is O(√κ log(1/ε)), with κ being the condition number. The authors argue that for ill-conditioned problems (large κ), the required number of full gradient evaluations can be computationally prohibitive.

To mitigate this, the paper introduces a novel hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which utilizes both full and stochastic gradients. The core idea is to structure the optimization into epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed at a reference point. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient," which combines this fixed full gradient with a variance-reducing stochastic term.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This effectively makes the number of expensive full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ, trading them for a larger number of cheaper stochastic gradient computations. The paper compares this complexity to standard full-gradient, stochastic, and other modern hybrid methods like SAG and SDCA, arguing for its competitiveness under certain conditions and highlighting its advantages in handling constraints and having low memory requirements.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Originality:** The proposed mixed-gradient update rule, `g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) - ∇f(w̄)`, is an elegant and novel approach to variance reduction. It allows the algorithm to leverage the accuracy of a full gradient computed at the start of an epoch while using cheaper stochastic gradients for local exploration. This hybrid strategy, which makes the number of full gradient calls independent of κ, is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous and detailed convergence analysis. The proof, which establishes a high-probability linear convergence rate, appears to be technically sound. The use of martingale concentration inequalities to bound the stochastic terms is appropriate and well-executed.

3.  **Generality and Broader Applicability:** Unlike methods such as SAG and SDCA, which are primarily designed for unconstrained finite-sum optimization problems, the proposed EMGD algorithm is formulated for general constrained optimization. This makes the approach potentially applicable to a wider range of problems, provided the projection step (Step 8) is efficient. Furthermore, its O(d) memory requirement is a notable advantage over SAG, which requires O(nd) storage.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

My primary concern relates to the practical significance of the results for the machine learning community, which is the main audience of NIPS.

1.  **Practical Relevance of the Complexity Gains:** The authors show that EMGD is computationally cheaper than Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent when κ ≤ n^(2/3) (for finite-sum problems). However, the paper would be much stronger if it included a discussion of whether this condition holds in practical machine learning scenarios.
    *   In many applications, strong convexity is introduced via an L2 regularizer, i.e., F(w) = (1/n)Σf_i(w) + (λ_reg/2)||w||². Here, the strong convexity parameter is dominated by λ_reg, while the smoothness parameter L depends on the data. The condition number κ = L/λ_reg can be very large if a small regularization parameter is used, which is common practice to avoid underfitting.
    *   Could the authors provide concrete examples of ML problems where the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3) is met and their algorithm offers a clear advantage? This would be crucial for demonstrating the paper's impact and relevance.

2.  **Probabilistic vs. Deterministic Convergence:** The convergence guarantee for EMGD holds with high probability, whereas the guarantees for competing deterministic methods (like accelerated gradient descent) are absolute. This is a potential drawback and a key distinction that should be more prominently discussed in the comparisons.

3.  **A Priori Knowledge of κ:** The algorithm's hyperparameters (specifically T, the number of iterations per epoch) depend directly on the condition number κ. This is a significant practical limitation, as κ is rarely known in advance. While the authors briefly mention this as future work, the current paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion on potential strategies for estimating or upper-bounding κ in practice. Without this, the applicability of the proposed method is limited.

**Detailed Comments for Improvement**

*   **Clarity in Preliminaries:** For improved clarity, it would be helpful to explicitly state that L ≥ λ and to formally define the condition number κ = L/λ in Section 3.1.
*   **Notation Consistency:** Please double-check for notational consistency throughout the manuscript. For example, in Equation (4), the definition of strong convexity for F should use the gradient ∇F, not ∇f.
*   **Equation (13):** Please check if absolute value signs have been used where vector norms are intended.
*   **Clarity of Derivations:** Some derivations could be streamlined. For example, the equality in line 286 seems to add little to the understanding and could potentially be removed to improve readability.
*   **Line 288:** When applying the property from Equation (7), please explicitly state which function it is being applied to.
*   **Lemma 1 (Equation 6):** The standard form of this inequality is `f(x) - f(x*) ≥ (λ/2) ||x - x*||²`. Please ensure your statement is precise and consistent.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization. The main contribution—achieving linear convergence with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number—is interesting and technically well-supported. However, the paper's significance for the machine learning community is not yet fully established, as the conditions under which it outperforms standard methods are not clearly situated in practical contexts. Addressing the questions regarding the practical relevance of the complexity gains and the requirement of knowing κ would significantly strengthen the submission.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper. I lean towards acceptance but strongly recommend that the authors revise the paper to better motivate its practical utility for the NIPS audience.
