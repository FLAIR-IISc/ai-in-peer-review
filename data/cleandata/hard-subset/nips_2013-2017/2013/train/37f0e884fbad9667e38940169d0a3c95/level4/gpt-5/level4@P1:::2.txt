Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a hybrid first-order method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that combines access to full gradients and stochastic gradients. The algorithm runs in epochs; at each epoch it computes one full gradient at the epoch anchor point and, within the epoch, performs “mixed” gradient steps that add a variance-reduced stochastic component to this fixed full gradient, along with a shrinking trust-region style constraint. The main theoretical claim is a high-probability linear convergence rate: an ε-optimal solution is obtained using O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full-gradient oracle and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle, where κ = L/λ is the condition number.

The work fits between two well-studied regimes. With only full gradients, optimal first-order methods for smooth, strongly convex objectives achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) iteration complexity (e.g., Nesterov). With only stochastic gradients, even for strongly convex objectives, the best rates are typically polynomial in 1/ε unless one exploits the finite-sum structure, as in SAG and SDCA, which attain linear rates in expectation. The proposed method aims to keep the number of full-gradient evaluations logarithmic in 1/ε (independent of κ) by offloading most computations to cheaper stochastic gradients. The paper is also related to the literature on hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012) and to variance-reduced stochastic methods for finite sums.

Strengths:
- Theoretical contribution: A clear and interesting result showing that mixing oracle types can decouple the number of full-gradient evaluations from κ, with guarantees that hold with high probability.
- Conceptual clarity: The “mixed gradient” construction provides an intuitive variance-reduction effect within a shrinking domain, which explains the linear convergence.
- Generality: Unlike SAG/SDCA, the method applies to constrained problems (assuming the projection in the trust region can be done efficiently) and uses only O(d) memory.

Main concerns and weaknesses:
- Dependence on knowing κ. The analysis and algorithm require the step size η and epoch length T to be set using L and λ (hence κ). The paper does not provide guidance, theory, or heuristics for estimating these constants, nor does it analyze robustness to misspecification. As a result, the main claim effectively holds under the assumption that κ is known a priori. At minimum, the paper should study what happens when κ is over- or under-estimated (e.g., impact on convergence rate and stability), either theoretically or empirically.
- Lack of experiments. Given that the main contribution is an algorithm, an experimental section is necessary to assess practical performance, sensitivity to κ, and wall-clock trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients. Comparisons should include SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SDCA, and accelerated full-gradient methods, across both unconstrained and constrained settings where applicable.
- Relation to existing hybrid methods. The paper does not discuss prior work on hybrid deterministic–stochastic algorithms (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012, and the references therein). A comparison of assumptions, rates (including whether guarantees hold with high probability or in expectation), computational costs per epoch/iteration, and memory would strengthen the contribution.
- Algorithmic design vs. alternation. The proposed updates use a per-iteration mixed gradient. It would be useful to relate this to an alternative design that interleaves deterministic and stochastic steps at different frequencies (at least in the unconstrained case), and to clarify whether the analysis or guarantees fundamentally exploit the specific “mixed” construction rather than alternation.
- Problem setting ambiguity. Section 3.1 frames the stochastic oracle via f(·) with F(w) = E[f(w)], suggesting a stochastic approximation setting, while other parts of the paper analyze the finite-sum case F(w) = (1/n)∑i fi(w). Please clarify up front which setting is primary (finite-sum, stochastic expectation, or both), and state clearly how the oracles are instantiated in each case. Note that SAG and SDCA target the finite-sum deterministic objective using randomized updates, not stochastic approximation.
- Scope: non-strongly convex case. While the smooth, strongly convex regime is important, many applications involve merely smooth (or even nonsmooth) convex objectives. It would be helpful to discuss whether EMGD or its analysis extends to the non-strongly convex case (cf. discussions such as Bach & Moulines, 2011), or to explain why such an extension is nontrivial.

Specific comments and suggestions:
- Algorithm hyperparameters: Provide practical guidance for selecting η and T when L and λ are unknown. Possible options include backtracking, adaptive step sizes, or online estimates of L and λ; even a heuristic procedure would be useful if supported by experiments. Analyze performance when κ is mis-specified (too large/too small).
- Empirical evaluation: Include experiments on standard convex empirical risk minimization tasks (e.g., logistic regression, least squares), reporting objective reduction vs. number of full-gradient evaluations and vs. total gradient evaluations, as well as wall-clock time. Evaluate sensitivity to κ estimates, and compare to SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov’s accelerated gradient.
- Related work: Add a discussion of hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012) and earlier randomized/deterministic blend approaches, positioning EMGD’s analysis (high probability, constrained support, memory) relative to them.
- Clarity and terminology: Use “condition number” (not “conditional number”); refer to L as the smoothness/gradient Lipschitz constant and λ as the strong convexity parameter. Clearly define the oracles and the domain constraint used in the projection step. Make explicit whether the bounds hold with high probability or in expectation in each comparison.
- Presentation: The algorithm is called EMGD; ensure consistent naming throughout. Clarify the optimization setting in Section 3.1 as noted above.

Quality: The analysis appears technically sound for the stated assumptions, with a clear inductive proof and a high-probability bound. However, the practical relevance is hard to assess without experiments or robustness analysis.

Clarity: Generally well written, but the problem setting and oracle model need to be stated more precisely. Minor terminology issues should be corrected.

Originality: Mixing full and stochastic gradients per iteration with a shrinking domain to obtain high-probability linear rates is interesting, though related in spirit to prior variance-reduction and hybrid methods. A deeper comparison to existing hybrid approaches is needed to highlight novelty.

Significance: Potentially significant if the method delivers strong empirical performance with modest sensitivity to κ. The lack of experiments and robustness analysis currently limits impact.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical insight: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls with high probability.
- Supports constrained problems and low memory footprint.
- Conceptually connects deterministic and stochastic optimization.

Cons:
- Requires prior knowledge of L and λ; no guidance or robustness analysis.
- No experimental validation or empirical comparisons.
- Incomplete discussion of related hybrid methods and alternate designs.
- Ambiguity about the primary optimization setting (finite-sum vs. stochastic expectation).
