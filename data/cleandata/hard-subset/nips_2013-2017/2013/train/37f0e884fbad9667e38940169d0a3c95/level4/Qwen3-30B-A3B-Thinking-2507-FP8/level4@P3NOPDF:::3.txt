

The paper proposes a novel hybrid stochastic/batch gradient descent algorithm that combines stochastic and batch gradients, achieving convergence to an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(k² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The convergence proof appears theoretically sound, though it contains minor errors requiring correction (detailed below).  

My primary concern is the algorithm’s relevance to machine learning—a critical consideration for NIPS. In standard ML settings, strong convexity arises from regularization, yielding μ = Θ(N), where N is the sample size and μ is the strong convexity parameter. Under this canonical assumption, the proposed method outperforms batch gradient descent (BGD) only when N = O(C³/L³), with C independent of N. This regime is unrealistic for practical ML applications, as N typically exceeds such bounds by orders of magnitude. For instance, with C = 0.1 and L = 1, N must be < 0.001—a scenario incompatible with real-world datasets. Consequently, the claimed advantage over BGD lacks practical justification.  

Furthermore, the convergence guarantee is high-probability, while BGD’s rate is deterministic. The authors must explicitly address this trade-off and demonstrate a *practical* advantage in terms of wall-clock time or generalization, not merely theoretical iteration counts. Without this, the paper fails to establish its significance for the ML community.  

**Minor corrections:**  
- Eq. (6): Replace "||w^* - \hat{w}||" with "||w^* - \hat{w}||^2".  
- Line 288: Specify the function used in Eq. (7).  
- Line 286: Remove the equality (it adds no insight).  
- Eq. (13): Replace absolute values with norms.  
- Clarify that L ≥ λ (stating this explicitly is advisable).  
- Eq. (4): Correct "x^" to "w^" and "f" to "F"; note the first max term dominates per Lemma 1.  
- Precisely define the condition number as κ = L/λ.  

The theoretical contribution is non-trivial, but the lack of a compelling ML relevance argument undermines the paper’s suitability for NIPS. The authors must:  
1) Re-evaluate the μ = Θ(N) assumption and quantify the practical regime where the hybrid method dominates BGD (e.g., via empirical comparisons on standard datasets).  
2) Contrast high-probability vs. deterministic convergence in terms of practical utility.  
3) Explicitly justify why the O(k² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient complexity translates to tangible benefits over BGD’s O(log(1/ε)) full gradients in ML contexts.  

Without these clarifications, the paper’s contribution remains confined to theoretical curiosity rather than addressing a meaningful ML optimization challenge. The current draft does not sufficiently demonstrate that the algorithm offers advantages over existing methods in practical settings, which is essential for NIPS.  

**Summary:** The method is theoretically interesting but lacks demonstration of practical relevance for ML. Theoretical novelty is offset by the unrealistic regime where the algorithm outperforms BGD. The minor corrections above are necessary for technical precision. The paper would benefit significantly from empirical validation on standard ML tasks to establish its utility. As written, it does not clearly establish a meaningful advantage over batch gradient descent for typical machine learning problems.
