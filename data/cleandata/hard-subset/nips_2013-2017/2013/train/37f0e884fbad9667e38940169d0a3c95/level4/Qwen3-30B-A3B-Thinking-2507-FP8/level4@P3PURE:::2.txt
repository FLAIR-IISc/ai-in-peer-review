

The paper introduces a "hybrid" deterministic-stochastic first-order optimization method that combines full gradient queries (deterministic oracle) and stochastic gradient queries. The key theoretical contribution claims that, under the assumption that the condition number $\kappa$ (i.e., the ratio of strong smoothness and strong convexity constants) is known *a priori*, and with access to a first-order hybrid oracle, a high-probability $O(\log(1/\epsilon))$ convergence rate for batch optimization can be achieved. This result is notable for eliminating the dependence on $\kappa$ in the convergence rate.

However, the paper has significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution. First, the theoretical analysis *only* addresses the idealized case where $\kappa$ is known exactly. The paper provides no analysis or discussion of the algorithm's behavior when $\kappa$ is misspecified (e.g., overestimated or underestimated), which is a critical practical limitation. The authors do not justify how $\kappa$ is obtained in practice—$\eta$ (a key hyperparameter in the update rule, Eq. 8) depends directly on $\kappa$, yet no estimation procedure or theoretical guidance for setting $\eta$ is provided. This renders the algorithm impractical without a method to estimate $\kappa$, which the paper omits. While the authors acknowledge the difficulty of analyzing unknown $\kappa$, they offer no experimental validation of the algorithm's robustness to misspecification, which would be a reasonable alternative.

Second, the paper lacks an experimental section entirely. Given that the main contribution is a novel algorithm, empirical validation is essential to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical claims. The authors should compare the algorithm against standard baselines (e.g., SGD, SAG, averaged SGD) on real datasets to demonstrate whether the $O(\log(1/\epsilon))$ rate manifests empirically and to evaluate sensitivity to $\kappa$ misspecification.

Third, the paper fails to contextualize its work within existing literature. It overlooks the related line of work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012) and does not discuss how the proposed algorithm relates to or differs from prior approaches (e.g., interleaving deterministic and stochastic steps versus the "mixed gradient" update). A comparison of convergence rates with these methods would strengthen the contribution.

Fourth, the problem setting is ambiguously defined. The paper does not clarify whether it addresses the *deterministic finite-sum problem* $\min_w \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n F_i(w)$ (solved by algorithms like SAG/SDCA) or the *stochastic approximation problem* $\min_w \mathbb{E}[F(w)]$. Section 3.1’s theoretical setup is misleading, and the claims later in the paper remain unanchored to one setting. This ambiguity must be resolved.

Finally, while the strong convexity/smoothness assumption is common in theory, the paper does not address the more practical non-strongly convex case (e.g., as discussed in Bach & Moulines, 2011), which limits the applicability of the result. The authors should acknowledge this restriction.

In summary, the theoretical result is interesting but hinges on an unrealistic assumption ($\kappa$ known exactly) without practical justification or experimental validation. The omission of experiments, related work, and clear problem formulation are major flaws. The paper would benefit from adding experiments testing $\kappa$ sensitivity, comparing with prior hybrid methods, and clarifying the optimization setting. Without these, the contribution’s practical value remains unverified.
