

The paper proposes a novel hybrid stochastic/batch gradient descent algorithm that combines stochastic and batch gradients. The authors establish convergence to an ε-accurate solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(k² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The convergence proof appears correct and novel, aside from minor technical errors detailed below.

My primary concern pertains to the algorithm's relevance for the machine learning community, which is the focus of NIPS. In standard ML settings, strong convexity is induced by the regularizer, implying the strong convexity parameter μ scales as μ = Θ(N), where N is the sample size and Θ(·) denotes proportionality with a constant independent of N. Under this typical assumption, the proposed method offers a speedup over batch gradient descent only when N = O(C³/L³), where C is the constant in μ = Θ(N) and L is the smoothness constant. This regime is not practically interesting for typical ML problems, as N is usually large. Furthermore, the convergence guarantee for the proposed algorithm holds only with high probability, while standard batch gradient descent provides deterministic convergence. This critical point must be thoroughly addressed to demonstrate a genuine advantage over batch gradient descent and establish the paper's relevance to ML optimization.

The paper's core contribution—novel hybrid stochastic/batch gradient descent—requires stronger justification for its practical advantage over standard batch gradient descent in typical ML optimization scenarios. Without this, the work's relevance to the ML community is questionable.
