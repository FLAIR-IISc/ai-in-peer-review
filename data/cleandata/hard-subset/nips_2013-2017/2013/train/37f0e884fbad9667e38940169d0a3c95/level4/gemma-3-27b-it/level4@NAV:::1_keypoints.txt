- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(k² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, making full-gradient calls independent of condition number k.
- Core idea: replace full-gradient epochs with O(k²) mixed-gradient steps per epoch, combining one full-gradient and many stochastic gradients, and average iterates to get constant suboptimality reduction.
- Provides a self-contained, straightforward proof of linear convergence with high-probability guarantees.
- Claims potential speedups over Nesterov’s method when the condition number k is sufficiently small.
- The algorithm is applicable to constrained optimization (given an efficient gradient mapping), unlike SAG which is unconstrained.
- EMGD’s proof is significantly simpler than SAG’s, which may aid insights and modifications.
- Theoretical comparison: EMGD outperforms Nesterov when k ≤ n^(2/3), while SAG outperforms EMGD in most regimes and is generally faster.
- In many practical ML settings with regularization ~C/n (k ≈ n/C'), SAG typically outperforms EMGD; authors should note this.
- Lacks experimental validation to demonstrate practical performance and relevant problem instances where EMGD is advantageous.
- Fails to sufficiently discuss limitations and drawbacks compared to existing methods (Section 3.4 needs expansion).
- Requires pre-specifying epoch length based on known k, unlike SCDA and SAG which adapt dynamically and may converge faster in practice.
- Does not discuss adaptive step-size strategies; SCDA offers automatic step sizes and SAG uses effective heuristics.
- Authors should add discussion of the sum-of-n-functions example clarifying where EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov each perform better or worse.
- Reviewer recommends citing Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods, noting differences in condition-number dependence.
- Paper is well-written, technically sound, original in technique combination, and potentially significant despite lacking experiments.
