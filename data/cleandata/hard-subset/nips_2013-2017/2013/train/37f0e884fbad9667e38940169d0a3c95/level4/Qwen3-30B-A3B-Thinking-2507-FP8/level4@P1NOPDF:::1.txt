SUMMARY: The authors study smooth strongly convex optimization over convex constraints, where the gradient mapping update is efficient. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence with a rate depending on √κ (κ = condition number), the authors consider settings with access to both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic gradient oracle. They propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm requiring only O(log 1/ε) full gradient calls (independent of κ) and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradient calls. The key insight is replacing each full gradient step (an "epoch") with O(κ²) mixed gradient steps using a single epoch-wise full gradient and varying stochastic gradients. Averaging iterates within an epoch yields a constant suboptimality decrease independent of κ, explaining the κ-independent epoch count. The convergence proof is self-contained and rigorous, though the paper lacks experiments.  

I found the algorithmic idea novel and compelling: mixing a few full gradient computations with many cheap stochastic steps. This work fits into recent efforts to maintain linear convergence using cheaper stochastic oracles. SAG [16] achieves this for finite-sum problems (e.g., regularized empirical loss minimization), where the full gradient is n times costlier than stochastic. SAG outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n/8, while EMGD does so when κ ≤ n^(2/3). For κ ≤ n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD achieve O(n log 1/ε),优于 Nesterov’s O(n^(5/4) log 1/ε). EMGD’s key advantages over SAG are: (1) it handles constrained optimization (assuming efficient gradient mapping), while SAG is unconstrained; and (2) its proof is significantly simpler than SAG’s, potentially enabling more insights and modifications, with a stronger high-probability guarantee (vs. SAG’s expectation). The memory/parallelization advantage mentioned is less clear, as SAG also uses mini-batches for parallelization and memory reduction.  

EVALUATION SUMMARY:  
**Pros:**  
- Novel, clean algorithm with solid theory.  
- Achieves linear convergence for finite-sum problems without κ multiplying n in the rate, while extending to constrained settings (unlike SAG).  
- Proof is self-contained and clear.  

**Cons:**  
- No experiments to demonstrate practical relevance or validate theoretical advantages.  
- Insufficient discussion of limitations (Section 3.4 needs substantial expansion).  
- Proof lacks high-level motivation for key design choices.  

**QUALITY:** The theory is sound. While experiments would strengthen the paper, the theoretical contribution is robust. The authors **must** expand Section 3.4 to address:  
1. **Regime comparisons**: For finite-sum problems, EMGD is outperformed by Nesterov when κ > n^(2/3), matches SAG for 1 ≤ κ ≤ n^(1/2), and is better than SAG (and Nesterov) for n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8. In typical ML settings (regularization parameter C/n, so κ ≈ n/C'), SAG still dominates, while EMGD does not.  
2. **Practical drawback**: EMGD requires fixing epoch size in advance (knowing κ), unlike SAG or SCDA [17], which adaptively adjust step counts and benefit from better local condition numbers or faster practical convergence. SCDA also has automatic step-size selection, while SAG uses a practical heuristic.  
3. **Citation**: The authors should cite Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012), which presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method with linear convergence but retains κ in the rate (unlike EMGD’s κ-independent full-gradient calls).  

**CLARITY:** The paper is well-written, with Table 1 offering a clear summary of convergence rates. The proof is rigorous but would benefit from high-level remarks explaining why key quantities (e.g., the averaging scheme) are chosen as they are.  

**ORIGINALITY:** The combination of techniques is novel.  

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The theoretical contribution is strong, especially given the simplicity of the EMGD proof compared to SAG’s complex analysis. However, practical relevance remains unproven.
