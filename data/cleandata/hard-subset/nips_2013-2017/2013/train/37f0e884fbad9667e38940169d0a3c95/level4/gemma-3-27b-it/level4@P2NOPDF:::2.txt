This paper introduces a hybrid deterministic-stochastic first-order optimization method aiming to circumvent the dependency on the condition number in convergence rates. The authors demonstrate a compelling theoretical result: under the assumption of a known condition number and the availability of a first-order hybrid oracle, a carefully balanced combination of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps achieves an O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate with high probability for batch optimization.

However, the paper suffers from significant limitations. The primary concern is the restrictive assumption of perfect knowledge of the condition number. The analysis does not address the algorithm’s behavior when this crucial hyperparameter is misspecified, which is highly likely in practical applications. While acknowledging the potential difficulty of analyzing the unknown condition number case theoretically, the authors do not compensate with any empirical evaluation. The complete absence of an experimental section is a major weakness, particularly given that the paper proposes a new algorithm. Demonstrating the algorithm's performance and robustness, or sensitivity to hyperparameter inaccuracies, through experimentation would substantially strengthen the contribution.

Specifically, the algorithm, denoted EGD, relies on a parameter η (Eq. 8) directly tied to the condition number κ (λ and L). The paper offers no guidance on setting or estimating κ, leaving a critical practical challenge unaddressed. Either a robust estimation procedure is required, or the analysis should consider the implications of misspecification. 

Furthermore, the work focuses exclusively on the strongly convex and smooth setting, potentially limiting its broader applicability. Exploring performance in non-strongly convex scenarios, as discussed in Bach & Moulines (2011), would expand the paper’s relevance.

The proposed algorithm’s formulation – blending gradients in each iteration – differs from the more common approach of alternating deterministic and stochastic steps. A discussion comparing EGD to algorithms that interleave these steps would be valuable. The authors also fail to acknowledge and contrast their work with related hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms, such as those presented by Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC, 2012). A comparison of convergence rates would be particularly insightful. 

Finally, the optimization setting is unclearly defined. It is not apparent whether the paper addresses deterministic optimization with a randomized algorithm or stochastic approximation. This ambiguity is reflected in the theoretical setup (Section 3.1) and requires clarification. The paper would benefit from explicitly stating its target problem and aligning the analysis accordingly.

In summary, while the theoretical result is noteworthy, the paper’s practical relevance is significantly hampered by the strong assumptions, lack of experimental validation, and limited discussion of related work. A thorough experimental evaluation, alongside a more nuanced discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and broader optimization settings, is essential to elevate this work.
