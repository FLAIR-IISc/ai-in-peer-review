The paper introduces a hybrid deterministic–stochastic first-order method that mixes queries to a full-gradient oracle with queries to a stochastic-gradient oracle. The main theoretical result is striking: if the condition number is known (i.e., both the strong convexity and smoothness constants of the objective are given) and if access to both oracles is available, then an appropriately chosen mixture of deterministic and stochastic steps yields, with high probability, an O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization. This removes the explicit dependence on the condition number from the convergence rate.

However, there are several significant concerns. Most importantly, the analysis assumes perfect prior knowledge of the condition number κ, and the paper does not address how the algorithm behaves when this hyper-parameter is misspecified. In effect, the current result shows that the dependence on κ can be removed only when κ is known to the algorithm in advance. If extending the theory to unknown κ is challenging, this gap could have been partially addressed empirically. Yet the paper contains no experiments, which is the second major concern. Since the main contribution is a new algorithm, it is essential to provide experiments to corroborate the theory and assess its practical relevance.

Algorithmically, EGD uses the update rule in Eq. (8), based on the “mixed gradient” defined in Eq. (7). This requires choosing the hyper-parameter η, which in turn effectively requires knowing κ (referred to as the “conditional number” in the paper), i.e., both the strong convexity modulus λ and the smoothness modulus L. The paper offers no guidance or theoretical principle for setting κ a priori. Two possibilities should be addressed: (1) κ is estimated, in which case an estimation procedure—at least a heuristic supported by experiments—should be described; or (2) κ is assumed known for theoretical purposes, in which case the analysis and/or experiments should study robustness to misspecification, including convergence behavior when κ (or equivalently η) is set too large or too small.

The restriction to smooth and strongly convex objectives, while common in theory and often reasonable, is still limiting. Non-strongly convex settings are also important in practice. It would be valuable to discuss these regimes and how the method behaves there; see Bach and Moulines (2011) for a theoretical comparison of convex versus strongly convex cases.

The idea of blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not new. Although the proposed method is not presented as an alternation of full and stochastic steps with distinct frequencies, but rather as a per-iteration mixed update (within an epoch) followed by a gradient-like step, it would be helpful to relate and compare EGD to the more direct alternation strategy, at least in the unconstrained case.

Related work on hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods is not reviewed. In particular, the paper should discuss and compare convergence rates with Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012), which also surveys earlier work in this area.

A thorough experimental study would substantially strengthen the paper, including comparisons with standard SGD, averaged SGD, and recent stochastic first-order methods such as SAG and SDCA.

A more minor concern is that the optimization setting is not clearly specified. The paper seeks to combine the exponential convergence typical of deterministic optimization with the condition-number behavior of stochastic methods, but it is unclear whether the target is the finite-sum deterministic problem (minimize F(w) = (1/n)∑ Fi(w)) using a randomized algorithm, or the stochastic approximation problem (minimize E[F(w)]). Note that SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for the finite-sum problem. The setup in Section 3.1 is misleading in this respect, and subsequent claims do not clarify the intended setting. This should be stated explicitly, which would be an easy fix.
