- Problem: optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex constraint set where gradient mapping updates are efficient.  
- Contribution: proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), mixing one full gradient per epoch with O(k^2) stochastic steps and averaging iterates.  
- Complexity: EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of condition number k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.  
- Core insight: averaging O(k^2) mixed-gradient iterates yields constant suboptimality reduction per epoch independent of k.  
- Theoretical novelty: achieves linear convergence while leveraging cheaper stochastic oracles; proof is simple and self-contained.  
- Comparison to Nesterov: EMGD can be faster than Nesterov when k is not too large (the full-gradient count does not scale with k).  
- Comparison to SAG: SAG outperforms EMGD in many regimes (EMGD theoretically better than Nesterov when k ≤ n^(2/3); SAG is better for many k ranges), though EMGD matches SAG complexity in limited regimes (e.g., k ≈ n^(1/2)).  
- Advantages over SAG: EMGD handles constrained problems (where gradient mapping is efficient) and has a simpler, high-probability convergence proof.  
- Practical caveats: no experimental validation is provided to assess empirical performance or practical impact.  
- Missing discussion: section 3.4 insufficiently addresses algorithm limitations and drawbacks, especially versus existing methods.  
- Practical limitation: EMGD requires predefining the number of steps per epoch based on known k, unlike SCDA and SAG which do not.  
- Step-size issues: SCDA has automatic step-size selection and SAG uses adaptive heuristics; EMGD lacks these practical adaptive features.  
- Regime note: in common ML setups with regularization C/n (k ≈ n/C), SAG can outperform EMGD — this practical regime should be discussed.  
- Related work: reviewer requests citation of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods.  
- Proof clarity: while correct, the proof needs more high-level commentary to explain motivations for chosen quantities and steps.  
- Writing/clarity: overall well-written; Table 1 is a helpful summary of convergence rates.  
- Specific improvements: expand discussion of sum-of-n functions example to clarify differences between EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov across k regimes.
