- Introduces EMGD, a hybrid first-order method mixing full and stochastic gradients, claiming O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient convergence removing √κ dependence.  
- Shows theoretical result: with known κ, EMGD attains high-probability linear convergence using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound with a clear inductive proof and proper contextualization.  
- Major weakness: analysis assumes condition number κ is known a priori, an unrealistic and critical limitation.  
- No method or analysis is provided for estimating κ or for performance under κ misspecification (over/underestimation).  
- No experimental validation; lacks empirical comparisons to SGD, SAG, SDCA, Nesterov, or other baselines on synthetic/real data.  
- Insufficient comparison to prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic work (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012); related work section is incomplete.  
- Ambiguous optimization setting: unclear if addressing deterministic empirical risk minimization with randomized algorithms or a stochastic approximation problem.  
- Quality: strong theory but undermined by the unrealistic known-κ assumption and lack of experiments.  
- Clarity: generally well written, but ambiguous problem setup and missing experiments weaken clarity.  
- Originality: mixing gradients is not entirely novel; main novelty is the κ-removal result, limited by the known-κ constraint.  
- Significance: theoretically interesting but practical relevance is unproven without κ-estimation analysis and empirical evidence.  
- Recommended fixes: analyze unknown/misspecified κ, add comprehensive experiments, and better situate comparisons with existing hybrid methods.
