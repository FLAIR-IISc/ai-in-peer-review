This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a setting where the optimal first-order method achieves a linear convergence rate that depends on the square root of the condition number, κ. The authors focus on ill-conditioned problems where this dependency leads to a high number of computationally expensive full gradient evaluations. The core proposal is a novel hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. The key result is that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution with only O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full gradient oracle—a number independent of κ—at the cost of O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle. The main algorithmic idea involves partitioning the optimization into "epochs." At the start of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. This is then refined over O(κ²) inner iterations using a "mixed gradient" that combines the fixed full gradient with fresh stochastic information. The analysis shows that this procedure guarantees a constant reduction in error per epoch, leading to the overall linear convergence rate.

This work situates itself among a recent line of research aiming to achieve linear convergence rates while primarily using inexpensive stochastic gradients. A notable breakthrough in this area is the Stochastic Average Gradient (SAG) algorithm, which is highly relevant for empirical risk minimization problems where the objective is a finite sum of `n` functions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Algorithmic Contribution:** The paper presents a novel and elegant algorithmic idea. The concept of mixing a small number of full gradient computations with a large number of inexpensive stochastic steps to eliminate the condition number dependence for the expensive oracle is both interesting and theoretically sound.
*   **Simple and Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The theoretical analysis is a significant strength. The proof of convergence is remarkably simple and self-contained, especially when compared to the complex analysis for the related SAG algorithm. This simplicity may lead to further insights and make future modifications more amenable to analysis. Furthermore, the convergence guarantee holds with high probability, which is a stronger result than the in-expectation guarantees of methods like SAG.
*   **Generality:** The proposed algorithm is more general than some competing methods. It naturally handles constrained optimization problems (provided the projection onto the constraint set is efficient), whereas SAG and SDCA are primarily designed for unconstrained problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **No Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness is the complete absence of experiments. While the theoretical contribution is strong, an empirical study is needed to demonstrate that the algorithm is practically viable and to identify real-world scenarios where its theoretical advantages translate into performance gains.
*   **Limited Discussion of Drawbacks:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the algorithm's limitations and practical drawbacks. Section 3.4 provides a comparison but would be significantly strengthened by addressing the algorithm's dependencies and contrasting them with more adaptive methods (details below).
*   **Lack of High-Level Intuition in Proof:** While the proof is correct and easy to follow step-by-step, it would benefit from more high-level commentary explaining the purpose of key definitions and steps. In its current form, some derivations feel slightly magical rather than well-motivated.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

The paper is well-written, and the theoretical contribution is solid. However, its value and context could be significantly clarified with the following additions and revisions.

1.  **Improved Comparison to Related Work (Section 3.4):** The current comparison is a good start, but it should be expanded to provide a more nuanced picture.
    *   For the finite sum setting (`F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w)`), assume a full gradient is `n` times more expensive than a stochastic one. The authors should explicitly state the regimes of κ where EMGD is superior to Nesterov's method (roughly κ ≤ n^(2/3)) and compare this to SAG (superior to Nesterov when κ ≤ n/8). This shows EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov in a wider regime.
    *   Crucially, the comparison should also show that EMGD is slower than SAG in all regimes (having the same O(·) complexity only when κ ≤ n^(1/2)).
    *   A significant practical scenario in machine learning involves regularization where the parameter is set as λ ~ 1/n. This leads to a condition number κ ~ n. In this important regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov's method, but EMGD does not. This is a critical limitation that should be discussed. Perhaps the authors can suggest other practical settings where κ < n^(2/3) is a reasonable assumption.
    *   Please cite and discuss "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" by M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt (SISC, 2012). This paper also presents a hybrid algorithm with a linear convergence rate. While its rate still depends on the condition number (making the EMGD result theoretically stronger for moderate κ), it is a highly relevant piece of prior art.

2.  **Discussion of Practicality:** The paper should honestly address the practical challenges of implementing EMGD.
    *   The number of inner iterations, `T`, depends directly on the condition number κ, which is often unknown in practice. This is a major drawback compared to SAG or SDCA, which do not require fixing such a parameter in advance.
    *   The fixed number of inner steps prevents the algorithm from capitalizing on faster-than-expected practical convergence or favorable local properties of the objective function. In contrast, methods like SDCA and SAG can terminate based on duality gap or other criteria. SDCA also benefits from automatic step-size selection, and SAG has an effective adaptive step-size heuristic.

3.  **Minor Comments and Typos:**
    *   Line 50: Suggest rephrasing to "are summarized in Table 1 and detailed in the Related Work section" to clarify where the citations for the rates can be found.
    *   Line 118: It would be helpful to specify that SDCA is defined for a more specific functional form than SAG.
    *   Line 132: The oracle definition `F(w) = E[f(w)]` should be explicitly stated to hold for all `w ∈ W`.
    *   Line 139: It would be clearer to also state `∇F(w) = E[∇f(w)]`.
    *   Line 224: The claim that SAG and SDCA cannot take advantage of distributed computing is not accurate. Both can be parallelized using mini-batches, which also helps reduce memory requirements. This point should be corrected.
    *   Line 304: In summations like `Σ (F(wt) - F(w*))`, please use parentheses to make it clear that the entire term is being summed.
    *   Line 366: The second inequality relies on the condition δ ≤ e^(-1/2), which is stated in assumption (5). It would be good to explicitly mention this in the derivation to justify the step.
    *   Typo (line 42): "an convex" -> "a convex"
    *   Typo (line 307): Use a norm symbol `||·||` instead of absolute value `|·|`.
    *   Consistency (line 366): For consistency, replace `log` with `ln`.
    *   Consistency (line 88 and elsewhere): The domain is referred to as `W` and `D` in different places. Please use one symbol consistently throughout the paper.

**Conclusion:**

The authors present a novel and interesting algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization with a clean, simple, and solid theory. The paper's primary weakness is the lack of an experimental section to illustrate the practical relevance of the algorithm. However, the theoretical contribution could stand on its own, provided the authors add a more complete discussion of the algorithm's properties, limitations, and relationship to prior work, as suggested in this review. With these revisions, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the conference.
