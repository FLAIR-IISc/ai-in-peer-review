This paper presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining calls to a deterministic oracle (querying full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (querying stochastic gradients). The authors demonstrate that this approach allows for the removal of the dependency on the condition number in the rate of convergence.

The authors present an interesting and striking theoretical result. Assuming the condition number is known (i.e., both strong convexity and strong smoothness constants of the objective are known), and assuming a first-order hybrid oracle (both stochastic and deterministic gradient can be queried) is available, they show that with a suitably chosen mix of deterministic gradient and stochastic gradient steps, one can achieve a O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization with high probability.

However, there are several concerns with the current state of the paper. First, the theoretical analysis only covers the case where the condition number is perfectly known beforehand and does not discuss the behavior of the algorithm when this hyper-parameter is misspecified. The current result states that the dependence on the condition number (kappa) can be removed from the convergence rate of a first-order optimization algorithm when this condition number is known beforehand to the algorithm. A discussion on the behavior of the algorithm when kappa is unknown or misspecified would strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, the paper lacks an experiments section, which is a major concern. Since the main contribution of the paper is a new algorithm, it would be beneficial to perform experiments to assess the theoretical results presented in the paper and study their relevance to the actual behavior of the algorithm on empirical data. Experimental analysis in cases where the hyper-parameter kappa is unknown or misspecified would be particularly valuable.

The proposed algorithm (EGD) relies on the update rule defined by Eq. 8 (page 4) using the so-called "mixed gradient" defined in Eq. 7. Therefore, EGD requires the hyper-parameter eta to be set (or estimated), which boils down to knowing the condition number kappa. However, the authors do not provide any guideline or theoretical argument allowing to set kappa beforehand. A discussion on how to estimate or set kappa would improve the paper.

Additionally, the smooth and strongly convex case can be too restrictive, and other settings (non-strongly convex) are also interesting. The non-strongly convex case is important, as it arises in several situations. A comparison with existing algorithms and a discussion on the applicability of the proposed algorithm to different settings would be beneficial.

The idea of blending deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps in a first-order optimization algorithm is not new. A comparison with similar algorithms, such as those that interleave deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps, would be valuable. Furthermore, the authors do not review a related line of work, namely hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms. Discussing and comparing the convergence rates with existing algorithms would strengthen the paper.

Finally, the optimization setting considered in the paper is not clearly stated. The authors should specify whether they intend to solve the deterministic optimization problem or the stochastic approximation problem. Clarifying the theoretical setup would improve the paper.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical result, it has several limitations that need to be addressed. The addition of an experiments section, a discussion on the estimation or setting of kappa, and a comparison with existing algorithms would significantly improve the paper.
