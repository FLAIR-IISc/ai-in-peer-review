SUMMARY AND RELATION TO PRIOR WORK
The paper studies first-order optimization of smooth, strongly convex objectives over a convex set when both full and stochastic gradients are available. Standard accelerated methods achieve a linear rate in terms of log(1/ε) but require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations, which is costly when the condition number κ is large. The authors propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid method that computes one full gradient per epoch and then performs a fixed number of “mixed” updates using a combination of that full gradient and stochastic gradients centered at the epoch anchor point. The key claim is that EMGD requires only O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations (independent of κ), together with O(poly(κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations, while maintaining a linear convergence rate with high probability.

This work connects to recent efforts to obtain linear convergence using stochastic information in finite-sum problems F(w) = (1/n)∑i fi(w), notably SAG and SDCA, which achieve O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) or O(n log(1/ε)) rates under certain conditions. Compared with SAG/SDCA, EMGD: (i) applies to constrained problems (given efficient projections), (ii) uses only O(d) memory, and (iii) establishes high-probability rates. The paper also places EMGD alongside classical deterministic results (Nesterov’s accelerated methods) and optimal stochastic rates for strongly convex problems.

STRENGTHS
- Conceptual novelty: a clean mixed-gradient update that leverages a single full gradient per epoch to reduce variance of stochastic updates via anchoring and shrinkage of the feasible ball.
- Theory: simple, self-contained proof establishing linear convergence with high probability; clear statement of oracle model and assumptions.
- Scope: handles constrained problems and does not rely on storing per-example gradients, unlike SAG/SDCA.

WEAKNESSES
- No experiments: the paper lacks empirical evidence that EMGD is practically competitive or that its advantages materialize on real datasets.
- Parameter knowledge: the number of within-epoch iterations (and step size) depends on κ, which is typically unknown; this limits practicality relative to SAG/SDCA, which do not require fixing the horizon and can adapt better to local curvature.
- Complexity in finite-sum setting: for many regimes EMGD is asymptotically slower than SAG/SDCA. In particular, when κ ≳ n1/2, SAG/SDCA tend to be more favorable; EMGD improves over full-gradient methods only up to roughly κ ≲ n2/3 as argued by the authors.
- Some claims in the comparisons section are too strong or incomplete (e.g., statements about distributed computing and memory use for SAG/SDCA). There is also an apparent inconsistency between the abstract (O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients) and the main theorem/algorithm text (O(κ^2 log(1/ε))).

DETAILED EVALUATION
Quality: The analysis appears technically sound and the proof is clear. The high-probability guarantee is a nice strengthening over results that hold only in expectation. However, practical issues (unknown κ, cost of projections onto W ∩ B(·), and the κ^2 factor in stochastic calls) are not fully discussed. An experimental section would substantially strengthen the contribution.

Clarity: The paper is well written overall, with a helpful summary of known rates. The proof would benefit from a brief high-level narrative explaining why anchoring plus domain shrinkage yields a bounded-variance mixed gradient and constant error reduction per epoch. Please reconcile the κ vs. κ^2 discrepancy for stochastic-gradient complexity and clarify all assumptions on oracles (explicitly state ∇F(w) = E[∇f(w)] for all w).

Originality: The idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients per epoch is a novel and elegant combination of known tools (variance reduction via centering, epoching, and constrained gradient mapping). Related hybrid methods should be cited and contrasted, e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012), which achieves linear-like behavior but still depends on κ in the rate.

Significance: The method extends linear-rate stochastic optimization to constrained problems with low memory and a simple analysis. Its practical impact remains to be demonstrated; nonetheless the theoretical contribution is of interest, especially the high-probability rate and the κ-independent number of full gradients.

ARGUMENTS FOR ACCEPTANCE
- Clear, novel algorithmic idea with a clean, high-probability linear-rate analysis.
- Broader applicability than SAG/SDCA (handles constraints) with O(d) memory.
- Useful perspective on trading few full gradients for many cheap stochastic updates.

ARGUMENTS AGAINST ACCEPTANCE
- No empirical validation; unclear practical competitiveness vs. SAG/SDCA/accelerated methods.
- Requires prior knowledge (or good estimate) of κ and a fixed epoch length.
- In finite-sum problems, asymptotic complexity is weaker than SAG/SDCA in several realistic regimes (e.g., common regularization scaling where κ ≈ n/C).

SUGGESTIONS FOR IMPROVEMENT
- Expand Section 3.4 with a quantitative finite-sum comparison. For example, under the cost model “full gradient = n stochastic gradients”: EMGD improves over Nesterov when κ ≲ n2/3; EMGD and SAG are comparable when κ ≲ n1/2; SAG/SDCA outperform EMGD for larger κ (up to their respective thresholds). Include the common ML regime where the strong convexity parameter scales like 1/n, yielding κ ≈ n/C, under which SAG/SDCA remain favorable while EMGD may not.
- Discuss practicality: how to set T and η without κ; suggest adaptive/doubling schemes or curvature estimation (as briefly mentioned in the conclusion). Clarify the computational cost of the projection onto W ∩ B(·).
- Correct and qualify claims about distributed and memory efficiency: SAG/SDCA can use mini-batching and parallelism, which also reduces memory footprint; please revise accordingly.
- Reconcile the stochastic-gradient complexity: abstract states O(κ log(1/ε)) while Theorem 1 and algorithm text imply O(κ^2 log(1/ε)).
- Add a short high-level intuition paragraph before the formal proof: mixed gradient = full gradient at anchor + centered stochastic gradient; smoothness and shrinking ball bound the stochastic term, yielding a constant contraction per epoch.
- Cite and contrast Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) as an earlier hybrid deterministic–stochastic method with linear convergence dependent on κ.

MINOR EDITORIAL COMMENTS
- Fix typos (e.g., “an convex” → “a convex”); keep domain notation consistent; use norm symbols where appropriate; be consistent with ln vs. log.
- Explicitly state for all w that ∇F(w) = E[∇f(w)] and that Of yields L-smooth f with F also L-smooth.
- Clarify the expectation vs. high-probability guarantees across methods when making comparative claims.

OVERALL
Quality: 7/10
Clarity: 8/10
Originality: 8/10
Significance: 7/10

I recommend acceptance if the authors substantially strengthen the comparison/discussion section, reconcile the κ vs. κ^2 issue, and add at least a small empirical illustration. Even without experiments, the theoretical contribution is clear and potentially impactful.
