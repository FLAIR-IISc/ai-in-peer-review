This paper proposes a hybrid deterministic–stochastic first-order method that mixes calls to a full-gradient oracle and a stochastic-gradient oracle. The main theoretical claim is striking: if the condition number is known (i.e., both the strong convexity constant λ and the smoothness constant L are known), then by appropriately mixing deterministic and stochastic gradient steps, one can achieve with high probability an O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate for batch optimization, effectively eliminating the dependence on the condition number in the rate.

While the theoretical result is interesting, I have several concerns about the current version of the paper.

1) Dependence on knowing the condition number and robustness to misspecification. The analysis assumes exact prior knowledge of the condition number κ = L/λ. The proposed algorithm (EGD) uses the update in Eq. 8 with the “mixed gradient” from Eq. 7 and requires a step-size parameter η that in turn depends on κ. As far as I can tell, the paper provides no guidance or theoretical argument for setting κ (and hence η) in practice. Two scenarios should be addressed:
- If κ must be estimated, then the paper should describe an estimation or calibration procedure (even a heuristic), and support it with numerical experiments.
- If κ is assumed known because the paper’s focus is theoretical, then the theory and/or experiments should analyze robustness to misspecification. In particular, what happens to the convergence rate when κ (or η) is set too large or too small? Currently, the result shows that dependence on κ can be removed from the rate only when κ is known in advance; the behavior when κ is unknown remains unaddressed.

2) Lack of experiments. Given that the main contribution is an algorithm, an empirical section is essential to assess the relevance of the theory to practical behavior. Experiments could (i) validate the predicted O(log(1/ε)) rate under approximate knowledge of κ; (ii) study sensitivity to κ misspecification; and (iii) compare against strong baselines. At minimum, comparisons with standard SGD, averaged SGD, and recent stochastic first-order methods such as SAG (and, where appropriate, SDCA) should be included.

3) Scope of assumptions. The analysis is restricted to the smooth and strongly convex setting. While standard in theory and often realistic, this is still limiting. The non-strongly convex case is also important in many applications. It would be valuable to discuss whether the approach and rates extend to the general convex case, and how the behavior might change across regimes; see, e.g., Bach & Moulines (2011) for a clear delineation of behaviors in convex vs. strongly convex settings.

4) Relation to hybrid/variance-reduction literature and algorithmic form. Blending deterministic and stochastic gradient information is not new. The proposed method is presented as a per-iteration mixed update (within an epoch), followed by a gradient-like update, rather than as an explicit alternation between full-gradient and stochastic-gradient steps with distinct frequencies. It would strengthen the paper to:
- Clarify the relationship to algorithms that explicitly interleave deterministic and stochastic steps (at least in the unconstrained case), and explain whether the mixed update can be interpreted as such an alternation.
- Discuss and compare convergence guarantees with prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods, in particular Friedlander & Schmidt, “Hybrid Deterministic–Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting” (SISC, 2012), which also surveys earlier work in this vein. A direct rate comparison and a positioning of the proposed contribution relative to that line of work are currently missing.

5) Clarify the problem setting. The paper aims to combine the exponential convergence typical of deterministic methods with the reduced dependence on κ seen in stochastic methods. However, it is not clearly stated whether the target is:
- Deterministic finite-sum optimization: minimize F(w) = (1/n) ∑_{i=1}^n F_i(w) using randomized algorithms (as in SAG and SDCA), or
- Stochastic approximation: minimize E[F(w)] given access to samples.
Section 3.1 is ambiguous on this point, and the later claims do not resolve the ambiguity. The paper should explicitly state which setting is analyzed and for which the guarantees hold, and, if both are considered, make the distinctions precise.

Overall assessment. The theoretical result—removing explicit κ dependence from the convergence rate under known κ by mixing full and stochastic gradients—is elegant and potentially impactful. However, the paper in its current form is incomplete. To be competitive, it should:
- Provide guidance on setting κ (and η) or analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Include an empirical section validating the theory and benchmarking against strong baselines (SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, and related methods).
- Clarify the optimization setting (finite-sum vs. stochastic approximation).
- Discuss extensions beyond the strongly convex case.
- Position the work relative to prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) and related variance-reduction techniques.

Addressing these points would substantially strengthen both the clarity and the practical relevance of the contribution.
