Summary:
The authors tackle the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. They aim to improve upon Nesterov's optimal first-order algorithm, which achieves linear convergence but has a constant dependent on the square root of the condition number k. The authors propose a hybrid algorithm, called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that leverages both expensive full gradient and cheap stochastic gradient oracles. EMGD requires O(log 1/eps) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of the condition number) and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle. This could lead to faster convergence in theory, provided the condition number is not too large.

The main idea behind EMGD is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with a fixed number of O(k^2) mixed gradient steps, combining the full gradient (computed once per epoch) with stochastic gradients (which vary within an epoch). By averaging the O(k^2) iterates within an epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant decrease in suboptimality, independent of the condition number. This is why the number of required full gradient step computations (the number of epochs) is independent of the condition number. The authors provide a simple and self-contained proof of their convergence rate, but no experimental results.

I enjoyed reading this paper and appreciate the novel idea of mixing full gradient computations with cheap stochastic gradient steps. This work contributes to a recent line of research attempting to utilize the cheaper stochastic oracle while maintaining a linear convergence rate. A notable breakthrough in this area is the SAG algorithm [16], which works for smooth strongly convex objectives that are the sum of n simple functions (e.g., regularized empirical loss minimization, where n is the number of training examples). Assuming the full gradient oracle is n times more expensive to compute than the stochastic gradient one, SAG is faster in theory than Nesterov's algorithm when the condition number k ≤ n/8. In contrast, EMGD is faster than Nesterov's algorithm in theory when k ≤ n^(2/3), but slower than SAG in all regimes (with the same big O when k ≤ n^1/2).

To illustrate the speed-up, consider the case where k = n^1/2. Both SAG and EMGD achieve O(n log(1/eps)), whereas Nesterov's algorithm requires O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)). The two primary advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) EMGD works for constrained optimization (assuming efficient gradient mapping updates), whereas SAG is currently limited to unconstrained problems; and (2) the convergence proof for EMGD is much simpler than SAG's, potentially yielding more insights and facilitating modifications with provable guarantees. The authors also mention a possible memory and parallelization advantage, although this is less clear, as SAG can be parallelized using mini-batches, which also reduces memory requirements.

Evaluation Summary:

Pros:
- Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theory.
- Achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization, similar to SAG, but with a more general algorithm (applicable to constrained optimization) and a simpler proof.
- The paper is clearly written, with a self-contained proof.

Cons:
- Lacks experimental results to demonstrate the algorithm's practical relevance.
- Fails to discuss the limitations and drawbacks of the algorithm, particularly in comparison to existing algorithms (Section 3.4 should be improved).
- The proof lacks high-level comments to justify the essential insights used in its construction.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound, and the theoretical contribution could stand on its own. However, the authors should extend Section 3.4 to discuss the limitations and drawbacks of their algorithm. They should also provide a more concrete discussion of the sum of n functions example, highlighting the differences with SAG and Nesterov. A significant drawback of EMGD is that the number of steps within an epoch must be fixed in advance (with knowledge of k), unlike SCDA [17] and SAG, which can adapt to the number of steps and potentially benefit from faster practical convergence or better local condition numbers. Furthermore, SCDA has automatic step-size selection, while SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic that works well in practice. The authors should add this discussion to the paper.

Addendum:
After discussing with other reviewers, it was pointed out that often in practice, the regularization parameter is C/n, resulting in a condition number of approximately n/C', a regime in which SAG outperforms Nesterov, but EMGD does not. This should be noted, and another practical setting where k < n^(2/3) should be mentioned. The authors should also cite [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting, M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt, SISC, 2012], which presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear rate of convergence, although the condition number still appears in the rate, making it less efficient than the proposed algorithm.

Clarity:
The paper is fairly clear, and I appreciated the summary in Table 1. However, the proof could be improved with high-level comments to motivate the defined quantities, which currently seem to fit together somewhat magically.

Originality:
This is a novel combination of known techniques.

Significance:
The practical relevance of the algorithm is not yet demonstrated, but the theoretical contribution could have an impact, particularly in the context of the difficult proof of convergence of SAG and the simple proof of SDCA (albeit for a restricted setup).
