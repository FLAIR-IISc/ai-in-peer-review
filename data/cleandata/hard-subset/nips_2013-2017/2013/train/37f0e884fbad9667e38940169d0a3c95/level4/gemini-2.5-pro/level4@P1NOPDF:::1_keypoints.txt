- Paper proposes Epoch-wise Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth, strongly convex optimization using few full gradients and many cheap stochastic gradients.  
- EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of condition number k) and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.  
- Core idea: replace each full-gradient epoch with O(k^2) mixed-gradient steps, averaging iterates to get condition-number-independent decrease.  
- Theoretical analysis is simple, self-contained, and provides high-probability linear convergence.  
- No experiments are provided to demonstrate practical performance or real-world relevance.  
- EMGD is novel and interesting, situated among recent hybrid/stochastic methods (e.g., SAG, SCDA).  
- Compared to SAG, EMGD handles constrained problems and has a much simpler proof, but is slower than SAG in all regimes.  
- EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov when k ≤ n^(2/3); SAG is faster for many practical regimes (e.g., k ≤ n/8).  
- For k = n^(1/2), EMGD and SAG have O(n log(1/ε)) complexity; Nesterov is O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)).  
- EMGD’s convergence guarantee is stronger (high probability) than SAG’s (in expectation).  
- Claimed memory/parallelization advantages over SAG are unconvincing since SAG can use mini-batches and be parallelized.  
- Major practical drawback: epoch length must be fixed using known condition number k, unlike SAG/SCDA which are adaptive.  
- Authors should add experiments and concrete examples where EMGD’s constraints-handling advantage matters.  
- Section 3.4 must be expanded to discuss limitations, comparisons, and practical drawbacks (step-size, adaptivity, etc.).  
- Include a concrete comparison for sum-of-n-functions case detailing regimes where EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov dominate.  
- Add discussion that with common regularization k ≈ n/C', EMGD can be worse than SAG; suggest practical settings where k < n^(2/3) holds.  
- Cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods and contrast rates/dependencies on k.  
- Proofs are rigorous but need more high-level intuition and commentary to help readers understand key constructs.  
- Many minor clarity and notation fixes are recommended (table references, domain notation, explicit statements, parentheses, norm notation).  
- Several typos and small edits are listed for correction (e.g., "an convex" → "a convex", consistent ln/log usage).  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and theoretically valuable; acceptance contingent on adding limitations discussion and improved exposition, though experiments would strengthen the paper.
