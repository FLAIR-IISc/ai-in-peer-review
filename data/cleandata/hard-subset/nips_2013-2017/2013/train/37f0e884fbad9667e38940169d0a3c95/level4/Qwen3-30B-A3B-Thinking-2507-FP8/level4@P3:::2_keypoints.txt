- Paper proposes EMGD, mixing full and stochastic gradients to get linear convergence for smooth, strongly convex problems without condition-number dependence.
- Claims EMGD needs O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, improving over standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) full-gradient methods when κ is known.
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound with a rigorous proof of Theorem 1 supporting the claimed convergence rate.
- The mixed gradient update (Equation 7) is a novel technical contribution.
- Clear distinction between full and stochastic gradient access and useful complexity comparison to SAG and SDCA (Table 2).
- Major weakness: algorithm requires exact prior knowledge of the condition number κ, which is unrealistic in practice.
- No analysis of behavior under κ misspecification (over- or underestimation), a critical practical gap.
- Paper contains no experiments, so practical performance and robustness to unknown/approximate κ are unvalidated.
- Related work section omits Friedlander and Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods, weakening contextualization and novelty claims.
- Optimization setting is ambiguous—unclear if addressing finite-sum deterministic minimization or stochastic approximation—this affects comparisons to SAG/SDCA.
- Quality: strong theory in the known-κ regime but incomplete concerning practical implementation and robustness.
- Clarity: generally organized but reduced by ambiguity in problem setting and absence of empirical results.
- Originality: mixed gradient idea is novel, but combining deterministic and stochastic steps is not entirely new.
- Significance: theoretical interest is limited by the impractical requirement of known κ and lack of empirical validation.
- Recommendation: reject; required improvements include analysis for κ misspecification, empirical comparisons with SAG/SDCA and standard methods, clarification of the optimization setting, and discussion of practical κ estimation.
