The paper presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining calls to a deterministic oracle (querying full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (querying stochastic gradients). This approach allows the authors to demonstrate that the dependency on the condition number can be dropped from the rate of convergence. 

The authors present an interesting and striking theoretical result. Assuming the condition number is known (i.e., both strong convexity and strong smoothness constants of the objective are known), and assuming a first-order hybrid oracle (both stochastic and deterministic gradients can be queried) is available, they show that with a suitably chosen mix of deterministic gradient and stochastic gradient steps, one can achieve a O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate for batch optimization with high probability.

However, there are several concerns with the current state of the paper. Firstly, the theoretical analysis only covers the case where the condition number is perfectly known beforehand, and does not address the behavior of the algorithm when this hyper-parameter is misspecified. The current result states that the dependence on the condition number (kappa) can be removed from the convergence rate of a first-order optimization algorithm when this condition number is known beforehand to the algorithm. The analysis for cases where kappa is unknown may be challenging, but experimental studies could have been conducted to investigate this. However, the paper lacks an experiments section, which is the second major concern. Given that the main contribution of the paper is a new algorithm, it would be reasonable to expect some experiments to assess the theoretical results and study their relevance to the actual behavior of the algorithm on empirical data.

Detailed comments:
The proposed algorithm (EGD) relies on the update rule defined by Eq. 8 (page 4) using the so-called "mixed gradient" defined in Eq. 7. Therefore, EGD requires the hyper-parameter $\eta$ to be set (or estimated), which boils down to knowing the condition number $\kappa$ (i.e., both the "strong convexity modulus" $\lambda$ and the "strong smoothness modulus" $L$). However, the authors do not provide any guideline or theoretical argument for setting $\kappa$ beforehand. There are two possibilities: either this parameter has to be estimated, in which case a corresponding estimation procedure is missing (even a heuristic procedure supported by numerical experiments would be acceptable), or this parameter is assumed known, in which case the theoretical analysis/experimental section should cover the cases where this hyper-parameter is misspecified, including studying the convergence rate when the hyper-parameter is set too large or too small.

Although the smooth and strongly convex case is popular in theoretical analysis and realistic in many situations, it can be too restrictive, and other settings (e.g., non-strongly convex) are also interesting. In particular, the non-strongly convex case is important, as it arises in several situations. See Bach & Moulines (2011) for a theoretical analysis of the different behaviors depending on the cases (convex vs strongly convex).

There are other concerns. Blending deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps in a first-order optimization algorithm is not a new idea. The algorithm presented in the paper is not written as an alternation of deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps with different frequencies for each type of step, but rather as one "mixed" update per iteration (within an epoch), followed by a gradient-like update step. It would be interesting to discuss how the proposed algorithm relates to and compares with a similar-in-spirit algorithm that interleaves deterministic gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps (at least in the "unconstrained" case).

The authors do not review a related line of work, namely hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms; see Friedlander & Schmidt (2012). Discussing and comparing the convergence rates would be valuable. See also the above reference for a review of older works on this topic.

A thorough experimental study would be a valuable addition to the paper, including a detailed comparison with regular SGD, averaged SGD, and recent proposals for stochastic first-order optimization (e.g., SAG).

A minor concern is that the optimization setting considered in the paper is not clearly stated. The purpose of the paper is to get the best of both worlds (deterministic optimization and stochastic optimization), namely exponential rate of convergence from the deterministic world and dependence on the condition number from the stochastic world. The authors do not specify clearly what they intend to solve: the deterministic optimization problem [Min_w F(w)=1/n \sum{i=1}^n F_i(w)] with a "stochastic" (or more precisely, "randomized") algorithm, or the stochastic approximation problem [Min_w E(F(w))]. Note that both SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms for solving the deterministic optimization problem [Min_w F(w)=1/n \sum{i=1}^n F_i(w)]. The theoretical setup stated in Section 3.1 is misleading in this respect, and none of the claims made later in the paper clarifies which setting is considered. This could easily be fixed.
