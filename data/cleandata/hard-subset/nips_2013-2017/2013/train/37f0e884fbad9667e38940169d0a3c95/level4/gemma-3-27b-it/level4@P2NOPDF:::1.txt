## Review of Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD)

**Quality: 7/10**
**Clarity: 8/10**
**Originality: 8/10**
**Significance: 7/10**

**SUMMARY:** The authors address the problem of optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions over convex constraint sets, where gradient computations are efficient. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence, its convergence rate depends on the square root of the condition number *k*. This paper proposes a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), leveraging both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. EMGD requires O(log 1/ε) calls to the full gradient oracle—independent of *k*—and O(*k*<sup>2</sup> log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle. For sufficiently small condition numbers, this offers a theoretical advantage. The core idea of EMGD is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with *O*(*k*<sup>2</sup>) mixed gradient steps, combining the full gradient (computed once per epoch) with stochastic gradients. By averaging iterates within each epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant decrease in suboptimality independent of *k*, resulting in a full gradient step count (and thus the number of epochs) independent of the condition number. The paper provides a clear and self-contained proof of the convergence rate, but lacks experimental validation.

I found this paper enjoyable and believe the concept of blending full and stochastic gradient computations is both novel and promising. This work contributes to a growing body of research aiming to leverage cheaper stochastic oracles while preserving linear convergence. The SAG algorithm [16] recently achieved a theoretical and practical breakthrough for smooth, strongly convex objectives expressible as the sum of *n* simpler functions, where the full gradient is reasonably assumed to be *n* times more expensive than the stochastic gradient. SAG outperforms Nesterov’s algorithm when *k* ≤ *n*/8. EMGD, in contrast, theoretically surpasses Nesterov’s algorithm when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>2/3</sup>, but is generally slower than SAG. Specifically, they share the same big-O complexity when *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>.  However, EMGD offers two key advantages over SAG: first, it extends to constrained optimization problems (given an efficiently computable gradient mapping), unlike the current unconstrained formulation of SAG; and second, its convergence proof is significantly simpler, potentially leading to new insights and easier modifications with provable guarantees (the result also holds with high probability compared to SAG’s in-expectation guarantee). The authors also suggest potential memory and parallelization benefits, though these are less definitive as SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which reduces memory requirements.

**EVALUATION SUMMARY:**

**Pros:**

* Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithmic idea with a clean and simple theory.
* Achieves linear convergence for regularized empirical loss minimization, similar to SAG, but without the condition number scaling with the number of training examples *n*, offering broader applicability and a simpler proof.
* The paper is clearly written and the proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**

* Lacks experimental evaluation to demonstrate practical performance gains. It also doesn't offer concrete examples illustrating the potential benefits of the theoretical advantages.
* The discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and drawbacks, particularly in comparison to existing methods, is insufficient and section 3.4 should be expanded.
* The proof could benefit from more high-level commentary to clarify the motivations behind key steps and defined quantities.

**QUALITY:** The paper is technically sound. While experiments would strengthen the submission, the theoretical contribution is valuable in its own right. The authors should significantly expand section 3.4 to address the algorithm's limitations and drawbacks. A more detailed discussion of the sum-of-*n* functions example, highlighting the differences with SAG and Nesterov, would be beneficial (e.g., EMGD is worse than Nesterov for roughly *k* > *n*<sup>2/3</sup>; SAG performs similarly to EMGD for 1 ≤ *k* ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>; outperforms EMGD (and Nesterov) for *n*<sup>1/2</sup> < *k* ≤ *n*/8; and is worse than Nesterov for *k* > *n*/8). A practical limitation of EMGD is the need to pre-specify the number of steps per epoch, requiring knowledge of *k*; SCDA [17] and SAG avoid this limitation and can potentially achieve faster practical convergence or benefit from better local condition numbers. Furthermore, SCDA offers automatic step-size selection, while SAG utilizes a practical adaptive step-size heuristic. This discussion should be included.

[**ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers:** a) As noted by another reviewer, in many machine learning applications, the regularization parameter is often scaled as *C*/n, resulting in a condition number of approximately *n*/C’. This regime often favors SAG over Nesterov, but disadvantages EMGD. This should be explicitly pointed out, alongside another practical scenario where *k* < *n*<sup>2/3</sup>. b) The authors should cite [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting. M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012], which also presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm with a linear convergence rate, though the condition number remains in the rate, preventing faster convergence than standard gradient descent. This work still represents a theoretical improvement over that rate when *k* is not excessively large relative to *n*.]

**CLARITY:** The paper is generally clear. Table 1 provides a helpful summary of convergence rates, presented in a concise and easily understandable manner that I haven’t encountered elsewhere. The proof is rigorous and followable, though the inclusion of high-level comments to explain the intuition behind key definitions and their interconnections would enhance readability.

**ORIGINALITY:** This paper presents a novel combination of established techniques.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The practical relevance of the algorithm remains to be demonstrated. However, the theoretical contribution is significant, particularly given the complexity of the SAG convergence proof and the relative simplicity of the EMGD proof, which may facilitate further research and modifications with provable guarantees.
