SUMMARY: The paper tackles optimization of a smooth, strongly convex objective over a convex set where gradient-mapping updates are efficient. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order method attains a linear rate with constants scaling as O(√κ log(1/ε)), its reliance on full gradients can be costly when κ is large. The authors assume access to both an expensive full-gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic-gradient oracle, and introduce a hybrid method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD). EMGD uses only O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations (independent of κ) and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients; when κ is not too large, this can be computationally advantageous. The algorithm partitions optimization into epochs; each epoch computes one full gradient and then performs O(κ^2) “mixed” steps that combine this full gradient with varying stochastic gradients. Averaging the iterates within an epoch yields a constant reduction in suboptimality that does not depend on κ, hence the number of epochs (and full-gradient calls) is κ-independent. The analysis is straightforward and self-contained; however, the paper includes no experiments.

I found the paper enjoyable to read. The idea of combining occasional full gradients with many inexpensive stochastic updates is fresh and appealing. This work connects to a line of recent research that leverages stochastic oracles while preserving linear convergence. A major theoretical and practical advance is SAG [16], applicable when the objective is a finite sum of smooth functions (e.g., regularized ERM with n examples), under the reasonable model that a full gradient costs n times a stochastic one. In that finite-sum setting, SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov when κ ≤ n/8. By comparison, EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but EMGD is asymptotically slower than SAG in all regimes (it matches SAG only when κ ≤ n^(1/2)). For example, if κ = n^(1/2), then both SAG and EMGD are O(n log(1/ε)), versus O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)) for Nesterov. Two key advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) EMGD handles constrained problems (assuming the projected/gradient-mapping step is efficient), whereas SAG is currently formulated for unconstrained settings; and (2) EMGD’s proof is substantially simpler and provides high-probability linear convergence, versus in-expectation guarantees for SAG. The claimed memory/parallelization benefits are less clear-cut, as SAG can employ mini-batching, which both parallelizes and reduces memory.

EVALUATION SUMMARY:
Pros:
- Proposes a novel hybrid strategy with clean, solid analysis.
- Achieves linear convergence for finite-sum problems without an n·κ factor, similar to SAG, while being applicable to constrained settings and offering a simpler proof with high-probability guarantees.
- Clear exposition with a self-contained argument.

Cons:
- No empirical results demonstrating practical gains or concrete case studies showing when the theoretical advantages matter in practice.
- Limited discussion of limitations and trade-offs relative to existing methods (Section 3.4 should be expanded; concrete suggestions below).
- The proof would benefit from higher-level intuition explaining the design choices and how the pieces fit together.

QUALITY: The technical development appears sound. While experiments would strengthen the submission, the theoretical contribution can stand alone. Section 3.4 should be expanded to cover limitations and drawbacks versus competing methods. The finite-sum setting deserves a more concrete comparison with SAG and Nesterov, emphasizing practical parameter regimes: EMGD is worse than Nesterov for approximately κ > n^(2/3); SAG and EMGD have the same order when 1 ≤ κ ≤ n^(1/2); SAG is better than EMGD (and Nesterov) when n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8; and SAG becomes worse than Nesterov for κ > n/8. A significant practical drawback is that EMGD requires fixing the number of inner iterations per epoch in advance, using knowledge of κ. In contrast, SDCA [17] and SAG do not require a priori fixing of the number of steps and often benefit from faster-than-worst-case behavior (or better local conditioning). SDCA also includes automatic step size selection, and SAG has effective adaptive step-size heuristics. Please add this discussion.

[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: (a) In many ML applications, the regularization scales as C/n, yielding κ ≈ n/C′—a regime where SAG still improves over Nesterov but EMGD does not. This should be noted, and it would help to provide another practical setting where κ < n^(2/3). (b) Please cite “Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting” (Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012), which also proposes a hybrid approach with linear convergence. Their rate still depends on κ, so the ultimate rate is not asymptotically better than standard gradient descent (though early iterations are cheaper). The current submission can improve theoretically over that rate when κ is not too large relative to n.]

CLARITY: The paper is generally clear. Table 1 is especially helpful and nicely synthesizes known rates. The proof is traceable but would be more instructive with added high-level intuition motivating the definitions and how they interlock, which presently can feel a bit ad hoc.

ORIGINALITY: The method is a novel synthesis of established ideas.

SIGNIFICANCE: Practical impact remains to be demonstrated, but the theoretical contribution is potentially influential. Given the complexity of SAG’s convergence analysis (and SDCA’s narrower scope), EMGD’s simple proof is a notable contribution.
