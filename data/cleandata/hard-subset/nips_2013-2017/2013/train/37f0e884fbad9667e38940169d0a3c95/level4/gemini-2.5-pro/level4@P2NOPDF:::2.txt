This paper introduces a hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithm for first-order optimization. The proposed method combines calls to a deterministic oracle (for full gradients) with calls to a stochastic oracle (for stochastic gradients). The central contribution is a striking theoretical result: under the assumption that the problem's condition number is known, the algorithm achieves a high-probability convergence rate of O(log(1/epsilon)). This rate is characteristic of deterministic methods but notably eliminates the dependency on the condition number often present in their convergence guarantees.

Despite the novelty of this theoretical finding, the paper has several significant weaknesses that temper its impact.

The most critical issue is the algorithm's reliance on a priori knowledge of the exact condition number, κ (derived from the strong convexity modulus λ and smoothness constant L). The entire theoretical analysis is predicated on this assumption. The paper provides no guidance on how to set this hyper-parameter in practice, nor does it analyze the algorithm's performance under misspecification (i.e., when the estimated κ is too large or too small). While proving convergence in the unknown-κ case may be challenging, the lack of any discussion or empirical investigation into the algorithm's sensitivity to this parameter is a major omission. Essentially, the paper shows how to remove the condition number from the convergence rate, but only by making it a required, perfectly-known input to the algorithm.

The second major weakness is the complete absence of an experimental section. For a paper introducing a new algorithm, empirical validation is crucial. Experiments would serve multiple purposes:
1.  To validate the theoretical convergence rates on real or synthetic data.
2.  To investigate the practical performance of the algorithm, especially its sensitivity to the choice of κ as discussed above. A heuristic for estimating κ, supported by numerical results, would be a valuable addition.
3.  To provide a comparative analysis against standard and state-of-the-art first-order methods, such as SGD, averaged SGD, and more recent variance-reduced methods like SAG.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Problem Formulation:** The paper's problem formulation is ambiguous. It is unclear whether the goal is to solve the finite-sum empirical risk minimization problem (min_w (1/n) Σ F_i(w)), for which randomized algorithms like SAG and SDCA are designed, or the more general stochastic approximation problem (min_w E[F(w)]). Clarifying the target problem is essential for contextualizing the results and would be a straightforward fix.

*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper could be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of related work. The idea of combining deterministic and stochastic steps is not new, and the authors miss an opportunity to compare their "mixed gradient" approach (Eq. 7) to simpler schemes, such as interleaving full gradient steps with stochastic ones. More importantly, the authors fail to cite or discuss a relevant line of work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods, exemplified by Friedlander and Schmidt (SISC, 2012). A comparison of the proposed algorithm and its convergence rates with the methods discussed in that work would be highly valuable.

*   **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is confined to the smooth and strongly convex setting. While this is a common starting point for theoretical analysis, many practical problems are not strongly convex. An extension of the analysis or at least a discussion of the algorithm's potential behavior in the non-strongly convex case (perhaps drawing parallels to the analysis in Bach & Moulines, 2011) would broaden the paper's applicability.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically appealing result but is currently incomplete. The strong assumption of a known condition number and the total lack of empirical validation significantly limit the contribution. Addressing these points, particularly by adding an experimental section that investigates the algorithm's practical behavior and sensitivity to its main hyper-parameter, would substantially improve the paper.
