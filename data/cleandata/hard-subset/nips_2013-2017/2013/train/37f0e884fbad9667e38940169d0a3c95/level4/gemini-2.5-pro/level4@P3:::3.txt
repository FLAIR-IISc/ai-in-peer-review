### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems. The central idea is to combine full (batch) gradient computations with stochastic gradient updates to reduce the dependency on the problem's condition number, κ. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" that combines this stored full gradient with a variance-reduced stochastic term. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients. This result is significant because it makes the number of expensive full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ, contrasting with the O(√κ log 1/ε) complexity of Nesterov's accelerated gradient method. The paper also compares EMGD to other state-of-the-art methods like SAG and SDCA, highlighting its advantages in handling constrained optimization and its lower memory requirements.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Contribution:** The core contribution of the paper is a novel and theoretically elegant algorithm. The concept of using a mixed gradient within epochs to achieve a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradient calls that is independent of the condition number is original. The theoretical result, which bypasses the standard lower bound for first-order deterministic methods by leveraging stochastic information, is a solid and interesting contribution to optimization theory. The proof appears to be sound.

2.  **Broader Applicability:** Compared to related state-of-the-art methods like SAG and SDCA, which also achieve linear convergence, EMGD has some notable advantages. It is directly applicable to constrained optimization problems (provided the projection is efficient), whereas SAG and SDCA are primarily designed for unconstrained objectives. Furthermore, EMGD has a much smaller memory footprint (O(d)) compared to SAG, which requires storing O(n) past gradients.

3.  **Stronger Convergence Guarantee:** The analysis shows that EMGD's linear convergence holds with high probability, which is a stronger guarantee than the in-expectation convergence provided for methods like SAG and SDCA.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Practical Relevance for Machine Learning:** My primary concern is the practical significance of the algorithm's performance gains in typical machine learning scenarios. The paper's analysis shows that EMGD is computationally cheaper than accelerated full gradient descent when κ ≤ n^(2/3). However, in many regularized empirical risk minimization problems, the strong convexity constant λ is determined by an L2 regularizer. For good generalization performance, the regularization parameter is often set on the order of O(1/n) or O(1/√n). Assuming the smoothness constant L is O(1), the condition number κ = L/λ scales as O(n) or O(√n).
    *   If κ = O(n), the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3) is not met for large datasets, meaning Nesterov's method would be superior.
    *   If κ = O(√n), the condition holds, but the overall complexity of EMGD, O((n + κ²)log(1/ε)) = O(n log(1/ε)), is the same as that of SAG and SDCA (which have complexity O((n+κ)log(1/ε))).
    This critical point about the scaling of κ in practical ML settings needs a more thorough discussion. The authors mention this in a footnote, but the claim that the required regime is "reasonable" should be substantiated with concrete examples to demonstrate the algorithm's relevance to the NIPS audience.

2.  **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares EMGD to SAG and SDCA, the computational complexity of EMGD, which is proportional to (n + κ²), appears less favorable than that of SDCA, which is proportional to (n + κ), in most regimes. The paper rightly points out other advantages (constraints, memory), but the discussion could be more balanced by also clearly acknowledging the less favorable iteration complexity and specifying the limited regimes (e.g., κ ≤ n^(1/2)) where it is on par.

3.  **Knowledge of the Condition Number:** A significant practical limitation is that the algorithm requires the condition number κ to be known beforehand to set the number of inner iterations, T. The authors correctly identify this as a drawback for future work, but its impact on the usability of the algorithm should be emphasized more.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   To strengthen the paper, I strongly encourage the authors to expand the discussion in Section 3.4 on the practical settings where the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3) holds. Providing examples of important machine learning problems where this is the case would significantly bolster the paper's claims of significance.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clear. However, the proof in Section 4.2 is quite dense. For instance, the transition from (15) to (17) could benefit from a bit more explanation to improve readability for a broader audience.
*   Could the algorithm be adapted to work with an estimate of κ, or in a parameter-free manner? Even a brief discussion of potential heuristics or adaptive strategies would add value.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with a rigorous high-probability convergence analysis.
*   Achieves a condition-number-independent number of full gradient evaluations, which is a significant and interesting theoretical result in optimization.
*   Offers clear advantages over some competing methods like SAG in terms of memory requirements and applicability to constrained problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical relevance for common machine learning problems is not clearly established. The condition required for EMGD to outperform standard accelerated gradient descent (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) may not hold in many typical settings.
*   The computational complexity appears less favorable than state-of-the-art stochastic methods like SDCA in many parameter regimes.
*   The requirement to know the condition number κ is a practical limitation.

**Conclusion:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting algorithm. However, its practical significance for the machine learning community is debatable and rests on assumptions about the condition number that may not hold in common applications. The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors provided a more compelling case for its relevance in practice.
