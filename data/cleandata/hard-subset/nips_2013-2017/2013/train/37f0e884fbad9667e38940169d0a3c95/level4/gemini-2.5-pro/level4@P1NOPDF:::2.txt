This paper presents a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization. The algorithm combines calls to a deterministic oracle (for full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (for stochastic gradients). The authors show that this approach can achieve a convergence rate that is independent of the problem's condition number.

The main theoretical result is both interesting and striking: under the assumption that a hybrid first-order oracle is available and that the condition number (i.e., the strong convexity and smoothness constants) is known, the proposed algorithm can achieve an O(log(1/epsilon)) convergence rate with high probability for the finite-sum optimization problem.

However, I have several major concerns with the paper in its current form.

First and foremost, the theoretical analysis relies critically on knowing the condition number beforehand. The paper does not address the algorithm's behavior when this crucial hyper-parameter is misspecified. In essence, the main result is that the dependence on the condition number ($\kappa$) can be removed from the convergence rate, but only if $\kappa$ itself is provided as an input to the algorithm. While a theoretical analysis for an unknown or misspecified condition number may be challenging, this is precisely where an empirical investigation would be invaluable.

This leads to the second major concern: the complete absence of an experimental section. For a paper introducing a new algorithm, experiments are essential to validate the theoretical claims and investigate the algorithm's practical performance and robustness. An empirical study could have explored the sensitivity to the condition number hyper-parameter and compared the algorithm against established baselines.

**Detailed Comments**

1.  **The Condition Number Hyper-parameter:** The proposed algorithm (EGD) relies on an update rule (Eq. 8) that uses a "mixed gradient" (Eq. 7). This update requires a hyper-parameter $\eta$, and setting it optimally requires knowledge of the condition number $\kappa$ (referred to as "conditional number" in the paper), which in turn requires knowing both the strong convexity modulus $\lambda$ and the smoothness constant $L$. The paper provides no guidance on how to set this parameter in practice. This presents a dilemma:
    *   If $\kappa$ is assumed to be known, the analysis should explore the algorithm's robustness to misspecification (e.g., the convergence rate when the parameter is set too high or too low).
    *   If $\kappa$ must be estimated, a procedure for doing so is needed—even a heuristic one—supported by numerical experiments.
    The paper currently addresses neither of these points.

2.  **Unclear Problem Setting:** The optimization setting is not clearly stated. The paper aims to combine the benefits of deterministic methods (exponential convergence) and stochastic methods. However, it is unclear whether the algorithm is designed to solve the finite-sum minimization problem [min_w F(w) = 1/n Σ F_i(w)] or the stochastic approximation problem [min_w E(F(w))]. It is worth noting that prominent related methods like SAG and SDCA are randomized algorithms designed for the finite-sum problem. The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is ambiguous in this regard, and this ambiguity is not resolved later. Clarifying the precise problem setting is essential and should be a straightforward fix.

3.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is confined to the smooth and strongly convex setting. While this is a common starting point, this assumption can be restrictive. An analysis of the non-strongly convex case would significantly broaden the paper's applicability, as this setting arises in many practical problems (e.g., see Bach & Moulines, 2011, for a discussion of different convergence behaviors).

4.  **Novelty and Comparison to Related Work:** The idea of blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not new. The paper presents its update as a single "mixed" gradient step rather than as an explicit alternation between full and stochastic gradient steps. It would be valuable to discuss how this formulation relates to, and compares with, a simpler scheme that interleaves the two types of steps.

    Furthermore, the authors do not discuss a relevant line of work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic algorithms, such as [Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting, M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt, SISC, 2012]. A discussion of this line of work and a comparison of convergence rates would be a valuable addition.

5.  **Need for Experiments:** As mentioned, a thorough experimental study is a crucial missing piece. Such a study should include a detailed comparison with standard baselines like SGD and averaged SGD, as well as more recent and relevant proposals for stochastic first-order optimization (e.g., SAG). This would help assess the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.
