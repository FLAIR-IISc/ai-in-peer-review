This paper introduces a hybrid optimization strategy that alternates between stochastic and full (batch) gradients. The authors prove convergence to an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

The convergence analysis appears correct and, to my knowledge, novel, aside from the minor issues listed below.

My primary concern is the practical relevance of the method in standard machine learning settings, which are the focus of this conference. In typical ML formulations, strong convexity arises from the regularizer. Under this modeling, the strong convexity parameter μ scales with the number of samples N (e.g., μ = C N for some C independent of N). Under this assumption, the proposed method is faster than batch gradient descent only when the number of samples satisfies N = O(C^3/L^3). This seems to be a restrictive and not particularly interesting regime for practical ML problems. Moreover, your convergence rate holds only with high probability, whereas the standard batch gradient descent guarantee is deterministic.

This point is crucial for assessing the algorithm’s practical utility. The paper should carefully analyze and discuss the realistic regimes (in terms of N, L, and μ/λ) where the hybrid method provides a clear advantage over batch gradient descent, and justify its relevance for the ML community under commonly used regularization scalings.

Minor comments:
- Equation (6) should be ||w* − ŵ||^2.
- Please specify in line 288 which function you apply equation (7) to.
- The equality on line 286 should be removed; it does not aid comprehension and may hinder it.
- In equation (13), the absolute values should be norms.
- Please explicitly state and justify that L ≥ λ; even if standard, it should be made clear.
- In equation (4), x̂ should be ŵ and f should be F; moreover, by Lemma 1, the first term inside the max is always larger than the second.
- Please define the condition number precisely as a function of λ and L.

Overall assessment: The paper proposes a novel hybrid stochastic/batch gradient method with an interesting and largely correct convergence analysis. However, it is currently unclear whether the algorithm offers a meaningful advantage over standard batch gradient descent for practical ML optimization problems under typical regularization-induced strong convexity. I encourage the authors to address this head-on with a careful, regime-specific comparison.
