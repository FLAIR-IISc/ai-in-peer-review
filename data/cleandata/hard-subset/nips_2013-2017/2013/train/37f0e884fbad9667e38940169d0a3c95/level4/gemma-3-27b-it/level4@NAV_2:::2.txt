The paper presents a novel hybrid deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization. The algorithm strategically mixes calls to a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). The authors demonstrate that this approach allows for the removal of the condition number dependency from the convergence rate. 

The authors present a compelling and significant theoretical result. Specifically, assuming a known condition number (i.e., knowledge of both the strong convexity and smoothness constants of the objective) and the availability of a first-order hybrid oracle, a carefully chosen mixture of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps achieves a high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate for batch optimization. 

However, several concerns warrant consideration. Primarily, the current theoretical analysis is limited to the scenario where the condition number is perfectly known *a priori*. The algorithm’s behavior under hyperparameter misspecification is not addressed. The current result establishes that the condition number dependence can be removed from the convergence rate of a first-order optimization algorithm *when* this condition number is known in advance. Analyzing performance with unknown or inaccurately estimated condition numbers, either theoretically or empirically, would strengthen the work. The absence of an experimental section is a second major concern. Given that the core contribution is a new algorithm, assessing the theoretical results through experiments and evaluating the algorithm's performance on empirical data would be highly beneficial.

Detailed comments:

The proposed algorithm (EMGD) relies on the update rule in Eq. 8 (page 4) utilizing the “mixed gradient” defined in Eq. 7. This requires setting the step size η, which is directly linked to the condition number κ. The paper does not provide guidance or theoretical justification for estimating κ beforehand. Therefore, two possibilities exist: either the parameter must be estimated (requiring an estimation procedure, even a heuristic supported by experiments), or it is assumed known, implying a primarily theoretical focus. If the latter is true, the analysis should address cases with misspecified hyperparameters, examining the convergence rate when κ is overestimated or underestimated.

While the smooth and strongly convex setting is common in theoretical analysis, it can be restrictive. Considering non-strongly convex cases, which also arise in practice, would broaden the applicability. See Bach & Moulines (2011) for a discussion of different behaviors in convex and strongly convex settings.

Blending deterministic and stochastic gradient steps is not novel. The current formulation, however, differs from traditional approaches that interleave these steps with varying frequencies. EMGD employs a single “mixed” update per iteration (within an epoch), followed by a gradient-like update. A discussion comparing EMGD to algorithms that explicitly interleave deterministic and stochastic steps would be valuable, particularly for unconstrained problems.

The paper omits a related body of work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization algorithms; see, for example, Friedlander & Schmidt (2012). Comparing convergence rates to these existing methods would enhance the contribution.

A comprehensive experimental study would significantly strengthen the paper. This should include comparisons with standard SGD, averaged SGD, and recent stochastic first-order optimization methods like SAG, SDCA, etc.

A minor concern is the lack of clarity regarding the optimization setting. The paper aims to combine the advantages of deterministic and stochastic optimization – exponential convergence from deterministic methods and reduced dependence on the condition number from stochastic methods. However, it does not clearly define whether the goal is to solve a deterministic optimization problem with a stochastic algorithm or a stochastic approximation problem. The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 is somewhat ambiguous in this regard, and subsequent claims do not fully resolve this. This could be addressed with a clearer statement of the problem.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel theoretical result with potentially significant implications for optimization in high-dimensional settings. The analysis is rigorous, and the proposed algorithm offers a compelling approach to reduce the computational cost associated with ill-conditioned problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The lack of experimental validation is a major weakness. The analysis is limited to the ideal case of a known condition number, neglecting the practical challenges of hyperparameter tuning. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work and a clearer definition of the optimization setting.
