## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission: Novel Hybrid Stochastic/Batch Gradient Descent

This paper proposes a novel hybrid stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm that leverages both stochastic and full-batch gradient information. The authors demonstrate a convergence guarantee of O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(k^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to achieve an ε-accurate solution. While the convergence proof appears largely correct and presents a potentially novel contribution, my primary concern lies with the practical relevance of this algorithm within the context of machine learning optimization, particularly given the focus of this conference.

The analysis hinges on the assumption of strong convexity with parameter μ. In many machine learning applications, this strong convexity arises from the regularization term, implying μ is often proportional to the number of samples, N (i.e., μ = CN, where C is independent of N). Under this common scenario, the paper's analysis indicates that the proposed method outperforms standard batch gradient descent only when the number of samples, N, is bounded by O(C^3/L^3). This condition represents a restrictive regime that does not seem practically significant for most large-scale machine learning problems. 

Furthermore, the established convergence rate is probabilistic, whereas batch gradient descent offers deterministic convergence. This difference is critical. The benefits of a probabilistic guarantee must clearly outweigh the certainty of batch gradient descent, and the paper currently lacks a compelling argument demonstrating such an advantage. A thorough discussion addressing this point is essential to justify the algorithm’s relevance to the machine learning community. The authors need to convincingly articulate scenarios where this hybrid approach provides a tangible benefit over existing methods, considering the trade-off between deterministic and probabilistic convergence rates.

**Specific Comments and Suggestions:**

* **Equation (6):** The left-hand side should be ||w^* - ŵ||^2, representing the squared distance between the optimal solution and the current estimate.
* **Line 288:** Clarify on which function equation (7) is applied. Providing context is necessary for understanding the application of this equation.
* **Line 286:** Remove the equality following the statement. It does not contribute to comprehension and introduces unnecessary clutter.
* **Equation (13):** Replace absolute values with norms to accurately represent the mathematical operation.
* **Clarification of L and λ:** Explicitly state the relationship L ≥ λ, even if intuitively obvious. This clarifies assumptions and prevents ambiguity.
* **Equation (4):** Replace x̂ with ŵ and f with F for consistency with notation. Also, acknowledge in the text that, based on Lemma 1, the first term within the maximum is always greater than the second. This connection strengthens the argument.
* **Condition Number Definition:** Provide a precise definition of the condition number as a function of λ and L. This is crucial for a complete understanding of the algorithm's performance characteristics.

In summary, while the theoretical contribution of this work is noteworthy, the current presentation fails to establish a convincing case for its practical utility within the mainstream machine learning landscape. Addressing the concerns regarding the sample size limitation and the probabilistic convergence rate is paramount. A more detailed discussion of the algorithm's potential advantages, specifically in relation to standard batch gradient descent and commonly employed regularization techniques, is required. Without such clarification, the relevance of this paper for the NIPS community remains questionable.
