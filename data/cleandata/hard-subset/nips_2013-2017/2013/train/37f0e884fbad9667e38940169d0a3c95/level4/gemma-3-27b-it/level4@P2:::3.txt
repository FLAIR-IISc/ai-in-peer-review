This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel hybrid algorithm combining full and stochastic gradient descent for strongly convex optimization. The key contribution is achieving an iteration complexity of O(log(1/ε)) for full gradients, independent of the condition number κ, while requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The authors provide a theoretical analysis supporting this convergence rate.

The core idea – strategically interleaving full and stochastic gradients – builds upon previous work in epoch gradient descent [9] and stochastic optimization techniques [9, 10, 21, 26]. The paper correctly identifies the trade-off between the faster convergence of full gradient methods and the lower per-iteration cost of stochastic gradients, aiming to leverage the benefits of both. The theoretical results appear sound, and the proof strategy, while detailed, seems logically consistent.

A significant strength of the paper is its clear presentation of the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees. The authors meticulously outline the algorithm’s steps and provide a detailed convergence analysis, including a discussion of relevant lemmas and inequalities. The comparison to existing methods (Nesterov’s algorithm, SAG, SDCA) in Table 2 is helpful for understanding the proposed method’s relative performance.

However, the primary weakness lies in the limited discussion of the algorithm’s practical relevance, particularly within the machine learning context, which is a core focus of NIPS. The authors acknowledge that strong convexity often arises from regularization terms in machine learning, leading to a condition number κ dependent on the number of samples *n*. The subsequent analysis suggesting a limited regime where EMGD outperforms batch gradient descent (κ ≤ n²/3) is concerning. This implies that, for many standard machine learning datasets, the benefits of EMGD may be marginal or non-existent. The deterministic convergence of batch gradient descent compared to the probabilistic guarantee of EMGD further weakens the argument. A more thorough exploration of scenarios where EMGD might truly excel – perhaps with specific data structures or problem formulations – is needed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel hybrid optimization algorithm with a theoretically sound convergence analysis.
*   Addresses a relevant problem – reducing the computational cost of full gradient methods for ill-conditioned problems.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the algorithm and its analysis.
*   Provides a thoughtful comparison to related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of practical relevance, especially in the context of machine learning.
*   The identified regime for improvement over batch gradient descent appears narrow and potentially unrealistic for many common machine learning tasks.
*   Probabilistic convergence guarantee compared to the deterministic guarantee of batch gradient descent.
*   The paper does not sufficiently address how to estimate the condition number κ in practical applications, a requirement for applying the algorithm.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound contribution, but its practical significance is questionable. The authors need to significantly strengthen the discussion of the algorithm’s real-world applicability and address the concerns regarding its performance compared to standard batch gradient descent. As it stands, the paper feels somewhat theoretical and lacks a compelling argument for its adoption within the machine learning community. I would lean towards **reject** pending major revisions that address these concerns.
