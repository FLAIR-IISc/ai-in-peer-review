The authors consider the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where access to both expensive full gradients and cheap stochastic gradients is available. The optimal first-order algorithm of Nesterov achieves linear convergence for such problems, but the constant depends on the square root of the condition number κ. The authors propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires only O(log 1/ε) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of the condition number) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle. Under certain conditions, this could be faster in theory. The core idea is to replace a full gradient step (an epoch) with a fixed number O(κ²) of mixed gradient steps, combining a full gradient (computed once per epoch) and stochastic gradients. By averaging the iterates within an epoch, they demonstrate a constant reduction in suboptimality, independent of the condition number, leading to the desired reduction in full gradient computations. A simple, self-contained proof of the convergence rate is provided, but no experiments are included.

I enjoyed reading this paper and believe the idea of mixing full and stochastic gradient computations is novel and interesting. This work builds on a series of recent papers aiming to leverage cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence. A recent theoretical and practical breakthrough is the SAG algorithm [22], which works for smooth, strongly convex objectives expressed as the sum of *n* simple functions (common in regularized empirical loss minimization, where *n* is the number of training examples).  Assuming the full gradient oracle is *n* times more expensive than the stochastic gradient one, SAG outperforms Nesterov's algorithm when the condition number κ ≤ *n*/8.  EMGD, in contrast, theoretically outperforms Nesterov’s algorithm when κ ≤ *n*<sup>2/3</sup>, but is generally slower than SAG. To illustrate, if κ = *n*<sup>1/2</sup>, both SAG & EMGD are O(*n* log(1/ε)), while Nesterov’s algorithm is O(*n*<sup>5/4</sup> log(1/ε)).  EMGD’s key advantages over SAG are its applicability to constrained optimization (provided the gradient mapping can be computed efficiently) and a significantly simpler proof, potentially offering more insights and easing modifications with provable guarantees. The authors also mention potential memory and parallelization advantages, though these are less clear as SAG can be parallelized using mini-batches, reducing memory requirements.

**EVALUATION SUMMARY:**

**Pros:**

*   Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theoretical foundation.
*   Like SAG, achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization where the condition number *κ* does not multiply the number of training examples *n* in the constant; but the algorithm is more general (works for constrained optimization) and the proof is much simpler.
*   The paper is clearly written, and the proof is self-contained.

**Cons:**

*   Lacks an experimental evaluation, hindering the assessment of practical relevance and providing no concrete examples to illustrate the theoretical advantages.
*   The discussion of limitations and drawbacks relative to existing algorithms (Section 3.4) is insufficient and should be expanded.
*   The proof lacks high-level comments clarifying the essential insights driving its construction.

**QUALITY:** The paper is technically sound. While experiments would be valuable, the theoretical contribution is substantial. The authors should strengthen Section 3.4, discussing the algorithm’s limitations and drawbacks, and provide a more nuanced comparison with SAG and Nesterov, particularly concerning the parameter regimes where each algorithm performs best (e.g., EMGD is worse than Nesterov for κ > *n*<sup>2/3</sup>; SAG is superior to EMGD for 1 ≤ κ ≤ *n*<sup>1/2</sup>). A major drawback is EMGD’s requirement to fix the number of steps within an epoch, relying on prior knowledge of *κ* – SCDA [23] and SAG avoid this constraint, potentially achieving faster practical convergence or adapting to better local condition numbers. Additionally, SCDA offers automatic step-size selection, while SAG incorporates an adaptive step-size heuristic that performs well in practice. These points should be addressed.

**CLARITY:** The paper is generally clear. Table 1 provides a helpful and concise summary of convergence rates, often absent in the literature. The proof is well-structured but would benefit from added high-level comments motivating the key definitions and their interrelationships.

**ORIGINALITY:** The work represents a novel combination of existing techniques.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The practical relevance remains to be demonstrated, but the theoretical contribution holds promise. The simplified proof, particularly in comparison to the complex proof of SAG, is a significant advantage and could facilitate further research and development.
