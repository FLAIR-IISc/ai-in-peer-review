### Summary

The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex set, under the assumption that the projected gradient step is computationally efficient. For this class of problems, Nesterov's optimal first-order algorithm achieves a linear convergence rate, where the constant in the rate depends on the square root of the condition number, κ.

The authors consider a setting where one has access to both an expensive full gradient oracle and a computationally cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. They propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires only O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full gradient oracle—a number independent of the condition number—and O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle. In theory, this approach can be faster than standard methods, provided the condition number is not excessively large.

The core idea of EMGD is to structure the optimization into epochs. Each epoch begins with a single full gradient computation. This full gradient is then used in conjunction with a fixed number, O(κ²), of cheaper stochastic gradient steps to generate a sequence of iterates. By averaging the O(κ²) iterates generated within an epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant factor reduction in suboptimality, independent of the condition number. This result explains why the total number of epochs (and thus full gradient computations) is independent of κ. The paper provides a simple, self-contained proof of this convergence rate but does not include any experimental results.

### Detailed Evaluation

I enjoyed reading this paper. The idea of mixing a few full gradient computations with a large number of cheap stochastic gradient steps is novel and interesting. This work joins a recent line of research aiming to leverage inexpensive stochastic oracles while retaining a linear convergence rate.

**Pros:**
*   The paper presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea supported by a clean, simple, and solid theoretical analysis.
*   The paper is clearly written, and the proof is self-contained. Table 1, in particular, provides a very clear summary of related convergence rates.
*   Like the SAG algorithm [16], EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate for regularized empirical loss minimization where the complexity is not a simple product of the number of examples, n, and the condition number, κ. Furthermore, EMGD is more general than SAG as it applies to constrained optimization problems, and its convergence proof is significantly simpler. This simplicity may yield new insights and make future modifications more amenable to theoretical guarantees. The analysis is also stronger in that it holds with high probability, whereas SAG's guarantee is in expectation.

**Cons:**
*   The paper lacks experiments to demonstrate the algorithm's practical utility or to provide concrete examples where its theoretical advantages are relevant.
*   There is no discussion of the algorithm's limitations or drawbacks, especially in comparison to existing methods. Section 3.4 should be significantly improved in this regard.
*   The proof, while correct, could be improved with high-level comments to motivate the steps and provide insight into its construction.

**Further Analysis and Suggestions for Improvement:**

A recent theoretical and practical breakthrough in this area is the SAG algorithm [16], which applies to smooth, strongly convex objectives that are a sum of n simple functions (a common case in machine learning). Assuming the full gradient is n times more expensive than a stochastic one, a detailed comparison is warranted.

*   **Complexity Comparison:**
    *   Nesterov's method is superior to EMGD when (roughly) κ > n^(2/3).
    *   EMGD is superior to Nesterov's method when κ < n^(2/3).
    *   SAG and EMGD have the same O(n log(1/ε)) complexity when 1 ≤ κ ≤ n^(1/2).
    *   SAG is faster than EMGD (and Nesterov) when n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8.
    The authors should add a concrete discussion of this "sum of n functions" example to highlight these differences.

*   **Practical Drawbacks of EMGD:** A major drawback for practitioners is that EMGD requires the number of steps within an epoch to be fixed in advance, which necessitates knowing the condition number κ. In contrast, algorithms like SCDA [17] and SAG do not require a fixed number of inner steps and can thus benefit from faster-than-expected practical convergence or adapt to local problem structure (e.g., better local condition numbers). Moreover, SCDA features automatic step-size selection, and SAG has an adaptive step-size heuristic that works well in practice. This discussion should be added to the paper.

*   **Common Machine Learning Regimes:** As noted by another reviewer, in many machine learning applications, the regularization parameter is set to C/n, leading to a condition number κ ≈ n/C'. In this regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov's method, whereas EMGD does not. The authors should acknowledge this and perhaps suggest other practical settings where the condition κ < n^(2/3) holds.

*   **Missing Citation:** The authors should cite and discuss "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" (M. Friedlander, M. Schmidt. SISC, 2012), which also presents a hybrid algorithm with a linear convergence rate. While that algorithm's rate still depends on the condition number (making it ultimately no faster than standard gradient descent, though it may converge faster initially), the current submission's theoretical improvement for small κ is a notable distinction.

*   **Clarity of Proof:** While the proof is logically sound, it would benefit from higher-level commentary. Providing intuition for the construction of certain quantities would make the proof less "magical" and more accessible to the reader.

*   **Parallelization:** The claim that SAG and SCDA cannot take advantage of distributed computing is incorrect. Both can be parallelized using mini-batches, which also serves to reduce their memory requirements. This point should be corrected.
