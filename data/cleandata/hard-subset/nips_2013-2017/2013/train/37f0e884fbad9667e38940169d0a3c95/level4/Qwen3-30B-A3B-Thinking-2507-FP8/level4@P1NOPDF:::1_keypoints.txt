- Problem/setup: smooth strongly convex optimization with convex constraints, using an expensive full-gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic-gradient oracle.  
- Main contribution: Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) achieving O(log 1/ε) full-gradient calls (κ-independent) and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic calls.  
- Key idea: replace each full-gradient epoch by O(κ²) mixed stochastic steps using a single epoch-wise full gradient and averaging iterates to get κ-independent epoch count.  
- Theoretical quality: self-contained, rigorous convergence proof with a high-probability guarantee.  
- Novelty: clean hybrid of full and stochastic gradients yielding linear convergence; proof simpler than SAG’s.  
- Comparative regime results: SAG outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n/8; EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3); for κ ≤ n^(1/2) both SAG and EMGD achieve O(n log 1/ε).  
- EMGD vs SAG advantages: handles constrained optimization (given efficient gradient mapping) and has a simpler proof; stronger high-probability guarantees.  
- Questioned claims: memory/parallelization advantage is unclear since SAG supports mini-batching for parallelization and memory reduction.  
- Pros summarized: novel algorithm, solid theory, linear convergence without κ·n factor, extends to constrained settings, clear proof.  
- Cons summarized: no experiments, insufficient discussion of limitations (Section 3.4), and proof lacks high-level motivation for key design choices.  
- Mandatory revision 1: expand Section 3.4 to present regime comparisons between EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov (detailed κ vs n regimes).  
- Mandatory revision 2: discuss practical drawback that EMGD requires fixing epoch size (knowing κ), unlike adaptive methods such as SAG or SCDA.  
- Mandatory revision 3: add citation to Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods.  
- Clarity note: overall well-written with a useful Table 1, but proofs would benefit from high-level explanations (e.g., why averaging is chosen).
