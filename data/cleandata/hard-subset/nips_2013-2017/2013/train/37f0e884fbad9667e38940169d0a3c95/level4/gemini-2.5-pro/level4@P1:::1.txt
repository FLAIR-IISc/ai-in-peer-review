SUMMARY:
The authors address the problem of optimizing a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex set where projected gradient steps are efficient to compute. For such problems, Nesterov's optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence, but the rate depends on the square root of the condition number, κ. This paper considers a setting where one has access to both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheap stochastic gradient oracle. The authors propose a hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires only O(log 1/ε) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of κ) and O(κ² log 1/ε) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle. For problems where the condition number is not excessively large, this trade-off can lead to a theoretical speedup.

The core idea of EMGD is to structure the optimization into "epochs." Each epoch consists of a single full gradient computation followed by a fixed number of "mixed gradient" steps. These steps use a combination of the single full gradient (computed at the start of the epoch) and fresh stochastic gradients. By averaging the iterates within an epoch, the authors demonstrate a constant reduction in the optimization error, which leads to a linear convergence rate where the number of required full gradients (epochs) is independent of the condition number. The paper provides a simple, self-contained proof of this convergence rate but does not include experiments.

EVALUATION:
I enjoyed reading this paper. The idea of mixing a small number of full gradient computations with a large number of cheap stochastic steps is novel and interesting. This work builds upon a line of recent research aiming to achieve linear convergence rates using stochastic oracles. A significant recent breakthrough in this area is the SAG algorithm, which applies to smooth, strongly convex objectives that are a finite sum of n functions (a common structure in regularized empirical risk minimization).

In the finite sum setting, where the full gradient is n times more expensive than a stochastic one, EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov's accelerated method when κ ≤ n²/³, while SAG is faster when κ ≤ n/8. EMGD is generally slower than SAG, though they share the same asymptotic complexity when κ ≤ n¹/². To illustrate, if κ = n¹/², both SAG and EMGD have a complexity of O(n log(1/ε)), whereas Nesterov's method is O(n⁵/⁴ log(1/ε)).

The primary advantages of EMGD over SAG are its generality and simplicity. First, as the authors note, EMGD is applicable to constrained optimization problems (assuming efficient projection), whereas SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems. Second, the convergence proof for EMGD is considerably simpler than that for SAG. This simplicity may yield further insights and make modifications to the algorithm more amenable to analysis. Furthermore, the convergence result for EMGD holds with high probability, which is a stronger guarantee than the in-expectation result for SAG. The authors also suggest a memory and parallelization advantage for EMGD, though this point is less clear, as SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which similarly reduces memory requirements.

**Pros:**
*   Presents a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and solid theoretical foundation.
*   Achieves a linear convergence rate where the number of expensive full gradient computations is independent of the condition number.
*   The algorithm is more general than some competitors (e.g., SAG, SDCA), as it naturally handles constrained optimization.
*   The paper is clearly written, and the convergence proof is self-contained and easy to follow.

**Cons:**
*   The lack of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the algorithm.
*   The paper does not adequately discuss the algorithm's limitations and drawbacks, particularly in comparison to existing methods. Section 3.4 requires significant improvement.
*   The proof, while technically correct, lacks high-level intuition that would help the reader understand the key insights behind its construction.

DETAILED COMMENTS AND SUGGESTIONS:
The paper is technically sound, and the theoretical contribution is valuable. However, to strengthen the paper, the authors should significantly expand their discussion of the algorithm's properties and limitations.

1.  **Expanded Comparison and Limitations:** Section 3.4 should be revised to provide a more nuanced comparison with related work. For instance, the authors should explicitly state the regimes where EMGD is theoretically slower than Nesterov's method (roughly when κ > n²/³ in the finite sum setting).
2.  **Practical Drawbacks:** A major drawback for practitioners is that EMGD requires the number of inner-loop iterations T to be set in advance, which depends on knowing the condition number κ. In contrast, algorithms like SAG and SDCA do not require fixing the number of inner steps and can therefore benefit from faster-than-expected practical convergence or adapt to local problem structure. Moreover, SDCA features automatic step-size selection, and effective adaptive step-size heuristics exist for SAG. This is a crucial practical point that should be discussed.
3.  **A Note on a Common ML Setting:** As another reviewer noted, in many machine learning applications, the regularization parameter is chosen to be proportional to 1/n, leading to a condition number κ that is O(n). In this regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov's method, but EMGD does not. The authors should acknowledge this common scenario and perhaps suggest other settings where the condition κ < n²/³ is more relevant.
4.  **Missing Citation:** The authors should cite and discuss "Hybrid Deterministic-Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting" by M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt (SISC, 2012). This paper also presents a hybrid algorithm with a linear convergence rate. While its rate still depends on the condition number (making the contribution of the current paper a theoretical improvement for moderate κ), it is highly relevant related work.
5.  **Clarity of Proof:** While the proof is easy to follow step-by-step, it would be more insightful if the authors provided some high-level motivation for the definitions of certain quantities (e.g., in eq. 9), which currently seem to fit together somewhat magically.
