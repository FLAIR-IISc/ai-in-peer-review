This paper introduces a novel hybrid optimization strategy that combines stochastic and full-batch gradients for stochastic gradient descent. The proposed algorithm is guaranteed to converge to an epsilon-accurate solution using a combination of O(log(1/eps)) full gradients and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) stochastic gradients.

The theoretical contribution, particularly the convergence proof, appears to be both novel and sound, notwithstanding some minor issues detailed below.

My primary concern, however, regards the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm within the machine learning context that is central to the conference. In many machine learning applications, strong convexity is introduced through a regularization term. The performance comparison between the proposed method and standard batch gradient descent critically depends on how the problem parameters, particularly the strong convexity constant `mu`, scale with the number of samples, `N`.

My analysis, based on the assumption that the value of `mu` is on the order of the number of samples `N` (i.e., `mu = O(N)`), suggests that the proposed hybrid method is computationally superior to standard batch gradient descent only when `N` is bounded by `O(C^3/L^3)` for some constant `C`. This appears to be a very restrictive condition, potentially limiting the algorithm's utility to small-scale problems and not the large-scale settings where stochastic methods are most valuable.

Furthermore, the convergence guarantee for the proposed algorithm is probabilistic (holding with high probability), whereas the guarantee for batch gradient descent is deterministic. This is another factor that should be considered when comparing the two methods.

Given these points, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the practical applicability of the algorithm. I urge the authors to clearly delineate the specific machine learning regimes (in terms of the relationships between `N`, `L`, and `mu`) where this hybrid approach offers a clear advantage over established methods. This discussion is essential to demonstrate the paper's relevance to the machine learning community.

In summary, while the paper presents a theoretically interesting algorithm with a novel analysis, its practical significance is not yet established. The authors need to provide a more compelling argument for the settings in which their method outperforms standard alternatives like batch or fully stochastic gradient descent.
