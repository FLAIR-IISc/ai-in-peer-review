

The paper introduces a "hybrid" deterministic-stochastic method for first-order optimization, combining queries to a deterministic oracle (full gradients) and a stochastic oracle (stochastic gradients). The authors demonstrate that this approach eliminates the dependence on the condition number in the convergence rate.  

The theoretical result is interesting: assuming the condition number κ (i.e., known strong convexity and strong smoothness constants) and access to a first-order hybrid oracle, a suitably mixed sequence of deterministic and stochastic gradient steps achieves a high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate for batch optimization.  

However, two major concerns arise. First, the theoretical analysis assumes perfect knowledge of κ *a priori* and does not address algorithm behavior under κ misspecification (e.g., κ set too large or too small). The current result only shows that κ dependence vanishes when κ is known; the authors should either provide theoretical analysis for unknown κ or support a heuristic estimation procedure with experiments. Second, the paper lacks an experimental section entirely. Given the paper's focus on a novel algorithm, empirical validation of the theoretical claims—comparing performance against SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, etc.—is essential to assess practical relevance.  

Additional concerns:  
- The algorithm (EGD, Eq. 8) requires hyperparameter η, which depends on κ. The paper provides no guidance for setting η or estimating κ, nor does it discuss the impact of η misspecification.  
- The smooth, strongly convex setting is restrictive; the authors should address non-strongly convex cases (e.g., Bach & Moulines, 2011).  
- The algorithm is not framed as interleaving deterministic/stochastic steps but as a "mixed" update per iteration. A comparison with such interleaved approaches (e.g., in the unconstrained case) would strengthen the contribution.  
- The related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012) is omitted, missing an opportunity for convergence rate comparison.  
- The optimization setting is ambiguous: the paper does not clarify whether it targets the deterministic problem (min_w (1/n)∑F_i(w)) or stochastic approximation (min_w E[F(w)]), though both SAG and SDCA solve the former. Section 3.1’s setup is misleading on this point.  

The core theoretical contribution is compelling but requires addressing the κ knowledge assumption and experimental validation to be fully convincing.
