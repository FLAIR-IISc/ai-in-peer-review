This paper presents EMGD, a hybrid first-order optimization method for smooth and strongly convex problems that utilizes both a full (deterministic) gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. The main contribution is a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate. Notably, the number of required full gradient evaluations is O(log 1/ε), which is independent of the problem's condition number κ, while the number of stochastic gradient evaluations is O(κ² log 1/ε). The core idea is a "mixed gradient" update performed within a series of epochs, where a single full gradient computed at the start of each epoch is used to control the variance of many subsequent stochastic steps within that epoch.

The paper's primary strength lies in its interesting and novel theoretical result. The goal of reducing the number of expensive full gradient computations, and specifically making this number independent of the condition number, is a significant objective in large-scale optimization. Achieving a linear convergence rate with only a logarithmic number of full gradient calls is a strong and appealing theoretical contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses that limit its impact.

1.  **Complete Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** For a paper that proposes a new algorithm, the absence of any experimental validation is a critical flaw. An empirical study is necessary to validate the theoretical convergence claims on real or synthetic data, assess the algorithm's practical performance against relevant baselines (e.g., accelerated gradient descent, SGD, SAG, SDCA), and investigate its sensitivity to hyperparameter choices. Without experiments, the practical utility of the proposed algorithm remains entirely speculative.

2.  **Unrealistic Assumption of a Known Condition Number:** The theoretical analysis, and consequently the algorithm's implementation, relies critically on knowing the condition number κ beforehand to set the step size η and the number of iterations per epoch T. This is a strong assumption that rarely holds in practice. The paper offers no guidance on how to set these parameters when κ is unknown, nor does it analyze the algorithm's robustness to a misspecified κ. This is a significant practical limitation. An experimental study would have been the natural place to explore the algorithm's performance under this more realistic scenario.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **Clarity of the Optimization Setting:** The paper is ambiguous about the precise optimization problem it aims to solve. The theoretical setup in Section 3.1 (F(w) = E[f(w)]) corresponds to the general stochastic approximation problem. In contrast, the comparisons to SAG and SDCA in Section 3.4 are only meaningful in the finite-sum setting (F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w)). The authors should explicitly define their target problem and ensure that the theoretical framework and comparisons are consistent.

*   **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper needs a more thorough discussion of related hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization methods. While Friedlander and Schmidt (2012) is cited, the comparison is too brief. A deeper engagement with this line of work, including a comparison of algorithmic strategies, assumptions, and convergence guarantees, is needed. Furthermore, it would be valuable to discuss how EMGD's "mixed gradient" formulation relates to simpler hybrid schemes that explicitly interleave full gradient steps and stochastic gradient steps.

*   **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the strongly convex case. While this is a common starting point, the non-strongly convex case is also of great practical importance. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the challenges or potential avenues for extending this approach to the merely convex setting.

In its current form, the paper presents a theoretically appealing idea but falls short of being a complete contribution. The lack of experiments and the strong, unaddressed assumption of a known condition number are major shortcomings that prevent a recommendation for acceptance. The paper could be substantially improved by providing a thorough empirical evaluation to demonstrate the practical viability and competitiveness of the proposed algorithm.
