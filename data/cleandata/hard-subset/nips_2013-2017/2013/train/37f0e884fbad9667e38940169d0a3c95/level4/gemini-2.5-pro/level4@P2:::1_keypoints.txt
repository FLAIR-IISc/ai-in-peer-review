- Paper proposes EMGD, an epoch-based hybrid algorithm that uses one full gradient per epoch plus O(κ²) mixed (full+stochastic) steps to get linear convergence.  
- Main theoretical claim: only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients are required.  
- Core idea: compute a full gradient at epoch start, perform mixed-gradient iterations combining that full gradient with fresh stochastic gradients, and average iterates to reduce error.  
- Theoretical analysis is self-contained, clean, and yields high-probability convergence bounds.  
- Novelty: a creative "mixed gradient" update combining epoch schemes and variance reduction to obtain a new theoretical result.  
- Strength: more general than some competitors (e.g., can handle constrained problems if projection is efficient).  
- Strength: proof simplicity and clarity may aid future modifications and understanding compared to more complex analyses like SAG.  
- Strength: convergence bound holds with high probability, stronger than in-expectation results for SAG/SDCA.  
- Major downside: no empirical experiments, so practical effectiveness and constants are unverified.  
- Major downside: insufficient discussion of limitations; Section 3.4 needs expansion and balanced comparison to existing methods.  
- Practical drawback: EMGD requires knowledge of κ to set inner-epoch iterations, unlike SAG/SDCA which adaptively benefit from local properties.  
- Comparative regime notes: EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3); SAG outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n/8; EMGD generally has worse κ-dependence than SAG/SDCA except when κ ≤ n^(1/2).  
- Important ML regime concern: with typical regularization κ ≈ O(n), EMGD gives no theoretical improvement over Nesterov, whereas SAG can improve — this should be acknowledged.  
- Missing prior art: should cite and discuss Friedlander & Schmidt (SISC 2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods with linear rates.  
- Proof feedback: technically clear but would benefit from more high-level intuition explaining key steps and definitions.  
- Specific clarity edits requested: rephrase line 50, note SDCA’s more restricted form (118–119), clarify F(w)=E[f(w)] and ∇F(w)=E[∇f(w)], correct the claim about parallelization (line 224), and unify domain notation (W vs. D).  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and potentially significant theoretical contribution, but needs experiments and a more thorough, nuanced comparison and discussion of trade-offs to establish practical relevance.
