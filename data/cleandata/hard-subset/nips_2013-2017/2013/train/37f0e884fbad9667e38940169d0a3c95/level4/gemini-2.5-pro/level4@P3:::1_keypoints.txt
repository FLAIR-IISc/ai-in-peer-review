- Paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm using occasional full gradients and many stochastic gradients to optimize smooth, strongly convex functions.  
- Main theoretical result: EMGD achieves ε-optimality with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic calls.  
- Algorithmic idea: compute one full gradient per epoch and run O(κ²) inner mixed-gradient stochastic refinements to get constant error reduction per epoch.  
- Strength: Novel and elegant hybrid approach that removes condition-number dependence for expensive full-gradient oracle.  
- Strength: Simple, self-contained convergence proof that yields high-probability (not just in-expectation) linear convergence.  
- Strength: More general than SAG/SDCA by naturally handling constrained problems with projections.  
- Weakness: No empirical experiments to demonstrate practical viability or show when theory translates to performance gains.  
- Weakness: Insufficient discussion of practical drawbacks, dependencies, and comparisons with adaptive methods.  
- Weakness: Proof lacks high-level intuition and motivation for key definitions and steps.  
- Suggestion: Expand related-work comparison—clarify regimes where EMGD outperforms Nesterov (κ ≲ n^(2/3)) and note EMGD is slower than SAG in most regimes.  
- Suggestion: Discuss realistic ML setting (λ ~ 1/n ⇒ κ ~ n) where SAG still outperforms EMGD, and identify practical regimes where κ < n^(2/3).  
- Suggestion: Cite and discuss Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods as relevant prior work.  
- Practicality concern: Inner iteration count T depends on unknown κ, unlike SAG/SDCA which are more adaptive and can use stopping criteria or adaptive stepsizes.  
- Practicality concern: Fixed inner-step schedule prevents exploiting faster empirical/local convergence and adaptive termination criteria.  
- Minor fixes: Multiple wording, consistency, and typographical corrections (e.g., oracle definitions, notation W vs D, norms, logs, typos, summation parentheses).  
- Correction needed: Claim that SAG/SDCA cannot use distributed computing is inaccurate—both can be parallelized (mini-batches) and this should be fixed.  
- Conclusion: Strong, novel theoretical contribution that needs empirical validation and a more thorough discussion of limitations and related work to strengthen its practical relevance.
