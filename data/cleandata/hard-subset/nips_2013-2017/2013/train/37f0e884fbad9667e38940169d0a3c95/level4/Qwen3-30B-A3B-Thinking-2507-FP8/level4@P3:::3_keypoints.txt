- Proposes EMGD: combines full and stochastic gradients to get linear convergence for smooth, strongly convex problems.
- Claims complexity O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, improving over standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) full-gradient complexity.
- Builds on SAG/SDCA literature but introduces a distinct mixed-gradient mechanism giving condition-number-independent full gradient access.

- Theoretical analysis is technically sound with a well-structured convergence proof and appropriate martingale arguments.
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; algorithm and notation are precise and consistent.
- Novelty: the mixed gradient approach is a new combination of techniques distinct from existing epoch-based methods.
- Significance: reduction in full-gradient evaluations and applicability to constrained optimization are potential advantages over SAG/SDCA.

- Practical relevance is limited: with typical ML regularization (λ = Ω(1/n)) giving κ = O(n), EMGD’s advantage only holds when κ ≤ n^(2/3), an unrealistic regime for large n.
- High-probability convergence guarantee is weaker than deterministic batch-GD guarantees and is not justified as preferable for practice.
- The connection between condition number and practical computational advantage is insufficiently explained; the κ ≤ n^(2/3) claim lacks justification.
- Complexity comparisons indicate EMGD is often outperformed by SAG/SDCA in practical settings.
- Lacks empirical evidence demonstrating practical benefits on realistic machine learning problems.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel method, rigorous linear-convergence proof, applicability to constrained problems, potential gains in specific settings.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical advantage for standard ML, weaker probabilistic guarantees, insufficient justification and empirical support.

- Recommendation: reject unless authors provide stronger empirical evidence, clearer justification of the relevant regime, and a better argument for the sufficiency of high-probability guarantees.
