

The paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that reduces the number of full gradient evaluations needed from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by combining full and stochastic gradients. This addresses a key computational bottleneck in ill-conditioned problems where full gradient evaluations are expensive. The authors theoretically analyze EMGD, showing it achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients for ε-optimality. They compare EMGD to existing methods, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA, and demonstrate its advantages in constrained optimization, storage requirements, and convergence guarantees (high probability vs. expectation).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and addresses a genuine computational challenge in optimization. The proof techniques, particularly the mixed gradient approach and induction-based convergence analysis, demonstrate technical rigor. The paper properly distinguishes EMGD from related work, especially in its handling of constrained optimization and storage requirements.

2. **Originality:** The core idea of combining full and stochastic gradients in a structured epoch-based manner represents a meaningful contribution to first-order optimization methods. While similar to semi-stochastic approaches, EMGD's specific design and theoretical guarantees for the strongly convex case are novel. The paper appropriately references relevant NIPS work (e.g., [9, 10, 21, 22, 23]) and clarifies how EMGD differs from these approaches.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear technical exposition. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise, and the convergence proof is logically structured. The comparison table (Table 2) effectively summarizes computational trade-offs.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Significance for Machine Learning:** The most critical weakness concerns the practical relevance to machine learning, which is the focus of NIPS. In typical ML problems, strong convexity arises from regularization (e.g., λ = τ = Ω(1/N) for L2-regularized problems), making the condition number κ = O(N). Under this standard ML assumption, the paper's claim that EMGD outperforms batch gradient descent requires κ ≤ O(n²/³), which translates to N = O(1) - a regime with no practical relevance to real-world ML problems (which involve large N). The paper fails to demonstrate a meaningful advantage in practical ML settings where N is large.

2. **Convergence Guarantee:** While EMGD achieves linear convergence with high probability, standard batch methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent provide deterministic convergence guarantees. The paper should clarify whether this probabilistic guarantee is a significant drawback in practice, especially for applications requiring strict performance guarantees.

3. **Technical Details:** Several minor but important issues require clarification:
   - The condition number definition (κ = L/λ) should be explicitly stated early in the paper.
   - In equation (6), the norm should be ||w* - w̄||², not ||w* - ŵ||².
   - The reference to "Lemma 1" in line 288 should specify which function (F or f) it applies to.
   - The equality in line 286 adds no value and should be removed.
   - Equation (13) should use norms instead of absolute values.
   - In equation (4), the notation should consistently use w* and F (not x* and f).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The algorithm is technically sound and represents a novel approach to optimization.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified.
- The paper correctly identifies a genuine computational challenge in optimization.
- EMGD has advantages in constrained optimization and storage requirements compared to SAG/SDCA.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The claimed advantage over batch gradient descent is only meaningful for trivial problem sizes (N = O(1)), which is irrelevant to practical machine learning.
- The high-probability convergence guarantee may be less desirable than deterministic guarantees for many applications.
- The paper fails to establish significance for the NIPS community by not demonstrating practical relevance to typical ML problems.

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a technically interesting contribution to optimization theory but fails to demonstrate meaningful significance for machine learning applications, which is the primary focus of NIPS. The theoretical novelty is valuable, but without a compelling argument for practical relevance in the ML context, the paper's contribution to the field is limited. The authors should substantially revise the paper to address the ML relevance issue, either by:
1) Showing that EMGD can be effectively applied to practical ML problems with realistic condition numbers, or
2) Clearly acknowledging the limitations and discussing when EMGD might be useful in specific ML contexts.

Without such revision, the paper's significance for NIPS is questionable. I recommend rejection with the understanding that a revised version addressing the ML relevance concern could be reconsidered. The technical quality is good, but significance for the ML community is paramount for NIPS.
