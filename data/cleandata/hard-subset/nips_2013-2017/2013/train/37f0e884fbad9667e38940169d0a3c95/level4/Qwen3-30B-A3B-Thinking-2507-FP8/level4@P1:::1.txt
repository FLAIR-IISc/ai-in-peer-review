SUMMARY: The authors address the problem of optimizing smooth, strongly convex functions over convex constraint sets, where gradient mapping updates can be computed efficiently. Nesterov's optimal first-order algorithm achieves linear convergence but with a constant depending on √κ, where κ is the condition number. The authors consider a setting where both expensive full gradients and cheap stochastic gradients are available. They propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which requires O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full gradient oracle (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic gradient oracle—provided κ is not too large. The key insight is replacing each full gradient step (epoch) with a fixed number of O(κ²) mixed gradient steps, which combine a single full gradient (computed per epoch) with stochastic gradients. By averaging the O(κ²) iterates within each epoch, they achieve a constant reduction in suboptimality independent of κ, resulting in a condition-number-independent number of epochs. The authors provide a clear, self-contained proof of convergence but include no experiments.

I found this paper engaging, as the idea of mixing a few full gradient computations with many cheap stochastic steps is novel and interesting. The work fits within a recent line of papers aiming to leverage cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence rates. A notable recent breakthrough was SAG [22], which works for smooth, strongly convex objectives that are sums of n simple functions (common in regularized empirical loss minimization). Assuming the full gradient oracle is n times more expensive than the stochastic one, SAG outperforms Nesterov's algorithm when κ ≤ n/8. In contrast, EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3), and is comparable to SAG when κ ≤ n^(1/2). For example, when κ = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD scale as O(n log(1/ε)), while Nesterov scales as O(n^(5/4) log(1/ε)).

The main advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) EMGD handles constrained optimization (assuming gradient mapping updates are efficiently computable), whereas SAG is currently restricted to unconstrained problems; and (2) EMGD's convergence proof is significantly simpler than SAG's, potentially yielding more insights and facilitating modifications with provable guarantees. The authors' claim about memory/parallelization advantages is less clear, as SAG can also be parallelized using mini-batches, which reduces memory requirements.

EVALUATION SUMMARY:

PROS:
- Novel, interesting algorithmic idea with clean, simple, and solid theory
- Achieves linear convergence for regularized empirical loss minimization with condition number not multiplying the number of training examples, while being more general (works for constrained optimization) and having a simpler proof than SAG
- Well-written paper with a self-contained proof

CONS:
- No experiments to demonstrate practical relevance or illustrate theoretical advantages
- Limited discussion of algorithm limitations (section 3.4 needs significant improvement)
- Proof lacks high-level commentary to justify key insights

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. While experiments would have been valuable, the theoretical contribution stands on its own. The authors should expand section 3.4 to address limitations, including a detailed comparison with existing algorithms. Specifically, they should clarify when EMGD is better than Nesterov (κ ≤ n^(2/3)), when it's comparable to SAG (κ ≤ n^(1/2)), and when it's worse (κ > n^(2/3)). A major practical drawback is that the number of steps per epoch must be fixed in advance (requiring knowledge of κ), unlike SAG and SDCA, which can adaptively adjust steps and benefit from better local condition numbers.

CLARITY: The paper is clear, with a helpful Table 1 summarizing key convergence rates. The proof is self-contained and follows logically, though it would benefit from high-level commentary explaining why certain quantities were chosen.

ORIGINALITY: This represents a novel combination of known techniques, offering a new perspective on hybrid optimization.

SIGNIFICANCE: The theoretical contribution is significant, particularly given the complexity of SAG's convergence proof. However, the practical relevance remains unproven without experiments. The simple proof of EMGD is a major contribution in the context of SAG's difficult proof and SDCA's restricted applicability.
