SUMMARY: The authors study optimization of a smooth, strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, under the assumption that the gradient mapping update can be computed efficiently. Nesterov’s optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence for this setting, but with a constant that scales with the square root of the condition number k. The paper considers a regime where one can compute an expensive full gradient of the objective and also access a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. The authors propose a hybrid method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that uses O(log(1/eps)) calls to the full-gradient oracle (independent of the condition number) and O(k^2 log(1/eps)) calls to the cheaper stochastic-gradient oracle—potentially faster in theory when k is not too large. The core idea is to replace each full-gradient step (an “epoch”) with a fixed number O(k^2) of mixed-gradient steps that combine the single full gradient computed at the start of the epoch with per-iteration stochastic gradients. Averaging the O(k^2) iterates in each epoch yields a constant reduction in suboptimality independent of k, which explains why the number of epochs (full-gradient computations) does not depend on k. The paper presents a simple, self-contained convergence proof, but provides no experiments.

I enjoyed reading the paper. Mixing a few full-gradient computations with many cheap stochastic-gradient steps is a novel and interesting idea. This work aligns with recent efforts to leverage stochastic oracles while preserving linear convergence. A recent theoretical and practical advance is the SAG algorithm [16], designed for smooth, strongly convex objectives that are sums of n simple functions (e.g., regularized empirical risk minimization, where n is the number of training examples). In that setting, it is reasonable to assume a full gradient costs n times a stochastic gradient. Then SAG is theoretically faster than Nesterov’s method when k ≤ n/8. In contrast, EMGD is theoretically faster than Nesterov’s method when k ≤ n^(2/3), but EMGD is slower than SAG in all regimes (it only matches SAG’s big-O when k ≤ n^(1/2)). For intuition, if k = n^(1/2), both SAG and EMGD have complexity O(n log(1/eps)), whereas Nesterov’s method has O(n^(5/4) log(1/eps)). Two key advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) as the authors note, EMGD applies to constrained optimization (assuming the gradient mapping can be computed efficiently), whereas SAG is currently defined only for unconstrained problems; and (2) EMGD’s convergence proof is much simpler than SAG’s and could yield additional insights and make variants more amenable to provable guarantees; their result is also stronger in that it holds with high probability rather than in expectation as in SAG. The authors also suggest potential memory/parallelization benefits, though this is less clear because SAG can be parallelized via mini-batches, which also reduces memory.

EVALUATION SUMMARY:
Pros:
- Proposes a novel and interesting algorithmic idea with clean, simple, and solid theory.
- Like SAG, attains a linear rate for regularized empirical loss minimization without multiplying the constant by n through the condition number k; EMGD is also more general (it handles constrained problems), and the proof is substantially simpler.
- The paper is clearly written and the analysis is self-contained.

Cons:
- No experiments are provided to demonstrate practical impact or to give concrete examples where the theoretical advantages matter in practice.
- Limited discussion of the algorithm’s drawbacks and limitations, especially relative to existing methods—Section 3.4 should be expanded (see suggestions below).
- The proof would benefit from more high-level commentary explaining the key insights that guided its construction.

QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. Although experiments would strengthen the submission, the theoretical contribution can stand on its own. The authors should expand Section 3.4 to discuss the method’s limitations and drawbacks. They should also provide a more concrete discussion of the “sum of n functions” setting to highlight differences among EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov’s method, as noted above—for example: EMGD is worse than Nesterov for (roughly) k > n^(2/3); SAG matches EMGD for 1 ≤ k ≤ n^(1/2); SAG is better than both EMGD and Nesterov for n^(1/2) < k ≤ n/8; and SAG is worse than Nesterov when k > n/8. A notable practical drawback of EMGD is that the number of inner steps per epoch must be fixed in advance (requiring knowledge of k). In contrast, both SDCA [17] and SAG do not fix the number of steps a priori and can thus benefit from faster practical convergence than worst-case bounds might suggest (or from better local conditioning). Moreover, SDCA has automatic step-size selection, and SAG uses an adaptive step-size heuristic that appears to work well in practice. This discussion should be added.

[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: a) As another reviewer noted, in ML practice the regularization parameter is often C/n, so the condition number is ≈ n/C′. In this regime, SAG still outperforms Nesterov, but EMGD does not; this should be made explicit (and perhaps another practical scenario with k < n^(2/3) should be identified). b) The authors should cite: M. Friedlander and M. Schmidt, “Hybrid Deterministic–Stochastic Methods for Data Fitting,” SISC, 2012, which also proposes a hybrid deterministic–stochastic algorithm with a linear rate. That method still has the condition number in the rate, so its ultimate rate is not better than standard gradient descent (it only accelerates early progress via cheaper steps). The present submission can still improve theoretically over that rate when k is not too large relative to n.]

CLARITY: The paper is fairly clear. I appreciated the summary in Table 1; I have not seen this laid out so clearly elsewhere. The proof is tight and followable, but would be more helpful with a few high-level remarks motivating some of the definitions, which currently fit together a bit too “magically.”

ORIGINALITY: A novel combination of known techniques.

SIGNIFICANCE: Practical relevance is not yet demonstrated, but the theoretical contribution could be impactful. Given the difficulty of SAG’s convergence proof (and the simplicity of SDCA’s proof, albeit for a narrower setting), EMGD’s simple proof is a significant contribution.
