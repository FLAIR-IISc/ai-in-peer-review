**SUMMARY**  
This paper addresses smooth strongly convex optimization over convex sets, where the gradient mapping update is efficient. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence with a rate dependent on √κ (κ = condition number), the authors propose a hybrid algorithm (Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent, EMGD) leveraging both an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic oracle. EMGD requires only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic calls, offering potential speedups when κ is not too large. The core idea replaces each full gradient step (epoch) with O(κ²) mixed steps using a fixed full gradient and varying stochastic gradients. Averaging these iterates within an epoch yields a suboptimality reduction independent of κ, enabling the epoch count to be κ-independent. The authors provide a clear, self-contained convergence proof but omit experiments.  

**EVALUATION**  
*Strengths*:  
- The hybrid approach (mixing full and stochastic steps) is novel and well-motivated.  
- EMGD achieves linear convergence for constrained problems (unlike SAG [16], which is limited to unconstrained settings), with a significantly simpler proof than SAG (which requires complex analysis for high-probability guarantees).  
- Theoretical comparison to SAG is insightful: For regularized empirical loss minimization (F = (1/n)∑fᵢ, where fᵢ are simple), EMGD outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n²/³ (vs. SAG’s κ ≤ n/8). When κ ≤ √n, both EMGD and SAG match Nesterov’s O(n log(1/ε)) rate, but for √n < κ ≤ n/8, SAG dominates EMGD. Crucially, EMGD’s constrained optimization capability and simpler proof are major advantages.  

*Weaknesses*:  
- **No experiments** to validate practical relevance or demonstrate speedups (e.g., in settings where κ ≤ n²/³).  
- **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: Section 3.4 requires expansion to clarify trade-offs versus SAG/SCDA [17]. Key drawbacks include:  
  (i) Fixed epoch length (requiring prior knowledge of κ), unlike adaptive methods (SCDA, SAG) that adjust steps dynamically;  
  (ii) EMGD is inferior to SAG for κ ≤ n/8 (a common regime in ML, e.g., when regularization is C/n, κ ≈ n/C).  
- **Proof lacks high-level intuition** for key constructions (e.g., the role of averaged iterates in eliminating κ-dependence).  
- **Overstated claims**: The assertion that SAG cannot leverage distributed computing is incorrect (mini-batching enables parallelization and reduces memory).  

*Recommendations*:  
1. **Add a concrete example** contrasting EMGD, SAG, and Nesterov for F = (1/n)∑fᵢ, explicitly stating:  
   - EMGD < Nesterov when κ > n²/³,  
   - EMGD ≈ SAG when 1 ≤ κ ≤ √n,  
   - SAG < EMGD when √n < κ ≤ n/8,  
   - Nesterov < SAG when κ > n/8.  
2. **Correct minor issues**:  
   - Fix notation inconsistencies (e.g., use Ω consistently instead of mixing D/Ω).  
   - Clarify line 132: "for all w" (not just a fixed w).  
   - Add "E[∇f(w)] = ∇F(w)" (line 139).  
   - Address line 224 (SAG’s parallelization via mini-batches).  
   - Correct typos ("an convex" → "a convex", inconsistent log/ln).  
3. **Cite Friedlander & Schmidt (2012)** for a related hybrid method (though their rate still depends on κ, unlike EMGD’s κ-independent full gradient cost).  

**QUALITY & SIGNIFICANCE**  
The technical contribution is sound and the theory is robust. While experiments would strengthen the case, the theoretical novelty (constrained optimization + simpler proof) and clear comparison to SAG/SCDA justify the contribution. The significance lies in demonstrating a theoretically faster alternative to Nesterov for constrained problems under mild κ assumptions, with a proof that may inspire further work. The main gap remains the lack of practical validation.  

**CLARITY**  
The paper is well-structured and readable, with Table 1 providing an excellent summary of convergence rates. The proof is self-contained but would benefit from brief high-level remarks explaining *why* key steps (e.g., averaging within epochs) eliminate κ-dependence.  

**ORIGINALITY**  
The combination of techniques (fixed-epoch mixed gradients) is novel, though it builds on recent hybrid methods.  

**FINAL ASSESSMENT**  
This is a strong theoretical contribution with clear advantages over prior work for constrained optimization. The authors must address the limitations discussion (Section 3.4), clarify the practical regime where EMGD excels (κ ≤ n²/³), and correct minor technical points. Without experiments, the practical impact remains unproven, but the theoretical framework is compelling. The revised paper would be suitable for acceptance pending these updates.
