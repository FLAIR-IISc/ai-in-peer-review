SUMMARY: The authors address optimizing a smooth strongly convex function over a convex constraint set, where the gradient mapping update is efficiently computable. While Nesterov’s optimal first-order method achieves linear convergence for this setting, its constant depends on the square root of the condition number κ. The authors consider a scenario with access to an expensive full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. They propose a hybrid algorithm (Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent, EMGD) requiring O(log 1/ε) full gradient oracle calls (independent of κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient oracle calls, offering theoretical speedup when κ is moderate. The core idea replaces a full gradient step (an epoch) with O(κ²) mixed gradient steps combining a single full gradient (computed once per epoch) and varying stochastic gradients. Averaging the O(κ²) iterates within an epoch yields a suboptimality decrease independent of κ, enabling epoch count independence from κ. The convergence proof is self-contained and complete, though no experiments are provided.  
I found the paper engaging; the hybrid approach of integrating limited full gradient computations with numerous stochastic steps is novel and compelling. This work aligns with recent efforts to leverage cheaper stochastic oracles while maintaining linear convergence. A significant theoretical and practical advance was SAG [16], effective for smooth strongly convex objectives as sums of n simple functions (e.g., regularized empirical loss minimization), where full gradient cost is n times stochastic gradient cost. SAG outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n/8, whereas EMGD does so when κ ≤ n^(2/3), and EMGD is slower than SAG for all regimes (same big-O for κ ≤ n^(1/2)). Key advantages of EMGD over SAG are: (1) applicability to constrained optimization (assuming efficient gradient mapping updates, unlike SAG’s unconstrained focus), and (2) a significantly simpler convergence proof (yielding stronger high-probability guarantees vs. SAG’s expectation-based proof). The potential memory/parallelization benefit is less clear, as SAG also supports mini-batch parallelization.  
EVALUATION SUMMARY:  
Pros:  
- Novel, interesting algorithmic idea with a clean, simple, and rigorous theoretical foundation.  
- Achieves linear convergence for regularized empirical loss minimization without κ scaling with n (unlike Nesterov), while being more general (constrained optimization) and having a simpler proof than SAG.  
- Well-written paper with a self-contained proof.  
Cons:  
- Absence of experiments to demonstrate practical relevance or illustrate theoretical advantages.  
- Insufficient discussion of limitations/drawbacks (Section 3.4 requires substantial improvement).  
- Proof lacks high-level motivation for key insights.  
QUALITY: The paper is technically sound. While experiments would strengthen it, the theoretical contribution is robust. The authors must expand Section 3.4 to address algorithm limitations (e.g., fixed epoch size requiring κ knowledge, unlike SCDA/SAG’s adaptive steps) and clarify the sum-of-functions example (EMGD vs. SAG/Nesterov: EMGD worse than Nesterov for κ > n^(2/3); equivalent to SAG for 1 ≤ κ ≤ n^(1/2); better than SAG for n^(1/2) < κ ≤ n/8; worse than Nesterov for κ > n/8). A major practical drawback is EMGD’s fixed epoch size (κ-dependent), whereas SCDA/SAG adapt dynamically. SCDA also offers automatic step-size selection.  
[ADDENDUM after discussion with other reviewers: a) In typical ML settings (e.g., regularization parameter C/n, κ ≈ n/C'), SAG remains superior to Nesterov, while EMGD does not—this should be noted. b) Cite [Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012] for a related hybrid deterministic-stochastic method (though their rate still depends on κ, unlike EMGD).]  
CLARITY: The paper is clear, with Table 1 providing an excellent summary of convergence rates. The proof is followable but would benefit from high-level motivation for key definitions.  
ORIGINALITY: The algorithm combines known techniques in a novel manner.  
SIGNIFICANCE: Practical relevance is unverified, but the theoretical contribution is meaningful. The simple EMGD proof is a major advance over SAG’s complex proof (and comparable to SDCA’s simpler proof, though SDCA is restricted to specific setups).
