This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid first-order method that combines a single full gradient per epoch with many stochastic gradients via a control-variate style “mixed” update g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄). For smooth, strongly convex objectives, the analysis claims a high-probability linear convergence rate using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ·log(1/ε)) (abstract) or O(κ^2·log(1/ε)) (Theorem 1) stochastic gradients. The method sits between deterministic accelerated methods and stochastic variance-reduction approaches, and is related to earlier hybrid deterministic–stochastic schemes (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) and finite-sum linear-rate methods such as SAG and SDCA.

Strengths
- Clear goal: reduce full-gradient calls to be κ-independent while retaining linear convergence.
- High-probability guarantee and a simple, memory-light update that can handle constraints.
- The epoch structure is conceptually clean and easy to implement.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on a priori knowledge of κ (L and λ). Both η and the epoch length T depend on κ, with no estimation strategy or robustness analysis. The method’s practical value hinges on this.
- No experiments. There is no validation of the theory, no study of misspecification (η too large/small), and no comparisons against standard baselines (SGD, averaged SGD, SAG/SVRG/SAGA, SDCA).
- Ambiguity in problem setting: the paper mixes stochastic approximation and finite-sum contexts, and Section 3.1’s oracle model is misleading without clarifying which setting the bounds target.
- Related work is incomplete; prior hybrid methods (Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) and modern variance-reduction literature deserve careful positioning.
- The abstract states O(κ·log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, but Theorem 1 and T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) imply O(κ^2·log(1/ε)), a notable discrepancy.
- The “mixed” update is not contrasted with simple interleaving of deterministic and stochastic steps; an ablation would help.
- Only strongly convex case is treated; no discussion of plain convex extensions.

Quality: Mathematically promising but incomplete; key assumptions (known κ) are impractical, and there is no empirical support. Some statements (e.g., characterization of strong convexity) are imprecise.

Clarity: Generally readable, but the oracle model, setting, and κ-dependence need clearer exposition; fix the O(κ) vs O(κ^2) inconsistency.

Originality: Moderately novel combination; relates to existing hybrid/variance-reduction ideas but lacks a thorough differentiation.

Significance: Potentially useful if κ-robust and competitive empirically; currently uncertain.

Suggestions: Add adaptive/parameter-free variants (doubling/backtracking; online L, λ estimation), robustness analysis under misspecified κ, and comprehensive experiments on finite-sum problems versus SGD, averaged SGD, SAG/SVRG/SAGA, SDCA, and a simple interleaving baseline; explore non-strongly convex extensions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clean idea with high-probability linear convergence and reduced full-gradient reliance.
- Broad applicability to constrained problems with low memory.

Arguments against acceptance
- No experiments; unclear practical impact.
- Dependence on known κ with no remedy.
- Incomplete related work and unclear setting; inconsistency in complexity claims.

Overall: Lean reject in current form; could become competitive with adaptivity and solid empirical validation.
