## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes a novel hybrid deterministic-stochastic first-order method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to achieve linear convergence for strongly convex optimization problems without dependence on the condition number κ in the number of full gradient evaluations. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient steps within epochs, leveraging the benefits of both. The authors claim to achieve O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations for finding an ǫ-optimal solution.

The work builds on the existing landscape of first-order methods, acknowledging both deterministic (e.g., Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) and stochastic approaches (e.g., SAG, SDCA). It positions itself within the line of research that attempts to balance the efficiency of stochastic gradients with the faster convergence of full gradients, referencing semi-stochastic methods. However, the paper fails to adequately situate itself within this context, specifically missing a crucial comparison to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), which explores similar hybrid approaches. 

The main strength of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis, demonstrating that, *given a known condition number*, EMGD achieves the claimed convergence rate. However, this assumption is a significant limitation. The paper provides no discussion or guidance on how to handle scenarios where κ is unknown or inaccurately estimated, or how the algorithm performs under such misspecification. The algorithm’s dependency on the hyperparameter η, which is explicitly tied to κ, further exacerbates this issue as no estimation procedure or heuristics are provided.  

The lack of experimental validation is a critical weakness. The authors present no empirical results to support their theoretical claims or investigate the algorithm’s behavior when κ is misspecified – a scenario likely to occur in practice. It would be highly valuable to analyze, or at least empirically test, convergence when η (and therefore κ) is set too large or too small. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t consider the non-strongly convex setting, limiting its broader applicability. 

The description of the optimization problem setting in Section 3.1 is misleading, conflating finite-sum deterministic and stochastic approximation. Finally, while the algorithm is described as a “mixed” update, the paper lacks a deeper discussion of its relationship to simpler interleaving strategies.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretical results provide a convergence guarantee under a specific, albeit strong, assumption.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in optimization – reducing the cost of ill-conditioned problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumption of known condition number drastically limits practical applicability.
*   Lack of experimental validation.
*   Missing comparison to relevant prior work (Friedlander & Schmidt 2012).
*   Limited scope – does not address non-strongly convex settings.
*   Potentially misleading problem setting description.



**Overall:** I recommend **rejection**. While the theoretical contribution is present, the significant limitations regarding practical applicability and lack of empirical evidence outweigh its strengths. Addressing the issues highlighted above, particularly providing guidance for unknown κ and including experimental results, would significantly improve the paper’s value.
