- Introduces GREEDI, a novel distributed protocol for maximizing submodular functions under cardinality constraints.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis showing GREEDI competes with the centralized greedy algorithm under natural conditions.  
- Includes extensive experimental evaluation on exemplar-based clustering and active set selection with large-scale datasets.  
- Experiments demonstrate scalability on Hadoop and show GREEDI outperforms other distributed benchmarks, approximating centralized greedy.  
- Presentation is clear and well-organized, making the method and analysis easy to follow.  
- Related work review is comprehensive and highlights the need for distributed submodular maximization.  
- Theoretical results are well-supported by proofs, giving solid performance guarantees.  
- Assumes the submodular function is available as a black box, which may not hold in practice.  
- Treatment of decomposable functions is brief and could be developed further.  
- Lacks detailed comparisons to other distributed optimization methods (e.g., gradient-based, parallel coordinate descent).  
- Does not analyze algorithm robustness to noisy or erroneous data; assumes clean inputs.  
- Additional experiments and comparisons could strengthen empirical claims.  
- Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution but would benefit from deeper treatment of decomposability, alternative baselines, and noise robustness.  
- Pro-acceptance points: novel effective algorithm, rigorous theory, comprehensive experiments, and clear presentation.  
- Con-acceptance points: black-box assumption, limited comparisons, insufficient discussion of decomposable functions and noise robustness, and need for more experimental baselines.
