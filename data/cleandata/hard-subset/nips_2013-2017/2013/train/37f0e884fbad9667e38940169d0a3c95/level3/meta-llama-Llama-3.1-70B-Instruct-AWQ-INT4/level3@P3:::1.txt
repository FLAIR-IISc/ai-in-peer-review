This paper proposes a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that utilizes both full and stochastic gradients to achieve a linear convergence rate for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The main idea behind EMGD is to divide the optimization process into epochs, where each epoch consists of a full gradient evaluation followed by multiple stochastic gradient evaluations. By leveraging the strengths of both full and stochastic gradients, EMGD is able to reduce the number of full gradients required from O(√κ log 1/ε) to O(log 1/ε), making it more computationally efficient for large-scale optimization problems.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of EMGD, including a convergence rate theorem that guarantees a linear convergence rate with a high-probability guarantee. The analysis is well-organized and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers with a basic understanding of convex optimization and stochastic gradient methods.

One of the key strengths of EMGD is its ability to handle constrained optimization problems, which is a significant advantage over other stochastic gradient methods such as SAG and SDCA. Additionally, EMGD has a much simpler proof compared to SAG, making it more appealing to researchers and practitioners.

The paper also provides a comprehensive comparison of EMGD with other optimization algorithms, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA. The comparison highlights the advantages and disadvantages of each algorithm, providing valuable insights for researchers and practitioners.

However, the paper lacks empirical evaluations to demonstrate the practical relevance of EMGD. While the theoretical analysis is sound, it is essential to validate the performance of EMGD on real-world datasets to establish its efficacy.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and drawbacks of EMGD. For instance, the algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ, which may not be readily available in practice. Additionally, the epoch length must be fixed a priori, which may not be suitable for all optimization problems.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical analysis. However, some of the notation and terminology could be clarified, particularly for readers who are not familiar with convex optimization and stochastic gradient methods.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of convex optimization, and EMGD has the potential to become a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners. With some additional empirical evaluations and discussions of the limitations and drawbacks, this paper could be even more impactful.
