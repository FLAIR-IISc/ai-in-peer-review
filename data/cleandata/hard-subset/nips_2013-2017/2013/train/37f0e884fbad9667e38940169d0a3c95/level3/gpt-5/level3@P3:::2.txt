Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization and proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid first-order method that uses both (i) a full-gradient oracle Og(w)=∇F(w) and (ii) a “function oracle” Of that induces unbiased stochastic gradients. Each epoch computes one full gradient at a reference point w̄k and then performs T mixed-gradient steps g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k) within a shrinking ball around w̄k. The analysis shows linear convergence with high probability: to reach ε suboptimality, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (T = Θ(κ2 log(1/δ))). The main selling point is that the number of full gradients is independent of the condition number κ.

Conceptually, the mixed gradient is a control-variate/variance-reduced estimator centered at ∇F(w̄k), much like ideas in semi-stochastic methods going back to Marti and Fuchs (1992, 1994), later hybrid methods (Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012), and contemporaneous variance-reduction methods for finite sums such as SAG (Le Roux et al., NIPS’12), SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, JMLR’13), and SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS’13). The paper briefly compares to SAG/SDCA for finite-sum problems but does not deeply engage with prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic schemes, nor with the close connection to variance-reduced estimators.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The high-probability linear-rate result under smoothness and strong convexity is technically plausible and follows standard tools (variance reduction, domain shrinkage, martingale bounds). However:
  - The analysis crucially requires knowledge of L and λ to set T and η (η = 1/(L√T) with T = Ω(κ2 log(1/δ))). No guidance is provided when κ is unknown or misspecified. This makes the stated guarantees fragile in practice.
  - There is an inconsistency: the abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, while Theorem 1 and Section 3.3 yield O(κ2 log(1/ε)). Please reconcile this discrepancy.
  - The mixed step is essentially a well-known variance-reduced estimator; the paper would benefit from clarifying the exact novelty beyond the shrinking-ball device and the high-probability bound.
- Empirical validation: There are no experiments. Given the practical motivation (“reduce expensive full-gradient calls”), empirical evidence comparing wall-clock time and robustness to κ-misspecification is important.
- Scope of assumptions: The hybrid-oracle assumption (ready access to both full and stochastic gradients) is strong and not always realistic in stochastic approximation settings. The paper should delineate settings where Og is available and justified.

Clarity
- Section 3.1’s problem setting is ambiguous. The Of oracle corresponds to a stochastic approximation model (F(w)=E[f(w)]), whereas later comparisons (SAG/SDCA) assume a finite-sum structure F(w) = (1/n)∑ fi(w). Please clearly separate the two regimes and state which guarantees apply where.
- Terminology such as “condition-number independent access of full gradients” is potentially misleading: independence holds only for the number of full-gradient calls; overall complexity still scales with κ via the number of stochastic steps (κ2).
- The paper would benefit from a discussion relating the “mixed” update to:
  - Simple interleaving of deterministic and stochastic steps (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012),
  - Control-variate variance reduction (e.g., SAG/SDCA/SVRG), and
  - Why the shrinking domain is necessary here and how it compares to step-size/epoch-length choices in those methods.

Originality
- Using one full gradient per epoch and a centered stochastic estimator is not new; similar estimators appear in SAG/SDCA/SVRG. The novelty appears to be: (i) high-probability guarantees in the expectation-oracle setting, (ii) the shrinking-radius projection to bound the stochastic part, and (iii) a focus on reducing full-gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)). These distinctions should be made explicit, with a careful comparison to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and contemporaneous SVRG.
- The paper does not address the non-strongly convex (merely convex) case, which limits breadth.

Significance
- Reducing the number of full-gradient evaluations is valuable when full gradients are costly (large-scale problems, distributed settings). However, the practical impact is unclear without experiments, especially since overall gradient work still scales with κ2 and the algorithm requires knowing κ to set η and T.
- Lack of discussion on robustness to misspecified κ and on problem regimes where Og is available reduces applicability.

Specific weaknesses and requests
- Unknown/misspecified κ: Provide analysis or at least experiments showing behavior when κ (and thus T, η) is set too small or too large. Consider adaptive schemes: doubling/halving T across epochs, line search/backtracking for L, and regularization-based lower bounds for λ. Discuss whether a doubling trick can recover guarantees without prior κ.
- Hyperparameter selection: Offer practical procedures to estimate L and λ (e.g., backtracking for L; probing strong convexity via regularization parameter; finite-sample Hessian bounds as briefly mentioned in the conclusion), and report sensitivity.
- Experiments: Include benchmarks on least-squares and logistic regression; compare against SGD/averaged SGD, Nesterov’s accelerated gradient, SAG, SDCA, and SVRG. Evaluate: number of full-gradient calls, total gradient computations, wall-clock time, robustness to κ misspecification, and constrained vs. unconstrained cases.
- Broadened settings: Discuss extensions to non-strongly convex problems (e.g., via regularization, quadratic growth/error-bound conditions, or restarts).
- Related work: Add and discuss Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013), and clarify differences. Also clarify how EMGD compares in storage, distributed implementations, and high-probability vs. in-expectation guarantees.
- Clarity fixes: Resolve the O(κ) vs O(κ2) inconsistency; make the oracle model(s) explicit; justify the projection step cost for constrained problems.

Pros
- Clear theoretical target: reduce the number of full-gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)).
- High-probability linear convergence under smooth strong convexity.
- Simple algorithmic structure; potentially attractive in distributed settings for the full-gradient step.

Cons
- No experiments; practicality not demonstrated.
- Requires a priori knowledge of L and λ; no robust or adaptive scheme provided.
- Inconsistency in stated stochastic-gradient complexity (κ vs κ2).
- Limited discussion and comparison with closely related hybrid/variance-reduced methods.
- Ambiguity in problem setting (finite sum vs expectation).

Recommendation
Lean toward rejection in its current form. The theoretical result is interesting, but the lack of empirical validation, the strong reliance on known κ, the inconsistency in complexity claims, and insufficient positioning relative to prior hybrid and variance-reduced methods limit both clarity and significance. A revised version with (i) corrected and clarified complexity statements, (ii) adaptive/robust parameter selection or analysis, (iii) thorough empirical comparisons to SGD/ASGD/SAG/SDCA/SVRG, and (iv) a clearer treatment of problem settings would make a much stronger case.
