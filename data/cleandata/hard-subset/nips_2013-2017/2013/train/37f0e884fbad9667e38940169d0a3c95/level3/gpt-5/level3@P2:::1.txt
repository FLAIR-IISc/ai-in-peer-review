Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth and strongly convex optimization that combines one full-gradient computation per epoch with multiple “mixed” stochastic steps. Each mixed step uses a variance-reduced gradient estimator of the form ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄), where w̄ is the epoch anchor. The algorithm also projects within a shrinking ball around the anchor and averages the iterates at the end of each epoch. The main theoretical result shows a linear convergence rate: to reach ε suboptimality, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations, with a high-probability guarantee. By allowing stochastic gradients, EMGD removes the condition-number dependence from the number of full-gradient calls (from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε))).

Relative to full-gradient methods (e.g., Nesterov’s accelerated methods; see [17,19]), EMGD reduces the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations at the expense of many cheaper stochastic steps. Relative to stochastic methods with polynomial dependence on 1/ε (e.g., [9,10,21]), EMGD achieves linear convergence. For finite-sum problems F(w) = (1/n)∑ fi(w), it is related to variance-reduction and hybrid schemes (SAG [22], SDCA [23], hybrid deterministic–stochastic [5]), but differs in handling constraints and in its oracle model and proof techniques. The mixed-gradient estimator is closely related in spirit to variance-reduction ideas (e.g., SAG/SDCA; the estimator is identical to what later became known as SVRG), though EMGD does not store per-example gradients and uses a shrinking trust region.

Strengths
- Clear and simple algorithm with a compact, self-contained proof. The high-probability linear convergence is stronger than in-expectation guarantees common in related work.
- Conceptually appealing: one full gradient per epoch anchors the estimator; the stochastic difference ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄) is controlled via smoothness and the shrinking ball.
- Memory-light (O(d)) and applicable to constrained problems (via projection onto W ∩ B(w̄; Δ)), unlike SAG/SDCA which typically target unconstrained problems and require O(n) memory.
- Parallelization benefit: the full-gradient computation can be distributed without affecting the convergence guarantees.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Complexity mismatch and tightness: The main theorem yields O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls. This is substantially worse than O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) for SAG/SDCA on finite sums, and worse than what one might expect from variance-reduction techniques (κ rather than κ^2). The paper should clarify whether κ^2 is inherent to the oracle setting or an artifact of the proof/step-size choice. Moreover, the abstract states O(κ log(1/ε)) in one place, whereas the body and Theorem 1 state O(κ^2 log(1/ε)); this inconsistency must be fixed.
- Practicality: No experiments are provided. It is hard to assess whether the reduced number of full gradients translates to real runtime wins over strong baselines (accelerated GD, SAG, SDCA) across regimes of n and κ. The claim that EMGD is preferable when κ ≤ n2/3 is purely asymptotic; empirical validation is needed.
- Parameter knowledge and adaptivity: EMGD requires knowing κ to set T per epoch. In practice this is often unknown. Related methods (e.g., SDCA, SAG variants) are more adaptive or come with step-size heuristics. The paper should discuss estimation (beyond a brief mention) or provide adaptive variants (e.g., doubling T until observed progress, or backtracking).
- Comparisons and related work: Section 3.4 is informative but could be more precise: clarify in what constrained settings SAG/SDCA fail or how they might be adapted with projections; discuss memory–time trade-offs. Please expand the comparison to hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods (Friedlander & Schmidt, SIAM SISC 2012 [5]) which are very relevant. Also, the mixed-gradient estimator is essentially the variance-reduced gradient used in later “SVRG”-style methods; a discussion of similarities/differences and complexity implications would be valuable.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Provide more intuition for key proof steps (why the shrinking ball is needed; how the Azuma bound controls the martingale term).
  - Clean up notation and typos: “epoches” → “epochs”; consistent use of log/ln; avoid duplicating equation labels (e.g., (2)); ensure assumptions (e.g., strong convexity) are stated in a standard form; fix “interstage” → “investigate”.
  - Table 1 and 2: ensure precise assumptions (finite-sum vs oracle setting), costs per iteration, and references supporting each rate.
  - The footnote on SDCA applicability breaks flow; integrate it into the text.

Quality
- The analysis is technically sound under the stated assumptions, with a clear high-probability argument via Hoeffding–Azuma. The κ^2 dependence likely reflects the conservative choice η=1/(L√T) and the shrinking domain. Tightness is questionable compared to related results; clarifying this would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but several notational/typo issues and places where intuition is terse. The comparisons section should be expanded and made more precise.

Originality
- The algorithmic template (full gradient at anchor + stochastic differences) is known in finite-sum variance reduction, but the constrained setting, oracle model, and the particular shrinking-ball analysis are a useful twist with a simple proof and high-probability guarantees.

Significance
- The idea of decoupling the number of full-gradient calls from κ is appealing and relevant for large-scale learning. However, the κ^2 factor in stochastic calls limits competitiveness in many regimes. Demonstrations on realistic problems (e.g., regularized logistic regression, ridge) are needed to assess impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Resolve the κ vs κ^2 inconsistency and discuss whether κ^2 is an analysis artifact; explore constant step-size without shrinking ball to approach κ dependence.
- Add experiments comparing EMGD to accelerated GD, SAG, and SDCA across varying n, κ, and constraint sets; measure wall-clock time, full-gradient calls, and memory.
- Provide an adaptive schedule for T/η not requiring prior κ; discuss practical step-size heuristics.
- Expand related work to include Friedlander & Schmidt [5] and closely related variance-reduction approaches; sharpen claims in Section 3.4.
- Improve exposition and fix typos/notation as noted above.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, memory-efficient algorithm with a clean, high-probability linear-rate analysis.
- Handles constrained problems and distributed full-gradient computation.
- Clear conceptual contribution: condition-number–independent number of full-gradient calls.

Arguments against acceptance
- No empirical validation; unclear practical benefit over strong baselines.
- κ^2 dependence in stochastic calls is likely suboptimal; abstract/body inconsistency.
- Comparisons to closely related methods are incomplete; adaptivity issues remain.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept if the authors fix the κ vs κ^2 inconsistency, expand comparisons and related work, and (ideally) add experiments demonstrating regimes where EMGD is practically beneficial. Otherwise, I lean to reject due to missing empirical evidence and suboptimal complexity.
