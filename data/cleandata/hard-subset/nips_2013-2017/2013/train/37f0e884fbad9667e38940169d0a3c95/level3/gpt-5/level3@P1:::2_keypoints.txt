- Proposes EMGD: interleave rare full gradients with frequent stochastic steps using a mixed unbiased estimator and shrinking-ball projected updates to achieve epoch-wise constant reduction.
- Main theorem claims high-probability linear convergence requiring O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients under L-smoothness and λ-strong convexity.
- The mixed-gradient estimator is essentially the variance-reduction control-variate used in SAG/SDCA and related semi-stochastic methods.
- Paper does not adequately situate EMGD within prior hybrid/variance-reduction literature or compare to finite-sum results that attain O((n+κ) log(1/ε)).
- Strength: clear, simple algorithm with an unbiased mixed estimator easy to compute given both oracles.
- Strength: provides high-probability (not just in-expectation) linear convergence analysis.
- Strength: number of full-gradient calls is independent of κ, appealing when full gradients are expensive and parallelizable.
- Strength: method handles constrained problems via projection onto W ∩ ball.
- Weakness: inconsistency in reported stochastic-gradient complexity—abstract states O(κ log(1/ε)) but analysis implies O(κ^2 log(1/ε)).
- Weakness: analysis requires knowledge of κ (L and λ) to set T and η, with no discussion of estimation or sensitivity to misspecification.
- Weakness: no experiments provided to validate empirical performance, κ-dependence, or constrained-setting advantages.
- Weakness: oracle model mixes general expectation and finite-sum settings, creating confusion and preventing direct comparison to SAG/SDCA.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of how shrinking-ball variance control compares to memory-based or SVRG-like variance-reduction techniques.
- Weakness: does not address merely convex (non-strongly convex) cases or provide standard fixes (regularization/restarts).
- Quality: proof is careful with martingale arguments, but contains constant-factor issues and the κ vs κ^2 inconsistency needs clarification.
- Quality: projection onto shrinking balls introduces hyperparameters tied to unknown distances to optimum with little guidance.
- Clarity: paper is readable and algorithm is clear, but Section 3.1 and prior-work positioning reduce clarity of contribution.
- Originality: estimator is standard; novelty is limited to high-probability analysis, shrinking-domain updates, and κ-independent full-gradient count.
- Significance: reducing full-gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)) is appealing, but overall stochastic complexity appears suboptimal and practical impact is uncertain without experiments.
- Pros for acceptance: high-probability linear convergence, simple updates, applicability to constrained problems, and emphasis on reducing full-gradient calls.
- Cons for acceptance: likely suboptimal stochastic complexity, inconsistent claims, missing experiments, and incomplete positioning vs related methods.
- Suggestions: reconcile/tighten stochastic-gradient bounds, add empirical comparisons (SGD/ASGD, accelerated FG, SAG, SDCA), and provide adaptive strategies for η, T, Δk without knowing κ.
- Suggestions: clarify finite-sum vs expectation settings and relate explicitly to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and variance-reduction literature.
- Recommendation: Reject — sound idea and interesting analysis but theoretical inconsistencies, incomplete prior-work comparison, and lack of experiments limit acceptability.
