This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a central problem in machine learning. The primary motivation is to reduce the computational burden of first-order methods on ill-conditioned problems, where the number of required full gradient evaluations can be large. Standard accelerated methods require O(sqrt(κ) log(1/ε)) full gradient computations. The authors propose a novel hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. The core idea is to structure the optimization into epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" that combines this single full gradient with variance-reducing stochastic terms.

The main theoretical result is that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This effectively makes the number of expensive full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ, trading them for a large number of cheaper stochastic gradient computations. The paper provides a self-contained convergence proof showing that the algorithm achieves a linear convergence rate with high probability. This work is related to recent variance-reduced stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA, which also achieve linear convergence, but EMGD offers a different approach by explicitly mixing full and stochastic oracles.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Significance:** The central idea of designing a hybrid algorithm to make the full-gradient complexity independent of the condition number κ is novel and theoretically significant. It directly tackles a known bottleneck in first-order optimization for ill-conditioned problems.

2.  **Quality of Theoretical Results:** The paper provides a strong theoretical contribution. The linear convergence guarantee holds with high probability, which is a stronger and often more desirable result than the in-expectation convergence guarantees provided by methods like SAG and SDCA. The convergence proof is elegant, simple, and largely self-contained, which adds to the paper's clarity and value.

3.  **Generality:** Unlike SAG and SDCA, which are primarily designed for unconstrained optimization of finite sums, the proposed EMGD algorithm is applicable to general constrained optimization problems (provided the projection in Step 8 is efficient). The authors also correctly point out potential advantages in memory (O(d) vs. O(n)) and parallelization, though these are not explored in depth.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of experiments. While the theoretical contribution is sound, its practical relevance is entirely unsubstantiated. It is crucial to see how EMGD performs in practice against relevant baselines, especially Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA. Such experiments would validate the theoretical complexity trade-offs and show if the O(κ²) dependence in the stochastic gradient term is manageable on real-world problems.

2.  **Limited and Potentially Misleading Comparisons:** The complexity comparison in Section 3.4 is a good start but needs to be more nuanced and comprehensive.
    *   The analysis shows EMGD is better than Nesterov's method only when κ ≤ n^(2/3). In many machine learning settings with L2 regularization λ = C/n, we have κ ≈ L/λ ≈ nL/C. In this common regime, the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3) may not hold, potentially making EMGD less competitive than Nesterov's method. The authors should discuss realistic scenarios where their condition is met.
    *   The comparison to SAG and SDCA is brief. In many regimes, SAG (O(n log(1/ε))) and SDCA (O((n+κ) log(1/ε))) appear to have better overall complexities than EMGD (O((n + κ²) log(1/ε))). The paper should honestly discuss these trade-offs and highlight the specific regimes (e.g., very small κ, large n, constrained problems) where EMGD has a distinct advantage.

3.  **Practicality Concerns:** The algorithm's design has practical drawbacks that are not discussed.
    *   The number of inner iterations, T, depends directly on a known condition number κ. In practice, κ is often unknown and difficult to estimate. This makes the algorithm hard to tune, unlike SAG and SDCA which are more adaptive. The authors relegate the estimation of κ to future work, but this is a critical limitation for the current proposal.
    *   The step size η is also fixed and depends on T. Modern methods often employ adaptive step-size or line-search mechanisms, which can lead to significant practical speedups.

4.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper should cite and discuss the hybrid deterministic-stochastic method of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), which also interleaves full and stochastic gradient steps. A comparison would help clarify this paper's specific contribution to this line of work.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Clarity of Analysis:** While the proof is mathematically clear, it would benefit from more high-level intuition. For example, why is the specific form of the mixed gradient chosen, and what role does the shrinking domain B(w̄; ∆k) play in ensuring convergence?
*   **Table 1:** This table is useful but lacks citations. Please cite the papers corresponding to each optimal iteration complexity listed.
*   **Minor Edits:**
    *   The notation for the domain is inconsistent (W in Section 1, X in Lemma 1).
    *   Please use consistent notation for logarithms (log vs. ln).
    *   There appear to be some typos, e.g., in the statement of Theorem 1, "m accesses to oracle Og" should probably be "m+1". Please proofread carefully.

### Summary

This paper presents a theoretically sound and novel algorithm, EMGD, with a strong high-probability linear convergence guarantee. Its main contribution—making the number of full gradient calls independent of the condition number—is an important theoretical step. However, the paper's claims of significance are severely weakened by a lack of any experimental results and a limited discussion of its practical drawbacks and comparison to state-of-the-art methods.

I would be willing to raise my score if the authors can provide even basic experiments in their rebuttal that demonstrate a setting where EMGD is empirically competitive, and if they expand their discussion to more thoroughly and honestly position their work relative to SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's method. As it stands, the paper is a nice theoretical idea whose practical value remains an open question.
