This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex functions. The goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, which for ill-conditioned problems can be a bottleneck for standard accelerated methods. The key idea is to combine a small number of full gradients with a larger number of cheaper stochastic gradients. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient," which is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient centered at the epoch's starting point. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, where κ is the condition number. This result is significant because the number of full gradient evaluations is independent of the condition number κ, unlike Nesterov's optimal method which requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients. The work is related to recent advances in stochastic optimization that achieve linear convergence, such as SAG and SDCA, but offers different trade-offs in terms of memory requirements and applicability to constrained problems.

**Quality**
The paper appears to be technically sound, and the convergence proof is novel and insightful. The analysis relies on a careful bounding of the variance of the mixed gradient estimator within a shrinking trust region, which leads to the linear convergence rate. The proof structure, based on induction over epochs, is clear and the derivations seem correct, pending some minor clarifications.

However, a major concern is the practical utility of the result, particularly in the context of machine learning. The authors argue that EMGD is computationally cheaper than accelerated gradient descent when κ ≤ n^(2/3) (for finite-sum problems with n terms). For many regularized loss minimization problems, the strong convexity parameter λ is determined by the regularization strength, while the smoothness constant L and thus the condition number κ = L/λ can depend on the data and potentially scale with n. A more thorough discussion is needed to establish in which practical ML regimes the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3) holds. Without this justification, the significance of the result for the NIPS community is unclear, as it may only offer an advantage in a narrow or "uninteresting" range of problems.

Furthermore, the theoretical guarantee is a high-probability bound, which is strong but weaker than the deterministic guarantee provided by full gradient methods. This trade-off should be discussed explicitly.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is presented well, and the main theoretical result is stated upfront. The comparison to related work in Table 2 is helpful. However, several parts of the analysis could be clarified to improve readability and aid reproducibility:
- In Section 3.1, please explicitly define the condition number as κ = L/λ and state that L ≥ λ is a consequence of the definitions of L-smoothness and λ-strong convexity.
- The use of notation in the proof in Section 4.2 could be more precise. For example:
    - In eq. (13), the term `|Vt|` should likely use norms, e.g., `||...||`, as the quantities inside are vectors.
    - Please clarify which function's strong convexity is being invoked in the inequality in eq. (6); it should be F.
    - In the first line of the proof of Theorem 1 (Section 4.1), the reference to eq. (5) for the inequality F(w̄¹) - F(w*) ≤ λ[Δ¹]²/2 is unclear; this seems to follow from the assumption on Δ¹ and Lemma 1.
    - The equality in the statement "Suppose Theorem 1 is true for m = k. That is, with a probability..." should be an inequality (≤) for clarity.

**Originality**
The proposed EMGD algorithm is novel. The specific construction of the "mixed gradient" `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is an original variance reduction technique. While it shares conceptual similarities with SAG and SVRG (which was concurrent work), its integration into an epoch-based framework with a shrinking domain is a unique contribution. The paper clearly situates its contribution with respect to existing deterministic and stochastic methods and makes a good effort to compare with the state-of-the-art (SAG, SDCA).

**Significance**
The paper presents a theoretically interesting result: decoupling the number of full gradient calls from the condition number is a significant achievement. This could potentially inspire further research into hybrid algorithms. However, the practical significance is questionable due to the concerns raised under the "Quality" section. The O(κ²) dependence for stochastic gradients is steep and may make the algorithm slow for ill-conditioned problems, even if full gradient calls are saved. The comparisons to SAG and SDCA are fair, highlighting EMGD's advantages in handling constrained optimization and having low memory requirements. These are valuable points. Nevertheless, the central claim of outperforming accelerated gradient descent hinges on an analysis of the condition number in typical ML settings, which is currently missing from the paper. If the authors can provide compelling examples or a more detailed analysis showing that their method is advantageous for real-world ML problems, the significance of the paper would be greatly enhanced.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel and elegant hybrid optimization algorithm.
2.  The main theoretical result—achieving a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number—is a strong and original contribution.
3.  The analysis provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation results of some competing methods.
4.  The method is more general than some competitors (e.g., SAG, SDCA) as it can handle constrained optimization and requires less memory.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The primary weakness is the questionable practical relevance to machine learning. The condition under which the proposed method outperforms accelerated gradient descent (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) is not well-justified for typical ML scenarios.
2.  The number of stochastic gradients scales with κ², which can be prohibitively large for ill-conditioned problems.
3.  The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ, a practical limitation that is common but still a drawback.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper with a novel algorithm and an interesting convergence result. The main reservation is its practical significance for the machine learning community. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can provide a more convincing argument for the applicability and computational advantage of their method on machine learning problems.
