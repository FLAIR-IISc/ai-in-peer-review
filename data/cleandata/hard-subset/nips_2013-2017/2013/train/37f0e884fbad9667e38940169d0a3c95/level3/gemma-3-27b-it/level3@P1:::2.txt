This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ in the iteration complexity for smooth and strongly convex optimization. The core idea is to leverage both full and stochastic gradients, aiming for O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations – independent of κ – alongside O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. The paper meticulously outlines the algorithm and provides a theoretical analysis demonstrating this convergence rate.

The work builds upon existing first-order optimization methods, referencing key contributions in deterministic and stochastic optimization (Boyd & Vandenberghe, Nesterov, Ghadimi & Lan). The authors correctly identify the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost, motivating the exploration of hybrid approaches.  The related work section provides a reasonable overview, although, critically, it overlooks related work directly combining deterministic and stochastic steps like Friedlander & Schmidt (2012).  The paper effectively contrasts EMGD with existing algorithms like SAG and SDCA, analyzing computational complexity under specific conditions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed EMGD algorithm offers a potentially valuable approach to mitigating the curse of ill-conditioning in optimization, especially when both full and stochastic gradients are readily available. The theoretical guarantee of O(log(1/ε)) full gradient complexity is a significant claim.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a detailed theoretical analysis, including a convergence theorem with a clear dependence on the condition number and number of epochs. The proof appears carefully constructed, relying on established lemmas concerning strongly convex functions.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The algorithm is clearly described (Algorithm 1), and the notation is generally consistent. The paper provides a relatively accessible explanation of the key concepts.
* **Comparative Analysis:** The comparison to existing algorithms (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) provides some context, highlighting potential advantages of EMGD under specific conditions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Assumption of Known Condition Number:** The most significant limitation is the requirement for *a priori* knowledge of the condition number κ. The paper acknowledges this but offers no discussion of robustness to misspecification or methods for estimating κ.  In practical applications, κ is rarely known exactly.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper presents *no* experimental results.  This is a crucial omission. Without experiments, it's impossible to assess the practical performance of EMGD, its sensitivity to the accuracy of κ, or its competitiveness against existing methods. Experiments are needed to evaluate performance with both correct and inaccurate κ estimates.
* **Limited Scope:** The analysis is restricted to strongly convex functions.  Many real-world optimization problems are only convex, not strongly convex.  Extending the analysis to this broader class of problems would significantly increase the paper's applicability.
* **Incomplete Related Work:** The related work section is not exhaustive, particularly regarding hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods. The omission of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) is a notable oversight.
* **Ambiguous Problem Setting:** Section 3.1 introduces function and gradient oracles, but doesn't clearly define the underlying optimization problem setting. Is it finite-sum deterministic optimization, or stochastic approximation? This ambiguity weakens the overall presentation.
* **Algorithm Insight:** The algorithm's update step is described as a “mixed” gradient descent. However, it’s unclear how this differs fundamentally from simply interleaving a full gradient step with stochastic gradient steps. More insight into why this particular mixing strategy leads to the claimed convergence rate is needed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm relies on a step size η dependent on κ.  The paper does not discuss the impact of setting η too large or too small, nor does it offer heuristics for selecting a suitable value.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contribution – achieving O(log(1/ε)) full gradient complexity – is potentially significant.
* The paper is well-written and presents a clear, rigorous analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumption of known condition number severely limits the practical applicability of the algorithm.
* The lack of experimental validation makes it impossible to assess the algorithm’s actual performance or robustness.
* The incomplete related work and limited scope further weaken the contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical result but suffers from significant practical limitations. The assumption of a known condition number and the absence of experimental validation are major drawbacks.  While the theoretical analysis is sound, the lack of empirical evidence and the narrow scope diminish the paper's overall impact. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the weaknesses by: (1) exploring methods for estimating the condition number, (2) conducting thorough experiments to validate the theoretical results and assess the algorithm's robustness, and (3) extending the analysis to the broader class of convex optimization problems.  A more complete related work section including relevant hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods is also necessary.
