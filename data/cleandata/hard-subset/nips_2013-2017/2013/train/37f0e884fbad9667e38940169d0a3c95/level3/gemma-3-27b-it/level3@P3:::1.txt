This paper proposes the Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization, aiming to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ when using first-order methods. The core idea is to combine full-gradient and stochastic-gradient steps within each epoch, achieving O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls. The algorithm’s theoretical strength lies in its linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee, surpassing the in-expectation result of SAG.

**Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The paper presents a well-defined algorithm and a seemingly rigorous theoretical analysis supporting its linear convergence. The proof appears complete, albeit dense.
* **Novelty:** The mixing of full and stochastic gradients for constrained smooth strongly-convex optimization is a novel approach. The authors successfully demonstrate a linear convergence rate, a significant achievement.
* **Theoretical Significance:**  Achieving condition number independence in the number of full-gradient evaluations is a valuable contribution, potentially enabling efficient optimization for ill-conditioned problems. The high-probability convergence guarantee is stronger than existing results for some algorithms.
* **Advantages over SAG:** The algorithm’s applicability to constrained problems and potential memory/parallelization benefits (though not fully demonstrated) are noteworthy. The simpler proof is also a positive aspect.
* **Clear Comparisons:** Table 2 provides a useful, though somewhat limited, comparison of computational complexities with related algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of experiments or concrete examples. Without empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of EMGD and validate its theoretical gains.
* **Incomplete Discussion:** Section 3.4, “Comparisons,” feels underdeveloped. A more detailed comparison to existing methods, especially SAG and SDCA, is crucial. The discussion of the regimes where EMGD outperforms other algorithms is too brief.
* **Practical Drawbacks:** The requirement to pre-specify the epoch length (and thus *k*) is a significant practical limitation. Adaptive step-size mechanisms, common in SCDA and SAG, are missing from EMGD. This dependence on knowing *k* a priori severely limits its usability in many real-world scenarios.
* **Step-Size and Adaptivity:** The lack of automatic or adaptive step-size selection is a practical concern. The paper does not address how to choose an appropriate step size without prior knowledge of the problem’s characteristics.
* **Realistic Regime Concerns:** The paper doesn’t adequately address scenarios where EMGD might *not* be advantageous. The point raised regarding regularization (C/n) is important and should be further emphasized – in many ML applications, EMGD might not outperform established methods.
* **Related Work Omission:** The paper should cite and contrast the hybrid deterministic-stochastic method presented by Friedlander & Schmidt (2012). This work is directly related to the approach taken in EMGD and a comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning within the literature.
* **Clarity Issues:** While the theoretical framework is solid, the high-level intuition behind the proof choices could be better explained. Clarification of Table 1 citations and assumptions (specifically SCDA applicability and the gradient expectation identity) is needed. The consistent use of notation for domain, norms, and log/ln would improve readability.

**Specific Suggestions:**

* **Expand Section 3.4:** Provide a more thorough comparison with SAG and SDCA, including a discussion of memory requirements, parallelization possibilities, and ease of implementation.
* **Address Limitations:**  Clearly state the limitations of EMGD, such as the need to know *k* beforehand and the potential for poor performance in specific scenarios (e.g., when regularization is small).
* **Discuss Step-Size Selection:**  Explore potential strategies for selecting the step size η, even if they are heuristic.
* **Add Related Work:** Include a citation and discussion of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012).
* **Clarify Notation:** Ensure consistent use of mathematical notation throughout the paper.
* **Add Intuition:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of the key ideas behind the proof.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with a strong convergence guarantee.
* The condition number independence result is a significant contribution.
* The proof appears complete and correct.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of experimental validation is a major drawback.
* The practical limitations (fixed epoch length, lack of adaptivity) are significant.
* The discussion of comparisons and limitations is incomplete.

**Overall Verdict:**

This is a technically sound and theoretically significant paper. The proposed EMGD algorithm represents a valuable addition to the landscape of optimization methods. However, the lack of experimental validation and the practical limitations raise concerns. I recommend acceptance with the strong encouragement that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by adding experiments and expanding the discussion of practical considerations. A detailed rebuttal addressing these points, with a commitment to incorporating the suggested changes, would also be acceptable.
