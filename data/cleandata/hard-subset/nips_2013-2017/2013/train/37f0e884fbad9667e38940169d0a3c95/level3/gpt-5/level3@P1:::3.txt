This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth, strongly convex objectives that interleaves rare full-gradient evaluations with frequent stochastic-gradient evaluations. EMGD divides optimization into epochs; in each epoch, it computes one full gradient at the epoch anchor w̄ and then performs T projected updates with a “mixed” gradient g̃t = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄), over a shrinking Euclidean ball centered at w̄. The key result is a linear convergence rate to an ε-optimal solution using O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient computations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient computations, where κ = L/λ is the condition number. The high-level idea is that the stochastic part of g̃t has bounded variance due to smoothness and shrinking domains, so each epoch achieves a constant error reduction.

Relation to prior work: For smooth, strongly convex problems, accelerated full-gradient methods achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations (Nesterov). Purely stochastic methods for strongly convex problems achieve O(1/(λ ε)) complexity and are sublinear in 1/ε. Finite-sum “variance-reduction” methods such as SAG and SDCA obtain linear rates O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) or O(n log(1/ε)) under finite-sum structure, typically unconstrained optimization, and with higher memory. EMGD is conceptually close to semi-stochastic/hybrid methods (e.g., Marti; Friedlander & Schmidt), but differs by ensuring a condition-number-independent number of full gradients, handling constrained domains via projections, and providing high-probability guarantees under a general stochastic function oracle (not restricted to finite sums).

Quality: The analysis is careful and—modulo minor issues noted below—appears correct. Theorem 1 shows that with T = Ω((L/λ)^2 log(1/δ)) = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) stochastic updates per epoch, the optimization error contracts by a factor of 1/2 per epoch. Hence after m = O(log(1/ε)) epochs, EMGD attains ε accuracy with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations. The proof leverages strong convexity, smoothness, a gradient mapping over a shrinking ball, and martingale concentration to bound the stochastic error. One clarity/correctness point that should be fixed: there is an inconsistency between the abstract’s O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient count and the theorem’s O(κ^2 log(1/ε)); the latter is what follows from T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) and m = O(log(1/ε)).

Clarity: The paper is generally well structured, with a clear algorithm box and a readable proof sketch. However, several notational and editorial issues impede readability:
- Define the condition number κ precisely as κ = L/λ early in the paper and state explicitly that L ≥ λ follows from smoothness and strong convexity assumptions.
- Fix the inconsistency between O(κ) vs O(κ^2) stochastic-gradient complexity across the abstract, introduction, and theorem statement.
- Minor corrections:
  - Equation (6): the bound should read ||w* − ŵ||^2 (consistent with the subsequent usage).
  - In (13), use norms rather than absolute values for vector quantities.
  - In line 286, removing the equality improves clarity.
  - Specify clearly which function is used for equation (7) (the context around the epoch anchor).
  - In (4), the notation x̂ should be ŵ, and f should be F; note that the first term in the max is larger by Lemma 1.
- State the requirement that κ (and thus T) must be known or estimated, and discuss how to set ∆1.

Originality: The “mixed gradient” construction with one full gradient per epoch, shrinking trust region, and a high-probability linear rate for general stochastic oracles (and constrained domains) is novel relative to prior semi-stochastic methods and finite-sum variance-reduction techniques circa NIPS 2013. The condition-number-independent count of full gradients is an appealing theoretical contribution.

Significance and practical impact: The method trades expensive full-gradient computations for many more stochastic-gradient steps. This is valuable when full gradients are much costlier than stochastic gradients and when constraints or memory considerations make SAG/SDCA less suitable. The high-probability guarantee is also stronger than the in-expectation rates typical of SAG/SDCA.

However, the practical benefits in common ML settings are not yet convincing. First, the stochastic-gradient complexity scales as O(κ^2 log(1/ε)), which can be large for ill-conditioned problems. Second, in many ML problems strong convexity is induced by L2 regularization; depending on how λ scales with dataset size and regularization strength, the regime in which EMGD improves wall-clock time over accelerated full-gradient methods may be narrow. As noted in the paper’s comparisons, for finite-sum problems Nesterov’s method is preferable unless κ ≤ n2/3, while SAG/SDCA can dominate for broader regimes. The paper would benefit from a careful, quantitative comparison to batch (accelerated) gradient descent under realistic parameterizations (e.g., logistic regression with bounded data norm), including when EMGD’s O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient passes outweigh its O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic passes. A discussion of wall-clock trade-offs and empirical validation would substantially strengthen the case for significance.

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous analysis with a novel mixed-gradient scheme.
- Condition-number-independent number of full-gradient evaluations.
- High-probability linear convergence under general stochastic oracles.
- Handles constrained problems; low memory footprint compared to SAG/SDCA.

Cons:
- O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient complexity may be large; unclear practical win over accelerated methods in typical ML regimes.
- Requires knowledge (or estimation) of κ to set T and step sizes.
- Inconsistency in stated stochastic complexity (κ vs κ^2) and several notational issues.
- Limited discussion/justification of relevance to common ML settings; no empirical evidence presented.

Suggestions for the authors:
- Reconcile the κ vs κ^2 stochastic complexity throughout and emphasize the true trade-off.
- Provide a principled comparison to accelerated full-gradient methods, including concrete thresholds in terms of n, κ, and relative costs of stochastic vs full gradients; include experiments on standard datasets.
- Discuss strategies to estimate κ and ∆1 in practice.
- Address the minor notational/clarity issues listed above, and explicitly state L ≥ λ and κ = L/λ.

Overall recommendation: A theoretically solid and original contribution, but the practical significance for mainstream ML optimization needs stronger justification and clearer presentation. I lean borderline; acceptance would be strengthened by fixing the inconsistencies and adding convincing empirical/quantitative comparisons to batch methods.
