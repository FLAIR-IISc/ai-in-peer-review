## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes a novel hybrid optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to mitigate the computational cost of full gradient evaluations in strongly convex optimization problems. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient steps within epochs, aiming for a convergence rate of O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The authors provide a theoretical analysis supporting this convergence claim. 

The work builds on existing literature in both deterministic and stochastic gradient descent, acknowledging prior advancements like Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA. The algorithm’s structure borrows from epoch gradient descent, adapting it to incorporate stochastic gradient information. The presented convergence proof appears technically sound and demonstrates a novel combination of techniques. Several minor technical corrections are noted (e.g., equation (6), specification of the function in line 288, removal of equality in line 286, absolute values to norms in (13), explicitly stating L ≥ λ, corrections in (4) and precise condition number definition), which the authors should address for clarity and rigor.

However, the central concern lies in the practical relevance of EMGD to modern machine learning. The analysis reveals that EMGD achieves a benefit over standard batch gradient descent *only* when the problem size N satisfies N = O(C³/L³), where C is a constant related to regularization. This is a rather restrictive condition, implying the method is unlikely to outperform batch GD in scenarios commonly encountered in large-scale machine learning. Furthermore, the convergence guarantee is high-probability only, while batch GD offers deterministic convergence. The authors need to thoroughly discuss this limitation, comparing EMGD’s performance against batch GD across a wider range of condition numbers and problem dimensions to convincingly justify any practical advantage. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel hybrid approach combining stochastic and full gradients.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and convergence proof.
*   Clear presentation of the algorithm and its underlying ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited practical relevance due to the restrictive condition for outperforming batch GD.
*   High-probability convergence, contrasting with batch GD's deterministic guarantee.
*   Insufficient discussion of the method’s performance in realistic ML settings.

**Overall:** While the paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution, its practical significance in the context of machine learning is questionable. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should significantly expand the discussion comparing EMGD to batch GD and address the restrictive condition for performance gains to demonstrate its potential value. Addressing the minor technical points would also be crucial.
