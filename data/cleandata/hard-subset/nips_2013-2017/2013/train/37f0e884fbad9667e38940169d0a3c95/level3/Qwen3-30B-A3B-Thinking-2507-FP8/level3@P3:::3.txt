

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel hybrid optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence for smooth strongly convex problems. The authors claim that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution using only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, removing the condition number dependence that appears in standard batch gradient descent (which requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients).

The paper builds upon existing work in convex optimization, particularly drawing from epoch gradient descent [9] and stochastic optimization methods like SAG [22] and SDCA [23]. The key innovation lies in the "mixed gradient descent" step that combines full and stochastic gradients to maintain linear convergence while reducing full gradient evaluations. The authors provide a high-probability convergence analysis showing that EMGD achieves the claimed complexity.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis is sound, with the convergence proof appearing correct (aside from minor errors noted below). The high-probability convergence guarantee is stronger than the expectation-based guarantees of SAG and SDCA.
- The algorithm is more general than SAG and SDCA, working for both constrained and unconstrained problems.
- The storage complexity (O(d) vs O(n) for SAG/SDCA) is a practical advantage for high-dimensional problems.
- The paper provides a clear comparison to related work, particularly in Table 2, which quantifies computational complexities.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant concern is the practical relevance to machine learning. In standard ML problems, we typically have regularized optimization problems where the strong convexity parameter λ is proportional to the regularization parameter τ (λ ≈ τ), and the smoothness parameter L is bounded by the data (L = O(1) for normalized features). This gives κ = L/λ = O(1/τ). For EMGD to offer a computational advantage over batch gradient descent, the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3) must hold. However, since κ = O(1/τ), this requires τ ≥ Ω(n^(-2/3)), which is a very restrictive regime. In practice, τ is often chosen to be much smaller (e.g., τ = O(1/n)), making κ very large (κ = O(n)), where EMGD would actually require more full gradient evaluations than batch GD. The paper fails to address this critical issue in ML settings, which significantly undermines the practical motivation.

The convergence is high-probability (1-mδ) while batch gradient descent guarantees deterministic convergence. The paper does not adequately address this trade-off or demonstrate when the high-probability guarantee is worth the computational cost.

Several minor technical errors need correction:
- Equation (6) should read ||w* - ŵ||² instead of ||w* - w||².
- Line 288 should specify which function is used for equation (7).
- The equality in line 286 should be removed for clarity.
- Absolute values in equation (13) should be replaced with norms.
- The condition L ≥ λ should be explicitly stated, even if obvious.
- In equation (4), x^ should be ŵ and f should be F; the first term in the max is always larger by Lemma 1.
- The condition number should be precisely defined as κ = L/λ.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a novel algorithmic approach with solid theoretical foundations. However, the practical relevance to machine learning is questionable due to the restrictive conditions required for computational advantage. The authors do not sufficiently justify why this method would be preferable to standard batch gradient descent in typical ML settings, where the condition number is typically large (κ = O(n)).

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1) A thorough discussion of the practical regime where EMGD outperforms batch GD in ML settings (which appears to be very limited).
2) A detailed comparison showing concrete computational savings in realistic ML problems.
3) Addressing the high-probability vs deterministic convergence trade-off.

While the algorithm is theoretically interesting, its practical significance for machine learning is unclear. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the conditions under which EMGD provides advantages, rather than simply claiming "O(log(1/ε)) full gradients" without context.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a novel technical contribution with sound theoretical analysis, but the practical relevance to machine learning is questionable. I recommend rejection due to the lack of justification for the method's practical advantages in standard ML settings, which is a critical requirement for NIPS. The authors should address the major concerns about ML relevance before reconsideration.
