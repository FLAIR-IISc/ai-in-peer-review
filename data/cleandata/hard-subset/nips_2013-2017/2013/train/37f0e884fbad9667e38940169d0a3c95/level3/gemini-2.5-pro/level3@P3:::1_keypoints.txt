- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): use one full gradient per epoch plus many stochastic steps to make full-gradient complexity independent of the condition number κ.  
- Main theoretical result: EMGD achieves ε-optimality with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, with linear convergence.  
- Novelty and elegance: simple, new hybrid idea combining deterministic and stochastic gradients to decouple full-gradient cost from κ.  
- Strong theoretical guarantees: high-probability linear convergence (stronger than in-expectation results of comparable methods).  
- Generality and simplicity: handles constrained problems (with efficient projection) and offers a simple, self-contained convergence proof.  
- Practical advantages: lower memory (O(d) vs O(n) for SAG) and amenable to parallel full-gradient computation.  
- Major weakness — no experiments: purely theoretical paper lacks empirical validation and practical comparisons to SAG, SCDA, accelerated GD.  
- Practicality concerns: epoch length T depends on κ (rarely known), making the method hard to use out-of-the-box; authors only defer κ estimation to future work.  
- Lack of adaptivity: fixed step-size η; paper should discuss/adopt adaptive schemes or heuristics used in practice.  
- Incomplete related work comparison: should cite and compare to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and give a more detailed comparison to SAG/SCDA, including regime-specific trade-offs.  
- Need clearer discussion of regimes where EMGD is preferable (dependence on n, κ, and oracle costs) and expand footnote discussions into main text.  
- Presentation issues: proofs need more high-level intuition; table numbering and assumptions need fixing; notation (W vs X) and log/ln should be standardized.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound and interesting; recommend acceptance conditional on substantial revisions — add experiments, expand limitations/related-work discussion, and clarify practical applicability.
