Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid first-order method for smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives that can query both a full-gradient oracle and a stochastic function oracle. Each epoch computes one full gradient at an anchor point and then performs T projected “mixed-gradient” steps in a shrinking ball centered at the anchor, where each step uses a stochastic-gradient correction relative to the anchor. The analysis establishes linear convergence with high probability using O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations, where κ = L/λ.

The work sits between deterministic accelerated methods (e.g., Nesterov, O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients) and finite-sum/semi-stochastic methods (e.g., SAG/SDCA with linear rates under additional structure and memory). Compared with SAG/SDCA, EMGD applies to constrained settings and requires only O(d) memory.

Strengths:
- Technical novelty: elegant “mixed-gradient” update that decomposes the step into a deterministic anchor gradient and a bounded stochastic correction, combined with a shrinking trust region, yielding linear convergence with high probability.
- Clear theoretical target: reducing the number of expensive full gradients to O(log(1/ε)) while offloading most work to cheaper stochastic gradients.
- Proof techniques are sound and, to my reading, correct aside from minor issues enumerated below. The high-probability linear rate is a nice feature.
- Broad applicability: works with constraints and does not need finite-sum structure beyond access to a stochastic function oracle; low memory footprint.

Weaknesses and major concerns:
- Relevance for typical ML regimes is unclear. In regularized ERM, strong convexity is usually from ℓ2-regularization; in practice λ is tuned to decrease with sample size (roughly λ = Θ(1/N)), while L often does not grow with N. Consequently κ ≈ L/λ grows with N, and EMGD’s O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient complexity becomes O(N^2), which can swamp the O(log(1/ε)) savings in full-gradient calls. Under a standard cost model where a full gradient costs Θ(N) stochastic gradients, EMGD outperforms batch/accelerated GD only in a narrow, arguably uninteresting regime (roughly N = O(C^3/L^3) under the assumptions outlined in the paper’s comparisons). The paper needs a careful, quantitative comparison to batch/accelerated GD under realistic ML scaling of λ and L to justify practical advantage.
- The guarantee is high-probability; batch (accelerated) gradient descent has deterministic linear convergence. This should be stated prominently in the main theorem and discussed as a trade-off.
- The paper’s narrative intermittently suggests O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients; Theorem 1 and the detailed proof clearly yield O(κ^2 log(1/ε)). Please reconcile and consistently state the κ^2 dependence.
- No empirical validation to demonstrate regimes where EMGD is advantageous in wall-clock time relative to AGD/SAG/SDCA, nor sensitivity to λ, L, and N.

Quality:
- Technical content is solid; the main argument is novel and well-executed. Assumptions are standard. Minor proof/notation issues should be fixed (see below).
- The cost model and practical relevance analysis are incomplete. A more careful accounting versus AGD (deterministic), SAG/SDCA (finite-sum), and semi-stochastic methods is needed, including the κ^2 factor and the impact of λ scaling with N.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized; the algorithm and proof roadmap are clear. However, several notational slips and equation references reduce readability (see minor comments). The comparisons section should more transparently derive the crossover conditions versus AGD under a stated cost model.

Originality:
- The mixed-gradient idea with a shrinking local constraint and the high-probability linear convergence bound with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients appear new. The result complements deterministic and finite-sum methods.

Significance:
- Theoretically interesting due to the κ-independent full-gradient complexity and high-probability linear rate. Practical significance hinges on demonstrating clear advantages in realistic ML regimes where λ typically decreases with N; as written, this remains uncertain.

Major recommendations for revision:
- Provide a clean, quantitative comparison to AGD under a cost model where a full gradient costs N stochastic gradients. Derive and plot the crossover as a function of κ, N, and ε, explicitly incorporating κ^2 for EMGD.
- Discuss the effect of λ chosen via regularization in ML (often λ ∝ 1/N), which implies κ ∝ N, and analyze whether EMGD still saves work.
- Empirical evaluation: compare EMGD to AGD, SAG, SDCA on synthetic and real datasets, varying N and λ. Report full-gradient counts, stochastic-gradient counts, and wall-clock time. Include constrained cases to highlight EMGD’s scope.
- Clarify the high-probability nature of the bound versus deterministic AGD, and state δ, union bounds across epochs, and practical choices.

Minor comments and corrections:
- Equation (6): when instantiated in Sec. 4.1, the inequality should involve ||w* − ŵ||^2 (using ŵ for the epoch average) to match the notation there.
- For (7) in Sec. 4.2, specify explicitly that the function is F and that the condition comes from strong convexity of F and the definition of Δ.
- Remove the equality sign in the chain around line 286 (the step labeled (5), (6)); it obscures the inequality flow.
- In the bound on Vt (currently written with absolute values), replace absolute values with norms consistently; e.g., use ||·|| for vector magnitudes.
- Explicitly explain why L ≥ λ from (3) and (4); it is stated but could be justified in a sentence for completeness.
- In (4) and the surrounding text, ensure x̂ is consistently written as ŵ; replace f with F where the full objective is intended. Note that by Lemma 1, the first term in the max in (14) (the proximal quadratic) dominates.
- Precisely define κ = L/λ once and use consistently; avoid mixing μ and λ. If μ is used, map μ ≡ λ.
- Typo in Sec. 3.2: “epoches” → “epochs.”
- Algorithm 1: clarify that the projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) is assumed efficient; otherwise discuss alternatives (e.g., unconstrained case).
- Check consistency between the abstract (κ vs κ^2 dependence) and Theorem 1; update the abstract if needed.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel hybrid method with a clean, correct, and interesting analysis.
- High-probability linear rate with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients; handles constraints; low memory.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical advantage over batch/accelerated GD in ML is unconvincing given κ^2 stochastic-gradient dependence and typical λ scaling with N.
- High-probability (not deterministic) guarantee.
- Missing empirical evidence and an in-depth, quantitative comparison to AGD/SAG/SDCA under realistic cost models.

Overall recommendation:
A theoretically solid and original contribution, but the ML relevance is currently undersubstantiated. I would support acceptance if the authors add a careful comparison to batch GD/AGD (both analysis and experiments) and reconcile the κ^2 dependence claim; otherwise, I lean toward rejection on significance grounds for NeurIPS.
