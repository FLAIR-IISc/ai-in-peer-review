- Paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), mixing full and stochastic gradients to speed smooth, strongly convex optimization.  
- EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations (κ-independent) and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations.  
- Paper is well-situated in convex optimization literature and compares to accelerated GD, SGD, SAG, and SDCA.  

Strengths:
- Technical soundness: algorithm is clearly presented and the convergence proof appears rigorous with linear rate.  
- Novelty: systematic epoch-based mixing of full and stochastic gradients is a new contribution.  
- Significance: potential to reduce cost for ill-conditioned, large-scale problems where full gradients are expensive.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written and organized.  
- Stronger guarantee: convergence holds with high probability, unlike SAG’s in-expectation guarantee.  

Weaknesses:
- No empirical validation: complete absence of experiments to assess practical performance and comparisons.  
- Parameter sensitivity: requires knowing condition number κ a priori to choose epoch length, a practical limitation.  
- Step-size and adaptivity: fixed step-size (η = 1/[L √T]) seems inflexible; lacks adaptive strategies used in practice.  
- Limited discussion of drawbacks: paper doesn’t fully address scenarios where EMGD may be non-competitive.  
- Related work omission: should acknowledge and compare to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) hybrid methods.  
- Clarification needed: Table 1 assumptions and the gradient expectation identity used in the proof require clearer explanation.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, theoretically sound algorithm with potential efficiency benefits.  
- Rigorous convergence analysis providing linear convergence guarantees.  
- Addresses an important optimization problem relevant to ML.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of experiments undermines assessment of practical relevance.  
- Practical limitations in parameter tuning (κ) and lack of adaptive step-size raise robustness concerns.  
- Insufficient discussion/comparison leaves the evaluation incomplete.  

Overall assessment:
- Promising theoretical contribution but practical impact is uncertain due to missing experiments; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Required revisions: include experiments, detail condition-number estimation/practical tuning, and expand comparisons to related work.
