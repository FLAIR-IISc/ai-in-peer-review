Paper proposes EMGD: an epoch-based hybrid algorithm that computes one full gradient per epoch and uses a mixed variance-reduced stochastic update within epochs.  
Theoretical claim: EMGD attains ε-accuracy with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, making full-gradient complexity independent of κ.  
Strength — important problem: targets reducing costly full-gradient computations in ill-conditioned large-scale optimization.  
Strength — novelty and theory: EMGD’s epoch/mixed-gradient design is novel and yields a high-probability linear-convergence result (in outer iterations).  
Strength — clear trade-off: replaces √κ dependence in full gradients with κ² dependence in cheaper stochastic gradients per epoch.  
Weakness — unrealistic assumption: requires knowing κ and L a priori to set η and inner iteration count T, with no practical procedure given.  
Weakness — no experiments: entirely theoretical, so practical performance, sensitivity to mis-specified κ, and empirical comparisons are untested.  
Weakness — insufficient related-work comparison: brief treatment of hybrid/semi-stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt) and limited discussion of connections to other variance-reduction techniques.  
Weakness — ambiguous problem setting: paper mixes stochastic approximation and finite-sum contexts, making comparisons to SAG/SDCA and claims unclear.  
Pros for acceptance: addresses a significant problem, proposes a novel algorithm, and presents a compelling theoretical result.  
Cons for rejection: impractical κ assumption, lack of empirical validation, weak comparative discussion, and unclear target setting.  
Recommendation: reject as-is but encourage revision to remove dependence on known κ (or provide estimation/robust tuning) and to include thorough experiments and clearer comparisons.
