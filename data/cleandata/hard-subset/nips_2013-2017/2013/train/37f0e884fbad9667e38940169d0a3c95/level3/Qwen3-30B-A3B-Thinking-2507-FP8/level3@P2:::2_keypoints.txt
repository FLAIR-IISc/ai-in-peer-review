- Proposes EMGD, a hybrid first-order method mixing full and stochastic gradients to reduce full-gradient evaluations for smooth strongly convex optimization.
- Claims O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient complexity, removing √κ dependence for full-gradient accesses.
- Builds on established convex optimization literature and targets ill-conditioned problems where full gradients are expensive.
- Theoretical analysis and proof of Theorem 1 are technically sound but rely on knowing the condition number κ exactly.
- Critical assumption that κ is known a priori is not addressed; no procedure or analysis for unknown or misspecified κ is provided.
- No experiments are included to validate theoretical claims or study empirical robustness to κ misspecification, a major practical weakness.
- Section 3.1 is misleading about the problem setting, presenting a general smooth strongly convex problem while later comparing to finite-sum methods.
- The relation between the mixed-gradient update and simpler interleaving of deterministic and stochastic steps is not clearly explained, obscuring novelty.
- Originality is overstated: prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012; Marti & Fuchs 1978) are not properly compared or differentiated.
- Significance is limited by the requirement to know κ, lack of empirical comparisons to SGD/SAG/SDCA, and omission of non-strongly convex settings common in practice.
- Arguments for acceptance: interesting theoretical contribution eliminating condition-number dependence for full-gradient calls and lower storage requirements than SAG/SDCA; works for constrained and unconstrained problems.
- Arguments against acceptance: unaddressed κ assumption, no experimental validation, inadequate contextualization with prior hybrid methods, and unclear problem specification.
- Recommendation: reject due to practical limitations, missing experiments, and insufficient contextualization.
- Suggestions for improvement: provide a κ-estimation procedure or analyze misspecification, add empirical comparisons on benchmarks, properly situate work among prior hybrid methods, clarify the finite-sum vs general setting, and analyze non-strongly convex cases.
