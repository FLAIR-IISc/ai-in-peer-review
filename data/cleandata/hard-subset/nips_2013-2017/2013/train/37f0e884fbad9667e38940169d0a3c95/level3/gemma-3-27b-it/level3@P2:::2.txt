This paper proposes a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ when solving smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient evaluations, aiming for an iteration complexity of O(log(1/ε)) – independent of κ – using O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations.

The problem addressed is important. Ill-conditioned problems frequently arise in machine learning and engineering, leading to slow convergence of traditional full-gradient methods. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between iteration count and per-iteration cost when employing stochastic gradients. EMGD attempts to exploit the benefits of both approaches. The presentation is reasonably clear, laying out the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees. The connections to existing literature on convex optimization and stochastic gradient methods are generally made, though some key related work is missed (see below).

However, the paper suffers from significant limitations regarding practicality and scope. The most substantial weakness is the strong assumption that the condition number κ is known *a priori*. While the authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, the lack of any discussion of strategies for estimating κ or handling misspecification severely limits the applicability of the algorithm. Without a means to estimate κ, the algorithm’s performance is highly sensitive to this parameter, and the theoretical guarantees become vacuous. A sensitivity analysis, or even a discussion of how performance degrades with incorrect κ, is missing. Specifically, how does the algorithm behave if η (which depends on κ) is set too large or too small? 

The comparison to related work is incomplete. The authors mention semi-stochastic methods and recent work on finite-sum optimization (SAG, SDCA), but notably fail to discuss or compare to earlier hybrid deterministic-stochastic approaches such as the work of Friedlander and Schmidt (2012). This omission is significant, as it prevents a clear positioning of the contribution relative to existing techniques. Furthermore, the paper lacks any experimental validation. The absence of experiments is a major drawback, as it prevents assessing the empirical performance of EMGD and validating the theoretical results. Comparing EMGD to standard baselines like SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, and SDCA in a controlled experimental setting would be crucial.

The algorithm’s connection to simpler approaches is also not adequately explored. The “mixed” gradient descent step seems largely equivalent to interleaving deterministic and stochastic gradient descent, yet the paper doesn’t delve into this connection. A deeper discussion of the advantages of this specific mixing strategy compared to more straightforward interleaving would be valuable. 

The treatment of the underlying optimization problem setting is also somewhat unclear. Section 3.1 outlines oracles for full and stochastic gradients but doesn't explicitly state whether the problem is a finite-sum deterministic optimization (F(w) = 1/n ∑ fi(w)) or a general stochastic approximation problem (F(w) = E[f(w)]). This distinction is important, as the theoretical guarantees and comparisons to other algorithms differ.

Finally, the paper focuses exclusively on strongly convex problems. While this simplification allows for stronger theoretical results, it limits the applicability of the algorithm in practice, where non-strongly convex settings are common. Investigating extensions to handle plain convex functions, or at least acknowledging this limitation, would broaden the impact of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of full and stochastic gradient descent.
*   Clear theoretical analysis demonstrating a logarithmic convergence rate.
*   Addresses an important problem in optimization, particularly for ill-conditioned problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Crucially relies on the *a priori* knowledge of the condition number κ, which is unrealistic in many applications.
*   Lacks any discussion or analysis of the algorithm's behavior with misspecified κ.
*   No experimental validation to support the theoretical results.
*   Incomplete comparison to related work, missing key references.
*   Limited scope, focusing only on strongly convex problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the theoretical contribution of this paper is potentially interesting, the strong assumptions and lack of experimental validation significantly limit its practical relevance. The reliance on knowing κ *a priori* is a major shortcoming, and the absence of any discussion of how to address this issue weakens the overall contribution. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the identified weaknesses, particularly by investigating methods for estimating κ or handling misspecification, and providing experimental results demonstrating the algorithm's performance on relevant benchmarks.
