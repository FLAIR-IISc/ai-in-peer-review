

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel hybrid optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradient oracles for smooth strongly convex optimization. The main theoretical claim is that EMGD achieves high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, eliminating the √κ dependence in the iteration complexity.

The paper demonstrates a theoretically interesting approach to reducing full gradient computations by leveraging stochastic gradients. The algorithm design is novel in its "mixed gradient descent" update, and the high-probability convergence guarantee is an important theoretical contribution compared to expected convergence rates in prior work. The authors correctly identify the practical problem of ill-conditioned problems requiring excessive full gradient computations.

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses. The theoretical result critically depends on knowing the condition number κ a priori, with no analysis or guidance for cases where κ is unknown or misspecified. The algorithm requires hyperparameter η that depends on κ, but no estimation procedure or heuristics are provided. The paper contains no experiments to validate the theory, study empirical behavior under misspecification, or compare against standard baselines (SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SDCA, etc.). The paper also fails to review and compare to prior hybrid deterministic-stochastic work (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012), and neglects to consider non-strongly convex settings, which are common in practice.

The optimization problem setting is confusingly described, with Section 3.1 being misleading about the oracle assumptions. The algorithm is presented as a "mixed" update but lacks discussion relating it to simple interleaving of deterministic and stochastic steps. The paper claims linear convergence but only demonstrates high-probability convergence, not the expected rate that would be more relevant for practitioners.

While the theoretical idea is interesting, the lack of practical considerations, empirical validation, and comparison to existing methods significantly limits the paper's contribution. The critical dependence on knowing κ a priori makes the method impractical for most real-world applications, and the absence of experiments to demonstrate the algorithm's behavior when κ is misspecified is a major oversight. The paper would benefit from addressing these issues to be considered for publication.
