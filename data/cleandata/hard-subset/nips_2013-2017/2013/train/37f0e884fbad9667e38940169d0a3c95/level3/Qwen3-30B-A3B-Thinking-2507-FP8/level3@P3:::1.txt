

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes the Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm for smooth strongly-convex optimization, addressing a gap in the literature regarding condition number dependence in gradient-based methods. The authors aim to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations required for convergence by strategically combining full and stochastic gradients. The key innovation is that EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, making the full gradient count independent of the condition number κ.

The paper builds on prior work in both deterministic (Nesterov's accelerated methods) and stochastic (SAG, SDCA) optimization. While SAG and SDCA achieve linear convergence for sum-of-functions problems with O(n log(1/ε)) complexity, they require Ω(n) storage and only provide convergence in expectation. EMGD, by contrast, handles both constrained and unconstrained problems with high-probability guarantees, requiring only Ω(d) storage (where d is the dimension). The authors correctly note that EMGD's theoretical complexity is comparable to SAG/SDCA when κ ≤ n^(1/2), but better when κ ≤ n^(2/3).

**Strengths:** The paper's theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified. The high-probability convergence guarantee is a significant improvement over SAG's expectation-based result, providing stronger theoretical assurances. The proof is notably simple and self-contained, a welcome contrast to the complex analyses common in optimization literature. The algorithm's applicability to constrained problems expands the scope beyond SAG/SDCA, which is particularly valuable for practical applications. The authors also correctly identify potential parallelization advantages through distributed computation of the full gradient.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from several critical omissions. Most notably, it lacks empirical validation or concrete examples demonstrating the algorithm's practical relevance. Without experimental results, it's difficult to assess whether the theoretical advantages translate to real-world performance gains. The paper also fails to adequately address limitations: the requirement for a priori knowledge of κ (making epoch length fixed) is a significant practical drawback compared to adaptive methods like SCDA and SAG. The absence of automatic step-size mechanisms, common in practical implementations, further limits its applicability.

The discussion of practical relevance is insufficient. The authors acknowledge that in typical machine learning settings with regularization ~ C/n (where κ ≈ n/C'), EMGD may not outperform SAG or Nesterov. They should explicitly identify realistic problem settings where EMGD would provide tangible benefits, rather than merely stating theoretical complexity comparisons. The comparison to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), who proposed a hybrid deterministic-stochastic method, is missing, which is a significant oversight.

The manuscript also has clarity issues. The proof intuition is not adequately explained, making it difficult to understand why the specific choices (like the mixed gradient construction) work. Table 1's citations and assumptions require clarification, particularly regarding SCDA's applicability and the gradient expectation identity. Notation inconsistencies (e.g., log vs ln, domain notation) need standardization throughout.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Theoretical contribution is novel and significant
- Simple, self-contained convergence proof
- High-probability convergence guarantee is stronger than SAG's
- Works for constrained optimization (unlike SAG/SDCA)
- Potential memory/parallelization advantages

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- No empirical validation to support theoretical claims
- Critical practical drawback (requires known κ) not adequately addressed
- Insufficient discussion of realistic scenarios where EMGD outperforms alternatives
- Missing comparison to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012)
- Lack of adaptive step-size mechanisms

**Overall verdict:** The paper makes a technically sound and theoretically significant contribution to optimization theory. The EMGD algorithm is novel, the proof is elegant, and the high-probability guarantee is a notable advancement. However, the lack of empirical validation and insufficient discussion of practical limitations significantly weaken the paper's impact. The authors should add experiments demonstrating the algorithm's behavior in realistic settings and address the identified shortcomings in their rebuttal. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, the paper's theoretical value is clear, but its practical relevance remains unproven, making it a borderline case for acceptance at NIPS.
