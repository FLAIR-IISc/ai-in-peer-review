- Proposes a novel hybrid deterministic–stochastic first-order method called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth, strongly convex optimization.
- Main theoretical claim: EMGD achieves high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence given a known condition number κ and a hybrid oracle.
- Analysis shows EMGD can reduce the number of full gradients from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) while trading off stochastic gradients.
- Strength: The algorithm combines strengths of deterministic and stochastic gradient methods in a novel way.
- Strength: Provides a thorough convergence-rate analysis and insight into trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients.
- Strength: Includes a detailed comparison showing EMGD can outperform existing algorithms in certain scenarios.
- Weakness: Analysis critically assumes the condition number κ is known a priori, which is unrealistic in many applications.
- Weakness: No analysis or guidance is provided for cases where κ is unknown or misspecified.
- Weakness: The algorithm requires a hyperparameter η that depends on κ, but no estimation procedure or heuristics are given.
- Weakness: No experiments are included to validate the theory or to study empirical behavior under misspecification.
- Weakness: Does not consider non-strongly convex settings, limiting practical applicability.
- Weakness: Fails to review or compare to prior hybrid deterministic–stochastic work.
- Weakness: Lacks empirical comparisons to standard baselines (SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SDCA, etc.).
- Suggestion: Provide analysis of algorithm behavior when κ is unknown or misspecified.
- Suggestion: Develop an estimation procedure or heuristics for the hyperparameter η.
- Suggestion: Add experiments to validate theoretical claims and examine robustness to misspecification.
- Suggestion: Extend or discuss applicability to non-strongly convex settings and compare to prior work there.
- Suggestion: Include empirical comparisons against standard baselines and prior hybrid methods.
- Argument for acceptance: Novel algorithm with thorough theoretical analysis and potential efficiency gains in reducing full-gradient calls.
- Argument against acceptance: Key practical issues (unknown κ, η tuning, no experiments, limited comparisons) undermine applicability and empirical credibility.
