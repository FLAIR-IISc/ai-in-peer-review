- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that mixes full and stochastic gradients to reduce full-gradient evaluations for smooth strongly-convex optimization.  
- Claims linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (condition-number independent) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.  
- Builds on deterministic (Nesterov) and stochastic (SAG, SDCA) literature and compares theoretical complexity to these methods.  
- EMGD provides high-probability convergence guarantees versus SAG’s expectation-based result.  
- Handles constrained and unconstrained problems, expanding applicability beyond SAG/SDCA.  
- Requires only O(d) storage, potentially better memory use than SAG/SDCA (Ω(n) storage).  
- Proof is simple, self-contained, and theoretically well-justified.  
- Identifies potential parallelization benefits via distributed computation of the full gradient.  
- Lacks empirical validation or experiments to demonstrate practical performance and relevance.  
- Requires a priori knowledge of the condition number κ, fixing epoch lengths and limiting practical applicability.  
- Does not include adaptive step-size mechanisms common in practical implementations.  
- Insufficient discussion of realistic problem settings where EMGD would outperform alternatives (e.g., typical ML regularization regimes).  
- Missing comparison to relevant hybrid method by Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), a significant oversight.  
- Proof intuition and justification for algorithmic design choices (e.g., mixed gradient construction) are inadequately explained.  
- Clarity and presentation issues: inconsistent notation (log vs ln, domain notation), unclear Table 1 citations/assumptions, and ambiguous statements about SCDA applicability and gradient expectations.  
- Overall verdict: technically novel and strong with elegant proof and stronger guarantees, but practical impact is unproven due to missing experiments and unresolved practical limitations, making it borderline for acceptance.
