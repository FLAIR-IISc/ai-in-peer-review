- Paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid of full and stochastic gradients claiming linear convergence for smooth strongly convex problems.
- Claims EMGD finds ε-optimal solutions using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, removing condition-number dependence of standard batch GD.
- Builds on epoch gradient descent and stochastic methods (SAG, SDCA) with a novel mixed gradient descent step and high-probability convergence analysis.
- Theoretical analysis and convergence proof are largely sound and provide stronger high-probability guarantees than expectation-based SAG/SDCA.
- Algorithm is more general (works for constrained and unconstrained problems) and has lower storage complexity O(d) vs O(n) for SAG/SDCA.
- Paper includes a clear comparison to related work, quantifying computational complexities (e.g., Table 2).
- Major practical concern: in typical ML regularized problems λ ≈ τ and L = O(1), so κ = O(1/τ) is often large, making EMGD advantageous only when κ ≤ n^(2/3), a restrictive regime.
- For common choices τ = O(1/n) (hence κ = O(n)), EMGD would require more full gradient evaluations than batch GD, undermining ML relevance.
- The paper does not adequately address the high-probability vs deterministic convergence trade-off compared to batch GD.
- Minor technical errors to correct: Eq.(6) should be ||w* - ŵ||²; clarify function used in line 288 for Eq.(7); remove equality in line 286; replace absolute values in Eq.(13) with norms.
- Additional fixes: explicitly state L ≥ λ, correct notation in Eq.(4) (x^ → ŵ, f → F), and define κ precisely as κ = L/λ.
- Overall assessment: novel and theoretically interesting but practical significance for ML is unclear due to restrictive conditions for advantage over batch GD.
- Suggested improvements: discuss practical regimes where EMGD outperforms batch GD, provide concrete computational comparisons on realistic ML problems, and address the high-probability vs deterministic trade-off.
- Recommendation: reject — sound theory but insufficient justification of practical ML advantages; major concerns about ML relevance must be addressed before reconsideration.
