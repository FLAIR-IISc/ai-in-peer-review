- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) combining full and stochastic gradients to reduce dependence on condition number κ.
- Claims O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls with linear convergence and high-probability guarantee.
- Technically sound algorithm with rigorous, though dense, theoretical analysis and apparently complete proofs.
- Novel mixing of full and stochastic gradients for constrained smooth strongly-convex problems.
- Theoretical significance: condition-number independence for full-gradient evaluations and stronger high-probability guarantees than some prior work.
- Advantages over SAG: applicability to constrained problems, potential memory/parallelization benefits, and a simpler proof.
- Table 2 offers a clear, if limited, comparison of computational complexities.
- Major weakness: complete absence of experiments or empirical validation to assess practical relevance.
- Section 3.4 “Comparisons” is underdeveloped and needs deeper comparisons to SAG and SDCA.
- Practical limitation: requires pre-specifying epoch length k, reducing usability in real-world settings.
- Lacks adaptive step-size mechanisms common in competing methods, making step-size selection unclear.
- Paper does not discuss regimes where EMGD may not be advantageous, e.g., small regularization (C/n) settings.
- Omits related work: should cite and compare to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods.
- Clarity issues: needs better high-level intuition for proof choices, clearer citations/assumptions in tables, and consistent notation (domain, norms, log/ln).
- Suggest expanding Section 3.4 to include memory, parallelization, and implementation comparisons with SAG/SDCA.
- Suggest explicitly stating limitations (need to know k, potential poor performance in some regimes).
- Suggest discussing heuristic or practical strategies for step-size selection.
- Suggest adding Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) to related work and clarifying notation throughout.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound algorithm with significant condition-number independence result and seemingly correct proof.
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of experiments, significant practical limitations (fixed epoch length, no adaptivity), and incomplete discussion/comparisons.
- Overall verdict: technically solid and valuable contribution but recommend acceptance only if authors add experiments and address practical and discussion shortcomings.
