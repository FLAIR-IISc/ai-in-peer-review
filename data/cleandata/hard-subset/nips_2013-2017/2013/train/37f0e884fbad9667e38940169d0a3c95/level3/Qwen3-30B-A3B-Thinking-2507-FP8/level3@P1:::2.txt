

The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid optimization algorithm for smooth strongly convex problems that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve O(log(1/ε)) full gradient access complexity (independent of condition number κ), while requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This contrasts with standard full-gradient methods (O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity) and purely stochastic methods (O(1/λε) complexity). The work builds on epoch-based methods (e.g., [9]) and leverages the observation that stochastic gradients can reduce full-gradient evaluations when κ is large, but the theoretical guarantee relies on κ being known a priori.

**Quality.** The theoretical analysis is technically sound under the assumption that κ is known, with Theorem 1 providing a high-probability linear convergence rate. However, the paper critically overlooks the practical necessity of estimating κ, which is rarely known in real-world problems. The analysis assumes κ is fixed and known for hyperparameter setting (η = 1/(L√T)), yet provides no guidance for cases where κ is misspecified or unknown. Crucially, the paper lacks empirical validation: no experiments test convergence under κ misspecification (e.g., η set too large/small), nor do they compare EMGD to standard baselines (SGD, SAG, SDCA) on practical problems. The absence of experiments undermines the claim of "computational efficiency" (Section 3.4), as the theoretical complexity reduction (κ² vs. √κ) is only meaningful if stochastic gradients are significantly cheaper than full gradients—a claim unsupported by data. The assumption that F(w) = E[f(w)] (Section 3.1) is misleadingly presented as a general setting, while the algorithm is primarily designed for finite-sum problems (Section 3.4), creating confusion about scope.

**Clarity.** The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise but the motivation for the "mixed gradient" (g̃ₖᵗ = ∇F(ŵᵏ) + ∇fₖᵗ(wₖᵗ) - ∇fₖᵗ(ŵᵏ)) is underexplained. The derivation of its bounded stochastic component (Section 4.2) is mathematically rigorous but obscures the intuitive connection to simpler hybrid approaches (e.g., interleaving full and stochastic steps). The problem setting (Section 3.1) incorrectly implies a general stochastic approximation framework, when the algorithm fundamentally relies on the finite-sum structure F(w) = (1/n)∑ᵢfᵢ(w) for meaningful complexity comparisons (Section 3.4). This misalignment reduces clarity for readers outside the finite-sum optimization niche.

**Originality.** The core idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients is not novel; Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) proposed a similar hybrid approach for finite-sum problems with comparable complexity guarantees. The paper fails to cite or discuss this work, misrepresenting its contribution as novel. While EMGD achieves a high-probability guarantee (unlike SAG/SDCA's expectation-based convergence), this is a minor theoretical refinement rather than a significant conceptual advance. The algorithm itself does not introduce a new mechanism beyond combining existing techniques.

**Significance.** The paper addresses a valid practical concern (high cost of full gradients in ill-conditioned problems), but the significance is severely limited by the κ-dependency assumption. In machine learning, κ is typically unknown and problem-specific (e.g., for regularized problems, κ = O(n/τ) [25]), yet the paper offers no estimation procedure. Without addressing this, the method remains impractical for most applications. The claimed computational advantage (κ ≤ n²/³ vs. Nesterov) is purely theoretical and unverified, as no experiments demonstrate speedups over baselines. The focus on strongly convex problems also excludes many important practical cases (e.g., non-strongly convex losses common in ML), reducing applicability.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pros:* Rigorous high-probability convergence guarantee under known κ; clear algorithmic design; useful for theoretical exploration of hybrid oracles.  
*Cons:* Critical assumption (known κ) unaddressed; no empirical validation; misses key prior work (Friedlander & Schmidt 2012); misleading problem setting; no analysis of κ misspecification; fails to compare with standard baselines (SGD, SAG, SDCA).  

The paper’s theoretical contribution is marginal due to the unresolved κ dependency and lack of empirical support. While the algorithm is well-motivated in its idealized setting, the absence of practical validation or handling of real-world assumptions (unknown κ, non-strong convexity) significantly diminishes its scientific value for NIPS. It would be a stronger contribution with experiments demonstrating robustness to κ misspecification and comparisons to state-of-the-art hybrid methods. As submitted, the work falls short of NIPS’s standards for quality and significance. Reject.
