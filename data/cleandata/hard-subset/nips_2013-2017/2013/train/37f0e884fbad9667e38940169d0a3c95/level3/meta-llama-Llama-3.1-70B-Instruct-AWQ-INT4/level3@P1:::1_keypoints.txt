- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid using full and stochastic gradients to get linear convergence for smooth, strongly convex problems.  
- EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, making full-gradient count independent of condition number.  
- Paper is well-organized with a clear introduction, related work, and algorithm description.  
- Provides detailed convergence analysis and computational-complexity comparisons with existing methods.  
- Achieves linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee, stronger than SAG’s in-expectation result.  
- EMGD handles constrained optimization, unlike SAG which is limited to unconstrained problems.  
- Proof is simpler than SAG’s, and EMGD may have memory and parallelization advantages.  
- Lacks experiments or concrete examples to demonstrate empirical performance and relevance.  
- Complexity comparison: EMGD beats Nesterov if κ ≤ n^(2/3), but SAG outperforms EMGD in many regimes and matches it when κ ≈ n^(1/2).  
- Authors should highlight realistic settings and regimes where EMGD is practically beneficial.  
- Paper omits a detailed discussion of limitations and drawbacks.  
- Epoch length must be fixed a priori, requiring knowledge of the condition number κ.  
- EMGD lacks automatic or adaptive step-size mechanisms present in SDCA and practical SAG heuristics.  
- Practical estimation of κ is a concern; authors mention Hessian estimation via subsets and concentration bounds but do not explore it.  
- Related work omission: should cite and contrast Friedlander and Schmidt (2012) hybrid deterministic-stochastic method.  
- Paper would benefit from high-level intuition for proof choices and clarification of Table 1 citations and assumptions.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and theoretically significant with a novel, simple proof, but needs experiments and expanded discussion to strengthen the paper.  
- Recommendations: add empirical experiments, discuss limitations in detail, explore κ estimation methods, and address suggested edits and related-work citations.
