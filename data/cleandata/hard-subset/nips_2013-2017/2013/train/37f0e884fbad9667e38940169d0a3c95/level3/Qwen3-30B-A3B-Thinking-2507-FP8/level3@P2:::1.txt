

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence for smooth and strongly convex problems with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ. The authors show that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, significantly reducing the full gradient dependency compared to standard methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent (which requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients).

The work builds on the epoch gradient descent framework [9] but introduces a novel "mixed gradient descent" step that combines full and stochastic gradients. This approach differs from recent stochastic methods like SAG [22] and SDCA [23] in several key ways: EMGD works for constrained optimization (unlike SAG/SDCA), requires only O(d) storage (vs. Ω(n) for SAG/SDCA), and achieves linear convergence with high probability (stronger than SAG/SDCA's expectation-based guarantee). The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with a clean convergence proof that carefully bounds the stochastic error terms.

**Strengths:**
1. Theoretical novelty: The mixed gradient approach and high-probability convergence guarantee represent a significant theoretical contribution. The proof is self-contained and elegantly handles the interplay between full and stochastic gradients.
2. Practical advantages: The constrained optimization capability, memory efficiency, and potential parallelization benefits are well-articulated and compelling.
3. Technical soundness: The convergence analysis is thorough, with precise conditions for the epoch length and step size.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper contains no experiments to demonstrate the practical benefits of EMGD. Without empirical results, it's difficult to assess whether the theoretical advantages translate to real-world performance gains.
2. **Practical limitation:** The algorithm requires knowing the condition number κ in advance, which is often not feasible in practice. This is a significant drawback compared to adaptive methods like SAG and SDCA.
3. **Limited practical regime identification:** The paper doesn't clearly identify realistic settings where EMGD would outperform existing methods. The complexity comparison (Table 2) shows EMGD only beats Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which may not be common in practical machine learning applications where regularization often leads to κ ≈ n/C.
4. **Incomplete related work:** The paper should more thoroughly contrast with Friedlander & Schmidt's hybrid deterministic-stochastic method (2012) and other recent approaches.
5. **Clarity issues:** Some aspects of the proof strategy and algorithm design lack intuitive explanation. The manuscript contains minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., log vs ln usage) and typos.

**Overall Evaluation:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The claims are well-supported by the convergence proof, though the absence of empirical validation slightly diminishes the quality assessment.

*Clarity:* The paper is generally well-organized, but could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the algorithm's design choices and proof strategy. Minor notational inconsistencies and typos should be addressed.

*Originality:* The paper presents a novel hybrid approach to optimization that significantly differs from recent SAG/SDCA work. The mixed gradient descent concept is original and well-motivated.

*Significance:* The theoretical contribution is significant for optimization theory, but the practical significance is limited by the requirement to know κ in advance and the lack of empirical validation. The paper doesn't clearly establish a compelling use case where EMGD would be preferred over existing methods.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel theoretical contribution with a clean proof showing linear convergence with high probability
- Works for both constrained and unconstrained optimization (unlike SAG/SDCA)
- Memory efficient (O(d) vs Ω(n) storage)
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed
- Potential parallelization advantages

*Con:*
- No empirical results to validate theoretical claims
- Requires knowledge of condition number κ before running the algorithm (significant practical limitation)
- Practical regime where EMGD outperforms existing methods is limited (κ ≤ n^(2/3))
- Lacks detailed comparison with related work (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012)
- Doesn't clearly establish compelling use cases for the proposed method

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to convex optimization with a novel algorithm and clean proof. However, the lack of empirical validation and the practical limitation of requiring knowledge of κ before running the algorithm significantly reduce its impact for the NIPS community. While the theoretical novelty is strong, the absence of any empirical results or clear practical justification makes it difficult to assess the method's real-world relevance.

I recommend rejection, but with a strong note that the paper could be strengthened significantly with empirical validation, a more thorough discussion of practical limitations, and clearer identification of the regimes where EMGD provides advantages. The authors should address these issues before reconsideration for a future conference.
