- Addresses optimization of smooth, strongly convex functions and aims to reduce full-gradient computations on ill-conditioned problems.
- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm using one full gradient per epoch plus variance-reduced stochastic updates within epochs.
- Main theoretical result: EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, giving condition-number-independent full-gradient complexity.
- Provides a self-contained, high-probability linear convergence proof.
- Strength: Novel hybrid approach that makes full-gradient complexity independent of κ, addressing a known bottleneck.
- Strength: Strong theoretical guarantees with high-probability linear convergence and a clear, elegant proof.
- Strength: Greater generality than SAG/SDCA—applicable to constrained problems and potentially lower memory and better parallelization.
- Weakness: No empirical validation; practical performance against Nesterov, SAG, and SDCA is untested.
- Weakness: Complexity comparison is limited and may be misleading—EMGD beats Nesterov only when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which may not hold in common L2-regularized regimes.
- Weakness: Comparison to SAG and SDCA is brief; in many regimes those methods may have better overall complexity than EMGD.
- Weakness: Practical tuning issues—inner iterations T and step size η depend on κ, which is often unknown and hard to estimate in practice.
- Weakness: Lacks discussion and citation of related hybrid deterministic-stochastic work (Friedlander & Schmidt 2012).
- Comment: Proof would benefit from more high-level intuition about the mixed gradient choice and shrinking domain role.
- Comment: Table 1 needs citations for the listed iteration complexities.
- Minor edits needed: inconsistent domain notation (W vs X), inconsistent log notation (log vs ln), and probable typos in theorem statements (e.g., "m accesses" vs "m+1").
- Summary: Theoretically strong and novel, but practical significance is unclear without experiments and a more thorough, honest comparison to existing methods; reviewer would reconsider with empirical results and expanded discussion.
