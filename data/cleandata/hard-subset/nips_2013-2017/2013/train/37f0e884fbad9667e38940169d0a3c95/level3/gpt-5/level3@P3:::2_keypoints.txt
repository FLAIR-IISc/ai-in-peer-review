- Paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): per-epoch full gradient at w̄k plus T mixed stochastic steps g̃t = ∇F(w̄k)+∇f(wt)−∇f(w̄k), claiming high-probability linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.  
- Mixed-gradient is a control-variate/variance-reduced estimator closely related to prior semi-stochastic and finite-sum methods (Marti & Fuchs, Friedlander & Schmidt, SAG/SDCA/SVRG).  
- Theoretical result is plausible but depends crucially on knowing L and λ to set T and η, making guarantees fragile in practice.  
- Paper contains an inconsistency: abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls while main theorem gives O(κ2 log(1/ε)); this must be reconciled.  
- The algorithmic novelty beyond known variance-reduced estimators is unclear; main contributions appear to be the shrinking-ball projection and a high-probability bound.  
- No experiments are provided; empirical validation (wall-clock, robustness to κ-misspecification) is essential given the practical motivation.  
- Hybrid-oracle assumption (access to both full and stochastic gradients) is strong and not always realistic; the paper should specify settings where Og is available.  
- Section 3.1 conflates expectation and finite-sum oracle models; the paper should clearly separate regimes and state which guarantees apply to each.  
- Terminology like “condition-number independent access of full gradients” is misleading because overall complexity still depends on κ via stochastic steps.  
- The relationship to simple interleaving schemes, control-variate variance reduction, and how shrinking domain compares to epoch/step-size choices in prior work is insufficiently discussed.  
- Originality is limited: one full gradient per epoch and centered estimator are standard; novelty claims should emphasize (i) high-probability guarantees, (ii) shrinking-radius projection, and (iii) O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient focus, with clear comparisons to Friedlander & Schmidt and SVRG.  
- The paper omits treatment of the merely convex (non-strongly convex) case, limiting applicability.  
- Practical impact is unclear: reducing full-gradient calls is valuable in some settings, but overall gradient work scaling with κ2 and reliance on known κ reduce practicality.  
- No discussion or analysis of robustness to misspecified κ; adaptive schemes (doubling/halving T, backtracking for L, regularization for λ) are not explored.  
- No guidance on hyperparameter selection or practical estimation of L and λ; sensitivity analysis is missing.  
- Specific experimental requests: benchmarks on least-squares and logistic regression versus SGD/ASGD/SAG/SDCA/SVRG, measuring full-gradient calls, total gradients, wall-clock time, and robustness to κ misspecification.  
- Ask for extensions/discussion to non-strongly convex settings (regularization, quadratic growth, restarts).  
- Related-work requests: add and compare to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013), and clarify differences in storage, distributed implementation, and high-probability vs in-expectation guarantees.  
- Clarity fixes requested: resolve κ vs κ2 inconsistency, make oracle models explicit, and justify the cost of projection for constrained problems.  
- Pros: clear theoretical target (reduce full-gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε))), high-probability linear convergence, and simple algorithm potentially useful in distributed settings.  
- Cons: no experiments, requires a priori knowledge of L and λ, inconsistency in stated stochastic-gradient complexity, limited comparison with related methods, and ambiguity in problem setting.  
- Recommendation: lean toward rejection; require corrected complexity statements, adaptive/robust parameter selection or analysis, thorough empirical comparisons, and clearer problem-setting treatment for a stronger submission.
