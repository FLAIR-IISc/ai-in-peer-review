- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm combining few full gradients with many cheaper stochastic gradients.  
- Operates in epochs: compute one full gradient at epoch start, then use a variance-reduced "mixed gradient" for intra-epoch updates.  
- Mixed gradient estimator: g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) − ∇fkt(w̄k), which centers stochastic updates at the epoch point.  
- Theoretical result: EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.  
- Key significance: number of full gradient evaluations is independent of condition number κ, unlike Nesterov’s O(√κ log(1/ε)).  
- Proof technique: variance bounding within a shrinking trust region and induction over epochs; convergence proof is novel and technically sound.  
- Major practical concern: claimed advantage holds only when κ ≤ n^(2/3) for finite-sum problems, which may be uncommon in ML—needs stronger justification.  
- O(κ²) stochastic-gradient dependence is steep and may render EMGD impractical for ill-conditioned problems.  
- Theoretical guarantee is high-probability rather than deterministic, a trade-off that should be explicitly discussed.  
- EMGD is more general than some competitors (e.g., handles constrained problems and has lower memory requirements than SAG/SDCA).  
- Originality: the mixed-gradient construction and epoch-based shrinking-domain integration are novel relative to SAG, SDCA, and concurrent SVRG.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written but needs notational fixes and explicit definition of κ = L/λ; several proof equations require clearer norms and references.  
- Specific proofreading issues: eq. (13) should use vector norms, clarify which function’s strong convexity is used in eq. (6), and correct inequality/equality phrasing in proofs.  
- Practical limitation: algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ, which can be a drawback in practice.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel hybrid algorithm, strong theoretical contribution (full-gradient independence from κ), high-probability convergence, and applicability to constrained/low-memory settings.  
- Arguments for rejection: unclear practical ML relevance, heavy O(κ²) stochastic cost, and dependence on an assumption (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) that lacks empirical or theoretical justification.  
- Recommendation: accept if authors provide stronger evidence of real-world applicability or examples where κ ≤ n^(2/3); otherwise practical significance is questionable.
