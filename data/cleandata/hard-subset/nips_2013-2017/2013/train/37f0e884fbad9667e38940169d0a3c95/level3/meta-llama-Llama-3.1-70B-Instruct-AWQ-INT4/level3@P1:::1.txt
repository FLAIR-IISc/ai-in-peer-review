The paper "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients" presents a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors' main contribution is a hybrid algorithm that uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, making the number of full gradients required independent of the condition number.

The paper is well-organized and provides a clear introduction to the problem, related work, and the proposed algorithm. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the convergence rate of EMGD and compare its computational complexity with existing methods. The theoretical strength of the paper lies in its ability to achieve linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation result of the Stochastic Average Gradient (SAG) algorithm.

One of the advantages of EMGD is its ability to work for constrained optimization problems, whereas SAG is limited to unconstrained problems. Additionally, EMGD has a simpler proof compared to SAG, and its potential memory and parallelization advantages are noted. However, the paper lacks experiments or concrete examples to demonstrate the empirical relevance of EMGD.

The complexity comparison with existing methods is also discussed, and the authors note that EMGD beats Nesterov's algorithm if the condition number κ is less than or equal to n^(2/3). However, SAG outperforms EMGD in many regimes and matches EMGD when κ is approximately equal to n^(1/2). The authors should highlight realistic settings where EMGD is beneficial.

The paper also lacks a detailed discussion of the limitations and drawbacks of EMGD. For instance, the epoch length must be fixed a priori, which requires knowledge of the condition number κ. In contrast, SAG and Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent (SDCA) are more adaptive. Additionally, EMGD lacks automatic or adaptive step-size mechanisms, which are present in SDCA and practical heuristics used for SAG.

The authors should also address the practical concern of estimating the condition number κ, which is required for EMGD. They mention that the Hessian matrix estimated from a subset of functions, combined with concentration inequalities for matrix, can be used to bound the eigenvalues of the true Hessian matrix and consequently κ. However, this is not explored in detail.

In terms of related work, the authors should cite and contrast Friedlander and Schmidt's (2012) hybrid deterministic-stochastic method. The paper could also benefit from high-level intuition for proof choices and clarification of Table 1 citations and assumptions.

Overall, the paper is technically sound and theoretically significant, with a novel and simple proof. However, the authors should add experiments and address the recommended discussions and edits to strengthen the paper.

Strengths:

* Novel hybrid algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients
* Linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee
* Ability to work for constrained optimization problems
* Simpler proof compared to SAG
* Potential memory and parallelization advantages

Weaknesses:

* Lack of experiments or concrete examples to demonstrate empirical relevance
* Limited discussion of limitations and drawbacks
* Epoch length must be fixed a priori, requiring knowledge of the condition number κ
* Lacks automatic or adaptive step-size mechanisms
* Practical concern of estimating the condition number κ

Recommendations:

* Add experiments to demonstrate the empirical relevance of EMGD
* Provide a detailed discussion of the limitations and drawbacks of EMGD
* Explore the estimation of the condition number κ in detail
* Address the recommended discussions and edits to strengthen the paper
