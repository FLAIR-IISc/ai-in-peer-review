This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems where both a full (batch) gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle are available. The primary motivation is to reduce the dependency on the condition number κ in the complexity of first-order methods. Standard accelerated gradient descent methods require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations. The proposed EMGD algorithm cleverly combines one full gradient evaluation with a series of stochastic gradient updates within each "epoch". The key contribution is a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves an ε-accurate solution with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls, making this component of the complexity independent of κ. This is paid for by requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient calls. The algorithm is presented as an alternative to methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA, with potential advantages in its applicability to constrained optimization, lower memory requirements, and a high-probability (as opposed to in-expectation) convergence guarantee.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality:** The proposed method is novel. The construction of the "mixed gradient," which uses a static full gradient from the beginning of an epoch to anchor a series of variance-reduced stochastic steps, is an interesting and original idea. The goal of designing an algorithm where the number of expensive full gradient computations is independent of the condition number is a valuable research direction.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the EMGD algorithm. The proof, which proceeds by induction on the epochs and uses martingale concentration inequalities to bound the stochastic terms, appears sound and supports the main claims. Achieving a high-probability linear convergence rate is a strong theoretical result.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significance and Practical Relevance:** The primary concern with this paper is the practical significance of its contribution, particularly in the context of machine learning. The authors argue that EMGD is computationally cheaper than accelerated gradient descent when κ ≤ n^(2/3) (assuming the cost of a full gradient is n times that of a stochastic gradient). However, the paper does not provide a compelling argument that this represents a common or important regime for machine learning problems. The justification in the footnote on page 4, which relies on the regularization parameter τ being sufficiently large, is not fully convincing without concrete examples. For many ill-conditioned problems, κ can be very large, potentially violating this condition. The paper's impact would be significantly strengthened by a careful discussion, supported by examples, of practical ML scenarios where this condition holds and EMGD offers a clear advantage over standard methods like L-BFGS or accelerated gradient descent. Without this, the proposed algorithm, while theoretically elegant, may have limited practical applicability.

2.  **Comparison of Convergence Guarantees:** The paper's convergence guarantee is a high-probability bound. While this is a strong type of guarantee for a stochastic method, it is fundamentally weaker than the deterministic guarantees provided by the full-gradient methods it is being compared against (e.g., Nesterov's method). This trade-off—sacrificing a deterministic guarantee for a reduced number of full gradient calls—is not discussed. Authors should acknowledge this and justify why this is a favorable trade-off.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   The central claim of the paper's practical utility rests on the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3). It is crucial that the authors provide a more thorough motivation for why this is a "reasonable scenario" in machine learning. Are there important classes of models or datasets where this occurs?
*   The notation for citing equations within other equations (e.g., line 286: `... (5), (6) <= ...`) can be confusing. It would improve clarity to state the justification in the preceding text rather than embedding it in the mathematical expression.
*   The statement of the inductive hypothesis in equation (7) (line 288) might be clearer if it explicitly references that this is assumed to be true from the previous epoch, as established in Section 4.1.
*   The definition of the condition number κ = L/λ is mentioned on page 3. For improved clarity, it would be beneficial to define it formally in the introduction or at the beginning of the preliminaries, as it is central to the paper's motivation.
*   The authors should double-check the analysis for consistency in notation. For instance, please ensure that norms (||·||) are used for vector quantities throughout, rather than absolute values.
*   While this may be obvious to experts, a brief sentence explaining *why* L ≥ λ follows from the definitions of L-smoothness and λ-strong convexity in Section 3.1 would make the paper more self-contained.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting hybrid optimization algorithm. However, its main weakness is the insufficient justification for its practical relevance in machine learning. The authors need to provide a much stronger argument for when their method's total computational cost is superior to that of standard, deterministic batch methods.
