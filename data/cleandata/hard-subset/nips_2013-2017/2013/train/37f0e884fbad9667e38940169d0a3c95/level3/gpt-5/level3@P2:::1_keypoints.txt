- Proposes EMGD: one full-gradient per epoch plus multiple variance-reduced mixed stochastic steps, with projection in a shrinking ball and epoch averaging.  
- Main theoretical claim: linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations (high-probability).  
- Key benefit: removes condition-number dependence from the number of full-gradient calls compared to pure full-gradient methods.  
- Situates EMGD relative to prior work: reduces expensive full-gradient calls vs accelerated methods and attains linear rates vs classic stochastic methods.  
- Relation to finite-sum methods: conceptually similar to variance-reduction/SVRG but differs in oracle model, constraint handling, and no per-example gradient storage.  
- Strength: clear, simple algorithm with a compact, self-contained proof and high-probability linear convergence.  
- Strength: conceptually appealing anchoring by full gradient and controlled stochastic differences via shrinking ball.  
- Strength: memory-efficient (O(d)) and applicable to constrained optimization via projections.  
- Strength: full-gradient step is parallelizable without affecting convergence guarantees.  
- Weakness: stochastic-gradient complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) is worse than SAG/SDCA and than expected from variance-reduction; need clarity if κ^2 is inherent or an artifact.  
- Weakness: inconsistent statements in the paper about κ vs κ^2 (abstract vs theorem); must be fixed.  
- Weakness: no empirical experiments, so practical runtime benefits vs baselines (accelerated GD, SAG, SDCA) are unverified.  
- Weakness: requires prior knowledge of κ to set epoch length T; lacks adaptive or practical parameter-selection strategies.  
- Weakness: related work and comparisons incomplete—need clearer discussion of constrained settings, memory–time trade-offs, and inclusion of hybrid methods (Friedlander & Schmidt).  
- Weakness: mixed-gradient estimator similarity to SVRG should be discussed in more detail, including complexity implications.  
- Clarity issues: needs more intuition for key proof steps (role of shrinking ball, Azuma bound), cleaner notation, typo fixes, and consistent log notation.  
- Presentation issues: Table 1/2 should state precise assumptions and costs per iteration; integrate footnotes into main text.  
- Quality assessment: analysis is technically sound, but κ^2 dependence may result from conservative choices (η=1/(L√T)) and shrinking domain.  
- Originality: algorithmic template is known, but extension to constrained/oracle setting and shrinking-ball analysis add useful novelty.  
- Significance: decoupling full-gradient calls from κ is appealing for large-scale learning, but κ^2 stochastic cost limits competitiveness without empirical validation.  
- Suggested improvements: resolve κ vs κ^2 inconsistency, explore less conservative step-sizes, and investigate whether κ dependence can be achieved.  
- Suggested improvements: add experiments across n and κ, report wall-clock time, full-gradient calls, and memory.  
- Suggested improvements: propose adaptive schedules for T/η (e.g., doubling or backtracking) and practical step-size heuristics.  
- Suggested improvements: expand related work discussion to include hybrid and SVRG-style methods and sharpen comparisons.  
- Acceptance argument: simple, memory-efficient method with clean high-probability linear-rate analysis that handles constraints and distributed full-gradient computation.  
- Rejection argument: lack of experiments, likely suboptimal κ^2 stochastic complexity, inconsistent presentation, and limited adaptivity.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept if authors fix κ inconsistency, expand comparisons/experiments, and address adaptivity; otherwise lean reject.
