This paper introduces the Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm, a novel approach to accelerate smooth and strongly convex optimization by strategically combining full and stochastic gradient evaluations. The core idea is to reduce the dependence on the condition number, κ, typically present in full-gradient methods, by incorporating stochastic gradients. EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations, independent of κ, alongside O(κ<sup>2</sup> log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, theoretically providing significant gains when full gradient computations are expensive.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in convex optimization, referencing key contributions from Boyd & Vandenberghe (2004), Nesterov (1988, 2006), and others. It appropriately situates itself within the landscape of both deterministic and stochastic optimization methods. The authors correctly highlight the trade-off between iteration complexity and computational cost per iteration, motivating the need for an algorithm like EMGD. The paper effectively contrasts EMGD with existing approaches such as accelerated gradient descent, stochastic gradient descent, SAG (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2014), and SDCA, providing a complexity comparison in Table 2.  

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm is clearly presented and the theoretical analysis appears rigorous. The convergence rate proof, while concise, establishes a linear convergence rate with a high-probability guarantee.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of systematically mixing full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework is a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:**  The potential to reduce the computational cost associated with ill-conditioned problems is significant, particularly in large-scale machine learning applications where full gradient evaluations can be prohibitively expensive.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The key concepts and the algorithm’s steps are clearly explained.
* **Stronger convergence guarantee:** The convergence rate holds with high probability, unlike SAG which has an in-expectation guarantee.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** A major weakness is the complete absence of experimental results. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, it’s crucial to demonstrate the algorithm’s practical performance on real-world datasets and compare it directly to existing methods like SAG and SDCA.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The algorithm requires knowing the condition number, κ, *a priori* to choose the epoch length.  This is a significant practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this but offer only high-level ideas for estimation, requiring further elaboration.
*   **Step-Size and Adaptivity:** The fixed step-size chosen (η = 1/[L √ T ]) feels somewhat inflexible. Existing methods like SDCA and practical implementations of SAG often employ adaptive step-size strategies to improve performance.
*   **Limited Discussion of Drawbacks:**  The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of the limitations and potential drawbacks of EMGD. While section 3.4 highlights some advantages, it doesn't fully address scenarios where EMGD might *not* be competitive.
*   **Related Work Omission:** The authors should acknowledge and compare their work to the hybrid deterministic-stochastic method presented by Friedlander & Schmidt (2012).
* **Clarification Needed:**  The presentation of Table 1 could be improved by explicitly stating the assumptions under which the iteration complexities hold. More explanation of the gradient expectation identity used in the proof would also be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with the potential to improve the efficiency of convex optimization in specific settings.
*   The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides a strong guarantee of linear convergence.
*   The work addresses a relevant and important problem in optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation significantly weakens the paper’s claims. It's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the algorithm without experimental results.
*   The practical limitations regarding parameter tuning (specifically, knowing κ) and the absence of adaptive step-size mechanisms raise concerns about the algorithm’s robustness and ease of use.
*   The limited discussion of drawbacks and comparisons to existing methods leaves the reader with an incomplete picture of EMGD’s strengths and weaknesses.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising theoretical contribution to the field of convex optimization. However, the lack of experimental results is a significant drawback.  While the theoretical analysis is strong, practical impact is uncertain without empirical validation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, they should include experiments demonstrating the algorithm’s performance, provide a more detailed discussion of practical considerations (e.g., condition number estimation), and expand the comparison to related work. The authors could also address these issues in a strong rebuttal.
