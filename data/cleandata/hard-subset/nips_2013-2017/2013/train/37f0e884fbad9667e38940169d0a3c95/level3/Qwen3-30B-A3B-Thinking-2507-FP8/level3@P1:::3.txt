

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm for smooth strongly convex optimization that combines full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations needed. The authors claim that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, compared to the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients required by batch gradient descent. The approach is grounded in the observation that while full gradient methods have better convergence rates but higher per-iteration cost, stochastic gradient methods have lower per-iteration cost but slower convergence. By blending both approaches, the authors aim to reduce the number of expensive full gradient evaluations.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature by introducing a hybrid method that achieves linear convergence with a condition number-independent number of full gradient accesses. This builds upon recent work on stochastic optimization (e.g., SAG, SDCA) and epoch-based methods (e.g., EGD). The theoretical analysis is thorough, with a detailed convergence proof showing that EMGD achieves the claimed complexity bounds. The proof is technically sound, though it contains minor issues that should be addressed (e.g., equation (6) should use ||w^* - ŵ||², equation (4) has inconsistent notation with ŵ instead of x^).

The main strength of this work is its theoretical novelty. The authors successfully demonstrate how to reduce the dependence on the condition number by leveraging stochastic gradients, which is a valuable insight for optimization theory. The algorithm design is elegant, using a mixed gradient that combines deterministic and stochastic components, and the convergence proof is well-structured using induction and martingale analysis.

However, the paper's significance for machine learning applications is questionable. The authors acknowledge that in typical ML problems with regularization (e.g., L2 regularization), the strong convexity parameter λ is proportional to the number of samples N, making κ = L/λ proportional to 1/N. In this common scenario, the condition number κ becomes small (not large), meaning the "ill-conditioned" regime the paper targets is rarely encountered in practice. The authors claim EMGD is faster than batch gradient descent when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but this condition is satisfied only when the regularization parameter is sufficiently large (τ > Ω(n^(-2/3))), which is precisely the regime where the problem is well-conditioned to begin with. This undermines the practical motivation for the method.

Another significant weakness is that the convergence guarantee is high-probability (1 - mδ), while standard batch gradient descent guarantees deterministic convergence. This probabilistic nature might be less appealing for practitioners who prefer deterministic guarantees, especially when the probability of failure accumulates over multiple epochs.

The paper also lacks a thorough comparison with batch gradient descent in practical ML settings. While Table 2 provides theoretical computational complexity comparisons, it doesn't address how the algorithm would perform on real ML datasets with varying condition numbers. The authors should provide empirical results or a more detailed discussion of when EMGD would be preferable to standard batch methods.

Regarding clarity, the paper is well-written overall, but several minor issues reduce its precision: the condition number should be explicitly defined as κ = L/λ (not just "the ratio"), equation (13) should use norms instead of absolute values, and line 286's equality should be removed for clarity. The related work section adequately covers the literature, though it could better distinguish EMGD's contribution from existing hybrid methods.

In terms of originality, the core idea of combining full and stochastic gradients is novel for achieving condition-number-independent full gradient access, though it shares conceptual similarities with semi-stochastic methods. The technical contribution is significant within optimization theory, but the practical relevance to machine learning is limited by the strong convexity assumptions that rarely lead to large condition numbers in typical ML problems.

The quality of the theoretical analysis is high, with careful attention to the proof details. The authors appropriately address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, though they understate the practical limitations regarding condition numbers in machine learning applications.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a novel theoretical contribution to optimization. However, its significance for the NIPS community is limited by the narrow practical relevance to machine learning applications, where the condition number is typically small rather than large. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more detailed discussion of practical scenarios where the method would be beneficial, and empirical validation on real datasets. The minor technical issues noted should be addressed before publication.

The paper should be rejected on significance grounds, as the practical advantage claimed is limited to an uninteresting regime of machine learning problems. While the theoretical contribution is valid, it doesn't advance the state of the art in a way that would be compelling for the NIPS community. The authors should consider reframing their work to focus on the theoretical insights rather than claiming practical advantages in ML settings where the condition number is typically small.
