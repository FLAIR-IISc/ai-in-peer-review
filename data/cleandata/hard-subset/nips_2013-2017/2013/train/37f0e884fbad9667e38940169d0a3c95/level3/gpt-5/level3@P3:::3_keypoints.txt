- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid first-order method using occasional full gradients and many stochastic corrections with shrinking trust regions.
- Claims linear convergence with high probability using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients for L-smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives.
- Positions EMGD between deterministic accelerated methods and finite-sum/semi-stochastic methods, with applicability to constrained problems and O(d) memory.
- Strength: technical novelty in the “mixed-gradient” update decomposing anchor full gradient and bounded stochastic correction with a shrinking ball.
- Strength: clear theoretical goal to minimize expensive full-gradient queries while using cheaper stochastic work.
- Strength: sound proof techniques giving a high-probability linear rate (apart from minor noted issues).
- Strength: broad applicability (constraints, no finite-sum requirement) and low memory footprint.
- Major weakness: practical relevance unclear because in ERM λ often scales ≈1/N, so κ grows with N and O(κ^2) stochastic cost can become O(N^2), negating savings.
- Major weakness: under a standard cost model (full gradient ≈ N stochastic gradients), EMGD only outperforms AGD in a narrow, possibly uninteresting regime; paper lacks careful quantitative comparison.
- Major weakness: guarantee is high-probability, while AGD gives deterministic linear convergence — this tradeoff should be emphasized.
- Major weakness: inconsistency in manuscript claiming O(κ) vs. proven O(κ^2) stochastic dependence; needs reconciliation and consistent statements.
- Major weakness: no empirical validation or wall-clock comparisons to AGD, SAG, SDCA, nor sensitivity analysis over N, λ, L.
- Quality: technically solid and novel, but cost model and practical-relevance analysis are incomplete.
- Clarity: generally well written, but notational slips, equation reference issues, and unclear comparisons reduce readability.
- Originality: mixed-gradient + shrinking local constraint and the O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient high-probability result appear novel and complementary to prior work.
- Significance: theoretically interesting, but practical impact is uncertain without addressing λ scaling with N and κ^2 stochastic cost.
- Major recommendation: provide quantitative comparison to AGD under cost model where full gradient costs N stochastic gradients, deriving crossover as function of κ, N, ε with κ^2 included.
- Major recommendation: analyze effect of λ chosen via regularization (λ ∝ 1/N) and whether EMGD still provides savings under that scaling.
- Major recommendation: add empirical evaluation vs AGD, SAG, SDCA on synthetic and real data, varying N and λ; report counts and wall-clock times, include constrained cases.
- Major recommendation: clarify high-probability nature of the bound, state δ and union bounds across epochs, and discuss practical parameter choices.
- Minor corrections: fix notation in Eq. (6) to use ||w* − ŵ||^2, clarify F in (7), and remove equality signs that mask inequalities.
- Minor corrections: use norms rather than absolute values in Vt bound, justify L ≥ λ, consistently use ŵ and F where appropriate, and ensure proximal term dominance per Lemma 1.
- Minor corrections: define κ = L/λ once and consistently (map μ ≡ λ if used), fix “epoches” typo, and clarify projection efficiency in Algorithm 1.
- Acceptance arguments: novel hybrid method, clean correct analysis, high-probability linear rate with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, handles constraints, low memory.
- Rejection arguments: practical advantage over AGD is unconvincing due to κ^2 stochastic dependence and typical λ scaling with N, high-probability (not deterministic) guarantee, and lack of experiments.
- Overall recommendation: theoretically solid and original but ML relevance under realistic scaling is unsubstantiated; support acceptance only if authors add quantitative AGD comparisons, experiments, and reconcile κ^2 dependence, otherwise lean toward rejection.
