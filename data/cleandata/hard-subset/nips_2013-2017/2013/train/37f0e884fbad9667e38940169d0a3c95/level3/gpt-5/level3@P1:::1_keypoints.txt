- Paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): per-epoch full gradient at averaged iterate plus T variance-reduced stochastic updates with shrinking ℓ2-ball projections.  
- Main theoretical claim: high-probability linear convergence with F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ λΔ1^2/2^{m+1} after m epochs.  
- Complexity stated: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls when T = Ω((L/λ)^2 log(1/δ)) and η = 1/(L√T).  
- Relative to accelerated full-gradient methods, EMGD removes κ from full-gradient count at the cost of κ^2 extra stochastic steps.  
- Compared to SAG/SDCA: supports constrained problems, uses O(d) memory, and provides high-probability guarantees.  
- Strength: conceptually simple hybrid mixing one full gradient per epoch with many cheap variance-reduced stochastic steps.  
- Strength: provides a clear, largely self-contained high-probability linear convergence proof using smoothness, strong convexity, and martingale concentration.  
- Strength: handles constrained optimization via projections on shrinking balls, unlike typical SAG/SDCA treatments.  
- Strength: memory-efficient and suitable for distributed computation of the full-gradient step.  
- Strength: good discussion of computational tradeoffs and regimes where EMGD can outperform accelerated methods (κ ≤ n^{2/3}).  
- Weakness: inconsistency in stochastic-gradient complexity—abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)) vs. Theorem 1/Section 3 implying O(κ^2 log(1/ε)); needs correction.  
- Weakness: no experiments; empirical validation vs SAG/SDCA and accelerated methods (across κ and n, and constrained cases) is missing.  
- Weakness: requires knowledge of κ (or L, λ) and Δ1 to set T, η, and initial radius; lacks discussion of estimation or adaptive strategies.  
- Weakness: comparison model assumes full-grad cost = n× stochastic; should broaden to mini-batching, data access patterns, and related hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods.  
- Weakness: projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k, Δk) may be expensive for general W; need discussion of tractable constraint classes and practical cost.  
- Weakness: function-oracle assumption is stronger than unbiased gradient oracle; clarify realizability (finite-sum) and extension to bounded-variance unbiased gradients.  
- Weakness: clarity/notation issues—log/ln inconsistency, reused equation numbers, norm/domain notation, minor typos, and incomplete table citations/assumptions.  
- Quality: technical development appears sound with explicit constants and careful bias/variance control; practical gap due to Δ1 and λ/L knowledge remains.  
- Clarity: algorithm and proof are readable overall, but noted inconsistencies and missing explanations detract and should be fixed.  
- Originality: novel combination of mixed gradients with shrinking domains for constrained strongly convex problems; related but distinct from variance-reduced methods.  
- Significance: advances tradeoff between full and stochastic gradients by removing κ from full-gradient count; most impactful for constrained, memory-limited, and distributed settings.  
- Limitation: κ^2 stochastic-step dependence may be prohibitive compared to SAG/SDCA in many finite-sum regimes.  
- Questions for authors: can EMGD be made adaptive to unknown κ; does analysis extend to proximal composite (nonsmooth) settings; can mini-batching reduce the κ^2 factor; sensitivity to radius and step-size misspecification?  
- Pros for acceptance: simple, elegant algorithm with high-probability linear convergence, constrained-problem support, and low memory.  
- Cons for acceptance: no empirical validation, potentially uncompetitive κ^2 factor, need for problem-constant knowledge, and complexity inconsistency.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid and conceptually interesting; lean toward acceptance contingent on fixing complexity inconsistency, expanding related work, improving clarity, and adding at least modest experiments.
