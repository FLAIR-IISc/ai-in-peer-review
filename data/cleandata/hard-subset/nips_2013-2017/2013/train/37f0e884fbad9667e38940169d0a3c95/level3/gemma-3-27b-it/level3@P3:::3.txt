This paper proposes a novel hybrid stochastic/batch gradient descent algorithm (EMGD) for strongly convex optimization, aiming to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ when full gradients are expensive. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, achieving a convergence rate of O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The theoretical analysis appears largely correct, although several minor points require attention. However, a significant concern revolves around the practical relevance of the method in machine learning contexts.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The hybrid approach, combining full and stochastic gradients in a specific manner, represents a novel contribution. The theoretical convergence analysis, despite minor issues, appears to be a non-trivial undertaking and provides valuable insights.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a reasonably rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the algorithm’s convergence properties. The proof structure, relying on induction, appears well-organized.
* **Clear Algorithm Presentation:** Algorithm 1 clearly outlines the EMGD procedure, making it easy to understand the method's mechanics.
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The authors provide a comparison of EMGD’s computational complexity against other algorithms (Nesterov’s, SAG, SDCA), highlighting potential benefits in specific scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Relevance/Scalability:** The most significant weakness is the limited practical applicability. The authors themselves acknowledge that the benefit of EMGD over batch gradient descent hinges on the condition N = O(C^3/L^3), where N is the dataset size, C is a constant, and L is the smoothness parameter. This regime is quite restrictive and unlikely to hold in many real-world machine learning problems. The method seems geared towards cases with extremely ill-conditioned problems *and* a small dataset size, which is a rare combination.
* **High-Probability vs. Deterministic Convergence:** EMGD achieves convergence only with high probability. In contrast, batch gradient descent guarantees deterministic convergence, which is a desirable property in many applications where reliability is paramount. The authors need to thoroughly justify why accepting a probabilistic guarantee is worthwhile.
* **Insufficient Comparison to Batch GD:** The paper lacks a detailed and compelling discussion comparing EMGD to standard batch gradient descent. The authors need to explicitly articulate the scenarios where EMGD would offer a practical advantage, beyond the theoretical condition on N.  They should also discuss the overhead associated with managing stochastic gradient evaluations.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** Several minor issues need addressing:
    * **Equation (6):** Should be ||w^* − ŵ||^2.
    * **Line 288:** Specify which function is used in equation (7).
    * **Line 286:** Remove the equality to improve clarity.
    * **Equation (13):** Replace absolute values with norms.
    * **Explicitly State L ≥ λ:** While likely obvious, explicitly stating this relationship improves clarity.
    * **Equation (4):** Replace x^ with ŵ, f with F. Also, the first term in the max is always larger by Lemma 1, which should be stated.
    * **Condition Number Definition:** Precisely define the condition number κ as a function of λ and L (κ = L/λ).



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a careful (though not flawless) analysis. It feels complete in terms of presenting the algorithm and its theoretical properties, but lacking in a convincing justification for its practical usefulness.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm is clearly presented. However, the lack of a thorough discussion comparing EMGD to batch GD hinders overall clarity regarding its potential benefits.

**Originality:** The hybrid approach is original and represents a non-trivial extension of existing optimization techniques. The analysis appears to build upon existing literature appropriately, but the practical impact remains questionable.

**Significance:**  Currently, the significance is unclear. While the theoretical contribution is noteworthy, the limited practical applicability casts doubt on its potential impact. If the authors can convincingly demonstrate scenarios where EMGD outperforms batch GD, its significance would increase considerably.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel hybrid algorithm with a rigorous theoretical analysis.
* Potential for reducing the number of full gradient evaluations in certain scenarios.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited practical relevance due to the restrictive condition on the dataset size (N).
* Probabilistic convergence compared to deterministic convergence of batch GD.
* Insufficient justification for practical advantage over batch GD.
* Minor technical issues requiring correction.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm, but its practical significance is questionable. The authors need to address the concerns regarding its relevance to machine learning applications and provide a more compelling justification for its potential advantages over standard batch gradient descent. I lean towards **rejecting** the paper in its current form, but would be open to reconsideration if the authors significantly strengthen the discussion of practical implications and address the minor technical issues. A revision that rigorously demonstrates a realistic scenario where EMGD outperforms batch GD is crucial for increasing its value to the NIPS community.
