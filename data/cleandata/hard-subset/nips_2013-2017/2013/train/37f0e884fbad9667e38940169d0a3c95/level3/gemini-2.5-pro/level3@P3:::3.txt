This paper proposes a novel hybrid optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex objective functions. The key idea is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, which can be a bottleneck for ill-conditioned problems. The algorithm proceeds in epochs, where each epoch involves computing one full gradient at the start, followed by a series of updates using a "mixed gradient." This mixed gradient combines the initial full gradient with a stochastic gradient term that tracks local changes. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, where κ is the condition number. This result is significant because it makes the number of full gradient evaluations independent of κ, unlike standard accelerated gradient descent which requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations. The work positions itself relative to both deterministic methods like accelerated GD and stochastic methods like SGD, SAG, and SDCA, aiming to combine the fast linear convergence of the former with a reduced dependency on full data passes.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality:** The proposed EMGD algorithm and its mixed gradient update rule are novel. The strategy of using a single full gradient per epoch as an anchor, corrected by cheap stochastic updates, to achieve a κ-independent full gradient count for linear convergence is a creative and interesting theoretical idea.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis. The proof, based on an inductive argument over epochs, appears technically sound and establishes the main claim of linear convergence with high probability. The theoretical result is strong and non-trivial.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the theoretical analysis, while dense, is logically structured. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work within the broader optimization literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Major Concern: Practical Relevance for Machine Learning:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its questionable practical advantage over standard methods in common machine learning settings. The authors claim an advantage over accelerated gradient descent (AGD) when their total computational cost is lower. Assuming a full gradient costs `n` units (for `n` data points), the cost of EMGD is O(n log(1/ε) + κ² log(1/ε)) while the cost of AGD is O(n√κ log(1/ε)). EMGD is thus faster when approximately κ² < n√κ, or κ < n^(2/3).

    However, in typical regularized loss minimization problems, F(w) = (1/n)Σ fᵢ(w) + (λ_reg/2)||w||², the condition number κ = L/λ is strongly influenced by `n`. The smoothness L is often independent of `n`, while the strong convexity parameter λ is dominated by the regularization λ_reg. To achieve good generalization, λ_reg is typically chosen to be small for large datasets, often scaling as O(1/n) or O(1/√n). If λ_reg = O(1/n), then the condition number κ becomes O(n). In this very common regime, the condition for EMGD to be superior (κ < n^(2/3)) becomes n < n^(2/3), which is false for n>1. This suggests that for a large class of important ML problems, EMGD would be significantly slower than standard AGD. The paper needs a much more careful discussion of this point to justify its practical relevance. The authors should either identify compelling, non-standard ML applications where κ is small relative to `n`, or temper their claims of practical superiority over batch methods.

2.  **High-Probability vs. Deterministic Guarantee:** The proposed convergence guarantee for EMGD holds with high probability, whereas the guarantee for batch gradient descent is deterministic. While a high-probability bound is strong, it is a strictly weaker form of convergence. This distinction should be clearly acknowledged as a disadvantage when comparing against deterministic methods.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **Comparison to Batch GD:** The paper's value would be greatly enhanced by a more thorough and convincing comparison with batch GD that addresses the scaling of κ with `n` in machine learning. The footnote on page 4, which suggests that τ > Ω(n^(-2/3)) is a "reasonable scenario," needs stronger evidence or citation, as τ = O(1/n) is arguably more common.
*   The condition number κ = L/λ should be defined slightly more formally, explaining its dependence on the smoothness and strong convexity parameters. While stated, its central role warrants a more precise definition in the preliminaries.
*   The paper mentions that L ≥ λ is "obvious." While true, for the sake of completeness, it would be better to explicitly state that this follows from the definitions of L-smoothness and λ-strong convexity.
*   There appear to be several minor notational errors or points that could be clarified. The authors are encouraged to carefully proofread the manuscript. For instance:
    *   In the application of Lemma 1 (equation (6)) in the proofs, please double-check that the norms are squared where appropriate (e.g., the user notes a potential issue with `||w* - w||²`).
    *   Please check equation (13) and ensure all norms are used correctly instead of absolute values if any exist.
    *   A suggestion was made that the equality in line 286 decreases clarity; please review this line.
    *   In line 288, when invoking condition (7), it would be clearer to state for which function this holds (presumably F).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper introduces a theoretically novel and interesting hybrid optimization algorithm. The core contribution—achieving linear convergence with a number of full gradient evaluations that is independent of the condition number—is a solid theoretical result. However, the paper's significance is hampered by a major concern regarding its practical applicability. The conditions under which the proposed method outperforms standard accelerated gradient descent seem to exclude many common large-scale machine learning scenarios. While the work is of theoretical interest, its practical impact remains unclear without a more compelling justification.
