This paper introduces the Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm for constrained smooth strongly-convex optimization. The core idea is to combine a single full-gradient computation per epoch with a large number of cheaper stochastic-gradient steps. By doing so, the authors aim to achieve a linear convergence rate where the number of expensive full-gradient calls, O(log(1/ε)), is independent of the problem's condition number (κ), trading this for O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic calls. This work fits into a line of research on hybrid and variance-reduced stochastic methods like SAG, SVRG, and SCDA, which also seek to attain fast linear convergence rates for finite-sum problems.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contribution. The proposed EMGD algorithm is novel in its specific blend of full and stochastic gradients for the constrained setting, and it is supported by a simple, self-contained convergence proof. Notably, the analysis provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is a stronger result than the in-expectation guarantees common to methods like SAG. The paper correctly identifies advantages over SAG and SCDA, such as its applicability to constrained problems and its potential for lower memory usage and easier parallelization.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that temper its impact. The most critical is the complete absence of experiments. Without empirical validation, it is impossible to assess the practical relevance of the algorithm or its theoretical complexity advantages. The claims about outperforming Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3) and matching SAG when κ ≈ n^(1/2) remain purely theoretical. The discussion also lacks a critical evaluation of the method's limitations. For instance, the epoch length T must be fixed a priori, requiring knowledge of κ, which is a major practical drawback compared to more adaptive methods. Furthermore, the paper omits discussion of a highly relevant hybrid deterministic-stochastic method by Friedlander & Schmidt (2012). The authors also need to expand their comparison to existing work (Section 3.4) and better motivate the practical machine learning settings where EMGD would be the preferred algorithm.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper with an interesting theoretical result and an elegant proof. The novelty and the high-probability guarantee are significant. However, the lack of any experimental results is a severe flaw that makes the work feel incomplete. I would recommend acceptance on the condition that the authors provide experimental results and substantially revise the discussion to address the algorithm's limitations and its relationship to prior work.
