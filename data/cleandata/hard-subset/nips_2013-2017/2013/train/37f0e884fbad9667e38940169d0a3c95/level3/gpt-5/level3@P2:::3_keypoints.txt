- Problem & approach: Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) alternating full-gradient epochs with control-variate stochastic updates and shrinking-ball projections for smooth, strongly convex optimization.  
- Main theoretical result: Achieves ε-optimality with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls (κ = L/λ), with high-probability guarantees via martingale concentration.  
- Relation to prior work: Conceptually related to semi-stochastic and variance-reduced methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA) but analyzed in an oracle model emphasizing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients.  
- Technical soundness: Theorem and proof strategy (epoch-wise variance reduction, shrinking domains, Azuma-Hoeffding) appear correct and well-executed; per-epoch T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) is justified.  
- Completeness: Theoretical contribution is complete, but lacks empirical evaluation and practical trade-off analysis versus batch methods.  
- Presentation issues: Several notational and editorial mistakes need fixing; dependence on κ and δ should be made consistently explicit.  
- Clarity: Algorithm and proof are readable, but manuscript would benefit from cleaner notation and a discussion of wall-clock speedups.  
- Originality: Interesting and clean mix of full and stochastic gradients under an oracle model; novelty relative to finite-sum variance-reduction methods needs deeper discussion.  
- Theoretical significance: Showing O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient complexity with high probability is appealing, but total stochastic cost O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) may limit impact.  
- Practical significance concern: For typical ML regimes (regularization-induced strong convexity), EMGD may only outperform batch methods in narrow parameter regimes; lacks runtime/cost modeling.  
- Major practical requests: Provide principled analysis of how L and λ scale with sample size, explicit wall-clock cost comparisons to accelerated gradient/SAG/SDCA, and experiments or synthetic studies to validate crossovers.  
- Deterministic vs probabilistic gap: EMGD gives high-probability linear rates while batch methods give deterministic rates—authors should discuss scheduling δ across epochs and implications.  
- Knowledge of κ: Algorithm requires κ (or an upper bound) to set T and η; authors should propose adaptive or online estimation strategies.  
- Pros: Simple mixed-gradient estimator, projection-friendly for constraints, low memory O(d), amenable to distributed full-gradient computation, high-probability linear convergence with few full gradients.  
- Cons: High stochastic-gradient complexity for ill-conditioned problems, unclear practical advantages versus accelerated methods, no experiments, multiple minor presentation issues.  
- Minor corrections: Fix notation in equations (e.g., ∥w*−ŵ∥^2, replace abs by vector norms, consistent κ = L/λ, consistent symbols x̂/ŵ and F/f), and reconcile abstract vs theorem on stochastic complexity.  
- Questions for authors: Can you provide κ-adaptive variants, sensitivity analysis for the shrinking-ball schedule, and finite-sum empirical comparisons to SAG/SDCA under memory/constraint limits?  
- Recommendation & confidence: Borderline reject; theory is solid but practical benefits are unconvincing without experiments and clearer regime analysis (reviewer confidence: high on theory, medium on practical critique).
