

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel first-order optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations required for smooth strongly convex optimization. The authors claim that by allowing access to both oracles, their method can achieve O(log(1/ε)) convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, eliminating the √κ dependence in the convergence rate.

The paper builds on well-established literature in convex optimization. For smooth strongly convex problems, standard full-gradient methods achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) convergence [17], while stochastic gradient methods typically require O(1/λε) iterations [9,10]. The authors correctly identify that when κ is large (e.g., in ill-conditioned problems), the full gradient evaluations become computationally expensive, motivating the hybrid approach. The paper's theoretical analysis extends this line of work by showing that by mixing full and stochastic gradients, the number of full gradient evaluations can be reduced to logarithmic dependence on ε.

**Quality**: The theoretical analysis is technically sound for the specific setting where the condition number κ is known a priori. The proof of Theorem 1 appears correct and follows standard techniques for analyzing first-order methods. However, the critical assumption that κ must be known exactly is not adequately addressed. The paper lacks any analysis or guidance for cases where κ is unknown or misspecified, which is a significant practical limitation. The authors should provide either a procedure for estimating κ or a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's behavior when κ is estimated with error. Additionally, the paper contains no experiments to validate the theoretical claims or study the empirical behavior under misspecification of κ, which is a major weakness for a paper claiming practical significance.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly presents the algorithm and its theoretical properties. However, Section 3.1 is misleading in its problem formulation, as it presents the setting as a general smooth strongly convex problem rather than the specific finite-sum setting (1) where the authors later compare with SAG and SDCA. The relationship between the proposed "mixed gradient" update and simpler approaches (such as interleaving deterministic and stochastic steps) is not clearly explained, making it difficult to understand the novelty of the approach.

**Originality**: The core idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients is not entirely novel. The paper fails to properly contextualize its work against prior hybrid deterministic-stochastic approaches, such as Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) "Hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods for data fitting" and Marti & Fuchs (1978) "Rates of convergence of semi-stochastic approximation procedures." The paper does not sufficiently explain how EMGD differs from these prior methods or why it represents a meaningful improvement. The claim of "novel algorithm" appears overstated given the lack of proper comparison to related work.

**Significance**: The theoretical result is interesting in that it shows a way to eliminate the condition number dependence on full gradient evaluations. However, the requirement to know κ exactly significantly limits the practical applicability. In most real-world optimization problems (e.g., machine learning), the condition number is not known a priori, and the paper provides no guidance for handling this common scenario. The lack of experimental results comparing EMGD to standard baselines (SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SDCA, etc.) further weakens the significance claim. The paper also fails to consider non-strongly convex settings, which are common in practice (e.g., regularized logistic regression without strong convexity).

**Arguments for acceptance**: The paper presents a theoretically sound algorithm with a clear theoretical contribution regarding condition number independence. The algorithm's ability to work for both constrained and unconstrained problems, with lower storage requirements than SAG/SDCA, is a positive aspect.

**Arguments against acceptance**: The critical assumption that κ must be known a priori is not addressed, and the paper lacks any analysis of the algorithm's behavior when κ is misspecified. The lack of experimental validation is a major weakness for a paper claiming practical significance. The paper fails to properly contextualize its work against prior hybrid methods, and the problem setting is not clearly specified.

**Recommendation**: I recommend rejection. While the theoretical contribution is potentially interesting, the paper's practical limitations (requiring exact knowledge of κ), lack of experimental validation, and failure to properly contextualize against prior work significantly diminish its impact. The authors should either provide a procedure for estimating κ or conduct experiments to study the algorithm's behavior when κ is unknown before considering publication.

**Suggestions for improvement**: The authors should:
1. Address the issue of unknown κ by either providing an estimation procedure or analyzing the algorithm's behavior under misspecification
2. Conduct experiments comparing EMGD with standard baselines (SGD, SAG, SDCA, etc.) on benchmark optimization problems
3. Properly contextualize the work against prior hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods
4. Clarify the problem setting (finite-sum vs. general smooth strongly convex)
5. Include an analysis of non-strongly convex settings, which are common in practice
