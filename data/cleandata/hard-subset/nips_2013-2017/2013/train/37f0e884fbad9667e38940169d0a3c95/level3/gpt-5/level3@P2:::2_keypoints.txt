- Proposal: EMGD mixes one full gradient per epoch with many stochastic corrections using g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇ft(wt) − ∇ft(w̄k), shrinking the feasible ball around the epoch anchor and averaging iterates.  
- Main theoretical claim: high-probability linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and (claimed inconsistently) either O(κ log(1/ε)) or O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls (κ = L/λ).  
- Motivation: reduce dependence of full-gradient count on κ to make full-gradient evaluations independent of condition number when they are expensive.  
- Strength: conceptually simple hybrid that uses one expensive full gradient per epoch plus cheap stochastic corrections and low memory compared to methods like SAG.  
- Strength: provides high-probability (not just in-expectation) linear convergence and handles constrained domains via projected updates.  
- Weakness: ambiguous oracle/cost model—paper mixes expectation-oracle and finite-sum settings without clearly separating assumptions or cost analyses.  
- Weakness: requires knowing κ (L and λ) to set step size and epoch length; no guidance or analysis for unknown or misspecified κ.  
- Weakness: no experiments, no robustness/sensitivity studies for η or κ, and no empirical baselines (SGD, SAG, SDCA, accelerated GD).  
- Weakness: inconsistency in stochastic complexity statements (O(κ) vs O(κ2)); Theorem 1 implies T = Ω(κ2 log(1/δ)), so stochastic complexity appears to be O(κ2 log(1/ε)).  
- Weakness: total oracle complexity can be worse than SAG/SDCA in finite-sum regimes, especially when κ is large.  
- Weakness: incomplete literature positioning—mixed update is a control-variate/variance-reduction idea and related prior hybrid/variance-reduction work (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012) is not fully discussed.  
- Weakness: does not treat plain convex (non-strongly convex) problems nor provide a regularization/smoothing path, limiting scope.  
- Quality: analysis is careful and uses standard tools plus martingale concentration, but omits practical aspects (parameter selection, misspecification behavior, realistic oracle model).  
- Clarity: algorithm presentation is clear but the problem setting and cost model are ambiguous; paper needs explicit separate treatments for expectation vs finite-sum cases and fixed complexity statements.  
- Originality: moderate—combines known control-variate variance-reduction ideas with an epoch-anchoring and shrinking-ball constraint, but feels incremental without stronger stochastic complexity or empirical validation.  
- Significance: potentially useful when full gradients are extremely costly and memory/constraints matter, but limited by κ2 stochastic dependence and lack of experiments.  
- Key questions for authors: provide adaptive schemes for unknown κ; reconcile O(κ) vs O(κ2); empirical/theoretical comparison to SAG/SDCA across n and κ; necessity of the shrinking-ball projection; extension to merely smooth convex case.  
- Pros summary: high-probability linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, works with constraints, low memory, clear analysis.  
- Cons summary: needs known κ, ambiguous oracle/cost model, inconsistency in complexity claims, no experiments, likely unattractive κ2 stochastic cost.  
- Recommendation: Reject—interesting but incremental theory, significant clarity and evaluation gaps; suggest fixing complexity inconsistency, improving positioning, adding adaptive parameter selection, and providing thorough experiments.
