This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ when solving smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The key idea is to combine full gradient evaluations with cheaper stochastic gradient evaluations within each epoch, achieving a linear convergence rate with O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients.

The work builds upon existing first-order methods for convex optimization, referencing foundational works by Boyd and Vandenberghe (2004) and Nesterov (2004, 2006). The paper clearly positions itself within the landscape of algorithms addressing the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost, contrasting full-gradient methods like accelerated gradient descent (Nesterov, 2006) with stochastic gradient methods (e.g., Ghadimi & Lan, 2013). It appropriately highlights the recent advancements in stochastic optimization, citing SAG (Schmidt et al., 2011) and SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013), and clearly states the goal of reducing full gradient evaluations without sacrificing convergence speed.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, providing a clear convergence rate for EMGD. The proof sketch provided is convincing, and the use of Lemma 1 (strongly convex function properties) is standard and appropriate. The claim of achieving linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee (unlike the in-expectation guarantee of SAG) is a notable strength.
* **Novelty:**  The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework, and the resulting independence of the full gradient count from the condition number κ, is a novel contribution. This addresses a significant challenge in ill-conditioned optimization problems.
* **Clarity (Theoretical Sections):** The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-defined. The explanations preceding the algorithm and the theorem are helpful in understanding the approach.
* **Significance:**  The potential for reducing the computational cost of optimization for large-scale, ill-conditioned problems is significant. The authors correctly point out the relevance to machine learning applications where such problems frequently arise. The discussion of potential parallelization benefits (leveraging distributed computing for full gradient computation) is also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of experimental results. While the theoretical analysis is sound, it’s crucial to demonstrate the practical performance of EMGD on real-world datasets or benchmark problems. This is particularly important given the complexity comparison (Section 3.4).
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:**  The authors acknowledge the need to know the condition number κ *a priori* as a drawback, but the discussion remains brief.  Exploring practical estimation techniques or adaptive strategies for determining κ would strengthen the paper.  Similarly, the lack of adaptive step-size selection is a limitation; SCDA and SAG incorporate mechanisms for this.
* **Incomplete Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper compares EMGD to other algorithms in terms of computational complexity (Table 2), the discussion lacks nuance. Specifically, the omission of Friedlander & Schmidt’s (2012) hybrid deterministic-stochastic method is a notable oversight, as it shares similarities with EMGD. A more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between EMGD, SAG, SDCA, and other relevant algorithms would be beneficial.
* **Clarity (Section 3.4):** The statements in Section 3.4 regarding when EMGD outperforms other algorithms (e.g., κ ≤ n²/3) need further justification and clarification. The “practical regime note” regarding regularization is insightful but could be expanded.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:**  Several minor points could improve clarity: adding a high-level intuition for some of the proof choices, clarifying the assumptions underlying Table 1’s citations (e.g., applicability conditions for SDCA), and consistently using either log or ln throughout the paper.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel algorithm with a solid theoretical foundation.
* Addresses an important problem in optimization.
* Potential for significant computational savings in specific scenarios.
* Clear and well-written (theoretical sections).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* Limited discussion of practical considerations.
* Incomplete comparison to related work.
* Potential limitations not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and theoretically significant contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed EMGD algorithm offers a novel approach to reducing the dependence on the condition number in strongly convex optimization. However, the lack of experimental results and the incomplete discussion of practical considerations raise concerns. I believe the paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, *contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses identified*. 

**Recommendation:**  I recommend a **weak accept** with the strong encouragement that the authors conduct experiments and address the aforementioned concerns, potentially via a rebuttal with concrete plans for these improvements.  If the authors can provide compelling evidence of EMGD’s performance and address the practical limitations, a stronger acceptance recommendation would be warranted.
