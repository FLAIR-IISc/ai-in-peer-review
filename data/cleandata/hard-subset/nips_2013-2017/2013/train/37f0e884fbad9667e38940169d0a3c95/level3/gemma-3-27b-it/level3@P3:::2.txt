This paper proposes a hybrid deterministic-stochastic first-order method, EMGD, for strongly convex optimization, aiming to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ when full gradient evaluations are expensive. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient steps within epochs, achieving a convergence rate of O(log(1/ε)) with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations – independent of κ. While the theoretical result is appealing, the paper suffers from significant limitations in its practical relevance and presentation.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical analysis, demonstrating the possibility of κ-independent full gradient complexity under specific conditions. The convergence analysis appears rigorous, building upon existing results in stochastic optimization. The motivation – reducing the cost of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned problems – is certainly relevant in many machine learning applications.

However, the weaknesses are substantial. The most critical flaw is the strong, and unaddressed, assumption that the condition number κ is *known a priori*. This is a major limitation, as accurately estimating κ can be as challenging as directly computing full gradients. The algorithm requires a step size η that depends on κ, and the paper provides *no* guidance on how to estimate κ or handle its misspecification. This severely limits practical applicability. Without addressing this, the theoretical guarantees become largely academic.  It’s essential to analyze, or even empirically test, the algorithm’s behavior when η (and hence κ) is set too large or too small, and to understand the sensitivity of performance to κ estimation errors.

Further, the paper lacks any experimental validation.  There are no simulations or real-world experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of EMGD, even under the idealized assumption of known κ.  This is particularly concerning given the algorithm’s dependence on κ.  Comparison to standard baselines like SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, and SDCA is completely absent, making it impossible to assess EMGD’s practical advantages.

The presentation lacks clarity in several areas. The algorithm is presented as a “mixed” update but offers little discussion relating it to simpler interleaving strategies. It’s unclear why the specific mixing strategy is necessary or superior to a straightforward alternation of full and stochastic gradient steps. Section 3.1 regarding the function oracle is misleading; it states that the assumption implies F(·) is L-smooth, which is already stated prior, and doesn’t add clarity. The optimization problem setting – finite-sum deterministic vs. stochastic approximation – isn't clearly specified throughout the paper.

The paper also misses relevant related work. It fails to adequately review and compare to prior hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods, specifically referencing Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), which presented similar ideas. This omission weakens the claim of novelty. Additionally, the analysis is restricted to strongly convex settings, neglecting the important case of plain convex optimization, where similar challenges with ill-conditioning arise.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretical result demonstrating κ-independent full gradient complexity (under the strong assumption of known κ).
*   Rigorously presented analysis building on existing optimization theory.
*   Relevant motivation for addressing ill-conditioned problems with expensive full gradients.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Crucial and unaddressed assumption of known condition number κ.
*   Lack of any experimental validation or comparison to baselines.
*   Insufficient discussion of the algorithm's relation to simpler interleaving strategies.
*   Omission of relevant related work (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012).
*   Restricted analysis to strongly convex settings.
*   Unclear problem setting and misleading wording in Section 3.1.



**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical result but falls short of being a strong contribution due to its significant practical limitations and lack of empirical evaluation. The assumption of known κ is a fatal flaw for real-world applicability, and the absence of experiments prevents any assessment of its performance. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper feels incomplete and would benefit greatly from addressing these shortcomings. I recommend **rejection** at this time, with encouragement to address the limitations outlined above before resubmission. Specifically, the authors should focus on developing heuristics for κ estimation and conducting thorough experiments to evaluate the algorithm's robustness to κ misspecification.
