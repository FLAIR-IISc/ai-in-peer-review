This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The main idea is to utilize both full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradients required, making the iteration complexity independent of the condition number. The algorithm is based on the epoch gradient descent and uses a combination of full and stochastic gradients to update the solution.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rate of EMGD, showing that it achieves a linear convergence rate with a high-probability guarantee. The theoretical strength of the paper lies in its ability to provide a simple and self-contained convergence proof. The novelty of the paper is in its use of mixed gradients for constrained smooth strongly-convex optimization.

The paper also provides a comparison of the computational complexity of EMGD with other algorithms, such as Nesterov's algorithm, SAG, and SDCA. The results show that EMGD beats Nesterov's algorithm if the condition number is less than or equal to n^(2/3), but SAG outperforms EMGD in many regimes and matches EMGD when the condition number is approximately n^(1/2).

However, the paper lacks concrete examples or experiments to demonstrate the empirical relevance of EMGD. The authors should consider adding experiments to show the practical benefits of their algorithm. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing methods, including a discussion of the limitations and drawbacks of EMGD.

One of the practical concerns with EMGD is that the epoch length must be fixed a priori, which requires knowledge of the condition number. This is in contrast to SAG and SDCA, which are more adaptive. The authors should consider discussing the implications of this limitation and potential ways to address it.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a discussion of the step-size and adaptivity of EMGD. Unlike SAG and SDCA, EMGD lacks automatic or adaptive step-size mechanisms, which could be a limitation in practice.
