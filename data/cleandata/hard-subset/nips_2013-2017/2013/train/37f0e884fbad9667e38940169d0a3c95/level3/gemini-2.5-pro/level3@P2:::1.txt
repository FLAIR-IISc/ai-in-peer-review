This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. The main idea is to structure the optimization into epochs. At the start of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. Then, a series of `T` updates are performed using a "mixed gradient," which combines the initial full gradient with a variance-reducing stochastic term. This approach cleverly trades a large number of expensive full gradient computations for many cheaper stochastic ones. The central theoretical result is that EMGD achieves an `ε`-optimal solution by computing `O(log 1/ε)` full gradients and `O(κ^2 log 1/ε)` stochastic gradients. This makes the number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number `κ`, a significant departure from classical methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, which requires `O(√κ log 1/ε)` full gradient calls. The work is positioned relative to recent methods that achieve linear convergence for finite-sum problems, such as SAG and SDCA.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution is the novel hybrid approach that makes the number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number. The construction of the "mixed gradient" as a variance-reduced estimator is elegant. Achieving linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee is a strong theoretical result, arguably more powerful than the in-expectation guarantees common to related stochastic methods like SAG.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convergence proof presented in Section 4 is self-contained, relatively simple, and easy to follow. This clarity and simplicity in the analysis is a commendable strength.
*   **Generality:** A key advantage of EMGD is its applicability to general constrained optimization problems (provided the projection is efficient). This contrasts sharply with methods like SAG and SDCA, which are primarily designed for unconstrained objectives over finite sums, thus broadening the potential impact of this work.

### Weaknesses

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of experiments. The claims are purely theoretical. Without empirical evidence, it is difficult to assess the practical relevance of EMGD. How does it compare to Nesterov's method, SAG, or SDCA on real datasets? How sensitive is it to its hyperparameters? An experimental section demonstrating the algorithm's performance in a relevant setting would substantially strengthen the paper.
*   **Practicality and Parameterization:** The algorithm requires the condition number `κ` to be known beforehand to set the epoch length `T`. This is a major practical limitation, as `κ` is often unknown in practice. While the authors mention estimating `κ` as future work, its absence makes the proposed method less practical than more adaptive algorithms like SDCA or heuristics used with SAG.
*   **Incomplete Comparison to Related Work:** The complexity comparison in Table 2 is useful but also reveals that EMGD is not always superior to its competitors. For example, its complexity is worse than SAG when `κ > n^(1/2)`. The paper should be more explicit about the practical regimes where EMGD is expected to outperform other methods. For instance, in common machine learning settings with regularization `λ = O(1/n)`, we have `κ = O(n)`, a regime where EMGD may not be competitive. Furthermore, the paper cites the hybrid deterministic-stochastic method of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) but fails to discuss the connections or differences, which is a missed opportunity to better contextualize the work.
*   **Clarity:** While the paper is generally well-written, the discussion of advantages in Section 3.4 regarding memory and parallelization is vague and could be substantiated further. The analysis section would benefit from more high-level intuition explaining the purpose of key steps in the derivation.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and elegant algorithm for smooth, strongly convex optimization.
2.  It provides a sound theoretical analysis with a strong high-probability convergence guarantee, a significant result.
3.  The method is applicable to constrained optimization problems, giving it broader potential applicability than competitors like SAG/SDCA.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The lack of any experimental results makes it impossible to judge the algorithm's practical performance and significance.
2.  The requirement to know the condition number `κ` is a serious practical drawback.
3.  The theoretical complexity is not always favorable compared to state-of-the-art methods, and the paper does not adequately characterize the regimes where EMGD excels.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

This is a theoretically interesting paper with a clean and novel result. To be acceptable for NIPS, I believe the authors need to address the lack of empirical validation and the practical limitations.

1.  **Experiments:** I strongly recommend adding an experimental section. This could involve synthetic data to verify the theoretical rates and real-world machine learning tasks (e.g., logistic regression on a standard dataset) to compare EMGD against Nesterov's method, SAG, and perhaps the method of Friedlander & Schmidt.
2.  **Discussion of `κ`:** Please be more upfront about the limitation of requiring knowledge of `κ`. The discussion in the conclusion is a good start, but this should be framed as a key drawback in the main body of the paper.
3.  **Expanded Comparisons:** Please expand Section 3.4. A detailed paragraph comparing your approach to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) is necessary. Also, please elaborate on the regimes where `κ ≤ n^(2/3)` is a realistic assumption in machine learning.
4.  **Minor Edits:**
    *   Please clarify the citations and assumptions for the complexities listed in Table 1.
    *   Ensure consistent notation (e.g., `log` vs. `ln`).
    *   The claims about memory and parallelization advantages in Section 3.4 need to be more concrete to be convincing.

Overall, this is a promising theoretical contribution. If the authors can provide convincing experiments and a more thorough discussion of the practical aspects of their algorithm in a rebuttal, I would be willing to raise my score.
