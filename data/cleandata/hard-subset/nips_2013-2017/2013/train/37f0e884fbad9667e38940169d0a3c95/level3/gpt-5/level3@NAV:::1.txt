Summary: The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid first-order method for smooth, strongly convex optimization that computes one full gradient per epoch and performs many inexpensive stochastic steps within the epoch. The key result is linear convergence with an epoch count O(log(1/ε)) independent of the condition number κ, while using O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls overall (the paper contains an inconsistency, sometimes stating O(κ log(1/ε)); please reconcile). Relative to Nesterov’s accelerated gradient (O(√κ log(1/ε))), this reduces expensive full-gradient evaluations; relative to SAG/SDCA, EMGD extends to constrained problems and comes with a high-probability rate rather than in-expectation. The approach relates to semi-/hybrid stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012), SAG, and SDCA, but is simpler and self-contained.

Quality: The analysis is technically solid and yields a clear high-probability linear rate with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls. The proof is streamlined, though it relies on a shrinking-radius constraint set and fixed per-epoch length. Practical aspects are underdeveloped: no experiments, no discussion of tuning, and limited guidance on when the κ2 stochastic work is advantageous. Please resolve the κ vs κ2 stochastic complexity discrepancy and clearly state assumptions (e.g., constrained projection solvability, strong convexity, and smoothness).

Clarity: Generally readable, but Section 3.4 needs expansion with a detailed, apples-to-apples comparison (cost models, storage, constraints). Clarify Table 1 citations/assumptions (e.g., SCDA applicability) and the use of the gradient-expectation identity. Fix typos and enforce consistent notation for the domain, norms, and log/ln.

Originality: Mixing one full gradient per epoch with many stochastic steps for strongly convex constrained problems and proving a simple, high-probability linear rate is novel. Please cite and contrast Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and related hybrid/semi-stochastic methods.

Significance: Theoretically significant: a clean mechanism to decouple full-gradient calls from κ. Empirical significance is unclear without experiments. In finite-sum settings, EMGD beats Nesterov when κ ≤ n2/3, but SAG/SDCA often dominate and match EMGD near κ ≈ n1/2. Discuss realistic ML regimes (e.g., regularization ~ C/n) where EMGD is competitive.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, simple, high-probability linear convergence proof
- Condition-number–independent number of full gradients
- Handles constrained problems; low memory footprint

Cons against acceptance:
- No experiments; unclear practical relevance
- Missing related work and nuanced comparisons
- Fixed epoch length and non-adaptive step-size; needs κ a priori
- κ vs κ2 stochastic complexity inconsistency; clarity/notation issues

Recommendation: Technically sound and potentially impactful; accept conditional on adding experiments and addressing the comparison, citation, and clarity issues.
