This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages both full and stochastic gradients to achieve a linear convergence rate for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The main idea is to divide the optimization process into epochs, where each epoch consists of a mixed gradient descent step that combines the full gradient and stochastic gradients.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm, showing that it can achieve a high-probability O(log(1/ε)) convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over the O(√κ log(1/ε)) rate achieved by full gradient methods. The analysis assumes that the condition number κ (L and λ) is known a priori, which is a crucial assumption.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm and its analysis. However, there are several limitations and areas for improvement. Firstly, the analysis assumes that the condition number κ is known, but in practice, this is often not the case. The authors do not provide any guidance on how to estimate or adapt to unknown or misspecified κ.

Secondly, the algorithm requires a hyperparameter η that depends on κ, but no estimation procedure or heuristics are provided. It is unclear how to set η in practice, and the authors do not provide any experiments to validate the theory or study the empirical behavior under misspecification.

Thirdly, the paper does not consider non-strongly convex settings, which are important in practice. The authors only focus on strongly convex problems, and it is unclear whether the algorithm can be extended to plain convex problems.

Finally, the paper lacks a clear comparison to prior hybrid deterministic-stochastic work, such as Friedlander & Schmidt (2012). The authors also do not provide any experiments to compare their algorithm to standard baselines (SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, SDCA, etc.).

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients. However, the idea of mixing deterministic and stochastic steps is not new, and the authors do not provide a clear discussion on how their algorithm relates to simple interleaving of deterministic and stochastic steps.

Overall, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of optimization, but it has several limitations that need to be addressed. With additional analysis, experiments, and comparisons to prior work, the paper has the potential to make a more significant impact.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel algorithm that achieves a linear convergence rate for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems.
* The analysis is thorough and provides a clear understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
* The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of optimization.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The analysis assumes that the condition number κ is known a priori, which is a crucial assumption that may not hold in practice.
* The algorithm requires a hyperparameter η that depends on κ, but no estimation procedure or heuristics are provided.
* The paper lacks a clear comparison to prior hybrid deterministic-stochastic work and standard baselines.
* The paper does not consider non-strongly convex settings, which are important in practice.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide additional analysis on how to estimate or adapt to unknown or misspecified κ.
* Develop an estimation procedure or heuristics for setting η in practice.
* Compare the algorithm to prior hybrid deterministic-stochastic work and standard baselines.
* Consider non-strongly convex settings and extend the algorithm to plain convex problems.
* Provide experiments to validate the theory and study the empirical behavior under misspecification.
