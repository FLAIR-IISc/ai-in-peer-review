- Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm combining full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with full-gradient access independent of condition number κ.
- Main theoretical result: EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.
- Differs from SAG/SDCA by handling constrained problems, requiring O(d) memory (vs Ω(n)), and providing high-probability linear convergence (not just expectation).
- Strength: The mixed-gradient approach and high-probability convergence guarantee are novel and supported by a self-contained, elegant proof.
- Strength: Practical advantages include constrained optimization support, memory efficiency, and potential for parallelization.
- Strength: Convergence analysis is technically thorough with clear conditions on epoch length and step size.
- Weakness: No empirical experiments are provided to validate practical benefits or runtime behavior.
- Weakness: Requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ, limiting practical applicability compared to adaptive methods.
- Weakness: Paper does not clearly identify realistic regimes where EMGD outperforms alternatives; EMGD only beats Nesterov when κ ≤ n^(2/3).
- Weakness: Related work comparison is incomplete (e.g., missing discussion of Friedlander & Schmidt 2012 and other hybrids).
- Weakness: Some proof and algorithm explanations lack intuition; minor notational inconsistencies and typos present.
- Quality: Technically sound with rigorous proofs, but weakened by absence of empirical validation.
- Clarity: Generally organized but would benefit from more intuitive explanations and notation fixes.
- Originality: Presents a novel hybrid optimization approach distinct from recent stochastic methods.
- Significance: Theoretical contribution is important, but practical impact is limited by κ requirement and lack of experiments.
- Pros for acceptance: Novel theoretical contribution, constrained-problem applicability, memory efficiency, rigorous analysis, and parallelization potential.
- Cons for acceptance: No experiments, requires known κ, limited practical regime advantage, incomplete related-work comparison, unclear use cases.
- Recommendation: Reject, with suggestion to add empirical validation, address κ dependence and related work, and clarify regimes where EMGD is preferable.
