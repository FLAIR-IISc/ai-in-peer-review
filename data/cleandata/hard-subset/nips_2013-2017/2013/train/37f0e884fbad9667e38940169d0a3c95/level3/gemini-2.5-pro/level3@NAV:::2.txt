This paper proposes a hybrid first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems. The method combines a full gradient oracle with a stochastic gradient oracle to achieve a fast convergence rate. The main theoretical contribution is a high-probability guarantee that an ε-optimal solution can be found with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient computations, a number independent of the problem's condition number κ. This is achieved at the expense of O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient calls. This work fits into the growing literature on variance-reduced and hybrid methods that aim for the best of both deterministic and stochastic worlds.

The primary strength of the paper is its novel theoretical goal of making the number of expensive full gradient calls independent of the condition number, which is a significant challenge for ill-conditioned problems. The analysis provides a high-probability bound, which is stronger than the more common in-expectation guarantees for similar stochastic methods.

Despite the interesting premise, the paper has several major weaknesses that undermine its contribution. The most critical is the assumption that the condition number κ is known a priori. The algorithm's key hyperparameter, η, depends directly on κ, yet the authors provide no procedure for estimating it or any analysis of the algorithm's performance under misspecification. This severely limits the practical utility of the proposed method.

Furthermore, the paper is entirely theoretical, lacking any experimental validation. Without experiments, the claims are not substantiated, and it is impossible to assess the algorithm's empirical behavior, especially its sensitivity to the choice of η. An empirical comparison against standard baselines like SGD, SAG, SDCA, and other hybrid methods is essential for a contribution in this area.

The related work section is also incomplete. It notably omits discussion of prior hybrid deterministic-stochastic approaches, such as Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), which is highly relevant. Finally, the analysis is limited to the strongly convex setting, ignoring the general convex case, and the problem formulation could be clearer in distinguishing between finite-sum and general stochastic approximation settings.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical direction, the work is premature. The reliance on a known condition number without providing practical guidance and the complete absence of empirical evaluation are critical flaws. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the estimation of κ and providing a thorough experimental comparison against relevant state-of-the-art methods.
