- Paper tackles smooth, strongly convex optimization and introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an epoch-based hybrid using one full gradient per epoch plus variance-reduced stochastic updates.  
- Main theoretical claim: EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, making full-gradient calls independent of condition number κ.  
- Contrasts with classical methods (e.g., Nesterov) that require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls; positioned relative to finite-sum methods like SAG and SDCA.  

Strengths:
- Originality: novel hybrid approach and elegant variance-reduced "mixed gradient" construction that decouples full-gradient count from κ.  
- Strong theoretical guarantee: linear convergence with high-probability bounds (stronger than many in-expectation results).  
- Generality: applicable to constrained optimization (with efficient projection), broader than SAG/SDCA which focus on unconstrained finite-sum problems.  

Weaknesses:
- No experiments: complete absence of empirical validation prevents assessment of practical performance and hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Practicality: requires knowing κ to set epoch length T, which is often unknown and limits practicality.  
- Incomplete comparisons: complexity not always superior (e.g., worse than SAG when κ > n^{1/2}); lacks discussion of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and practical regimes where EMGD outperforms others.  
- Clarity gaps: vague claims about memory and parallelization; analysis needs more high-level intuition for key steps.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Presents a novel, elegant algorithm for a fundamental optimization problem.  
- Provides a clear, self-contained theoretical analysis with strong high-probability convergence.  
- Broader applicability to constrained problems than many competitors.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of empirical results precludes judging practical significance.  
- Need to know κ is a major practical drawback.  
- Theoretical complexity is not uniformly better than state-of-the-art; paper does not characterize favorable regimes well.  

Detailed suggestions:
- Add experiments (synthetic and real tasks, compare to Nesterov, SAG, SDCA, and Friedlander & Schmidt) to validate theory and show practical behavior.  
- Explicitly discuss the κ requirement and propose/implement estimation or adaptive schemes.  
- Expand comparisons, especially with Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), and clarify regimes (e.g., when κ ≤ n^{2/3}) where EMGD is advantageous.  
- Minor edits: clarify citations/assumptions in tables, ensure consistent notation (log vs ln), and make memory/parallelization claims concrete.
