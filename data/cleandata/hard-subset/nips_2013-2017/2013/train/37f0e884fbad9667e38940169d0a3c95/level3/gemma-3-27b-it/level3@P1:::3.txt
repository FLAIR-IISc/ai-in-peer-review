This paper introduces the Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization, aiming to reduce the computational cost associated with ill-conditioned problems. The core idea is to combine full gradient and stochastic gradient information to achieve a convergence rate independent of the condition number κ. Specifically, EMGD achieves an ǫ-optimal solution using O(log 1/ǫ) full gradients and O(κ<sup>2</sup> log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradients. The theoretical analysis appears sound, building upon existing results in stochastic and deterministic optimization, referencing foundational works like Boyd & Vandenberghe (2004) and Nesterov (1988, 2013).

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical novelty and rigor. The algorithm offers a potentially valuable trade-off between the number of full gradient evaluations (expensive) and stochastic gradient evaluations (cheap) for ill-conditioned problems. The proof of convergence, while detailed, demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying optimization theory, drawing connections to related work such as stochastic average gradient (SAG) [Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013] and stochastic dual coordinate ascent (SDCA) [Lan, 2012]. The inclusion of a high-probability convergence guarantee is also a positive aspect, differentiating it from some algorithms with only expected convergence rates. The comparison to existing methods (Table 2) is a useful contribution.

However, the paper’s significance is questionable due to its limited practical relevance to contemporary machine learning applications. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, stating the method's advantage holds when κ ≤ n<sup>2/3</sup> (where *n* is likely the number of data points). In many machine learning contexts, strong convexity arises from regularization, implying κ ≈ C/λ, where C is a measure of data complexity and λ is the regularization parameter. Consequently, the condition for EMGD to outperform full gradient descent becomes λ ≈ C<sup>3</sup>/n<sup>3</sup>, a scenario unlikely to occur in practice. This substantially limits the applicability of EMGD in scenarios where it could offer benefits. Furthermore, while achieving a linear convergence rate is positive, batch gradient descent offers deterministic convergence, which may be preferable in certain applications. The authors should address this trade-off more directly.

Clarity is generally good, though some minor improvements are needed. Specifically, equation (6) should read ||w<sup>*</sup> − ŵ||<sup>2</sup>. The function used in line 288 for equation (7) requires clarification.  The equality in line 286 should be removed to enhance readability. In equation (13), absolute values should be replaced with norms. The paper would benefit from explicitly stating L ≥ λ, even if it’s considered obvious.  In equation (4), *x*<sup>*</sup> should be *ŵ*, and *f* should be *F*. The authors also note that the first term is larger based on Lemma 1, so acknowledging this explicitly would enhance clarity. A precise definition of the condition number κ as a function of λ and L is also necessary.

The originality of the work lies in the specific combination of full and stochastic gradients within the EMGD framework and the accompanying convergence analysis. It builds upon existing techniques (epoch gradient descent [Hazan et al., 2014]) but introduces a novel hybrid approach. However, the potential practical impact is diminished by the limitations discussed above. The related work section is comprehensive, covering relevant literature in both deterministic and stochastic optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel algorithm with a rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Potentially valuable trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations for specific problem structures.
*   High-probability convergence guarantee.
*   Comprehensive related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited practical relevance to standard machine learning problems due to the restrictive condition on the condition number.
*   Lack of a strong justification for why EMGD would be preferred over standard batch gradient descent in typical ML settings.
*   The benefits are unclear compared to existing efficient methods for large-scale problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound and novel algorithm, its practical significance is questionable.  I recommend **rejection** pending a substantial revision that addresses the concerns regarding its relevance to machine learning applications. The authors need to provide a compelling argument, backed by analysis or experiments, demonstrating scenarios where EMGD offers a clear advantage over existing methods, particularly standard batch gradient descent.  Simply acknowledging the limitations is insufficient; a deeper investigation into the conditions under which EMGD is beneficial is required.
