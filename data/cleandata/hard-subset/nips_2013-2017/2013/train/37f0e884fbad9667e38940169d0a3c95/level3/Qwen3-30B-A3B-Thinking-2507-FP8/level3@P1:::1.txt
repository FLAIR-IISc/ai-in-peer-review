

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm for smooth strongly convex optimization that achieves linear convergence with condition number independent access to full gradients. The key insight is to mix full and stochastic gradients within each epoch, allowing the algorithm to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log 1/ε) to O(log 1/ε), while using O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients. This addresses a fundamental limitation in traditional gradient methods for ill-conditioned problems, where the condition number κ creates a computational bottleneck.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a clear and self-contained convergence analysis. The main result (Theorem 1) demonstrates that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution with high probability, providing a stronger guarantee than SAG (which only achieves convergence in expectation). The proof is elegant, leveraging the mixed gradient construction to maintain bounded stochastic error through the shrinking domain constraint. This approach is novel for constrained optimization, as existing methods like SAG and SDCA are limited to unconstrained problems.

Theoretical strengths are significant: EMGD achieves linear convergence (O(log 1/ε)) with a high-probability guarantee, unlike SAG/SDCA whose linear convergence holds only in expectation. The algorithm also offers potential memory advantages (Ω(d) vs. Ω(n) storage) and parallelization benefits (the full gradient step can be distributed), though these are not fully developed. The comparison to prior work in Section 3.4 is valuable but needs expansion to better contextualize EMGD's position relative to SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's accelerated method.

However, several weaknesses limit the paper's impact. The most significant is the absence of empirical validation - no experiments demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical claims. Without empirical evidence, it's unclear whether the algorithm's theoretical advantages translate to real-world performance gains. The paper also lacks discussion of practical limitations: EMGD requires prior knowledge of κ (the condition number) to set epoch length, unlike adaptive methods like SAG. In realistic machine learning settings where regularization parameter τ ~ C/n (leading to κ ~ n/C'), the paper's claim that EMGD outperforms Nesterov (when κ ≤ n²/³) may not hold in practice, as SAG typically outperforms Nesterov when κ ≤ n/8.

The paper's significance is theoretically sound but practically limited without experiments. While the algorithm is elegant and the proof is clean, the lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess whether this approach offers meaningful advantages over established methods like SAG or SDCA in actual applications. The authors should clarify in which specific scenarios EMGD provides a practical advantage, as the current analysis suggests it may not outperform SAG in most machine learning contexts.

Clarity is generally good but could be improved. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of why the mixed gradient construction works, particularly regarding the shrinking domain constraint. Some notation inconsistencies (e.g., for domain constraints, log vs. ln usage) should be addressed, and the relation to Friedlander & Schmidt's hybrid method (2012) should be explicitly discussed. The claim about computational complexity in Table 2 needs better justification for the specific conditions under which EMGD outperforms other methods.

The quality of the theoretical contribution is high, with careful analysis and sound proofs. The authors are honest about the limitations, though they could elaborate more on when the algorithm would be preferable. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding empirical results demonstrating the practical benefits of EMGD, particularly in comparison to SAG and SDCA on standard machine learning problems.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper with a novel theoretical contribution to optimization. The main strength is the elegant proof of linear convergence with high probability. However, the lack of empirical validation and insufficient discussion of practical limitations and comparative advantages significantly reduces the paper's impact. With additional experiments and a more detailed discussion of practical applicability, this would be an excellent contribution. As is, it's a solid theoretical paper that could be strengthened by addressing these practical concerns. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the empirical validation and comparative discussion in the final version.
