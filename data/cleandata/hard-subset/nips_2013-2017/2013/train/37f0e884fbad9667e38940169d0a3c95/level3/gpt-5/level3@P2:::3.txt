Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization and aims to reduce the number of expensive full-gradient computations required to reach an ε-optimal solution. The proposed algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), alternates epochs; at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at a reference point w̄k, and within the epoch it performs “mixed gradient” steps using the control-variate style estimator ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), together with projections onto shrinking balls around w̄k. The analysis shows linear convergence: an ε-suboptimal solution is obtained with O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full-gradient oracle and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle (κ = L/λ is the condition number). The high-probability guarantee is obtained via martingale concentration.

Relative to deterministic first-order methods (e.g., Nesterov’s accelerated gradient with O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients), EMGD trades most full-gradient evaluations for cheaper stochastic ones while keeping the number of full gradients O(log(1/ε)) and independent of κ. Relative to stochastic methods, EMGD achieves linear convergence under smooth strong convexity. The approach is conceptually related to semi-stochastic and variance-reduced methods that interleave full and stochastic gradients and use control variates, and it is compared to SAG and SDCA in the finite-sum setting.

Quality
– Technical soundness: The main theorem and its proof strategy (epoch-wise variance reduction, shrinking domains, and Azuma-Hoeffding concentration) appear correct and well-executed. The per-epoch bound T = Ω((L/λ)^2 log(1/δ)) clarifies why the total number of stochastic gradients scales as O(κ^2 log(1/ε)). The high-probability guarantee is a nice feature.
– Completeness: The theoretical contribution is complete. However, there is no empirical evaluation, and key practical trade-offs with standard batch methods in common ML regimes are not convincingly analyzed.
– Presentation issues: There are several small notational and editorial mistakes (listed below) that should be fixed. Also, an explicit and careful comparison to batch gradient descent in typical ML parameter regimes is currently missing.

Clarity
The algorithm and proof are generally readable. The shrinking-ball projection and the control-variate mixed gradient are clearly defined, and the oracle model is explicit. That said, the manuscript would benefit from cleaning up notation, making the dependence on κ and δ consistently explicit, and adding a careful discussion of when the proposed trade-off leads to actual wall-clock speedups.

Originality
The idea of combining a periodically refreshed full gradient with per-iteration stochastic corrections to reduce variance is interesting in this oracle setting and is presented with a clean high-probability analysis and a constraint-friendly projection step. The approach is in the spirit of “semi-stochastic” and incremental/variance-reduced methods (SAG/SDCA and related hybrid methods), but the algorithmic mix and analysis under an oracle model, with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, is a valuable angle. Within the finite-sum setting, the novelty relative to contemporaneous variance-reduction methods should be discussed more thoroughly.

Significance
Theoretical significance: Establishing O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient complexity with high probability is appealing. However, the total cost includes O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients per the theorem. In many ML settings with strong convexity induced by regularization, typical parameter scalings may limit practical gains over standard batch methods. The paper’s own comparison notes regimes where other methods (SAG/SDCA) can be superior in computational complexity. The absence of experiments and a careful complexity-to-runtime mapping weakens the practical impact.

Major concerns and suggestions
– Practical relevance to ML: The paper should more carefully analyze realistic regimes for L and λ when strong convexity arises from regularization. As noted in my notes, if effective strong convexity grows with sample size (e.g., λ ≈ C·N under certain data/regularization couplings), EMGD can beat batch methods only in a narrow and arguably uninteresting regime (N = O(C^3/L^3) in the stated back-of-the-envelope). Please provide:
  • A principled discussion of parameter regimes in modern ML (classification/regression with bounded features) and how κ scales with N and regularization.
  • Concrete wall-clock models (cost of one full gradient vs one stochastic gradient) and comparisons to accelerated gradient, SAG, SDCA in these regimes.
  • Experiments illustrating the claimed advantages, or at least synthetic studies to validate the predicted crossovers.
– Deterministic vs probabilistic guarantees: Batch gradient methods enjoy deterministic linear convergence under smooth strong convexity, whereas EMGD’s rate holds with high probability. Please discuss the implications (and how δ should be scheduled across epochs).
– Knowledge of κ: The method assumes knowledge of κ (or an upper bound) for setting T and η; the conclusion mentions this as a drawback. Please elaborate practical strategies to estimate or adapt to κ online.

Pros
– Clean algorithm with a simple mixed-gradient estimator and projection, applicable to constrained problems.
– High-probability linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, independent of κ.
– Low memory footprint (O(d)) and conceptually amenable to distributed computation for the full-gradient step.

Cons
– Total stochastic gradient complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) can be large for ill-conditioned problems.
– Practical advantage over accelerated batch methods in common ML regimes is not convincingly justified.
– No empirical results; limited discussion of realistic cost models and crossover regimes.
– Several minor presentation/notation issues.

Minor comments and corrections
– Equation (6): the normed term should read ∥w* − ŵ∥^2 in the context where you use it; ensure consistent notation with Lemma 1.
– Around line 286: remove an unnecessary equality that reduces clarity.
– Equation (7): explicitly state which function the condition refers to and the context for Δ1 and the domain ball.
– In (13), replace absolute values by vector norms where appropriate.
– State and briefly justify L ≥ λ explicitly (you later define κ = L/λ, but making the inequality explicit helps readability).
– In (4), the symbol x̂ should be ŵ, and f should be F; also note the first term in the max is larger by Lemma 1.
– Precisely define κ = L/λ where L is the Lipschitz constant of ∇F and λ is the strong convexity parameter. Ensure this definition is consistently used throughout.
– Check for consistency between abstract and theorem regarding the stochastic gradient complexity (κ vs κ^2).

Questions for the authors
– Can you provide adaptive variants that do not require κ a priori?
– How sensitive is performance to the shrinking-ball schedule (Δk/√2)? Would a data-driven radius or no projection work with a similar proof?
– In the finite-sum setting, how does EMGD perform empirically relative to SAG/SDCA under memory constraints and with constraints on W?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contribution is solid and the high-probability analysis is clean, but the paper currently lacks convincing evidence of practical benefit in ML settings and does not fully clarify when its trade-offs lead to real computational gains over strong baselines. Strengthening the empirical and comparative analysis and addressing the clarity issues would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Reviewer confidence
High on the theoretical reading and comparisons; medium on the practical regime critique pending empirical evidence.
