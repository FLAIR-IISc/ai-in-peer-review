This paper proposes a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems. The central goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, which for accelerated methods scales as O(√κ log 1/ε), where κ is the condition number. The key idea is to leverage access to both a full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle. The proposed EMGD algorithm operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes one full gradient. Then, within the epoch, it performs a series of updates using a "mixed gradient" that combines the initial full gradient with variance-reducing stochastic terms. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients. This result is significant because it makes the number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ, effectively trading many full gradients for a larger number of cheap stochastic gradients. The work is positioned relative to classical deterministic methods (Nesterov's AGD), stochastic methods (SGD), and more recent variance-reduced methods for finite-sum problems (SAG, SDCA).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a very important problem in large-scale optimization: the high cost of full gradient computations for ill-conditioned problems. The goal of making the number of full gradient calls independent of the condition number is novel and ambitious. The theoretical result, achieving a linear convergence rate in terms of full gradient calls, is a strong and potentially impactful contribution.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous, high-probability convergence proof for the proposed algorithm. The analysis, based on an epoch-based scheme with a shrinking optimization domain, is technically sound and leads to the main claim of O(log 1/ε) full gradient complexity.
3.  **Algorithm Design:** The "mixed gradient" update `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is an interesting variance reduction technique, where a single full gradient calculated at the beginning of an epoch serves as an anchor to control the variance of subsequent stochastic steps within that epoch.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Unknown Parameters:** The most significant weakness of this work is its reliance on prior knowledge of the problem's condition number κ (or the smoothness L and strong convexity λ parameters). Both the step size η = 1/[L√T] and, more critically, the number of inner iterations T ≥ 1152(L²/λ²)ln(1/δ) depend directly on these parameters. In most practical scenarios, κ is unknown and difficult to estimate accurately. The paper offers no guidance, heuristics, or analysis for how to set these hyperparameters when κ is unknown.
2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical and contains no experiments. This is a major omission. An experimental section is crucial to:
    *   Validate the theoretical convergence rates in practice.
    *   Compare EMGD against relevant and strong baselines such as Nesterov's AGD, SGD with averaging, SAG, SDCA, and other hybrid methods on real or synthetic datasets.
    *   Investigate the practical performance and robustness of the algorithm, especially its sensitivity to the choice of T and η. Without experiments, the practical utility of the proposed method remains entirely speculative. For instance, how does the algorithm behave if the provided estimate of κ is incorrect?
3.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to discuss or compare against prior work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods. For example, Friedlander & Schmidt (2012, "Hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods for data fitting") propose methods that also interleave full gradient (or Newton-like) steps with stochastic gradient steps and demonstrate strong empirical performance. A discussion of such work is necessary to properly contextualize the paper's contribution to this specific subfield.
4.  **Ambiguous Problem Setting:** There is a lack of clarity in the problem setup. Section 3.1 assumes a general stochastic approximation setting where F(w) = E[f(w)]. However, the comparisons in Section 3.4 are made against algorithms like SAG and SDCA, which are specifically designed for the finite-sum setting F(w) = (1/n)Σ fi(w). While the finite-sum setting is a special case of stochastic approximation, the structure it provides is key to the performance of SAG and SDCA. The paper should be more precise about its target problem domain and ensure its assumptions and comparisons are consistent.
5.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is limited to the strongly convex setting. While this is a common starting point, many important machine learning problems are convex but not strongly convex. A discussion of potential extensions to the non-strongly convex case would strengthen the paper's perceived impact.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    1.  Addresses a significant problem in optimization with a novel approach.
    2.  Presents a strong and interesting theoretical result: linear convergence in terms of full gradient evaluations, independent of κ.
    3.  The algorithm and its analysis introduce potentially useful techniques (epoch-based variance reduction).

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    1.  The algorithm and theory critically depend on knowing the condition number κ, a major practical limitation that is not addressed.
    2.  The complete absence of experiments makes it impossible to assess the practical value, robustness, or competitiveness of the proposed method.
    3.  Key related work on hybrid deterministic-stochastic optimization is missing.
    4.  The problem setting is not defined with sufficient clarity, leading to potentially mismatched comparisons.

In its current form, the paper presents an interesting theoretical idea but falls short of being a complete contribution due to the strong, unrealistic assumptions and the lack of any empirical validation. The weaknesses, particularly the reliance on a known κ and the absence of experiments, significantly undermine the paper's potential impact and practical relevance.
