Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method that mixes one full gradient with many stochastic gradients per epoch. Each epoch anchors at w̄k, computes ∇F(w̄k) once, and then performs T projected “mixed” gradient steps with update g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇ft(wt) − ∇ft(w̄k), where ft is drawn from a function oracle. The algorithm constrains updates to a shrinking ball around w̄k and averages the iterates. The main theoretical claim is high-probability linear convergence: to reach ε accuracy, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, where κ = L/λ is the condition number. The motivation is to make the number of full-gradient evaluations independent of κ, which is attractive when full gradients are expensive.

Relative to deterministic first-order methods, EMGD aims to improve on Nesterov’s accelerated methods that require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients for smooth strongly convex problems. In stochastic and finite-sum optimization, there is a substantial literature: epoch-based strongly convex SGD (e.g., Hazan & Kale; Lan; Rakhlin et al.) with O(1/(λ ε)) rates; “semi-stochastic” or hybrid methods that interleave stochastic and deterministic steps (e.g., Marti; Marti & Fuchs; Friedlander & Schmidt, SISC 2012); and variance-reduction methods for finite sums with linear rates such as SAG (Le Roux et al.) and SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang). EMGD’s mixed gradient is a control-variate construction closely related in spirit to this variance-reduction family, though EMGD additionally includes a shrinking trust-region-like projection and provides high-probability bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple hybrid that uses one full gradient per epoch plus cheap stochastic corrections.
- Clear theoretical goal: reduce the dependence on κ in the number of full-gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)).
- High-probability linear convergence analysis (as opposed to in-expectation) and applicability to constrained domains via projected updates.
- Low memory footprint compared to methods like SAG that store past gradients.

Weaknesses
- Oracle/modeling mismatch and unclear problem setting. Section 3.1 adopts an expectation oracle Of with F(w) = E[f(w)] but later comparisons assume a finite-sum setting where ∇F is computed by a full pass. The cost model and assumptions should be clearly separated for expectation vs. finite-sum cases.
- κ must be known. The step size η and the per-epoch length T depend on L and λ; there is no analysis or guidance for unknown or misspecified κ, which is common in practice.
- Sensitivity not studied. No experiments and no robustness analysis for over/under-estimated κ or η; no empirical comparison against baselines (SGD/averaged SGD, SAG, SDCA, accelerated GD).
- Complexity inconsistency. The paper states both O(κ log(1/ε)) and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients; Theorem 1 implies T = Ω(κ2 log(1/δ)), so the total stochastic complexity is O(κ2 log(1/ε)) per the theorem. This inconsistency must be resolved.
- Total oracle complexity is not competitive in finite-sum regimes. Compared to SAG/SDCA (O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) under their assumptions), EMGD’s O(log(1/ε)) full gradients plus O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients can be worse when κ is large.
- Incomplete positioning relative to prior hybrid/deterministic-stochastic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) and variance-reduction methods; the “mixed” update can be framed and discussed as a control-variate variance-reduction technique.
- Does not treat plain convex (non-strongly convex) cases or provide a path via regularization/smoothing; this limits scope.

Quality
The analysis is careful and the high-probability linear rate is proved using standard smooth/strongly convex tools, martingale concentration, and an epoch-based trust region. However, critical practical aspects are missing: how to choose η, T, and m without knowing κ; what happens under misspecification; and empirical validation. The assumption of a full-gradient oracle side-by-side with an expectation oracle is not realistic unless F is a finite sum; this should be reconciled. The complexity statement inconsistency (O(κ) vs O(κ2)) needs correction.

Clarity
The algorithm is presented clearly, but the setting and cost model are ambiguous (expectation vs. finite-sum). The manuscript would benefit from:
- Explicitly stating two settings and giving separate complexity/cost discussions.
- Reconciling the O(κ) vs. O(κ2) stochastic complexity.
- A more thorough related-work section on hybrid methods and variance reduction (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012; stronger discussion of SAG/SDCA assumptions and results).
- Minor editorial fixes (typos, consistent notation, equation numbering).

Originality
Moderate. The control-variate “mixed” gradient and epoch-anchoring are conceptually close to variance-reduction methods and epoch-based SGD. The novelty is in the specific combination with a shrinking ball constraint to control variance and in highlighting the independence from κ in the count of full-gradient calls. Without empirical validation or sharper stochastic complexity, the methodological advance feels incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful when full gradients are extremely costly and stochastic gradients are cheap, and when constraints and low memory are important. However, the κ2 dependence in total stochastic-gradient calls and the lack of experiments limit impact. Competing methods (SAG/SDCA) offer stronger practical performance in common finite-sum scenarios.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide an adaptive scheme (e.g., doubling/halving, backtracking) to eliminate the need to know κ and analyze convergence under misspecification?
- Please reconcile the O(κ) vs. O(κ2) stochastic complexity and state the tightest bound supported by your proof.
- In the finite-sum setting, how does EMGD compare empirically and theoretically to SAG/SDCA across varying n and κ?
- Is the shrinking-ball projection essential? Can you analyze a variant without this constraint?
- Can you extend or adapt the method to the merely smooth convex case (e.g., via regularization or restart)?

Pros
- High-probability linear convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients.
- Works with constraints; low memory.
- Clear and self-contained analysis.

Cons
- Requires known κ; no robustness study.
- Ambiguous oracle/cost model; inconsistency in complexity claims.
- No experiments; limited comparison to closely related hybrid/variance-reduction methods.
- κ2 factor in stochastic complexity can be unattractive.

Recommendation
Reject. The theoretical result is interesting but incremental relative to existing hybrid/variance-reduction literature, and the paper in its current form has substantial clarity and evaluation gaps. Strengthening the positioning, fixing the complexity inconsistency, providing adaptive parameter selection and thorough experiments against strong baselines would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
