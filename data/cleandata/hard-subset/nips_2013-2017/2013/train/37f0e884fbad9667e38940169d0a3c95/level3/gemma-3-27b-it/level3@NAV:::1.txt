## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper presents a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to achieve linear convergence for smooth, strongly convex optimization problems while reducing the dependence on the condition number κ typically seen in first-order methods. The core idea – interleaving full-gradient and stochastic-gradient steps within epochs – is a clever approach to balance computational cost and convergence speed. The authors demonstrate that EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, theoretically bypassing the √κ dependence of traditional full-gradient methods.

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical contributions. The authors provide a relatively simple and self-contained convergence proof guaranteeing linear convergence with a high-probability bound – a stronger result than the in-expectation convergence of SAG. The algorithm’s applicability to constrained problems, unlike SAG, and potential advantages in memory/parallelization are also noteworthy, though not fully substantiated.  The complexity analysis (Table 2) offers a useful comparison with Nesterov, SAG, and SDCA, highlighting regimes where EMGD might be preferable.

However, the paper suffers from a significant practical limitation: the complete absence of experimental evaluation. While theoretically sound, the practical relevance of EMGD remains unclear without demonstrating its performance on benchmark problems. The discussion of limitations is also surprisingly brief.  The authors acknowledge the need to know κ *a priori*, a significant drawback compared to adaptive methods like SCDA and SAG. Furthermore, the lack of automatic step-size tuning in EMGD, contrasting with techniques used in practical implementations of SAG and SCDA, is a concern.  The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) which employs a similar hybrid approach. 

Clarity could be improved by adding high-level intuition behind key proof choices and clarifying assumptions in Table 1 (e.g., SCDA applicability, the gradient expectation identity). Minor edits addressing notation consistency (domain, norms, log/ln) are also needed.  Specifically, highlighting scenarios where EMGD genuinely outperforms SAG or Nesterov, considering regularization in machine learning (k ≈ n/C'), would strengthen the paper's impact. 

**Overall:** This is a technically strong and theoretically significant contribution with a novel, relatively simple proof. However, the lack of empirical validation and insufficient discussion of limitations hinder its immediate impact.  I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these points, either through revisions or in a rebuttal outlining plans for experimentation and a more thorough analysis of practical considerations.
