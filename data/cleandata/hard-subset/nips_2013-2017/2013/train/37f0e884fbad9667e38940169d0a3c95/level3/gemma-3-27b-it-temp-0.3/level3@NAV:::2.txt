## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes a novel hybrid deterministic-stochastic first-order method, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to achieve linear convergence for strongly convex optimization problems without dependence on the condition number κ in the number of full gradient evaluations. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient steps within epochs, leveraging the strengths of both. The authors claim to achieve O(log(1/ε)) full gradient complexity, a significant improvement over standard methods when κ is large.

The paper builds upon existing work in both deterministic and stochastic optimization, referencing key results from Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA. However, it notably fails to adequately contextualize its contribution within the broader landscape of hybrid deterministic-stochastic methods, particularly overlooking the earlier work of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) which already explores similar interleaving strategies. This omission is a significant weakness.

**Strengths:** The proposed algorithm presents a potentially valuable approach for ill-conditioned problems where full gradient evaluations are expensive. The theoretical analysis, while heavily reliant on a known condition number, demonstrates a clear path to achieving the desired convergence rate under that assumption. The problem setting is relevant and addresses a practical challenge in large-scale optimization.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant limitation is the strong assumption of *a priori* knowledge of the condition number κ. The paper offers no guidance on how to estimate κ or how the algorithm behaves when κ is misspecified or poorly estimated. The algorithm introduces a hyperparameter η dependent on κ, without providing any heuristics or estimation procedures. This severely limits the practical applicability of the method. Furthermore, the lack of experimental validation is a major drawback. The authors do not demonstrate the algorithm’s performance on any datasets, nor do they explore its sensitivity to errors in κ estimation. The analysis is restricted to strongly convex settings, neglecting the important case of plain convex optimization. Finally, the connection to simple interleaving of deterministic and stochastic steps is not thoroughly discussed, potentially downplaying the novelty of the approach. The description of the optimization problem in Section 3.1 is also misleading, blurring the lines between finite-sum and stochastic approximation settings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The theoretical result, *if* κ is known, is a positive contribution. The idea of a hybrid approach is intuitively appealing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:** The strong assumption of known κ, lack of practical guidance for its estimation, absence of experiments, limited scope (strongly convex only), and failure to adequately address related work significantly weaken the paper. 

**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting theoretical result but falls short of being a strong contribution due to its practical limitations and lack of empirical validation. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing guidance on κ estimation and including experimental results, before resubmission.
