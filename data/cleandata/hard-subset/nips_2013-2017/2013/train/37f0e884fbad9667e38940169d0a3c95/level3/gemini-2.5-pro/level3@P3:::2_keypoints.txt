- Proposes EMGD, a hybrid algorithm using one full gradient per epoch plus cheaper stochastic gradients to reduce full-gradient evaluations.
- Main theoretical claim: EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, with high-probability linear convergence.
- Uses a mixed, variance-reduced update g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) to control noise during epochs.
- Strength: tackles the practical problem of ill-conditioned optimization by aiming to make full-gradient calls independent of κ.
- Strength: intuitive hybrid strategy and a strong high-probability convergence guarantee (linear rate).
- Major weakness: analysis and parameterization require the condition number κ to be known a priori, with no guidance for unknown κ.
- Major weakness: complete absence of experiments — no empirical validation, sensitivity analysis to κ misspecification, or runtime/baseline comparisons.
- Major weakness: inadequate comparison to related work, notably failing to discuss/compare with Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and deeper links to SAG/variance-reduction methods.
- Major weakness: ambiguity in problem setting — paper oscillates between general stochastic approximation and finite-sum formulations without clarity.
- Major weakness: limited scope — analysis restricted to strongly convex problems with no treatment or discussion of merely convex cases.
- Acceptance pro: introduces an interesting theoretical result (κ-independent full-gradient count) and strong probabilistic guarantees.
- Rejection con: impractical κ assumption, missing experiments, insufficient related-work positioning, and unclear framing justify recommending rejection pending substantial revisions.
