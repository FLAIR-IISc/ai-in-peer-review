

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel hybrid algorithm for smooth strongly-convex optimization that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition number-independent full-gradient access. The key innovation lies in replacing each full-gradient epoch with O(k²) mixed gradient steps, yielding O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(k² log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls. The authors demonstrate linear convergence with high-probability guarantees, a stronger result than SAG's in-expectation convergence.

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. First, it provides a simple, self-contained convergence proof that avoids the complex analysis common in related work. Second, it extends stochastic optimization to constrained problems where SAG and SDCA fail, while maintaining linear convergence. Third, the high-probability guarantee represents a meaningful improvement over existing methods. The complexity analysis correctly identifies regimes where EMGD outperforms Nesterov's method (when κ ≤ n^(2/3)) and matches SAG when κ ≈ n^(1/2).

However, the paper has notable limitations that prevent it from being fully convincing. The most significant is the complete absence of empirical validation - no experiments, benchmark comparisons, or concrete examples demonstrate the algorithm's practical relevance. The authors also fail to adequately discuss limitations, such as the requirement to know κ a priori (which is unrealistic in most machine learning settings where κ ≈ n/C'), and the lack of adaptive step-size mechanisms present in SCDA and SAG. The comparison to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) is missing, and Section 3.4 requires substantial expansion to properly contextualize EMGD relative to existing methods.

While the theoretical contribution is sound and novel, the lack of empirical evidence and incomplete discussion of practical limitations significantly weaken the paper's impact. The authors should address these issues by adding experiments, clarifying realistic application scenarios (particularly noting that EMGD may not outperform SAG in typical ML settings with regularization ~ C/n), and expanding the discussion of limitations. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. As presented, it remains an interesting theoretical result but lacks the practical grounding expected at NIPS.
