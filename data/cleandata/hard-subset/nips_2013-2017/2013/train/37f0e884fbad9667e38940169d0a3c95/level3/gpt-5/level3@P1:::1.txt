This paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization when both full gradients and stochastic gradients are available. The main contribution is Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which runs in epochs; each epoch computes one full gradient at the averaged iterate w̄k, then performs T mixed updates using stochastic functions f(·) sampled from a function oracle. The mixed gradient at iteration t is ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), i.e., a control-variates correction using the full gradient at w̄k. Updates are projected onto an ℓ2-ball centered at w̄k whose radius shrinks by √2 each epoch. The analysis shows a high-probability linear convergence: after m epochs, F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ λΔ1^2/2^{m+1}. Choosing η = 1/(L√T) with T = Ω((L/λ)^2 log(1/δ)) yields O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls to reach ε accuracy. Relative to Nesterov’s accelerated full-gradient methods O(√κ log(1/ε)), EMGD removes κ from the count of full gradients at the cost of κ^2 more stochastic steps. Compared to SAG and SDCA, EMGD applies to constrained problems, uses O(d) memory, and provides high-probability guarantees.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple hybrid that mixes a single full gradient per epoch with many cheap, variance-reduced stochastic steps.
- Linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee; clear, largely self-contained proof using smoothness, strong convexity, and martingale concentration.
- Handles constrained optimization via projections on W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk), unlike SAG/SDCA which are typically unconstrained.
- Memory-efficient (no O(n) storage) and amenable to distributed computation for the full-gradient step.
- Good discussion of computational tradeoffs in the finite-sum case and when EMGD can beat accelerated full-gradient methods (κ ≤ n^{2/3}).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Inconsistency on stochastic gradient complexity: abstract claims O(κ log(1/ε)), while Theorem 1 and Section 3 imply O(κ^2 log(1/ε)). Please correct and make the dependence uniform throughout.
- No experiments. Empirical results are important to validate the practical relevance, especially versus SAG/SDCA and accelerated full-gradient methods across regimes of κ and n, and for constrained problems.
- Requires knowledge of κ (or L, λ) to set T and η, and of Δ1 satisfying Δ1 ≥ √(2/λ)(F(0) − F*). These are rarely known; discuss how to estimate or adapt (e.g., doubling/halving epochs, backtracking, or adaptive step-sizes), and sensitivity to misspecification.
- Comparisons in Section 3.4 assume full-grad cost is n× stochastic; broaden the discussion (mini-batching, data access patterns) and include variance-reduced methods (e.g., hybrid deterministic–stochastic methods of Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012). Closer connections to control variates/variance reduction would improve positioning.
- Practicality of the projection in Step 8: projecting onto W ∩ B(w̄k, Δk) may be expensive for general W; discuss classes of constraints where this is efficient and how it compares with unconstrained methods.
- Clarify the function-oracle assumption. It is stronger than an unbiased gradient oracle; explain when it is realizable (finite-sum sampling) and whether the guarantees extend to unbiased stochastic gradients with bounded variance.
- Clarity/notation issues to fix: unify use of log/ln; avoid reusing equation numbers; ensure consistent norms and domain notation; correct minor typos (e.g., SCDA → SDCA); expand Table 1 citations and assumptions; explain the high-level intuition (shrinking ball bounds the stochastic term).

Quality
- Technical development appears sound, with careful control of bias/variance via the mixed gradient and a correct use of Hoeffding–Azuma. The constants and conditions (e.g., δ ≤ e^{-1/2}) are explicit. The requirement on Δ1 and knowledge of λ/L is a practical gap that should be addressed.

Clarity
- Generally well organized; Algorithm 1 and the proof are readable. The noted inconsistencies and missing explanations detract and should be fixed. Section 3.4 would benefit from a more nuanced, data-dependent cost model.

Originality
- The particular mixed-gradient scheme with shrinking domains for constrained strongly convex problems and a simple high-probability proof is novel within the cited literature. It is related to semi-stochastic/variance-reduced methods but distinct in design and scope.

Significance
- Conceptually advances the tradeoff between full and stochastic gradients by eliminating κ from the number of full-gradient evaluations. Impact is strongest for constrained problems, memory-limited settings, and distributed environments. However, the κ^2 dependence in stochastic steps can be large, and without experiments the practical gains are unclear.

Questions for authors
- Can EMGD be made adaptive to unknown κ (e.g., increasing T per epoch until sufficient decrease is observed)?
- Does the analysis extend to a proximal composite setting (nonsmooth regularizers) with proximal steps instead of projections?
- Can mini-batching reduce the κ^2 factor in stochastic steps?
- How sensitive is performance to the radius schedule Δk and to step-size misestimation?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, elegant algorithm and proof; high-probability linear convergence.
- Addresses constrained problems with low memory footprint.
- Clear tradeoff: O(log(1/ε)) full gradients independent of κ.

Cons for acceptance
- No empirical validation.
- κ^2 factor may be uncompetitive versus SAG/SDCA in many finite-sum regimes.
- Requires knowledge of problem constants; lacks adaptive variants.
- Inconsistency in stated complexity and some clarity issues.

Overall assessment
Technically solid and conceptually interesting, with a clean analysis and a useful perspective on mixing full and stochastic gradients, especially for constrained problems. I lean toward acceptance contingent on fixing the complexity inconsistency, adding a related-work discussion of hybrid/variance-reduction methods, improving clarity, and including at least modest experiments to demonstrate when EMGD is practically preferable.
