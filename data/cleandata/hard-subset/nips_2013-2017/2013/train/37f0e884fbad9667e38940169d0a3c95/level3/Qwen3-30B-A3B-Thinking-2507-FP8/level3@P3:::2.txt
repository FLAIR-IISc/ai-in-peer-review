

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradient oracles to achieve linear convergence with condition number-independent full gradient access. The authors claim that EMGD requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach ε-optimality, improving upon the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity for full-gradient methods.

The work builds on the observation that while full gradient methods achieve faster convergence (O(√κ log(1/ε))), they require many expensive full gradient evaluations for ill-conditioned problems. The authors propose using stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations, presenting EMGD as a solution that achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradient access. The algorithm divides optimization into epochs, where each epoch uses one full gradient and O(κ²) stochastic gradients to achieve a constant reduction in optimization error.

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed is relevant: reducing the computational burden of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned problems.
- The theoretical analysis provides a clear convergence guarantee with high probability.
- The paper identifies an interesting trade-off between full and stochastic gradients.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper has several significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution:

1. **Critical assumption without justification:** The analysis crucially assumes the condition number κ is known a priori, with no discussion of how to estimate κ in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion but provide no concrete guidance or estimation procedure, rendering the algorithm impractical for most applications.

2. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper contains no experiments to validate the theoretical claims or study the algorithm's behavior under κ misspecification. Without empirical evidence, the practical relevance of the theoretical results remains unclear.

3. **Missing comparisons:** The paper fails to compare with prior hybrid methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt 2012) or standard baselines like SGD, averaged SGD, SAG, and SDCA. The computational complexity comparison in Table 2 is insufficient without empirical validation.

4. **Theoretical limitations:** The work is restricted to strongly convex problems, ignoring the more common non-strongly convex settings (e.g., plain convex) that are important in practice. The algorithm also lacks discussion of how it relates to simple interleaving of deterministic and stochastic steps.

5. **Misleading problem specification:** Section 3.1 presents a confusing setup where the authors claim the function oracle Of returns "a random function f(·)" with F(w) = E[f(w)], but later assumes F(w) = (1/n)∑f_i(w), creating confusion about the problem setting.

**Quality:** The technical analysis appears sound under the stated assumptions, but the paper fails to carefully address the practical limitations of these assumptions. The lack of experiments to validate the theory and the absence of discussion about κ estimation significantly weaken the quality of the contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the problem setting in Section 3.1 is misleading, and the algorithm description lacks sufficient context about how it relates to simpler approaches.

**Originality:** The hybrid approach is somewhat novel, but the paper fails to properly situate its contribution relative to prior work, particularly Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) who also proposed hybrid methods.

**Significance:** The theoretical result is interesting but limited by its reliance on knowing κ a priori. Without practical guidance for estimating κ or empirical validation, the significance of the work is substantially reduced. The claim that EMGD has "advantages in other aspects" over SAG and SDCA (e.g., constrained optimization, lower storage requirements) is not substantiated with experiments.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper identifies a meaningful problem (reducing full gradient dependence on κ)
- The theoretical analysis is technically sound under its assumptions
- The algorithm design is novel in its specific approach to mixing gradients

*Con:*
- The critical assumption of known κ is not addressed practically
- No experiments to validate theory or study behavior under misspecification
- No comparison to prior hybrid work or standard baselines
- Limited to strongly convex settings without addressing more common non-strongly convex cases
- The problem setup is confusingly presented

This paper presents an interesting theoretical result but falls short of being a complete scientific contribution due to its practical limitations and lack of empirical validation. The key assumption of known condition number is not addressed with any practical guidance, and the absence of experiments to validate the theory significantly reduces its impact. Without addressing these limitations, the paper's contribution remains largely theoretical with unclear practical relevance.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by: (1) providing guidance for estimating κ, (2) including experiments demonstrating the algorithm's behavior under κ misspecification, (3) comparing with prior hybrid methods and standard baselines, and (4) clarifying the problem setting. As presented, the work is an incremental theoretical contribution that fails to sufficiently address practical implementation challenges.
