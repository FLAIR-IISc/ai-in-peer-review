This paper proposes a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The main idea is to divide the optimization process into epochs, with each epoch consisting of a full gradient evaluation followed by multiple stochastic gradient evaluations. The algorithm achieves a constant reduction in optimization error at each epoch, leading to a linear convergence rate.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm, including a proof of linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee. The analysis shows that EMGD can reduce the number of full gradients required from O(√κ log 1/ε) to O(log 1/ε), independent of the condition number κ. The paper also provides a comparison with existing algorithms, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent and the stochastic average gradient (SAG) algorithm.

The strengths of the paper include:

* A novel and simple algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence.
* A thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm, including a proof of linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee.
* A comparison with existing algorithms, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent and the SAG algorithm.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

* The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ, which may not be available in practice.
* The paper lacks experimental results to demonstrate the empirical performance of the algorithm.
* The algorithm may not be the most efficient in terms of computational cost, as it requires multiple stochastic gradient evaluations at each epoch.

To address these weaknesses, the authors could consider the following:

* Providing experimental results to demonstrate the empirical performance of the algorithm.
* Investigating ways to estimate the condition number κ in practice.
* Comparing the computational cost of the algorithm with existing algorithms, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent and the SAG algorithm.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. With some additional experimental results and analysis, the paper could be even stronger.
