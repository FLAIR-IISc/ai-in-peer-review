This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex functions, aiming to reduce the computational cost associated with ill-conditioned problems by leveraging both full and stochastic gradients. The core idea is to interleave full gradient steps with multiple stochastic gradient steps within each epoch, achieving a linear convergence rate with O(log 1/ǫ) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradients, independent of the condition number κ.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the algorithm and its theoretical analysis. It situates the work within the context of existing first-order optimization methods, referencing relevant literature on both deterministic and stochastic gradient descent, including accelerated gradient methods (Nesterov, [16, 18, 19]) and recent advances in stochastic optimization like SAG [22] and SDCA [23]. The theoretical analysis appears sound and the proof of the convergence rate seems reasonably rigorous, although several minor points (detailed below) require attention. The authors also attempt to benchmark their algorithm against existing methods in terms of computational complexity, particularly within the context of machine learning.

A key strength of this work is the development of a novel algorithm that genuinely attempts to bridge the gap between the faster convergence of full gradient methods and the lower per-iteration cost of stochastic gradient methods. The achievement of a logarithmic dependence on 1/ǫ for the number of full gradients is a significant theoretical result. The inclusion of a high-probability convergence guarantee is also commendable, differentiating it from methods that offer convergence only in expectation.

However, the paper suffers from a critical weakness regarding its practical relevance to machine learning. The authors acknowledge this to some extent in their discussion, but it needs more explicit and thorough treatment.  The analysis reveals that the advantage of EMGD over standard batch gradient descent hinges on a specific relationship between the condition number (κ), the dimension of the data (n), and the regularization parameter (L). Specifically, the method is more efficient than batch GD only when κ ≤ n²/3. In many machine learning scenarios, strong convexity arises from a regularization term, leading to a condition number proportional to the data dimension: μ ≈ C·n, where C is a constant. This implies that the proposed algorithm only outperforms batch gradient descent in a relatively narrow and arguably uninteresting regime where n = O(C³/L³). A detailed discussion of this limitation, with concrete examples illustrating when EMGD *might* be advantageous in practice, is crucial.

Another concern is the trade-off between deterministic and probabilistic convergence. While EMGD offers a high-probability convergence guarantee, standard batch gradient descent provides deterministic convergence. In many applications, particularly those requiring high precision, a deterministic guarantee is preferable. The authors should address this trade-off more explicitly.

Several minor points require clarification:

*   **Equation (6):** Should read ||w\* − ŵ||².
*   **Line 288:** Specify which function is being referenced in equation (7).
*   **Line 286:** Remove the equality sign for clarity.
*   **Equation (13):** Replace absolute values with norms for consistency and mathematical accuracy.
*   **Explicitly state L ≥ λ:** Although obvious to those familiar with the field, explicitly stating this relationship improves clarity.
*   **Equation (4):** In the first term, ‘x^’ should be ‘ŵ’ and ‘f’ should be ‘F’; the maximum term is always larger due to Lemma 1.
*   **Define κ:** Precisely define the condition number as κ = L/λ.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel algorithm that effectively combines full and stochastic gradient information.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and a rigorous convergence analysis.
*   High-probability convergence guarantee.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited practical relevance to machine learning due to the restrictive condition for outperforming batch gradient descent.
*   Trade-off between deterministic and probabilistic convergence.
*   Lack of a thorough discussion comparing EMGD to batch gradient descent in realistic ML settings.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of optimization. However, its practical impact is questionable. While the theoretical results are interesting, the authors have not convincingly demonstrated a scenario where EMGD offers a significant advantage over standard batch gradient descent in commonly encountered machine learning applications. I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should significantly expand the discussion of practical limitations and consider exploring scenarios where the proposed method could be genuinely beneficial. A more thorough comparison to batch gradient descent, including empirical results, is also highly recommended.
