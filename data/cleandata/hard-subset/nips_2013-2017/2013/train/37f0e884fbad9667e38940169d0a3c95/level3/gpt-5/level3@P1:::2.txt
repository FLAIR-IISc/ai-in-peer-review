This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid first-order method that interleaves rare full-gradient computations with frequent stochastic-gradient steps. The core idea is to form a “mixed” gradient at each inner iteration t within epoch k: g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) − ∇fkt(w̄k), which is an unbiased estimator of ∇F(wkt), and to update wkt by a projected step onto a shrinking ball around w̄k. Each epoch uses one full gradient (at w̄k) and T stochastic gradients; choosing T appropriately yields a constant-factor reduction per epoch, hence linear convergence in the number of epochs. The main theorem establishes high-probability linear convergence: O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach ε-suboptimality, under L-smoothness and λ-strong convexity. The paper contrasts EMGD with full-gradient accelerated methods (Nesterov) and finite-sum variance-reduced methods (SAG/SDCA), claiming advantages in storage, constraint handling, and high-probability guarantees.

Relation to prior work: The estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is essentially the variance-reduced gradient used in semi-stochastic/variance-reduction methods, e.g., SAG [22], SDCA [23], and the hybrid deterministic–stochastic line (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012). EMGD’s epoch structure and periodic full gradients are closely related in spirit to these works; however, the paper does not fully situate EMGD within this landscape. In particular, the finite-sum methods achieve linear convergence with gradient complexities O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) and do not require a shrinking domain. EMGD’s analysis for a general stochastic-oracle model is interesting, but the comparisons and positioning relative to prior hybrid methods are incomplete.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic structure, with a simple mixed-gradient estimator that is unbiased and easy to compute given both oracles.
- High-probability linear convergence is established, not just in expectation.
- The number of full gradients is independent of κ, which can be practically appealing when full gradients are very expensive and parallelizable.
- The method applies to constrained problems (projection onto W ∩ ball), unlike some finite-sum methods that target unconstrained problems.

Weaknesses:
- The claimed stochastic-gradient complexity appears inconsistent: the abstract first states O(κ log(1/ε)) but the main theorem and algorithmic choices imply O(κ2 log(1/ε)). This discrepancy should be resolved; as written, the bound is κ2, which is worse than state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods (SAG/SDCA, and related) that scale in κ, not κ2.
- The analysis relies on knowing κ (or L and λ) to set both T and η; there is no discussion of how to estimate these or how sensitive the method is to misspecification.
- No experiments are provided. Given the proliferation of closely related methods with different practical trade-offs (SGD/ASGD, SAG, SDCA, accelerated FG), empirical evidence is crucial to validate the high-probability benefits, the dependence on κ, and the constrained-setting advantages.
- The oracle model in Section 3.1 mixes general stochastic approximation (F = E[f]) with comparisons dominated by the finite-sum setting (F = (1/n)∑ fi). The paper should more clearly separate the two regimes and give guarantees specialized to the finite-sum case to enable apples-to-apples comparisons with SAG/SDCA.
- The “mixed” step is extremely close to simply interleaving full and stochastic steps; discussion comparing EMGD’s variance control (via shrinking balls) to existing variance-reduction (e.g., memory-based SAG or control-variate SVRG-like estimators) is missing.
- Important practical cases such as merely convex (non-strongly convex) problems are not addressed; adding standard regularization or restart schemes could extend applicability.

Quality:
- The main proof is careful and uses a martingale difference argument to obtain high-probability bounds. However, there appear to be constant-factor issues (e.g., inequality (13) uses −λ‖wt − w*‖2 rather than the more standard −(λ/2)‖wt − w*‖2), and the κ vs. κ2 inconsistency is significant. Clarifying these details is necessary.
- The requirement to project onto shrinking balls introduces extra hyperparameters (Δ1 and the decay schedule) tied to unknown distances to optimum; guidance is limited.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and the algorithm is clearly specified. Nonetheless, Section 3.1’s oracle setup and the finite-sum comparisons create confusion about the target setting. The lack of a discussion tying EMGD to prior mixed/stochastic-deterministic methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) and closely related variance-reduction estimators reduces clarity of contribution.
- Notation is mostly consistent; tightening constants and correcting minor inequalities would help.

Originality:
- The mixed-gradient estimator is standard in the variance-reduction literature; the novelty here is the high-probability analysis with shrinking-domain updates and the explicit goal of κ-independent full-gradient counts. Relative to SAG/SDCA (and related semi-stochastic methods), the incremental novelty appears limited.

Significance:
- The idea of reducing full-gradient usage to O(log(1/ε)) is conceptually appealing, but the total complexity scaling O(κ2 log(1/ε)) for stochastic gradients appears suboptimal. Without experiments demonstrating practical gains in wall-clock time or robustness, the impact is uncertain.

Pros for acceptance:
- High-probability linear convergence with simple updates and applicability to constrained problems.
- Clear focus on reducing full-gradient calls; potentially attractive in distributed settings.

Cons for acceptance:
- Likely suboptimal stochastic complexity (κ2 vs. κ) and inconsistency in claims.
- Missing experimental validation and sensitivity analysis to κ misspecification.
- Incomplete positioning relative to closely related hybrid and variance-reduced methods.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reconcile and, if possible, tighten the stochastic-gradient complexity to O(κ log(1/ε)); discuss whether the shrinking-ball device is essential or if an alternative analysis suffices.
- Add experiments comparing EMGD to SGD/ASGD, accelerated FG, SAG, SDCA (and closely related semi-stochastic methods) on both finite-sum and stochastic-approximation problems, including constrained cases.
- Provide adaptive strategies for η, T, and Δk that do not require prior knowledge of κ (e.g., doubling/halving schedules, line-search variants, or restarts), and study robustness to misspecification.
- Clarify the problem setting (finite-sum vs. general expectation) and relate more explicitly to Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and other hybrid methods; explain the connections to control-variate/variance-reduction estimators.
- Consider extensions to non-strongly convex objectives (via regularization or restart).

Recommendation: Reject in current form. The idea is sound and the high-probability guarantees are interesting, but the theoretical bound appears suboptimal and inconsistent across sections, the positioning with respect to prior work is incomplete, and the lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess practical significance.
