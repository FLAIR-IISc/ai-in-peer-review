Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a hybrid algorithm for smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization that combines one full-gradient computation per epoch with many cheap stochastic steps using a “mixed” (control-variate) gradient: g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k). Each epoch runs T = Θ(κ2 log(1/δ)) mixed steps within a shrinking ball around the epoch anchor w̄k, averages the iterates, and halves the radius. The main result is linear convergence with high probability using O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls. The key insight is that anchoring at w̄k and subtracting ∇f(w̄k) tightly controls variance so that the epoch count (hence number of full gradients) no longer depends on κ.

Relative to prior work: Nesterov’s accelerated methods need O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients; SAG and SDCA achieve linear convergence for finite-sum problems but in expectation (SAG) and primarily in the unconstrained setting, and with Ω(n) memory. EMGD’s high-probability guarantee and applicability to constrained problems are notable. The hybrid idea connects to earlier semi-stochastic/hybrid methods (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012), but EMGD gives a simpler, self-contained analysis and an explicit condition-number–independent count of full gradients.

Quality
- Technically sound. The convergence proof is clean, well-structured, and leverages strong convexity, smoothness, gradient mapping within a shrinking domain, and Azuma–Hoeffding. The high-probability linear rate is a strength.
- Important clarification: the paper states both O(κ log(1/ε)) and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls in different places. The theorem and parameter choices imply T = Θ(L2/λ2) = Θ(κ2), hence the total is O(κ2 log(1/ε)). Please fix this inconsistency throughout (including abstract and conclusions).
- No experiments are provided. This limits assessment of practical relevance, robustness to misspecification of κ, and the cost of projections for constrained problems.

Clarity
- Generally readable with a simple algorithmic structure and proof. However:
  - Expand the high-level intuition for why the mixed gradient and shrinking ball yield variance control and epoch-wise error halving (a brief “proof plan” before the details would help non-experts).
  - Table 1/2: add precise assumptions for each method (finite-sum requirement, constrained vs. unconstrained, memory needs, guarantee type—high-prob vs. expectation).
  - Be consistent about ln/log, norms, domain notation, and equation numbering (there are repeated indices and a few notational slips). Define all acronyms consistently (SDCA—not “SCDA”).
  - Make explicit the unbiased-gradient identity E[∇f(w)] = ∇F(w) and smoothness assumptions that ensure Jensen’s steps; clarify the function-oracle model and its relation to finite-sum sampling.
  - Correct typos (e.g., “epoches”), and ensure B(x; r) is defined upon first use.

Originality
- The particular hybrid “one full gradient per epoch + many variance-reduced stochastic steps with an anchor and shrinking trust region” is a neat and simple construction, with a self-contained proof and a high-probability linear rate.
- Related hybrid work should be discussed more explicitly, especially Friedlander & Schmidt (2012, SIAM SISC), as well as early semi-stochastic schemes. Contrast assumptions, rates, and constrained applicability.

Significance
- Theory: The condition-number–independent count of full-gradient evaluations is a nice result; the high-probability guarantee improves over SAG’s in-expectation rate.
- Practice: Advantages include applicability to constrained problems, O(d) memory, and potential to parallelize the full-gradient step. However, practical impact is unclear without experiments, and the need to fix T a priori using κ limits usability.

Comparative complexity (finite-sum F = (1/n)∑ fi)
- Assuming a full gradient costs n stochastic gradients:
  - EMGD beats Nesterov when κ ≤ n2/3; it matches SAG (up to logs) around κ ≈ n1/2; SAG outperforms EMGD in many regimes; SDCA can be best when n is large relative to κ. Please expand Section 3.4 with a more systematic regime diagram and wall-clock proxies.
  - Potential memory and parallelization benefits for EMGD are intriguing but currently speculative; clarify what is achievable without harming convergence and compare to parallelizable variants of SAG/SDCA.

Practical considerations and missing discussion
- No experiments: include comparisons on standard ML tasks, especially constrained problems (where EMGD may shine), and ablations varying κ, n, and projection costs. Report both gradient calls and time.
- Adaptivity: EMGD currently needs κ to set T and η; contrast with SAG/SDCA and practical heuristics that adapt step-sizes. Consider:
  - A doubling schedule for T until sufficient epoch progress is observed.
  - Backtracking or variance-based step-size tuning within epochs.
  - Online estimation of L/λ (e.g., using sampled Hessian spectra) with safeguards.
- Realistic ML regimes: with regularization scaling as C/n, κ ≈ n/C′; in such settings EMGD may not beat SAG or Nesterov. Please identify realistic constrained or high-projection-cost cases where EMGD is preferable.
- Limitations: discuss dependence on projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) and how expensive projections affect overall complexity; sensitivity to anchor choice and epoch averaging; and robustness when f’s are only approximately smooth.

Pros
- Simple, self-contained analysis with a high-probability linear rate.
- Condition-number–independent number of full-gradient calls.
- Works for constrained problems; O(d) memory footprint.
- Clear variance-reduction intuition via mixed gradients and shrinking trust regions.

Cons
- No empirical validation; unclear practical impact.
- Stochastic complexity is O(κ2 log(1/ε)), which is worse than SAG/SDCA in many regimes.
- Requires κ to set epoch length and step-size; lacks adaptive mechanisms.
- Comparisons and related-work discussion (esp. Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012) are too brief.

Requested revisions
- Fix κ vs. κ2 stochastic complexity inconsistencies; unify notation/citations; clean typos.
- Expand Section 3.4 with detailed, assumption-aware comparisons, including storage, guarantee type, constrained applicability, and regimes (κ vs. n).
- Add experiments (including constrained setups) and discuss parallelization evidence.
- Add a subsection on limitations and when EMGD is (not) preferable; propose/adopt adaptive T, η.

Verdict
Technically solid and theoretically meaningful. The hybrid idea and proof are simple and elegant, and the high-probability linear rate with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients is valuable. However, the paper needs experiments and a richer comparison/discussion to justify practical relevance and to correct inconsistencies. I lean toward acceptance if the authors commit to the above revisions in the rebuttal; otherwise, borderline.
