### Summary

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors identify a key bottleneck in classical first-order methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent: the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations, O(√κ log(1/ε)), scales with the condition number κ, which can be very large for ill-conditioned problems.

To overcome this, the paper proposes a novel hybrid algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD). The core idea is to structure the optimization into epochs. At the start of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed to provide a stable descent direction. This is followed by a series of O(κ²) cheaper stochastic gradient steps that refine the solution locally. By averaging the iterates within an epoch, the algorithm achieves a constant factor reduction in error, leading to an overall linear convergence rate. The main theoretical result is that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution by computing only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients—a number independent of the condition number κ—and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This work creatively combines ideas from deterministic and stochastic optimization, relating to prior work on epoch-based methods and recent variance-reduction techniques like SAG and SCDA, but with a distinct approach and theoretical guarantee.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Idea:** The central proposal—to use one full gradient to anchor an epoch of many stochastic steps to make the full-gradient complexity independent of the condition number—is a simple, elegant, and novel contribution to the optimization literature. It provides a new perspective on how to effectively combine deterministic and stochastic information.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper establishes a linear convergence rate with a high-probability guarantee. This is a stronger and often more desirable result than the in-expectation convergence proven for related methods like SAG, as it provides more robust assurances on the performance of a single run.

3.  **Generality and Simplicity:**
    *   Unlike SAG and SCDA, which are primarily designed for unconstrained finite-sum minimization, EMGD naturally handles general convex constraints, provided the projection onto the constrained set is efficient. This significantly broadens its applicability.
    *   The convergence proof is remarkably simple, self-contained, and easy to follow, which is a strength in itself and enhances the paper's pedagogical value.

4.  **Potential Practical Advantages:** The authors rightly point out that EMGD has potential advantages in terms of memory (requiring O(d) storage versus O(n) for SAG) and amenability to parallelization (for the full gradient computation), which are critical considerations for large-scale machine learning.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Complete Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is that the paper is purely theoretical. Without any experiments, it is impossible to assess the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm. Key questions remain unanswered: How does EMGD perform compared to SAG, SCDA, or accelerated gradient descent in practice? How sensitive is it to its parameters (η, T)? The addition of experiments, even on synthetic data or standard benchmark datasets, is crucial for demonstrating the method's value to the NIPS community.

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations and Practicality:**
    *   **A Priori Knowledge of κ:** The epoch length `T` depends directly on the condition number κ, which is rarely known in practice. This is a major practical drawback, making the algorithm difficult to deploy "out of the box." The authors mention estimating κ as future work, but this critical limitation should be explicitly stated and discussed in the main body of the paper.
    *   **Lack of Adaptivity:** The step-size η is fixed and non-adaptive. Many state-of-the-art methods (e.g., SCDA) incorporate adaptive mechanisms or benefit from practical heuristics that significantly improve performance. A discussion of how EMGD might be made more adaptive would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison to Related Work:**
    *   The paper should cite and discuss the hybrid deterministic-stochastic method of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012), which also combines full gradients with stochastic (or incremental) steps and is highly relevant.
    *   Section 3.4 ("Comparisons") is too brief. The complexity analysis shows that EMGD is not universally superior; for example, SAG outperforms it in many regimes. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the specific, realistic settings (in terms of n, κ, and the relative cost of gradient oracles) where EMGD is expected to be the method of choice. The footnote regarding regularization is a good starting point but should be expanded into a more thorough discussion.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   **Proof Intuition:** While the proof is mathematically sound, it would benefit from more high-level intuition. For instance, a brief explanation of why the "mixed gradient" construction is key to controlling variance or why averaging is necessary would improve readability.
    *   **Table Clarity:** The paper refers to "Table 2" but the table is unnumbered. Furthermore, the assumptions behind the complexities in the table should be made explicit in the caption or text (e.g., the fine print on SDCA's applicability is mentioned in a footnote but is an important distinction). The assumption that a full gradient is exactly `n` times the cost of a stochastic one is a simplification that should be noted.
    *   **Minor Edits:** There are several minor inconsistencies that should be fixed: the domain is denoted by `W` in some places and `X` in others (Lemma 1); the use of `log` and `ln` should be standardized.

### Overall Verdict

This paper presents a technically sound and theoretically interesting contribution. The core idea of eliminating the condition number from the full-gradient complexity is novel, and the accompanying high-probability linear convergence result is significant. The simple proof and applicability to constrained problems are additional strengths.

However, the paper's value as a contribution to NIPS is severely hampered by a complete lack of empirical results and a limited discussion of its practical drawbacks and the specific regimes where it would be advantageous. While the theoretical contribution is clear, its practical impact is not.

I would recommend acceptance on the condition that the authors make substantial revisions. At a minimum, they should significantly expand the discussion of limitations, provide a more detailed and nuanced comparison to related work (including Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012), and clearly articulate the practical scenarios where EMGD is expected to excel. The inclusion of even basic experiments comparing EMGD to key baselines would transform this from a purely theoretical exercise into a much stronger and more complete paper.
