

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel hybrid optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients for smooth strongly convex problems. The key theoretical contribution is showing EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, where κ is the condition number. This represents a significant reduction from the O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity of standard full gradient methods.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a carefully constructed algorithm and proof. The mixed gradient update cleverly combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve the claimed convergence rate. The analysis is detailed and appears technically sound, with only minor issues in notation that should be addressed (e.g., equation (6) should use squared norm, L ≥ λ should be explicitly stated, equation (13) should use norms instead of absolute values).

However, the paper's significance is limited by its practical relevance to machine learning. In most ML applications, strong convexity is induced by regularization (e.g., L2 regularization), making the strong convexity parameter λ proportional to the number of samples (λ ≈ C/N), which makes κ proportional to N. The paper claims EMGD is advantageous over standard full gradient methods when κ ≤ N^(2/3), but this requires N ≤ (C/L)^3, an uninteresting regime since typically L is constant and C/N is small (making N large). In practical ML settings, κ is typically large, so the claimed advantage of EMGD is not applicable.

The paper also lacks a thorough comparison with standard batch gradient descent, which is the most common approach for strongly convex problems in ML. The authors should provide a detailed computational cost analysis of the trade-off between full and stochastic gradients, which would help establish whether the proposed method offers practical advantages. Additionally, EMGD achieves linear convergence with high probability, whereas batch gradient descent provides deterministic convergence guarantees—a significant practical difference.

The paper has several minor notational issues that should be corrected:
- Equation (6) should read ||w^* - ŵ||^2 instead of ||w^* - ŵ||
- The condition number should be explicitly defined as κ = L/λ
- Line 286 should remove the equality sign to improve clarity
- The relationship L ≥ λ should be explicitly stated
- In equation (4), x^ should be ŵ and f should be F

Strengths:
- Novel hybrid approach combining full and stochastic gradients
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence proof
- Well-structured algorithm description

Weaknesses:
- Limited practical relevance to machine learning due to uninteresting regime where advantage holds
- Insufficient comparison with standard batch gradient descent
- High-probability convergence (less desirable than deterministic in many applications)

The paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution, but its practical significance is questionable. The method is novel and the analysis is sound, but the claimed advantage only applies to a regime that is unlikely to be encountered in real ML applications. Without a compelling demonstration of practical utility in standard ML settings or a more convincing argument for the method's advantages, the paper does not make a strong case for acceptance at NIPS. The authors should either demonstrate the method's effectiveness in a real ML application or provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost trade-offs that would establish practical relevance.
