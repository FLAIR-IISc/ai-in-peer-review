

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel hybrid optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients for smooth strongly convex problems. The authors claim that EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, removing the condition number dependence from the full gradient count compared to standard batch gradient descent (which requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients). The theoretical analysis shows that EMGD converges with high probability, contrasting with the expected convergence of SAG and SDCA.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by presenting a new algorithmic framework that successfully removes the condition number dependence on full gradient accesses. The convergence proof is technically sound and appears correct, with only minor notational errors requiring correction (e.g., equation (6) should use ||w* - ŵ||², equation (4) should use ŵ instead of x^, and absolute values in equation (13) should be norms). The algorithm's high-probability convergence guarantee is stronger than the expected convergence of SAG and SDCA, which is a notable theoretical advantage.

However, the paper fails to adequately justify the practical relevance of EMGD to machine learning. In standard ML settings, strong convexity from regularization gives λ ≈ τ (regularization parameter), while L is typically a constant, making κ ≈ 1/τ. The authors claim EMGD outperforms batch GD when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which corresponds to τ ≥ n^(-2/3). This is an uninteresting regime in practice, as τ is often much smaller (e.g., τ = 1/n or smaller). The paper lacks a thorough computational cost analysis in realistic ML scenarios, which is crucial for establishing practical advantages.

The paper also doesn't sufficiently contrast the high-probability convergence guarantee with the deterministic convergence of batch GD. While the theoretical novelty is clear, the practical significance remains questionable without a detailed comparison of computational costs in realistic ML settings, especially considering that most ML problems use regularization with small τ.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution but fails to convincingly demonstrate its practical relevance to machine learning. The authors should substantially revise the discussion of practical advantages to address the critical concern about the uninteresting regime where EMGD would outperform batch GD.
