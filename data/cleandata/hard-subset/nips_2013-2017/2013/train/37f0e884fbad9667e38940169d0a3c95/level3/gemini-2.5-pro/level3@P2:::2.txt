This paper proposes a new first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems. The goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, which for methods like accelerated gradient descent is dependent on the condition number κ. EMGD is a hybrid algorithm that utilizes both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. The core idea is an epoch-based scheme where, at the start of each epoch, one full gradient is computed. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" that combines this fixed full gradient with a variance-reducing stochastic term. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD achieves an ε-accurate solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient calls. This makes the number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number, which is a desirable property for ill-conditioned problems. The work is related to the literature on both deterministic and stochastic optimization, particularly recent work on variance-reduced methods like SAG and SDCA that also achieve linear convergence for finite-sum problems.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses the significant challenge of reducing the computational burden of full gradient methods in ill-conditioned settings. The objective of making the number of full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ is well-motivated and could have a substantial impact.

2.  **Novel Algorithm and Theoretical Result:** The proposed EMGD algorithm, with its epoch-based structure and mixed-gradient update, is novel. The main theoretical result—achieving a high-probability linear convergence rate in terms of outer iterations (full gradient calls)—is strong and interesting from a theoretical standpoint.

3.  **Clear Trade-off:** The paper clearly articulates the trade-off it aims to exploit: replacing a dependence on √κ full gradients with a dependence on κ² stochastic gradients per epoch, which can be beneficial if stochastic gradients are significantly cheaper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unrealistic Assumption about κ:** The primary weakness of the paper is its reliance on the strong assumption that the condition number κ (and the smoothness constant L) is known a priori. Key algorithm parameters, including the step size η and the number of inner iterations T, are functions of L and λ. The paper provides no discussion on how to set these parameters in practice where κ is almost always unknown. This omission severely limits the practical applicability of the proposed method. The brief discussion in the conclusion about estimating κ is speculative and does not resolve this fundamental issue.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical and lacks any experimental results. Without experiments, it is impossible to assess the practical performance of EMGD. An empirical study is crucial to:
    *   Validate the theoretical convergence rate.
    *   Investigate the algorithm's sensitivity to the choice of η and T, especially when κ is misspecified. For instance, what happens if the κ used to set the parameters is too large or too small?
    *   Compare EMGD against relevant baselines, including accelerated gradient descent, SGD, and contemporary variance-reduced methods like SAG, SDCA, and other hybrid approaches (e.g., Friedlander & Schmidt, 2012). The complexity comparisons in Table 2 are useful, but empirical performance is needed to make a convincing case.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper cites several relevant works, the discussion is not deep enough. The comparison to hybrid or "semi-stochastic" methods like that of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) is particularly brief. The paper should more clearly differentiate EMGD from these prior approaches and explain its advantages. The mixed gradient update `∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w_t) - ∇f(w̄)` is a specific form of variance reduction, and its connection to other techniques should be discussed more thoroughly.

4.  **Ambiguity in Problem Setting:** The problem setup is not clearly specified. Section 3.1 describes a general stochastic approximation setting (F(w) = E[f(w)]), for which the analysis is developed. However, Section 3.4 compares EMGD primarily to methods like SAG and SDCA, which are designed for the finite-sum setting (F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w)). These are distinct settings, and the paper should clarify which one is the primary target and ensure that the comparisons and claims are consistent with that setting.

### Summary of Pro and Con Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization.
*   Proposes a novel algorithm with a strong, interesting theoretical result (κ-independent full gradient complexity).
*   The theoretical analysis appears to be sound.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The critical assumption that the condition number κ is known is unrealistic and unaddressed, making the algorithm impractical as presented.
*   The complete absence of experiments makes it impossible to verify the claims or understand the practical behavior of the algorithm.
*   The paper does not adequately compare its approach to closely related hybrid/semi-stochastic methods.
*   The problem setting is not clearly defined, leading to confusion between the stochastic approximation and finite-sum optimization settings.

### Conclusion

While the theoretical idea of decoupling the number of full gradient evaluations from the condition number is compelling, the paper in its current form is incomplete. The strong and unaddressed assumption about knowing κ, combined with a complete lack of empirical validation, are major flaws. The work is more of a theoretical curiosity than a practical algorithm at this stage. I recommend rejection, but I believe the paper contains promising ideas. I would encourage the authors to address the practical issue of setting hyperparameters without knowledge of κ and to provide a thorough experimental evaluation against state-of-the-art methods in a revised version.
