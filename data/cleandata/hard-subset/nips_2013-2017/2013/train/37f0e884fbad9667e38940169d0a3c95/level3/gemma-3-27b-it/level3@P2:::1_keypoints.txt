- Proposal: Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), combining full and stochastic gradients per epoch to reduce dependence on condition number κ.
- Theoretical result: Proves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.
- Positioning: Clearly situates EMGD among full-gradient and stochastic methods and cites relevant prior work (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA).
- Technical soundness: Theoretical analysis and proof sketch are rigorous, using standard strongly convex lemmas and providing high-probability guarantees.
- Novelty: Epoch-based mixture of full and stochastic gradients and independence of full-gradient count from κ is a novel contribution for ill-conditioned problems.
- Clarity (theory): Mathematical notation and algorithm presentation are clear and helpful for understanding the method.
- Significance: Potentially important for reducing computational cost in large-scale, ill-conditioned machine learning problems and amenable to parallelization.
- Major weakness: No empirical evaluation—absence of experiments on real datasets or benchmarks undermines practical claims.
- Practical limitations: Requires prior knowledge of κ and lacks adaptive step-size strategies; practical estimation or adaptation is not explored.
- Related work gap: Incomplete comparison—omits discussion of Friedlander & Schmidt (2012) and lacks nuanced trade-off analysis against SAG, SDCA, etc.
- Clarity issue (Section 3.4): Claims about regimes where EMGD outperforms others (e.g., κ ≤ n²/3) need better justification and expansion.
- Minor clarity fixes: Add high-level proof intuition, clarify assumptions in Table 1, and use consistent log/ln notation.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically solid algorithm addressing an important optimization problem with potential computational savings.
- Arguments against acceptance: Lack of experiments, limited practical discussion, incomplete related-work comparison, and unexplored limitations.
- Overall assessment: Technically sound and significant contribution but requires experiments and stronger practical discussion to be fully convincing.
- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on authors conducting experiments and addressing practical and related-work concerns.
