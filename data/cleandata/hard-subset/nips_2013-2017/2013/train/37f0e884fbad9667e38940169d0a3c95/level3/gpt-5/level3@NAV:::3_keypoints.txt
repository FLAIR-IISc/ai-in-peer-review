- Proposal: EMGD — hybrid method interleaving one full gradient per epoch with frequent stochastic-gradient steps using a mixed-gradient update over a shrinking ball.  
- Theoretical claim: Linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients w.h.p.  
- Positioning: Sits between deterministic accelerated methods (O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients) and variance-reduced/incremental methods (e.g., SAG/SDCA).  
- Main technical devices: Mixed-gradient construction and shrinking-domain analysis.  
- Novelty: Hybrid update and proof technique are original relative to classical semi-stochastic and epoch GD schemes.  
- Analysis quality: Convergence analysis appears sound and the high-probability guarantee is clearly stated.  
- Comparison gap: Needs careful, quantitative comparison to batch/accelerated GD under ML scalings (F as a sum) to show practical advantage.  
- Practical-regime concern: Under common regularized-sum scaling (μ ≈ C·N), EMGD is faster than batch GD only if N = O(C^3/L^3), which seems uninteresting.  
- Probabilistic vs deterministic: Paper should discuss the trade-off between EMGD’s high-probability guarantees and batch GD’s deterministic guarantees.  
- κ inconsistency: Text references O(κ log(1/ε)) but Theorem 1 states O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients — reconcile this mismatch.  
- Clarity/fixable errors: Eq.(6) should be ‖w* − ŵ‖^2; specify function used for Eq.(7) (line 288); remove equality in line 286; replace absolute values with norms in (13); explicitly state L ≥ λ; correct (4) x̂→ŵ and f→F and note first term is always larger by Lemma 1; define/use κ=L/λ consistently.  
- Significance concern: Limited mainstream ML impact unless authors demonstrate realistic wall-clock speedups, provide empirical validation, and model full vs stochastic gradient costs with constants.  
- Pros for acceptance: Novel, technically solid analysis; simple algorithm; few full gradients with high-probability linear convergence; works with constraints and modest memory.  
- Cons against acceptance: Unclear practical advantage over batch GD, high-probability vs deterministic trade-off, minor inconsistencies/clarity issues, and no experiments.
