

# NIPS Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors address the limitation of standard first-order methods, which require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations for smooth and strongly convex problems (where κ is the condition number). EMGD combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, independent of the condition number κ.

The key insight is that EMGD uses one full gradient per epoch and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, resulting in a total of O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves linear convergence with high probability, which is stronger than the expectation-based convergence of methods like SAG and SDCA.

The paper relates to prior work on accelerated gradient methods (Nesterov [17]), stochastic gradient methods (Roux et al. [22], Shalev-Shwartz and Zhang [23]), and epoch gradient descent (Hazan and Kale [9]). However, it notably omits comparison to the influential Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method by Johnson and Zhang [1], which addresses a similar problem.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by demonstrating that the number of full gradient evaluations can be reduced to O(log(1/ε)) independent of κ, which is a substantial improvement over the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) rate.

2. **Novel Algorithm Design**: The mixed gradient approach (combining full and stochastic gradients) is clever and well-motivated. The specific form g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k) is well-justified by the analysis.

3. **Stronger Convergence Guarantee**: The high-probability convergence guarantee (Theorem 1) is stronger than the expectation-based convergence of SAG and SDCA, which is a significant theoretical advantage.

4. **Practical Advantages**: The paper correctly identifies several practical advantages over SAG and SDCA:
   - Works for both constrained and unconstrained problems
   - Requires only O(d) storage (vs. Ω(n) for SAG/SDCA)
   - Can leverage distributed computing for the full gradient computation

5. **Clear Theoretical Analysis**: The proof of Theorem 1 is detailed and technically sound, with careful bounding of all terms.

## Weaknesses

1. **Omission of SVRG**: The most significant weakness is the omission of comparison to SVRG (Johnson and Zhang, 2013), which is the most influential method for this specific problem. SVRG also aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations while maintaining linear convergence. The paper should explicitly compare EMGD to SVRG, which is a standard baseline for such problems.

2. **Assumption of Known κ**: The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ to set the parameter T. The paper acknowledges this as a drawback but provides only a vague suggestion about estimating κ. For practical implementation, this is a major limitation. The paper should either:
   - Provide a practical method for estimating κ during optimization
   - Demonstrate that the algorithm is robust to overestimation of κ
   - Compare the cost of estimating κ to the benefits of reduced full gradient evaluations

3. **Large Constants in Complexity**: The condition T ≥ 1152L²/λ² log(1/δ) implies a very large constant factor (1152) in the complexity. For moderately large κ, this could make the algorithm impractical, as the number of stochastic gradients per epoch (O(κ²)) becomes prohibitively large.

4. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical. It would be valuable to include empirical results demonstrating the practical performance of EMGD compared to SVRG, SAG, and Nesterov's method on real-world optimization problems.

5. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper doesn't compare to more recent methods like SARAH/SPIDER (Nguyen et al., 2017), which achieve similar complexity to SVRG with better practical performance.

6. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that the full gradient computation can be distributed, but provides no details on how this would work in practice, including communication costs and scalability.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add comparison to SVRG**: The authors should compare EMGD to SVRG in both theory and practice. SVRG uses a single full gradient at the beginning of each epoch and then uses stochastic gradients with variance reduction, achieving O(κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations. EMGD's O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluation rate is better, but the trade-off in stochastic gradient evaluations needs to be clearly explained.

2. **Address the κ estimation problem**: Provide a more concrete method for estimating κ during optimization, perhaps using a small sample of the data to estimate the Hessian's eigenvalues, and analyze the cost of this estimation.

3. **Provide empirical results**: Include experiments on standard machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression, ridge regression) comparing EMGD to SVRG, SAG, and Nesterov's method, measuring both convergence rate and computational time.

4. **Clarify the practical significance**: The paper states that EMGD has lower computational cost than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3). This should be compared to SVRG, which has a similar theoretical bound. The paper should clarify how EMGD compares to SVRG in terms of computational complexity.

5. **Add a discussion of related work**: The related work section should be expanded to include SVRG, SARAH, and other recent methods for variance-reduced stochastic optimization.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by showing how to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)) for smooth and strongly convex optimization. The algorithm design is novel and the analysis is technically sound. However, the omission of SVRG and the lack of practical validation are significant weaknesses.

I would recommend **acceptance with major revision**. The authors should address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by adding a comparison to SVRG and providing a more detailed discussion of the κ estimation problem. The paper has strong theoretical foundations but needs to better connect these to the current state of the art and practical implementation.

## Final Assessment

**Quality**: 8/10 - The theoretical contribution is strong, but the omission of SVRG and lack of empirical validation weaken the quality.

**Clarity**: 8/10 - Well-written and organized, but the omission of SVRG could confuse readers about the relationship to the current state of the art.

**Originality**: 9/10 - The mixed gradient approach is novel and the theoretical result is significant.

**Significance**: 7/10 - The theoretical result is significant, but the practical significance is limited by the assumption that κ is known and the lack of empirical validation.

**Overall**: The paper makes an important theoretical contribution but needs to better connect to the current state of the art (particularly SVRG) and provide more practical guidance for implementation. With these revisions, it would be an excellent contribution to the field.
