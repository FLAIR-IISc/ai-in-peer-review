### **Summary**

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, focusing on reducing the number of expensive full gradient evaluations, which typically depends on the problem's condition number, κ. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient. Then, within the epoch, it performs a series of updates using a "mixed gradient," which is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient anchored at the epoch's starting point. The key theoretical result is that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients. This makes the number of full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ, effectively trading a dependency on κ in full gradients (as seen in accelerated methods) for a dependency on κ in the cheaper stochastic gradients. The paper contrasts this with other linear-convergent methods like SAG and SDCA, highlighting EMGD's applicability to constrained problems, lower memory requirements, and a high-probability convergence guarantee.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel Problem Formulation and a Strong Theoretical Result:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel approach to bypassing the established O(√κ log 1/ε) lower bound on full gradient calls for first-order methods. By introducing a hybrid oracle model (access to both full and stochastic gradients), the authors achieve a clean separation of complexities. The main result—achieving a κ-independent number of full gradient evaluations—is a significant and interesting theoretical contribution. This provides a new perspective on the trade-offs available in optimization when different types of gradient information are accessible.

2.  **Generality of the Algorithm:** Compared to popular variance-reduced methods like SAG and SDCA, which are designed for finite-sum objective functions and unconstrained problems, EMGD is presented in a more general framework. It can handle constrained optimization problems (provided the projection in Step 8 is efficient) and does not require the objective to be a finite sum. This broader applicability is a valuable feature.

3.  **High-Probability Convergence Guarantee:** The analysis provides a convergence guarantee that holds with high probability, which is a stronger result than the in-expectation guarantees common to many stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA. The proof technique, using an inductive argument over epochs and a martingale concentration inequality, is sound and well-executed.

4.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The algorithm is stated clearly, and the main ideas behind the proof are conveyed effectively in the analysis section. The comparison to related work is helpful for positioning the contribution.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Potentially Prohibitive Stochastic Gradient Complexity:** The most significant weakness is the O(κ²) dependence in the number of stochastic gradient calls. While the paper successfully removes κ from the full gradient term, it reintroduces it with a quadratic dependency in the stochastic term. For problems with even a moderately large condition number, O(κ²) can be very large, potentially making the algorithm computationally more expensive than alternatives. For example, the total work for SVRG or SDCA is often O((n+κ)log(1/ε)), which can be much smaller than EMGD's O(n log(1/ε) + κ² log(1/ε)) total work (assuming full gradient costs n stochastic ones). The paper's comparison in Section 3.4 seems overly optimistic, relying on a specific regime where κ ≤ n²/³, and does not fully acknowledge how restrictive the κ² term is.

2.  **Missing Comparison to Key Related Work:** The "mixed gradient" `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the variance-reduction technique used in SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013). The overall structure of EMGD, with its outer loop (epoch) computing a full gradient and an inner loop performing stochastic updates, is also highly reminiscent of SVRG. While the paper cites SAG and SDCA, the omission of SVRG is a major oversight, as it is arguably the most similar prior work in terms of algorithmic structure. A direct comparison of the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees with SVRG is essential for properly contextualizing this work's novelty.

3.  **Practicality Concerns:** The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the problem parameters L and λ (and thus κ) to set the number of inner-loop iterations `T`. The authors acknowledge this as a drawback in the conclusion but only briefly suggest potential solutions. This requirement is a significant barrier to practical application, as these parameters are often unknown.

4.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While this can be acceptable, the central claim is about improving computational efficiency. Without any experiments, it is difficult to assess the practical impact of the proposed trade-off. A simple empirical comparison against Nesterov's AGD and SVRG on a synthetic problem with tunable κ would have substantially strengthened the paper by demonstrating the regimes where EMGD is actually superior.

### **Detailed Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proofs appear correct, and the claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis provided. However, the evaluation of the work's limitations, particularly the O(κ²) complexity, is not sufficiently critical in the main body of the paper. It is a complete theoretical contribution but would be stronger with empirical support.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement, algorithm, and theoretical results are presented in a logical and understandable manner. The proof sketch in Section 4 is helpful for grasping the main analytical steps.

*   **Originality:** The core idea of achieving a κ-independent full gradient complexity by using a hybrid oracle model is original and insightful. The algorithm itself, however, appears to be a novel combination of established techniques: the variance reduction scheme from SVRG and the epoch-based shrinking-domain analysis from EGD (Hazan & Kale). The failure to cite and discuss SVRG significantly weakens the paper's claim to algorithmic novelty.

*   **Significance:** The paper's main theoretical result is significant and could inspire future research into hybrid optimization algorithms. It advances our understanding of the fundamental trade-offs in first-order optimization. However, the practical significance of the proposed EMGD algorithm is questionable due to the severe O(κ²) dependence on the condition number and the requirement of knowing κ beforehand. It is more likely that researchers will build on the *idea* presented here rather than using the EMGD algorithm directly in practice.
