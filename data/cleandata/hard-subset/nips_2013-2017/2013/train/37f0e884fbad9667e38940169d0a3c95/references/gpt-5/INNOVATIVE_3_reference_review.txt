Summary
The paper studies first-order methods for smooth and strongly convex optimization and targets reducing the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations. It proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch, the algorithm computes one full gradient at a snapshot point w̄, and then performs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w_t) − ∇f(w̄), coupled with projected gradient steps onto a shrinking ball centered at w̄. The analysis shows that each epoch halves the suboptimality (and shrinks the distance to the optimum), yielding linear convergence. The algorithm achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach ε-accuracy, with a high-probability guarantee.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned between deterministic accelerated methods (Nesterov; O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations with full gradients) and purely stochastic methods (sublinear rates). It compares with SAG and SDCA, which achieve linear rates on finite-sum problems. A very closely related line is missing in the discussion: variance-reduced stochastic gradient methods such as SVRG/Prox-SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), SAGA, and related methods. SVRG uses the same gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f_i(w_t) − ∇f_i(w̄) and a snapshot/full gradient per epoch, and achieves O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) gradient complexity on finite sums with inner-loop length O(κ), i.e., the number of full gradients is already O(log(1/ε)) and independent of κ. The paper’s estimator and epoch structure therefore overlap heavily with SVRG; the use of a shrinking projection and the high-probability analysis are distinguishing details, but the core idea is not new.

Strengths
- Clear goal: reducing dependence on full gradients while retaining linear convergence.
- Simple, implementable algorithmic template with an intuitive mixed-gradient estimator.
- High-probability linear convergence guarantee, with clean analysis using smoothness/strong convexity and a martingale argument.
- Works for constrained domains (via projection onto shrinking balls) and uses O(d) memory, addressing two practical concerns with SAG/SDCA.
- Explicit condition-number–independent count of full gradients is attractive conceptually, especially when full gradients can be parallelized or distributed.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: The central mechanism (snapshot full gradient + variance-reduced mixed gradients) coincides with SVRG; this work does not acknowledge or compare to that literature. Moreover, the complexity here is strictly worse: the required inner-loop length scales as T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)), whereas SVRG/SAGA/SDCA achieve linear convergence with T = Θ(κ) (or Θ(n)) and overall O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) gradient evaluations. The claimed “condition-number–independent access of full gradients” is already achieved by SVRG (one full gradient per epoch, epochs = O(log(1/ε))).
- Assumptions/setting: The two-oracle model presumes exact access to ∇F(·). In the general expectation setting (not finite-sum), such an oracle is rarely available; in finite-sum problems, computing ∇F is one pass over n data points, bringing us back to known finite-sum regimes where SVRG et al. are directly applicable.
- Dependence on κ and δ: EMGD requires knowing κ (and δ) to set T and η. The paper acknowledges this drawback but does not offer a practical remedy (e.g., doubling/tripling strategies, adaptive epoch lengths).
- Projection onto a shrinking ball introduces additional hyperparameters (Δ1) and requires problem-dependent radii; this may complicate practice and is not needed in related methods.
- Comparisons: The quantitative comparison with SAG/SDCA omits SVRG/SAGA, and the claims about convergence “in expectation” vs “with high probability” are not carefully positioned; high-probability variants exist in this literature. No empirical evaluation to illuminate constants or practical behavior.
- Presentation issues: Several notational slips and typos (e.g., repeated equation numbering, minor grammatical errors), and some steps (e.g., conditions in Theorem 1) could be streamlined for readability.

Questions for the authors
1. How does EMGD differ technically from SVRG beyond the use of a shrinking projection and a high-probability martingale bound? Can your analysis be tightened to obtain T = Θ(κ) rather than Θ(κ^2)?
2. Can the projection be removed (or replaced by a standard proximal step) while retaining your high-probability guarantees?
3. Can you provide an adaptive scheme that does not require prior knowledge of κ (e.g., epoch-doubling, line-search, or variance-based stopping rules)?
4. In the general expectation setting without finite-sum structure, when is access to the full-gradient oracle realistic? If only unbiased estimates are available, can your approach be extended to use mini-batch approximations to ∇F(w̄) with similar guarantees?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Tighten the related work to include SVRG/SAGA and clarify the novelty relative to these methods.
- Clarify the cost model in the finite-sum case and provide a fair complexity table including SVRG/Prox-SVRG and SAGA.
- Fix typos (e.g., “epoches”), duplicate equation numbering, and improve consistency in notation (use consistent indices and hats/bars).
- Consider adding a short empirical section or at least simulated evidence to illustrate the effect of κ on inner-loop length and the benefit of high-probability control.

Verdict
While the paper aims at an appealing goal and presents a clean analysis with high-probability guarantees and support for constrained problems, the core algorithmic idea appears to duplicate known variance-reduced methods (notably SVRG) and achieves weaker complexity (κ^2 vs κ). Given the strong overlap and missing comparisons, the contribution feels incremental rather than a bold, novel direction. I recommend rejection in its current form. A revised version that clearly positions the work relative to SVRG/SAGA, improves the κ dependence, removes the need for known κ, and includes empirical validation would be considerably stronger.
