

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors address the limitation of standard first-order methods where the number of full gradient evaluations scales with √κ for ill-conditioned problems (κ = L/λ, the condition number). By combining full gradients with stochastic gradients, EMGD achieves linear convergence (O(log(1/ε)) complexity) while reducing the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), making it condition number independent.

The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis showing that EMGD requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach an ε-optimal solution. The authors compare EMGD with several related algorithms (Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, SDCA) in terms of computational complexity, highlighting advantages in storage requirements (O(d) vs. O(n) for SAG/SDCA) and applicability to constrained optimization problems.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis. The convergence proof of Theorem 1 is rigorous and well-structured, with careful attention to the probabilistic guarantees. The authors properly define the problem setting, make reasonable assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity), and provide a detailed proof of the main result. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the need to know κ in advance) and discusses how these might be addressed in future work.

The theoretical claims are well-supported by the analysis, and the proof techniques build upon standard convex optimization tools (e.g., strong convexity, smoothness, martingale inequalities) in a logical and well-executed manner. The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution, not just a position paper.

### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and motivation, the related work section comprehensively surveys relevant literature, and the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and well-explained. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the key conditions (e.g., T ≥ 1152L²/λ² log(1/δ)) are clearly specified.

The proof is detailed but accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization. The authors effectively use intermediate lemmas (Lemma 1) to break down the complex proof into manageable parts. The paper would be easy for a qualified reader to reproduce the results.

### Originality
The paper presents a novel approach to combining full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition number independent access to full gradients. While epoch-based methods (like epoch gradient descent) and mixed gradient approaches have been explored before, the specific combination of one full gradient per epoch with many stochastic gradients to achieve condition number independent full gradient access is a clear contribution.

The paper distinguishes itself from related work (SAG, SDCA, Nesterov's method) through its theoretical guarantees and practical advantages (storage requirements, applicability to constrained problems). The comparison table (Table 2) effectively demonstrates how EMGD fits into the landscape of existing methods.

### Significance
The contribution addresses a practical problem in large-scale optimization: the high computational cost of full gradient evaluations for ill-conditioned problems. By reducing the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), the algorithm could lead to significant computational savings in applications where full gradients are expensive to compute.

The paper's theoretical contribution is significant for the optimization community, and the practical advantages (storage requirements, constrained optimization applicability) make it potentially valuable for machine learning practitioners. The paper clearly articulates the conditions under which EMGD outperforms existing methods (κ ≤ n^(2/3)), providing a useful guideline for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

### Lack of Empirical Validation
The paper is purely theoretical and lacks empirical results to validate the theoretical claims. While theoretical analysis is important, the absence of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of EMGD, particularly how the constants in the theoretical bounds affect real-world performance. This is a notable omission in a machine learning conference like NIPS.

### Practical Limitation: Knowledge of κ
The paper requires knowledge of the condition number κ to set the algorithm parameters (e.g., the number of iterations per epoch T). While the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest future work on estimating κ, this is a significant practical drawback. In many real-world applications, κ is not known a priori, and the paper doesn't provide a practical method for estimating it.

### Computational Complexity Comparison
The computational complexity comparison in Table 2 shows that EMGD may not be the most efficient method in all cases. For example, when κ > n^(1/2), EMGD's complexity is higher than SAG and SDCA. The paper could have provided more discussion on when EMGD is the preferred choice over other methods.

### Potential Overstatement of Contribution
The paper claims "the number of full gradients required is reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), independent from the condition number." However, the number of stochastic gradients required still depends on κ (O(κ² log(1/ε))). This nuance could be made clearer, as the dependence on κ in the stochastic gradient count might still be problematic for very large κ.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in convex optimization. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the literature on first-order optimization methods. The proposed EMGD algorithm provides a novel approach to combining full and stochastic gradients with a significant theoretical advantage for ill-conditioned problems.

The main weaknesses are the lack of empirical validation and the requirement to know the condition number κ in advance. These are important limitations for a machine learning conference like NIPS, where practical impact is highly valued.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical contribution is strong, the writing is excellent, and the paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of optimization. While the lack of empirical results is a drawback, the paper's theoretical rigor and clear presentation compensate for this limitation. The paper is clearly distinct from previous work, and the significance of the contribution is well-justified.

The paper would be strengthened by adding a brief empirical validation section (even if it's just a simple synthetic experiment) and by providing more detail on how to estimate κ in practice. However, these are improvements rather than necessary requirements for acceptance.

## Final Thoughts

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, providing optimization researchers with a new algorithm and theoretical insights for smooth and strongly convex problems. The approach of combining full and stochastic gradients in a novel way is both elegant and practical, and the analysis is thorough and well-executed. The authors have made a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in first-order optimization methods.
