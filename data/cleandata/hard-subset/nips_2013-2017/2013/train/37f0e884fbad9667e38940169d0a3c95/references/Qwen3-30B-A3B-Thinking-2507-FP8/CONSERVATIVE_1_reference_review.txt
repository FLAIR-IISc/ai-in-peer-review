

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm for smooth strongly convex optimization that claims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. The authors achieve this by combining full gradients with stochastic gradients, evaluating one full gradient and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch. The theoretical analysis shows linear convergence with the claimed reduction in full gradient access, and the paper compares EMGD with existing methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA.

## Strengths
The paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis is technically sound. The proof of the convergence rate is detailed and follows a clear inductive approach. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature regarding the trade-off between full and stochastic gradients for ill-conditioned problems. The paper also correctly references related work and provides a thorough comparison with existing methods in Table 2.

## Weaknesses
The primary weakness is the lack of empirical validation. The paper makes a strong claim about computational efficiency but provides no experiments to support this claim. The comparison in Table 2 assumes that full gradients are n times more expensive than stochastic gradients, but this assumption is not justified with empirical evidence. Without experiments showing actual runtime improvements, the practical significance of the claimed reduction in full gradient evaluations remains unproven.

The paper also fails to address a critical practical limitation: the requirement that κ must be known beforehand. This is a significant drawback for real-world applications where the condition number is typically unknown and must be estimated, which can be challenging. The authors mention this limitation in the conclusion but don't provide a solution or empirical validation of their proposed estimation method.

Furthermore, the claimed advantage of EMGD over SAG and SDCA is questionable. The paper states that EMGD has lower computational complexity than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but the authors don't provide any evidence that this condition is commonly satisfied in practical machine learning problems. The paper also claims that EMGD "works for both constrained and unconstrained optimization," but this is a minor advantage since SAG and SDCA can be adapted to constrained problems with similar modifications.

The most concerning aspect is the lack of clear evidence that the O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations required by EMGD are actually more efficient than the O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations required by standard methods. The paper assumes that stochastic gradients are "significantly more efficient" to compute, but this is not justified with any empirical data. In practice, the computational cost of O(κ²) stochastic gradients could easily outweigh the savings from reducing full gradient evaluations, especially when κ is large.

## Critical Assessment
While the theoretical analysis is sound, this paper falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS. The authors present a novel algorithm with an interesting theoretical claim but fail to provide sufficient evidence to support its practical value. The field has a rich history of methods that balance full and stochastic gradients (e.g., the "semi-stochastic" methods mentioned in the paper), and EMGD doesn't convincingly demonstrate why it should replace these established approaches.

The paper's main contribution appears to be theoretical rather than practical. The claim of "condition number independent access of full gradients" is misleading because the total computational cost (full + stochastic gradients) is actually O(κ² log(1/ε)), which grows with κ. The paper's focus on reducing full gradient evaluations at the expense of a quadratic increase in stochastic gradient evaluations doesn't translate to a clear practical advantage without empirical validation.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the paper lacks the necessary empirical validation to support its claims about computational efficiency. The field has seen many theoretical advances that fail to translate to practical improvements, and this paper is a prime example of this disconnect. Without experiments demonstrating real-world benefits, the paper doesn't meet the high standards of NIPS for methodological rigor and practical significance.

The authors would need to provide extensive experimental results showing that EMGD outperforms existing methods on standard machine learning problems, with careful analysis of the trade-offs between full and stochastic gradient evaluations. They should also address the practical challenge of estimating κ and demonstrate that their method is advantageous in real-world scenarios where the condition number is unknown. Until these issues are addressed, the paper's contribution remains limited to theoretical novelty without clear practical significance.
