### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. Standard first-order methods with access to full gradients, such as Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, achieve a linear convergence rate but require O(√κ log(1/ε)) gradient evaluations, where κ is the condition number of the objective function. For ill-conditioned problems (large κ), this can be computationally prohibitive. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle to reduce the number of required full gradient computations to be independent of the condition number κ.

The EMGD algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes one full gradient. Within the epoch, it performs a series of updates using a "mixed gradient," which combines the stored full gradient with a variance-reduced stochastic term. This update is a form of variance reduction, similar in spirit to methods developed for finite-sum objectives like SAG [22] and SDCA [23]. The main theoretical result is that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This successfully trades a large number of expensive full gradient computations for a larger number of cheaper stochastic gradient computations, making the full gradient complexity independent of κ.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is well-defined, and the convergence analysis in Section 4 appears rigorous. The proof strategy relies on an inductive argument over the epochs, showing a constant factor reduction in the optimization error at each epoch. Within each epoch's analysis, the authors use standard techniques from convex optimization combined with the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality to bound the martingale difference sequence arising from the stochastic gradient terms. This allows them to establish a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation results often provided for similar stochastic algorithms.

However, the claims should be evaluated in the context of the assumptions. The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ (or its constituent smoothness L and strong convexity λ parameters) to set the number of inner-loop iterations T. The paper acknowledges this limitation in the conclusion, but it is a significant practical hurdle. The paper is purely theoretical and lacks empirical validation, which makes it difficult to assess the practical performance and sensitivity to parameter settings.

### Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by contextualizing it within the broader field of convex optimization and clearly stating the paper's objective. The algorithm is presented in a clean pseudocode format. The theoretical results are stated precisely in Theorem 1, and the proof sketch in Section 4.1 provides a helpful high-level overview of the analysis. The writing is direct and easy to follow for a reader familiar with convex optimization.

### Originality

The primary contribution is the theoretical result that the number of full gradient evaluations can be made independent of the condition number κ. This is a novel and interesting goal that bypasses the established lower bounds for first-order methods by employing a hybrid oracle model (access to both full and stochastic gradients).

However, the novelty of the proposed method itself is somewhat diminished by its strong similarity to existing variance reduction techniques, particularly Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which is not cited. The "mixed gradient" `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the gradient estimator used in SVRG for the general expectation setting. The epoch-based structure is also the core framework of SVRG. While the authors compare their work to SAG and SDCA, which are relevant for finite-sum problems, SVRG is a more direct and crucial point of comparison. The paper's contributions over SVRG appear to be (1) the analysis for the general `F(w) = E[f(w)]` setting with a high-probability bound, and (2) the explicit incorporation of constraints via projection. While valuable, the omission of SVRG makes the paper's positioning of its originality incomplete.

### Significance

The paper addresses an important problem: mitigating the impact of high condition numbers in large-scale optimization. The idea of trading expensive full gradients for cheaper stochastic ones is highly relevant. The theoretical result is significant and could inspire further research into hybrid optimization algorithms.

The practical significance is less clear due to two main factors. First, the dependency of the stochastic gradient complexity on O(κ²) is quite high. For finite-sum problems, SVRG achieves a total complexity of O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. The proposed method's complexity would be O((n + κ²) log(1/ε)), which is worse than SVRG for any κ > n. The comparisons in Table 2 show advantages over Nesterov's method in certain regimes (κ ≤ n²/³) but fail to compare against the most relevant state-of-the-art variance-reduced stochastic methods. This high dependency on κ may limit the algorithm's utility for the very ill-conditioned problems it is designed for.

Second, the paper's advantages over methods like SAG and SDCA—handling constraints, lower memory, and amenability to distributed computation—are valid and important. These aspects increase the potential significance of the work, especially for problems that do not fit the unconstrained finite-sum structure. However, without experiments, the practical benefits remain speculative.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Presents a novel and theoretically interesting result: achieving full gradient complexity O(log(1/ε)) that is independent of the condition number κ.
*   The framework is general, applicable to constrained optimization and objectives defined by expectations, not just finite sums.
*   The analysis is technically sound and provides a strong high-probability convergence guarantee.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   Fails to cite and compare against highly relevant prior work, most notably SVRG, which uses an almost identical algorithmic structure and update rule. This significantly overstates the method's originality.
*   The number of required stochastic gradients scales with O(κ²), which is substantially worse than the O(κ) scaling of SVRG, making the proposed algorithm less attractive for ill-conditioned problems.
*   The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ, a significant practical limitation.
*   The paper lacks any empirical evaluation to demonstrate the claimed benefits or analyze the practical performance of the algorithm.
