Summary
This paper studies first-order optimization for smooth, strongly convex objectives and proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD). The algorithm alternates epochs; at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at a reference point, and within the epoch it uses a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄), combined with projected gradient updates within a shrinking ball around w̄. The main claim is a high-probability linear convergence rate while requiring only O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations (independent of the condition number κ), at the cost of O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations. The analysis is clean, using smoothness, strong convexity, and a martingale concentration bound to show per-epoch error contraction.

Relation to prior work
The mixed gradient estimator is identical to that used by variance-reduction methods such as SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), and closely related to S2GD/Prox-SVRG/SAGA/SARAH lines. Those methods also compute one (or occasional) full gradient per epoch and achieve linear convergence for strongly convex problems. In the finite-sum setting F(w) = (1/n)∑ f_i(w), SVRG-type methods attain total gradient complexity O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient passes—likewise independent of κ. SAG/SDCA similarly achieve linear convergence with different tradeoffs. The paper discusses SAG/SDCA but does not compare to SVRG/S2GD and variants, which is a significant gap given the near-identical estimator and epoch structure.

Strengths
- Clear goal: reduce the number of full-gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)) while retaining linear convergence; the paper achieves this under a mixed-oracle model and provides a high-probability guarantee.
- Clean, self-contained analysis using an epoch schedule, shrinking trust region, and Azuma–Hoeffding bound; the proof technique may be of independent pedagogical interest.
- Storage is O(d) and the method is stated to work with constraints via projected steps, which is practically relevant.
- High-probability linear convergence (rather than in-expectation) is a nice aspect.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited. The core idea (one full gradient per epoch plus variance-reduced estimator ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) + ∇F(w̄)) is the defining feature of SVRG and its descendants. Those methods are not cited or compared against, and they already guarantee O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations independent of κ. This overlap substantially diminishes the contribution.
- Suboptimal dependence on κ. The paper’s complexity requires T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) inner iterations per epoch, leading to O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, which is worse than O(κ log(1/ε)) in SVRG/Prox-SVRG/SDCA. The shrinking-ball device appears to be the source of looseness; it also complicates the method.
- Oracle model practicality. The analysis assumes simultaneous access to (i) a full-gradient oracle Og and (ii) a function oracle Of returning a random f with L-smoothness. Outside finite-sum settings, a true full-gradient oracle is typically unavailable; inside finite-sum, the model reduces to computing a full pass plus samples—i.e., exactly the standard finite-sum scenario. The paper should either embrace the finite-sum model and analyze in terms of n and κ, or justify the general oracle.
- Tuning and unknown constants. The algorithm requires knowledge of L, λ (hence κ) and an initial radius Δ1 satisfying inequalities involving F(0) − F(w*), which are rarely known. Competing VR methods typically avoid such dependence or provide adaptive schedules.
- Missing empirical evaluation. Given the unfavorable κ^2 factor and the claim of practical advantages (constraints, low memory), experiments would help substantiate where EMGD is preferable to SVRG/SAGA/SDCA.
- Clarity issues and minor errors: e.g., the update in Algorithm 1 seems to miss a 1/2 in the quadratic term; related work omits closely related VR papers; the “lower Lipschitz” inequality (‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖) is stronger than standard strong convexity—clarify assumptions.

Significance
The idea of reducing the number of full gradients to O(log(1/ε)) is important, but it is already realized by SVRG-like methods with better κ dependence. Without a tighter complexity, new algorithmic twist, or broader scope (e.g., non-finite-sum stochastic settings without a full-gradient oracle, constraints with provable advantages, or distributed variants with rigorous speedup), the contribution over established VR methods appears incremental.

Quality
The theoretical development is mostly sound and self-contained, and the high-probability analysis is neat. However, the dependence on κ is weak compared to the state of the art, and the practical relevance under the dual-oracle model is unclear.

Clarity
Generally readable, but:
- Fix the proximal update expression in Algorithm 1.
- Clearly state assumptions (e.g., twice differentiability and uniform Hessian bounds if needed).
- Expand related work to include SVRG/S2GD/Prox-SVRG/SARAH and contrast guarantees and oracle costs.
- Discuss how to set Δ1 and handle unknown κ; consider adaptive epoch lengths.

Originality
Limited, given overlap with variance-reduction literature that uses the same gradient estimator and achieves the headline “κ-independent number of full gradients.”

Pros for acceptance
- High-probability linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients is a clean message.
- Constraint handling and O(d) memory emphasized.

Cons for acceptance
- Overlaps strongly with SVRG-type methods; missing citations and comparisons.
- Inferior κ dependence (κ^2 vs κ).
- Requires nonstandard oracles and knowledge of κ/Δ1; no experiments.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper’s main idea is not sufficiently novel relative to established variance-reduction methods, and its complexity is weaker. I would be more positive if the authors (i) position the work precisely relative to SVRG/S2GD and improve the κ dependence to O(κ), (ii) remove the need for κ/Δ1 via adaptive schedules, and/or (iii) demonstrate a compelling advantage (e.g., constrained or distributed settings) with theory and experiments.
