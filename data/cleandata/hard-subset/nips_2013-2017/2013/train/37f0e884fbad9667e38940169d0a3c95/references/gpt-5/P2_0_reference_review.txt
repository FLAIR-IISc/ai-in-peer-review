Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for minimizing a smooth, λ-strongly convex objective F over a convex set W, under access to two oracles: a full gradient oracle Og returning ∇F(w), and a function oracle Of returning a random smooth function f with F(w) = E[f(w)]. EMGD proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch k, it computes one full gradient at a reference point w̄k. Inside the epoch it performs T “mixed-gradient” steps with the estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), while constraining iterates to a shrinking ball around w̄k. The main claim is a linear convergence guarantee: an ε-accurate solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations (independent of κ = L/λ), and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient computations. The analysis provides a high-probability rate via a martingale argument, with the shrinking ball controlling the stochastic variance.

This “mixed gradient” estimator is essentially identical to the variance-reduced estimator used in SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which also computes one full gradient per epoch and uses gradients at both current and reference points to reduce variance. In the finite-sum setting F = (1/n) Σ fi, relevant contemporaneous methods include SAG (NIPS 2012), SDCA (JMLR 2013), and subsequently SAGA/Finito/MISO. The paper discusses SAG and SDCA, but does not cite or compare to SVRG, which is the closest in spirit and, importantly, achieves O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) total gradients with only O(d) memory and no shrinking trust region. The present work extends the oracle model beyond finite-sum by allowing a generic function oracle E[f] = F, but it also assumes availability of an exact full gradient oracle Og, which in practice is most natural in the finite-sum case.

Quality:
- Technical correctness: The high-level proof strategy is standard and largely sound. The estimator’s variance is controlled by L‖wt − w̄k‖ through smoothness and the shrinking ball constraint. The per-epoch halving of error is obtained by choosing T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)), with a step size η = 1/(L√T), and using Hoeffding–Azuma for a martingale difference sequence. The strong convexity inequality stated as ‖∇F(w)−∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w−w′‖ follows from λ-strong convexity via monotonicity and Cauchy–Schwarz, so it is acceptable though nonstandard. The analysis is careful about high-probability bounds and uses a union bound across epochs.
- Caveats: The κ dependence appears suboptimal compared to known results; SVRG achieves O(κ) inner iterations per epoch, not O(κ^2). The shrinking-domain device feels like an artifact to bound variance; modern variance reduction avoids such constraints. The requirement to know κ a priori (for T and radius schedule) is a practical limitation; no adaptive strategy is provided. Some derivations have minor notational glitches (duplicate equation numbers, missing constants) that should be cleaned up.

Clarity:
The paper is readable, but there are several issues: repeated/misnumbered equations, typos, and occasional nonstandard statements (e.g., using the gradient norm lower bound as the definition of strong convexity). Algorithm 1’s update could benefit from explicit proximal/projection notation. The related work section overlooks the most directly related SVRG and subsequent SAGA/Finito/MISO literature, which hinders the reader’s ability to place the contribution accurately. No experiments are provided to illustrate practical behavior or the impact of the shrinking ball, which weakens the paper’s communicative value for a broad NeurIPS audience.

Originality:
The core estimator and epoch structure are not novel; they are equivalent to SVRG’s variance-reduction scheme. The main differences are (i) using a shrinking trust region to bound variance and (ii) framing the result in a general expectation-oracle model while still relying on an exact full gradient oracle. Given that, in the finite-sum setting where Og is available, existing methods already provide stronger complexity bounds and simpler algorithms without trust regions, the novelty is limited. If the intent is to cover a non-finite-sum setting with an Og oracle, that should be made explicit and contrasted with alternatives; however, the existence of Og already implies significant structure or cost that typically aligns with finite-sum problems.

Significance:
Conceptually, emphasizing that the number of full-gradient evaluations can be made independent of κ is useful, but this property is already present in SVRG-type methods. The obtained κ^2 dependence in the number of stochastic gradients is weaker than the state-of-the-art κ dependence. The claimed advantages (works with constraints; O(d) memory; high-probability rate) are positive, but not unique—SVRG extends to proximal/constraints, uses O(d) memory, and high-probability bounds can be derived. Without empirical evidence and with a weaker complexity dependence, the impact is limited relative to existing literature.

Pros:
- Clear, high-probability linear convergence analysis in a simple framework.
- Low memory footprint; straightforward per-iteration computation.
- Works with constraints provided projections are efficient.
- Highlights the tradeoff between full and stochastic gradients and the independence of full-gradient calls from κ.

Cons:
- Very close to SVRG in design; missing citation and comparison to SVRG and related methods.
- Worse κ dependence (κ^2 vs κ) and arguably unnecessary shrinking-ball constraint.
- Assumes prior knowledge of κ and requires tuning T and radius schedule; no adaptivity.
- Relies on an exact full gradient oracle; in practical non-finite-sum settings this is unrealistic, while in finite-sum settings stronger methods exist.
- No experimental validation; several clarity/notation issues.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Cite and compare thoroughly to SVRG, SAGA, Finito/MISO, and proximal/accelerated variants; clarify what is genuinely new (e.g., general oracle model, high-probability bound under minimal assumptions).
- Tighten the analysis to improve κ dependence to O(κ); consider removing the shrinking-ball constraint in favor of standard variance-reduction arguments.
- Provide experiments on convex, strongly convex benchmarks (with and without constraints), comparing EMGD to AGD, SVRG, SAG, SDCA, including wall-clock and oracle counts.
- Remove dependence on known κ via adaptive schedules (doubling trick) or online estimation of L and λ; clarify how to choose T and ∆k in practice.
- Fix notation, equation numbering, and formal definitions to improve readability.

Recommendation: At present, I do not recommend acceptance due to limited novelty over SVRG-class methods, weaker complexity dependence, and lack of empirical validation. With a revised positioning versus existing variance-reduction literature, improved bounds, and experiments, this work could be a useful contribution.
