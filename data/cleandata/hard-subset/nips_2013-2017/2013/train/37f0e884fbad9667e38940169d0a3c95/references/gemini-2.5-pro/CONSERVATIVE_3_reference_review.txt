### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors aim to reduce the dependency on expensive full gradient computations, particularly in ill-conditioned settings where the condition number `κ` is large. They propose a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which operates in epochs. In each epoch, the algorithm computes one full gradient at a reference point and then performs a series of updates using a "mixed gradient" that combines this full gradient with stochastic gradient information. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD achieves an `ε`-optimal solution with `O(log(1/ε))` full gradient evaluations and `O(κ² log(1/ε))` stochastic gradient evaluations. This makes the number of costly full gradient computations independent of the condition number `κ`.

### Review

This paper tackles an important and well-studied problem: accelerating first-order methods for smooth and strongly convex optimization by reducing the reliance on full gradient computations. The motivation to make the number of full gradient calls independent of the condition number `κ` is strong and relevant to the NIPS community. The theoretical analysis appears to be technically sound and follows a clear inductive argument over epochs. However, the work suffers from significant weaknesses in terms of originality, comparison to the state of the art, and empirical validation, which call its contribution into question.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Motivation:** The paper is motivated by a practical and significant challenge in large-scale optimization: the high cost of full gradient evaluations for ill-conditioned problems. The goal of removing the `√κ` dependency from the full gradient complexity of accelerated methods is well-posed.
2.  **High-Probability Bound:** The analysis provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is theoretically stronger than the in-expectation results common for many stochastic algorithms.
3.  **Generality:** The authors correctly point out that their framework can handle constrained optimization problems (provided the projection is efficient) and has lower memory requirements than methods like SAG, which are valuable practical considerations.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The central idea of the proposed algorithm—using an outer loop that computes a full gradient and an inner loop that uses variance-reduced stochastic gradients of the form `∇F(w̄) + ∇f_t(w_t) - ∇f_t(w̄)`—is not new. This is precisely the mechanism behind Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) [1]. The proposed "mixed gradient" appears identical to the SVRG update. The epoch-based structure of EMGD is also the defining characteristic of SVRG. The absence of a citation and comparison to SVRG is a major oversight. This significantly undermines the claimed novelty of the proposed method, which appears to be a re-derivation of an existing, well-established algorithm.

2.  **Theoretical Complexity:** While the number of full gradients is independent of `κ`, the number of stochastic gradients per epoch, `T`, is `O(κ²)`. This results in a total stochastic gradient complexity of `O(κ² log(1/ε))`. This is substantially worse than existing variance-reduced methods. For the finite-sum setting, SVRG and SAGA achieve a total complexity of `O((n + κ) log(1/ε))` stochastic gradient evaluations. The proposed method is therefore only competitive with SVRG if `κ²` is on the order of `n+κ`, which is a very restrictive condition. For ill-conditioned problems (large `κ`), a `κ²` dependency is prohibitive and makes the method less practical than the very methods it should be compared against. The comparison in Table 2 is insufficient as it omits SVRG and other key variance-reduced methods, and the condition `κ ≤ n^(2/3)` for outperforming Nesterov's method highlights the limited regime where EMGD might be beneficial.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. For a paper proposing a new optimization algorithm intended for practical benefit, the complete absence of experiments is a critical weakness. Without empirical results, it is impossible to verify the practical performance of EMGD. How does it compare to standard baselines like AGD, SGD, and particularly SVRG on real-world datasets? How sensitive is the algorithm to its parameters? Claims of computational efficiency must be substantiated with empirical evidence, which is currently lacking.

4.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The algorithm requires setting the number of inner-loop iterations `T` based on knowledge of the condition number `κ = L/λ`. This is a common but significant limitation of many theoretical algorithms, as these parameters are rarely known in practice. The authors relegate this issue to future work, but it is a primary barrier to adoption. Furthermore, the use of a shrinking `l2`-ball constraint `B(w̄k; ∆k)` seems to be an artifact of the proof technique and adds unnecessary complexity to the algorithm, especially for unconstrained problems. Standard analyses of SVRG do not require such a constraint.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem in optimization.
*   The theoretical result of achieving a `κ`-independent number of full gradient calls is interesting.
*   The analysis provides a high-probability convergence bound, which is a strong theoretical guarantee.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Critically low originality:** The proposed method appears to be a rediscovery of the well-known SVRG algorithm, which is not cited or compared against.
*   **Sub-optimal theoretical rate:** The `O(κ²)` dependency on stochastic gradients is worse than state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods, making the algorithm less compelling, especially for the ill-conditioned problems it targets.
*   **No empirical validation:** The lack of experiments makes it impossible to assess the practical utility of the algorithm and its performance relative to established methods.
*   **Unrealistic assumptions:** The requirement of knowing `κ` for parameter setting limits the algorithm's practical applicability.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper is well-written and addresses an important problem, its contributions are severely limited by a lack of originality and a failure to engage with the most relevant prior art, specifically the literature on variance reduction (e.g., SVRG). The resulting algorithm has a worse theoretical dependency on the condition number than established methods and is not supported by any empirical evidence. Based on these significant shortcomings, I cannot recommend acceptance. The paper would need to be substantially revised to properly position the work within the existing literature, improve the theoretical rates to be competitive, and provide a thorough empirical comparison against relevant baselines.

[1] Johnson, R., & Zhang, T. (2013). Accelerating stochastic gradient descent using predictive variance reduction. In Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS).
