

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization that aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations needed from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. The key insight is combining one full gradient with O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch to achieve linear convergence. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper compares EMGD with standard methods (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) and claims computational advantages when κ ≤ n^(2/3).

## Quality
The theoretical contribution is technically sound. The proof of Theorem 1 follows standard techniques for analyzing convex optimization algorithms but applies them to a novel mixed gradient approach. The analysis carefully tracks the interplay between the full gradient and stochastic gradient components, particularly how the domain shrinking (B(w̄ᵏ; Δᵏ)) controls the stochastic error. The condition on T (number of stochastic gradients per epoch) being O(κ²) appears reasonable given the smoothness assumptions.

However, the paper has significant limitations:
1. The assumption that κ must be known beforehand is restrictive in practice, and the authors only mention this as future work without proposing a practical estimation method.
2. The paper lacks experimental results to validate the theoretical claims. Without empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess the practical computational advantage over existing methods.
3. The claimed computational complexity advantage (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) is based on the assumption that full gradients are n times more expensive than stochastic gradients, but the paper doesn't quantify the actual computational savings in practice.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and convergence analysis are presented in a logical sequence. The notation is consistent, and the key steps in the proof are well-explained. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation challenges, particularly regarding how to handle the domain constraint in Step 8 for constrained optimization problems.

## Originality
The core idea of combining full and stochastic gradients to reduce full gradient evaluations is novel. While the paper cites "semi-stochastic" methods in related work, the specific mixed gradient approach with one full gradient per epoch is distinct from existing methods. The paper clearly differentiates itself from SAG and SDCA by highlighting advantages in storage requirements (O(d) vs. Ω(n)), applicability to constrained problems, and high-probability convergence guarantees (vs. expectation-based guarantees for SAG/SDCA).

However, the paper doesn't sufficiently address whether this approach has been explored in the broader optimization literature. For instance, the "stochastic primal-dual" methods in [27] or "incremental gradient methods" with occasional full gradient updates might share similar ideas.

## Significance
The problem addressed is highly significant in machine learning, where full gradient evaluations become prohibitively expensive for large datasets. The theoretical reduction in full gradient evaluations could be impactful for ill-conditioned problems. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the O(κ²) dependence on the number of stochastic gradients per epoch. For problems with large condition numbers, this could offset the gains from reducing full gradient evaluations.

The comparison table (Table 2) is useful but lacks practical context. The paper claims EMGD has lower computational complexity than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but this threshold might be restrictive for many practical machine learning problems where κ can be much larger.

## Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous proof
- Well-structured comparison with existing methods
- Highlights important practical advantages over SAG/SDCA (storage, constrained problems, high-probability convergence)
- The mixed gradient approach is elegant and simple

## Weaknesses
- No empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Requires known condition number κ (a significant practical limitation)
- O(κ²) stochastic gradient complexity per epoch may be problematic for ill-conditioned problems
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges

## Recommendation
This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to convex optimization that addresses a meaningful problem in large-scale machine learning. The main innovation—reducing full gradient evaluations while maintaining linear convergence through a mixed gradient approach—is novel and well-justified. However, the lack of experimental results and the restrictive assumption that κ must be known beforehand significantly limit the paper's impact.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Adding empirical results comparing computational efficiency with existing methods
2. Proposing a practical method to estimate κ (e.g., using matrix concentration inequalities as mentioned in the conclusion)
3. Providing more detailed discussion of the practical trade-offs for different problem settings

Given the strong theoretical foundation but lack of empirical validation, I recommend **weak acceptance**. With minor revisions addressing the practical limitations and adding experimental validation, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.

## Overall Assessment
The paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution to the optimization literature with a novel algorithm that reduces full gradient evaluations. However, the practical significance is somewhat diminished by the requirement for known condition number and lack of empirical results. The authors should address these limitations to strengthen the paper's impact for the NIPS community.
