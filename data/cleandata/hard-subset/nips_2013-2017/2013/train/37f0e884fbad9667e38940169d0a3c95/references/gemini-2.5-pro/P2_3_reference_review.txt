### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex functions. The main goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, which in classic accelerated methods scales as O(√κ log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. The authors propose a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which combines full and stochastic gradient information. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes one full gradient. Then, within the epoch, it performs a series of updates using a "mixed gradient" which serves as a variance-reduced stochastic gradient. The main theoretical result is that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The number of full gradient evaluations is thus independent of the condition number κ, which is the paper's central claim. The authors also highlight advantages over methods like SAG and SDCA, such as applicability to constrained problems, lower memory usage, and a high-probability convergence guarantee.

### Review

This paper tackles an important and well-studied problem in large-scale optimization. The goal of reducing the dependency on the condition number for expensive full gradient computations is well-motivated and significant. The proposed algorithm and its analysis present a clear trade-off: a logarithmic number of full gradients in exchange for a polynomial number (in κ) of stochastic gradients. While the paper has several strengths, it also suffers from major weaknesses, particularly concerning originality and its positioning within the existing literature.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its presentation, and the proof sketch in the analysis section follows a logical, inductive structure. The use of a shrinking domain and martingale concentration inequalities to bound the error terms is a standard but correctly applied technique. The final high-probability convergence bound is a strong theoretical result.

However, the work feels incomplete due to the total absence of empirical validation. For a paper proposing a new optimization algorithm, especially one that presents a trade-off (fewer full gradients for more stochastic ones), experiments are crucial to demonstrate in which practical regimes this trade-off is actually beneficial. Without experiments, the claims of computational efficiency improvements are purely theoretical and difficult to assess.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, Algorithm 1 is easy to understand, and the main theorem is stated precisely. The related work section provides a decent, if incomplete, overview of the field. The analysis section is quite dense and could benefit from more intuition, especially regarding the role of the shrinking domain constraint and its interaction with the variance of the mixed gradient.

A minor point on clarity: the term "mixed gradient" is not standard. The formulation `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the variance-reduced gradient used in SVRG. Using established terminology would make the paper's connection to prior work more transparent.

**Originality:** This is the paper's most significant weakness. The core algorithmic structure—an outer loop that computes a full gradient and an inner loop that uses it as an anchor for variance-reduced stochastic updates—is the cornerstone of the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). The "mixed gradient" proposed here is identical to the SVRG gradient estimator. The epoch-based structure is also identical. The paper extensively compares itself to SAG and SDCA but completely fails to mention or cite SVRG, which is arguably the most relevant and structurally similar predecessor.

SVRG also achieves a linear convergence rate. Its total complexity is typically analyzed as O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, which translates to O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (assuming an inner loop length proportional to κ). This is substantially better than the O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient complexity of EMGD.

While the analysis technique in EMGD, involving a shrinking Euclidean ball constraint, might have some novelty, the fundamental algorithm it is analyzing is not new. The paper's framing of its contribution as novel is therefore misleading. The key contribution seems to be a different analysis of an SVRG-like algorithm, which unfortunately leads to a worse dependency on κ.

**Significance:** The question the paper asks—can we make the number of full gradient accesses independent of κ?—is highly significant. Achieving this would be a major advance. The paper does achieve this, but at a very high cost: an O(κ²) dependency on the number of stochastic gradients. This trade-off is unlikely to be favorable in many practical situations.

Let's consider the computational cost comparison the paper makes. The cost of a full gradient is roughly `n` times the cost of a stochastic gradient.
*   Nesterov's AGD: O(n√κ log(1/ε))
*   EMGD: O((n + κ²) log(1/ε))

EMGD is better if `n + κ² < n√κ`, which is rarely true unless κ is very small. The paper's own comparison suggests it is competitive only when κ ≤ n^(2/3). In the context of ill-conditioned problems where κ is large, this condition is very restrictive. Compared to SVRG's O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) complexity, EMGD appears strictly worse in its κ dependency.

The stated advantages (handling constraints, low memory) are valid points. SVRG and SAG in their basic forms are for unconstrained problems, and SAG requires O(nd) memory. The ability of EMGD to handle constraints is a genuine advantage, but this should have been framed as an extension of the SVRG-style variance reduction framework to constrained settings, rather than a completely new algorithm.

---

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper addresses a very important problem and presents a clear theoretical result: the number of full gradient computations is independent of κ.
2.  The derived convergence bound holds with high probability, which is stronger than the in-expectation results of some competing methods.
3.  The algorithm naturally handles constraints, an advantage over standard SAG/SVRG/SDCA.
4.  The analysis, while for a known algorithmic template, provides a different perspective using a shrinking domain.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Major Originality Issue:** The proposed algorithm is nearly identical to SVRG, which is not cited or discussed. This represents a critical gap in positioning the work and overstates its novelty.
2.  **Worse Theoretical Complexity:** The resulting stochastic gradient complexity of O(κ² log(1/ε)) is significantly worse than that of SVRG (O(κ log(1/ε))) and other modern variance-reduced methods, making the proposed trade-off questionable from a computational standpoint.
3.  **No Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any experiments to validate its theoretical claims or to demonstrate a practical regime where its specific trade-offs are beneficial.
4.  **Practicality:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ, which is a common but important limitation that is only briefly mentioned as future work.

**Conclusion:**

While the goal of the paper is laudable and the analysis is technically executed, the work suffers from a fatal flaw in its failure to acknowledge and compare against its most immediate predecessor, SVRG. This oversight undermines the claims of novelty. Furthermore, the resulting algorithm is theoretically less efficient than existing methods in terms of its dependence on the condition number. Coupled with a complete lack of empirical evidence, the paper does not make a convincing case for its contribution to the state of the art. Therefore, I recommend rejection. A future version of this work would need to be substantially reframed in the context of SVRG, provide a compelling theoretical or empirical reason why the O(κ²) complexity is acceptable or can be improved, and demonstrate its advantages in experiments.
