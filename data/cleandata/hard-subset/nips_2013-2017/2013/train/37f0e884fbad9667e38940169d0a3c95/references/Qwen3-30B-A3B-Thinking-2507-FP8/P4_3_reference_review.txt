

# Review

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that reduces the number of full gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)) independent of the condition number κ, while using O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The authors address a practical limitation in optimization for large-scale machine learning problems where full gradient computations are expensive.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper builds upon established results in convex optimization (e.g., Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent with O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity for full gradients [17], and SAG/SDCA with linear convergence for finite-sum problems [22, 23]). The authors correctly identify that while stochastic methods avoid full gradients, they typically have slower convergence (polynomial in 1/ε), while full-gradient methods have better convergence but high computational cost for large datasets. The paper's key insight is that by combining full and stochastic gradients, one can achieve linear convergence with full gradient evaluations independent of κ.

The work relates to recent NIPS papers on stochastic optimization [9, 10, 21, 22, 23, 26] but presents a distinct approach that differs from SAG and SDCA in several key aspects: (1) works for both constrained and unconstrained problems, (2) requires only O(d) storage (vs. O(n) for SAG/SDCA), (3) achieves convergence with high probability (vs. expectation for SAG/SDCA), and (4) has a different computational complexity profile.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with a complete proof of the convergence rate (Theorem 1). The proof uses standard optimization techniques (strongly convex properties, martingale inequalities) correctly and is well-structured. The conditions for convergence are clearly stated and justified.

2. **Novelty**: The combination of full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition-number-independent full gradient evaluations is novel. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior semi-stochastic methods [12, 13] and SAG/SDCA by providing specific theoretical advantages.

3. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation, algorithm description (Algorithm 1), and theoretical results are presented logically and accessibly. The comparisons in Table 2 effectively highlight the computational complexity trade-offs.

4. **Significance**: The result that full gradient evaluations can be reduced to O(log(1/ε)) independent of κ is theoretically significant for ill-conditioned problems. The paper demonstrates how this approach can be advantageous in practical machine learning scenarios (e.g., when κ ≤ n^(2/3)).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper lacks any empirical results to support the practical advantages claimed. Without experiments on real datasets or comparisons with existing methods, it's difficult to assess the actual computational benefits.

2. **Condition Number Knowledge Requirement**: The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ beforehand, which is often difficult to obtain in practice. While the authors mention future work on estimating κ, this limitation isn't adequately addressed in the current paper.

3. **Limited Comparison with Other Methods**: The comparison with SAG/SDCA is somewhat limited. The paper states EMGD has lower computational cost than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but doesn't thoroughly compare with other methods that might also use mixed gradient approaches.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper states that "the constrained problem in Step 8 can be solved efficiently" but provides no details about when this is true or how it would be implemented in practice.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to convex optimization. The EMGD algorithm is novel, well-analyzed, and addresses a practical problem in optimization for large-scale machine learning. The theoretical results are significant and correctly positioned within the literature.

The main limitations are the lack of empirical validation and the requirement for prior knowledge of the condition number. However, for a theoretical contribution to optimization, the absence of experiments is less critical than in a more applied ML paper. The paper's focus on theoretical rigor and clear comparison with existing work aligns well with NeurIPS standards.

The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature, presents a novel approach with rigorous analysis, and provides concrete theoretical advantages over prior work. While the practical implementation challenges are noted, the theoretical contribution is substantial and makes a meaningful advance in understanding the trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to convex optimization that is well-positioned within the NeurIPS community. The technical soundness is excellent, the novelty is clear, and the significance is appropriate for the conference. The absence of empirical results is a limitation but not a deal-breaker for a theoretical contribution. The paper should be accepted.
