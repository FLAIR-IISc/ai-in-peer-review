Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for minimizing smooth, strongly convex objectives F over a convex domain. The algorithm alternates between occasional full gradient evaluations at the epoch anchor w̄k and many per-iteration “mixed” gradients of the form ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), where f is drawn from a function oracle with Ef[f] = F and each f is L-smooth. EMGD uses shrinking trust regions (projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk)) and epoch averaging to ensure that the stochastic component is well controlled. The main theorem establishes linear convergence with high probability: to reach ε error, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, where κ = L/λ.

Relation to prior work
- Deterministic first-order methods: Nesterov’s accelerated gradient achieves O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients for smooth, strongly convex F, which is optimal among first-order methods accessing only full gradients.
- Stochastic methods: Standard SGD has O(1/(λε)) iteration complexity; with strong convexity and appropriate averaging/schedules, one can reach optimal O(1/(λε)). For finite-sum structure F = (1/n)∑ fi, variance-reduced methods (SAG, SDCA) achieve linear convergence with complexities O(n log(1/ε)) or O((n + κ) log(1/ε)).
- The mixed-gradient estimator used here, ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄), is essentially the SVRG control-variate estimator (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013). EMGD augments this with shrinking-ball projections and a conservative epoch schedule.

Technical quality
- The analysis is careful and uses standard tools: smoothness/strong convexity, martingale concentration (Azuma–Hoeffding), and epoch averaging. The high-probability linear rate follows by bounding the variance via the shrinking-ball constraint. The proof is technically sound given the assumptions.
- However, the resulting stochastic-gradient complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) is quite loose compared to state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods (e.g., SVRG/SAGA/SDCA) that achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) or O(κ log(1/ε)) in oracle models that allow reevaluation of the same f. Given the identical gradient estimator, a tighter analysis without the shrinking-ball projection typically yields O(κ) dependence.
- The function-oracle model assumes that on a single draw of f one can query gradients at multiple points (wt and w̄k). This is natural for finite-sum or persistent-sample settings but stronger than the standard stochastic gradient oracle. The paper should clarify the scope of problems where Of is realistic.
- The constrained step projects onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk). For general W, this projection can be harder than projecting onto W alone; the paper assumes Step 8 can be solved efficiently but does not discuss when this holds.
- The theorem requires knowledge of κ to set T and η and a bound Δ1 on the initial radius; the paper acknowledges this as a drawback but provides only sketchy guidance.

Originality
- Conceptually, mixing full and stochastic gradients to obtain a control-variate estimator and linear convergence is not new (SAG/SDCA; SVRG uses the exact same estimator). The shrinking-ball mechanism and the high-probability analysis on this estimator in a general function-oracle model are the main differentiators. However, SVRG develops a simpler, unconstrained update with constant step size and tighter iteration complexity (O(κ)), and should be discussed/cited.
- Claims about advantages over SAG/SDCA (memory, constraints) are partially valid, but modern variants (e.g., proximal versions, projection-friendly formulations, limited-memory schemes) narrow these gaps. At minimum, the discussion should be updated and nuanced.

Significance
- The stated goal—reducing the number of full gradients to O(log(1/ε)) independent of κ—is achieved and is practical when full gradients are very expensive and stochastic gradients are cheap. High-probability guarantees are useful.
- That said, the κ^2 dependence in the number of stochastic gradients is likely too large in ill-conditioned settings; methods with O(κ) dependence dominate in most regimes. Without empirical results, it is difficult to assess whether EMGD’s tradeoff yields wall-clock gains.
- The ability to handle constraints and low memory are positives; still, a clearer characterization of when the projection and oracle model are feasible in practice is needed.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but there are issues: duplicate equation numbering, typos (“epoches,” “interstage”), and some assumptions introduced late (Δ1). The comparison table is referenced but not included in the text provided.
- The strong convexity property is invoked correctly, though stating the standard inequality ⟨∇F(w) − ∇F(w′), w − w′⟩ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖^2 would help readers.
- The paper lacks experiments. For a NIPS audience, even a small empirical study contrasting EMGD with accelerated gradient and variance-reduced methods under different n/κ and full/stochastic cost ratios would greatly strengthen the submission.

Pros
- Clear target: condition-number–independent number of full-gradient calls.
- High-probability linear convergence with low memory and simple per-iteration updates.
- Potential applicability to constrained problems.

Cons
- Gradient estimator and epoch structure overlap strongly with SVRG; novelty is limited and complexity bounds are weaker (κ^2 vs κ).
- No experiments; practical impact and tradeoffs are unsubstantiated.
- Requires knowledge of κ and efficient projection onto W ∩ ball; scope of applicability is not fully clarified.
- Comparisons to more recent variance-reduction literature are incomplete.

Suggestions for improvement
- Position the work explicitly relative to SVRG/SAGA/MISO and explain whether the shrinking-ball projection yields any real advantages (e.g., constraints, high-probability) and whether one can tighten the analysis to O(κ).
- Provide experiments measuring wall-clock time under controlled full/stochastic cost ratios and across κ, comparing to AGD, SVRG/SAGA, SDCA.
- Clarify the oracle model and practical settings where Of is available; discuss streaming scenarios.
- Discuss how to set Δ1 and estimate κ in practice; consider adaptive schemes or doubling tricks to remove prior knowledge.
- Improve clarity and fix typos/equation numbering.

Overall evaluation
Quality: moderate; sound analysis but suboptimal bounds and missing experiments.
Clarity: reasonable but needs polishing and missing empirical section.
Originality: limited relative to SVRG; needs stronger differentiation.
Significance: potentially useful in specific regimes, but currently not compelling versus existing methods.

Recommendation: Weak reject unless the authors can (i) more thoroughly relate to and differentiate from SVRG/SAGA and (ii) provide empirical evidence and/or tighter κ dependence.
