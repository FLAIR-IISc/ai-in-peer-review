

# Review

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm that achieves linear convergence for smooth strongly convex problems while reducing the number of full gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)), independent of the condition number κ. The key insight is that by combining full gradients with stochastic gradients in a carefully designed mixed gradient approach, the algorithm avoids the O(√κ) dependence that plagues standard full gradient methods.

The paper makes a bold and novel contribution to the optimization literature. While it's well known that stochastic gradient methods achieve better iteration complexity (O(1/ε)) than full gradient methods (O(√κ log(1/ε))) for smooth strongly convex problems, this work flips the tradeoff: it shows how to maintain the fast convergence rate of full gradient methods while dramatically reducing the number of full gradient computations. The authors prove that EMGD requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach ε-optimality, representing a fundamental shift in the complexity landscape for this problem class.

This is not merely an incremental improvement over existing methods. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from related work:
- Unlike standard full gradient methods (Nesterov, 2004), EMGD reduces the full gradient dependence from O(√κ) to O(1)
- Unlike SAG/SDCA (Roux et al., 2012; Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013), EMGD achieves condition number independent full gradient access
- Unlike semi-stochastic methods (Ghadimi & Lan, 2012), EMGD achieves linear convergence with high probability (not just in expectation)

The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with a clean inductive proof of the convergence rate. The paper also provides valuable comparisons showing that EMGD can outperform existing methods when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is a reasonable condition in many machine learning applications (e.g., with regularization). The authors thoughtfully address limitations, such as the need to know κ in advance, and propose potential solutions through regularization or sampling.

The paper's strengths are clear: it identifies a fundamental gap in the literature, proposes a bold new approach that fundamentally changes the complexity landscape, and provides a rigorous theoretical analysis. The contribution is not just another optimization variant but represents a new direction in understanding how to leverage both full and stochastic gradients for optimal performance.

While the paper acknowledges that EMGD may have higher computational complexity than SAG/SDCA for large κ, this limitation doesn't undermine the core novelty. The authors correctly position their work as addressing a different aspect of optimization efficiency (reducing full gradient access) rather than absolute computational cost. The practical advantages they highlight (constrained optimization support, lower storage requirements, distributed computing compatibility) further strengthen the contribution.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly contextualized within the existing literature. The technical details are presented with appropriate mathematical precision, and the experimental validation (while not the focus) aligns with the theoretical claims.

This work represents a significant conceptual advance in optimization theory. It's exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values over incremental improvements. The core idea of achieving condition number independent access to full gradients is both surprising and powerful, with clear implications for large-scale machine learning applications where full gradient computations are expensive.

The paper meets all the key criteria: it's of high quality with sound theoretical analysis, clearly written, highly original, and significant for the field. The minor limitations (like needing to know κ) are acknowledged and addressed in the discussion, and don't detract from the fundamental novelty.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper introduces a genuinely new concept that changes how we think about optimization complexity for smooth strongly convex problems, and it does so with rigorous theoretical backing. The contribution is substantial enough to warrant publication in NIPS.
