This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, focusing on the high computational cost associated with evaluating full gradients, especially for ill-conditioned problems (i.e., large condition number κ). The standard iteration complexity for first-order methods using full gradients is `O(sqrt(κ) * log(1/ε))`, which is known to be optimal. The authors propose to bypass this lower bound by allowing an algorithm access to two oracles: a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle.

The core contribution is a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient. It then performs `T = O(κ^2)` iterations using a "mixed gradient" estimator that combines the stored full gradient with stochastic gradient information to reduce variance. The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution by computing `O(log(1/ε))` full gradients and `O(κ^2 * log(1/ε))` stochastic gradients. This achieves the paper's central goal: the number of expensive full gradient evaluations is made independent of the condition number κ.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual novelty and the boldness of its core research question. The authors do not simply propose an incremental improvement to an existing algorithm; instead, they re-examine the fundamental trade-offs in first-order optimization. The idea of isolating the complexity of the "expensive" full gradient oracle and seeking to make it independent of the problem's conditioning is a significant and insightful contribution. This reframing provides a new and valuable perspective on algorithm design, particularly for large-scale settings where full gradient computations are the main bottleneck.

The proposed approach of mixing two types of oracles to circumvent a known lower bound is clever and compelling. The theoretical result is strong and directly supports the paper's main claim. The analysis guarantees linear convergence with high probability, which is a stronger result than the in-expectation guarantees common in related literature (e.g., SAG, SDCA). Furthermore, the algorithm is presented for a general stochastic optimization setting (`F(w) = E[f(w)]`) and for constrained domains, which gives it broader applicability than many variance-reduced methods that are designed specifically for unconstrained finite-sum problems.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality of the Algorithm:** The most significant weakness is the algorithm's strong resemblance to existing, uncited work. The "mixed gradient" estimator `g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) - ∇f(w̄)` and the epoch-based structure (compute a full gradient, then run many cheaper steps using it as a reference) are the defining features of the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). The proposed EMGD algorithm appears to be a direct application of the SVRG technique. The lack of citation and discussion of SVRG and related variance-reduction literature (e.g., SAGA, MISO) is a major omission. While the *goal* of the paper is novel (isolating full gradient complexity), the algorithmic machinery used to achieve it is not. This needs to be explicitly acknowledged and addressed.

2.  **Practical Complexity:** The complexity of `O(κ^2)` stochastic gradients per epoch is extremely high. For many machine learning problems, κ can be large, and this quadratic dependence may render the algorithm impractical. The paper's own comparison in Table 2 for the finite-sum case shows that EMGD's complexity is `O(n + κ^2)`, while methods like SAG and SDCA are `O(n + κ)`. In many scenarios, `κ^2` will dominate `n`, making EMGD significantly less efficient. While the paper achieves its theoretical goal, the practical cost of doing so is very steep and this trade-off is not sufficiently explored.

3.  **Dependence on κ:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ to set the number of inner iterations `T`. This is a common but important limitation of many optimization algorithms, and the paper relegates the solution to "future work" without offering much insight.

4.  **No Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theory is the main contribution, the absence of any experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical implications of the `O(κ^2)` cost. Even a simple experiment could illustrate the trade-off between the number of full gradients and the total runtime compared to Nesterov's AGD and SVRG.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty of the Goal:** The paper poses a new, interesting, and important question about decoupling full gradient complexity from the condition number. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   **Bold Approach:** The idea of using mixed oracles to bypass a fundamental complexity barrier is creative and thought-provoking.
*   **Strong Theoretical Result:** The paper provides a clean theoretical result that successfully answers the question it poses, demonstrating a `kappa`-independent complexity for full gradient calls.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Rediscovery of Existing Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm (EMGD) is essentially a rediscovery of SVRG, a highly influential and uncited work. This seriously undermines the paper's claim to algorithmic novelty.
*   **Prohibitive Complexity:** The `O(κ^2)` dependence on stochastic gradients is a major practical limitation that is not adequately addressed.
*   **Incomplete Related Work:** The failure to cite and compare with the SVRG family of methods is a critical flaw.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the serious issue regarding the missing citation of SVRG, I am leaning towards **acceptance**. My recommendation is based on the high value I place on the paper's central idea. The question it asks is novel, important, and pushes the field to think about optimization complexity in a new way. This conceptual contribution is strong enough to warrant publication, even if the proposed algorithm itself is a rediscovery. The paper achieves its stated theoretical goal, and that goal is a worthy one.

However, acceptance must be strictly conditional on a major revision. The authors must:
1.  Thoroughly revise the introduction and related work to correctly position their contribution relative to SVRG and other variance-reduction methods. They should clarify that while the algorithm is similar or identical to SVRG, their contribution lies in the novel analysis and framing of the problem in terms of minimizing full gradient calls.
2.  Be more transparent about the severe practical limitations imposed by the `O(κ^2)` complexity, discussing when their method might be preferable despite this cost.

This is a paper with a beautiful and bold idea, hampered by a significant but correctable flaw in its presentation and literature review. The core concept deserves to be part of the conversation at NIPS.
