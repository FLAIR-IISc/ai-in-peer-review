

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors address a well-known challenge in convex optimization: when the condition number κ is large, standard first-order methods using full gradients require O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations to reach ε-optimality, which can be prohibitively expensive for large-scale problems. The key insight of the paper is that by combining both full and stochastic gradients, the number of full gradient evaluations can be reduced to O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ, while still maintaining a linear convergence rate.

The authors prove that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This is a significant improvement over standard full gradient methods (which require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients) and stochastic methods (which typically have polynomial convergence in 1/ε). The paper compares EMGD with related work, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA, and identifies advantages in terms of generality (works for constrained problems), storage requirements (Ω(d) vs. Ω(n)), and convergence guarantees (high probability vs. expectation).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper presents a clean and rigorous theoretical analysis showing that the number of full gradient evaluations can be made independent of the condition number κ, which is a significant theoretical insight for optimization.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly identifies the problem (condition number dependence in full gradient methods) and the key challenge (reducing full gradient access without sacrificing convergence rate).

3. **Comprehensive Related Work**: The paper provides a thorough review of related work in convex optimization, correctly situating the proposed approach within the literature.

4. **Detailed Analysis**: The convergence proof is well-structured and carefully developed, with clear statements of assumptions and conditions.

5. **Practical Insights**: The authors identify specific regimes where their method is advantageous (κ ≤ n^{2/3}) and discuss practical considerations like the need to know κ in advance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: The paper lacks any empirical results to validate the theoretical claims. NIPS strongly values empirical evidence alongside theoretical contributions, especially for optimization algorithms where practical performance is crucial.

2. **Practical Limitation: Need for Known κ**: The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ in advance, which is a significant practical limitation. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't provide a concrete solution for how to estimate κ in practice (beyond a brief mention of using regularizers).

3. **High Stochastic Gradient Complexity**: The O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations could be prohibitively expensive for large κ, potentially offsetting the benefits of reduced full gradient evaluations.

4. **Comparison with SAG/SDCA**: The comparison with SAG and SDCA (Table 2) is somewhat superficial. The paper claims EMGD has advantages in storage and convergence guarantees but doesn't quantify these advantages in terms of actual runtime or scalability.

5. **Limited Discussion of Practical Scenarios**: The paper doesn't provide concrete examples of real-world problems where κ ≤ n^{2/3}, making it hard to assess the practical relevance of the proposed method.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The proofs appear correct and well-structured. However, the lack of experimental validation is a significant weakness for a NIPS submission. The authors are careful about stating their assumptions and limitations, which is a strength. The paper is a complete theoretical contribution but lacks empirical validation.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The algorithm and its components are clearly explained. Some technical terms (like "epoch gradient descent") could be briefly defined for readers unfamiliar with the specific literature. The notation is consistent throughout, which aids clarity.

**Originality**: The paper presents a novel combination of full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition-number-independent full gradient access. While the idea of combining full and stochastic gradients isn't entirely new (semi-stochastic methods exist), the specific algorithm and analysis are novel. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related methods in Section 2.

**Significance**: The theoretical contribution is significant for understanding the trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients in convex optimization. The result could be useful for large-scale optimization problems where full gradient computation is expensive. However, the practical impact is limited by the requirement to know κ in advance and the high number of stochastic gradient evaluations for large κ. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method will be widely adopted in practice.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro Arguments**:
- The theoretical contribution is novel and addresses a well-known challenge in convex optimization.
- The analysis is rigorous and carefully developed.
- The paper clearly articulates the trade-offs and provides a precise characterization of the algorithm's performance.
- The comparison with existing methods is thorough and identifies specific advantages.

**Con Arguments**:
- The lack of experimental results is a major weakness for a NIPS submission.
- The requirement to know κ in advance is a significant practical limitation.
- The high stochastic gradient complexity (O(κ² log(1/ε))) may make the algorithm impractical for large κ.
- The paper doesn't provide concrete examples of real-world problems where the proposed method would be advantageous.

## Recommendations

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to convex optimization. While the theoretical results are sound and novel, the lack of experimental validation and the practical limitation of requiring knowledge of κ in advance significantly weaken the paper's impact for NIPS. I would recommend a major revision that includes:

1. Experimental results comparing EMGD with standard methods (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) on real-world optimization problems.
2. A thorough discussion of how to estimate κ in practice, possibly with a simple heuristic that could be incorporated into the algorithm.
3. A more detailed analysis of the practical trade-offs, including runtime comparisons.
4. A clearer articulation of the specific problem domains where κ ≤ n^{2/3} is satisfied.

Without these additions, the paper's contribution, while theoretically interesting, would be limited in its practical impact and would not meet NIPS's typical standards for empirical validation.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper has a solid theoretical contribution that addresses a meaningful problem, but the lack of experimental validation and the practical limitation of requiring knowledge of κ in advance are significant weaknesses. With the suggested revisions, this paper could be a strong contribution to NIPS, but as it stands, it does not provide sufficient evidence of practical relevance.

**Final Score: 4 (Weak Reject)**
