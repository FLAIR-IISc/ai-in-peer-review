## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm aiming to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods for strongly convex optimization by strategically interleaving full and stochastic gradient evaluations. The core idea—dividing the optimization into epochs and employing a mixed gradient—is presented as a means to achieve a linear convergence rate with a full-gradient complexity independent of the condition number, κ.

The motivation is well-articulated. The trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost is a central consideration in optimization, and reducing the dependence on κ, particularly for ill-conditioned problems, is a valuable goal. The authors correctly identify the theoretical limitations of standard full-gradient methods and position their work within the context of existing approaches like epoch gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA.

However, despite a clearly stated objective and a reasonable high-level approach, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that raise serious concerns about its novelty and practical impact. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper identifies a relevant and important problem – the high cost of full gradients when κ is large.
*   **Logical Motivation:** The combination of full and stochastic gradients to address the condition number dependence is a sensible direction to explore.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a theoretical convergence analysis, establishing a linear convergence rate and quantifying the number of full gradient evaluations.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The authors acknowledge and briefly compare their approach to existing methods like SAG and SDCA.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core concept of interleaving full and stochastic gradients is not entirely new. Semi-stochastic gradient methods, as referenced in the related work, already explore this space. The specific implementation in EMGD, while clearly defined, doesn't appear to represent a substantial conceptual leap beyond existing strategies. The authors need to more precisely articulate *how* EMGD differs fundamentally from semi-stochastic methods and provide a compelling justification for its advantages.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** This is a significant drawback. While the theoretical analysis is presented, the paper *completely lacks* any experimental evaluation. Without empirical results, it’s impossible to assess the practical performance of EMGD, its sensitivity to parameters, or its effectiveness compared to established algorithms like SDCA or even basic semi-stochastic approaches. The authors state EMGD might not be the best regarding computational cost, but without data this is mere speculation.
*   **Assumptions and Practicality:** The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions (e.g., knowledge of κ) that may not hold in practice. The paper briefly mentions estimating κ but lacks any details on how this might be achieved reliably. The algorithm also requires solving a constrained optimization problem at each iteration (step 8), which could be computationally expensive, potentially offsetting the benefits of reduced full-gradient evaluations. This aspect isn't sufficiently discussed.
*   **Comparison Table Interpretation:** The interpretation of Table 2 feels somewhat selective. The claim that EMGD is advantageous only when κ ≤ n<sup>2/3</sup> is presented without a deeper analysis of the constants hidden within the big-O notation. A more rigorous comparison considering practical scenarios is needed.
*   **Writing Quality:** While generally clear, the presentation could be more concise. Some sections feel repetitive.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Absence of experimental validation.
*   Limited novelty compared to existing methods.
*   Potentially impractical assumptions.

**For:**

*   Well-defined theoretical analysis.
*   Clear problem statement and motivation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a weak submission. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the lack of empirical validation is a fatal flaw. The claims of achieving condition number independence are difficult to evaluate without practical demonstration. Furthermore, the limited novelty of the approach and the potential practical challenges associated with parameter estimation and constrained optimization raise significant concerns. The paper feels incomplete and speculative. 

**Recommendation:** Reject. 

The authors should significantly strengthen the paper by:

1.  **Providing extensive experimental results** comparing EMGD to state-of-the-art algorithms (SDCA, semi-stochastic methods) on a range of datasets and problem sizes.
2.  **Clearly articulating the novelty** of EMGD compared to existing approaches, focusing on specific advantages and limitations.
3.  **Addressing the practical challenges** related to parameter estimation and constrained optimization.
4.  **Providing a more thorough and nuanced comparison** to related work, including a detailed analysis of the constant factors in the complexity bounds.
