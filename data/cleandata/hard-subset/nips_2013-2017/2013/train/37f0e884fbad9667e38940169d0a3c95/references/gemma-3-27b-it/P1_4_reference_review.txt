## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm designed to reduce the computational cost of solving smooth, strongly convex optimization problems, particularly when the condition number (κ) is large. The core idea is to interleave full gradient evaluations with stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, achieving a linear convergence rate while requiring only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations – independent of κ.  The authors position this work as bridging the gap between the faster convergence of full-gradient methods and the lower per-iteration cost of stochastic gradient methods. They provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating the convergence rate and compare EMGD’s computational complexity to existing methods like SAG and SDCA.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper appropriately situates itself within the landscape of convex optimization, referencing key works in both deterministic and stochastic gradient descent. It correctly highlights the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost.  The connection to Epoch Gradient Descent (EGD) is well-established, and the authors effectively position EMGD as an extension that leverages stochastic gradients to improve upon EGD’s full-gradient dependence. The discussion of SAG, SDCA, and their complexities provides a useful comparative framework.  However, while mentioning related work in distributed computing to potentially mitigate the full gradient cost (step 4 in Algorithm 1), this is briefly touched upon and could benefit from further elaboration.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework, aiming for condition number independence, is a compelling approach.  This addresses a practical concern in large-scale optimization, where full gradient evaluations can be prohibitively expensive.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a formal convergence analysis (Theorem 1) that supports the claim of linear convergence and the reduced number of full gradient evaluations.  The proof outline provided in the appendix gives a good indication of the technical rigor.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind the work – reducing the computational burden of large κ in full-gradient methods. The introduction does a good job of highlighting the problem and potential benefits.
* **Comparative Analysis:** The comparison to existing methods like Nesterov, SAG, and SDCA, particularly in terms of computational complexity (Table 2), is valuable for understanding the algorithm’s strengths and weaknesses.
* **Potential for Practical Impact:**  The algorithm is well-suited for problems where stochastic gradients are readily available and full gradients are expensive, making it potentially impactful in machine learning applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations:**  While theoretically sound, the paper lacks a practical evaluation.  It’s unclear how sensitive the performance is to the choice of parameters (η, ∆1, T) and whether the algorithm performs well in practice compared to existing methods.  Experiments would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The condition on T (T ≥ 1152L<sup>2</sup>/λ<sup>2</sup> ln(δ)) seems restrictive and might be difficult to satisfy in practice.  Further investigation into the sensitivity of the convergence rate to the choice of T would be beneficial.
* **Assumption of Known Condition Number:** The algorithm currently requires knowing the condition number κ beforehand, as noted by the authors. While they mention potential ways to estimate it, this is acknowledged as a drawback and warrants further development.
* **Clarity of the Mixed Gradient:** The justification for the specific form of the mixed gradient (g̃<sub>kt</sub>) could be elaborated further.  Why this particular combination of full and stochastic gradients leads to the observed convergence properties isn't fully intuitive from the presentation.
* **Limited Discussion of Domain Constraint:** The use of a shrinking domain (W ∩ B(w̄<sub>k</sub>; ∆<sub>k</sub>)) in step 8 of Algorithm 1 is mentioned but lacks a detailed discussion of its practical implications.  How efficiently can this constrained optimization step be solved in different scenarios?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm addressing a significant challenge in large-scale optimization.
* The convergence analysis is rigorous and supports the claims made.
* The comparative analysis provides valuable context for understanding the algorithm's potential benefits.
* The topic is relevant to the NIPS community, aligning with the conference's focus on machine learning and optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of experimental validation is a significant weakness.
* The restrictive parameter condition on T could limit the algorithm’s practical applicability.
* The dependence on knowing the condition number upfront is a practical limitation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of optimization. The idea of EMGD is promising, and the convergence analysis is well-executed. However, the absence of experimental results and the practical limitations regarding parameter tuning and condition number estimation significantly weaken the paper.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement for the authors to address the weaknesses by including experimental results and further investigating the practical considerations before resubmission. If the authors can demonstrate the algorithm’s performance on real-world datasets and address the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity and the condition number, this could become a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
