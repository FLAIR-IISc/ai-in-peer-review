Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth, strongly convex optimization that uses both (i) an oracle returning the full gradient ∇F(w) and (ii) a “function oracle” returning a random f with F = E[f], each f being L-smooth. At the start of each epoch, EMGD computes one full gradient at the epoch anchor w̄k. Within the epoch, it uses a mixed (variance-reduced) gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), and performs T projected updates onto a shrinking ball B(w̄k, Δk). With appropriate T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)), step size η = 1/(L√T), and Δk+1 = Δk/√2, the method reduces suboptimality by a constant factor per epoch, yielding linear convergence. The paper emphasizes that the number of full gradient computations is O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ, while O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients are used.

This idea is closely related to variance-reduction methods that form control-variate estimators using a periodically refreshed anchor, most notably SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014), and their accelerated variants. In the finite-sum setting (F = (1/n)∑ fi), SVRG/SAGA achieve linear convergence with total work O((n + κ) log(1/ε)), and also use O(log(1/ε)) “full passes” (full gradients or equivalent). Compared to these, EMGD’s epoch structure and gradient estimator are very similar, but EMGD requires a shrinking-ball constraint and yields a weaker per-epoch complexity O(κ^2) for constant-factor progress.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The proof contains substantive issues.
  - The paper states (4): ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖ “as strong convexity.” This is incorrect. Strong convexity implies ⟨∇F(w) − ∇F(w′), w − w′⟩ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖^2, not the norm inequality. The norm lower bound is generally false. Several later steps reference (4).
  - In (13), the inequality F(wt) − F(w∗) ≤ ⟨∇F(wt), wt − w∗⟩ − λ‖wt − w∗‖^2 is cited from (4). The correct strong convexity inequality yields a −(λ/2) term, not −λ. Constants can be repaired, but as written the proof is incorrect.
  - The update objective in Algorithm 1 Step 8 and equation (11) omits the usual 1/2 factor on the quadratic term. Later the analysis argues the subproblem is 1-strongly convex, which is consistent only if the quadratic has the 1/2 coefficient. As written, there is a mismatch in constants/dimensions.
  - Multiple equation numbering inconsistencies (e.g., “(2)” reused) and mis-citations make the proof hard to follow and error-prone.
  - The high-probability bound uses Hoeffding-Azuma with a per-iteration bound |Vt| ≤ 4LΔ^2; this relies on both the ball constraint and smoothness; that part is reasonable. However, the necessity of a shrinking ball to control variance is a nontrivial practical requirement and not discussed experimentally.

- Completeness: No experiments are provided to assess practical performance, the cost of projections onto W ∩ B(·, ·), or sensitivity to hyperparameters.

Clarity:
- The manuscript has numerous typos and grammatical errors: “epoches,” “interstage,” inconsistent notation (k vs kt indices), and missing factors in equations. Tables referenced (Table 1, Table 2) are not included here; several references lack full bibliographic details. The exposition of assumptions mixes nonstandard definitions (see (4)) and hides important requirements (need for λ, L, κ, Δ1).
- The algorithm requires solving a projection/prox onto W ∩ B(w̄k, Δk) each iteration; the computational implications are not discussed.

Originality:
- The mixed gradient estimator is essentially the SVRG control variate estimator. The novelty claimed—“condition-number-independent access to full gradients”—is also shared by SVRG-like methods which refresh full gradients periodically, leading to O(log(1/ε)) such computations. EMGD’s need for a shrinking trust-region-like ball and its κ^2 per-epoch complexity seem weaker than the O(κ) epoch complexity of SVRG/SAGA in the finite-sum setting. Key related work (SVRG, SAGA, Prox-SVRG, SARAH/SARAH++, Katyusha, Catalyst) is not cited or discussed, nor are known high-probability analyses available for some of these methods.

Significance:
- Theoretical contribution: A linear-convergent scheme under an expectation-based “function oracle” is potentially interesting, but the complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients is not competitive with state of the art in the finite-sum regime (O((n+κ) log(1/ε))). In the expectation setting (not finite-sum), more discussion is needed to situate EMGD versus variance-reduction techniques that only rely on sampling f and do not require shrinking domains.
- Practical impact: Lacking experiments and given the need to know κ, λ, L and to tune T, η, Δ1, as well as the projection onto shrinking balls, the practical utility is unclear.

Pros:
- Clear goal: reduce full gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)).
- High-probability linear rate under the given oracle model.
- Storage efficiency compared to SAG/SAGA/SDCA.

Cons:
- Critical technical errors: incorrect strong convexity assumption (4) and misuse of constants in (13); inconsistent quadratic coefficient in the update.
- Suboptimal complexity: O(κ^2) stochastic gradients per epoch versus O(κ) in standard VR methods; comparisons to SVRG/SAGA missing.
- Requires knowledge of κ and an impractical Δ1 bound involving F(w∗); no adaptive strategy provided.
- Heavy reliance on projections onto W ∩ B(·), with no empirical assessment.
- Numerous typos, formatting issues, and missing references/tables; clarity suffers.

Questions for authors:
1. Please correct the definition and uses of strong convexity. Can the proof be repaired with the standard inequality (with λ/2) and without (4)?
2. Why not use a constant step size and avoid the 1/√T scaling, to obtain O(κ) per-epoch complexity as in SVRG? Is the shrinking ball essential?
3. How does EMGD compare empirically to SVRG/SAGA/SDCA on standard datasets, both in wall-clock and oracle calls?
4. How to set Δ1 without knowledge of F(w∗)? Can you propose an adaptive scheme for κ, L, λ and epoch length?
5. What is the cost of the projection onto W ∩ B in typical constrained ML problems?
6. Please clarify the claim that SAG/SDCA only have linear convergence in expectation; there are high-probability variants.

Minor/formatting issues:
- “epoches” → “epochs”; “interstage” → “investigate”; several punctuation and indexing errors (g̃k vs g̃kt).
- Missing 1/2 factor in the quadratic term in Algorithm 1 and equation (11).
- Equation numbering is inconsistent (reused (2), references to wrong equations).
- References are incomplete; key related work (SVRG, SAGA, Prox-SVRG, SARAH, Katyusha) is missing.
- Tables 1 and 2 are referenced but not present; quantitative comparisons are not reproducible as written.

Reproducibility: Insufficient. No experiments; several parameters depend on unknown quantities; some steps are underspecified.

Recommendation: Reject.

Confidence: High.
