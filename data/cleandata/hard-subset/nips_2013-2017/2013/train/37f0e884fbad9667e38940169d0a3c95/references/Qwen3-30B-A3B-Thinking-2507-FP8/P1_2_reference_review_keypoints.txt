- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that mixes full and stochastic gradients to reduce full-gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)), independent of condition number κ.  
- EMGD achieves ε-optimality with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.  
- Theoretical contribution: rigorous proof that full-gradient access can be made κ-independent while retaining linear convergence.  
- Clear problem formulation: targets condition-number dependence in full-gradient methods and reducing full-gradient access.  
- Comprehensive related work situating EMGD among Nesterov, SAG, SDCA, and other methods.  
- Detailed, well-structured convergence analysis with clear assumptions and conditions.  
- Practical insight: method is advantageous in regimes like κ ≤ n^{2/3} and notes need to know κ in advance.  
- Major weakness: no experimental results to validate theoretical claims.  
- Practical limitation: algorithm requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ.  
- High stochastic-gradient cost O(κ² log(1/ε)) may negate benefits for large κ.  
- Comparison with SAG/SDCA is superficial and lacks runtime or scalability quantification.  
- Limited discussion and examples of real-world problems where κ ≤ n^{2/3}.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound and rigorous but empirically unvalidated.  
- Clarity assessment: generally well-written and organized; some terms could be briefly defined for broader readership.  
- Originality assessment: novel specific algorithm and analysis despite related semi-stochastic literature.  
- Significance assessment: theoretically important, but practical impact limited by κ knowledge and high stochastic cost.  
- Pro arguments: novel theoretical result, rigorous analysis, clear trade-offs, and thorough comparisons.  
- Con arguments: missing experiments, κ estimation requirement, high stochastic complexity, and unclear practical use cases.  
- Recommended revisions: add experiments comparing EMGD to Nesterov/SAG/SDCA; include κ estimation guidance; provide runtime trade-off analysis; identify domains where κ ≤ n^{2/3}.  
- Overall recommendation: weak reject (Final score: 4) due to lack of empirical validation and practical limitations.
