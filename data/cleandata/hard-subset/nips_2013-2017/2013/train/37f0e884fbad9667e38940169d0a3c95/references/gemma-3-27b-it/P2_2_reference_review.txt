## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm designed to achieve linear convergence for smooth, strongly convex problems while minimizing the number of expensive full gradient evaluations. The key idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradients within epochs, leveraging the benefits of both. The authors claim to reduce the number of full gradient accesses from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), independent of the condition number κ.  They provide theoretical analysis supporting their claims and compare their method to existing approaches like SAG and SDCA.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Reducing the computational cost of full gradient evaluations, particularly in large-scale problems with ill-conditioning, is a significant challenge.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of epoch-based optimization with a mixed gradient descent strategy is a reasonable and potentially effective approach to tackle the problem.
* **Strong theoretical guarantees:** The paper provides a formal convergence analysis demonstrating linear convergence and explicitly quantifying the number of full and stochastic gradient evaluations.
* **Comparison to existing work:** The authors thoughtfully position their method relative to existing algorithms (Nesterov’s, SAG, SDCA) in terms of computational complexity and highlight potential advantages, such as broader applicability and stronger convergence guarantees.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the core ideas and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations & Hidden Constants:** While the theoretical results are promising, the practical implications aren’t fully explored. The constants hidden within the O-notation (e.g., in the number of iterations *T* per epoch) could significantly impact performance. The dependence on knowing or estimating κ beforehand is a practical limitation, and the proposed solutions in the conclusion feel somewhat underdeveloped.
* **Complexity of Implementation:**  The algorithm, while conceptually clear, may be more complex to implement than simpler alternatives like stochastic gradient descent.  The overhead of managing epochs and the mixed gradient calculation could be non-trivial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper lacks any empirical evaluation. While theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the practical benefits of EMGD on real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the paper. A comparison to state-of-the-art solvers on standard benchmark problems would be highly valuable.
* **Relationship to Existing Epoch-Based Methods:** While the authors acknowledge epoch gradient descent, the connection to and differentiation from other epoch-based methods (beyond simple mention) could be further clarified.
* **Assumptions on Oracles:** The assumption of access to both a gradient oracle and a function oracle might not always be realistic in all applications.  



**Evaluation based on NIPS criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The claims are well-supported within the scope of the assumptions made. However, the lack of empirical validation weakens the overall assessment of quality.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner.
* **Originality:** The combination of epoch-based optimization and a mixed gradient approach appears to be a novel contribution. However, the originality could be further highlighted by more clearly differentiating the method from related epoch-based techniques.
* **Significance:**  If the theoretical guarantees translate to practical benefits, the proposed algorithm could be significant. Reducing the number of full gradient evaluations has the potential to accelerate optimization in large-scale problems. However, without empirical validation, the actual significance remains uncertain.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem in optimization.
* Presents a novel algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
* Provides a thorough analysis and comparison to existing methods.
*  Clear and well-written presentation.

**Con:**

* Lack of empirical evaluation.
* Practical implementation concerns (hidden constants, κ estimation).
* Limited discussion of limitations and potential drawbacks.
* The connection to existing work and improvements could be more thoroughly discussed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical results are solid, and the approach is promising. However, the *lack of empirical validation* is a significant concern.  While the authors have done a good job of theoretically justifying their method, the absence of experimental results makes it difficult to assess its practical impact.

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.** I recommend acceptance with the strong caveat that the authors include substantial experimental results in a revised version. This should include comparisons to state-of-the-art solvers on standard benchmark datasets, as well as an investigation of the sensitivity of the algorithm to the hidden constants and the accuracy of κ estimation. Without empirical validation, the paper remains primarily a theoretical contribution.  Addressing the limitations outlined in the review would greatly strengthen the manuscript and increase its impact.
