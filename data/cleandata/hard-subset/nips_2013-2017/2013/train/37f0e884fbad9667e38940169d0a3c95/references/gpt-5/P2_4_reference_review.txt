Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization with access to two oracles: a full gradient oracle Og and a “function” oracle Of that induces unbiased stochastic gradients. The goal is to reduce the number of full-gradient evaluations so it does not scale with the condition number κ. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes a full gradient at an anchor point w̄k. Within the epoch, it performs projected updates using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), i.e., a control variate anchored at w̄k, while constraining iterates to a shrinking ball around w̄k. The analysis shows a linear convergence rate: to obtain ε-suboptimality, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients with high probability.

This estimator and epoch structure are closely related to variance-reduced stochastic methods developed around the same period, notably SAG/SDCA (which the paper cites) and especially SVRG/SAGA (not cited). The mixed gradient equals the classic SVRG control-variate estimator anchored at a snapshot point where a full gradient is computed. Differences include the use of explicit shrinking-radius projections and a proof technique that bounds the stochastic part by smoothness and the radius.

Quality (technical soundness and support):
- The high-level idea is sound: the mixed gradient is unbiased for ∇F(wt), and the variance is controlled by the local distance to the anchor. The proof uses a standard combination of smoothness, strong convexity, martingale concentration (Azuma), and an epoch-wise contraction. The lower bound ||∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)|| ≥ λ||w − w′|| follows from strong convexity via monotonicity and Cauchy–Schwarz; this use appears correct.
- The main quantitative bound requires T = Ω((L/λ)^2 log(1/δ)) stochastic steps per epoch, leading to O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients overall. This is substantially worse than known variance-reduction bounds (e.g., O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) for finite-sum; O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic steps per full gradient in SVRG-style analyses in oracle models). The contraction argument via shrinking balls is conservative and seems to be the source of the κ^2. It’s unclear whether tighter analysis could bring this to O(κ).
- The paper lacks any empirical evaluation; for NeurIPS this is a significant omission, especially given practical claims (memory advantage, handling constraints).
- Some implementation details are glossed over: the cost of solving the projected subproblem over W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk), how Δ1 is chosen in practice, and the reliance on knowing κ (acknowledged as a drawback).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and the main idea is explained. However, there are many stylistic and grammatical issues, typos (“epoches”), and some equations are hard to follow due to formatting. Algorithm 1’s projection step and its computational implications should be discussed more concretely.
- The comparison section mixes oracle and finite-sum models; the cost model assumptions (full gradient n times more expensive) should be clearly separated from the abstract oracle model.
- Missing citations to highly related work (SVRG, SAGA) make it harder for readers to position the contribution.

Originality:
- The mixed gradient estimator and epoch anchoring are essentially identical to SVRG’s snapshot/full-gradient plus control variate. The novel aspect here is the shrinking-ball projection proof technique to get high-probability linear convergence with condition-number-independent full-gradient count in a general oracle model. However, the omission of SVRG/SAGA makes it appear less novel than it is; as written, the contribution is incremental relative to the variance reduction literature.
- The claim that EMGD attains linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients is not unique; SVRG-style methods also use one full gradient per epoch, i.e., O(log(1/ε)), and achieve better dependence on κ in stochastic steps.

Significance:
- The idea of reducing full-gradient calls is important in large-scale settings, and handling constraints and low memory are practical advantages. The high-probability guarantee is also appealing.
- However, the κ^2 dependence in stochastic gradient complexity, the need for projections onto shrinking balls, and lack of experiments limit practical impact. Compared to state-of-the-art variance reduction (SAG/SDCA/SVRG/SAGA), the theoretical and empirical advantages are not convincingly established.

Pros:
- Clear goal: condition-number-independent number of full gradients.
- High-probability linear convergence under standard smooth/strongly convex assumptions.
- Works with constraints and uses only O(d) memory.
- Simple algorithmic structure.

Cons:
- Missing key related work (SVRG/SAGA) and insufficiently differentiated contribution; the estimator is identical to SVRG’s.
- Worse dependence on κ (κ^2) than existing methods; no attempt to tighten analysis.
- No experiments to substantiate computational claims or compare to SAG/SDCA/SVRG/SAGA.
- Practical requirements: projections onto shrinking balls, knowledge of κ, choice of Δ1.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add and discuss SVRG/SAGA and clarify how EMGD differs; if the main novelty is the shrinking-ball analysis and high-probability bound, say so explicitly and attempt to improve the κ dependence.
- Provide experiments on standard datasets comparing wall-clock and oracle calls vs SAG/SDCA/SVRG/SAGA/accelerated GD, including constrained problems to showcase EMGD’s niche.
- Discuss computational cost of the projection step and provide alternatives for unconstrained settings.
- Remove or mitigate the need to know κ (adaptive step sizes/epoch lengths).
- Clean up writing, notation, and formatting; detail how Δ1 is chosen.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper contains a sensible algorithm with a correct analysis and an appealing high-probability guarantee, but the contribution is overshadowed by closely related variance-reduction methods with stronger complexity, key related work is missing, and there is no empirical validation. With a tighter analysis, proper positioning relative to SVRG/SAGA, and experiments, this could become a stronger submission. Confidence: high.
