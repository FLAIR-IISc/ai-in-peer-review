**Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of minimizing smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental task in machine learning. The authors identify the high cost of full gradient evaluations in classical methods like accelerated gradient descent (AGD), whose complexity scales with the square root of the condition number, O(√κ). To mitigate this, they propose a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both full and stochastic gradient oracles. The algorithm operates in epochs, where each epoch consists of one full gradient evaluation followed by O(κ²) stochastic gradient steps using a variance-reduced-style "mixed gradient". The main theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate, requiring only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, thus making this component's complexity independent of the condition number κ.

**Strengths**
1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The goal of reducing the number of expensive full gradient computations, particularly for ill-conditioned problems (large κ), is of significant practical importance in large-scale machine learning. Making this number independent of κ is an appealing theoretical target.
2.  **Sound Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous and detailed proof of a high-probability linear convergence rate for the proposed algorithm. This type of high-probability bound is stronger than the in-expectation guarantees common to many related stochastic algorithms (e.g., SAG, SVRG).
3.  **Identified Niches:** The authors clearly discuss potential advantages of their method over established competitors like SAG and SDCA, such as its applicability to constrained optimization problems, its low memory footprint (O(d)), and its high-probability convergence guarantee.

**Weaknesses**
Despite its theoretical merits, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses that call its contribution and practical utility into question.

1.  **Unfavorable Computational Complexity vs. Established Methods:** The core claim of the paper is the reduction in full gradient calls. However, this comes at the cost of requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. The paper's own comparison in Section 3.4 reveals that the total computational cost of EMGD (assuming a full gradient costs 'n' stochastic gradients) is O((n + κ²) log(1/ε)). This complexity compares unfavorably with state-of-the-art methods like SAG and SDCA. For instance, SDCA has a complexity of O((n + κ) log(1/ε)). EMGD is only superior to SDCA if κ² < κ, which is impossible since κ ≥ 1. It is more expensive than SDCA for all κ > 1. Similarly, SAG has a complexity of O(n log(1/ε)) (when n ≥ 8κ). EMGD is only competitive when κ² is small relative to n. The paper claims EMGD is better than Nesterov's AGD when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but established methods like SDCA are better than AGD in a much broader regime (κ ≤ n²). Therefore, the proposed algorithm appears to be less efficient than existing, widely adopted methods in most practical settings.

2.  **Complete Lack of Empirical Validation:** This is a major flaw. The paper is entirely theoretical and presents no experimental results. Claims of computational efficiency must be substantiated with empirical evidence. Without experiments comparing EMGD to relevant baselines like AGD, SAG, and SDCA on either synthetic or real-world datasets, it is impossible to assess the practical performance of the algorithm. It remains entirely speculative whether the claimed benefits would materialize in practice or be overshadowed by large constant factors or other overheads. For a field as mature as first-order convex optimization, a new algorithm that claims improvements in computational cost must be rigorously benchmarked against established methods.

3.  **Strong and Impractical Assumptions:** The algorithm's design and analysis rely on knowing the condition number κ (or the smoothness L and strong convexity λ parameters) beforehand. This information is required to set the number of inner iterations T = O(κ² ln(1/δ)). While many optimization algorithms require knowledge of such parameters, the quadratic dependence on κ makes the algorithm's performance highly sensitive to this setting. An inaccurate estimate of κ could lead to a drastically incorrect value for T, potentially invalidating the convergence guarantee. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation and propose it as future work, but for a submission to a top-tier conference, this is a significant practical hurdle that undermines the completeness of the work.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**
This paper presents a theoretically interesting algorithm that achieves a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradient calls that is independent of the condition number. However, the work is not yet ready for publication at NIPS. The primary concerns are the algorithm's questionable practical utility due to its high stochastic gradient complexity (O(κ²)) compared to well-established methods like SAG and SDCA, and the complete absence of empirical results to validate its performance. The strong reliance on a priori knowledge of κ further limits its applicability. While the theoretical idea is novel, the paper fails to demonstrate that it constitutes a tangible advancement over the state of the art in practice.

I recommend **rejection** for this paper in its current form. The authors should conduct a thorough experimental evaluation against strong, relevant baselines and address the practical issue of requiring knowledge of κ before the work can be considered a significant contribution.
