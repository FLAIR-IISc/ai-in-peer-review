### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex functions, a setting where the iteration complexity of optimal methods typically depends on the condition number κ. The authors aim to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations to be independent of κ. They propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which combines full and stochastic gradients. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes one full gradient. Within the epoch, it performs a series of updates using a variance-reduced stochastic gradient estimator, constrained to a shrinking Euclidean ball. The main theoretical result is that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. The authors argue that this approach is advantageous for constrained optimization, has low storage requirements, is amenable to parallelization, and provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation guarantees of some related methods like SAG and SDCA.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The theoretical analysis appears to be largely sound, following an inductive argument over epochs and leveraging a martingale concentration inequality to bound the variance of the stochastic terms. The high-probability convergence guarantee is a definite strength over the more common in-expectation results.

However, there are several weaknesses regarding the technical soundness and completeness:

1.  The dependence on the condition number has been shifted from the number of full gradients to the number of stochastic gradients per epoch, which is O(κ²). This dependence is severe and potentially prohibitive for even moderately ill-conditioned problems. The complexity comparison in Table 2 shows that the proposed method is only competitive in a narrow regime where κ is small relative to n (κ ≤ n¹/²).
2.  The update step in Algorithm 1 (line 8) requires solving `argmin w∈W∩B(w̄k;∆k) ...`. Projecting onto the intersection of a general convex set `W` and a ball `B` often does not have a closed-form solution and can be as difficult as the original problem, severely limiting the algorithm's practical applicability. The paper dismisses this with a brief clause "provided that the constrained problem... can be solved efficiently," without discussing when this condition holds.
3.  The parameter `T` (iterations per epoch) requires `T ≥ 1152 (L/λ)² ln(1/δ)`. The constant 1152 is exceptionally large, suggesting that the analysis may be loose and that a very large number of stochastic gradients would be needed in practice to satisfy the theoretical conditions.
4.  The initialization of the domain size `∆1` depends on `F(0) - F(w*)`, which assumes knowledge of the optimal function value, a common but impractical assumption that is not acknowledged as a limitation.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally readable, but suffers from a significant number of minor yet distracting flaws that undermine its professionalism and clarity.

1.  **Inconsistent Notation:** The notation for iterates is inconsistent. The text states "we use the superscript for the index of epoches, and the subscript for the index of iterations," which would imply `w_t^k`. However, Algorithm 1 and the surrounding text use `w̄k`, `wkt`, and `w̄k+1`, which is confusing. Consistency is crucial for a technical paper.
2.  **Formatting Errors:** There is a duplicated equation number: both the smoothness definition and a subsequent inequality are labeled as (2). This cascades, making following the derivation in Section 4 difficult. Several references in the bibliography are formatted incorrectly (e.g., Agarwal et al. has stray commas, Bertsekas is missing a year, Boyd is missing a publisher), indicating a lack of care in preparation.
3.  **Vague Statements:** Assertions such as "as long as τ > Ω(n−2/3), which is a reasonable scenario" (footnote 2) and the hand-wavy argument about parallelization for SAG/SDCA being "unclear" are not sufficiently substantiated.

**Originality:**

The goal of making full gradient evaluations independent of κ is interesting. However, the core technical components are not as novel as presented.

1.  **Major Omission of Related Work:** The "mixed gradient" `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) +∇fkt(wkt)−∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the gradient estimator used in the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). SVRG is a highly influential, directly related algorithm that also uses an epoch-based structure with one full gradient and subsequent variance-reduced stochastic steps to achieve linear convergence. The failure to cite and compare against SVRG is a critical omission. This significantly reduces the perceived novelty of the proposed gradient update scheme.
2.  The main novelty lies in combining this SVRG-style update with a shrinking search domain to achieve the κ-independent full gradient complexity. While this combination is new, its practical benefits are questionable given the aforementioned issues with the projection step and the O(κ²) stochastic gradient complexity.

**Significance:**

The paper addresses an important problem: reducing the cost of optimization for ill-conditioned functions. The claimed advantages—handling constraints, high-probability bounds, low storage—are valuable.

However, the significance of the contribution is severely limited by its drawbacks. The narrow regime where EMGD is theoretically superior to existing methods (like SAG, SDCA, and the unmentioned SVRG) makes it unlikely to be widely adopted. The practical hurdles (projection, parameter tuning) further detract from its potential impact. While the idea of trading off full gradients for stochastic ones in this specific manner is intriguing, the current algorithm and analysis do not present a compelling case for its general use over state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
*   The primary goal of achieving a full-gradient complexity independent of the condition number κ is novel and theoretically interesting.
*   The algorithm provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is a stronger result than the in-expectation guarantees of some competing methods.
*   The framework naturally handles constrained optimization, an advantage over methods like SAG and SDCA which are typically presented for unconstrained problems.

**Against Acceptance:**
*   **Critical Omission:** The paper fails to cite or compare with SVRG, whose gradient estimator is identical to the one proposed. This is a major gap in the discussion of related work and overstates the method's novelty.
*   **Impracticality:** The algorithm relies on an often-intractable projection step and requires knowledge of problem-dependent parameters (κ for setting T, F(w*) for setting ∆1) that are unavailable in practice.
*   **Poor Complexity Trade-off:** The O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients per epoch is extremely high and makes the algorithm uncompetitive against methods like SVRG (`O(κ)`) for most ill-conditioned problems.
*   **Lack of Polish:** The manuscript is marred by inconsistent notation, duplicated equation numbers, and poorly formatted references, indicating a lack of thoroughness.

### Recommendation

I recommend **rejection**.

While the paper is founded on an interesting premise, the execution is flawed. The critical omission of SVRG, the severe O(κ²) dependence on the condition number, the impracticality of the projection step, and the numerous minor errors in presentation collectively outweigh the novelty of the theoretical result. The work is not yet in a state suitable for publication at NIPS. The authors should, at a minimum, thoroughly revise the paper to address the comparison with SVRG, justify the practicality of the algorithm, and correct the notational and formatting inconsistencies.
