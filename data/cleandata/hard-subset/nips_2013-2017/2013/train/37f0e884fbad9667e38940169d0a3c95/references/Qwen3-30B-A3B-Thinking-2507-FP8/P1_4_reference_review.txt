

# NIPS Peer Review

## Summary

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel first-order optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that achieves linear convergence with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ. The key insight is that by combining both full and stochastic gradients in a carefully designed epoch-based algorithm, the authors reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), while requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution to the optimization literature, positioning itself as a bridge between full-gradient methods (which require O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations) and stochastic methods (which typically have slower convergence rates).

The work builds on epoch gradient descent [9] and relates to recent advances in stochastic optimization like SAG [22] and SDCA [23], but distinguishes itself by achieving linear convergence with a condition number-independent number of full gradient evaluations. The authors carefully analyze the algorithm's convergence properties and compare it with existing methods, particularly for the case where the objective function is a sum of n smooth functions (F(w) = (1/n)∑ᵢ=1ⁿ fᵢ(w)).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a new algorithm with linear convergence rate that requires only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, a substantial improvement over the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) rate. This addresses a fundamental limitation in optimization for ill-conditioned problems.

2. **Clear Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides a detailed and rigorous convergence analysis with a well-structured proof. The induction-based argument for Theorem 1 is clear and follows standard techniques in optimization theory, making it accessible to the optimization community.

3. **Comprehensive Comparisons**: The paper effectively compares EMGD with existing methods (Nesterov's accelerated gradient, SAG, SDCA) and demonstrates that EMGD can be more computationally efficient when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is a reasonable condition in many machine learning applications.

4. **Practical Considerations**: The authors thoughtfully address practical aspects, including the storage requirements (Ω(d) vs. Ω(n) for SAG/SDCA), applicability to constrained optimization problems, and the potential for distributed implementation to reduce the cost of full gradient computation.

5. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, logically structured, and the algorithm is clearly presented with pseudocode. The theoretical results are properly contextualized within the existing literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Experimental Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no experimental results to demonstrate the practical performance of EMGD. While theoretical contributions are valuable, NIPS increasingly values empirical validation of theoretical claims. Without experiments, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical improvement.

2. **Assumption of Known Condition Number**: The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ to set parameters like the number of iterations per epoch (T). The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but provides only vague suggestions for estimating κ in practice. This is a significant practical drawback, as κ is often unknown in real-world applications.

3. **High Stochastic Gradient Complexity**: The algorithm requires O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, which might be prohibitively expensive for large κ. The paper doesn't sufficiently address when this trade-off is beneficial compared to using standard methods.

4. **Limited Practical Discussion**: While the paper compares computational complexity, it doesn't clearly articulate when EMGD would be preferable in practice. For example, it doesn't address how the cost of computing stochastic gradients compares to the cost of computing full gradients in various settings.

5. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent as the standard for full gradient methods, but doesn't engage with more recent advances in accelerated methods that might have different complexity characteristics.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to convex optimization by showing how to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations for smooth strongly convex problems from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)). The proof is sound, the analysis is thorough, and the comparison with related work is appropriate. However, the lack of experimental validation and the assumption of known condition number represent significant limitations for practical applicability.

The contribution is novel and well-motivated, but the paper would benefit from addressing these practical limitations. The theoretical result is important for the optimization community, but the absence of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical impact.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The theoretical contribution is strong and represents a meaningful advancement in the field of optimization. To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Add experimental results demonstrating the practical performance of EMGD compared to baseline methods, particularly focusing on how the algorithm behaves as the condition number κ varies.

2. Address the issue of unknown condition number by providing a more concrete method for estimating κ in practice, or discussing the impact of estimation errors on the algorithm's performance.

3. More clearly articulate the practical scenarios where EMGD would be preferred over existing methods, including a discussion of the trade-offs between full gradient and stochastic gradient computation costs.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of how to combine full and stochastic gradients effectively. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.

## Pro/Con List

**Pro:**
- Theoretical contribution is significant and well-supported
- Clear, rigorous analysis of the convergence properties
- Comprehensive comparison with relevant existing work
- Well-written and well-organized
- Addresses important practical considerations (storage, constrained optimization)

**Con:**
- No experimental validation of theoretical claims
- Requires knowledge of condition number κ (often unknown in practice)
- High stochastic gradient complexity (O(κ² log(1/ε)))
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations
- Does not provide concrete guidance for estimating κ

This paper represents a strong theoretical contribution that would be valuable to the optimization community. The minor issues identified are primarily practical implementation concerns that could be addressed with a few additional experiments and clarifications.
