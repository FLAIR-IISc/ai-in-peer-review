Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization and proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an epoch-based method that combines one full gradient per epoch with many stochastic gradients. Within each epoch, the algorithm uses a “mixed” gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k) and performs projected gradient steps over a shrinking Euclidean ball centered at the epoch anchor w̄k. The analysis shows a high-probability linear rate: after m epochs, EMGD achieves F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ O(2−m), using O(m) full gradients and O(m κ^2) stochastic gradients; thus to reach ε accuracy, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The stated motivation is to make the number of full-gradient evaluations independent of κ.

The paper positions itself against: (i) deterministic accelerated methods (O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations with full gradients), (ii) stochastic methods with polynomial dependence in 1/ε, and (iii) finite-sum methods with linear convergence such as SAG and SDCA. The algorithm and its estimator g̃ are essentially a control-variate gradient estimator centered at the anchor point, closely related to variance-reduced methods.

Strengths:
- Clear and clean analysis yielding high-probability linear convergence under smoothness and strong convexity.
- The focus on “condition-number-independent” access to full gradients (O(log(1/ε)) calls) is appealing in large-scale regimes where full gradients are costly.
- Works in constrained domains (via projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk)), uses only O(d) memory, and provides a high-probability result rather than just in expectation.
- The presentation of the epoch-based scheme and the proof outline (using martingale bounds and domain shrinkage) is pedagogical.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Novelty is limited relative to known variance-reduced stochastic gradient methods. The “mixed gradient” g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is exactly the control-variate estimator of SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which also uses one full gradient per epoch and achieves linear convergence. The paper does not cite SVRG, and the proposed scheme and analysis are very close in spirit. Moreover, SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014) and related VR methods also provide linear rates with low memory or proximal/constrained extensions. The omission of SVRG is a serious gap in related work.
- Complexity is weaker than the state of the art: EMGD’s stochastic gradient complexity scales as O(κ^2 log(1/ε)), while SVRG/SAGA/SDCA achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) (finite-sum) and O(κ log(1/ε)) dependence on κ in analogous oracle settings. The κ^2 factor arises from the ball-shrinkage variance control; modern analyses control variance without the extra κ. As a result, EMGD is dominated in regimes where κ is large.
- The method requires knowledge of κ (to set T and Δ schedule) and performs projections onto shrinking balls. This adds tuning and computational overhead; modern VR methods avoid such shrinking domains and do not require κ a priori (or use adaptive strategies).
- The claim of “condition-number independent access to full gradients” is not unique: VR methods also use a constant number (O(log(1/ε))) of full gradients (or “full passes”) independent of κ. In finite-sum settings, each full gradient already costs n stochastic gradients; EMGD does not reduce this cost relative to SVRG.
- No empirical results are provided to support practical benefits (e.g., constrained problems, memory footprint, or high-probability behavior) or to validate constants. This makes it difficult to assess significance in practice.

Quality:
- The main theorem is technically sound in structure, but there are minor issues/typos: the condition in (5) appears to use ln δ (negative) whereas later the proof uses ln(1/δ); please fix. Assumption (4) is stated as a bound on gradient differences; this follows from strong convexity via strong monotonicity but should be stated precisely to avoid confusion. The projection update in Algorithm 1 could be clarified (the proximal subproblem appears to be η⟨·, g̃⟩ + (1/2)‖·‖^2).
- The proof technique is standard (martingale difference + Hoeffding-Azuma + smoothness/strong convexity), and the high-probability bound is appreciated.

Clarity:
- Generally clear and well organized; the epoch/anchor framework is explained. A few notational slips (see above) and missing references reduce clarity of positioning.
- The discussion comparing to SAG/SDCA is fair but incomplete without SVRG (and later SAGA/Prox-SVRG), especially since your estimator is exactly SVRG’s.

Originality:
- The core idea (control-variate/mixed gradient) and epoch structure align closely with SVRG. The added shrinking-ball mechanism and high-probability statement are incremental. Given existing VR literature, the contribution does not constitute a significant new concept.

Significance:
- In its current form, the theoretical bounds are weaker (κ^2), and practical implications are unsubstantiated by experiments. The constrained-setting compatibility and high-probability result could be useful, but these can be attained by existing VR frameworks (e.g., Prox-SVRG) with better complexity.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Situate the work with SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013) and SAGA, discussing similarities/differences. If there is a substantive distinction (e.g., general oracle beyond finite-sum, constrained projections with guarantees, high-probability constants), make it explicit.
- Tighten the analysis to remove the κ^2 factor; variance-reduction analyses bound E‖∇f(w) − ∇f(w*) − (∇F(w) − ∇F(w*))‖^2 to get O(κ) dependence without shrinking balls.
- Remove the need for prior κ via doubling/epoch-length adaptation or line-search, and consider proximal/composite variants.
- Add empirical evaluation on finite-sum and constrained problems to validate runtime and memory claims versus SVRG/SAGA/SDCA/accelerated methods.
- Fix typos and clarify assumptions (δ, strong convexity statements, projection subproblem).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clean, high-probability linear-rate analysis; constrained setting support; memory efficiency.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overlaps strongly with SVRG; weaker complexity (κ^2); missing key related work; no experiments; requires κ and shrinking-ball projections.

Recommendation: Reject. The paper’s central idea is not sufficiently novel relative to established variance-reduced methods, and the theoretical guarantees are weaker than the state of the art. With tighter analysis, proper positioning, and empirical support, a future version could be competitive.
