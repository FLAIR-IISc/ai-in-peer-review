Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization with access to two oracles: a full-gradient oracle ∇F(w) and a “function” oracle that yields random smooth functions f with F(w)=E[f(w)], enabling stochastic gradients. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) runs in epochs; at the start of each epoch it computes a full gradient at a “snapshot” point w̄k, and then performs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k). Iterates are projected onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) and Δk is halved (up to constants) each epoch. With η = 1/(L√T) and T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)), the analysis shows a constant error reduction per epoch, yielding linear convergence: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, with high probability.

This “mixed gradient” is the standard variance-reduction control variate used in SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013): compute a full gradient at a snapshot, then use ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k) + ∇F(w̄k) as an unbiased, lower-variance estimate of ∇F(wt). Related finite-sum methods with linear convergence include SAG (NIPS 2012), SAGA (NIPS 2014), SDCA (JMLR 2013; NIPS variants), and subsequent accelerated VR methods. The paper also borrows the epoch/shrinking-trust-region idea from epoch-SGD.

Strengths:
- Clear high-probability linear convergence theorem under smoothness and strong convexity, with a detailed (if somewhat technical) proof using martingale concentration.
- Conceptually simple algorithm that uses only O(d) memory and (in principle) accommodates constraints via projections.
- The goal of making the number of full-gradient computations independent of κ is useful, particularly when full-gradient evaluation is comparatively costly.
- The discussion of computational trade-offs in the finite-sum case is appreciated.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty: The core update g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k) is exactly the SVRG control variate; the paper neither cites nor distinguishes itself from SVRG. EMGD’s additional shrinking-ball projection is a known device from epoch methods and appears auxiliary rather than fundamental to variance reduction.
- Suboptimal complexity: The method requires T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)) stochastic gradients per epoch, leading to O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations overall. State-of-the-art variance-reduction methods (SVRG/SAGA/SDCA and successors) achieve O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) or O((n + √(nκ)) log(1/ε)) in various regimes, i.e., κ rather than κ^2 dependence, and without shrinking domains. Even in the pure expectation setting (beyond finite-sum), more recent VR analyses obtain κ (not κ^2) dependence under similar assumptions.
- No experiments: There is no empirical validation against established baselines (SAG, SAGA, SVRG, SDCA, accelerated full-gradient methods). Given the proposed method’s additional projection onto a shrinking ball (which can be nontrivial computationally), and its κ^2 dependence, empirical evidence is crucial to support practical benefits.
- Assumptions and practicality: The method requires prior knowledge of κ and an initial radius Δ1 satisfying a nontrivial bound. The algorithm also assumes we can efficiently project onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) at every inner step; this is restrictive beyond simple constraints. The claimed advantage in distributed computation is only sketched.
- Clarity and presentation issues: There are several typos, duplicate equation numbers, and nonstandard definitions. In particular, Eq. (4) presents “strong convexity” as a lower Lipschitz bound on the gradient norm; the standard condition is gradient strong monotonicity 〈∇F(w)−∇F(w′), w−w′〉 ≥ λ‖w−w′‖^2. Some footnotes and comparisons (e.g., about storage, constrained problems, and “high probability” vs “in expectation”) read as overstatements or are insufficiently justified.

Quality:
The theoretical argument is mostly careful and complete, but it leans on conservative bounds that induce the κ^2 factor, and it hinges on the shrinking-ball mechanism. Important related work (SVRG/SAGA and subsequent variants) is missing, and no experiments are provided. Several claims in the comparisons section are either incomplete or potentially misleading without broader context.

Clarity:
The paper is readable but needs cleanup (notation, equation numbering, standard definitions), clearer positioning with respect to variance-reduction literature, and a more precise cost model. The constrained projection step should be discussed in depth.

Originality:
Limited. The main update is known (SVRG), and the epoch/shrinking approach is established. The specific combination yielding a high-probability result with κ^2 stochastic-gradient complexity is incremental at best.

Significance:
Given the weaker κ^2 dependence, lack of empirical support, and missing comparison to SVRG/SAGA, the impact appears limited. The claim about condition-number-independent access to full gradients is already achieved by SVRG in the finite-sum setting, with better overall complexity and without shrinkage.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Situate EMGD explicitly relative to SVRG, SAGA, SDCA, and newer VR methods; clarify what is genuinely new.
- Tighten the analysis to obtain κ (not κ^2) stochastic-gradient complexity, ideally removing the shrinking ball.
- Provide empirical comparisons on standard strongly convex tasks (ridge/logistic regression) under both constrained and unconstrained settings; include wall-clock and pass-based metrics.
- Address unknown κ via adaptive/doubling schemes; justify or eliminate the need for Δk.
- Clarify the computational cost of the projection step and when it is efficiently implementable.

Pros:
- Clear, self-contained high-probability analysis.
- Memory-light and, in principle, extends to constraints.

Cons:
- Core idea overlaps with SVRG; missing citations and positioning.
- Suboptimal κ^2 dependence; no empirical validation.
- Practical requirements (κ known, projections onto shrinking balls) and presentation issues.

Recommendation: Reject.
