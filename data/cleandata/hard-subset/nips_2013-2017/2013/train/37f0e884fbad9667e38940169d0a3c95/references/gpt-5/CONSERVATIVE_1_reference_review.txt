Summary
This paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization when both full gradients and stochastic gradients are available. The main contribution is Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which runs in epochs; each epoch computes one full gradient at a reference point w̄, then performs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w_t) − ∇f(w̄), with projections onto W ∩ B(w̄; Δ). The radius Δ shrinks by √2 each epoch, and the final output is an average over the epoch iterates. The analysis shows a linear convergence rate with high probability: EMGD attains ε-suboptimality using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (independent of κ) and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, with explicit constants (T ≳ 1152 κ^2 ln(1/δ)).

Relation to prior work
The algorithmic idea of variance reduction via a control variate ∇f(w_t) − ∇f(w̄) + ∇F(w̄) is well known in the finite-sum literature (SAG, SDCA). Crucially, the proposed “mixed gradient” is exactly the SVRG estimator (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which alternates occasional full gradients with many cheap stochastic gradients and achieves linear convergence. More recent refinements (SAGA, Katyusha, and others) sharpen dependencies to O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) in the finite-sum setting and often give high-probability bounds. The paper cites SAG and SDCA but omits SVRG and follow-ons, and does not empirically or theoretically compare to those, despite the close connection. The constrained setting and high-probability proof are welcome, but the κ^2 dependence is substantially worse than the O(κ) (or n+κ) that variance-reduced methods achieve in finite-sum problems.

Strengths
- Clear target: reduce the number of costly full gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ.
- Simple algorithm; storage O(d); no need to maintain per-component gradients (unlike SAG).
- High-probability linear convergence analysis with explicit constants, and a clean martingale argument.
- Handles constraints via projections (if Proj(W ∩ ball) is efficient).
- Transparent tradeoff discussion between full and stochastic gradient costs.

Weaknesses
- Novelty/significance: The mixed gradient estimator is the SVRG estimator; the paper neither cites nor compares to SVRG/SAGA and later variance reduction methods. These deliver O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) gradient evaluations and also use O(log(1/ε)) full gradient computations, making EMGD’s O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient complexity uncompetitive when κ is large.
- No experiments. Given the number of related, mature methods with strong empirical track records, a new algorithm should include experimental validation, especially to justify claims about practical advantages (constraints, memory, distributed full gradients).
- Strong parameter knowledge: The analysis requires knowing L, λ (to set η and T), and an initial radius Δ1 that upper-bounds the distance to w*. The latter depends on F(w*) and is typically unknown; the paper does not provide an adaptive strategy (e.g., doubling) to remove this requirement. The conclusion mentions needing κ, but not Δ1, which is equally problematic.
- Conservative complexity: The required inner-epoch length T scales as κ^2, which is much worse than the O(κ) scaling known for SVRG-style methods. The paper does not justify why κ^2 is necessary in this model or provide lower bounds that would explain the gap.
- Comparisons are incomplete or overstated: 
  - “High-probability vs expectation” advantage over SAG/SDCA is less compelling today; high-probability guarantees exist for several variance-reduced methods.
  - The claim that SAG/SDCA do not apply to constraints is too strong; projected variants exist (though analysis can be trickier).
  - Distributed claim for full gradients is reasonable, but the same can benefit SVRG/SAGA epochs; the paper does not discuss communication or synchronization costs.
- Clarity/Presentation: Some typos and awkward phrasing (“epoches”, “interstage”), and a few abrupt insertions (e.g., SDCA rewrite footnote). The related work section misses highly relevant developments.

Quality
The core analysis appears technically sound given the stated assumptions. The high-probability bound is correctly derived via Azuma with bounded differences ensured by the shrinking ball. However, the practical requirements (λ, L, Δ1) are nontrivial, and the κ^2 dependence looks suboptimal relative to the state of the art. Missing references and lack of experiments limit confidence in the practical value.

Clarity
Generally readable, with a clear algorithm description and theorem. Proof outline is detailed. Nonetheless, the manuscript would benefit from proofreading, a more careful related work section, and a discussion on the feasibility and cost of the projection onto W ∩ B(w̄; Δ) in typical ML tasks.

Originality
Limited. The estimator and epoch structure match SVRG; the shrinking-ball device per epoch is a known technique for controlling variance/noise. The work’s main distinctives are the specific high-probability analysis and the focus on counting full gradients independent of κ, but these do not appear to surpass existing methods.

Significance
Moderate to low in current form. The κ^2 dependence undermines the main practical benefit, and the absence of empirical evidence makes it hard to recommend adoption over established variance-reduced methods.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please compare theoretically and empirically to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), SAGA, and related VR methods. Can your analysis be tightened to O(κ) instead of O(κ^2)?
- Can you remove the need to know λ, L, and Δ1 via adaptive step-sizes and a doubling trick on the radius?
- Discuss the cost of projections onto W ∩ B(·). In what common constrained problems is this cheap?
- Provide experiments on standard ERM tasks (logistic/least squares), with constrained and unconstrained cases, benchmarking wall-clock and epochs vs strong baselines.
- Clarify the distributed computing claims and whether they also apply to SVRG/SAGA epochs.

Pros
- Simple method; storage-efficient; high-probability linear rate; supports constraints; reduces full-gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)).

Cons
- Overlaps heavily with SVRG; suboptimal κ^2 dependence; lacks experiments; requires unknown parameters; incomplete related work and comparisons.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The idea is interesting, but the contribution over established variance-reduction methods is insufficient, the complexity is weaker, and the lack of empirical validation is problematic for NIPS. Strengthening the analysis (κ instead of κ^2), adding comprehensive comparisons (theory and experiments), and addressing parameter/adaptivity issues could make this suitable for future submission.
