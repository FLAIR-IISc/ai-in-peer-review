## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for strongly convex and smooth optimization, aiming to reduce the dependence on the condition number when using a mix of full and stochastic gradients. The idea is sound and addresses a relevant practical concern in large-scale optimization. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the results are clearly presented. However, the practical impact and comparison to existing methods need further clarification. I lean towards a **weak accept** with a request for more detailed discussion of practical considerations and a more nuanced comparison to related work like SDCA and SAG.

**1. Summary of Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the common issue in first-order optimization: the iteration complexity scaling with the condition number (κ) when using full gradients. Recognizing the trade-off between the computational cost of full gradients and the slower convergence of stochastic gradients, the authors propose EMGD, an algorithm that cleverly combines both. The core idea of dividing the optimization into epochs, using a single full gradient per epoch alongside multiple stochastic gradients, is inspired by epoch gradient descent. The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of convex optimization, drawing comparisons to gradient descent, accelerated gradient descent, stochastic gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA. The authors accurately summarize the theoretical iteration complexities of these methods (Table 1), setting the stage for demonstrating the benefit of EMGD.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework, aiming for condition number independence in the *number of full gradient evaluations*, is a valuable contribution. This is distinct from simply interleaving full and stochastic gradients as in semi-stochastic methods.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence analysis appears sound, with a clear theoretical guarantee of linear convergence (O(log 1/ǫ)) for the number of full gradient evaluations. The proof sketch is detailed enough to allow assessment of its validity.
* **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented in a clear and concise manner. The mathematical notation is well-defined, and the key concepts are explained effectively.
* **Identified Practical Problem:** The paper directly addresses a practical bottleneck in large-scale optimization: reducing the number of expensive full gradient evaluations.
* **Discussion of Limitations & Future Work:** The authors acknowledge the limitation of requiring prior knowledge of the condition number and suggest avenues for future research, such as estimating κ during optimization.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Significance:** While the theoretical result of O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient evaluations is appealing, the paper doesn’t sufficiently address the *practical* implications.  The cost of the O(κ<sup>2</sup>) stochastic gradient evaluations per epoch needs more scrutiny.  For large κ, this could easily outweigh the benefit of reducing full gradient calls. The authors state EMGD is more efficient than stochastic gradient methods, but offer only a high-level justification.
* **Comparison to SDCA & SAG is Limited:**  The comparison in Table 2 is helpful, but a more detailed analysis of the constant factors and the overall computational cost is needed. Specifically, the assumption that a full gradient is *n* times more expensive than a stochastic gradient is crucial and needs justification. The argument that EMGD is beneficial only when κ ≤ n<sup>2/3</sup> feels quite restrictive.  It's unclear if there are scenarios where EMGD outperforms these algorithms in practice.
* **Domain Constraints:** The algorithm includes a projection step onto a ball (W ∩ B(w̄k; ∆k)). While acknowledged as potentially solvable, the cost of this projection is not considered in the computational complexity analysis. This could be a significant bottleneck for high-dimensional problems.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:**  The paper lacks any empirical evaluation. While theoretical results are important, demonstrating the performance of EMGD on benchmark datasets would significantly strengthen the paper and provide insights into its practical applicability.  
* **Assumption of Oracles:** The assumption of access to function oracle `Of` is somewhat abstract. A more explicit discussion of how this oracle would be implemented in a real-world scenario would be helpful.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis. However, the lack of empirical validation weakens the overall assessment.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the concepts are explained in a straightforward manner.
* **Originality:** The combination of epoch-based descent with mixed gradients is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field.
* **Significance:**  The potential to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations is significant, particularly for large-scale problems. However, the practical significance remains unclear without empirical validation and a more thorough comparison to existing algorithms.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel approach with theoretical guarantees.
* Addresses a relevant practical problem in large-scale optimization.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Rigorous analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* Practical significance is not adequately demonstrated.
* Comparison to SDCA and SAG could be more nuanced.
* The cost of stochastic gradients and projection step is not fully considered.




**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.** I recommend acceptance pending the following revisions:

* **Include empirical results:** Perform experiments on benchmark datasets to demonstrate the performance of EMGD and compare it to existing methods like SDCA and SAG.
* **Detailed cost analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost, considering the cost of stochastic gradients, projection step, and the impact of the condition number.
* **Nuanced comparison to SDCA/SAG:** Elaborate on the scenarios where EMGD would be advantageous compared to these algorithms, even with the potential higher cost of stochastic gradient evaluations. Consider providing a more detailed analysis of constant factors.
* **Discussion of oracle implementation:** Provide a more concrete discussion of how the function oracle `Of` would be implemented in practice.
