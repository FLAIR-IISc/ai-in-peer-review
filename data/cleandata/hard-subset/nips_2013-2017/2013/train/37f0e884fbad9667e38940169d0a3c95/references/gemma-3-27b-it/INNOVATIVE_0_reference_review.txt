This paper tackles a significant problem in optimization – reducing the computational cost of full-gradient methods for strongly convex functions, particularly when the condition number is large. The core idea of interleaving full and stochastic gradients in an “Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent” (EMGD) algorithm to achieve a condition-number-independent iteration complexity is bold and, if successful, would represent a genuine advance. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between per-iteration cost and iteration complexity and aim to bridge the gap. The connection to epoch gradient descent and related work (SAG, SDCA) is well established.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The central idea of systematically mixing full and stochastic gradients to achieve a logarithmic iteration complexity independent of the condition number is genuinely novel. While semi-stochastic methods exist, the structured approach via epochs and the specific analysis presented here feel like a substantial contribution.
*   **Significance:** A reduction in the number of full gradient evaluations is critical for large-scale problems. If the theoretical guarantees hold in practice, this algorithm has the potential to be widely applicable in machine learning and other fields.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes a convergence analysis (Theorem 1) that supports the claimed logarithmic complexity. The use of Lemma 1 and related concentration inequalities demonstrates a solid understanding of the theoretical tools necessary for this type of analysis.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm is clearly described (Algorithm 1), and the mathematical notation is generally well-handled. The comparison to existing methods (Table 2) provides helpful context.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Considerations:** The practical implications and potential difficulties are not fully addressed. The dependence on knowing or estimating the condition number *κ* is a significant hurdle. The paper acknowledges this but only briefly touches upon potential solutions. A more detailed discussion of practical estimation techniques would be beneficial.
*   **Constant Factors:** The analysis likely hides substantial constant factors. While achieving O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient iterations is great, the actual runtime might be overshadowed by hidden constants if *κ* is very large. This is common in theoretical analyses, but the authors could acknowledge this limitation more explicitly.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks empirical evaluation. While the theoretical analysis is sound, demonstrating the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the contribution. Showing that the algorithm performs competitively with state-of-the-art solvers, and perhaps even exhibits the promised speedup for ill-conditioned problems, is crucial.
*   **Domain Constraint:** The gradient mapping with domain constraint in step 8 of Algorithm 1 seems computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional problems. This is not discussed at all.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is highly innovative and addresses a critical bottleneck in optimization.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong evidence that the algorithm achieves its claimed guarantees.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The potential impact of this work is significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of empirical validation leaves the practical viability of the algorithm uncertain.
*   The dependence on knowing *κ* and the potential for large constant factors raise concerns about real-world performance.
*   The domain constraint step is not discussed in terms of its computational complexity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a compelling and potentially groundbreaking contribution to the field of optimization. The novelty of the approach and the theoretical rigor of the analysis are commendable. While the lack of empirical evaluation and the practical challenges associated with condition number estimation are significant concerns, I believe the potential impact of this work outweighs these weaknesses. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following in a rebuttal:

1.  **Empirical Results:** Provide preliminary empirical results on a few benchmark datasets to demonstrate the algorithm's performance.
2.  **Condition Number Estimation:** Discuss practical methods for estimating *κ* and their impact on the algorithm's performance.
3.  **Computational Cost of Constraint:** Elaborate on the computational cost of the projection step in algorithm 1 and how it affects overall runtime.

Even without a complete rebuttal, I am inclined to give this paper the benefit of the doubt due to the boldness and originality of the idea. The program committee may want to prioritize this paper for further discussion.
