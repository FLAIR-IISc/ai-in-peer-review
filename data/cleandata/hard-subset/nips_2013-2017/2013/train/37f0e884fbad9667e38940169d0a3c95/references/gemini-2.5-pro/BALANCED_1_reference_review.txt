This paper proposes a new first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex problems. The primary motivation is to reduce the number of computationally expensive full gradient evaluations, particularly for ill-conditioned problems where the condition number κ is large. The standard optimal rate for deterministic first-order methods is O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient calls. The proposed EMGD algorithm leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle to achieve an ε-optimal solution with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls, making this number independent of the condition number κ. This is achieved by dividing the optimization into epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, one full gradient is computed. Within the epoch, a series of updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" that combines this initial full gradient with a variance-reduced stochastic term. The analysis shows that each epoch reduces the error by a constant factor, leading to a linear convergence rate with a total complexity of O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

The proposed method is related to several lines of work. It builds upon the epoch-based structure of algorithms like EGD [9], but cleverly avoids an increasing number of oracle calls per epoch. It fits into the category of hybrid or semi-stochastic methods that combine deterministic and stochastic information. Most importantly, it aims to achieve the linear convergence rates seen in variance-reduction methods like SAG [22] and SDCA [23] for finite-sum problems, while being applicable to more general stochastic objectives and constrained optimization problems.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Goal:** The central goal of making the number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ is highly significant. For many large-scale problems, computing a full gradient is the primary bottleneck, and its dependence on κ in methods like AGD can be prohibitive. The proposed approach to bypass this lower bound by using stochastic gradients is a conceptually elegant and interesting idea.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness and Strong Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis to support its claims. The convergence proof, based on an inductive argument over epochs, is clear and appears to be correct. The result is a high-probability convergence bound, which is stronger and often more desirable in practice than the in-expectation guarantees provided by many competing stochastic algorithms like SAG and SDCA.

3.  **Generality of the Framework:** A key advantage of EMGD is its generality. Unlike SAG, SDCA, and other prominent variance-reduction methods (e.g., SVRG, SAGA), EMGD is not restricted to the finite-sum setting (F(w) = (1/n)Σf_i(w)). It only requires access to a stochastic oracle that produces unbiased gradients. Furthermore, the algorithm explicitly handles constrained optimization problems (via the projection in Step 8), which is a significant advantage over many variance-reduction methods that are primarily designed for unconstrained problems.

4.  **Low Memory Requirement:** The paper correctly points out that, unlike SAG which requires storing n past gradients, EMGD has a minimal memory footprint of O(d), which is a crucial practical advantage for problems with a very large number of components (n).

### Weaknesses

1.  **High Dependence on κ in Stochastic Oracle Calls:** The most significant weakness of the proposed method is its heavy reliance on the condition number for the number of stochastic gradients. The complexity of O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients is very steep. For problems where κ is large (the exact regime where this method is supposed to be beneficial), a quadratic dependence can be debilitating and may render the algorithm slower than alternatives. The paper's own comparison shows EMGD is only superior to Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which can be a restrictive condition.

2.  **Crucial Missing Comparison to SVRG:** The paper's related work section is missing a discussion of arguably the most relevant prior work: Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) [Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013]. SVRG uses a nearly identical algorithmic structure: an outer loop (epoch) computes a full gradient, and an inner loop performs stochastic updates using a variance-reduced gradient of the form ∇f_i(w_t) - ∇f_i(w̃) + ∇F(w̃). SVRG achieves a total complexity of O((n+κ)log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations (where a full gradient is counted as n stochastic ones). For constant κ, this matches SAG, but it often performs better for large κ. The total work of EMGD, assuming a full gradient costs n, is O(n log(1/ε) + κ² log(1/ε)). In most practical regimes (e.g., n > κ), SVRG's complexity is superior to EMGD's. The conceptual similarity is so strong that this omission significantly undermines the paper's claims of novelty and its competitive positioning.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the absence of any experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of EMGD. An empirical comparison against Nesterov's AGD, SAG, and especially SVRG on standard datasets would be necessary to: (a) validate the theory, (b) understand the practical impact of the O(κ²) term, and (c) demonstrate in which regimes, if any, EMGD is practically superior.

4.  **Practicality Concerns:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ to set the number of inner iterations T. The authors acknowledge this as a drawback and suggest estimating it as future work. However, this is a major practical hurdle. Setting T incorrectly could lead to divergence or extremely slow convergence. This makes the algorithm difficult to use out-of-the-box compared to methods that are less sensitive to hyperparameter tuning.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Thoroughly Discuss and Compare with SVRG:** The authors must add SVRG and related methods (like SAGA) to the related work section. A detailed theoretical comparison of the total computational complexity of EMGD and SVRG is essential. This would provide a more honest and accurate picture of where EMGD fits within the landscape of variance-reduced methods.

2.  **Provide Empirical Results:** I strongly recommend adding an experimental section. This should include experiments on benchmark machine learning problems (e.g., regularized logistic or linear regression) where κ can be controlled via the regularization parameter. Comparing the convergence of EMGD against AGD, SGD, SAG, and SVRG would be incredibly insightful and would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution by demonstrating its practical relevance.

3.  **Address the O(κ²) Complexity:** The authors should discuss the high O(κ²) stochastic complexity more directly. Is this an artifact of the analysis, or is it fundamental to the algorithm's design? Could techniques from accelerated methods be incorporated into the inner loop to potentially improve this dependence from κ² to κ? Exploring this would be a valuable direction.

4.  **Elaborate on Practical Implementation:** To improve the paper's practical utility, the authors could provide more guidance on dealing with the unknown κ. Even if a full solution is future work, a brief discussion of existing heuristics for estimating L and λ or adaptive search strategies for the inner loop size T would be helpful for practitioners.

### Conclusion

The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm with the significant goal of making full gradient complexity independent of the condition number. Its generality to constrained and non-finite-sum problems is a notable strength. However, the current manuscript has several major weaknesses that prevent a recommendation for acceptance. The omission of a comparison to SVRG—a highly similar and often superior algorithm—is a critical flaw. Additionally, the very high O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients and the complete lack of empirical validation make the practical significance of the proposed method unclear.

While the core idea is promising, the paper in its current form feels incomplete. I would encourage the authors to perform the suggested comparisons and experiments. With a more complete evaluation and positioning relative to the state-of-the-art, this work could represent a strong contribution.

**Decision:** Reject (with encouragement for resubmission).
