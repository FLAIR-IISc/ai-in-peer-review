### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing smooth and strongly convex functions, particularly in settings where the condition number κ is large, making algorithms that rely on full gradients computationally expensive. The standard optimal rate for first-order methods, O(√κ log(1/ε)), requires a number of full gradient evaluations that depends on κ. The authors propose a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle to reduce the number of required full gradient computations, making it independent of the condition number κ.

The proposed EMGD algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at the current solution anchor. It then performs a series of T inner iterations using a "mixed gradient," which is a form of variance-reduced gradient: it combines the fixed full gradient from the start of the epoch with a stochastic term that corrects for the difference between the current iterate and the anchor point. The updates are performed within a shrinking search domain. The authors prove that this procedure achieves a linear convergence rate. The main theoretical result is that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This successfully shifts the dependency on the condition number from the expensive full gradient calls to the cheaper stochastic ones. The paper compares this complexity to standard full gradient methods and other linear-rate stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA, arguing for advantages in handling constrained optimization, low memory requirements, and providing a high-probability convergence guarantee.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Goal:** The central idea of making the number of expensive full gradient computations independent of the condition number is both novel and highly significant. It directly targets a major bottleneck in large-scale optimization for ill-conditioned problems and provides a new perspective on trading off different types of oracle access.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis culminating in a high-probability linear convergence guarantee (Theorem 1). The result—achieving an ε-solution with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls—is impressive and achieves the paper's stated goal. The proof technique, based on an inductive argument over epochs and the use of a martingale concentration inequality, appears to be sound.

3.  **Generality of the Framework:** Unlike several competing methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA) that are primarily designed for finite-sum objective functions, the proposed algorithm and its analysis apply to the more general stochastic optimization setting where F(w) = E[f(w)]. Furthermore, the algorithm explicitly handles constrained optimization problems through the projection step (Step 8), which is a notable advantage.

4.  **Practical Advantages over Competing Methods:** The paper makes a compelling case for several practical benefits. The memory requirement of O(d) is a significant improvement over methods like SAG, which requires O(n) storage. The high-probability convergence bound is stronger than the in-expectation results typical for many stochastic methods. The structure of the algorithm, with its single full gradient call per epoch, is also well-suited for distributed computation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Missing Comparison to Key Related Work (SVRG):** The proposed "mixed gradient" `g̃_kt = ∇F(w̄^k) + ∇f_kt(w_kt) - ∇f_kt(w̄^k)` and the epoch-based structure are almost identical to the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) algorithm (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). SVRG also uses an outer loop (epoch) to compute a full gradient and inner loops with the same variance-reduced update. The analysis of SVRG for finite-sum problems yields a complexity of O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, which translates to O(log(1/ε)) full gradient passes. The omission of any discussion or comparison with SVRG is a major flaw. The paper's originality is significantly diminished without a clear explanation of how EMGD differs from or improves upon SVRG, perhaps by extending its analysis to the general expectation setting or providing a different analytical perspective.

2.  **High Stochastic Gradient Complexity:** The number of stochastic gradients required is O(κ² log(1/ε)). The T ≥ O(κ²) inner iterations per epoch is a very strong requirement. For moderately large κ, this could be computationally prohibitive and significantly worse than the O(κ) dependence seen in SVRG or SDCA for the finite-sum case. The paper's comparison in Section 3.4 seems overly optimistic by focusing only on specific regimes (e.g., κ ≤ n²/³). A more direct and honest comparison of the κ² dependence against the κ dependence of other methods is needed.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While this can be acceptable, the practical viability of the trade-off presented is not demonstrated. An experimental study would be crucial to understand for which values of κ and relative costs of full/stochastic gradients EMGD is actually beneficial. Given the potentially very large number of inner iterations (T ~ κ²), experiments could reveal if the theoretical constants are favorable or if the algorithm is practical only for small κ.

4.  **Dependence on Known Parameters:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the smoothness (L) and strong convexity (λ) parameters to set the number of inner-loop iterations T. This is a common but important limitation of many accelerated and variance-reduced methods. The paper acknowledges this as future work, but it remains a significant weakness for practical deployment.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the algorithm is clearly presented. However, the proof in Section 4.2 is quite dense. For instance, the transition in equation (14) from a constrained optimization problem to `η/2 ||∇g_t(w_t)||²` could benefit from a brief explanation or citation of the relevant duality result for clarity.
*   **Originality:** As mentioned, the originality is questionable due to the strong similarity to SVRG. The primary contribution may lie in the specific analysis that isolates the number of full gradient calls and applies to a general stochastic (not just finite-sum) setting. This needs to be made explicit.
*   **Significance:** The conceptual contribution is high. If the algorithm proves practical, it could influence the design of optimization methods for ill-conditioned problems. However, its significance is currently limited by the lack of comparison to SVRG and the high `κ²` dependence in its complexity.

### Summary of Review and Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting and conceptually appealing idea: designing an optimization algorithm for smooth, strongly convex problems where the number of expensive full gradient evaluations is independent of the condition number κ. The proposed EMGD algorithm and its theoretical analysis successfully demonstrate that this is possible, which is a strong result.

However, the work suffers from a critical weakness: it fails to acknowledge and compare itself to the highly similar SVRG algorithm. This omission undermines the claim of novelty. Furthermore, the resulting complexity for stochastic gradients, O(κ² log(1/ε)), may be uncompetitive compared to existing variance-reduced methods in many practical scenarios. The lack of experiments makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's practical value.

While the core idea is valuable, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. A major revision is required to thoroughly position the work with respect to SVRG, provide a more balanced discussion of the computational complexity, and ideally include empirical results to validate the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant problem in optimization with a novel goal.
*   Provides a strong, non-trivial theoretical result with a high-probability bound.
*   The framework is general (handles constraints and expectations, not just finite sums).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Fails to cite or compare with SVRG, a seminal and highly similar algorithm.
*   The stochastic gradient complexity has a high O(κ²) dependence, which may be uncompetitive.
*   No empirical results to demonstrate practical utility.
*   Requires knowledge of problem parameters (L, λ).
