

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm for smooth strongly convex optimization that aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by combining full and stochastic gradients. The authors claim that EMGD achieves linear convergence with a number of full gradients independent of the condition number κ, while requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper compares EMGD with existing methods like SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's accelerated gradient method.

## Strengths
The paper makes a reasonable attempt to address an important problem in optimization. The idea of combining full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of expensive full gradient evaluations is conceptually sound. The algorithm design borrows from epoch gradient descent and introduces a novel "mixed gradient" approach that could be of interest to the optimization community. The theoretical analysis demonstrates a linear convergence rate with a condition number-independent full gradient count, which is a significant claim.

## Weaknesses

1. **Fundamental Technical Flaw in Theorem 1**: The most serious issue is in Theorem 1, where the authors claim "with a probability at least 1−mδ". This guarantee is mathematically invalid because 1−mδ can be negative when mδ > 1 (e.g., m=10, δ=0.2). This is a critical error that undermines the entire probabilistic guarantee of the algorithm. The authors should have stated the guarantee as 1−δ with a union bound over m epochs (i.e., probability at least 1−mδ would require δ ≤ 1/m, which is not stated).

2. **Inconsistent Mathematical Notation**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies. For example:
   - "O( √ κ log 1ǫ )" should be "O(√κ log(1/ε))"
   - Inconsistent use of "ǫ" vs "ε"
   - Inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions (e.g., "O(κ2 log 1ǫ )" should be "O(κ² log(1/ε))")
   - In equation (19), the square root should cover the entire expression: "√(2T ln(1/δ))" not "√2T ln(1/δ)"

3. **Citation Error**: The paper references "the optimal O(1/λε) iteration complexity [1]" but reference [1] is not properly listed in the bibliography (it's listed as "Information-theoretic lower bounds on the oracle complexity of stochastic convex optimization, A. Agarwal, P.L. Bartlett, P. Ravikumar, M.J. Wainwright, IEEE Transactions on Information Theory,, , ,").

4. **Misleading Claims**: The paper claims "the number of full gradients required is reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε))" without acknowledging that this comes at the cost of O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper doesn't properly analyze whether the total computational cost is actually better, especially if stochastic gradients are expensive to compute.

5. **Inaccurate Comparison with SAG/SDCA**: The paper claims "the linear convergence of SAG and SDCA only holds in expectation, whereas the linear convergence of EMGD holds with a high probability." This is inaccurate - SAG and SDCA also have high-probability convergence guarantees under appropriate conditions (see [22, 23]).

6. **Algorithmic Detail Error**: In Algorithm 1, step 10 states "w̄k+1 = 1T+1 ∑T+1 t=1 w k t", but the summation should be from t=1 to T+1, not from T+1 to T+1.

7. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper switches between "stochastic gradient" and "stochastic gradient descent" without clear distinction, which could confuse readers.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an interesting problem and contains some novel elements, the fundamental flaw in Theorem 1 (the invalid probability guarantee) is severe enough to invalidate the main contribution. This is not a minor oversight - it's a critical mathematical error that would prevent the algorithm from being used with confidence.

The numerous formatting inconsistencies and technical errors throughout the paper further undermine its credibility. While the authors have made a reasonable attempt to address an important problem, the paper as written does not meet NIPS's standards for technical rigor.

I recommend rejection due to the critical mathematical error in the main theorem, which fundamentally undermines the paper's contribution. The authors would need to completely revise their theoretical analysis to provide a valid probabilistic guarantee before the paper could be considered for acceptance.

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from:
1. Correcting the probability guarantee in Theorem 1
2. Properly citing all references
3. Clarifying the tradeoff between full and stochastic gradient evaluations (is the total computational cost actually better?)
4. Providing a more accurate comparison with existing semi-stochastic methods

The authors should also consider whether the claimed "condition number independent" full gradient access is truly beneficial in practice, given the O(κ²) increase in stochastic gradient evaluations.
