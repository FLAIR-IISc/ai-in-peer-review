Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for minimizing a smooth, λ-strongly convex objective F over a convex set W when two oracles are available: (i) a full-gradient oracle Og returning ∇F(w), and (ii) a function oracle Of that returns a random smooth function f such that F(w) = E[f(w)]. EMGD proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes a full gradient at the epoch anchor w̄k. Within the epoch, it performs T projected updates using a “mixed” (control-variate) gradient g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇ft(wt) − ∇ft(w̄k), and constrains iterates to a shrinking Euclidean ball around w̄k. The analysis shows that per epoch, the error contracts by a constant factor with high probability if T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)), yielding an overall iteration complexity of O(log(1/ε)) epochs, i.e., O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The authors compare to accelerated full-gradient methods (O(√κ log(1/ε))) and to incremental methods such as SAG and SDCA on finite-sum problems; they emphasize EMGD’s low memory footprint and applicability to constrained problems.

This mixed-gradient idea closely mirrors variance-reduced stochastic methods developed for finite-sum problems: SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014), and related methods (MISO/Finito, Prox-SVRG/SAGA, SARAH/SPIDER). In particular, SVRG uses the identical control variate ∇f_i(w_t) − ∇f_i(w̃) + ∇F(w̃) with one full-gradient per epoch and achieves linear convergence with O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) gradient computations, i.e., O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. Proximal variants handle constraints or composite regularizers. SDCA also enjoys linear convergence and often high-probability bounds.

Strengths
- Goal is well-motivated: reducing the number of full-gradient evaluations independent of κ is valuable for large datasets.
- Algorithm is simple, memory-light (O(d)), and the analysis provides a high-probability linear convergence statement.
- The oracle model covers both finite-sum and more general expectations; the method naturally allows projections, which is helpful for constrained problems.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical issues
- The strong convexity condition (Eq. (4)) is incorrect: ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖ does not follow from λ-strong convexity and is generally false. The standard forms are F(u) ≥ F(v) + ⟨∇F(v), u − v⟩ + (λ/2)‖u − v‖^2 and ⟨∇F(u) − ∇F(v), u − v⟩ ≥ λ‖u − v‖^2. This mistake propagates (e.g., Eq. (13) uses a −λ‖·‖^2 term instead of −(λ/2)‖·‖^2).
- The analysis is suboptimal: it yields an O(1/√T) within-epoch decrease requiring T = Θ(κ^2) to halve the error, due to Azuma-type bounds and norm-bounding via a shrinking ball. Standard variance-reduction analyses (SVRG/SAGA) give T = Θ(κ) without trust-region shrinking and with constant step sizes, often in expectation and sometimes with high-probability bounds via Freedman-type inequalities.
- The algorithm requires knowledge of κ to set T and η; no adaptive mechanism is provided.

Originality
- The core estimator g̃t is identical to SVRG’s control-variate gradient; the overall structure (epochs with one full gradient each) is also SVRG-like. The use of a shrinking ball and the specific high-probability analysis are differences, but they lead to worse κ-dependence (κ^2 vs κ) and do not appear to provide a compensating benefit. Key related work (SVRG, Prox-SVRG, SAGA) is not cited or compared against.

Significance
- The main advertised benefit—O(log(1/ε)) full gradients independent of κ—is already achieved by SVRG and subsequent methods, typically with fewer stochastic gradients (O(κ log(1/ε)) rather than O(κ^2 log(1/ε))). Claims that EMGD improves over full-gradient methods are thus not compelling relative to the current state of the art.
- No empirical results are provided to demonstrate practical advantages (e.g., constrained settings, wall-clock performance, robustness).

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but there are several typos (“epoches”), missing constants, and nonstandard/incorrect definitions (Eq. (4)). Algorithmic details such as the projection step onto W ∩ B(·; ∆) deserve discussion of feasibility. Equation numbering and references could be tightened.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear goal and simple algorithm.
- High-probability linear convergence statement under a broad oracle model.
- Low memory and nominal applicability to constrained problems.

Cons for acceptance
- Substantial overlap with established variance-reduced methods; missing key citations (SVRG/SAGA).
- Inferior κ-dependence (κ^2) compared to the state of the art (κ).
- Technical inaccuracies in the strong convexity assumptions/inequalities.
- Requires prior knowledge of κ; lacks adaptivity.
- No experiments; limited and partly inaccurate comparisons (e.g., high-probability claims for SAG/SDCA).

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the strong convexity assumptions and all derivations that rely on them (use standard inequalities and constants).
- Situate the contribution relative to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), Prox-SVRG, SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014), SDCA high-probability results, Katyusha (accelerated variance reduction), SARAH/SPIDER, and more recent constrained/composite variants. Update claims about expectation vs high-probability guarantees.
- Strengthen the analysis to achieve O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients per epoch with constant step sizes, removing the shrinking-ball device. High-probability bounds can be obtained via self-bounding variance and Freedman inequalities.
- Provide an adaptive strategy that does not require κ (e.g., doubling tricks, line-search, or local curvature estimation).
- Add empirical evaluations on finite-sum and constrained problems, reporting both number of full/stochastic gradients and wall-clock time, and comparing against AGD, SVRG/Prox-SVRG, SAGA, and SDCA.

Recommendation
Reject. While the goal is important and the presentation is mostly clear, the main idea overlaps with established variance-reduced methods, the analysis is technically flawed and suboptimal (κ^2), key related work is missing, and there is no empirical evidence. With corrected theory, full comparison to contemporary methods, adaptivity, and experiments, a future version could be much stronger.
