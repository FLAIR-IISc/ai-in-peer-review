

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes an algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The key claim is that by combining full gradients with stochastic gradients, the method achieves linear convergence (O(log 1/ε)) while requiring only O(log 1/ε) full gradient evaluations, independent of the condition number κ. This contrasts with the standard O(√κ log 1/ε) complexity for full gradient methods.

The paper builds upon related work in convex optimization, particularly referencing SAG and SDCA for problems where F(w) = (1/n)∑f_i(w). The authors claim EMGD has advantages in terms of storage requirements, applicability to constrained problems, and high-probability convergence guarantees compared to SAG/SDCA. The theoretical analysis establishes a convergence rate with explicit conditions on T and δ.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical presentation. The algorithm design is logical, and the theoretical analysis appears technically sound within its assumptions. The comparison with existing methods (SAG, SDCA) is thorough and highlights specific advantages of EMGD.

**Weaknesses:** The main weakness is the fundamental theoretical claim that the number of full gradient evaluations becomes condition number independent. This directly contradicts established lower bounds for first-order methods. The paper fails to adequately address why the standard lower bound of O(√κ log 1/ε) does not apply to their method. In their analysis, they assume access to both full gradients and stochastic gradients, but the standard lower bound for first-order methods assumes access to only one type of gradient information. However, the paper does not provide a convincing argument that this assumption bypasses the fundamental lower bound.

The paper also lacks empirical validation. NIPS typically values both theoretical and experimental results. Without experiments demonstrating the claimed computational advantage in practical settings, the theoretical claims remain unverified. The paper acknowledges this limitation but does not provide any experimental evidence to support their theoretical analysis.

Another significant concern is the requirement to know κ beforehand. While the authors briefly mention that in some cases (e.g., with a strongly convex regularizer) κ can be estimated, this remains a major practical limitation that would need to be addressed for real-world applications. The paper does not discuss how this limitation might affect the algorithm's applicability in practice.

The claimed computational advantage (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) is very restrictive. For most machine learning problems, κ can be large (e.g., in ill-conditioned problems), meaning the advantage of EMGD would not hold in many practical scenarios. The paper's comparison in Table 2 shows that EMGD only outperforms Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which may not be common in practice.

**Originality:** The core idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients isn't entirely novel, as the paper acknowledges in the related work section (citing "semi-stochastic" methods). While the specific algorithm (EMGD) appears to be new, the approach is incremental rather than groundbreaking.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is questionable given the theoretical concerns and restrictive conditions for practical advantage. Without empirical validation, the paper's contribution remains largely theoretical and unverified.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound within its theoretical framework, the main claim contradicts established lower bounds in optimization theory without sufficient justification. The lack of empirical validation and the restrictive practical conditions further diminish the paper's contribution. The field of convex optimization has a long history of carefully analyzing such theoretical claims, and this paper fails to meet the rigorous standards expected at NIPS.

**Pro and Con Summary:**
- Pro: Well-written theoretical analysis, clear comparison with related methods, interesting algorithm design
- Con: Fundamental theoretical claim contradicts known lower bounds, no empirical validation, restrictive practical conditions (requires knowing κ, only advantageous when κ ≤ n^(2/3)), lacks practical significance

This paper would be more appropriate for a theoretical optimization journal rather than NIPS, where empirical validation and practical relevance are highly valued. The claims need stronger theoretical justification or empirical evidence to support the condition number independence claim before being considered for publication at NIPS.
