

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm for smooth strongly convex problems that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with condition number independent access to full gradients. The authors claim that while standard full gradient methods require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, EMGD only requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (independent of κ), while using O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing linear convergence with high probability, and compares EMGD to existing methods like SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent.

## Quality
The paper's technical quality is strong. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful bounding of error terms using martingale inequalities and properties of strongly convex functions. The main theorem (Theorem 1) is well-supported by the detailed proof, which employs appropriate mathematical tools for the problem domain. The authors are careful to state all necessary assumptions (L-smoothness, λ-strong convexity, and the structure of the stochastic oracle).

However, the paper has notable limitations. First, it assumes the condition number κ is known beforehand, which is a significant practical constraint since κ is rarely known in advance. Second, the paper lacks empirical validation - no experiments demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm, which is particularly surprising for a NeurIPS paper. Third, the computational complexity analysis assumes that stochastic gradients are "efficiently computable" without specifying what this means in practice or how it compares to full gradient computation.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The introduction provides a perfect setup for the problem, clearly articulating the trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients. The related work section is comprehensive and well-structured, effectively contextualizing the contribution. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and well-commented, and the proof is logically presented with clear intermediate steps. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout.

The paper excels in making complex mathematical concepts accessible. The authors carefully explain why the mixed gradient approach works (the stochastic part being well-bounded due to the shrinking domain), which is crucial for understanding the algorithm's innovation.

## Originality
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on convex optimization. While the idea of combining full and stochastic gradients isn't entirely novel (citing works like [12]), the specific approach of using a mixed gradient with a shrinking domain to achieve condition number independent full gradient access is novel. The paper effectively differentiates its contribution from SAG and SDCA by highlighting key advantages: applicability to both constrained and unconstrained problems, lower storage requirements (O(d) vs O(n)), and high-probability convergence guarantees (vs. expectation for SAG/SDCA).

The main limitation in originality is that the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from other "semi-stochastic" methods in the literature. While it cites [12, 13], it could better explain how EMGD differs from these approaches in both theory and practice.

## Significance
The significance of this work is moderate. The theoretical result is valuable - reducing the dependence on the condition number from O(√κ) to O(1) for full gradient access is an important theoretical advancement. For large-scale machine learning problems where the condition number can be very large, this could lead to substantial computational savings.

However, the practical significance is limited by the lack of empirical validation. The paper states that EMGD can be applied to regularized machine learning problems with reasonable assumptions, but without experiments, it's difficult to assess the real-world impact. The paper also doesn't address how the proposed method compares to state-of-the-art stochastic methods in terms of practical runtime, which would be crucial for NeurIPS.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical contribution with rigorous proof
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Good differentiation from related work
- Practical implications for ill-conditioned problems

**Weaknesses:**
- Assumes condition number κ is known beforehand (significant practical limitation)
- Lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims
- Doesn't fully distinguish from existing semi-stochastic methods
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations

This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to convex optimization, but its impact would be significantly enhanced with empirical validation and a more thorough discussion of practical limitations. The theoretical results are important, but the absence of experiments is a notable gap for a NeurIPS submission.

## Recommendation
I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of optimization algorithms. However, the lack of empirical results and the assumption that κ is known beforehand are significant limitations for a NeurIPS paper. With minor revisions (particularly adding a brief empirical evaluation), this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

The paper meets most of the NeurIPS criteria, but the missing empirical component prevents it from being a strong candidate for acceptance. It's a valuable theoretical contribution, but would benefit from practical validation to fully demonstrate its significance.
