## NIPS Review: Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the computational cost of solving smooth, strongly convex optimization problems, particularly when the condition number (κ) is large.  The core idea is to strategically interleave full gradient evaluations with cheaper stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs. The authors demonstrate that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate, requiring O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, independent of κ, a significant improvement over standard full-gradient methods which require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients. The algorithm builds on concepts from epoch gradient descent and leverages the benefits of both full and stochastic gradient information.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of first-order optimization. It acknowledges the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost of full vs. stochastic gradients.  The authors thoroughly review relevant work on deterministic, stochastic, and semi-stochastic methods (including SAG, SDCA, and EGD), highlighting their respective complexities and limitations. The connection to epoch gradient descent [9] is clearly stated, and the motivation for improving upon existing methods by reducing the dependence on the condition number is well-justified. The comparisons to SAG and SDCA in Table 2 are relevant and insightful, placing the contribution in context.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed EMGD algorithm offers a genuinely interesting approach to tackling the high computational cost associated with full gradient evaluations for ill-conditioned problems. The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework is a clever idea.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis, establishing a linear convergence rate and demonstrating the independence of the full gradient count from the condition number (κ).  The theorem is well-stated, and the proof sketch provided in the appendix is helpful.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented, and the notations are mostly consistent. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contribution.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge the practical limitation of needing to estimate the condition number κ and discuss potential approaches for doing so in future work.  The comparison with existing algorithms like SAG and SDCA, considering the cost of full gradients, is a valuable addition.
* **Strongly Convex Focus:** Focusing on strongly convex problems is a sensible choice, as it allows for more aggressive convergence rates and a cleaner theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Implementation Details:** While the algorithm is clearly described conceptually, the paper lacks details regarding practical implementation. For example, how is the domain size Δk updated in practice?  What specific techniques are used to efficiently solve the constrained optimization problem in step 8 of Algorithm 1?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The analysis relies on setting specific parameters (η, ∆1, T). The sensitivity of the algorithm’s performance to these parameters is not discussed.  Are robust parameter tuning strategies available?
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical validation. While the theoretical results are compelling, demonstrating the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets or benchmark problems would significantly strengthen the contribution.  Comparing EMGD’s runtime against standard full-gradient methods and stochastic algorithms on problems with varying condition numbers would be highly informative.
* **Proof Detail:** While a proof sketch is provided, a fully detailed proof would be necessary for a high-confidence acceptance. The reviewers will need to be able to verify the claims made in the paper.
* **Assumption on Oracle Of:** The assumption that the function oracle Of provides a random *smooth* function f(·) feels slightly artificial. It would be more common to assume a stochastic gradient oracle, directly providing an unbiased estimate of the full gradient. This distinction makes it harder to immediately relate the work to applications where only noisy gradient estimates are available.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The algorithm addresses a significant practical problem – the computational cost of full gradients for ill-conditioned optimization.
* The theoretical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides a solid foundation for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation is a major weakness.
* The practical implementation details are not fully fleshed out.
* The sensitivity to parameter settings is not explored.
* A fully detailed proof is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a novel contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical results are compelling, and the algorithm has the potential to be very useful in practice. However, the lack of empirical evaluation and the missing implementation details are significant concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject.**  I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form. However, I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly by including a comprehensive empirical evaluation. If they can demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness on real-world problems and provide more practical guidance, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance in a future submission.  I would be happy to reconsider a revised version of this paper.
