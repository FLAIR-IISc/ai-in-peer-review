Summary and context
The paper studies first-order optimization for smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives F, and asks how to reduce the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations while retaining linear convergence. It assumes access to two oracles: (i) a full-gradient oracle Og(w) = ∇F(w), and (ii) a function oracle Of returning a random f with F(w) = Ef[f(w)] and each f L-smooth (so stochastic gradients are available). The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) partitions optimization into epochs; at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient ∇F(w̄k), and then performs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), updating by a projected/proximal step over a shrinking ball B(w̄k; Δk). With appropriately chosen T, step size, and epoch schedule, the paper proves a linear convergence rate with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls (with high probability), where κ = L/λ.

Relation to prior work: The approach fits in the semi-stochastic/variance-reduction family that interleaves periodic full gradients with stochastic corrections. The mixed-gradient estimator is the standard control-variate form used later in SVRG-type methods, and the paper compares with SAG and SDCA in the finite-sum setting. It emphasizes applicability to constrained problems, low memory usage, and high-probability bounds.

Evaluation by criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level idea and the analysis technique (epoching, control variate, bounded variance via a shrinking domain, martingale concentration) are standard and appear broadly correct. The bounded-variance argument relies on keeping iterates within a ball of radius Δ, which is enforced by projection; Azuma–Hoeffding is used to obtain high-probability guarantees. The conclusions (one full gradient per epoch and linear convergence) are plausible.
- However, constants are large and the dependence on the condition number is suboptimal: the result requires T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) stochastic steps per epoch. In the finite-sum case, comparable methods (e.g., SAG/SDCA) achieve O((n+κ) log(1/ε)), and variance-reduced estimators of the same form can achieve O(κ) dependence without shrinking-domain projections. The analysis here appears loose relative to that benchmark.
- Some issues reduce confidence/readability: the proximal objective in Step 8 lacks a 1/2 factor (later used in the proof); equation numbering and references have minor inconsistencies; a few statements would benefit from more careful justification (e.g., conditions under which the projection onto W∩B is tractable, and implications for runtime).
- No experiments are provided to support practical claims or to illustrate the computational trade-offs, which would be valuable given the algorithmic focus.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized (introduction, related work, algorithm, theorem, proof sketch), and the core idea is clearly conveyed.
- There are several notational/typographical issues: duplicated equation labels, minor algebraic omissions (factor in proximal term), and some informal phrasing that could be tightened. The exposition of the assumptions on Of (random f vs. standard stochastic gradient oracle) would benefit from a crisper statement.
- The comparison section is helpful, but the computational thresholds would be clearer with explicit total gradient-evaluation counts and consistent cost models.

Originality
- The mixed-gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is the classic control-variate used in semi-stochastic variance-reduction methods; the novelty here is limited. The main distinct element is the shrinking-domain projection to ensure bounded variance and to obtain high-probability bounds. However, related methods achieve linear convergence and condition-number–independent full-gradient counts without the κ^2 factor, and the paper does not adequately position itself against that literature.
- The related work does not cite closely related variance-reduction methods that use exactly this estimator and show O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) complexity. Failing to discuss these makes it hard to assess the contribution’s novelty and tightness.

Significance
- The question—reducing the number of full-gradient evaluations while retaining linear convergence—is important, and the high-probability result and applicability to constrained problems are potentially useful.
- That said, the κ^2 dependence makes the stochastic-gradient cost high; in regimes where full gradients are expensive, this can still be a net win, but in the finite-sum setting the method appears non-competitive with best-known results. Without experiments or tighter analysis, the practical significance is uncertain.

Pros
- Clear goal and simple algorithmic structure; one full gradient per epoch and linear convergence with high probability.
- Works with constraints (provided projections onto W ∩ ball are tractable) and uses only O(d) memory.
- Provides a clean analysis template (epoching + concentration) and an explicit computational trade-off.

Cons
- Suboptimal κ^2 dependence for stochastic steps; likely improvable given existing variance-reduction analyses.
- Missing discussion/citations of closely related methods using the same estimator and achieving better complexity.
- No empirical validation; several clarity/notation issues; requires knowledge of κ and Δ1.
- Projection onto W ∩ B can be nontrivial; practicality of this step is not demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten the analysis to target O(κ) stochastic steps per epoch; consider removing the shrinking-domain device in favor of standard variance-reduction arguments that control variance via periodic full gradients.
- Situate the work against variance-reduced methods (e.g., SVRG/SAGA and their constrained variants) and clarify similarities/differences; if the high-probability bound or constrained setting is the main novelty, emphasize and formalize that.
- Add experiments on representative smooth strongly convex problems (both constrained and unconstrained), reporting wall-clock time vs. accuracy and the relative costs of full vs. stochastic gradients.
- Clarify and correct notation (proximal term factor; equation labels); discuss how to handle unknown κ (e.g., doubling/truncation or adaptive schedules).
- Discuss the computational cost of projecting onto W ∩ B and provide examples where this is efficient.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is important and the paper presents a clear algorithm with a rigorous high-probability analysis. However, the method appears to re-derive a known estimator with a looser κ^2 dependence, lacks comparison to closely related variance-reduction work, and provides no empirical evidence. With tighter complexity, richer positioning, and experiments, this could become a stronger contribution.
