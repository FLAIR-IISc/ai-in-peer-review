### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for minimizing smooth and strongly convex functions. The primary contribution is an algorithm that achieves a linear convergence rate while requiring a number of full gradient evaluations that is independent of the problem's condition number, κ. Specifically, EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The method works by dividing the optimization into epochs. At the start of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed at an anchor point. Within the epoch, updates use a variance-reduced stochastic gradient, which combines this anchor gradient with stochastic gradient information at the current iterate. The algorithm operates on a progressively shrinking search domain, which is key to controlling the variance and ensuring linear convergence. The authors position this work as a way to bypass the O(√κ) full gradient complexity lower bound for first-order methods by leveraging a second, stochastic oracle.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Goal:** The objective of making the number of expensive full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ is highly significant, especially for ill-conditioned problems common in machine learning. The approach of using a hybrid oracle (full and stochastic) to circumvent established lower bounds for single-oracle methods is a clever and interesting research direction.

2.  **General Framework:** Unlike many state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods (e.g., SAG, SAGA, SDCA) that are designed specifically for finite-sum problems, the proposed EMGD framework is more general. It applies to the broader stochastic optimization setting where `F(w) = E[f(w)]` and, importantly, can handle constrained optimization problems, a feature often lacking in its finite-sum counterparts.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous proof of linear convergence with high probability. This is a stronger guarantee than the in-expectation convergence results typical for many stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA. The theoretical analysis, while using established tools like the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality, is sound and well-presented.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The algorithm is described clearly, the motivation is well-articulated, and the theoretical analysis is structured logically and is easy to follow for an expert in the field.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Severe Dependence on κ²:** The most critical weakness is the dependence of the stochastic gradient complexity on κ². The number of inner-loop iterations `T` must be at least O(κ² log(1/δ)). This means the total computational cost, assuming stochastic gradients are `n` times cheaper than full gradients, is O((n + κ²) log(1/ε)). For problems with even a moderately large condition number, this κ² term can dominate and make the algorithm computationally prohibitive, ironically undermining the goal of efficiently solving ill-conditioned problems.

2.  **Weak Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to SAG and SDCA in Section 3.4 is not entirely favorable to EMGD for the important finite-sum case. The complexity of SAG/SAGA/SDCA is typically O((n+κ)log(1/ε)). EMGD's O((n+κ²)log(1/ε)) complexity is strictly worse. The paper argues EMGD is better than Nesterov's AGD when κ ≤ n²/³, but in this regime, SAG/SDCA would likely be far superior. While EMGD's generality is a key advantage, its performance in the well-studied finite-sum setting appears uncompetitive. The related work section is also missing a key reference: Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) [Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013]. The mixed gradient `g̃_kt` is precisely the SVRG estimator. EMGD can be viewed as an epoch-based, projected version of SVRG, and this connection should be explicitly discussed.

3.  **Practicality Concerns and Lack of Experiments:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the smoothness (L) and strong convexity (λ) constants to set the number of iterations `T`. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, but it remains a significant practical barrier. More importantly, the paper lacks any empirical validation. Without experiments, it is difficult to assess the practical performance of EMGD. How does it compare against AGD, SGD, and SVRG in real-world scenarios? How sensitive is it to estimations of κ? An experimental section would have substantially strengthened the paper by demonstrating scenarios where its unique trade-offs are beneficial.

4.  **Novelty of Technique:** While the goal is novel, the techniques employed are a combination of existing ideas. The epoch-based structure comes from Hazan and Kale (2011), the variance-reduced gradient is identical to SVRG, and the shrinking domain is a known technique in stochastic optimization. The contribution lies in the specific combination and analysis, but the building blocks are not new.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   **Analysis:** The proof sketch in Section 4 is clear. The key is that by restricting the iterates `w_kt` to a ball `B(w̄_k, Δ_k)`, the term `||w_kt - w̄_k||` is bounded, which in turn bounds the variance of the gradient estimator `∇f_kt(w_kt) - ∇f_kt(w̄_k)`. This control of variance allows for linear progress within an epoch. The logic is sound.

*   **Clarity of Claims:** The claim in the abstract and introduction that the number of full gradients is "independent from the condition number" is the main selling point and is technically true. However, it is slightly misleading without immediately mentioning the κ² cost transferred to the stochastic oracle. A more balanced presentation of the trade-off in the introduction would be beneficial.

*   **Potential Niche:** The most compelling case for this algorithm is for problems that are not finite sums (e.g., true expectation minimization) and involve complex constraints where projection is feasible. In this setting, methods like SAG/SAGA/SDCA do not apply, and EMGD offers a path to linear convergence that standard SGD does not. This niche should be highlighted more strongly as the primary contribution over existing work.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and well-written contribution to convex optimization. The core idea of achieving a full-gradient complexity independent of κ is intriguing and novel. However, the practical significance of the result is limited by the heavy O(κ²) dependence in the stochastic gradient complexity, making it likely uncompetitive with state-of-the-art methods like SVRG/SAGA for the common finite-sum setting. The lack of experimental validation further makes it difficult to gauge its real-world utility. While the theoretical ideas are interesting, the paper in its current form feels incomplete as a contribution to a top-tier conference like NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Borderline Reject. The paper has a solid theoretical core but significant weaknesses in terms of practical competitiveness and empirical validation. I would be willing to reconsider if the authors could provide compelling experimental evidence of a setting where EMGD outperforms relevant baselines (e.g., SVRG for unconstrained problems, or projected SGD for constrained ones) or provide a stronger motivation for why the κ² dependence is acceptable in certain important problem classes.
