## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex functions. The key idea is to strategically combine full and stochastic gradients within epochs to achieve a linear convergence rate while reducing the number of expensive full gradient evaluations from the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), independent of the condition number κ. The authors provide a theoretical analysis supporting their claims and compare EMGD favorably to existing methods in specific scenarios.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant problem:** Reducing the computational cost of full gradient evaluations is crucial for large-scale optimization problems, particularly in machine learning. The dependence on the condition number is a significant bottleneck, and this paper directly tackles that issue.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework is a clever idea, effectively leveraging the strengths of both approaches.  The approach of mixing gradients within an epoch, while building on the epoch gradient descent idea, is a non-trivial contribution.
* **Theoretical guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating linear convergence and the reduction in full gradient evaluations. The proof appears well-structured (though detailed inspection is difficult within the review timeframe).
* **Comparison to existing methods:**  The authors carefully compare EMGD to related work, including full-gradient methods, stochastic gradient methods, and other linear convergence algorithms like SAG and SDCA.  The table in Section 3.4 provides a useful overview.
* **Discussion of practical considerations:** The authors acknowledge the limitation of requiring knowledge of the condition number and suggest potential approaches for estimation in future work. They also highlight the applicability to machine learning problems with regularizers.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Condition number dependency in stochastic gradient count:** While the *number of full gradients* is independent of κ, the number of *stochastic gradients* scales as O(κ² log(1/ε)). This might be problematic if stochastic gradient evaluation is also computationally expensive, potentially negating the benefits of reducing full gradient evaluations. The paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss this trade-off.
* **Practicality of parameter tuning:** The algorithm has several parameters (step size, initial domain size, number of iterations per epoch, number of epochs) that require careful tuning. The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice.  The dependence on parameters like δ in Theorem 1 suggests sensitivity to parameter choices.
* **Limited empirical evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical validation.  While the theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the practical performance of EMGD on real-world datasets is crucial to establish its effectiveness.  Without experiments, it’s hard to assess the constant factors hidden in the asymptotic analysis.
* **Clarity of some technical details:**  The notation is sometimes dense, and the derivation in the analysis section could benefit from more explanation. For example, the connection between Lemma 2 and bounding BT could be made more explicit.
* **Related work could be more comprehensive:** While the authors cover many relevant papers, the rapidly evolving field of stochastic optimization warrants a more exhaustive review of recent advancements, particularly those involving variance reduction techniques beyond the methods explicitly mentioned.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper presents a technically sound algorithm with rigorous theoretical analysis. The claims are well-supported, and the work appears complete within its scope. However, the lack of empirical validation is a significant drawback.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The writing is generally clear, but the density of the notation and the lack of detailed explanations in the analysis section hinder readability. 
* **Originality (8/10):** The combination of full and stochastic gradients in the proposed epoch-based framework is a novel approach. The paper builds upon existing ideas (epoch gradient descent, variance reduction) but introduces a unique and potentially valuable contribution.
* **Significance (7/10):** The potential to reduce the computational cost of full gradient evaluations is significant. If the algorithm performs well in practice, it could have a considerable impact on large-scale optimization problems. However, the dependence of the stochastic gradient count on κ limits the overall significance.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees.
* Addresses an important problem in optimization.
* Offers a potentially significant improvement over existing methods in specific scenarios.
* Well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks empirical validation.
* The scalability of the algorithm is limited by the O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient complexity.
* Parameter tuning may be challenging in practice.
* Some technical details could be clarified.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with a request for significant revisions.  The paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, but the lack of empirical validation is a major concern. The authors should prioritize adding experimental results to demonstrate the practical performance of EMGD and investigate the trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations more thoroughly. Addressing the clarity issues and providing more guidance on parameter tuning would also strengthen the paper.  If the authors can address these concerns, the paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
