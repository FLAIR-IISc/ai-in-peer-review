

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by mixing full and stochastic gradients. The authors build on the observation that while full gradients yield faster convergence, they're expensive for large datasets, while stochastic gradients are cheap but lead to slower convergence. EMGD divides optimization into epochs, using one full gradient per epoch and O(κ²) stochastic gradients, achieving linear convergence with condition-number-independent full gradient access.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel approach:** The mixed gradient idea is well-motivated and represents a meaningful combination of deterministic and stochastic techniques. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from related work like SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's accelerated methods.

2. **Thorough theoretical analysis:** The convergence proof is detailed and rigorous, with careful handling of the stochastic component using martingale inequalities. The analysis of the mixed gradient's properties is particularly strong.

3. **Clear comparative advantages:** The paper effectively highlights several practical advantages over existing methods: applicability to constrained problems (unlike SAG/SDCA), lower storage requirements (Ω(d) vs Ω(n)), and high-probability convergence guarantees (unlike SAG/SDCA which only guarantee convergence in expectation).

4. **Practical relevance:** The discussion of the computational cost comparison (Table 2) and the connection to machine learning problems (with regularized objectives) makes the theoretical results more accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Misleading claim about condition-number independence:** The paper claims "the number of full gradients required is reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), independent from the condition number." However, the stochastic gradient count is O(κ² log(1/ε)), so the total computational cost depends on κ. The paper should clarify that the *number* of full gradients is independent of κ, but the *total computational cost* still depends on κ (through the stochastic gradient count). This is a critical distinction that needs to be more carefully articulated.

2. **Practical limitation of requiring known κ:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ beforehand, which is often not known in practice. The paper mentions future work on estimating κ, but this limitation significantly affects practical applicability. The paper should provide more concrete discussion of how this limitation might be addressed in practice.

3. **Overstated comparison with SAG/SDCA:** The computational complexity comparison in Table 2 assumes full gradients are exactly n times more expensive than stochastic gradients. In reality, the cost ratio is typically Θ(n), not exactly n, which makes the comparison less precise. The paper should clarify this assumption.

4. **Restrictive strong convexity assumption:** The paper assumes each f_i is λ-strongly convex to rewrite the problem, which limits applicability. Many machine learning problems don't satisfy this assumption, and the paper doesn't discuss how this might be addressed.

5. **Probability guarantee issue:** The convergence guarantee holds with probability at least 1-mδ, where m is the number of epochs. As m increases (to achieve better accuracy), the probability of success decreases, which is a practical limitation compared to methods with constant probability guarantees.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to optimization with a novel algorithm that achieves linear convergence with condition-number-independent full gradient access. The theoretical contributions are sound, and the paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of hybrid optimization methods.

The main weakness is the misleading claim about condition-number independence, which needs to be clarified. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More precise language about the condition-number dependence of the *total* computational cost
2. A more thorough discussion of the practical challenges in estimating κ
3. Clarification of the assumptions needed for the strong convexity requirement

The significance of this work is moderate. While the algorithm provides a theoretically interesting approach, the practical advantages over SAG and SDCA are limited to specific regimes (κ ≤ n^{2/3}), and the requirement to know κ beforehand is a significant practical limitation. The paper would be stronger if it provided more concrete empirical results showing the practical advantage of EMGD over existing methods.

**Recommendation:**
The paper has clear theoretical contributions but has some important limitations that need to be addressed before it can be accepted. I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions: (1) clarify the condition-number independence claim to emphasize it only applies to the *number* of full gradients, not the total computational cost; (2) provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of requiring known κ; (3) strengthen the comparison with existing methods by addressing the exact cost ratio assumption. With these revisions, the paper would make a solid contribution to the literature.
