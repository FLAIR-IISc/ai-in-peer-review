

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth strongly convex optimization, claiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by incorporating stochastic gradients. The authors argue that this results in computational efficiency improvements when the condition number κ is large, particularly when the objective function is a sum of n functions.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a novel algorithmic approach that combines full and stochastic gradients, which is a reasonable idea for reducing full gradient evaluations. The theoretical analysis establishes a linear convergence rate, which is a significant improvement over the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) rate. The paper also provides a clear comparison with existing methods, noting advantages in handling constrained problems, storage requirements, and convergence guarantees (high probability vs. expectation).

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising theoretical claims, several critical technical issues undermine the paper's validity:

1. **Fundamental flaw in complexity analysis:** The paper claims "the computational complexity of EMGD is lower than Nesterov's algorithm [17] as long as the condition number κ ≤ n^(2/3)". However, if a full gradient is n times more expensive than a stochastic gradient, EMGD's total cost is O(n log(1/ε) + κ² log(1/ε)), while Nesterov's cost is O(√κ log(1/ε)). For EMGD to be better, we require n + κ² < √κ, which is impossible for κ > 1. The paper's analysis fails to demonstrate this comparison correctly, suggesting a critical misunderstanding of computational complexity.

2. **Confusing probability parameter:** In Theorem 1, the condition δ ≤ e^(-1/2) (approximately 0.6065) is stated, but δ typically represents a small failure probability (e.g., 0.01). This is likely a typo, but it creates confusion about the probability guarantee. The authors should clarify whether this should be δ ≤ e^(-1/2) (meaning δ is small) or δ ≥ e^(-1/2) (which would be unusual).

3. **Inconsistent notation:** The paper uses both "ǫ" and "ε" to represent the optimization error, which is inconsistent and unprofessional.

4. **Missing table:** The paper references "Table 2" in Section 3.4 but does not include the table in the submission, making the comparison claims difficult to verify.

5. **Reference formatting:** Several references have incomplete information (e.g., "IEEE Transactions on Information Theory,, , , "), which is unprofessional for a NIPS submission.

6. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims "the number of full gradients required is reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε))" without acknowledging that the number of stochastic gradients increases to O(κ² log(1/ε)). For ill-conditioned problems (large κ), this could make the algorithm impractical despite the reduced full gradient count.

7. **Proof details:** The proof (Section 4.2) contains a potential error in Equation (14), where the bound on the martingale difference seems to be missing a factor of η. The authors should clarify this step.

**Conclusion:** While the paper's core idea has merit, the technical flaws in the complexity analysis, inconsistent notation, and unprofessional formatting significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The fundamental flaw in the computational complexity comparison is particularly concerning, as it suggests the authors may not fully understand the trade-offs they're claiming. The paper would benefit from a complete revision to address these issues before being considered for publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The technical errors are substantial enough to invalidate the main claims, and the paper does not demonstrate sufficient understanding of the complexity trade-offs it purports to address. The authors should thoroughly revise the complexity analysis and fix the notation inconsistencies before resubmission.
