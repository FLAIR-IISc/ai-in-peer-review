Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for minimizing L-smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives with access to both a full gradient oracle and a “function oracle” that induces stochastic gradients. EMGD runs in epochs: at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at an anchor point w̄k; within the epoch it performs T projected “mixed-gradient” steps using the estimator ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), and shrinks a trust region B(w̄k;Δk) by a factor √2 per epoch. With T = Θ(κ2 log(1/δ)) and stepsize η = 1/(L√T), EMGD halves the suboptimality per epoch with high probability, yielding O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations.

Relation to prior work: The estimator is exactly the variance-reduced SVRG control variate (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013); the algorithm also shares the “one full gradient per epoch” structure with SVRG. The paper compares to Nesterov, SAG, and SDCA, but does not cite SVRG/SAGA/Prox-SVRG/Katyusha, nor place the contribution versus those methods.

Strengths
- Clear goal: reducing the number of full gradient computations to O(log(1/ε)) independent of κ.
- High-probability linear convergence analysis (under a shrinking ball) is provided.
- Memory footprint O(d), and the approach naturally extends to constrained problems via projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k;Δk).
- The mixed gradient estimator is unbiased for ∇F(wt), though the paper does not emphasize this.
- The proof structure (martingale concentration + variance control via smoothness and radius shrinkage) is conceptually clean.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Major novelty gap:
- The estimator and outer/inner epoch structure are essentially SVRG. EMGD differs mainly by (i) forcing a projection to a shrinking ball and (ii) using a conservative stepsize η=1/(L√T). This yields O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, which is substantially worse than SVRG’s O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) in finite-sum settings (or O(κ log(1/ε)) oracle calls in streaming variants). The claimed advantage “full gradients independent of κ” is exactly what SVRG already provides (one full gradient per epoch). The lack of citation and head-to-head theoretical comparison is a critical omission.

Incorrect definitions and mathematical slips:
- Equation (4) is not a correct characterization of strong convexity. Strong convexity implies ⟨∇F(w)−∇F(w′), w−w′⟩ ≥ λ‖w−w′‖2, not ‖∇F(w)−∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w−w′‖. The latter is a much stronger and generally false assumption.
- Several factors of 1/2 associated with L-smoothness inequalities are missing (e.g., using L‖·‖2 instead of (L/2)‖·‖2), and there are duplicated equation indices (two “(2)”s).
- The update in Algorithm 1 appears malformed: “+ 1 ‖w−wkt‖2” is dimensionally wrong; it should be + (1/2)‖w−wkt‖2 (or 1/(2η)-scaled), as used later in the analysis.
- The condition “T ≥ 1152 L2/λ2 ln δ” has the wrong sign; it should be ln(1/δ). This mistake appears repeatedly.
- The proof bounds mix L-smoothness constants loosely; several steps (e.g., (16)) drop 1/2 constants.

Assumptions/practicality:
- The method requires knowing κ to set T and η, and requires a valid initial radius Δ1 ≥ √(2/λ)(F(0)−F(w∗)), which depends on w∗. This is acknowledged but not resolved; the suggested remedies are speculative and not analyzed.
- The shrinking-ball projection per inner iteration adds nontrivial computational overhead, especially for constrained W. No discussion of projection cost or feasibility for common W beyond “if it can be solved efficiently.”
- The large constants (e.g., 1152) and conservative stepsize η=1/(L√T) make the practical utility questionable.

Comparisons and claims:
- The statement that “SAG/SDCA linear convergence holds only in expectation, whereas EMGD is high-probability” is overstated; high-probability or almost-sure variants exist for variance-reduced methods under standard assumptions, and SDCA achieves O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) without requiring each fi to be strongly convex (smoothness of losses suffices; strong convexity can come from the sum). The claim that SDCA requires each fi to be λ-strongly convex is misleading.
- The “distributed full gradient” advantage is not unique to EMGD; SVRG and related methods can parallelize the full-gradient pass similarly.
- Tables 1 and 2 are referenced but not included; several quantitative comparisons rely on these.

Clarity/formatting/presentation:
- Numerous typos (e.g., “epoches”, “interstage”), inconsistent notation (B(x; r) vs B(x, r)), broken footnote markers, missing/duplicated equation numbers, and incomplete references (missing years/venues). These impede readability and reproducibility.
- The related work omits key variance-reduction literature (SVRG, SAGA, Prox-SVRG, MISO/Finito), which is central to positioning this contribution.
- No experiments to assess practical performance or to justify the shrinking-ball design vs standard variance-reduced methods.

Pros
- Simple algorithmic template; clear high-level objective (reducing full gradient calls).
- High-probability analysis technique might be of some independent interest.
- Applicability to constrained settings and low memory footprint.

Cons
- Substantial overlap with SVRG; inferior stochastic-gradient complexity (κ2 vs κ) due to conservative analysis/design.
- Incorrect definition of strong convexity and multiple mathematical/typographical errors.
- Requires κ and a priori bound on Δ1; added projection overhead each iteration.
- Missing key related work and misleading statements about SDCA/SAG; absent empirical validation.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: High

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the definition of strong convexity and fix all scaling constants and typos; include the missing tables.
- Reposition the work relative to SVRG/SAGA/Prox-SVRG, clearly stating the novelty. If the high-probability guarantee under constrained domains is the main contribution, emphasize that and compare theoretically.
- Consider dropping the shrinking ball and using standard variance-reduction analysis to obtain O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients with a constant stepsize.
- Provide experiments comparing against SVRG/SAGA/SDCA/Nesterov on synthetic and real problems, with wall-clock and projection costs reported.
- Address parameter selection without knowing κ and Δ1 (e.g., doubling/halving schedules, adaptive estimates).
