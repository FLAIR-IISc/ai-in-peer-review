

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel first-order optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations needed from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by mixing full and stochastic gradients. The authors observe that while standard first-order methods with full gradients achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity, stochastic methods typically have slower convergence. EMGD addresses this by using one full gradient per epoch and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, achieving linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients.

The paper builds on the epoch gradient descent framework [9] and relates to recent advances in variance-reduced stochastic methods like SAG [22] and SDCA [23]. It distinguishes itself by claiming condition-number-independent full gradient access while maintaining linear convergence, though at the cost of higher stochastic gradient complexity.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by formally establishing a method to reduce full gradient evaluations while maintaining linear convergence. The analysis is rigorous, with careful attention to the probability bounds and dependencies on problem parameters.

2. **Novel Algorithm Design**: The mixed gradient construction (g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)) cleverly combines full and stochastic gradients while maintaining convergence guarantees through the shrinking domain constraint.

3. **Practical Considerations**: The paper acknowledges important practical aspects: the algorithm works for both constrained and unconstrained problems, requires only O(d) storage (vs. O(n) for SAG/SDCA), and can be distributed to reduce full gradient computation time.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable Practical Advantage**: The claimed advantage of O(log(1/ε)) full gradients comes at the cost of O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This is problematic because:
   - Standard SVRG achieves O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations with similar full gradient access (one full gradient per epoch), making EMGD's stochastic gradient complexity O(κ) higher.
   - The paper's computational complexity comparison (Table 2) assumes full gradient is n times more expensive than stochastic gradient, but for large n, O(κ²) vs O(κ) stochastic gradients becomes a significant penalty.

2. **Condition Number Dependence**: The algorithm requires knowledge of κ to set the number of iterations per epoch (T ≥ 1152L²/λ² ln(1/δ)). While the paper acknowledges this as a limitation, it's a critical practical drawback since κ is typically unknown in practice. The proposed solution (estimating κ via sampling) is vague and may not be feasible for large-scale problems.

3. **Comparison to SVRG**: The paper doesn't adequately address how EMGD compares to SVRG [12], which also achieves linear convergence with O(κ log(1/ε)) complexity and O(1) full gradients per epoch. The paper's claim of "reducing the number of full gradients" is misleading because SVRG already uses one full gradient per epoch (like EMGD), but with much better stochastic gradient complexity.

4. **Theoretical Limitations**: The condition δ ≤ e^{-1/2} (in Equation 5) is quite restrictive, and the high probability guarantee (1-mδ) requires careful tuning of δ for each epoch, which may not be practical.

## Significance and Originality

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach, the practical significance is questionable. The O(κ²) factor in stochastic gradient complexity significantly undermines the claimed advantage over existing methods. The work doesn't represent a substantial improvement over SVRG, which has become the standard for such problems.

The paper's novelty is in the mixed gradient construction, but this is a relatively minor variation on existing variance-reduction techniques. The authors don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from SVRG or other similar methods, which makes the contribution seem incremental.

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, the claimed advantage is not meaningful in practice due to the O(κ²) stochastic gradient complexity. The comparison with existing methods is superficial and misleading, particularly with respect to SVRG. The paper would need to demonstrate a clear practical advantage or significantly better theoretical complexity to be competitive with existing state-of-the-art methods.

The paper makes a minor theoretical contribution but fails to demonstrate a meaningful improvement over existing approaches that would justify its publication in NeurIPS. The claimed "condition number independent access" is misleading because while full gradient access is independent of κ, the stochastic gradient access is highly dependent on κ², which is the dominant term in practice.

For future work, the authors should either:
1) Show a method with O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (like SVRG) but with condition-number-independent full gradient access, or
2) Demonstrate a practical advantage for specific problem classes where κ is small but n is large.

Without these improvements, the paper does not advance the state of the art in a meaningful way.
