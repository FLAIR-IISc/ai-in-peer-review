This paper addresses the problem of minimizing a smooth and strongly convex function in a setting where both full gradients and cheaper stochastic gradients are accessible. The primary goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, which for standard accelerated methods is dependent on the condition number κ. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which operates in epochs. In each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at the epoch's starting point and then performs a series of updates using a "mixed gradient" that combines this fixed full gradient with a stochastic correction term. The main theoretical result shows that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution using O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients, thereby making the number of full gradient calls independent of the condition number κ.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Problem Framing and Goal:** The paper's primary strength is its explicit goal of minimizing the number of full gradient evaluations, making it independent of the condition number. This is a well-motivated and practically relevant problem, especially for ill-conditioned large-scale optimization. The trade-off between a few expensive full gradients and many cheap stochastic gradients is a key theme in modern optimization, and this paper provides a new perspective on it.

2.  **Algorithm Design:** The proposed EMGD algorithm is an elegant combination of several ideas: the epoch-based structure from Hazan and Kale (2011), a variance-reduction-like gradient estimator, and a shrinking-domain projection step. The intuition that the full gradient acts as an anchor and the stochastic updates provide local refinement within a shrinking ball is clear and compelling.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** The paper provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation results common for many stochastic methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA). Achieving a linear convergence rate (logarithmic dependence on 1/ε) is a significant theoretical accomplishment.

4.  **Practical Considerations:** The discussion in Section 3.4 highlights important practical advantages over finite-sum methods like SAG and SDCA. The ability to handle general constraints (provided the projection is efficient), the low O(d) memory requirement, and the potential for straightforward distributed computation of the full gradient are all valid and important points.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Omission of Critical Related Work (Originality):** The most significant weakness is the failure to cite and compare against the highly relevant family of stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) methods. The "mixed gradient" `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the gradient estimator used in SVRG (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013) and is conceptually very similar to that in SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014). These methods were developed specifically for the finite-sum setting `F(w) = (1/n)∑fi(w)` and also achieve linear convergence.

    This omission critically impacts the paper's originality claim. EMGD can be viewed as a variant of SVRG. However, its analysis and resulting complexity appear to be substantially worse.

2.  **High Stochastic Gradient Complexity (Quality & Significance):** The number of required stochastic gradients is O(κ² log 1/ε). This quadratic dependence on the condition number is extremely high and is a major drawback. For comparison, SVRG achieves a total complexity of O((n + κ) log 1/ε) stochastic gradient evaluations. In the finite-sum setting, one EMGD epoch costs `n` (for the full gradient) plus `T ≈ O(κ²)` stochastic gradients. For SVRG, an epoch costs `n` plus `O(κ)` stochastic gradients. The O(κ²) factor makes EMGD uncompetitive with SVRG for any reasonably ill-conditioned problem (κ >> 1), which is the very setting the paper aims to address. The claim of improving upon the `O(√κ)` full gradient complexity of Nesterov's method is weakened when the cost is shifted to a `O(κ²)` stochastic complexity.

3.  **Suboptimal Analysis:** The O(κ²) dependence seems to arise from a loose analysis rather than an inherent property of the gradient estimator. The proof bounds the variance term `‖∇gt(wt)‖²` and the martingale difference term using the diameter of the search ball `∆`. This contrasts with the standard SVRG analysis, which uses a carefully constructed potential function to show a geometric reduction in variance, leading to an `O(κ)` dependence for the inner loop length. It is highly probable that a tighter analysis, perhaps borrowing techniques from the SVRG literature, could improve the dependence from O(κ²) to O(κ), which would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

4.  **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The algorithm requires setting `T`, the number of iterations per epoch, to be at least `O(κ²)`. This quadratic dependence on a typically unknown quantity `κ` makes the algorithm very difficult to tune in practice. While the authors mention estimating κ as future work, the sensitivity to this hyperparameter is a practical limitation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The goal of making full gradient evaluations independent of κ is important and well-motivated.
*   The algorithm can handle general convex constraints, unlike standard SAG/SVRG/SDCA.
*   The high-probability convergence guarantee is theoretically stronger than in-expectation results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The work fails to acknowledge or compare against SVRG, which uses the same gradient estimator and achieves a significantly better theoretical complexity. This is a major gap in the related work and undermines the claim of originality.
*   The O(κ²) stochastic gradient complexity is uncompetitive and makes the method impractical for the ill-conditioned problems it targets.
*   The analysis appears to be suboptimal compared to existing techniques for similar algorithms.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper addresses an interesting and important problem with an elegant algorithmic idea, its contribution is critically diminished by the omission of SVRG and related methods. The proposed EMGD algorithm is essentially a variant of SVRG, but with a significantly worse theoretical complexity (O(κ²) vs. O(κ) for the inner loop). This makes the proposed method less efficient than the existing state-of-the-art for the widely-studied finite-sum problem. Although the method's applicability to general constraints and its high-probability bound are noteworthy, these advantages do not outweigh the fundamental issues of originality and suboptimal complexity. Therefore, in its current form, the paper is not ready for publication at NIPS. A major revision would be required, including a thorough comparison with variance-reduced methods and a significant improvement of the complexity analysis.
