Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization with access to (i) a full gradient oracle Og and (ii) a “function” oracle Of that returns a random smooth component f with F(w) = E[f(w)]. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) runs in epochs. Each epoch computes one full gradient at a reference point w̄k, and then performs T inner steps using a “mixed” gradient g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), with projections onto a shrinking Euclidean ball centered at w̄k. The analysis shows a linear rate: to reach ε accuracy, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient computations (via Of), with high probability.

The paper places this between full-gradient methods (e.g., Nesterov acceleration with O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients) and stochastic methods (with polynomial-in-1/ε rates). It also compares to recent finite-sum methods with linear rates (SAG, SDCA).

The mixed gradient estimator is essentially the variance-reduced estimator used in SVRG-style methods. In the finite-sum setting F = (1/n)∑ fi, SAG [Roux et al., NIPS 2012], SDCA [Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013], and SVRG [Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013] achieve linear convergence with overall gradient costs on the order of (n + κ) log(1/ε), with limited memory (SVRG) and without shrinking-domain projections. The paper does not cite SVRG or SAGA [Defazio et al., NIPS 2014], which are very closely related and important for positioning this contribution.

Strengths
- Clear presentation of an epoch-based method with a clean high-probability analysis using martingale concentration.
- The idea of decoupling the count of full gradients from κ (one per epoch) can be attractive when full gradients are expensive and stochastic gradients are cheap and readily available.
- Works for constrained problems (via projection) and uses only O(d) memory.
- Provides explicit constants and a transparent proof structure.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and positioning: The mixed gradient estimator is the same as SVRG’s; the paper omits SVRG (and SAGA) from related work and comparisons. In the finite-sum setting, EMGD appears to be a variant of SVRG with ball projections and a more conservative analysis.
- Complexity suboptimality: The inner-epoch length T scales as Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)), yielding O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients overall. This is substantially worse than the O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) (finite-sum) or O(κ log(1/ε)) (per-pass) behavior established for variance-reduced methods. Even relative to plain accelerated full-gradient descent, κ^2 can be much larger than n√κ in many regimes, making EMGD unattractive computationally unless κ is small.
- Oracle model and practicality: The function-oracle assumption (the ability to query the same random f at multiple points within an inner loop) matches the finite-sum case (sampling an index i and reusing it), but is less standard in general stochastic optimization. The paper emphasizes general expectation oracles yet relies on being able to form ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k) for the same f; this is not available from a standard unbiased stochastic-gradient oracle that returns an independent sample each time.
- Dependence on κ: While the number of full gradients is independent of κ, the algorithm requires knowing κ to set T and step sizes. The suggested heuristics for estimating κ are informal.
- No empirical evaluation: There are no experiments to validate the theory, compare wall-clock performance, or assess sensitivity to κ, δ, and the projection radius schedule. Given the claimed practical motivation (reducing full-gradient costs), this is a significant omission.
- Claims about “high probability” versus “in expectation” are somewhat overstated. Comparable high-probability guarantees exist (or can be derived) for variance-reduced methods with slight modifications; the advantage is not decisively demonstrated.

Quality
The analysis appears mathematically careful, though constants are loose and the κ^2 dependence is pessimistic. The comparisons section is incomplete and misses closely related contemporaneous work (SVRG/SAGA). Practical implications are not substantiated empirically.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Algorithm 1 and the proof sketch are readable. However:
- The oracle model and its relation to standard finite-sum and streaming settings should be clarified.
- The rationale and necessity of the shrinking-ball projections deserve more discussion, as they are not typical in related methods and may complicate implementation.
- The comparisons would benefit from a uniform cost model and clearer accounting of full vs. stochastic gradient costs.

Originality
Limited. The estimator and epoch structure align closely with variance-reduction schemes developed contemporaneously or earlier (SVRG). The use of a shrinking trust-region projection and a high-probability analysis is a modest stylistic variation, but the core idea is not new, and the theoretical guarantees are weaker in κ.

Significance
Given the weaker κ dependence and lack of experiments, the practical impact is questionable relative to established methods (SAG/SDCA/SVRG/SAGA). The constrained-setting applicability and low memory are positives, but SVRG also enjoys low memory and can handle simple constraints/proximal terms. Without tighter bounds (e.g., reducing κ^2 to κ) or compelling empirical evidence, the contribution seems incremental.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Can the κ^2 factor in T be improved to κ under your analysis, perhaps by avoiding the shrinking-ball step and leveraging standard variance-reduction lemmas?
- How does EMGD relate precisely to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013)? Please compare algorithms and guarantees and state any advantages EMGD offers.
- In the non-finite-sum stochastic setting, how is the “same f” reused within an epoch? If Of returns independent draws each call, g̃t is no longer unbiased for ∇F(wt). Please formalize the oracle.
- What is the cost of the projection step for common constraints, and how sensitive is the method to the Δk schedule?
- Can you provide experiments comparing EMGD to accelerated GD, SVRG, SAG, and SDCA across varying n and κ?

Pros
- Clear, rigorous high-probability analysis
- Low memory, simple per-iteration operations
- Potentially useful in constrained problems

Cons
- Overlaps with known variance-reduction methods; missing key related work
- Suboptimal κ^2 dependence; likely inferior compute complexity in many regimes
- Oracle model practicality unclear
- No empirical validation
- Requires prior knowledge of κ

Recommendation
I lean to reject. The paper’s central idea is not sufficiently novel relative to established variance-reduction methods, the theoretical guarantees are weaker in their κ dependence, the practical oracle assumption is unclear outside finite-sum, and the lack of experiments makes it difficult to justify significance. Tightening the analysis to O(κ) inner complexity, clarifying the oracle, positioning against SVRG/SAGA with thorough experiments, and detailing constrained/proximal extensions would substantially strengthen the submission.
