Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization with access to two oracles: (i) a full gradient oracle for F, and (ii) a “function” oracle that returns a random smooth function f with Ef[f(w)] = F(w), enabling stochastic gradients. The main aim is to reduce the number of full-gradient evaluations from the optimal O(√κ log(1/ε)) for first-order deterministic methods to O(log(1/ε)), independent of the condition number κ = L/λ, by supplementing with many stochastic gradients. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) computes one full gradient per epoch, runs T inner iterations using a “mixed gradient” ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄), projects onto a shrinking ball around the anchor w̄, averages the iterates, and halves the ball radius per epoch. The analysis yields a linear convergence rate with O(log(1/ε)) calls to the full-gradient oracle and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle (high probability).

The algorithmic idea of variance reduction via an anchored full gradient plus a correction term ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is closely related to finite-sum variance-reduction methods such as SAG (Roux et al. 2012), SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang 2013), and especially SVRG (Johnson & Zhang 2013), which also compute a full gradient periodically and use the same mixed gradient estimator. In comparison, SAG/SDCA achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) or O(n log(1/ε)) complexities; classical SVRG achieves O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(d) memory, without shrinking-domain projections. The paper cites SAG/SDCA but omits SVRG and later SAGA/SVRG variants; this is a significant gap because EMGD’s estimator essentially matches SVRG’s, while the complexity T = Θ(κ^2) is substantially worse than the Θ(κ) inner-iteration lengths typical in SVRG/SAGA analyses.

Strengths
- Clear objective: reduce dependence on κ for full-gradient evaluations by mixing full and stochastic gradients.
- High-probability linear convergence guarantee, with a simple proof structure (epoch-based, martingale concentration).
- Works with a function oracle that allows two-point evaluation of the same f, matching practical finite-sum settings; low memory overhead.
- Discussion of constrained settings via projection onto W ∩ ball is potentially useful.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: The core mixed-gradient estimator is essentially identical to SVRG’s; the main claimed novelty (κ-independent number of full gradients) is already achieved by SVRG (one full gradient per epoch, hence O(log(1/ε)) full gradients). The paper does not cite or compare to SVRG, and its complexity is weaker: O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients vs. O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) for SVRG/SAGA/SDCA in the finite-sum regime.
- Technical correctness/precision: The paper defines strong convexity as ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖, which is not the standard definition. Later, inequality (13) uses F(w) − F(w∗) ≤ ⟨∇F(w), w − w∗⟩ − λ‖w − w∗‖^2, whereas the usual bound has a factor λ/2. These issues affect constants and detract from rigor.
- Algorithmic burden: Requires knowledge of κ to set T and η, and uses a shrinking ball whose initial radius must upper bound the distance to the optimum. This can be impractical and is not required by competing methods (SVRG/SAGA), which use simple step-size/epoch schedules or doubling tricks.
- Empirical validation: None. Given the method is largely a variant of known VR schemes with worse dependence on κ, experiments are important to justify practical benefits (e.g., constrained settings).
- Comparisons: The finite-sum comparisons omit key methods (SVRG, SAGA), and the claimed advantages over SAG/SDCA (storage, constraints, high-probability guarantees) are at least partially shared by SVRG/SAGA.

Quality
- The high-level analysis via martingale concentration is standard and plausible, but the strong convexity misstatement and factor-of-2 issue should be corrected. The κ^2 dependence appears to stem from the variance bound and the shrinking-domain device; tighter variance bounds (as in SVRG) can yield κ rather than κ^2.
- No experiments to support claims about computational advantages.

Clarity
- Generally readable and organized, but there are nonstandard definitions, typos (“epoches”), and some proofs rely on imprecise statements (see above). Step 10 averages T+1 iterates; a brief justification would help. A clearer discussion of the function oracle and its realization in finite-sum problems would aid readers.

Originality
- Limited. The estimator and epoch structure mirror SVRG; the shrinking projection is a twist but not clearly beneficial and worsens dependence on κ. Missing related work undermines the claim of novelty.

Significance
- As written, the contribution is incremental at best and, in complexity, strictly weaker than existing variance-reduced methods. Without improved bounds, broader applicability, or compelling experiments (e.g., constrained problems where SVRG/SAGA are hard to apply), the impact is limited.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please compare explicitly with SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013) and SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014) and clarify what EMGD offers beyond these, both theoretically and practically.
- Can the κ^2 factor be improved to κ by tightening the variance analysis and/or removing the shrinking ball, following SVRG-style proofs?
- Can you remove the need to know κ via doubling/epoch adaptation? Can you estimate the anchor radius automatically?
- Add experiments on finite-sum problems (including constrained cases) comparing to accelerated GD, SVRG/SAGA, SAG, and SDCA under equal gradient cost models.
- Please fix the strong convexity definition and all inequalities depending on it; use the standard definition (or Hessian bounds) to avoid confusion.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean high-probability analysis of a mixed-gradient scheme; potentially useful for constrained settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Overlaps heavily with known VR methods; weaker complexity (κ^2 vs. κ); missing key related work; technical imprecision; no experiments.

Overall recommendation
Given the overlap with SVRG-like methods, weaker complexity guarantees, lack of empirical validation, and technical issues, I do not recommend acceptance in its current form. A revised version that situates the work within the variance-reduction literature, corrects the analysis, improves the κ dependence, and includes experiments could be much stronger.
