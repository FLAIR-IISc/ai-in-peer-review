

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that reduces the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by combining full and stochastic gradients. The algorithm divides optimization into epochs, each using one full gradient and O(κ²) stochastic gradients, achieving linear convergence. The authors compare EMGD with Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA, showing that EMGD can be computationally advantageous when κ ≤ n^(2/3) for problems of the form F(w) = (1/n)∑f_i(w).

The work builds on recent advances in convex optimization, particularly the epoch gradient descent (EGD) and semi-stochastic methods. It relates to NIPS papers like SAG (Roux et al., 2012) and SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013), which also achieve linear convergence for finite-sum problems, but EMGD differs by using mixed gradients and providing high-probability convergence guarantees.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by providing a novel algorithm that reduces the dependence on condition number κ for full gradient evaluations. The proof of linear convergence with high probability is rigorous and well-structured.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively frames the problem of reducing full gradient evaluations for ill-conditioned problems, which is a practical concern in machine learning.

3. **Comprehensive Comparison**: The authors provide a detailed theoretical comparison with relevant methods (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA), showing where EMGD has advantages. The discussion of computational complexity under different κ regimes is particularly valuable.

4. **Practical Insights**: The paper acknowledges important practical considerations, such as the need for κ to be known beforehand, and discusses potential approaches for estimating κ in machine learning contexts.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is entirely theoretical without any experimental results. While the theoretical analysis is sound, empirical validation would strengthen the practical relevance of the claims, especially regarding computational efficiency.

2. **Practical Assumption of Known κ**: The requirement to know κ beforehand is a significant limitation for practical applications. While the authors mention future work on estimating κ, the current approach lacks a practical method for this critical step.

3. **Computational Cost of Stochastic Gradients**: The O(κ²) stochastic gradient evaluations per epoch could be prohibitively expensive for large κ. The paper doesn't adequately address whether this tradeoff is beneficial in practice, especially compared to methods like SAG or SDCA that have lower per-iteration costs.

4. **Superficial Comparison with SAG/SDCA**: The comparison with SAG and SDCA is limited to theoretical computational complexity. A more detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges and empirical performance would strengthen the claims.

5. **Constrained Optimization Details**: The paper mentions that EMGD works for constrained problems but doesn't elaborate on how the constrained optimization in Step 8 is handled efficiently, which is crucial for practical implementation.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to convex optimization by introducing a novel algorithm that reduces the number of full gradient evaluations. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the comparison with existing methods is thorough. However, the lack of empirical validation and the practical assumption of knowing κ beforehand significantly limit the paper's impact.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Include Empirical Results**: Add experiments on standard machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression, ridge regression) to demonstrate the practical benefits of EMGD compared to existing methods.

2. **Address κ Estimation**: Develop and test a practical method for estimating κ in the context of machine learning problems. This could involve sampling-based approaches as mentioned in the conclusion.

3. **Refine Computational Analysis**: Provide a more detailed analysis of the practical computational cost, including the tradeoff between full and stochastic gradient evaluations in different scenarios.

4. **Expand on Constrained Optimization**: Elaborate on how the constrained optimization in Step 8 can be efficiently implemented for common constrained problems in machine learning.

5. **Compare with Recent Methods**: Include a comparison with more recent methods like SVRG or SAGA, which have become standard for finite-sum optimization problems.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance
**Pro**:
- Novel theoretical contribution addressing a practical optimization challenge
- Rigorous proof of linear convergence with high probability
- Comprehensive comparison with relevant existing methods
- Clear identification of regimes where EMGD is advantageous

**Con**:
- No empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Practical assumption of known condition number κ
- Limited discussion of implementation challenges for constrained problems
- O(κ²) stochastic gradient evaluations could be prohibitively expensive in practice

## Final Recommendation
While the theoretical contribution is strong, the lack of empirical validation and the practical limitation of requiring known κ significantly weaken the paper's impact. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with the suggested improvements (particularly including empirical results and addressing the κ estimation problem), this could become a strong submission. The paper's theoretical insights are valuable, but the current version lacks the practical validation needed for NIPS.
