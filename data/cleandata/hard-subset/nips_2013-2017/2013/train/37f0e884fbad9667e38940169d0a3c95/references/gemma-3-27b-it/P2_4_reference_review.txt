## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the computational cost of solving strongly convex, smooth optimization problems, particularly when full gradient evaluations are expensive. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, aiming for a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradient accesses independent of the condition number (κ). The authors demonstrate theoretically that EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations to reach an ε-optimal solution. The paper also positions this work within the context of existing first-order methods and provides comparisons with algorithms like SAG and SDCA.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The problem of reducing the cost of full gradient evaluations in large-scale optimization is highly relevant, especially in machine learning.
* **Novel Approach:** The combination of epoch-based optimization with mixed gradient descent is a thoughtful approach to achieving condition number independence.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating linear convergence and bounding the number of full gradient accesses. This is a significant strength.
* **Comparison to Existing Work:** The authors clearly position their work relative to established algorithms (Nesterov’s acceleration, SAG, SDCA) and highlight the advantages and disadvantages of EMGD.  The discussion of computational complexity (Table 2) is helpful.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the need to know the condition number and suggest potential avenues for estimation.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from introduction to analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Condition Number Dependency Remains (in Stochastic Gradients):** While the *number* of full gradients is independent of κ, the number of stochastic gradients scales as κ². This means that if stochastic gradient evaluations are also expensive, the overall computational benefit might be limited. This tradeoff isn’t fully explored in terms of real-world impact.
* **Practicality of Domain Shrinkage:** The algorithm relies on shrinking the domain size at each epoch (∆<sub>k+1</sub> = ∆<sub>k</sub>/√2). This domain restriction, while theoretically sound, might pose challenges in practice, particularly if the optimal solution lies outside the shrinking domain. The paper doesn’t fully address how to handle such scenarios or the impact on convergence in practice.
* **Assumptions:** The algorithm relies on relatively strong assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity) and a function oracle providing unbiased stochastic gradients.  The robustness of the method to violations of these assumptions is not discussed.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any experimental results. While the theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the performance of EMGD on real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the contribution. The comparison with existing algorithms would be much more convincing with empirical evidence.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The algorithm seems more complex to implement than standard methods like stochastic gradient descent. This added complexity needs to be justified by significant performance gains.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the analysis appears rigorous. The theoretical results are well-supported. However, the lack of empirical validation weakens the overall quality. **Score: 8/10**
* **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is described in a straightforward manner. **Score: 9/10**
* **Originality:** The combination of epoch-based descent with mixed gradient evaluation is a novel contribution.  The problem addressed is well-motivated and the proposed solution is distinct from existing approaches. **Score: 8/10**
* **Significance:** The potential to reduce the cost of full gradient evaluations is significant, particularly in large-scale machine learning. However, the practical impact is uncertain without empirical validation and further analysis of the κ² dependence in stochastic gradients. **Score: 7/10**

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a relevant and important problem in optimization.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm.
* Provides a clear and comprehensive analysis.
* Well-positioned within the existing literature.

**Con:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* The κ² dependence in stochastic gradient evaluations may limit practical benefits.
* Potential challenges with the domain shrinkage approach.
* Increased implementation complexity.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with the caveat that the authors *must* include experimental results demonstrating the performance of EMGD on relevant datasets.  Specifically, experiments should:

1.  Compare EMGD to existing methods (SGD, SAG, SDCA) on various datasets with different condition numbers.
2.  Investigate the impact of the κ² term on the overall computational cost.
3.  Explore the robustness of the algorithm to violations of the smoothness and strong convexity assumptions.
4. Provide insights into the practical considerations of domain shrinkage.

Without experimental validation, the significance of the theoretical contributions remains uncertain.  The paper presents a promising approach, but its practical value needs to be demonstrated.
