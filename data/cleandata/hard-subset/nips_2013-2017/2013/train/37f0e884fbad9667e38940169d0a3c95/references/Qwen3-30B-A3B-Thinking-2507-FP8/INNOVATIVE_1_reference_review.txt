

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm that aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations needed for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The key insight is that by combining one full gradient evaluation with O(κ²) stochastic gradient evaluations per epoch, the algorithm achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (vs. O(√κ log(1/ε)) for standard methods), making the full gradient access independent of the condition number κ.

The paper's main contribution is a bold approach to a well-known problem in optimization: the computational cost of full gradient evaluations for large datasets. While previous work like SAG and SDCA achieve linear convergence for finite-sum problems, they require Ω(n) storage and only guarantee convergence in expectation. EMGD offers a different tradeoff: it requires only O(d) storage (where d is the dimension), achieves high-probability convergence, and works for both constrained and unconstrained problems. The paper convincingly demonstrates that EMGD can outperform Nesterov's accelerated method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), a reasonable regime in many machine learning applications.

The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with Theorem 1 providing a clear convergence guarantee. The proof strategy of bounding the stochastic term using Hoeffding-Azuma inequality is standard but well-executed, and the paper appropriately acknowledges the need to know κ in advance (though it suggests practical estimation approaches in the future work section).

**Strengths:**
- **Novel concept:** The mixed gradient approach is genuinely new and addresses a significant practical limitation in optimization.
- **Clear theoretical contribution:** The paper establishes a clean theoretical separation from existing methods (SAG/SDCA) with high-probability convergence guarantees.
- **Practical relevance:** The storage and convergence guarantees make the method appealing for real-world applications where memory is limited.
- **Well-organized presentation:** The paper is clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Computational cost tradeoff:** While the paper shows EMGD reduces full gradient access, it doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of the actual computational cost tradeoff (full vs. stochastic gradients). The claim that "this tradeoff could lead to a significant improvement in computational efficiency" needs more concrete justification.
- **Practical implementation details:** The paper doesn't address how to efficiently compute the full gradient in practice, which is crucial for the method's applicability.
- **Comparison with recent work:** The paper cites "very recent works" [9,10,21,26] but doesn't clearly distinguish EMGD from other mixed gradient methods that may have appeared in the literature.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Introduces a fundamentally new approach to a well-studied problem with significant theoretical and practical implications.
- Solves a concrete problem (reducing full gradient access) in a novel way that could impact large-scale machine learning.
- Theoretical results are clean and well-supported, with clear distinctions from existing methods.
- The paper is well-written and technically sound.

*Con:*
- The computational cost analysis is somewhat superficial; the paper doesn't quantify the actual speedup in practice.
- The assumption that κ is known in advance is a practical limitation that isn't fully addressed.
- The paper doesn't provide empirical validation to complement the theoretical results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and bold approach to a significant problem in optimization. While the computational cost tradeoff could be better justified and some practical implementation details are missing, the core idea is sufficiently novel and theoretically sound to warrant acceptance. The paper's main contribution is the conceptual shift of using mixed gradients to achieve condition-number-independent full gradient access, which is a meaningful advance in the field.

The paper aligns well with NIPS priorities by introducing a new theoretical framework that could inspire future work in optimization for machine learning. The novelty of the approach significantly outweighs the minor weaknesses in the computational cost analysis and the assumption about knowing κ.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is substantial, and the approach offers a fresh perspective on a long-standing problem in optimization. While empirical validation would strengthen the paper, the theoretical novelty and clear distinction from existing methods make this a strong candidate for NIPS. The paper successfully demonstrates that the number of full gradient evaluations can be reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) with a tradeoff in stochastic gradient evaluations, which is a meaningful theoretical advance with practical implications.
