## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for strongly convex smooth optimization, aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations by strategically combining them with stochastic gradients. The core idea of interleaving full and stochastic gradients within epochs to achieve a condition number independent iteration complexity is interesting and potentially impactful. However, the presentation lacks sufficient detail in certain areas, and the comparisons to existing methods feel somewhat selective. While the theoretical results are sound, the practical implications and potential limitations require further discussion. I lean towards **weak accept** with a strong recommendation for revisions addressing the points below.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in optimization: the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost. When dealing with ill-conditioned problems (high κ), full gradient methods become computationally expensive. EMGD attempts to bridge the gap by leveraging cheaper stochastic gradients to reduce the reliance on full gradients while maintaining a linear convergence rate.  The authors correctly position their work within the context of existing first-order methods, summarizing complexities for full gradient, stochastic gradient, and semi-stochastic approaches (Table 1 is helpful). They accurately highlight the limitations of existing methods and emphasize the potential benefits of their hybrid approach. The connections to Epoch Gradient Descent [9] and recent works on SAG [22] and SDCA [23] are well established.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The idea of carefully interleaving full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework to achieve condition number independence is a novel contribution.  It offers a potentially valuable trade-off, especially for large-scale problems where full gradient evaluations are expensive.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis appears sound.  The convergence proof, leveraging lemmas from prior work, seems robust, and the claim of O(log 1/ε) full gradient complexity is significant.  The use of the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality is appropriate for bounding the stochastic component.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind the work - addressing the computational bottleneck of full gradient methods for ill-conditioned problems.
* **Potential Practical Impact:** If the practical overhead of switching between full and stochastic gradients is manageable, this algorithm could be beneficial in several applications, particularly in machine learning with large datasets.

**3. Weaknesses & Areas for Improvement:**

* **Algorithm Detail and Implementation:** Algorithm 1 lacks crucial implementation details. The "argmin" step in line 8 is a projection onto the intersection of W and a ball. How this is solved efficiently is critical for practical performance but is not discussed. Is this a closed-form solution?  An approximate solution?  The computational cost of this step needs to be analyzed. The choice of the step size η = 1/[L√T] is presented without justification and is potentially suboptimal.
* **Comparison to Related Work - Incompleteness:** While the paper mentions SAG and SDCA, the comparison feels somewhat superficial. The authors claim EMGD *can* be applied to the same problem settings as SAG/SDCA but don't provide a detailed, apples-to-apples comparison of per-iteration cost and overall runtime. Table 2 is selective and only shows scenarios where EMGD performs well; a more comprehensive comparison considering a wider range of κ values would be more persuasive. Specifically, the authors should discuss the memory requirements of EMGD versus SAG/SDCA.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't adequately address practical considerations.  For example: 
    * What is the impact of inaccurate estimation of the condition number κ?  Is the algorithm robust to errors in κ?  (The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it's a significant concern).
    * What are the assumptions on the function oracle Of?  In practice, obtaining truly independent function evaluations might be challenging.
    * The discussion of distributed computing in section 4.4 feels tacked on and lacks details.
* **Assumptions and Limitations:** The assumption that F(w) is strongly convex and smooth is stated but its impact on the applicability of the method should be discussed. Are there alternative formulations or modifications that could relax these assumptions?
* **Clarity of Notation:**  Some notation is inconsistent or unclear (e.g., the multiple uses of "w" with superscripts and subscripts).

**4. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical results are strong and offer a significant improvement in full gradient complexity compared to standard methods.
* The core idea is novel and potentially impactful for large-scale optimization problems.
* The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of implementation details and practical considerations weakens the paper's impact.
* The comparison to related work is incomplete and somewhat biased.
* The robustness to inaccurate condition number estimation is not addressed.



**5. Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.**  I believe the core idea of EMGD is valuable and the theoretical results are solid. However, the paper requires substantial revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

* Provide more details on the implementation of the algorithm, particularly the projection step.
* Present a more comprehensive and unbiased comparison to related algorithms like SAG and SDCA, considering both iteration complexity and per-iteration cost.
* Discuss the practical implications of the algorithm, including the impact of inaccurate condition number estimation and the requirements of the function oracle.
* Clarify the notation and improve the overall presentation.



I encourage the authors to address these points and resubmit the paper for further review. Addressing these concerns would significantly enhance the paper's contribution and make it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
