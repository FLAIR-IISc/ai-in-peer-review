- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that combines one full gradient per epoch with stochastic gradients to solve smooth, strongly convex problems.
- Key claim: reduces full gradient accesses to O(log(1/ε)), making them independent of the condition number κ, while using O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.
- EMGD builds on epoch gradient descent and uses a "mixed gradient" plus a shrinking domain constraint to bound stochastic error.
- Strength: significant technical contribution by removing κ-dependence for full gradient accesses, beneficial for large-scale problems.
- Strength: rigorous, well-structured convergence analysis with a detailed proof of Theorem 1.
- Strength: practical advantages over SAG/SDCA — works for constrained problems, needs only O(d) storage, gives high-probability guarantees, and is easily distributed.
- Strength: clear problem formulation, consistent notation, and logical presentation of assumptions and results.
- Weakness: requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ, a major practical limitation not fully addressed.
- Weakness: unclear complexity comparison with SAG/SDCA; derivation that EMGD matches their order when κ ≤ n^(1/2) is not well explained.
- Weakness: lacks practical implementation guidance for choosing epoch length T and domain shrinkage rate; theoretical T may be conservative.
- Weakness: purely theoretical work with no empirical validation to demonstrate practical benefits.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison with existing "semi-stochastic" methods that interleave full and stochastic gradients.
- Quality: technically sound and rigorous, but would be stronger with practical κ-estimation discussion, clearer complexity derivation, and experiments.
- Clarity: generally well-written, but could better explain intuition for why the mixed gradient is well-bounded and clarify κ vs. n relationship.
- Clarity: motivation for step size η = 1/(L√T) and epoch length T needs clearer justification.
- Originality: novel combination of one full gradient per epoch with stochastic updates; distinct from purely stochastic linear-convergence methods.
- Significance: high for large-scale optimization since full-gradient calls are costly and reducing them to O(log(1/ε)) is impactful.
- Significance: EMGD can outperform full-gradient methods under reasonable κ vs. n regimes and offers advantages over SAG/SDCA in storage and constraint handling.
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with acknowledged practical limitations; acceptance recommended.
- Recommendation: accept — significant theory, clear writing, practical limitations noted but not fatal.
- Specific: authors should elaborate on practical methods to estimate κ, beyond using regularizer-based upper bounds.
- Specific: clarify derivation and assumptions behind the κ vs. n threshold used in complexity comparisons (Table 2).
- Specific: add empirical validation (even on synthetic problems) to support theoretical claims.
- Specific: expand comparison to semi-stochastic methods [12] to better position EMGD.
- Specific: provide more intuition for why the mixed gradient approach reduces variance and works in practice.
- Specific: give practical guidance on selecting parameters like T and initial domain size Δ1.
