### **Summary**

This paper addresses the high computational cost of first-order optimization methods for smooth and strongly convex functions, particularly when the condition number κ is large. The standard accelerated gradient method requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, which can be prohibitive. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which combines full and stochastic gradient information to reduce the number of required full gradients to O(log(1/ε)), making it independent of the condition number κ. The algorithm operates in epochs, where each epoch involves one full gradient calculation and O(κ²) stochastic gradient steps. The core idea is to use the full gradient as a stable anchor and a mixed gradient estimator for variance reduction, with updates performed within a shrinking trust region. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing linear convergence with high probability and discuss advantages over related methods like SAG and SDCA, such as applicability to constrained problems and lower memory requirements.

### **Review**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to an important problem in large-scale optimization. The goal of decoupling the number of expensive full gradient computations from the condition number is highly relevant. However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its relation to prior work, practical assumptions, and lack of empirical validation, which undermine its contribution.

#### **Quality**

**Strengths:**
*   The theoretical analysis appears to be sound. The paper provides a detailed proof sketch for its main convergence theorem, showing linear convergence with a high-probability bound, which is stronger than a guarantee in expectation.
*   The derivation is clear and leverages standard tools from convex optimization analysis, such as properties of smoothness/strong convexity and the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **A Priori Knowledge of κ:** A major weakness is that the algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ to set the number of inner iterations `T = O(κ² log(1/δ))`. In most practical applications, κ is unknown and difficult to estimate. The paper acknowledges this limitation only briefly in the conclusion, but it is a critical flaw that severely limits the practical applicability of the proposed method. A method that requires knowing the very quantity it is designed to overcome the dependence on is problematic.
*   **No Experimental Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While theory-focused papers are valuable, a paper proposing a new optimization algorithm for practical problems should include at least some empirical evidence. Experiments would be crucial to (1) validate the theoretical rates, (2) assess the sensitivity to the estimation of κ, and (3) provide a fair comparison to the methods it claims to have advantages over (e.g., SAG, SDCA, and especially Nesterov's AGD). The claimed advantages regarding constrained optimization and memory are unsubstantiated without empirical results.

#### **Clarity**

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the motivation is easy to follow.
*   The notation is consistent and the proof sketch in Section 4.2, while dense, logically lays out the steps of the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Omission of SVRG:** The most significant issue regarding clarity and relation to prior work is the complete omission of the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method [Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013]. The "mixed gradient" estimator used in EMGD, `g̃_kt = ∇F(w̄^k) + ∇f_kt(w_kt) - ∇f_kt(w̄^k)`, is precisely the gradient estimator used in SVRG. SVRG also uses an epoch-based structure, performs one full gradient per epoch, and achieves a linear convergence rate. The failure to cite, discuss, and compare against SVRG is a major oversight that misrepresents the novelty of the proposed technique. The core mechanism is not new, and the paper should have framed its contribution as an *analysis* or *modification* of an SVRG-like estimator in a constrained, shrinking-ball setting.

#### **Originality**

**Strengths:**
*   The specific goal of achieving a number of full gradient evaluations that is independent of κ is a novel and valuable pursuit.
*   The combination of the SVRG-style estimator with a shrinking domain constraint `B(w̄^k; Δ_k)` appears to be the main novel element that facilitates the specific theoretical result.

**Weaknesses:**
*   As mentioned above, the core gradient estimation technique and the epoch-based structure are not new and were introduced in SVRG. This significantly reduces the originality of the proposed algorithm. The contribution is narrowed down to the specific analysis within the shrinking ball framework.
*   The idea of using epochs and reducing variance is central to a whole family of algorithms (SAGA, MISO, etc.), and the paper would be much stronger if it situated itself accurately within this literature.

#### **Significance**

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses a problem of high significance in machine learning and optimization. If the claims held up in practice, an algorithm that mitigates the effect of ill-conditioning on the most expensive operations would be a major contribution.
*   The theoretical result is intriguing and could inspire further research into algorithms with similar properties.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **High Stochastic Complexity:** While the number of full gradients is independent of κ, the number of stochastic gradients per epoch is O(κ²), leading to a total stochastic complexity of O(κ² log(1/ε)). This is significantly worse than the O(κ) dependence of methods like SVRG and SDCA (whose total complexity is often O((n+κ)log(1/ε))). The paper's own comparison in Table 2 shows EMGD is only competitive when κ is very small (κ ≤ n^(1/2)), which is precisely the regime where ill-conditioning is less of a problem. For severely ill-conditioned problems (large κ), the O(κ²) cost would likely make the algorithm impractical.
*   **Limited Practical Impact:** Due to the requirement of knowing κ and the high stochastic complexity, the practical impact of this work seems limited. The advantages it claims over SAG/SDCA (handling constraints, low memory) are also features of SVRG, which is a more direct and important baseline that is omitted.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Pros:**
1.  Addresses the important problem of reducing expensive full gradient computations in ill-conditioned settings.
2.  Presents a novel theoretical result: achieving a number of full gradient calls O(log(1/ε)) that is independent of the condition number κ.
3.  The analysis provides a strong high-probability convergence guarantee.

**Cons:**
1.  **Critical Omission of Prior Work:** Fails to cite or compare with SVRG, which uses the identical gradient estimator and a similar epoch structure, misrepresenting the work's novelty.
2.  **Impractical Assumptions:** The algorithm requires a priori knowledge of the condition number κ to set its hyperparameters, which is unrealistic in practice.
3.  **High Computational Cost:** The dependence on O(κ²) for the number of stochastic gradients is worse than state-of-the-art methods like SVRG/SDCA, making its practical utility questionable, especially in the ill-conditioned regime it targets.
4.  **No Experiments:** The paper lacks any empirical validation, making it impossible to assess the algorithm's real-world performance or verify its claimed advantages.

### **Conclusion**

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting result for an important problem, its significant flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance. The failure to properly position the work relative to SVRG is a major scholarly issue. Furthermore, the combination of impractical assumptions (knowing κ) and a high stochastic gradient complexity (O(κ²)) likely renders the algorithm less effective than existing methods in the very settings it aims to improve. The lack of experiments leaves all practical claims unsubstantiated. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the paper by including a detailed comparison with SVRG, addressing the hyperparameter dependency on κ, and providing empirical results to support their claims. In its current form, the paper is not ready for publication at NIPS.
