

# Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth strongly convex problems that combines full gradients and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations. The key theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient accesses (independent of the condition number κ), compared to the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) for full gradient methods. The algorithm uses one full gradient per epoch and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, resulting in a total of O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to achieve ε-optimality.

The paper relates well to previous work on first-order optimization methods. It correctly cites the optimal complexity for full gradient methods (Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) and stochastic methods (SGD, SAG, SDCA). The paper distinguishes itself by addressing the specific problem of reducing full gradient evaluations while maintaining linear convergence, which is not the focus of prior work on stochastic optimization.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound, with a detailed proof of the convergence rate (Theorem 1). The proof strategy using induction and careful bounding of error terms is well-executed. The paper properly acknowledges the assumption that κ must be known beforehand, which is a practical limitation that the authors note for future work.

The paper provides a thorough comparison with existing methods, including computational complexity analysis for the special case where the objective function is a sum of n smooth functions (F(w) = (1/n)∑f_i(w)). The analysis shows EMGD can be more efficient than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is a reasonable scenario in machine learning problems with regularization.

However, I have a concern about the O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients. While the paper correctly states that the number of full gradients is reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), the O(κ²) stochastic gradient requirement might be prohibitive for large κ. The paper should more clearly address whether this dependence can be improved or if there are practical scenarios where κ is sufficiently small to make this acceptable.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively frames the problem and states the contribution. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise, and the notation is consistently used throughout. The comparison with related work (Table 2) is particularly helpful for contextualizing the contributions.

The main clarity issue is the computational cost per iteration. The paper states "the computational complexity of EMGD is lower than Nesterov's algorithm as long as the condition number κ ≤ n^(2/3)" but doesn't explicitly state the computational cost in terms of operations. It would be helpful to clarify whether a single full gradient evaluation is n times more expensive than a stochastic gradient evaluation (as the paper suggests in the comparison), and how this affects the total computational cost.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear and novel contribution to the field. The specific approach of mixing full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition-number-independent access to full gradients is not present in previous work. While the idea of using both full and stochastic gradients has been explored (e.g., in semi-stochastic methods), the paper's specific algorithm and theoretical guarantees are novel.

The paper properly distinguishes itself from related work, particularly in the comparison section where it highlights advantages over SAG and SDCA: (1) works for constrained optimization, (2) requires only O(d) storage (vs. O(n) for SAG/SDCA), (3) can be distributed more easily, and (4) achieves high-probability convergence (vs. expectation-based convergence for SAG/SDCA).

## Significance

The significance of this work is moderate to high. For ill-conditioned problems where κ is large, reducing the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) could lead to significant computational savings, as full gradients are typically expensive for large-scale problems. The paper identifies a practical regime (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) where EMGD outperforms Nesterov's method, which is relevant for many machine learning applications with regularization.

The advantages over SAG and SDCA (constrained optimization, lower storage, distributed computing potential) also add to the significance. However, the O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients might limit the practical applicability for problems with large κ, which is a limitation to acknowledge.

## Strengths

- Novel algorithm that achieves condition-number-independent full gradient access
- Strong theoretical guarantees with detailed proof
- Comprehensive comparison with related work
- Identification of practical advantages over SAG and SDCA
- Clear problem statement and well-structured paper

## Weaknesses

- The O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients might be prohibitive for large κ
- The requirement that κ must be known beforehand is a practical limitation
- The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify the computational cost per iteration
- The practical impact for large κ scenarios could be better explained

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution to the field of optimization, with a novel algorithm that addresses an important practical limitation of full gradient methods. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the paper properly contextualizes its contribution within the literature.

The main issue to address in revision is the O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients. The authors should more clearly explain why this dependence is necessary and whether it can be improved. They should also clarify the computational cost per iteration and the practical implications of the O(κ²) term.

The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of how the condition number κ can be estimated in practice, as the authors mention this as a future work direction. This would help readers understand the practical applicability of the method.

This paper is a solid contribution to the field of optimization, and I believe it would be valuable to the NIPS community. The theoretical results are significant and could inspire further work on hybrid optimization methods.
