## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for strongly convex smooth optimization, aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations to O(log 1/ε) independent of the condition number κ. The idea of strategically mixing full and stochastic gradients within epochs is interesting and addresses a practical concern in large-scale optimization. While the theoretical analysis seems solid and the results are promising, the practical implications and comparisons with other state-of-the-art algorithms need further clarification. I lean towards **Accept with Minor Revisions**, contingent on addressing the points outlined below.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the well-known trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost in convex optimization.  Full gradient methods offer faster convergence rates (O(√κ log 1/ε)) but are expensive for large datasets. Stochastic gradient methods are cheaper per iteration but converge slower (often polynomially in 1/ε). EMGD proposes to bridge this gap by intelligently combining both, achieving a linear convergence rate (O(log 1/ε)) with a number of full gradients independent of κ. 

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature. It accurately summarizes the iteration complexities of deterministic and stochastic methods (gradient descent, accelerated gradient descent, stochastic gradient descent, SAG, SDCA) and highlights the motivation for reducing full gradient evaluations. The connection to Epoch Gradient Descent (EGD) is also appropriately mentioned. The authors are aware of and cite relevant work effectively.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of systematically mixing full and stochastic gradients within epochs, aiming for condition number independence in full gradient access, is a valuable contribution.  This is not a trivial extension of existing methods.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis appears robust and provides a clear convergence guarantee. The proof sketch outlines a logical inductive argument.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and motivates the proposed solution. The trade-offs are well-defined.
* **Potential for Practical Impact:** Reducing the number of expensive full gradient evaluations is particularly relevant in scenarios with very large datasets where each gradient calculation is computationally demanding.
* **Discussion of Limitations and Future Work:** Acknowledging the need for condition number estimation and potential for distributed computing improvements demonstrates thoughtfulness.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations & Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm relies on knowing (or estimating) the condition number κ beforehand. The paper briefly mentions estimation in future work but doesn’t discuss how sensitive the performance is to inaccuracies in κ. The choice of the step size η and the number of iterations per epoch (T) seems crucial, and their impact on performance, especially for ill-conditioned problems, warrants further discussion.
* **Comparison with Existing Methods:** While the table in Section 3.4 attempts to compare EMGD with other algorithms, it's somewhat superficial. A more detailed empirical comparison with SAG and SDCA (particularly on relevant problem instances with varying condition numbers) is needed to demonstrate a clear advantage. The assumption of n times more expensive full gradients might not always hold.
* **Domain Constraint in Algorithm 1:** The projection step onto W ∩ B(w̄k, ∆k) in Algorithm 1 is a potential bottleneck, especially for complex constraints.  The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of this projection or how it impacts the overall performance.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any empirical results. Demonstrating the effectiveness of EMGD on standard benchmark datasets would significantly strengthen the contribution. 
* **Clarity in Notation:** While generally clear, the notation could be slightly improved. For example, defining `fkt` (random function at iteration t) directly in the algorithm instead of relying on the function oracle description would enhance readability.




**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Line 78:**  "Our objective is to reduce the iteration complexity from O( √ κ log 1/ǫ ) to O(log 1 ǫ )" - this is not quite accurate. The algorithm aims to reduce the *number of full gradient evaluations* to O(log 1/ǫ). The overall iteration complexity will still involve the stochastic gradient evaluations.
* **Section 3.4:** The statement that EMGD is more efficient than Nesterov’s algorithm *only* if κ ≤ n²/3 feels somewhat weak. It would be helpful to provide a clearer illustration of when EMGD becomes advantageous.
* **Algorithm 1, step 8:** What if the constrained minimization problem in step 8 is difficult to solve efficiently?  Is there a sensitivity analysis on the complexity of this step?
* **Section 4:** The proof sketch is high-level. Providing more details on the key steps (e.g., bounding AT and BT) would increase confidence in the correctness of the analysis.
* **Future Work:** Suggest exploring adaptive step-size strategies (e.g., using line search) to avoid the need for precise knowledge of κ.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel algorithm addressing a practical problem.
* Strong theoretical guarantees.
* Clear and well-motivated contribution.

**Con:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* Limited comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms.
* Practical issues related to condition number estimation and projection step are not fully addressed.




**Recommendation:**

I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**.  Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Include empirical results** on standard benchmark datasets to demonstrate the performance of EMGD.
2. **Provide a more detailed comparison** with SAG and SDCA, including a discussion of the computational cost of each algorithm and the conditions under which EMGD outperforms them.
3. **Discuss the sensitivity** of the algorithm to inaccurate estimates of the condition number κ.
4. **Address the computational cost** of the projection step in Algorithm 1 and its potential impact on performance.
5. **Improve notation clarity** where appropriate.



Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field.
