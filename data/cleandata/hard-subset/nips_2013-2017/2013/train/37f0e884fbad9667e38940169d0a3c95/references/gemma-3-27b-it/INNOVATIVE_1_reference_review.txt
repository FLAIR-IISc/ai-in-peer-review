This paper tackles a significant problem: reducing the computational cost of full-gradient methods for strongly convex optimization, particularly when the condition number is large. The core idea – interleaving full and stochastic gradients within epochs to achieve a condition number-independent iteration complexity – is genuinely novel and bold. It’s a clever bypass of the typical √κ dependence, and the theoretical result of O(log 1/ǫ) full gradients is compelling. The connection to epoch gradient descent is well-motivated, and the attempt to place the work within the broader landscape of optimization algorithms (SAG, SDCA) is appreciated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of mixing full and stochastic gradients within epochs to achieve condition number independence is a strong contribution. It challenges a fundamental limitation of traditional methods.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a convergence analysis, demonstrating a linear convergence rate and establishing the desired complexity bounds.
*   **Potential Impact:** A reduction in the number of full gradient evaluations could be significant for large-scale problems where these evaluations are expensive.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The motivation and problem setting are clearly defined, and the trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients are well-explained.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The authors thoughtfully compare their method to existing algorithms like SAG and SDCA, acknowledging both advantages and disadvantages.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Considerations:** While theoretically sound, the practical implementation and overhead of switching between full and stochastic gradients within each epoch are not fully discussed. The constant factor involved in the O(κ²) stochastic gradients within each epoch could be substantial.
*   **Assumption of Known Condition Number:** The algorithm relies on knowing the condition number κ beforehand, which is often not the case in practice. While the authors acknowledge this and briefly mention future work on estimation, it remains a significant limitation.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks any empirical results. While the theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the practical performance of EMGD on real-world datasets is crucial. It's unclear how EMGD would perform compared to existing solvers in realistic scenarios.
*   **Proof Complexity:** The proof sketch provided in the appendix is lengthy and somewhat difficult to follow. While a full proof is expected, making the key ideas more accessible would be beneficial.
*   **Oracle Model:** The function oracle `Of` feels a little abstract. While mathematically convenient, it doesn't fully capture the nuances of how stochastic gradients are obtained in practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of optimization algorithms for large-scale problems.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the convergence rate and complexity.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of existing methods.
*   The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation is a significant drawback. It's difficult to assess the practical viability of the algorithm without experimental results.
*   The dependence on knowing the condition number is a practical limitation.
*   The potential overhead of switching between full and stochastic gradients within each epoch is not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a very promising paper with a genuinely novel and significant idea. The theoretical results are strong, and the approach has the potential to make a real impact on the field. However, the lack of empirical validation is a significant concern. I lean towards acceptance, primarily because of the novelty and boldness of the approach.  I believe the idea is worth pursuing, and the theoretical foundation is solid enough to warrant publication even without immediate experimental results.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept**, but with a strong encouragement to the authors to include empirical validation in a follow-up work. The reviewers and the community would greatly benefit from seeing how this algorithm performs in practice. I would also suggest the authors more carefully consider and discuss the practical implications of knowing the condition number and the overhead of the mixed gradient computation.
