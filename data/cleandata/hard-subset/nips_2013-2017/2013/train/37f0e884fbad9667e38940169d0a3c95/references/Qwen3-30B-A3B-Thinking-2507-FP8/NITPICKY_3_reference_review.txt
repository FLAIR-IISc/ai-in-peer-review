

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), claiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) for smooth and strongly convex optimization by combining full and stochastic gradients. The main contribution appears to be a novel algorithm that achieves linear convergence with condition-number-independent full gradient access.

## Major Technical Flaws

The paper contains several critical technical errors that undermine its claims:

1. **Algorithmic Definition Error**: In Algorithm 1, step 8 states "wkt+1 = argmin w∈W∩B(w̄k;∆k) η〈w −wkt , g̃kt 〉+ 1‖w −wkt ‖2". This is missing a coefficient for the norm term. The correct form should be "η〈w −wkt , g̃kt 〉+ (1/2)‖w −wkt ‖²" or similar, which is essential for the convergence analysis.

2. **Mathematical Notation Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent spacing and formatting throughout mathematical expressions (e.g., "O( √ κ log 1ǫ )" should be "O(√κ log(1/ε))"). In equation (2), "‖∇f(w)−∇f(w′)‖" should have consistent spacing around the minus sign.

3. **Probability Parameter Misuse**: The paper states "δ ≤ e^(-1/2)" in equation (5), but δ is typically a small probability parameter (e.g., 0.01), not a constant bounded by ~0.6065. This is a fundamental misunderstanding of probabilistic analysis conventions.

4. **Misleading Computational Complexity Claim**: The paper claims "computational complexity of EMGD is lower than Nesterov's algorithm [17] as long as κ ≤ n^(2/3)", but doesn't properly account for the cost of full vs. stochastic gradients. The paper assumes a full gradient is n times more expensive than a stochastic gradient, but the actual computational cost of EMGD is O(n log(1/ε) + κ² log(1/ε)), while Nesterov's method would be O(n√κ log(1/ε)). The comparison is misleading without specifying the exact cost relationship.

5. **Proof Error in Equation (14)**: The paper writes "max w (〈∇gt(wt),wt −w〉 − ‖wt −w‖² 2η)" which should be "‖wt −w‖²/(2η)" with proper fraction formatting.

6. **Misrepresentation of SAG/SDCA**: The paper claims "the linear convergence of SAG and SDCA only holds in expectation, whereas EMGD holds with high probability." However, the "high probability" bound for EMGD is 1 - mδ (where m is the number of epochs), which becomes weak for large m. The comparison is inaccurate.

## Minor but Significant Issues

1. **Table Reference**: The paper references "Table 1" in the introduction but doesn't include it in the provided text.

2. **Smoothness Assumption**: The paper assumes "each fi(·) is smooth" but doesn't specify the smoothness constant for each fi, which is critical for the overall problem's smoothness constant L.

3. **Constrained Optimization Claim**: The paper claims EMGD "works for both constrained and unconstrained optimization problems," but provides no concrete example or explanation of how the constrained problem in Step 8 would be solved efficiently.

4. **Condition Number Knowledge**: The paper acknowledges that EMGD requires knowing κ beforehand but doesn't provide a practical method to estimate κ, which is a significant practical limitation.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper introduces a novel algorithmic approach, the technical errors and misleading claims significantly undermine its contribution. The paper's central claim about computational efficiency is not properly supported, and several mathematical errors in the proof and algorithm description weaken the theoretical foundation.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Correcting all mathematical notation and formatting errors
2. Providing a clear comparison of computational costs (not just asymptotic complexity)
3. Clarifying the relationship between the smoothness constants of individual functions and the overall problem
4. Correcting the probability parameter usage in the analysis

## Strengths

The paper makes a reasonable attempt to bridge full and stochastic gradient methods. The idea of using mixed gradients to reduce full gradient evaluations is conceptually interesting, and the algorithm design is well-structured.

## Weaknesses

The technical flaws are substantial enough to question the validity of the main claim. The paper's theoretical analysis contains multiple errors that would require significant reworking. The claims about computational efficiency are misleading without proper cost accounting.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper contains too many technical errors and misleading claims that undermine its main contribution. While the idea of combining full and stochastic gradients is interesting, the execution is flawed, and the paper does not provide a solid theoretical foundation for its claims.

The authors should address the mathematical errors, clarify the computational cost comparison, and properly account for the probability parameters before resubmission. Without these corrections, the paper does not meet NIPS standards for technical correctness.
