Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies first-order optimization for L-smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives F over a convex set W, under access to two oracles: (i) a full gradient oracle Og(w) = ∇F(w), and (ii) a function oracle Of that returns a random smooth function f with F(w) = Ef[f(w)] and allows evaluating ∇f(·). The authors propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch, the algorithm computes a full gradient at the epoch anchor w̄k. Within the epoch, it performs T projected updates constrained to a shrinking ball around w̄k, using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇ft(wt) − ∇ft(w̄k). This is a control-variate estimator with zero mean error at wt, whose variance is bounded via L-smoothness and the shrinking radius.

The main theorem establishes, with high probability, linear convergence: after m epochs, F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ (λ Δ1^2)/2^{m+1}, using m full gradients and mT stochastic oracle calls. With step size η = 1/(L√T), and T ≥ c (L/λ)^2 log(1/δ), the algorithm achieves an ε-optimal solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, where κ = L/λ.

EMGD is related to epoch/stagewise methods and variance-reduced stochastic methods. The “mixed” gradient is the same control-variate form used in SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) for finite-sum problems, which also computes one full gradient per epoch and uses inner-loop stochastic updates with a variance-reduced estimator. Compared to SAG/SAGA (RouX et al., NIPS 2012; Defazio et al., 2014) and SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, JMLR 2013), EMGD does not require storing per-component gradients but assumes access to a full gradient oracle and a function oracle that allows evaluating ∇f at two points for the same sampled f. Unlike SVRG/SAGA/SDCA, EMGD’s analysis applies to a more general expectation oracle (not necessarily finite-sum), at the cost of a worse κ dependence in the number of stochastic steps and the need for a shrinking trust region.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The analysis is clear in structure (epochwise induction, decomposition into deterministic and martingale parts, Azuma-Hoeffding bound). The role of the shrinking ball is explicit: it upper-bounds the gradient estimator range to enable a high-probability bound.
- The convergence statement is high-probability linear, with a precise choice of η and T. The requirement T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) yields the stated complexity.
- Assumptions and derivations are mostly standard. The use of strong convexity via ⟨∇F(w)−∇F(w′), w−w′⟩ ≥ λ||w−w′||^2 and L-smoothness are appropriate. The paper uses ||∇F(w)−∇F(w′)|| ≥ λ||w−w′||, which follows from strong convexity by Cauchy–Schwarz.
- Some minor issues/typos: ln δ should be ln(1/δ); Step 8’s update lacks clear constants (the norm-square term is written as “+ 1 ||w − wt||^2”); a few grammatical errors (“interstage”).
- The oracle model requires that for a sampled f one can compute both ∇f(wt) and ∇f(w̄k). This is stronger than a standard stochastic gradient oracle but matches finite-sum or supervised learning settings where per-example gradients are available.

Clarity
- The algorithm and proof are generally well organized, with a clear epoch structure and the reasoning behind the mixed gradient. The ball constraint and its shrinking schedule are clearly tied to the probabilistic analysis.
- The paper would benefit from clarifying Step 8 (coefficients), fixing the ln(·) typos, and explicitly stating the per-iteration cost (two evaluations of ∇f) and projection complexity onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk).

Originality
- Using a control-variate estimator of the form ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is well known (SVRG). The novelty here lies in (a) applying it in a general expectation-oracle setting (not necessarily finite-sum), (b) providing a high-probability linear convergence proof by constraining iterates to a shrinking ball, and (c) emphasizing that the number of full gradient evaluations is O(log(1/ε)) independent of κ. However, the dependence on κ in stochastic steps (κ^2) is worse than SVRG/SDCA/SAGA (κ or n+κ), and the need for shrinking domains is atypical in variance reduction literature.
- The paper should cite and compare to SVRG and SAGA, as they are central related work with similar per-epoch full gradient use and linear rates.

Significance
- The observation that one can decouple “expensive full gradients” from κ by using a control variate is useful, but not new in the finite-sum setting. Extending the analysis to a general Orlicz-like function oracle with high-probability guarantees is of interest, though the κ^2 factor limits practical gains versus state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods.
- Applicability to constrained domains is a plus, provided the projection onto W ∩ ball is efficient. The low memory footprint relative to SAG/SAGA is also valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, rigorous high-probability linear-convergence analysis in a general oracle model.
- Simple algorithm with minimal storage and one full gradient per epoch.
- Works with constraints (when projections are tractable).
- Decouples the count of full gradients from κ.

Cons for acceptance
- Key estimator is essentially SVRG’s; missing discussion/citation reduces perceived novelty.
- κ^2 dependence in stochastic steps is significantly worse than SVRG/SAGA/SDCA (κ or n+κ).
- Requires knowledge of κ (and Δ1) to set T and the shrinking schedule; no adaptivity.
- Practical costs not fully addressed: two ∇f evaluations per inner step; projection onto intersection may be nontrivial; no empirical validation.
- Some comparison claims (e.g., about high-probability vs expectation) are debatable; modern analyses provide high-probability guarantees for related methods.

Questions/clarifications
- Can the κ^2 dependence be improved to κ by refining the variance bound (e.g., without shrinking balls or via alternative martingale bounds)?
- How sensitive is performance to mis-specified κ and Δ1? Can you provide an adaptive schedule (e.g., doubling/halving T or Δk) with provable guarantees?
- What is the exact per-iteration cost in the finite-sum case (one or two component gradients)? Can ∇f(w̄k) be cached across inner iterations?

Suggestions for improvement
- Cite and compare thoroughly with SVRG and SAGA; discuss why EMGD’s approach and oracle model differ, and whether the κ^2 factor is intrinsic to the general setting.
- Provide an adaptive variant that does not require κ or Δ1.
- Clarify Step 8 and fix typos; add discussion on projection complexity and per-iteration gradient cost.
- If space allows, include experiments comparing EMGD to SVRG/SAGA/SDCA and accelerated full-gradient baselines, focusing on regimes where full gradients are expensive.

Overall, the paper presents a clear analysis of a variance-reduced epoch method in a general oracle setting, but its novelty relative to SVRG-like methods and its κ^2 dependence limit its impact.
