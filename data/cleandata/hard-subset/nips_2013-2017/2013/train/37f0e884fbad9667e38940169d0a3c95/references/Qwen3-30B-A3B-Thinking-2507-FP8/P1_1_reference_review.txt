

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The authors address a significant limitation of standard first-order methods: the dependence of iteration complexity on the condition number κ (O(√κ log(1/ε)) for full gradient methods). Their key insight is that by combining one full gradient evaluation per epoch with stochastic gradient evaluations, they can reduce the number of full gradient accesses to O(log(1/ε)), making it independent of the condition number.

The paper builds upon the epoch gradient descent framework [9] and introduces a "mixed gradient" that combines a full gradient (computed at the beginning of each epoch) with stochastic gradients (computed during the epoch). The algorithm maintains a shrinking domain constraint, which ensures the stochastic part of the gradient is well-bounded. The authors prove that EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

This work relates to recent advances in stochastic optimization, particularly SAG [22] and SDCA [23], which also achieve linear convergence for problems with a finite sum structure. However, EMGD differs by using a combination of full and stochastic gradients rather than relying solely on stochastic gradients, and it provides high-probability convergence guarantees rather than just expectation guarantees.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper presents a clear theoretical contribution that resolves a fundamental limitation of standard optimization methods. The reduction of full gradient accesses from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) is significant for large-scale problems where full gradient computations are expensive.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The convergence analysis is thorough and well-structured. The proof of Theorem 1 is detailed, and the authors carefully establish the conditions under which their algorithm achieves linear convergence.

3. **Practical Advantages**: The paper correctly identifies several practical advantages of EMGD over existing methods:
   - Works for both constrained and unconstrained problems
   - Requires only O(d) storage (vs. O(n) for SAG/SDCA)
   - Provides high-probability convergence guarantees (vs. expectation guarantees for SAG/SDCA)
   - Can be distributed with minimal impact on convergence rate

4. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly defines the problem, the assumptions, and the theoretical guarantees, making it easy to understand the contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Condition Number Knowledge Requirement**: The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ beforehand, which is a significant practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this as a drawback in the conclusion but don't provide a compelling solution or analysis of how to estimate κ in practice. This is a major hurdle for real-world applications.

2. **Complexity Comparison Ambiguity**: The comparison with SAG and SDCA in Section 3.4 is somewhat confusing. The paper states that EMGD has computational complexity on the same order as SAG and SDCA when κ ≤ n^(1/2), but the derivation of this bound isn't clear. The relationship between κ and n (where n is the number of functions in the finite sum) is not sufficiently explained.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper lacks discussion about practical implementation details, such as how to choose the epoch length T and the domain shrinkage rate. The theoretical analysis provides conditions for T, but these might be conservative for practical use.

4. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical validation. While the theoretical contribution is strong, some experimental results would help demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.

5. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper mentions "semi-stochastic" methods [12] that interleave stochastic and full gradient descent but doesn't provide a detailed comparison. It would be helpful to understand how EMGD differs from these methods.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with a well-structured theoretical analysis. The proofs are rigorous and the main theorem (Theorem 1) is well-supported. However, the paper would be stronger with:
- A more detailed discussion of how to estimate κ in practice
- A clearer derivation of the complexity comparison with SAG and SDCA
- Some empirical results to validate the theoretical claims

The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitation of requiring knowledge of κ, but this remains a significant practical constraint.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, algorithm description, and theoretical results are presented logically. The notation is consistent throughout. However, some sections could benefit from additional explanation:

- The intuition behind why the mixed gradient works (why the stochastic part is well-bounded) could be made more intuitive.
- The relationship between the condition number κ and the number of functions n (in the finite sum case) needs to be clarified.
- The motivation for the specific choice of step size η = 1/[L√T] and epoch length T could be better explained.

## Originality Assessment

The paper presents a novel approach to combining full and stochastic gradients for optimization. The key idea of using one full gradient per epoch with mixed gradient descent is original and addresses a gap in the literature. While related to "semi-stochastic" methods [12], the specific combination of full and stochastic gradients in the manner proposed is new.

The paper adequately references related work, including SAG, SDCA, and other recent advances in stochastic optimization. However, a more thorough comparison with "semi-stochastic" methods would strengthen the position of the contribution.

## Significance Assessment

The significance of this work is high for large-scale optimization problems where full gradient computations are expensive. The reduction of full gradient accesses to O(log(1/ε)) is a substantial theoretical advance that could lead to practical computational savings in many machine learning applications.

The paper demonstrates that EMGD can be more efficient than full gradient methods when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is a reasonable condition in many machine learning problems (as noted in the paper with the regularized optimization example). The practical advantages over SAG and SDCA (constrained optimization, lower storage requirements, high-probability convergence) further enhance the significance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of convex optimization. The main result is novel, well-supported, and addresses a practical limitation of existing methods. The paper would be even stronger with additional experimental validation and a more detailed discussion of how to handle the condition number knowledge requirement in practice.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and the paper is well-written. While the requirement of knowing κ beforehand is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and the paper still makes a valuable contribution to the field. The practical advantages over existing methods further support its significance.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. **Condition Number Estimation**: Please elaborate on how to estimate κ in practice. The paper mentions that for regularized problems, the regularizer can provide an upper bound on κ. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed discussion of practical estimation methods.

2. **Complexity Comparison**: Clarify the relationship between κ and n that leads to the complexity comparison in Table 2. It's currently unclear why κ ≤ n^(1/2) is the threshold for EMGD having the same order as SAG/SDCA.

3. **Empirical Validation**: While the paper is purely theoretical, consider adding a small set of experiments to validate the theoretical claims, even if only on synthetic problems.

4. **Comparison with Semi-Stochastic Methods**: Expand the comparison with "semi-stochastic" methods [12] to better position your work in the literature.

5. **Intuition for Mixed Gradient**: Provide more intuitive explanation for why the mixed gradient approach works (beyond just the technical proof).

6. **Practical Parameter Selection**: Include more guidance on how to choose parameters like T and the initial domain size ∆1 in practice.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Novel theoretical contribution that reduces full gradient access to condition number independent O(log(1/ε))
- Clear and rigorous theoretical analysis with well-supported convergence guarantees
- Practical advantages over existing methods (constrained optimization, lower storage, high-probability convergence)
- Addresses a significant practical limitation of standard optimization methods

**Con:**
- Requires knowledge of condition number κ beforehand (significant practical limitation)
- Lack of empirical validation to support theoretical claims
- Complexity comparison with SAG/SDCA could be clearer
- Limited discussion of how to handle the κ knowledge requirement in practice

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and it represents a valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization.
