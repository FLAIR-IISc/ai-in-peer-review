This paper presents Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations in strongly convex, smooth optimization by strategically combining full and stochastic gradients. The core idea – dividing the optimization into epochs with mixed gradients – builds upon epoch gradient descent. The authors claim to achieve a convergence rate independent of the condition number, reducing the full gradient complexity from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)).

While the problem addressed is relevant – reducing the computational cost of full gradients is crucial for large-scale problems – and the proposed approach has merit, the paper suffers from a surprisingly high number of minor flaws, hindering its overall impact and raising concerns about the thoroughness of the work. These flaws, while individually small, accumulate to create a sense of imprecision and a lack of polish that is unacceptable for NIPS.

**Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** The reduction of full gradient computations is a practically important goal.
* **Novelty (Incremental):** The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework represents an incremental, albeit not groundbreaking, addition to existing techniques.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a theoretical convergence analysis, claiming a logarithmic dependence on 1/ε for full gradient evaluations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Inconsistency:** The notation is inconsistent. For instance, ‘Of’ is described as a function oracle, yet is used to obtain a *random function* fkt(·). This is confusing and requires careful parsing. The use of superscripts and subscripts for indices (wk1 vs. wkt) is similarly awkward.
* **Lack of Rigor in Preliminaries:** The justification for F(·) being L-smooth based on the function oracle definition feels rushed. Simply stating the implication without detailed explanation weakens the argument.
* **Algorithm Description Ambiguity:** Step 8 in Algorithm 1 is vaguely worded: “Update the solution by…”.  It lacks specificity about the optimization problem being solved *within* the ball B(w̄k; ∆k). Is this a projected gradient step? If so, it needs to be explicitly stated.
* **Theorem 1 Conditions:** The condition δ ≤ e−1/2 in Theorem 1 feels arbitrary and unsubstantiated. Its impact on practical applicability isn’t discussed. The conditions on T and ∆1, while mathematically defined, lack intuitive explanation.
* **Overstated Comparisons:** The claim that EMGD works for both constrained and unconstrained problems is somewhat misleading. The constrained problem within step 8 must be solvable efficiently, which isn’t always the case.
* **Computational Complexity Comparison (Table 2):** The table’s assumptions (e.g., full gradient is *n* times more expensive) are not universally true and should be clearly stated as such. The discussion following the table feels somewhat contrived to highlight EMGD’s advantages.
* **Analysis Detail Deficiency:** The proof sketch in Section 4 is overly brief. Key steps (like bounding AT and BT) are stated without sufficient justification, relying heavily on unspecified results (like the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality without defining the martingale difference sequence rigorously). The connection between the stochastic gradients in the martingale difference sequence and the smoothness parameter is not adequately made.
* **Minor Typos & Grammatical Errors:** Scattered throughout the paper are minor grammatical errors and typos (e.g., “w̄k+1 = 1T+1 ∑T+1 t=1 w k t ”). While not critical individually, they detract from the paper’s overall professionalism.
* **Reference Formatting:** Some references lack complete information (e.g., missing page numbers).

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The problem is important, and the algorithm provides a potentially useful approach for reducing full gradient computations. The theoretical analysis, despite its shortcomings, provides a starting point for further research.

**Con:** The numerous minor flaws (notational inconsistencies, lack of rigor in justification, algorithm ambiguity, incomplete analysis, and presentation issues) suggest a lack of meticulousness in the preparation of this manuscript.  The improvements over existing methods appear incremental, and the overstated comparisons weaken the paper's claims. The lack of clarity and detail in the analysis raises concerns about its robustness.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper, in its current form, is **below the standard for NIPS**. While the core idea is reasonable, the execution is plagued by too many minor flaws. The paper needs substantial revisions to address these issues and ensure clarity, rigor, and polish. A more detailed analysis, clearer notation, and more carefully crafted comparisons are essential. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses and resubmit in the future. The potential of the idea warrants further development, but a significantly improved manuscript is required for acceptance.
