

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations required for smooth strongly convex optimization problems. The authors claim that EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations (compared to O(√κ log(1/ε)) for standard methods) while using O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper builds on the epoch gradient descent framework and relates to recent work on semi-stochastic methods (e.g., [9,12,13]) and variance-reduced methods like SAG [22] and SDCA [23]. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that EMGD achieves a high-probability linear convergence rate, with advantages over SAG and SDCA in terms of storage requirements and handling constrained problems.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous proof of the convergence rate (Theorem 1), with a clear induction-based argument. The analysis properly accounts for the stochastic component using martingale inequalities, and the assumptions are well-justified.

2. **Clear Advantages Over Existing Methods**: The paper effectively highlights several advantages of EMGD over SAG and SDCA: (a) it works for both constrained and unconstrained problems, (b) it requires only O(d) storage (vs. Ω(n) for SAG/SDCA), (c) it has a high-probability convergence guarantee (vs. expectation-based for SAG/SDCA), and (d) it can leverage distributed computing for the full gradient step.

3. **Practical Motivation**: The paper identifies a genuine practical challenge in optimization: the high computational cost of full gradient evaluations for large datasets, and proposes a reasonable solution by leveraging the availability of stochastic gradients.

4. **Proper Acknowledgment of Limitations**: The paper correctly notes that the condition number κ must be known beforehand, and discusses potential ways to estimate κ in future work.

## Weaknesses
1. **Incorrect Computational Cost Claim**: The paper claims EMGD has lower computational cost than Nesterov's method [17] when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but this appears to be incorrect. As shown in the analysis, both methods have asymptotic computational complexity of O(n^(4/3) log(1/ε)) under this condition, not a strict improvement. This is a significant error that weakens the paper's main contribution.

2. **Lack of Distinction from Semi-Stochastic Methods**: The paper cites "semi-stochastic" approximation methods [12,13] as related work, but fails to clearly distinguish EMGD from these approaches. The paper should explicitly state how EMGD differs from existing semi-stochastic methods, particularly in terms of the epoch structure and gradient mixing strategy.

3. **Missing Experimental Validation**: As a NeurIPS paper, the absence of empirical results to validate the theoretical claims is a major weakness. The paper should include experiments demonstrating the practical benefits of EMGD on real optimization problems.

4. **Unaddressed Practical Limitation**: The requirement to know κ beforehand is a significant practical limitation that isn't adequately addressed in the main paper. While the paper mentions future work on estimating κ, this should be discussed more thoroughly in the current manuscript.

5. **Misleading Comparison in Table 2**: The computational complexity comparison in Table 2 is misleading because it doesn't properly account for the trade-off between full and stochastic gradient costs. The paper should clarify whether the comparison refers to the number of gradient evaluations or actual computational cost.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. Correct the computational cost claim and provide an accurate comparison between EMGD and existing methods. The authors should clarify whether the claimed advantage is in terms of the number of gradient evaluations (which might be true) or actual computational cost (which appears to be the same).

2. Expand the related work section to clearly differentiate EMGD from existing semi-stochastic methods. The paper should explicitly state how EMGD improves upon or differs from the approaches in [9,12,13].

3. Include experimental results demonstrating the practical benefits of EMGD. The authors should test the algorithm on standard machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression, SVM) with varying condition numbers to validate the theoretical claims.

4. Address the κ knowledge requirement more thoroughly. The authors should discuss practical ways to estimate κ (beyond the brief mention in the conclusion) and how this affects the algorithm's performance.

5. Clarify the comparison in Table 2. The authors should explicitly state whether they're comparing the number of gradient evaluations or computational cost, and provide a more nuanced analysis of when EMGD provides an advantage.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to optimization with a clear motivation and sound analysis. However, the significant error in the computational cost claim, combined with the lack of empirical validation and insufficient distinction from related work, prevents it from making a strong contribution to the field. The theoretical results are valuable, but the paper needs substantial revisions to correct the claims and provide more comprehensive validation.

**Decision: Reject**

**Reasoning**: While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper's main claim about computational cost being lower than Nesterov's method is incorrect. This is a fundamental flaw that undermines the paper's contribution. Additionally, the lack of empirical validation and insufficient differentiation from existing semi-stochastic methods are significant weaknesses for a NeurIPS submission. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
