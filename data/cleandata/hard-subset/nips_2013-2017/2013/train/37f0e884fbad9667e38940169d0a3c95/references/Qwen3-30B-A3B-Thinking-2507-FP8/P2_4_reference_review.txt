

# Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that significantly reduces the number of full gradient evaluations needed from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. The algorithm cleverly combines one full gradient per epoch with O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, achieving linear convergence. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis showing EMGD achieves an ǫ-optimal solution with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper compares EMGD with related methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA, showing it offers advantages in constrained optimization, storage requirements, and convergence guarantees.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. Theorem 1 and its proof demonstrate linear convergence with condition number independent full gradient access, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The proof follows standard optimization techniques and is well-structured, though the complexity of the proof may make it challenging for some readers. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach, noting that EMGD may not be computationally optimal when κ > n^(1/2), and they acknowledge the assumption that κ is known beforehand.

The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation. While theoretical analysis is strong, the absence of experimental results to demonstrate the practical performance of EMGD is a significant gap. Without empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess how the algorithm performs in practice compared to existing methods, especially given the high number of stochastic gradients (O(κ²)) required per epoch.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and motivation, and the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise. The theoretical analysis is presented in a logical sequence, with careful definitions of assumptions and key properties. The comparison with related work (Table 2) is particularly helpful for understanding the context and contributions.

The paper could be improved by providing more intuitive explanations of why the mixed gradient approach works and why the number of stochastic gradients is O(κ²). A brief discussion of how the constant factors in the O(κ²) term might behave in practice would also be valuable.

## Originality
The core contribution of reducing the full gradient access complexity from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) is novel and significant. While the idea of combining full and stochastic gradients is related to "semi-stochastic" methods mentioned in the related work, the specific design of EMGD and its theoretical guarantees for condition number independent access represent a meaningful advancement.

The paper properly situates its contribution within the literature, acknowledging related work on stochastic optimization and semi-stochastic methods. The novelty is clear: EMGD achieves a condition number independent number of full gradient evaluations while maintaining linear convergence, which no existing method does.

## Significance
The significance of this work is high for the optimization community, particularly for large-scale machine learning applications where full gradient evaluations are expensive. The reduction in full gradient evaluations could lead to substantial computational savings for ill-conditioned problems, which are common in practice.

The paper demonstrates that EMGD can be more efficient than full gradient methods when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is a reasonable condition in many machine learning problems (e.g., when regularization parameter τ > Ω(n^(-2/3))). The advantages in handling constrained problems and requiring less storage (Ω(d) vs. Ω(n)) are also significant practical contributions.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of empirical results and the assumption that κ is known. The authors note that they plan to address the κ estimation in future work, but this limitation should be acknowledged as a significant barrier to immediate practical application.

## Overall Assessment
This is a technically sound paper with a significant theoretical contribution to optimization theory. The main strengths are the novel theoretical guarantee of condition number independent full gradient access and the thorough theoretical analysis. The main weaknesses are the lack of empirical validation and the assumption that the condition number κ is known.

While the paper is strong theoretically, the absence of experimental results is a serious drawback for a NeurIPS submission, where empirical validation is often expected. The authors should provide experimental results comparing EMGD with existing methods, especially given the high computational cost of O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch.

Given the importance of the problem and the strength of the theoretical contribution, I recommend acceptance with major revisions to include experimental results. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to optimization theory that could have significant practical implications, but the lack of empirical validation prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form.

**Recommendation**: Major Revision (with the expectation of adding experimental results)
