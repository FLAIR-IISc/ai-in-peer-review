### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing smooth and strongly convex functions, a setting where first-order methods typically exhibit a convergence rate dependent on the condition number κ. The key goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, making this count independent of κ. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which combines full and stochastic gradient information. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes one full gradient. Within the epoch, it performs a series of updates using a "mixed gradient" estimator, which is a variance-reduced-style estimate anchored by the full gradient from the start of the epoch. The main theoretical result is that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This successfully trades a κ-dependent number of full gradients for a κ²-dependent number of (cheaper) stochastic gradients. The paper provides a high-probability convergence analysis and discusses advantages over related methods like SAG and SDCA, such as handling constrained problems and having lower memory requirements.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Important Goal:** The objective of decoupling the number of full gradient evaluations from the condition number κ is a well-motivated and significant research direction, particularly for large-scale problems where full gradients are the primary computational bottleneck.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a clear linear convergence guarantee (Theorem 1) that holds with high probability, which is a stronger result than the in-expectation guarantees common to many stochastic optimization algorithms (e.g., SAG, SDCA).
3.  **Generality:** The proposed algorithm is applicable to general constrained convex optimization problems, provided the projection onto the feasible set is efficient. This gives it a broader scope than methods like SAG or SDCA, which are typically designed for unconstrained finite-sum problems.
4.  **Low Memory Footprint:** The algorithm has an O(d) memory requirement, which is a significant advantage over methods like SAG that require O(nd) storage to keep track of past gradients.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practicality Hampered by Dependence on κ:** A major weakness is that the algorithm's parameters, specifically the number of inner iterations `T`, depend directly on κ (`T` must be at least `O(κ²)`). In most practical scenarios, κ is unknown and difficult to estimate accurately. The paper acknowledges this as future work, but it severely limits the out-of-the-box applicability of the proposed method.
2.  **High Stochastic Gradient Complexity:** The requirement of `O(κ²)` stochastic gradients per epoch is quite high. Other popular variance-reduced methods, such as SVRG and SAGA, typically have an inner loop complexity that scales linearly with `n` or `κ`, not quadratically. This may make EMGD less competitive than these methods, especially for moderately to poorly conditioned problems.
3.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Technique:** The mixed gradient estimator `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is identical in form to the variance-reduced gradient estimator used in SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013). The main novelty lies in the specific epoch-based schedule and the corresponding analysis that isolates the dependence of full gradients, rather than in the gradient estimation technique itself. The relationship to SVRG should be more explicitly discussed.
4.  **Absence of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is clear, the lack of any experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of EMGD. An empirical study could validate the theory, show how the algorithm behaves with estimated or mismatched values of κ, and provide a direct comparison against relevant baselines like accelerated gradient descent and SVRG on real-world problems.

### Detailed Review

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proof of Theorem 1, outlined in Section 4, follows a clear inductive argument and leverages standard techniques from convex optimization analysis, including Hoeffding-Azuma inequality for bounding the stochastic terms. The claims are well-supported by the provided theoretical analysis. However, the work feels incomplete from a practical standpoint. The assumption that κ is known is a very strong one, and without a procedure to handle an unknown κ, the algorithm is more of a theoretical curiosity than a practical tool. The honesty about the trade-offs in Section 3.4 is commendable, but the high `O(κ²)` stochastic complexity is a significant drawback that is not fully addressed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement is motivated well, the algorithm is presented cleanly in Algorithm 1, and the main theorem is stated precisely. The analysis section provides a good sketch of the main proof ideas, making the technical contribution accessible. The notation is consistent and easy to follow. A small improvement would be to explicitly name the mixed gradient as a variance-reduced estimator and cite SVRG when it is introduced, as the resemblance is immediate to experts in the field.

**Originality:** The originality of the paper is mixed. The core idea of using a variance-reduced gradient estimator is not new and is central to methods like SAG, SVRG, and SAGA. The epoch-based structure has also been used before (e.g., in Hazan & Kale's EGD). The primary contribution is the novel combination and analysis of these elements to explicitly achieve a full-gradient complexity that is independent of κ. This specific goal and the corresponding analysis are original and contribute a new perspective to the literature on variance reduction. However, the work would be stronger if it were better contextualized with respect to SVRG, which uses the same estimator but with a different outer/inner loop structure that also achieves linear convergence. The analysis in this paper focuses on a different metric (number of full gradients), which is a valid and interesting contribution.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant from a theoretical perspective, as they demonstrate a new type of trade-off possible between full and stochastic gradient computations. The paper clearly shows that it's possible to "pay" with more stochastic gradients to make the number of full-gradient computations independent of the problem's conditioning. This could inspire further research into hybrid algorithms. However, the practical significance is currently limited by the two major issues: the need to know κ and the high `O(κ²)` stochastic gradient cost. For the finite sum case (`F(w) = 1/n Σ f_i(w)`), methods like SVRG or Catalyst-accelerated SVRG often provide better overall complexity `(O((n+κ)log(1/ε)))` without needing to know κ a priori. Therefore, it is unclear if there is a practical regime where EMGD would be the method of choice over existing state-of-the-art methods. The advantages of handling constraints and low memory are valid but may not be enough to overcome the other limitations.

In conclusion, this is a solid theoretical paper with an interesting and novel result. The goal is important, and the analysis is sound. However, its practical impact is questionable due to strong assumptions and potentially unfavorable complexity compared to closely related, existing methods.
