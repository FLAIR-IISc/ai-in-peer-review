

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization that reduces the number of full gradient evaluations required from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. The approach combines full gradients (with limited access) and stochastic gradients (with frequent access) to achieve linear convergence. The authors build upon prior work on epoch gradient descent [9] and recent advances in stochastic optimization [22, 23], positioning their method as a bridge between full-gradient and stochastic-gradient approaches. They provide theoretical guarantees showing EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradient accesses and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient accesses.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis with a complete convergence proof (Theorem 1). The proof is well-structured, utilizing appropriate lemmas and carefully bounding key terms (AT and BT). The analysis properly accounts for both deterministic and stochastic components of the algorithm.

2. **Novelty:** The paper clearly articulates the novelty of reducing full gradient access from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) while maintaining linear convergence. This is distinct from previous approaches like SAG [22] and SDCA [23], which only use stochastic gradients. The paper adequately references related work, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent [17] and recent stochastic methods [9, 10, 21].

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear section headings. The algorithm is presented with pseudocode (Algorithm 1), and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The proof techniques are properly explained, making the theoretical contributions accessible to experts.

4. **Significance:** The problem addressed is important in large-scale optimization where full gradient computation is expensive. The paper demonstrates that EMGD can be computationally superior to full-gradient methods when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is reasonable in many machine learning applications (e.g., with regularization).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Experimental Validation:** The paper contains no empirical results to support the theoretical claims. Without experiments, it's difficult to assess the practical benefits of EMGD, especially regarding the claimed computational advantages over SAG and SDCA. This is a significant omission in a machine learning conference like NeurIPS.

2. **Practical Limitation:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ beforehand, which is often not available in practice. While the paper mentions this limitation and suggests future work, it doesn't provide a practical method for estimating κ. This severely limits the algorithm's applicability.

3. **Assumptions:** The paper assumes that full gradients are n times more expensive than stochastic gradients (Table 2), but doesn't justify this assumption or provide evidence for it. This assumption is critical to the computational complexity comparison but lacks empirical support.

4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares EMGD to SAG and SDCA, it doesn't address how EMGD might compare to other recent methods like SVRG [26] or Katyusha [27], which also aim to reduce full gradient evaluations.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

*For acceptance:*
- Presents a theoretically sound algorithm with clear novelty in reducing full gradient access.
- Provides rigorous convergence guarantees for a non-trivial optimization problem.
- Well-written and properly contextualized within the existing literature.
- Addresses an important practical problem in large-scale optimization.

*Against acceptance:*
- Lacks experimental validation to support theoretical claims.
- Contains a significant practical limitation (requiring knowledge of κ).
- Makes strong assumptions about computational costs without empirical justification.
- Does not adequately address how to estimate κ in practice.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to convex optimization with a clear theoretical advance. The core idea of combining full and stochastic gradients to reduce full gradient access is novel and well-supported by analysis. However, the absence of empirical validation and the practical limitation of requiring knowledge of κ before optimization significantly weaken the paper's contribution. While the theoretical results are strong, the lack of practical demonstration and the unaddressed limitation of κ knowledge make it difficult to assess the real-world value of this approach.

The paper would benefit from additional experimental results comparing EMGD to state-of-the-art methods on standard machine learning problems, as well as a more detailed discussion of practical methods for estimating κ. Without these elements, the paper's impact is limited to theoretical optimization rather than providing a practical algorithm for machine learning practitioners.

Given the conference's focus on both theoretical and practical advances in machine learning, the lack of experimental validation is a significant drawback. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the absence of empirical results and the unaddressed limitation of κ knowledge prevent this paper from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.
