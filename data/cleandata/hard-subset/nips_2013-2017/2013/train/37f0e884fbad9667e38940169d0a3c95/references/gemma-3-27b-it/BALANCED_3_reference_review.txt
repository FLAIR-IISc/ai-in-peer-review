This paper introduces a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods in smooth, strongly convex optimization by strategically incorporating stochastic gradients. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradients within epochs, aiming for a linear convergence rate with a reduced dependency on the condition number κ.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the trade-off between the iteration complexity of full-gradient versus stochastic gradient methods and the computational expense of each gradient evaluation. The problem addressed is relevant and important, especially in large-scale machine learning applications where full gradient computations are costly. The connection to existing work, including epoch gradient descent (EGD), SAG, and SDCA, is appropriately discussed, and the authors clearly position their contribution within the landscape of first-order optimization methods. The theorem establishing the convergence rate is a central result, and the authors attempt to quantify the computational complexity gains over existing algorithms, particularly Nesterov's method.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly establishes the problem and justifies the motivation for seeking algorithms that reduce the reliance on expensive full gradients.
* **Novelty:**  The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework appears novel and represents a potentially valuable approach.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a convergence analysis, proving a linear convergence rate under specific assumptions. While the proof details are extensive (as expected), the main theorem is clearly stated.
* **Comparisons to Related Work:** The authors make a reasonable attempt to compare their algorithm's computational complexity to that of other methods like Nesterov's, SAG, and SDCA, highlighting potential advantages.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The concluding remarks acknowledge the need for practical estimation of the condition number κ and suggest avenues for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The reliance on knowing the condition number κ beforehand significantly limits the practical applicability of the algorithm. While the authors acknowledge this and suggest future work addressing it, it remains a substantial hurdle.
* **Constant Factors:**  The analysis focuses on asymptotic complexity (O-notation). The constants hidden within the O-notation are crucial in practical performance, and these are not thoroughly discussed.  For instance, the factor of κ² in the number of stochastic gradients might be substantial in ill-conditioned problems.
* **Proof Details & Readability:** While mathematically sound, the proof in Section 4 is very dense and difficult to follow without significant effort. More intermediate steps and explanations would enhance readability.  The notation is also quite heavy.
* **Empirical Evaluation Missing:**  A significant weakness is the complete lack of empirical evaluation. Without experimental results, it is difficult to assess the practical performance of EMGD compared to existing methods, even with the theoretical analysis.  The authors claim potential improvements, but these remain unverified.
* **Algorithm 1 - Clarity:**  Step 8 involves minimizing over a constrained domain. The efficiency of this step is not discussed.  If solving this constrained minimization is expensive, it could negate the benefits of reducing full-gradient evaluations.  Furthermore, the use of `∆k` as a shrinking radius doesn't have clear justification and its impact on the convergence is not discussed as intuitively as it could be.
* **Comparison Tables - Limited Scope:** Table 2 only compares computational complexities, not runtime. Furthermore, it only covers a few algorithms. A more comprehensive comparison to other relevant algorithms, including variance reduction methods, would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The algorithm presents a novel approach to reducing the cost of full-gradient optimization.
* The theoretical analysis demonstrates a linear convergence rate, which is a strong result.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivations and contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumption of knowing the condition number κ significantly limits the algorithm's practicality.
* The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical performance and potential benefits.
* The dense and complex proof and lack of clarity surrounding certain algorithmic steps (e.g. Step 8) hinders understanding.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting algorithm with a potentially valuable contribution to the field of optimization. However, the strong assumption regarding the condition number and the absence of empirical evaluation raise significant concerns. While the paper demonstrates technical soundness and originality, it falls short of demonstrating practical significance.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Address the Condition Number Assumption:** Investigate methods for estimating the condition number online or adapt the algorithm to work effectively without prior knowledge of κ.
* **Include Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct thorough experiments comparing EMGD to state-of-the-art optimization algorithms on a variety of datasets and problem sizes.
* **Improve Proof Readability:**  Break down the proof into smaller, more manageable steps with more intuitive explanations.
* **Clarify Algorithm 1:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the constrained minimization step in Algorithm 1.
* **Expand Comparative Analysis:**  Include a more comprehensive comparison to a wider range of algorithms, considering runtime performance and practical considerations.
* **Discuss Limitations:** Explicitly address the limitations of the algorithm and potential scenarios where it might not perform well.
