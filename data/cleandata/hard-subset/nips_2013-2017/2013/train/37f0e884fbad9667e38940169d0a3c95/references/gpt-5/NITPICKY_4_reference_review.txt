Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for minimizing smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives with access to two oracles: a full gradient oracle Og and a “function oracle” Of returning a random smooth function f such that F(w) = E[f(w)]. EMGD runs in epochs; each epoch computes one full gradient at the epoch anchor w̄k and T “mixed” gradients of the form ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), with updates restricted to a shrinking ball around w̄k. The main theorem shows linear convergence with high probability: after m epochs, F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ λΔ1^2 / 2^{m+1}, using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ))). The appeal is that the number of full gradients is independent of κ.

Relation to prior work
The mixed gradient estimator is essentially a control variate identical to SVRG’s estimator: ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k) + ∇F(w̄k). This literature (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013; prox-SVRG variants) is not cited. In finite-sum settings, SVRG and SAGA achieve linear convergence with total gradient complexity O((n + κ) log(1/ε)), also using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, typically with better κ dependence (κ rather than κ^2) and without shrinking domains. SAG and SDCA are discussed, but the omission of SVRG leads to an incomplete and potentially misleading positioning.

Quality (soundness)
The high-level proof strategy—variance reduction via a control variate plus variance control via shrinking balls—can work. However, there are numerous technical and presentation issues that need careful correction:
- Strong convexity: The paper states “F is λ-strongly convex, that is, ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖” (Eq. 4). This is not the standard definition. While λ-strong convexity implies this lower bound via Cauchy-Schwarz, the paper should use the standard form ⟨∇F(w) − ∇F(w′), w − w′⟩ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖^2 and derive consequences as needed.
- Factor-of-two inconsistencies:
  - In (13) the inequality F(wt) − F(w*) ≤ ⟨∇F(wt), wt − w*⟩ − λ‖wt − w*‖^2 should have the coefficient λ/2 under the usual strong convexity inequality.
  - L-smoothness is used as F(y) ≤ F(x) + ⟨∇F(x), y − x⟩ + L‖y − x‖^2 in (16); standard is L/2.
  - The proximal subproblem in Algorithm 1/eq. (11) uses η⟨w − wt, g̃t⟩ + ‖w − wt‖^2, without the usual 1/(2η) scaling and with missing 1/2 in the quadratic. The claim that the objective is “1-strongly convex” doesn’t match the written quadratic term. These mismatches propagate into constants and may affect correctness.
- Probability/notation issues:
  - Theorem 1 requires T ≥ 1152 L^2/λ^2 ln δ, but this must be ln(1/δ) since ln δ < 0 for δ < 1. This sign error appears multiple times.
  - Equation numbers are reused (two “(2)”s), and some references are internally inconsistent.
- Assumptions and oracle model:
  - The Of oracle returning a function f to be reused at multiple points is stronger than a standard stochastic gradient oracle; this should be clearly highlighted when comparing to methods that assume only stochastic gradients at queried points.
  - The algorithm and proof require knowing κ (via L and λ) to set T and η and to initialize Δ1 with a non-constructive bound involving F(w*). Practical strategies (doubling/epoch-adaptive choices) are not provided beyond brief remarks.
- Some steps are overly loose, yielding κ^2 dependence (via η = 1/(L√T) and Azuma bound). Known analyses (e.g., SVRG) achieve O(κ) stochastic steps per epoch with constant stepsize without shrinking domains. It seems likely your analysis can be tightened substantially.

Clarity
The manuscript has many grammatical, typographical, and formatting issues that impede readability:
- Frequent typos and awkward phrasing: “epoches,” “interstage,” inconsistent spacing around ε, κ, logs; “In other words, … log 1 ǫ” vs “log 1ǫ.”
- Missing constants (1/2) and inconsistent norms/strength claims as noted.
- Footnote 1 (about SDCA) is incorrect/misleading and appears inserted mid-text in the algorithm section, disrupting flow. SDCA typically requires strong convexity of the overall objective (e.g., via ℓ2-regularization), not each fi.
- Table 2 is referenced but not included; some inequalities in the comparison section are hard to verify without the table. A stray “n2.2” appears to be a formatting artifact.
- References are incomplete (many missing venue/year/pages) and key related work (SVRG) is not cited.

Originality
The specific estimator and epoch structure are very close to SVRG; the main difference is the shrinking trust-region to control variance in a general function-oracle setting. Without citing SVRG, the novelty is hard to assess. As written, the claimed contribution “condition-number independent number of full gradients” is already achieved by SVRG in the finite-sum setting, and your κ^2 stochastic complexity is worse. If the goal is to go beyond finite-sum to expectation settings with reusable f(·), that distinction must be made crisp and contrasted with contemporaneous variance-reduction methods for general expectations.

Significance
Reducing full-gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)) is useful, but the practical impact is limited by:
- κ^2 dependence (vs. κ in the state of the art),
- stronger oracle requirements (function oracle),
- need to know κ and initialize Δ1,
- absence of experiments illustrating computational benefits in realistic settings and against SVRG/SAGA/SDCA.
Moreover, several claims in the comparison section (e.g., “high probability is much stronger than expectation”) are overstated without a fair discussion; most linear-rate methods can be equipped with high-probability guarantees.

Pros
- Clear high-level goal: reduce expensive full-gradient calls while retaining linear convergence.
- High-probability analysis via martingale bounds is conceptually appealing.
- Handles constrained domains (projection onto W ∩ ball), which is less common in variance-reduced methods and worth emphasizing.

Cons (acceptance-critical)
- Missing and mispositioned related work (notably SVRG); the core estimator is not novel.
- κ^2 stochastic gradient complexity and shrinking-domain mechanism are uncompetitive with existing methods.
- Numerous technical inaccuracies (strong convexity, smoothness constants, ln δ vs ln 1/δ) and formatting errors.
- Practicality concerns (oracle model, parameter knowledge, missing experiments).
- Several comparison statements are imprecise or incorrect (SDCA assumptions, storage/time claims).

Questions for authors
1. Please position EMGD against SVRG/Prox-SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and related variance-reduction methods. Can your analysis be tightened to O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch without domain shrinkage?
2. Can you provide an adaptive scheme removing the need to know κ and Δ1?
3. In the expectation setting with function oracle Of, can you formalize when such an oracle is realistic versus standard stochastic gradient access?
4. Please correct the factor-of-two issues throughout and the ln(1/δ) sign errors, and provide the missing Table 2 and complete references.
5. Empirical validation would help substantiate the claimed computational advantages.

Recommendation: Reject
While the goal is worthwhile, the paper in its current form suffers from (i) incomplete and inaccurate positioning relative to closely related prior work (especially SVRG), (ii) numerous technical and presentation errors, and (iii) inferior κ-dependence. Substantial revision is needed for correctness, clarity, and fair comparison. My confidence is high.
