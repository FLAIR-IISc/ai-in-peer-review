Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth, strongly convex optimization that has access to both a full-gradient oracle Og (returning ∇F(w)) and a function oracle Of (returning a random smooth component f with Ef[f(w)] = F(w)). EMGD runs in epochs: at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient ∇F at a reference point w̄k; within the epoch it uses a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), updates with projected gradient steps onto a shrinking ball around w̄k, averages iterates, and shrinks the radius by √2 across epochs. The main theorem shows linear convergence with high probability: to reach ε-suboptimality, EMGD needs O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, where κ = L/λ. The paper positions EMGD as reducing dependence on κ in terms of the number of full-gradient calls, and contrasts it with full-gradient methods (e.g., Nesterov) and recent incremental methods (SAG, SDCA).

Relation to prior work
The mixed gradient estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is essentially the variance-reduced estimator used by SVRG-type methods. While the paper cites SAG and SDCA, it does not discuss closely related SVRG/SAGA variants that also compute one full gradient per epoch and enjoy linear convergence with O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) complexity in finite-sum settings, and O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations. The methodological core of EMGD (occasional full gradients plus variance reduction around a snapshot) is therefore not clearly novel relative to this line of work. The paper’s addition of shrinking-ball projections and a high-probability analysis are differences, but their practical and theoretical necessity is not well justified.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal: reduce the number of expensive full-gradient calls to be independent of κ, while maintaining linear convergence.
- Theoretical analysis with high-probability guarantees; the proof is reasonably self-contained.
- Algorithm is memory-light (no O(n) storage) and can handle simple constraints via projection onto W ∩ ball.
- Discussion of distributed computation for the full-gradient step is sensible.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to established variance-reduced methods is unclear. The core gradient estimator and epoch structure mirror SVRG; yet those works are not cited or contrasted. Moreover, SVRG achieves per-epoch contraction with T = O(κ) inner steps in the strongly convex case, whereas EMGD requires T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)). This κ^2 factor appears suboptimal and suggests the analysis (and possibly the algorithmic device of shrinking domains) is loose.
- No empirical evaluation. The central claim is a computational trade-off (few full gradients, many cheap stochastic gradients). Without experiments on ill-conditioned problems and varying n/κ, it is difficult to assess practical benefits against SAG/SDCA/SVRG/SAGA or even accelerated GD. The comparison table asserts regimes where EMGD is competitive, but evidence is theoretical and some claims (e.g., “may be not the best one, but advantages in other aspects”) remain speculative.
- Assumptions and practicality: the need to know κ (or tight L, λ) to set T and η; the use of a shrinking ball around the snapshot; and the requirement of both Og and Of. In finite-sum problems, Og corresponds to summing over n, which the method seeks to reduce, but in general expectation settings access to Og may be unrealistic, while state-of-the-art methods do not assume such an oracle. The constrained projection onto a shrinking domain is also unusual and may complicate implementation; its necessity is not convincingly argued.
- Clarity and presentation: some statements are nonstandard or potentially incorrect. For example, the condition “‖∇F(w)−∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖” is not the usual definition of strong convexity; the standard inequality is ⟨∇F(w)−∇F(w′), w−w′⟩ ≥ λ‖w−w′‖^2. While the proof later uses standard strong convexity, the presentation is confusing. There are also typos and notational glitches (inconsistent numbering, footnotes, etc.). The comparison to SAG/SDCA asserts “high-probability” vs “expectation” as a decisive advantage; in practice, high-probability bounds can also be established for variance-reduced methods under similar assumptions.

Quality
Technically, the proof technique (martingale concentration to control the variance term, epoch-wise contraction) is standard and appears sound given the assumptions. However, the κ^2 inner-iteration requirement suggests either a suboptimal analysis or an unnecessary projection device. The lack of experiments and omission of comparisons to highly relevant VR methods hurt the credibility and completeness.

Clarity
The paper is readable but has several imprecise statements (strong convexity), minor errors, and lacks a clean comparison to closely related methods. The algorithm could be simplified in presentation (projected gradient step as projection of wt − η g̃ onto W ∩ ball).

Originality
Limited. The mixed gradient idea and epochic structure are well known (SVRG and successors). The main differentiators (shrinking-ball constraint and high-probability bound) do not convincingly advance the state of the art and come with worse κ dependence.

Significance
Moderate to low in current form. The aim—reducing full-gradient calls—is important, but stronger results already exist with better κ dependence and broader adoption. Without empirical validation or sharper theory, impact is unlikely.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant computational trade-off.
- Memory-efficient and handles constraints.
- High-probability convergence analysis.

Cons
- Overlaps heavily with SVRG/SAGA-style variance reduction; missing citations and comparisons.
- Suboptimal κ^2 dependence; unclear necessity of shrinking-domain mechanism.
- No experiments to substantiate practical gains.
- Strong assumptions (known κ, dual oracles) and presentation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate EMGD relative to SVRG/SAGA and clarify genuine novelty; ideally tighten analysis to O(κ) inner steps or explain why κ^2 is unavoidable here.
- Remove or justify the shrinking-ball projection; show how it affects theory and practice.
- Provide empirical results on ill-conditioned problems comparing wall-clock and oracle calls to Nesterov, SAG, SDCA, SVRG/SAGA, including constrained cases if that is a selling point.
- Discuss adaptive choices for T and η without knowing κ; or provide a robust tuning strategy.
- Correct the strong convexity statements and improve clarity.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. The paper’s core idea is not sufficiently distinct from established variance-reduction methods, the theoretical bounds are weaker (κ^2), key related work is missing, and there is no empirical validation. With stronger comparisons, tighter analysis, and experiments, a revised version could be more compelling.
