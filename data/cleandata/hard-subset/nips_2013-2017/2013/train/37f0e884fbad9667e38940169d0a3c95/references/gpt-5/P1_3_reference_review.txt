Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies first-order methods for minimizing a smooth, λ-strongly convex objective F over a convex set W. The key goal is to reduce the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations while retaining a linear convergence rate. The authors propose Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which proceeds in epochs; at the start of each epoch k it computes one full gradient ∇F(w̄k), then runs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(⋅)(wt) − ∇f(⋅)(w̄k), where f is a random function from an oracle with Ef[f] = F. Updates are projected onto an ℓ2-ball B(w̄k; Δk) whose radius shrinks by √2 per epoch, and the step size is η = 1/(L√T). With T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)), they prove a constant-factor decrease of the suboptimality per epoch with high probability, yielding after m epochs: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.

The paper positions itself against: (i) full-gradient accelerated methods with O(√κ log(1/ε)) iteration complexity, (ii) stochastic methods with polynomial dependence on 1/ε, and (iii) finite-sum methods with linear rates such as SAG and SDCA. The proposed mixed estimator is exactly the control-variates gradient used in variance-reduced methods (e.g., SVRG of Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013; Prox-SVRG; SAGA). Compared to those, EMGD achieves linear convergence but with T scaling as κ^2 rather than κ, and uses shrinking trust regions and high-probability concentration arguments to control variance.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The high-level proof strategy (per-epoch contraction via smoothness, strong convexity, and a martingale bound) is standard and appears correct. The use of a shrinking ball to bound the variance term and enable Hoeffding–Azuma is neat and yields a clean high-probability guarantee.
- However, there are issues to address:
  - The paper states as the definition of strong convexity the inequality ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖, which is not the standard definition (and is stronger than needed). The analysis later rightly uses the standard inequality F(w*) ≥ F(w) + ⟨∇F(w), w* − w⟩ + (λ/2)‖w − w*‖^2; the definition should be corrected.
  - The cost model assumes a “function oracle” that returns a random f from which gradients at two points can be computed in one “call.” In finite-sum settings this is reasonable (two gradients for the same i), but the accounting should be explicit (one inner step effectively computes two component gradients).
  - The algorithm requires knowing κ (to set T) and an initial radius Δ1 that depends on F(w*), both impractical; while the conclusion acknowledges κ, the paper should propose concrete adaptive schemes (e.g., doubling/halving schedules) and initialization strategies independent of F(w*).
  - The projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) may be nontrivial for general W; the claimed advantage for constrained problems depends on this being efficiently computable.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable, and the main theorem and its proof are laid out in detail. Some notation and typesetting need fixing (e.g., the proximal update objective in Step 8 seems misformatted; ensure the quadratic term is (1/2η)‖w − wt‖^2). “Epochs” is misspelled in several places. The strong convexity definition should be corrected and the assumption on smoothness/strong convexity made precise (e.g., Hessian bounds).
- Comparisons would benefit from a uniform, explicit cost model: count full gradients, component gradients, and memory, and clarify whether a “function oracle call” entails one or two component gradient evaluations.

Originality:
- The gradient estimator is identical to SVRG’s control variate: ∇F(w̃) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̃). EMGD’s novelty lies in the shrinking ball and the high-probability analysis, but algorithmically it overlaps substantially with existing variance-reduced methods. Importantly, the paper does not cite or compare to SVRG/Prox-SVRG/SAGA, which achieve linear convergence with O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) component-gradient evaluations in finite-sum problems and require only O(κ) inner steps per epoch (vs. EMGD’s O(κ^2)). This omission is a significant gap in related work and comparison.
- The claimed advantages over SAG/SDCA (memory, constraints, high-probability rates) also apply to SVRG/Prox-SVRG; this should be acknowledged and discussed.

Significance:
- The goal—reducing the number of full gradients to O(log(1/ε))—is valuable. The high-probability guarantee and low memory footprint are useful. However, the stochastic-gradient complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) is suboptimal relative to the O(κ log(1/ε)) of SVRG/Prox-SVRG and the O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) of SDCA/SAGA, and there are no experiments to demonstrate practical advantages (e.g., constrained problems, distributed full-gradient computation). As it stands, the contribution is incremental and weaker than the current state of the art.

Pros:
- Clear motivation to reduce full-gradient usage; linear convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients.
- Simple algorithm; low memory; applies to constrained settings if projections are available.
- High-probability convergence proof with transparent use of concentration.

Cons:
- Overlap with existing variance-reduction methods; missing crucial related work (SVRG/Prox-SVRG/SAGA) and comparisons.
- Worse dependence on κ (κ^2) than best-known methods; requires knowledge of κ and Δ1.
- No empirical evaluation; some technical inaccuracies (strong convexity definition; formatting).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add and discuss comparisons with SVRG/Prox-SVRG/SAGA (the estimator is the same), including theoretical complexities and practical trade-offs. Can your analysis be tightened to achieve T = O(κ) without shrinking balls?
- Correct the definition of strong convexity and clean up the update’s mathematical expression.
- Provide adaptive schemes to remove the need for κ and Δ1 (e.g., increasing T until sufficient decrease; doubling/halving epoch length; adaptive radii).
- Clarify the oracle cost model and the cost of computing two component gradients per inner step.
- Include experiments on both unconstrained and constrained problems, and possibly distributed settings, to substantiate practical benefits.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. The paper’s main idea is not sufficiently novel relative to established variance-reduced methods, and its κ^2 dependence and lack of empirical validation weaken its significance. With a tighter analysis, proper positioning w.r.t. prior art, and experiments, a revised version could be competitive.
