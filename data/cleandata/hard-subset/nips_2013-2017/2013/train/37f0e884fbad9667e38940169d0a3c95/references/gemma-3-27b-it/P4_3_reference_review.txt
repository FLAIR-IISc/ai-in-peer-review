This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex functions, aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations by leveraging stochastic gradients. The core idea is to divide the optimization process into epochs, performing one full gradient evaluation and O(κ²) stochastic gradient evaluations per epoch, achieving a linear convergence rate and reducing the number of full gradient calls to O(log 1/ǫ), independent of the condition number κ. The paper positions this work as bridging the gap between the faster convergence of full gradient methods and the computational efficiency of stochastic gradient methods.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The algorithm is clearly presented, and the convergence analysis, while dense, seems logically structured and builds upon existing results in the field. The theorem statements are precise, and the proof sketch outlines a reasonable inductive argument. However, the details within the proof, particularly relating to bounding terms AT and BT using Hoeffding-Azuma's inequality, are compressed and might require substantial effort to fully verify. The authors acknowledge a limitation – the need to know or estimate the condition number κ beforehand – and briefly suggest future directions for addressing this. The completeness of the work is reasonable for a conference paper; it presents a novel algorithm, analysis, and comparison to related methods. The authors acknowledge potential drawbacks and future work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and the proposed approach. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is clearly presented in a numbered list. However, the convergence analysis section is quite dense and requires a strong background in optimization to fully grasp. More detailed explanations of key steps in the proof, particularly around bounding terms like AT and BT, would improve clarity. The connections between different steps in the proof aren't always explicit, and a reader unfamiliar with similar analyses might find it challenging to follow. While the related work section is comprehensive, it’s presented as a list of references with brief descriptions, rather than a more narrative overview of the field’s evolution.

**Originality:** The core idea of combining full and stochastic gradients in an epoch-based manner to achieve condition number independence in the number of full gradient evaluations is novel. While epoch-based gradient descent has been explored (cited as [9]), the specific combination with stochastic gradients and the resulting convergence guarantees appear to be a new contribution. The paper appropriately references related work in both deterministic and stochastic optimization, positioning its contribution within the existing literature. The comparisons to SAG and SDCA are relevant and highlight the trade-offs of the proposed method.

**Significance:** The results have the potential to be significant, particularly for large-scale convex optimization problems where full gradient evaluations are expensive and the condition number is large. Reducing the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log 1/ǫ) to O(log 1/ǫ) can lead to substantial computational savings. The algorithm's applicability to both constrained and unconstrained problems, and its potential for parallelization, further enhances its practical relevance. However, the reliance on knowing or accurately estimating the condition number κ is a practical limitation that needs to be addressed. While the paper mentions future work in this direction, it remains to be seen how effectively this limitation can be overcome.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel algorithm with a clear theoretical guarantee.
*   Addresses a practical problem in large-scale optimization (reducing full gradient evaluations).
*   Technically sound analysis with a well-defined convergence rate.
*   Properly contextualized within the existing literature.
*   Potential for significant computational savings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The convergence analysis is dense and potentially difficult to verify in full detail.
*   The reliance on knowing/estimating the condition number κ is a practical limitation.
*   The related work section, while comprehensive, lacks a cohesive narrative.
*   The proofs are abbreviated, and some steps lack sufficient explanation.



**Overall Assessment:**  This is a solid paper with a novel contribution to the field of convex optimization. The algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and has the potential for practical impact. While the convergence analysis is dense and the condition number dependence is a limitation, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** contingent on the authors addressing the clarity issues in the convergence analysis section, potentially by expanding on key steps and providing more intuitive explanations. Further, clarifying the practical implications of needing to estimate κ would be beneficial.
