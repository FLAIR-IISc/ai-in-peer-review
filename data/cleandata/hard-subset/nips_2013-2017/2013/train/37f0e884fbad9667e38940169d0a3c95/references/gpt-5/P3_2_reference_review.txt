Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for minimizing smooth, strongly convex objectives F over a convex set W, when two oracles are available: (i) a full-gradient oracle Og that returns ∇F(w), and (ii) a stochastic oracle Of that returns a random smooth function f with Ef[f(w)] = F(w), allowing stochastic gradients. EMGD proceeds in epochs: at the start of each epoch it computes a full gradient at a snapshot point w̄k; within the epoch it performs projected gradient steps using a “mixed” variance-reduced gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), but constrains iterates to a shrinking ball centered at w̄k. The analysis shows linear convergence: O(log(1/ε)) calls to Og and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) calls to Of suffice to reach ε-suboptimality with high probability, where κ = L/λ is the condition number. The paper positions EMGD as a way to make the number of full-gradient evaluations independent of κ, trading them for cheaper stochastic evaluations.

Relation to prior work
The mixed gradient g̃ is the core idea behind variance-reduction methods. Closely related finite-sum methods include SAG (Roux et al., 2012) and SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013), both cited, which attain linear convergence. A very relevant omission is SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which also forms g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f_i(w) − ∇f_i(w̄), computes one full gradient per epoch, uses O(κ) inner steps, O(d) memory, and achieves O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) component-gradient evaluations with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient computations—i.e., the same qualitative independence of the number of full gradients from κ that EMGD claims. More recent variants (e.g., SAGA, Prox-SVRG) similarly achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)). The paper does differentiate itself from SAG/SDCA via memory and constraints support, but it does not compare to SVRG, which also uses O(d) space and readily handles constraints via projections.

Strengths
- Clear goal and motivation: reduce the number of full-gradient calls in ill-conditioned problems by leveraging cheap stochastic gradients.
- The algorithm and analysis are conceptually simple. The shrinking-domain device cleanly bounds the stochastic term and yields high-probability linear convergence.
- The result applies beyond the finite-sum setting, to any distributional oracle Of where f is smooth and unbiased, provided Og is available.
- High-probability guarantees (via Azuma–Hoeffding) are a nice aspect versus purely in-expectation rates.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty relative to SVRG: The mixed gradient update is essentially identical to SVRG’s estimator. EMGD’s distinctive ingredient is the shrinking ball constraint and the κ^2 scaling of inner iterations. However, the paper neither cites nor compares to SVRG, which already achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and superior total complexity O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) without the κ^2 factor. This omission undermines the originality/significance claims, especially for finite-sum problems where Og corresponds to summing n component gradients.
- Complexity suboptimality: The dependence O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) on the number of stochastic gradients is much worse than O(κ log(1/ε)) achieved by SVRG/SAGA/SDCA. The paper’s own comparison to SAG/SDCA suggests competitiveness only when κ ≤ n^{1/2}; against SVRG, EMGD is dominated for most regimes.
- Oracle model practicality: Requiring access to both Og and Of is restrictive. In many large-scale problems F is an expectation over an unknown distribution (streaming), in which case Og is not available; in finite-sum problems, Og costs Θ(n) calls to Of, so the benefit must be argued against methods that use n component gradients per epoch optimally (e.g., SVRG). The paper discusses relative costs but does not provide empirical evidence to substantiate the tradeoff.
- Assumptions/parameters: The method requires knowing κ to set T, and uses Δ1 ≥ max{√(2/λ)(F(0) − F(w*))}, which depends on the unknown optimum value. The paper acknowledges this and suggests heuristics, but the current algorithm is not parameter-free.
- Constrained projection: Each inner step projects onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk), which can be significantly more expensive than projection onto W alone and is nontrivial in many problems. Claims about ease of handling constraints should discuss this cost more carefully.
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript has numerous typos and formatting issues (e.g., “epoches”, missing reference details, equation numbering inconsistencies, ln δ vs ln(1/δ), and some nonstandard statements such as defining strong convexity via gradient norm lower bounds). Related work coverage should be expanded (SVRG in particular). No experiments are provided, even small-scale synthetic ones, to illustrate the computational tradeoffs.

Technical soundness
The high-level proof technique (variance-reduced estimator + shrinking domain + martingale concentration) is standard and plausible. The induction argument and bounds appear consistent, yielding the stated T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)). The use of Hoeffding–Azuma requires bounded martingale differences, which the shrinking domain provides. Some constants are loose (e.g., 1152) but acceptable for theory. However, the absence of comparison to sharper analyses achieving κ rather than κ^2 suggests the rate is not tight. Also, the claim that strong convexity “implies” ||∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)|| ≥ λ ||w − w′|| is a consequence of strong monotonicity, but it would be clearer to state strong convexity in its standard form and derive what is needed.

Significance
The idea of reducing dependence on full-gradient evaluations is important. However, given existing variance-reduced methods that already achieve O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient computations with better total complexity and similar memory, the incremental contribution appears limited unless the authors can (a) clearly position EMGD as extending SVRG-style methods to a more general oracle model (and justify the need for Og in that model), (b) remove the κ^2 factor, or (c) demonstrate substantial empirical wall-clock gains in regimes where Of is much cheaper than Og and constraints matter.

Suggestions for improvement
- Cite and compare thoroughly to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and, if within scope, SAGA/Prox-SVRG. Discuss similarities of the estimator and differences (shrinking domain, high-probability guarantees).
- Aim to improve the stochastic gradient complexity from O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) to O(κ log(1/ε)), possibly by refining the analysis without domain shrinking, or by combining with acceleration/prox techniques.
- Provide experiments on finite-sum problems (logistic regression, ridge) comparing EMGD to AGD, SVRG, SAG, and SDCA across varying κ and cost ratios of Og vs Of, including constrained settings if that is a claimed advantage.
- Address parameter selection (κ, Δ1) with practical, provable strategies (e.g., doubling tricks, adaptive epoch lengths) and discuss projection costs onto W ∩ ball.
- Improve clarity and fix typographical and referencing issues; present standard definitions and clean proofs.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear objective; simple algorithm; high-probability linear convergence.
- Potentially broader oracle model than finite-sum; low memory; discussion of constraints.

Cons:
- Overlaps substantially with SVRG methodology; key prior work not cited.
- Inferior κ^2 dependence; unclear practical benefit without experiments.
- Parameter dependence and projection complexity; presentation issues.

Verdict
Leaning to reject in its current form due to missing critical related work and weaker complexity. With a tighter rate, proper positioning vs SVRG/SAGA/SDCA, and experimental validation, the contribution could be made significantly stronger.
