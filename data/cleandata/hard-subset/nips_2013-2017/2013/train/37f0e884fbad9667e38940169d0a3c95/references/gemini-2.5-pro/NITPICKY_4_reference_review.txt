### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a new first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex objective functions. The primary goal is to reduce the number of full gradient computations, making it independent of the problem's condition number, κ. The algorithm operates in epochs, where each epoch consists of one full gradient evaluation followed by O(κ²) stochastic gradient steps. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate, requiring O(log 1/ε) full gradient and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradient evaluations to find an ε-optimal solution. This is contrasted with accelerated gradient methods which require O(√κ log 1/ε) full gradient evaluations. The paper also compares EMGD to methods for finite-sum problems like SAG and SDCA, claiming advantages in handling constraints, memory requirements, and providing high-probability convergence guarantees.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:**
The paper addresses an important problem: mitigating the high computational cost of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned settings. The theoretical result—achieving a number of full gradient evaluations independent of κ—is appealing. The derivation of a high-probability convergence bound, rather than a bound in expectation, is also a notable strength.

However, the paper suffers from several significant technical and methodological flaws that undermine its contributions.

1.  **Major Technical Error:** Equation (4) states that for a λ-strongly convex function, `‖∇F (w)−∇F (w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w −w′‖`. This property is not true for general strongly convex functions; it is a defining property of L-smoothness (with L instead of λ), and its inverse holds only for very specific functions (e.g., quadratics). The standard definition of strong convexity is `F(w') ≥ F(w) + <∇F(w), w'-w> + (λ/2) ||w'-w||²` or the equivalent `<∇F(w) - ∇F(w'), w - w'> ≥ λ||w-w'||²`. Stating an incorrect property as a basis for the problem setup raises serious concerns about the paper's technical rigor. While the analysis may implicitly rely on the correct definitions, this error is fundamental.

2.  **Dependence on Unknown Parameters:** The algorithm's key parameters, particularly the number of inner iterations `T`, depend directly on the smoothness constant `L` and the strong convexity constant `λ` (i.e., `T` is proportional to `κ²`). In most practical scenarios, these constants are unknown. The paper acknowledges this as a drawback in the conclusion, but relegates the solution to "future work" with only vague suggestions. This significantly limits the practical applicability of the proposed method.

3.  **Potentially Loose Analysis:** The constant in the requirement for `T`, `1152`, is exceptionally large. While often such constants can be improved, its magnitude suggests that the analysis may be quite loose, potentially making the practical requirements on the number of inner loop iterations prohibitive even if κ were known.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally readable, but there are several points of confusion and a lack of polish.

1.  **Inconsistent Notation:** The symbol for the desired accuracy `ε` is written as `ǫ` throughout the paper, which is non-standard and visually jarring.
2.  **Incomplete/Unclear Formatting:** The list of factors in the introduction is formatted with bullet points (`.`) that are not standard. Footnote 2 in Section 3.1 appears to be incomplete, containing only the number "2.".
3.  **Algorithmic Details:** In Algorithm 1, Step 10, the new solution `w̄k+1` is defined as the average of `T+1` points, which includes the starting point of the epoch, `w_1^k = w̄^k`. This is an unusual choice compared to standard averaging schemes and is not explicitly justified.

**Originality:**
This is the paper's most significant weakness. The proposed "mixed gradient" estimator `g̃kt = ∇F (w̄k) +∇fkt (wkt )−∇fkt (w̄k)` is precisely the estimator used in the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). The overall EMGD algorithm can be interpreted as a variant of SVRG, where SVRG's outer loop is combined with a shrinking projection ball.

The paper completely fails to cite or discuss SVRG, SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014), or other highly relevant variance-reduced methods. These methods also achieve linear convergence for finite-sum problems using a similar gradient estimator. While EMGD is presented in a more general stochastic setting (not limited to finite sums) and provides a high-probability bound, the core algorithmic idea is not novel. This omission misrepresents the contribution of the paper and its place within the literature. The comparison is limited to older methods or methods like SAG/SDCA, while ignoring the most structurally similar work.

**Significance:**
The goal of the paper is significant. However, due to the issues with originality and practical implementation (dependence on κ), the actual impact is questionable. The claimed advantages over SAG/SDCA (handling constraints, memory) are also shared by SVRG. The primary novel contribution appears to be the high-probability analysis for this type of variance-reduced estimator in a general stochastic and constrained setting. While theoretically interesting, this contribution is overshadowed by the failure to acknowledge the prior art for the core algorithm and the unresolved practical issues.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The high-probability convergence guarantee is a theoretical improvement over the in-expectation results common for similar variance-reduced methods.
2.  The framework is presented for general stochastic objectives and constrained domains, which is broader than the typical finite-sum setting of SVRG/SAG/SDCA.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Critical Omission of Related Work:** The failure to cite and compare against SVRG, whose gradient estimator is identical, is a major flaw that misrepresents the paper's originality.
2.  **Fundamental Technical Error:** The incorrect statement of the strong convexity property in Equation (4) casts doubt on the paper's technical soundness.
3.  **Limited Practicality:** The algorithm's reliance on the a priori knowledge of the condition number κ is a major practical barrier that is not adequately addressed.
4.  **Numerous Minor Flaws:** A collection of inconsistencies in formatting, notation, and clarity suggests a lack of careful preparation.

**Decision:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper has some theoretically interesting aspects, the combination of a major technical error (Eq. 4) and a critical failure to acknowledge and position the work relative to highly relevant prior art (SVRG) is disqualifying. The work, in its current form, does not meet the standards of quality and originality expected at NIPS. A major revision addressing the relationship to variance reduction literature, correcting the technical statements, and providing a more credible path toward practical implementation would be required before this work could be considered for publication.
