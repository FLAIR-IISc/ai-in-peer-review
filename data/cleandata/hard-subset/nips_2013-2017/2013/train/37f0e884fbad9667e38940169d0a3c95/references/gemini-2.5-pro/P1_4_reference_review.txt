### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex functions. The primary goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, which typically scales with the condition number κ (or its square root) of the problem. The authors propose a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which cleverly combines full gradient and stochastic gradient evaluations. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. Then, a series of iterations are performed using a "mixed gradient," which is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient anchored by the full gradient from the start of the epoch. A key feature is that the optimization at each step is constrained to a shrinking ball around the epoch's starting point.

The main theoretical contribution is showing that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate. Specifically, it finds an ǫ-optimal solution by performing O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient computations and O(κ² log 1/ǫ) stochastic gradient computations. This successfully makes the number of full gradient calls independent of the condition number κ, trading them for a larger number of cheaper stochastic gradient calls. The work is positioned relative to classical accelerated methods (which require O(√κ log 1/ǫ) full gradients) and modern stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA (which apply to finite-sum problems).

### Strengths

1.  **Significant and Novel Goal:** The paper tackles a very important problem in large-scale optimization: the high cost of full gradient computations in ill-conditioned problems. The goal of making the number of full gradient calls independent of κ is highly desirable, and the proposed trade-off (substituting full gradients with many stochastic gradients) is a novel and interesting approach to bypass the established lower bounds for first-order methods that use only full gradients.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness and Rigor:** The paper provides a complete and detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed EMGD algorithm. The proof, based on an inductive argument over epochs, is clear and appears to be technically sound. The use of a shrinking domain is a key element that allows the variance of the stochastic part of the gradient to be controlled, leading to the linear convergence. The result is a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation guarantees common to many stochastic algorithms.

3.  **Generality of the Algorithm:** Unlike prominent related methods like SAG or SDCA that are specifically designed for finite-sum objective functions and unconstrained problems, EMGD is presented in a more general stochastic optimization framework where F(w) = E[f(w)]. Furthermore, the algorithm explicitly incorporates constraints by design (via the projection in step 8), making it applicable to a broader class of problems, provided the projection is efficient.

4.  **Practical Advantages:** The authors clearly highlight several practical advantages beyond the complexity analysis. These include low memory requirements (O(d)), which is a significant advantage over methods like SAG that require storing n gradients, and its amenability to distributed computation for the full gradient step.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on κ² for Stochastic Gradients:** The main trade-off is replacing O(√κ) full gradients with O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch. While this is a good trade when stochastic gradients are extremely cheap, the κ² dependence is quite severe. For moderately or severely ill-conditioned problems, this number can become prohibitively large, potentially making the algorithm slower than competitors like SAG or SDCA, whose dependence on κ is linear. The paper's own comparison in Table 2 shows EMGD is only competitive for a limited range of κ.

2.  **Requirement of a Known Condition Number:** The algorithm's parameters, specifically the number of iterations per epoch `T`, depend directly on L and λ (and thus κ). This is a significant practical limitation, as these values are often unknown in practice. The paper acknowledges this in the conclusion and suggests estimation methods as future work, but this makes the current algorithm difficult to apply "out of the box." An adaptive version of the algorithm would be a much stronger contribution.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the absence of any experiments makes it hard to gauge the practical performance of EMGD. An empirical study could have demonstrated: (a) how the algorithm performs in practice, especially compared to SAG/SDCA on finite-sum problems, (b) the sensitivity of the algorithm to the estimation of κ, and (c) whether the theoretical bounds are tight. Even a simple proof-of-concept experiment would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

4.  **Relationship to SVRG is not Discussed:** The form of the "mixed gradient," `g̃_kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f_kt(w_kt) - ∇f_kt(w̄k)`, is identical to the gradient estimator used in the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). While the overall algorithm structure (shrinking ball, epoch length `T=O(κ²)`) is different from SVRG, the core variance reduction technique is the same. The paper fails to cite SVRG or discuss the relationship between the two methods. This is a major omission in the related work and framing of the contribution. The novelty lies in how the SVRG-style estimator is used within a new epoch structure, not in the estimator itself.

### Overall Evaluation and Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization with a significant goal: removing the condition number dependence from the number of full gradient evaluations. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the claims are well-supported by the proofs. The algorithm's generality and practical advantages like low memory are also compelling.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The quadratic dependence on κ for the number of stochastic gradients is a major concern for ill-conditioned problems. The practical utility is hampered by the need to know κ beforehand and the complete lack of experimental validation. Most importantly, the paper misses a crucial connection to the SVRG literature, which developed the same variance-reduced gradient estimator.

Despite these weaknesses, the core idea is novel and the theoretical result is a solid contribution to the optimization literature. The trade-off it proposes is interesting and could inspire further work. I believe the paper is a good candidate for acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the weaknesses in the final version, especially by:

1.  Adding a thorough discussion of the relationship to SVRG and citing it appropriately.
2.  Explicitly discussing the limitation of the κ² dependence in the main body of the paper.
3.  Ideally, adding a small experimental section to provide a proof-of-concept and contextualize the theoretical findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important problem with a novel approach.
*   Strong, rigorous theoretical result with a high-probability guarantee.
*   More general framework (constraints, not just finite-sum) than some key competitors.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Severe O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients.
*   Requires knowledge of problem parameters (κ).
*   Complete lack of experiments.
*   Crucial omission of related work (SVRG).

**Recommendation:** Accept (conditional on addressing the SVRG omission).
