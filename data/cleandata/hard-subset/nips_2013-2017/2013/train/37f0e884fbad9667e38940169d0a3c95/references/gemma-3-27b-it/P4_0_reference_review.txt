This paper introduces a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the computational cost associated with full gradient evaluations in smooth, strongly convex optimization. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, aiming for a logarithmic iteration complexity – independent of the condition number κ – while leveraging the efficiency of stochastic gradients. The authors demonstrate theoretical convergence guarantees and compare EMGD with existing methods, highlighting potential benefits in specific scenarios.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound algorithm with a well-defined convergence analysis. The proof of Theorem 1 appears rigorous, building upon established lemmas and inequalities. The assumptions (smoothness, strong convexity, oracle access) are clearly stated. However, the connection between the presented analysis and practical implementation details could be strengthened. For example, the algorithm relies on knowing κ beforehand, which is often not the case in practice, and the paper briefly mentions addressing this issue in future work, but doesn’t elaborate on potential strategies. The discussion of the practical limitations and potential remedies feels somewhat cursory. While the paper establishes theoretical guarantees, a more comprehensive evaluation, perhaps through simulations, would bolster confidence in its practical effectiveness.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is clearly presented in pseudocode. The related work section provides a useful overview of existing optimization methods. However, some sections, particularly within the analysis, could benefit from further simplification.  For instance, the derivation in Section 4.2, while technically correct, is quite dense and may be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with the relevant tools (e.g., Hoeffding-Azuma inequality). More intuitive explanations and intermediate steps would improve accessibility. The organization is logical, progressing from introduction and related work to algorithm description, analysis, and conclusion.

**Originality:** The approach of combining full and stochastic gradients in a specific epoch-based manner with guaranteed linear convergence represents a novel contribution. While the idea of interleaving gradient evaluations is not entirely new (as acknowledged in the related work – semi-stochastic approximation), EMGD's theoretical guarantees and specific algorithmic structure distinguish it from previous work. The result of achieving a condition number independent number of full gradient evaluations is a noteworthy advancement.  The comparisons to SAG and SDCA, especially regarding storage requirements and potential for distributed computation, further highlight the algorithm’s unique aspects.  The paper appropriately cites relevant prior research and positions its contributions within the existing landscape.

**Significance:**  The problem addressed – reducing the computational burden of full gradient evaluations – is highly relevant, particularly in large-scale machine learning applications.  If the theoretical benefits translate to practical performance gains, EMGD could offer a valuable alternative to existing optimization algorithms. The potential to reduce the number of full gradient computations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) is significant, especially when κ is large and evaluating full gradients is expensive. The discussion of distributed computing and the possibility of reducing the cost of full gradient computations further enhance the potential impact.  The algorithm's applicability to both constrained and unconstrained problems widens its scope.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel algorithm with a clear and rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem: reducing the cost of full gradient evaluations.
*   Achieves a potentially significant improvement in iteration complexity (independent of condition number).
*   Well-written and logically structured paper.
*   Good comparison to related work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ.
*   Lack of empirical validation (simulations) to demonstrate practical performance.
*   The analysis in Section 4.2 is dense and could be more accessible.
*   Discussion of practical limitations is somewhat limited.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and solid theoretical foundation. While the lack of empirical results is a concern, the theoretical guarantees and potential benefits of the proposed algorithm warrant acceptance. Addressing the minor clarity issues in the analysis and acknowledging the need for practical strategies to estimate κ would further strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
