This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm aiming to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods for strongly convex optimization by strategically interleaving full and stochastic gradient evaluations. The core idea—reducing the number of full gradient steps by leveraging stochastic gradients—is intuitively appealing, especially given the expense of full gradient computations in large-scale settings. The authors claim to achieve a convergence rate independent of the condition number κ, specifically O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient evaluations.

While the motivation is sound and the presented theorem formally establishes a logarithmic dependence on 1/ǫ for the number of full gradient evaluations, I find the overall contribution incremental and the presentation lacking in crucial comparative rigor. The paper feels overly focused on a theoretical result with limited discussion of its practical implications and a somewhat superficial comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods.

A primary concern lies in the assumption of access to *both* full and stochastic gradients. This assumption significantly weakens the practical relevance of the work. Many applications necessitate choosing between these two options, not having convenient access to both. The paper doesn’t convincingly argue why this mixed access scenario is common enough to warrant a dedicated algorithm. Furthermore, the computational complexity analysis in Table 2, while attempting to contextualize EMGD, feels selectively chosen. It highlights scenarios where EMGD performs favorably but downplays situations where algorithms like SDCA and SAG demonstrate superior performance. The claim that EMGD’s complexity is "on the same order" as SAG and SDCA only under a specific constraint (κ ≤ n1/2) feels disingenuous.

The theoretical analysis, while presented, is not fully convincing without further supporting evidence. Specifically, the choice of *T* (number of iterations per epoch) appears somewhat arbitrary and its derivation isn't sufficiently justified. The impact of *T* on the overall performance isn't thoroughly explored. The discussion of how to *estimate* κ in practice, acknowledged as a drawback, feels rushed and lacks concrete details. Simply mentioning matrix sampling and concentration inequalities doesn't constitute a practical solution.

The writing quality is adequate, but suffers from some repetition and a lack of conciseness. The introduction spends considerable time stating the well-known trade-offs between full and stochastic gradient methods.  The analysis section relies heavily on referencing lemmas and inequalities without adequately explaining their relevance to the reader unfamiliar with the referenced work. 

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of interleaving full and stochastic gradients to potentially reduce computational cost is reasonable.
*   The theoretical analysis establishes a logarithmic convergence rate with respect to 1/ǫ for full gradient evaluations.
*   The paper acknowledges limitations and potential future work (e.g., κ estimation).

**Weaknesses:**

*   The assumption of simultaneous access to full and stochastic gradients limits practical applicability.
*   The comparative analysis to existing methods (SAG, SDCA) is incomplete and selectively presented.
*   The choice of *T* lacks sufficient justification.
*   The discussion of κ estimation is superficial and lacks practical details.
*   The writing could be more concise and accessible.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a formally proven theoretical result, albeit under specific assumptions.
*   The core idea, while not revolutionary, is a valid attempt to address a practical challenge in optimization.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The practical relevance is questionable due to the strong assumptions.
*   The comparative analysis is weak and does not convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The paper lacks sufficient experimental validation to support the theoretical claims.
*   The presentation could be significantly improved for clarity and conciseness.




**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the theoretical result is interesting, the limitations and lack of robust comparative analysis outweigh the potential benefits. The paper feels like a preliminary investigation rather than a mature contribution ready for NIPS.  More thorough experimentation, a more balanced comparison to existing methods, and a more detailed discussion of practical considerations are needed to justify acceptance. The assumption of simultaneously having access to both full and stochastic gradients is a significant roadblock to practical adoption, and the paper does not sufficiently address this limitation.
