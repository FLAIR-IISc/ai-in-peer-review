### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex functions. The goal is to design an algorithm that achieves a linear convergence rate (i.e., logarithmic dependence on the target precision ε) while requiring a number of full gradient computations that is independent of the problem's condition number, κ. This is motivated by the high computational cost of full gradients in large-scale settings and the poor convergence of purely stochastic methods for high-precision solutions.

The authors propose a novel algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD). The algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed at a reference point. Within the epoch, a series of updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" estimator. This estimator combines the pre-computed full gradient with a stochastic correction term, forming a variance-reduced gradient estimate. This is reminiscent of other variance reduction techniques such as SVRG and SAGA. The algorithm also incorporates updates within a shrinking ball, and the reference point for the next epoch is the average of the iterates from the current one.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This result successfully makes the number of expensive full gradient calls independent of κ, trading them for a larger number of cheaper stochastic gradient calls. The authors compare their method to standard full-gradient, stochastic, and other variance-reduced methods (SAG, SDCA), arguing for advantages in handling constraints, memory requirements, and providing a high-probability convergence guarantee.

### Strengths

1.  **Important and Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem in large-scale machine learning: reducing the reliance on expensive full gradient computations for ill-conditioned problems without sacrificing the fast linear convergence rate characteristic of strongly convex optimization. The motivation is clear and compelling.

2.  **Novel Theoretical Result:** The primary result—achieving a full-gradient complexity of O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ—is a strong and interesting theoretical statement. It presents a new point in the trade-off space between full-gradient and stochastic methods.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The algorithm is presented clearly, the assumptions are stated explicitly, and the main theorem is prominently featured. The analysis section provides a good sketch of the proof, which aids in understanding the key mechanisms behind the convergence guarantee.

4.  **Stronger Convergence Guarantee:** The analysis provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the guarantees in expectation that are common for many stochastic variance-reduced methods like SAG and SVRG. This adds to the theoretical robustness of the proposed method.

5.  **Generality:** The framework of EMGD is presented in a way that can handle general convex constraints, provided that projection onto the intersection of the domain and the shrinking ball is efficient. This is a potential advantage over methods like SAG and SDCA, which are typically analyzed for unconstrained problems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Missing Comparison to SVRG:** The paper's most significant weakness is the omission of any discussion or comparison to the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) algorithm [Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013]. The "mixed gradient" estimator used in EMGD, `∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) - ∇f(w̄)`, is precisely the estimator used in SVRG. Furthermore, the overall two-loop structure of EMGD (an outer loop/epoch that computes a full gradient, and an inner loop of stochastic updates) is the defining characteristic of SVRG. While EMGD's use of a shrinking projection ball and iterate averaging is different, the core algorithmic idea is extremely similar to SVRG. This omission significantly undermines the paper's claim to originality.

2.  **High Stochastic Complexity:** The stochastic gradient complexity of EMGD is O(κ² log(1/ε)). This dependence on κ² is substantially worse than that of other state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods. For instance, SVRG, SAGA, and SDCA typically achieve a complexity of O((n+κ) log(1/ε)), which is only linear in κ. The O(κ²) factor means that for even moderately ill-conditioned problems, the cost of the stochastic steps in EMGD could become prohibitively expensive, potentially outweighing the benefit of fewer full gradients. The paper's comparison (Table 2) suggests the method is only competitive when κ is relatively small (κ ≤ n²/³), which is the regime where the high dependency on κ is less of a concern.

3.  **Practicality Concerns:**
    *   The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ (or L and λ) to set the number of inner-loop iterations T. This is a common requirement in theoretical analyses but a major hurdle for practical application. While the authors relegate this to future work, it is a key limitation of the current method.
    *   The paper lacks any experimental validation. While a purely theoretical contribution can be valuable, experiments would greatly strengthen the paper by (a) demonstrating the algorithm's practical performance, (b) validating the theoretical rates, especially the κ² dependency, and (c) providing an empirical comparison against the highly relevant baselines like SVRG and Nesterov's AGD.

4.  **Questionable Claims in Comparison:** Some claims in the comparison section (3.4) are debatable. For example, the claim of lower storage requirements (O(d)) is also true for SVRG, so it is not a unique advantage over all other variance-reduced methods. Similarly, the potential for distributed computation of the full gradient is a feature shared by SVRG and mini-batch versions of other similar algorithms.

### Suggestions for Improvement

Despite the decision to reject, the paper has a promising direction. To improve the work for a future submission, I strongly recommend the following:

1.  **Address the SVRG Connection:** The authors must thoroughly position their work with respect to SVRG. They should explicitly cite SVRG, acknowledge the similarity of the gradient estimator and the epoch structure, and clearly articulate the novel contributions of EMGD. The key differences appear to be the shrinking projection ball and the specific analysis leading to the high-probability bound and the κ² rate. The benefits and drawbacks of these differences should be the central point of the discussion.

2.  **Investigate the O(κ²) Complexity:** It would be highly valuable to understand the source of the κ² complexity. Is it an artifact of the proof technique, or is it fundamental to the algorithm's structure (e.g., a consequence of the shrinking ball)? If the analysis can be tightened to achieve a complexity linear in κ, the method would become significantly more compelling.

3.  **Add Empirical Evaluation:** I urge the authors to include an experimental section. A comparison of EMGD with Nesterov's AGD, SVRG, and SAG on standard machine learning tasks (e.g., logistic/ridge regression) on datasets with varying condition numbers would provide invaluable insight. This would allow readers to see where the theoretical trade-offs manifest in practice.

4.  **Refine the Practicality Discussion:** The authors should be more upfront about the limitation of requiring knowledge of κ. Even a brief discussion of standard heuristics for setting such parameters (e.g., via a grid search or estimation) would make the paper more grounded.

In its current form, the paper's omission of SVRG and the high O(κ²) complexity prevent a recommendation for acceptance. However, by addressing these major points, the work could be developed into a strong and interesting contribution.
