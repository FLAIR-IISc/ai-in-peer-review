### Summary of the Paper

This paper tackles the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors identify a key bottleneck in classical first-order methods like accelerated gradient descent: the number of expensive full gradient evaluations, O(√κ log(1/ε)), scales with the square root of the condition number κ. For ill-conditioned problems where κ is large, this becomes computationally prohibitive.

The central and novel contribution of this work is a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both full and stochastic gradient oracles to completely remove the dependence on κ from the number of full gradient calls. The proposed algorithm operates in epochs. In each epoch, it computes a single full gradient and then performs a series of O(κ²) updates using a variance-reduced stochastic gradient. The authors prove that this scheme achieves a linear convergence rate, requiring only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations to reach an ε-optimal solution. This result is significant because it suggests a new way to trade off a large number of cheap computations (stochastic gradients) for a minimal number of expensive ones (full gradients), challenging the established complexity lower bounds by changing the underlying oracle model.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness:** The paper's core premise—achieving a full-gradient complexity that is independent of the condition number—is a bold and highly original idea. While variance-reduction techniques (like SAG, SVRG, SDCA) have a similar spirit, they are typically analyzed in a finite-sum setting and their complexity in terms of full gradient passes still implicitly depends on κ. This paper explicitly reframes the problem and provides a new target for algorithm design in a hybrid oracle setting. This conceptual contribution is significant and could inspire a new line of research.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** The main result, achieving linear convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls, is powerful and surprising. It effectively bypasses the classical lower bound by introducing the stochastic oracle. The analysis, which proves this convergence with high probability rather than just in expectation, is also a notable strength compared to related methods like SAG and SDCA.

3.  **Generality:** The proposed algorithm is presented for general constrained convex optimization, provided the projection onto the constrained set is efficient. This makes it potentially more broadly applicable than methods like SAG or SDCA, which are primarily designed for unconstrained finite-sum problems. The authors also highlight other practical advantages, such as lower memory requirements and better suitability for distributed computation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Severe Dependence on κ in Stochastic Complexity:** The most significant weakness is that the dependence on the condition number is not eliminated but rather shifted to the stochastic gradient complexity, where it appears as O(κ²). This quadratic dependence is substantially worse than the linear dependence on κ found in state-of-the-art methods like SDCA or SVRG. For problems where κ is very large, requiring O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch could render the algorithm computationally infeasible, directly contradicting its goal of efficiently solving ill-conditioned problems.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the absence of any experiments is a major omission. Without empirical evidence, it is impossible to gauge the practical performance of EMGD. An experimental comparison against Nesterov's acceleration, SVRG, and SDCA would be essential to understand whether the O(κ²) term is a loose analytical artifact or a real, crippling performance bottleneck.

3.  **Practicality Concerns:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ (or L and λ) to set the number of inner iterations `T`. This is a common limitation in theoretical optimization papers but a significant barrier to practical application, as these parameters are rarely known a priori. The authors acknowledge this and suggest it as future work, but it remains a weakness of the current submission.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The core idea of making full gradient evaluations independent of κ is highly novel and thought-provoking. It introduces a new perspective on the trade-offs between different gradient oracles.
*   The paper provides a strong, non-trivial theoretical result (high-probability linear convergence) for a general class of problems.
*   This work has the potential to open up a new direction of research in designing hybrid algorithms that strategically combine oracles of different costs and accuracies.

### Arguments for Rejection:

*   The O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradient evaluations is a severe limitation that may make the algorithm impractical for the very ill-conditioned problems it aims to solve. This is a significantly worse dependence than state-of-the-art alternatives.
*   The complete lack of experiments makes the practical relevance of the algorithm and its theoretical bounds difficult to assess.

### Recommendation: Accept

Despite significant weaknesses, particularly the O(κ²) stochastic complexity and the lack of experiments, I recommend accepting this paper. My recommendation is driven by the exceptional novelty and boldness of its central idea. The goal of designing an algorithm with a κ-independent number of full gradient evaluations is a genuinely new and important research direction. This paper provides the first (to my knowledge) concrete algorithm and proof that such a result is possible.

While the current implementation of this idea (EMGD) may not be the final word and might be impractical due to its quadratic dependence on κ, it serves as a crucial proof of concept. NIPS should be a venue for such ambitious and conceptually groundbreaking work, even if the initial execution has flaws. The paper clearly lays out its contributions and limitations, and the idea itself is significant enough to stimulate further research that may overcome the weaknesses identified here. This is precisely the kind of work that can push the boundaries of the field, prioritizing a novel concept over a methodologically perfect but incremental improvement.
