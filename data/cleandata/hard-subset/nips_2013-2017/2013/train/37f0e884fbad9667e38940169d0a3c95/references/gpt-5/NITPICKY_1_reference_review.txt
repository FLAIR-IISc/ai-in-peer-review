Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for minimizing smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives. The method alternates epochs; at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at an anchor point w̄k, and then runs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), where f is drawn from a function oracle Of with Ef[f] = F, and each f is L-smooth. Updates are constrained to a shrinking Euclidean ball around w̄k. The analysis shows a per-epoch constant error reduction, yielding linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations, where κ = L/λ.

Relation to prior work
- Full-gradient methods: For smooth, strongly convex F, Nesterov’s accelerated gradient attains O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients.
- Stochastic/finite-sum methods: SAG/SDCA achieve linear rates with O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) gradient evaluations under finite-sum structure. The proposed gradient estimator is the same as that used by variance-reduced methods (e.g., SVRG: Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013; SAGA: Defazio et al., 2014), which also use one full gradient per epoch and achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) without shrinking domains and with simpler updates.

Strengths
- Clear goal: reduce the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)), independent of κ, while allowing stochastic gradients in between.
- The mixed gradient estimator is unbiased and variance-controlled via anchoring; the analysis is technically competent, with a martingale concentration argument and an epoch-wise contraction proof.
- The paper covers constrained problems (via projection onto W ∩ ball), which many VR methods do not emphasize.
- High-probability linear convergence statement (via union bound across epochs).

Weaknesses (technical and presentation)
- Novelty relative to SVRG is unclear and key related work is missing. The mixed gradient g̃ is exactly the SVRG estimator. EMGD differs mainly by the shrinking-ball constraint and a conservative step size, yielding worse stochastic complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) vs. O(κ log(1/ε)) in SVRG for the finite-sum case. Not citing or comparing to SVRG (NIPS 2013) is a serious omission; SAGA is also relevant. Claims of novelty over “semi-stochastic” methods are thus not well supported.
- Complexity is inferior to state of the art. The κ^2 factor is substantially worse than SVRG/SDCA/SAGA (κ), and for finite-sum settings the method is generally dominated. The paper claims EMGD’s complexity is “on the same order as SAG and SDCA when κ ≤ n1/2”, but VR methods are O(n + κ) whereas EMGD’s stochastic work is κ^2 log(1/ε), which can be much larger unless κ is tiny; the comparison table is not shown and the text arguments are weak.
- Oracle model assumptions limit applicability. EMGD assumes access to an exact full-gradient oracle (Og) even when F is defined as an expectation (not finite sum), which is unrealistic. In the finite-sum regime, Og amounts to a full pass, but in the general expectation setting Og is typically unavailable; this distinction is not discussed.
- Proof uses a shrinking-ball projection, which complicates implementation for general constrained W (projection onto intersection) and is not needed by VR methods. The text asserts this is “similar to standard gradient descent,” but feasibility and computational cost of Step 8 are not analyzed.
- Parameter knowledge: The algorithm requires κ (via L and λ) to set T and η; the paper acknowledges this but provides only high-level suggestions.
- Several inaccuracies/typos that hinder clarity and confidence:
  - Equation numbering is reused ((2) appears twice); Algorithm 1’s proximal term is malformed (“+ 1 ‖w − wkt ‖2” missing 1/2); later it becomes “+ 1/2 ‖w − wt‖^2”.
  - Theorem 1 condition: “T ≥ 1152 L^2/λ^2 ln δ” should be ln(1/δ).
  - Multiple misspellings: “epoches,” “interstage,” inconsistent use of Ω vs O for storage (should be O(n) and O(d)).
  - Some claims are imprecise: “linear convergence of SAG and SDCA only holds in expectation” is not strictly accurate (high-probability variants exist or can be derived); “the only step depending on n is step 4” ignores communication/latency in distributed scenarios.
  - The smooth convex rate is written as O(L/√ε); dimensionally this omits a radius term (standard O(√(L/ε)) when normalized).
  - References are incomplete and in several cases lack years/venues; key VR works are missing.
- No experiments. Given the relatively modest theoretical novelty and worse complexity than prior VR methods, empirical validation would be important to motivate the method (e.g., constrained problems where EMGD’s projection is cheap and memory-light advantage matters).

Quality
The analysis is mostly sound, but conservative. There are several presentation errors and missing comparisons. The main estimator and epoch structure duplicate known VR techniques without demonstrating a tangible advantage.

Clarity
The paper is readable but marred by typos, inconsistent notation/equation formatting, and missing references. Algorithm 1 needs correction. The comparison section has unsubstantiated complexity claims.

Originality
Limited. The key idea (one full gradient per epoch plus gradient differences) is established in SVRG. The shrinking-ball approach is a variant but appears to worsen complexity.

Significance
Limited in current form. Compared to existing VR methods, the theoretical bounds are weaker, and practical guidance/experiments are absent. The constrained-setting angle could be interesting, but requires clearer algorithmic and computational advantages.

Pros for acceptance
- Attempts to reduce full-gradient dependence to O(log(1/ε)).
- High-probability linear convergence proof.
- Handles constrained domains (in principle) with low memory.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing and overlapping with prior art (SVRG/SAGA), with weaker κ dependence.
- Unrealistic oracle assumption for general expectation objectives.
- Numerous typos/formatting issues; incomplete references.
- No empirical results.
- Questionable comparison claims.

Recommendation: Reject

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the work relative to SVRG/SAGA and clearly articulate what is new (and why the κ^2 factor is unavoidable in your oracle model). If your contribution targets expectation settings where Og is available (e.g., via distributed exact passes), state this explicitly.
- Remove the shrinking-ball constraint unless essential; derive tighter bounds (aim for κ instead of κ^2).
- Correct Algorithm 1 and all typos, fix theorem conditions, and complete references.
- Provide empirical evaluations, especially on constrained problems where EMGD may have an advantage (e.g., simple projections, low memory).
- Clarify feasibility and cost of projection onto W ∩ B(w̄; Δ).
