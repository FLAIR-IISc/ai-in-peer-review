This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods for strongly convex and smooth optimization by strategically incorporating stochastic gradients. The core idea of interleaving full and stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs to achieve a convergence rate independent of the condition number is interesting, building upon the concept of epoch gradient descent. However, I find the claims of significant improvement and the overall presentation somewhat overstated, particularly given the limited empirical evaluation.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis. The authors provide a convergence proof demonstrating a logarithmic iteration complexity regarding the error tolerance, which is a desirable outcome. The connection to existing work on stochastic and semi-stochastic gradient methods is reasonably well-established, and the authors correctly position their contribution relative to SAG and SDCA. The discussion of the algorithm's applicability to machine learning problems with regularizers is a welcome addition.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the motivation, while valid, feels slightly contrived. The emphasis on reducing the number of *full* gradient evaluations feels less relevant in modern settings where efficient full gradient computations (e.g., using GPUs, distributed computing) are increasingly common. The assumption of access to *both* full and stochastic gradients is also limiting and potentially unrealistic in many scenarios. The authors acknowledge the need for knowing the condition number beforehand as a drawback, but the suggested future work on estimation feels tacked on and lacks detail.

More importantly, the empirical evaluation is severely lacking. Table 2 provides a *theoretical* comparison of computational complexities, which is insufficient. There are *no* experimental results demonstrating the performance of EMGD on real datasets or synthetic problems. Without such experiments, it is impossible to assess the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm. The authors claim EMGD could be more efficient than SAG and SDCA in specific cases, but this is not substantiated by any evidence.  The dependence on n in Table 2 is highlighted as a point of comparison, but a practical demonstration of this difference is crucial. 

Furthermore, the algorithm itself appears complex. The shrinking domain constraint in step 8 adds overhead and potentially limits its applicability. The analysis of the algorithm’s robustness to noise in stochastic gradients is also absent. The assumptions around smoothness and strong convexity, while standard, restrict the generalizability of the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical convergence analysis is sound and provides a guarantee of logarithmic complexity.
*   The paper correctly identifies a potential trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations.
*   The algorithm builds upon established techniques (epoch gradient descent) and addresses a relevant problem in optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation is a major shortcoming.
*   The practical relevance of reducing full gradient evaluations is questionable in many modern settings.
*   The algorithm's complexity and the need to estimate the condition number pose challenges.
*   The comparison to existing methods relies solely on theoretical computational complexity, lacking practical demonstration.
*   The writing, while generally clear, sometimes feels verbose and repetitive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the theoretical contribution is valid, the lack of empirical evidence and the limited practical relevance significantly detract from its overall quality. The paper feels incomplete and more akin to a position paper outlining a theoretical possibility rather than a fully developed research contribution. To warrant acceptance, the authors must provide compelling experimental results demonstrating the performance of EMGD on relevant datasets and a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art optimization algorithms. A more detailed discussion of practical considerations, such as condition number estimation and robustness to noise, would also be beneficial.
