Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth and strongly convex optimization that interleaves occasional full-gradient computations with many stochastic-gradient steps. Each epoch computes one full gradient at a reference point and then performs T mixed-gradient steps using the estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄), together with a projection onto a shrinking ball around w̄ and averaging. The main claim is that EMGD achieves linear convergence and requires only O(log(1/ε)) accesses to the full-gradient oracle (independent of the condition number κ), at the cost of O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations, with a high-probability guarantee. The method is positioned against accelerated full-gradient methods (with O(√κ log 1/ε) iterations), stochastic methods, and finite-sum variance-reduction methods such as SAG and SDCA.

Relation to prior work
The proposed “mixed gradient” estimator is essentially the variance-reduced gradient used in SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013), i.e., g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄), which yields linear convergence with one full gradient per epoch and many stochastic steps. Closely related incremental variance-reduced methods include SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014) and subsequent refinements (Finito/MISO, Katyusha). Compared to SAG/SDCA/SVRG, the analysis here delivers a high-probability result and supports constrained domains via a projection step, but the iteration complexity per epoch scales as T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)), which is worse than the O(κ) dependence achieved by SVRG/SAGA/SDCA. The paper cites SAG/SDCA but omits SVRG and subsequent VR literature that use the same estimator and already establish O(log(1/ε)) full gradients. This omission makes the novelty questionable.

Strengths
- Clear and careful high-probability analysis yielding linear convergence under smoothness and strong convexity.
- The oracle model separates full-gradient and stochastic “function” oracles, and the algorithm uses only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients.
- Works with constrained domains (projection onto W ∩ ball) and requires O(d) memory.
- The presentation of the epoch structure and the derivation using martingale difference bounds are pedagogically useful.

Weaknesses
- Originality: The key estimator and epoch idea match SVRG; the main claimed qualitative advantage (full-gradient count independent of κ) is already achieved there. Not citing SVRG (and follow-ups) is a major gap.
- Suboptimal complexity: The per-epoch length T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)) is significantly worse than O(κ) in SVRG/SAGA/SDCA. Thus the total stochastic gradient count O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) is inferior to state of the art O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) in finite-sum settings or O(κ log(1/ε)) in appropriate oracle models.
- Practical evaluation is absent. There are no experiments comparing EMGD to VR baselines (SAG/SVRG/SAGA/SDCA), making it hard to assess empirical trade-offs, especially given the κ^2 dependence and the need to know κ.
- Assumptions and model: The “function oracle” Of is less standard than the finite-sum model and may obscure per-iteration cost in practice. In finite-sum problems, the full-gradient call costs Θ(n), which the paper argues can be distributed, but this argument equally applies to SVRG/SDCA and is not unique to EMGD.
- Some clarity issues and minor errors (see below), and the need to know κ a priori for setting T.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the proof is complete and careful for the stated result. However, the analysis is conservative (κ^2 dependence), and the lack of comparison to the most relevant prior (SVRG) undermines the positioning.

Clarity
Generally readable and well organized, but with several typos and notation issues. The update in Algorithm 1 (step 8) appears to be missing a 1/2 factor in the proximal term. Some constants in Theorem 1 could be simplified, and the role of the shrinking-ball constraint in tightening variance could be better motivated.

Originality
Limited. The core estimator and “one full gradient per epoch” structure are known from SVRG; high-probability analysis and constrained setting are incremental extensions.

Significance
Moderate to low. Given stronger existing results with better κ dependence and established practice, the contribution is unlikely to shift the state of the art unless the κ^2 dependence can be tightened and empirical advantages demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate EMGD within the variance-reduction literature: add and compare to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013), SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014), MISO/Finito, and later accelerated variants (e.g., Katyusha), discussing full-gradient counts, memory, constrained/proximal extensions, and high-probability guarantees.
- Tighten the analysis to achieve O(κ) stochastic steps per epoch; current κ^2 dependence is a key limitation.
- Clarify whether the shrinking-ball projection is essential; discuss proximal variants for general constraints/regularizers, aligning with proximal-SVRG.
- Provide experiments on standard benchmarks to support the claimed practical trade-offs (especially when κ ≤ n2/3).
- Discuss adaptivity to unknown κ (e.g., doubling schemes, adaptive epoch lengths).
- Fix typographical errors and missing constants (e.g., step 8’s proximal term).

Minor comments/typos
- “epoches” → “epochs”; “interstage” → “investigate”; several formatting glitches around equations.
- Step 8 likely needs (1/2)||w − w_t||^2.
- Assumption (4) is oddly stated as a Lipschitz-gradient lower bound; typically strong convexity is defined via function inequality or monotonicity of gradients; please align notation.

Decision
Recommendation: Reject (score: 4/10). While the paper is technically sound and the high-probability constrained analysis is neat, the core idea overlaps heavily with prior variance-reduced methods, the complexity is suboptimal (κ^2), key related work is missing, and there is no empirical validation. With a tighter κ dependence, a thorough comparison to SVRG/SAGA/SDCA, and experiments, this could become a stronger submission.

Reviewer confidence: High.
