- Overall: Proposes EMGD that interleaves full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with full-gradient accesses independent of condition number; reviewer recommends weak accept pending more experiments.
- Problem focus: Tackles reducing full-gradient computation cost for strongly convex smooth optimization, especially when κ is large.
- Core contribution: EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, removing √κ dependence.
- Positioning: Well situated among deterministic and stochastic methods and inspired by epoch gradient descent.
- Novelty: The epoch-based interleaving of full and stochastic gradients to avoid κ-dependence is an original idea.
- Theoretical rigor: Provides sound proofs of linear convergence and bounds on full and stochastic gradient evaluations using strong convexity and smoothness.
- Clarity: Algorithm and notation are clearly presented and easy to follow.
- Motivation: Clearly motivates reducing full-gradient burden in ill-conditioned large-scale problems.
- Practical considerations: Authors acknowledge limitations like needing κ a priori and compare storage/applicability advantages versus SAG and SDCA.
- Limited experiments: Paper lacks comprehensive empirical evaluation on standard ML datasets to validate practical benefits.
- κ estimation: Reliance on known condition number is a practical limitation with only cursory discussion of estimation strategies.
- Computational overhead: No detailed analysis of constant-factor overhead from computing mixed gradients per iteration versus pure stochastic methods.
- Comparative baselines: Missing comparisons to other relevant approaches (e.g., low-rank Hessian approximations) that reduce full-gradient evaluations.
- Domain shrinkage: Impact and optimality of the domain size shrinkage strategy (∆k+1 = ∆k/√2) are not fully explored.
- Significance: If empirically validated, EMGD could make full-gradient methods practical for ill-conditioned problems and add valuable tools for certain ML tasks.
- Acceptance arguments (pro): Novel, theoretically sound, well-presented, and addresses an important practical challenge.
- Acceptance arguments (con): Lacks empirical validation, depends on κ knowledge, insufficient practical implementation/cost analysis, and limited broader comparisons.
- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on addressing experimental validation, κ estimation methods, and practical computational cost/implementation details.
