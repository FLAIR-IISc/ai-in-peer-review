

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence (O(log(1/ε))) while reducing the number of full gradient evaluations from the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)). The key insight is that by using a "mixed gradient" (combining one full gradient per epoch with multiple stochastic gradients) and employing a shrinking domain constraint, the algorithm achieves faster convergence without increasing the number of full gradient computations proportionally to the condition number. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that EMGD requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, and demonstrates advantages over existing methods (Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, SDCA) when the condition number κ ≤ n^(2/3).

The work builds on epoch gradient descent [9] and connects to recent advances in stochastic optimization [22, 23], but makes a distinctive contribution by addressing the condition number dependency in full gradient methods through the strategic use of stochastic gradients. The paper effectively situates itself within the broader context of first-order optimization methods, correctly identifying the trade-offs between full and stochastic gradients.

## Quality

The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis. The proof of Theorem 1 is rigorous and well-structured, employing standard techniques from convex optimization (strong convexity inequalities, gradient mapping) combined with martingale analysis (Hoeffding-Azuma inequality) to handle the stochastic components. The assumptions (L-smoothness, λ-strong convexity) are standard in the field and well-motivated.

However, the paper lacks empirical validation to support the theoretical claims. While theoretical analysis is the focus, the absence of any experimental results to demonstrate the practical advantages of EMGD (especially the claimed computational advantages when κ ≤ n^(2/3)) is a significant weakness. The paper also doesn't address how the condition number κ would be estimated in practice, which is a major practical limitation.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise, and the notation is consistent throughout. The relationship between the theoretical guarantees and practical implications is explained clearly, particularly in the comparison section (Table 2). The connection to machine learning applications (sum of functions) is appropriately made, though it could be strengthened by providing a concrete example.

One minor clarity issue is the explanation of why the domain shrinks by a factor of √2 each epoch (Step 10 of Algorithm 1), which appears to be a technical choice without sufficient motivation. The paper could better explain this design choice.

## Originality

The core idea of combining full and stochastic gradients to reduce condition number dependency is novel. While some works (like [12]) have explored semi-stochastic methods, EMGD's specific approach—using a fixed number of full gradients per epoch with a mixed gradient update—appears to be a fresh contribution. The paper effectively differentiates itself from SAG [22] and SDCA [23] by highlighting advantages in constrained optimization, storage requirements, and convergence guarantees (high probability vs. expectation).

The paper's originality is strong, particularly in the theoretical contribution of achieving condition number-independent full gradient access with linear convergence. The novelty is clearly articulated, and the related work section appropriately references previous approaches.

## Significance

The problem addressed is highly significant for large-scale machine learning, where full gradient evaluations can be prohibitively expensive for ill-conditioned problems. The potential to reduce full gradient evaluations from O(√κ) to O(1) (in terms of condition number dependence) represents a meaningful theoretical advance with practical implications.

However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the practical limitations: the requirement to know κ beforehand, the computational advantage only holding when κ ≤ n^(2/3), and the lack of empirical validation. For many real-world problems, κ may exceed this threshold, reducing the practical impact.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees
- Novel algorithmic approach to the condition number problem
- Clear comparison with existing methods, highlighting advantages in constrained optimization and storage
- Well-written with good organization

**Weaknesses:**
- No empirical results to validate theoretical claims
- Requires knowledge of condition number κ (a practical limitation)
- Computational advantage only holds under specific conditions (κ ≤ n^(2/3))
- Lacks discussion of constant factors in complexity (e.g., T ≥ 1152L²/λ² ln(1/δ))

## Recommendation

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to optimization, addressing a fundamental problem (condition number dependency) with a novel algorithm. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the paper is well-written. The approach could lead to practical improvements in large-scale machine learning.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** The lack of empirical validation is a major drawback, as it's hard to assess the practical significance without experiments. The requirement to know κ beforehand is a significant practical limitation that isn't adequately addressed. The computational advantage is limited to specific conditions that may not hold in practice.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty. The paper would be significantly strengthened with empirical validation demonstrating the practical advantages, especially for the specific machine learning applications mentioned in the introduction. The requirement to know κ is a notable limitation that should be addressed in future work.

While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the lack of empirical results and the practical limitation of requiring knowledge of κ slightly reduce the paper's impact. However, the theoretical advances are significant and could inspire future work addressing these limitations. The paper represents a solid contribution to the optimization literature that would be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors would need to address the empirical validation and condition number estimation issues in a revised version). The paper is theoretically sound and novel, but the practical significance would be better demonstrated with experiments.
