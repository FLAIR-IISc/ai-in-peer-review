- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) combining one full gradient per epoch with stochastic gradients to get linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient accesses independent of κ.  
- EMGD uses an epoch framework and a "mixed gradient" to achieve constant error reduction per epoch.  
- Requires O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients in addition to the O(log(1/ε)) full gradients.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous; Theorem 1 is well-supported using induction, martingale inequalities, and strong-convexity arguments.  
- Paper properly establishes smoothness and strong-convexity conditions and provides high-probability convergence guarantees.  
- Lacks any experimental validation, a significant omission for a machine learning conference.  
- Requires knowing the condition number κ in advance and does not detail how to estimate it in practice.  
- Clear, well-structured writing with consistent notation and a precise algorithm description (Algorithm 1).  
- Related work is comprehensively covered; connections to SAG, SDCA, epoch methods, and Nesterov are well-explained.  
- Novel contribution: condition-number-independent full-gradient access via a new mixed-gradient approach.  
- Significance: potentially large savings in full-gradient computations for ill-conditioned problems, but practical gains depend on problem regime.  
- EMGD outperforms Nesterov only when κ ≤ n^(2/3); SAG/SDCA may still have better complexity in many settings.  
- Advantages highlighted: lower storage requirements, applicability to constrained problems, and high-probability guarantees.  
- Weaknesses highlighted: no experiments, need κ beforehand, potentially suboptimal computational complexity in many cases, and sparse implementation/practical details.  
- Suggested improvements: add empirical results, discuss κ estimation (e.g., Hessian estimation), clarify when κ ≤ n^(2/3) holds in practice, simplify intuitive explanation of the algorithm, and clarify the meaning of "computational complexity" in comparisons.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — strong theoretical contribution but needs experimental validation to establish practical value.
