## NIPS Review: Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods for strongly convex and smooth optimization by strategically incorporating stochastic gradients. The idea is sound and addresses a relevant practical challenge – the expensive nature of full gradient evaluations in large-scale problems. While the paper demonstrates a theoretical result achieving condition number independent iteration complexity, I have concerns about the practical implications and the comparisons made to existing work, which prevent me from recommending a strong acceptance. I lean towards a **Weak Reject** with significant revisions required.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost in optimization. Full gradients offer faster convergence rates (O(√κ log(1/ε))) but are expensive, while stochastic gradients are cheaper but slower (typically polynomial in 1/ε).  EMGD attempts to bridge this gap, achieving a linear convergence rate (O(log(1/ε))) with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ. The related work section provides a reasonable overview of first-order methods, covering deterministic, stochastic, and semi-stochastic approaches like SAG and SDCA. However, the discussion regarding the computational complexities in Table 2 feels somewhat cherry-picked and doesn't fully acknowledge the overhead potentially introduced by the epoch-based structure of EMGD.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of epoch-based descent with a mixed gradient (full + stochastic) is a potentially valuable contribution.  The theoretical analysis leading to condition number independent full gradient access is a significant result.
* **Clear Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a relatively clear (though dense) proof of the linear convergence rate. Lemma 1 and the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality are appropriately leveraged.
* **Addresses a Practical Problem:** Reducing the number of full gradient evaluations is crucial for scaling optimization algorithms to large datasets.
* **Potential for Parallelization:** The authors correctly point out the potential to leverage distributed computing to reduce the cost of the full gradient evaluation step.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations:** The algorithm’s performance hinges on the selection of several hyperparameters (η, ∆1, T, m). The paper lacks a discussion on how these parameters might be chosen in practice, particularly the initial domain size ∆1 which appears heavily tied to the problem’s specific scaling.  Tuning these parameters could be non-trivial.
* **Computational Cost Analysis - Incompleteness:**  While the paper claims a reduction in full gradient evaluations, the analysis of *overall* computational cost feels incomplete.  Each epoch requires O(κ²) stochastic gradient evaluations.  If κ is large, this cost could easily outweigh the savings from reducing the number of full gradients.  A more detailed comparison to existing algorithms considering both full and stochastic gradient evaluation costs is needed. The fact that each iteration in the epoch performs a projection (Step 8) adds a computational cost that isn’t fully accounted for in the discussion.
* **Comparison to Existing Work – Limited Scope:**  The comparison to SAG and SDCA feels somewhat limited. The authors highlight scenarios where EMGD has lower complexity, but do not discuss the practical performance of these algorithms.  SAG and SDCA have benefited from significant engineering optimizations and may outperform EMGD in practice even if the theoretical complexity is slightly higher.  Furthermore, the assumption that the full gradient is *n* times more expensive than a stochastic gradient might not always hold true, especially with specialized hardware or data structures.
* **Assumptions:** The algorithm assumes access to *both* full and stochastic gradients. This assumption may not be realistic in all scenarios.  The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately discuss the implications.
* **Clarity in Algorithm Presentation:** Algorithm 1, while conceptually clear, could benefit from better notation and more concise descriptions. The use of multiple indices (superscript for epoch, subscript for iteration) can be confusing.
* **Minor: Typo/Grammar:** There are a few minor grammatical errors throughout the paper.

**4. Originality & Significance:**

The core idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework is original and demonstrates a creative approach to addressing a known challenge. The theoretical result of achieving condition number independent full gradient access is significant. However, the practical significance is questionable without a more thorough analysis of the computational cost and a comparison to highly optimized existing algorithms.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel algorithm with a strong theoretical guarantee.
* Addresses a relevant and important problem.
* Potential for parallelization.

**Con:**

* Limited discussion of practical considerations (hyperparameter tuning).
* Incomplete computational cost analysis.
* Limited comparison to highly optimized existing algorithms.
* Assumption of access to both full and stochastic gradients.
* Clarity in Algorithm presentation can be improved.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject.** The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution but lacks the practical grounding and comprehensive analysis required for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend the authors significantly revise the paper, addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, they should:

* Provide a more detailed computational cost analysis, including the cost of stochastic gradient evaluations and the projection step.
* Conduct a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms like SAG and SDCA, including empirical results.
* Discuss strategies for choosing hyperparameters in practice.
* Clarify the practical implications of the assumption of access to both full and stochastic gradients.
* Improve the clarity of the algorithm presentation.
