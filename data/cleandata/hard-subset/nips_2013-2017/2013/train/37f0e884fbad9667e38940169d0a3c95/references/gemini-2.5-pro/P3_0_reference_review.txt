### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors identify a key bottleneck in classical first-order methods: for ill-conditioned problems (large condition number κ), algorithms like accelerated gradient descent require a number of full gradient evaluations that scales with `O(√κ)`. To overcome this, the paper proposes a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle.

The core idea of EMGD is to structure the optimization into epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed at the current solution anchor point. Then, for a large number of inner iterations (`T = O(κ^2)`), the algorithm performs updates using a "mixed gradient" estimator. This estimator combines the fixed full gradient from the start of the epoch with a variance-correction term derived from stochastic gradients. The authors prove that this procedure reduces the optimization error by a constant factor in each epoch, leading to a linear convergence rate. The main theoretical result is that EMGD finds an ε-optimal solution using `O(log(1/ε))` full gradients and `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))` stochastic gradients. This makes the number of expensive full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ, which is the paper's primary claim.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The goal of reducing the dependency on the condition number for the number of full gradient calls is highly significant and practical. For large-scale problems where computing a full gradient is the dominant cost and the problem is ill-conditioned, an algorithm achieving this goal would be very valuable.

2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a complete theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm. The proof for Theorem 1, based on an inductive argument over epochs and the use of martingale concentration inequalities to bound the stochastic terms, appears sound. The high-probability convergence guarantee is stronger than a guarantee in expectation, which is a notable plus.

3.  **Generality:** The framework of EMGD is quite general. It applies to constrained optimization problems (provided the projection is efficient) and does not strictly require the finite-sum structure (`F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w)`) that many recent variance-reduced methods depend on. It only assumes a stochastic oracle `F(w) = E[f(w)]`. This broadens its potential applicability.

4.  **Discussion of Practical Advantages:** The paper thoughtfully discusses practical advantages beyond computational complexity, such as lower storage requirements (`O(d)`) compared to methods like SAG (`O(nd)`), and better amenability to distributed computation. These are important considerations for practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Major Omission of Related Work:** The most significant weakness is the failure to cite and compare against the highly relevant literature on variance-reduced stochastic gradient methods. The proposed "mixed gradient" estimator, `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)`, is precisely the gradient estimator used in Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) [Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013]. The EMGD algorithm can be viewed as an instance of SVRG where the "snapshot" gradient `∇F(w̄k)` is updated only once per epoch of `T` iterations. This omission severely undermines the paper's claim of originality for the core algorithmic component. Other related methods like SAGA [Defazio et al., NIPS 2014] and MISO [Mairal, ICML 2015] are also central to this line of work and should have been discussed.

2.  **Sub-optimal Complexity:** Because the paper does not compare to SVRG/SAGA, its complexity analysis is presented in a vacuum that makes it appear more favorable than it is. For the finite-sum setting, SVRG achieves a complexity of `O((n+κ) log(1/ε))` stochastic gradient evaluations. The proposed EMGD has a total stochastic gradient complexity of `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))`. In many machine learning settings, `n` is large, but `κ` can be much smaller than `n`. However, if `κ` is large, the `κ^2` dependence of EMGD is significantly worse than the linear `κ` dependence of SVRG and SAGA. The comparison in Table 2 is incomplete and potentially misleading because it omits these direct and superior competitors. EMGD appears competitive only if `κ` is very small, which is precisely the regime where this method is least needed.

3.  **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ (or `L` and `λ`) to set the number of inner iterations `T`. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation in the conclusion, but it is a critical one. While they suggest methods for estimating it, this adds another layer of complexity and potential fragility to the algorithm, making it less practical out-of-the-box compared to methods that are more robust to these parameters.

4.  **Clarity of Contribution:** Given the strong similarity to SVRG, the paper's novelty needs to be re-contextualized. The contribution is not the mixed gradient estimator, but rather the specific epoch structure with a shrinking domain constraint and the corresponding high-probability analysis. While interesting, this is a more incremental contribution than the paper suggests. The introduction frames the work as a novel way to bypass a fundamental `√κ` lower bound, but the variance-reduction community has already been successfully addressing this for years in the finite-sum setting.

### Detailed Comments

*   The analysis relies on a shrinking search ball `B(w̄k; ∆k)`. This is an interesting device that helps control the variance `‖wt - w̄‖`. However, it's not well-motivated why this is superior to or different from the standard analysis of SVRG, which does not require such a constraint. Does this constraint provide the high-probability bound, whereas standard SVRG analysis focuses on expectation? This would be a valuable point to clarify.
*   In Section 3.4, the comparison `κ ≤ n^(2/3)` for being better than Nesterov's method is an interesting result, but the comparison to SAG/SDCA is more critical. The conclusion that EMGD is worse than SAG/SDCA when `κ > n^(1/2)` highlights its limited applicability in the finite-sum setting where these methods excel.
*   The high-probability convergence result is a genuine strength. Most analyses of SVRG/SAGA focus on convergence in expectation. It would strengthen the paper significantly to explicitly frame this as a key advantage and explain why their analytical approach (e.g., the shrinking domain) is necessary to achieve it.

### Recommendation:

This paper tackles an important problem and presents a theoretically sound algorithm. However, its significance and originality are severely diminished by a failure to engage with the most relevant body of prior work, specifically variance-reduced methods like SVRG. The core update rule is not novel, and the resulting complexity is worse than state-of-the-art methods in the standard finite-sum setting.

While the high-probability guarantee and generality to constrained/non-finite-sum problems are valuable, the paper would require a major revision to be acceptable. The authors must:
1.  Thoroughly revise the related work to properly position their algorithm relative to SVRG, SAGA, etc.
2.  Clearly state that the novelty lies in the specific epoch/shrinking-domain structure and the resulting high-probability analysis, not the gradient estimator itself.
3.  Provide a direct and honest comparison of computational complexity against SVRG/SAGA, acknowledging the regimes where EMGD is inferior.

As it stands, the paper misrepresents the state of the art and overclaims its contribution. For these reasons, I recommend rejection in its current form.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated and important problem.
*   Provides a rigorous high-probability convergence proof, which is stronger than typical in-expectation results for similar methods.
*   The method is more general than many competitors (works for constrained problems and general stochastic objectives).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Fails to cite or compare with SVRG, despite using an identical gradient estimator. This is a critical omission.
*   The `O(κ^2)` dependency on stochastic gradients is worse than the state of the art (`O(κ)` in SVRG/SAGA).
*   The claims of novelty and significance are overstated due to the missing context of variance-reduction literature.
*   Practicality is limited by the requirement to know the condition number κ.
