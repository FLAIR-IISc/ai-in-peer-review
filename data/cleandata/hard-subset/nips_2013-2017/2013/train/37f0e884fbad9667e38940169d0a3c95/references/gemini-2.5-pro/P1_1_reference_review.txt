### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing smooth and strongly convex functions, particularly in settings where the condition number κ is large, making algorithms that depend on κ full gradient evaluations computationally expensive. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which combines full gradient and stochastic gradient computations. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed at the current solution anchor. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" which is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient estimator, using the full gradient as a control variate. The main theoretical result is that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This key result makes the number of expensive full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ, trading this dependency for a larger number of cheaper stochastic gradient computations. The authors compare this complexity to standard full-gradient methods, stochastic methods, and specialized finite-sum methods like SAG and SDCA, highlighting advantages in terms of generality (handling constraints), memory requirements, and obtaining a high-probability convergence guarantee.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a significant and well-motivated problem. Reducing the reliance on full gradient computations for ill-conditioned problems is a crucial challenge in large-scale machine learning and optimization. The goal of making the number of full gradient calls independent of κ is novel and highly desirable.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed EMGD algorithm. The proof, based on induction over epochs and using a martingale concentration inequality for the inner loop analysis, appears sound. The resulting high-probability convergence guarantee is stronger than the guarantees in expectation that are common for many stochastic algorithms (like SAG and SDCA).

3.  **Generality of the Approach:** Unlike methods such as SAG and SDCA which are specifically designed for finite-sum objective functions and unconstrained problems, EMGD is presented in a more general stochastic optimization framework (F(w) = E[f(w)]). Furthermore, its update rule naturally incorporates projection onto a convex set W, making it applicable to constrained optimization problems, which is a significant advantage.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The algorithm is presented cleanly, the motivation is easy to follow, and the theoretical claims are stated precisely. The analysis section, while dense, lays out its main ideas and steps coherently.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Missing Key Related Work:** The "mixed gradient" estimator `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) − ∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the gradient estimator used in the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). SVRG is a highly influential algorithm that also operates in epochs, computes one full gradient per epoch, and achieves a linear convergence rate. The omission of any mention, citation, or comparison to SVRG is a major weakness. The proposed EMGD algorithm is essentially a variant of SVRG applied with a specific step-size and a shrinking projection domain. The analysis technique is also different, but the core algorithmic idea is not new. This significantly impacts the paper's claim to originality.

2.  **High Complexity in Stochastic Gradients:** The number of stochastic gradients required per epoch is O(κ²). This is a very high dependence on the condition number. In contrast, standard analyses of SVRG and related methods like SAGA show a complexity that is linear in κ (e.g., O((n+κ)log(1/ε)) total stochastic gradient complexity for SVRG in the finite-sum setting). This makes EMGD potentially much less efficient than existing variance-reduced methods, especially for moderately ill-conditioned problems. The authors do not justify why this quadratic dependence is necessary or whether it is an artifact of their analysis.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is the main focus, the absence of any experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of EMGD. An empirical comparison against accelerated gradient descent and, more importantly, against SVRG would be necessary to demonstrate the practical value of the proposed algorithm and its specific analysis (e.g., the shrinking ball projection). Experiments would also shed light on how the O(κ²) complexity manifests in practice.

4.  **Practicality Concerns:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the smoothness (L) and strong convexity (λ) constants to set the number of inner iterations T. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion and suggest methods for estimation, but this remains a significant practical hurdle. Many modern optimization methods are adaptive or less sensitive to precise knowledge of these parameters.

### Detailed Comments

*   The main contribution needs to be re-framed in light of SVRG. Is the contribution a new analysis of an SVRG-like algorithm for the general expectation setting (not just finite sums)? Is it the combination with the shrinking domain projection? The current framing suggests the invention of the mixed gradient estimator, which is not accurate. The paper must include a thorough discussion of SVRG, comparing the algorithms, theoretical guarantees (e.g., high-probability vs. expectation), and complexities.
*   The analysis leading to the O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients (Section 4.2) should be revisited. Specifically, the bounds on terms AT and BT seem loose. For instance, the bound on AT uses a worst-case `||w_t - w̄|| ≤ Δ`, which might be improved. Clarifying if this `κ²` dependence is fundamental to the algorithm/analysis or could be improved would strengthen the paper.
*   The comparison to SAG/SDCA in Section 3.4 is useful, but the most relevant comparison to SVRG is missing. SVRG shares the low memory footprint and amenability to distributed computation that the authors claim as advantages for EMGD over SAG/SDCA.

### Justification of Rating

*   **Quality:** The technical analysis appears correct, but the work is incomplete due to the failure to acknowledge and compare with cornerstone prior work (SVRG). The O(κ²) complexity also raises questions about the tightness of the analysis and the method's efficiency.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
*   **Originality:** This is the paper's weakest point. The core algorithmic idea of the mixed gradient is not original and was introduced in SVRG. The novelty lies in the specific analysis with the shrinking domain, but this is an incremental contribution at best, and the paper does not frame it as such.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant. However, the proposed solution appears less efficient (O(κ²) vs. O(κ) for SVRG) than existing methods and its novelty is questionable. Therefore, its potential impact on the field is limited unless the authors can clearly articulate a setting where EMGD outperforms SVRG (perhaps due to the high-probability guarantee or better performance in the general expectation setting).

### Recommendation

I recommend **rejection** for this paper in its current form. The failure to cite and compare against SVRG is a critical flaw that misrepresents the paper's contribution and originality. While the problem is important and the analysis is detailed, the work feels incomplete and potentially less performant than existing, highly related methods. If the authors were to thoroughly revise the paper to position it as a new analysis or variant of SVRG, highlighting its specific advantages (e.g., the high-probability bound for the general stochastic setting) and providing a compelling reason for the O(κ²) complexity (or improving it), the paper could be a candidate for acceptance at a future venue. The addition of empirical results would also be essential to demonstrate its practical relevance.
