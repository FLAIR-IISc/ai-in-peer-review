Summary
The paper studies smooth and strongly convex optimization with access to both a full-gradient oracle and a stochastic “function” oracle. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) computes one full gradient per epoch and, within each epoch, performs T projected updates using a mixed gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄). With appropriately shrinking trust regions and T = Ω(κ2 log(1/δ)), the method achieves a constant error contraction per epoch, yielding linear convergence. The main advertised benefit is that the number of full-gradient evaluations is O(log(1/ε)), independent of the condition number κ, at the expense of O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The authors compare complexity with Nesterov’s accelerated gradient (full gradients only) and with SAG/SDCA for finite-sum objectives, and argue EMGD trades expensive full gradients for cheaper stochastic gradients, works with constraints, and uses O(d) memory.

Relation to prior work
The mixed gradient estimator is essentially identical to that used in variance-reduced methods such as SVRG (and, in finite-sum settings, SAGA/SDCA/MISO), i.e., ∇F(ŵ) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(ŵ). Those methods also use one full gradient per epoch and achieve linear convergence with total gradient complexity O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) for finite sums. Compared to that literature, EMGD imposes a shrinking ball constraint to control the variance and requires T = Ω(κ2), which is significantly larger than the Ω(κ) inner-loop lengths common in variance-reduced methods. The paper cites SAG and SDCA but omits closely related SVRG-style work; more importantly, it does not clearly position the novelty of EMGD relative to these established techniques that achieve better κ-dependence and stronger practicality.

Strengths
- Clean and mostly rigorous high-probability analysis yielding linear convergence under standard smoothness and strong convexity assumptions.
- The number of full-gradient evaluations is O(log(1/ε)), aligning with the intuition behind variance reduction and attractive when full gradients are expensive and stochastic gradients are cheap.
- Handles convex constraints via projections onto W ∩ B(·), and uses only O(d) memory.
- The proofs carefully control martingale terms and make the contraction per epoch explicit.

Weaknesses
- Originality is weak: the mixed gradient is identical to SVRG’s estimator, and the epoch structure mirrors established variance-reduction schemes; the main difference is the shrinking trust region, which leads to a worse κ-dependence (κ2) on the number of stochastic gradients.
- No experiments. For a NeurIPS paper, empirical validation against strong baselines (accelerated full-gradient, SAG/SAGA, SVRG, SDCA) is essential to substantiate practical benefits, sensitivity to κ, and the overhead of projections onto shrinking balls.
- Practicality concerns: (i) Requires prior knowledge of κ (acknowledged) and also an initial radius Δ1 that depends on F(0) − F(w*), which is generally unavailable; (ii) The constant 1152 in T ≥ 1152 L2/λ2 ln(1/δ) suggests a very large inner loop; (iii) Projections onto W ∩ B(w̄; Δ) may be substantially more costly than projections onto W alone.
- The finite-sum complexity is generally dominated by established variance-reduced methods: SDCA/SVRG/SAGA achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) vs EMGD’s O(n log(1/ε) + κ2 log(1/ε)); for moderate/large κ, κ2 is prohibitive. The claimed regimes where EMGD is competitive are narrow.
- Oracle model realism: having both an exact full-gradient oracle and a sampling oracle is natural for finite sums, but in expectation problems one typically cannot compute ∇F exactly; discussion of how Og is realized in practice is thin.
- Some technical presentation issues: the strong convexity condition (4) is not the standard definition; the prox update in Step 8 appears to have typographical issues (missing 1/2 factor); several typos; δ ≤ e−1/2 is an odd standing assumption; the “high-probability vs in-expectation” advantage over SAG/SDCA is overstated given existing tail bounds.

Quality
The core argument is sound in spirit and the high-probability contraction per epoch is well structured, but the κ2 dependence is pessimistic relative to the state of the art that uses the same estimator without a shrinking ball. The paper lacks empirical support and does not convincingly argue for scenarios where EMGD’s tradeoff is preferable in practice.

Clarity
Generally readable, with a clear algorithm box and a self-contained proof sketch. However, consistency and precision could be improved (definition of strong convexity, prox step formatting, and several typos). The comparison section should be tightened and made more quantitative.

Originality
Limited. The main mechanism is a known variance-reduced estimator; the novelty (shrinking domain to bound variance) leads to inferior complexity. The positioning relative to SVRG/SAGA-type methods is insufficient.

Significance
Modest. The condition-number–independent count of full gradients is not unique to this work; established methods already offer that with significantly better total complexity and strong empirical performance. Without experiments or improved theory (e.g., reducing κ2 to κ, removing the trust-region, or handling unknown κ adaptively), the contribution is incremental.

Pros
- Clear high-probability linear convergence proof
- Works with constraints; low memory
- Conceptually clean tradeoff between full and stochastic gradients

Cons
- Weak novelty; essentially SVRG-like estimator with worse κ dependence
- No experiments; unclear practical benefit
- Requires κ and a dubious Δ1; large inner-loop constants
- Comparisons to strong baselines are incomplete

Questions for authors
- Can you reduce the κ2 dependence to κ with your framework (e.g., via variance reduction analysis without shrinking balls)?
- How do you choose Δ1 and handle unknown κ in practice? Any adaptive/doubling strategies?
- Why is the shrinking ball necessary given known analyses of the same estimator without such constraints?
- Can you provide empirical results comparing to accelerated gradient, SAG/SVRG/SAGA/SDCA on standard problems (LS, logistic), including constrained cases?

Recommendation
Reject. The paper’s main idea overlaps substantially with established variance-reduction methods while offering weaker complexity and no empirical validation. Strengthening the theory to improve κ dependence, clarifying novelty, and adding thorough experiments would be necessary for acceptance.
