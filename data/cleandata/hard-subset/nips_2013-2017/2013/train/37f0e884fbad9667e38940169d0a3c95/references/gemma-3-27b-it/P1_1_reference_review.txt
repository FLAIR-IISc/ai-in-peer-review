## NIPS Review: Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the computational cost associated with full gradient evaluations in smooth, strongly convex optimization. The authors observe that while full gradient methods achieve faster convergence rates (O(√κ log(1/ε))) compared to stochastic gradient methods, the dependence on the condition number κ can be prohibitive for large datasets. EMGD aims to bridge this gap by strategically combining full and stochastic gradients within epochs, achieving a linear convergence rate (O(log(1/ε))) with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of κ. The core idea involves evaluating one full gradient and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, leveraging the benefits of both approaches.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself within the broader landscape of first-order optimization, referencing key works in deterministic (Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) and stochastic optimization (SGD, SAG, SDCA).  It correctly identifies the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost. The authors appropriately highlight the limitations of existing methods in scenarios with large condition numbers and convincingly argue that their approach offers a beneficial alternative, particularly when stochastic gradients are cheaper to compute. The connections to epoch gradient descent [9] are clearly stated and motivate the algorithm’s structure. The comparison with SAG and SDCA in Table 2 is insightful.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practical problem:**  The paper tackles a significant challenge in large-scale optimization – the computational cost of full gradient evaluations. The motivation is clear and well-articulated.
* **Novel Algorithm:** EMGD offers a conceptually sound approach to balancing the benefits of full and stochastic gradients. Dividing the process into epochs and mixing gradients within them is a potentially effective strategy.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide a theoretical convergence analysis, demonstrating a linear convergence rate and showing that the number of full gradient evaluations is independent of the condition number.  This is the primary contribution of the paper.
* **Clear Comparisons:** Table 2 provides a useful comparison of the computational complexity of EMGD with other related algorithms.
* **Discussion of Limitations & Future Work:** The authors acknowledge the need for condition number estimation in practice and suggest potential avenues for addressing this, demonstrating a realistic perspective on the work.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors' arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical analysis is promising, the practical implications of the O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch are not fully explored. For very large κ, this could still be computationally expensive. A discussion of strategies to mitigate this (e.g., mini-batching, sampling schemes) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any experimental results. Demonstrating the effectiveness of EMGD on real-world datasets, and comparing its performance to existing algorithms, is crucial to validate the theoretical claims. The lack of experimental validation significantly weakens the paper.
* **Assumption Dependence:** The algorithm relies on the assumption of both full and stochastic gradient access. While stated clearly, this assumption limits its applicability to certain problem settings.
* **Constant Factors:** While the authors prove the asymptotic complexity, the constant factors hidden within the O(log(1/ε)) bound are not addressed. These constants could potentially be large enough to negate the benefits of the algorithm in practice.
* **Proof Details:** While the main ideas of the proof are outlined, some details are glossed over.  A more complete presentation of the proof would increase confidence in the correctness of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially impactful contribution to the field of optimization.  The core idea of EMGD is innovative and addresses a genuine practical challenge. However, the lack of empirical evaluation is a significant weakness.  Without experimental validation, it's difficult to assess the true benefits of the algorithm and its practical feasibility.  The practical implications of the κ² dependence in the number of stochastic gradients also needs further consideration.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and a provable linear convergence rate with condition number independence.
* Addresses a relevant and important problem in large-scale optimization.
* Clear presentation and well-motivated approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical evaluation. This is a *major* drawback.
* Limited discussion of practical considerations regarding the computational cost of O(κ²) stochastic gradients.
* Some details of the proof are not fully fleshed out.




**Rating:**  **Weak Reject**

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejecting this paper in its current form. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the absence of experimental results and a more thorough discussion of practical considerations significantly detract from its overall impact. I would encourage the authors to conduct experiments and address the weaknesses outlined above before resubmitting.  If they can demonstrate practical performance gains, this paper could be a strong contribution to NIPS.
