## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm designed to achieve linear convergence for smooth, strongly convex optimization problems while reducing the dependence on the condition number (κ) when accessing full gradients. The core idea is to interleave full gradient evaluations with stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, aiming to reduce the total number of full gradient computations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)).  The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating this improved complexity and compare EMGD to existing methods like SAG, SDCA, and full gradient descent, highlighting its advantages in certain scenarios, particularly regarding memory usage and applicability to constrained problems.

**Related Work:**

The paper effectively positions itself within the landscape of first-order optimization methods. It clearly outlines the trade-offs between full-gradient and stochastic-gradient approaches, referencing key works in both deterministic (Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) and stochastic optimization (SAG, SDCA, Epoch Gradient Descent). The discussion of related work is comprehensive and provides good context for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of full and stochastic gradients within an epoch structure to achieve condition number independence in the number of full gradient evaluations is a potentially significant contribution. This addresses a practical bottleneck in large-scale optimization, where full gradient computations are expensive.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a well-defined convergence theorem (Theorem 1) demonstrating linear convergence and the claimed reduction in the number of full gradient evaluations. The proof sketch is provided, and the analysis appears sound.
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The authors thoughtfully compare EMGD to other relevant algorithms (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) in terms of computational complexity, highlighting scenarios where EMGD offers advantages.  The discussion about memory usage and applicability to constrained optimization are valuable points.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The acknowledgement of the need to estimate the condition number κ in practice and the discussion of potential methods for doing so (sampling, leveraging regularizers) demonstrate an awareness of real-world implementation challenges.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the mathematical notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality of Implementation:** While the theoretical analysis is compelling, the practical implementation details are somewhat sparse. The cost of determining the epoch size and step size (η) based on the condition number, particularly in real-world scenarios where κ is unknown, is not fully addressed. The algorithm assumes access to both a gradient oracle and a function oracle, which might require careful consideration in certain applications.
* **Constant Factors:** The analysis focuses on asymptotic complexity. The constants hidden within the O(log(1/ε)) terms could be significant in practice, potentially diminishing the benefits of EMGD compared to simpler algorithms, especially for small values of ε.  A more detailed discussion of these constants would be beneficial.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical results. While the theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the performance of EMGD on benchmark datasets would significantly strengthen the contribution and provide practical validation of the theoretical claims.  Comparing EMGD's runtime to SAG, SDCA, and full gradient descent on real-world problems would be highly valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of L-smoothness and λ-strong convexity, while standard, may not hold in all practical scenarios.  The robustness of the algorithm to violations of these assumptions is not discussed.
* **Theorem 1’s Conditions:** The conditions required for Theorem 1 to hold (specifically regarding δ and ∆1) seem quite strict and could limit the applicability of the algorithm.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel algorithm with strong theoretical guarantees for reducing the dependence on the condition number in full-gradient optimization.
* The analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the interplay between full and stochastic gradients.
* The comparison to existing methods is thoughtful and highlights potential advantages of EMGD.
* The paper addresses an important practical problem in large-scale optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical evaluation raises concerns about the practical performance of the algorithm.
* The strict conditions for Theorem 1 and the potential impact of constant factors could limit the algorithm's applicability.
* The limited discussion of implementation details and practical considerations makes it difficult to assess the real-world feasibility of EMGD.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of optimization. The idea of interleaving full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition number independence is novel and addresses a practical challenge in large-scale learning. However, the lack of empirical validation is a major concern. 

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.** I recommend accepting this paper with the caveat that the authors are strongly encouraged to include empirical results in a revised version. Demonstrating the performance of EMGD on benchmark datasets would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase its impact. Addressing the concerns regarding constant factors and the practicality of implementation would also be beneficial.  Without empirical validation, the theoretical contribution, while valuable, remains somewhat speculative.
