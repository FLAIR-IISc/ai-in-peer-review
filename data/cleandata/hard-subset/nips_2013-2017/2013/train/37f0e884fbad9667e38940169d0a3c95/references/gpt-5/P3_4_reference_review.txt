Summary
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization with access to both (i) a full gradient oracle Og(w)=∇F(w) and (ii) a function oracle that yields f with Ef[f(w)]=F(w) and L-smoothness, enabling unbiased stochastic gradients. The goal is to make the number of full-gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ=L/λ while achieving linear convergence. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) runs in epochs; each epoch computes one full gradient at the epoch reference point w̄k and then performs T inner updates using the “mixed” gradient g̃=∇F(w̄k)+∇f(w)−∇f(w̄k), i.e., a control-variate estimator. EMGD also constrains each inner iterate to a shrinking ℓ2-ball around w̄k. The analysis shows a per-epoch constant contraction of the suboptimality, yielding O(log(1/ϵ)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ϵ)) stochastic gradients with high probability. The authors compare to Nesterov’s accelerated methods (full gradient), SGD variants, and finite-sum methods such as SAG and SDCA, and argue benefits of EMGD in memory and constrained settings.

Relation to prior work
- Deterministic first-order methods: The background on gradient and accelerated methods is accurate.
- Stochastic optimization: The discussion of rates in convex and strongly convex settings is standard.
- Finite-sum variance-reduced methods: The mixed gradient g̃=∇F(w̄)+∇f(w)−∇f(w̄) is precisely the SVRG control variate estimator (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which also uses one full gradient per epoch and achieves linear convergence with O((n+κ) log(1/ϵ)) gradient computations for finite-sum problems, without the shrinking ball. The paper cites SAG and SDCA but does not discuss SVRG (or later SAGA), which is very close methodologically and typically achieves better κ dependence. This omission is a major gap in the related work and comparison.
- Semi-stochastic and hybrid methods: The paper mentions older semi-stochastic works but those do not capture the modern variance-reduction line.

Technical quality
- The proof strategy is clear: rewrite the objective using the reference point gradient, control the stochastic term via L-smoothness and the shrinking ball to bound variance, and use Azuma-Hoeffding for a high-probability bound. The induction across epochs and the final union bound are standard and correct.
- The key limitation is the κ^2 dependence in the number of stochastic gradients per epoch (T=Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ))). This stems from bounding the variance by L^2‖w−w̄‖^2 and forcing ‖w−w̄‖≤Δ via a shrinking ball. In contrast, SVRG-style analyses bound the variance by the function suboptimality and obtain T=O(κ), leading to O((n+κ) log(1/ϵ)) complexity in the finite-sum case, or O(κ log(1/ϵ)) stochastic gradients per full gradient in a generic oracle setting. Thus, EMGD’s stochastic gradient complexity is suboptimal by a factor of κ.
- Assumptions and practicality:
  - EMGD requires knowledge of κ to choose T and η and to guarantee per-epoch contraction; the authors acknowledge this and propose heuristics, but the method as stated is not adaptive.
  - The analysis requires an initial radius Δ1 that contains w*, expressed via F(0)−F(w*), which is not available; in constrained problems Δ1 could be set to the domain diameter, but in unconstrained problems this is problematic.
  - The per-iteration update involves projection onto W∩B(w̄k;Δk); this is easy for simple W but could be costly otherwise. The paper claims support for constrained problems, but the practical efficiency hinges on the projection cost.
- Minor issues: Some notation/typos in Algorithm 1 (missing 1/2 in the proximal term in the pseudo-code; “epoches”), and occasional imprecisions in the assumptions (e.g., strong convexity stated via gradient Lipschitz from below).

Originality
The algorithmic idea—using control variates with one full gradient per epoch—is not new and closely matches SVRG. The main distinguishing feature is the use of a shrinking trust region to control variance, but this leads to a weaker κ dependence. Without a clear advantage over SVRG/SAGA (e.g., better theory, broader applicability, or stronger guarantees), the originality is limited.

Significance
- Positive: The aim to decouple the number of full gradients from κ is meaningful; the high-probability linear convergence and low memory requirements are valuable; applicability to constrained problems is a plus.
- Negative: The κ^2 dependence renders the method less competitive than SVRG/SDCA in both finite-sum and generic oracle regimes. The paper lacks empirical evaluation to demonstrate regimes where the trade-off (very few full gradients but many stochastic gradients) is practically superior (e.g., when full gradients are extremely costly and stochastic gradients are very cheap and parallelizable).

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, with a complete proof and sensible structure. However, the related work needs substantial expansion to include SVRG and SAGA, and the comparisons should be updated accordingly. The algorithm pseudo-code should be corrected, and practical guidance for setting Δ1 and adapting to unknown κ should be clarified.

Pros
- Clear problem motivation; reduces full-gradient calls to O(log(1/ϵ)).
- High-probability linear convergence proof with simple tools.
- Low memory footprint; handles constrained domains.

Cons
- Misses key related work (SVRG) that uses the same estimator and achieves better κ dependence.
- Suboptimal stochastic gradient complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ϵ)).
- Requires knowledge of κ and an initial radius containing w*; relies on projections onto shrinking balls.
- No experiments to validate practical benefits.

Questions and suggestions
- Please compare directly to SVRG/SAGA (theoretically and experimentally). Can your analysis be tightened to achieve T=O(κ) without the shrinking ball?
- Can you make EMGD adaptive to unknown κ (e.g., via doubling/line-search) and to Δ1?
- In the finite-sum case, what is the wall-clock trade-off versus SDCA/SVRG when full gradients can be parallelized?
- Provide experiments illustrating regimes where EMGD excels (e.g., heavy regularization, very large n with inexpensive per-sample gradients, constrained settings).

Recommendation
I lean toward rejection in its current form due to incomplete related work and weaker theoretical guarantees than closely related methods. With a thorough comparison to SVRG/SAGA and an improved analysis (or compelling empirical evidence) narrowing the κ^2 gap, the contribution could become competitive.
