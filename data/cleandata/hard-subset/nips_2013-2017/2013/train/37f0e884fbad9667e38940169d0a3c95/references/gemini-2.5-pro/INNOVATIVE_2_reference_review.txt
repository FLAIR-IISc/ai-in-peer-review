### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex functions, a setting where the optimal complexity for methods using full gradients has a dependence on the condition number κ, typically `O(√κ log(1/ε))`. The authors propose a novel and ambitious goal: to design an algorithm where the number of expensive full gradient evaluations is independent of κ. To achieve this, they introduce a hybrid model that allows access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle.

The proposed algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), divides the optimization process into epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient to serve as an anchor. Within the epoch, it performs a series of updates using a "mixed gradient," which is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient constructed from the anchor gradient and stochastic gradient information at the current iterate. By progressively shrinking the domain of optimization in each epoch, the algorithm ensures that the variance of the mixed gradient is well-controlled, leading to a constant reduction in error per epoch. The main theoretical result is that EMGD achieves an ε-accurate solution with `O(log(1/ε))` full gradient calls and `O(κ² log(1/ε))` stochastic gradient calls. This effectively trades a large number of cheap stochastic gradients to drastically reduce the number of expensive full gradients, making the latter independent of the problem's conditioning.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Originality and Significance:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its exceptional novelty and the significance of the problem it tackles. The goal of eliminating the condition number dependence for full gradient calls is a bold departure from the standard line of research in first-order methods. While the community has long accepted the `√κ` lower bound as a fundamental limit, this work cleverly bypasses it by changing the computational model, allowing for a second, stochastic oracle. This reframing of the problem is creative and opens up a new and exciting direction for research in hybrid optimization algorithms. The idea of consciously trading a massive number of cheap operations (stochastic gradients) for a minimal number of expensive ones (full gradients) is conceptually powerful and could have a significant impact on how we design algorithms for large-scale settings where different oracles have vastly different costs.

Furthermore, the proposed EMGD algorithm offers several advantages over related state-of-the-art methods like SAG and SDCA. Its applicability to general constrained convex optimization problems is a major plus, as many variance-reduced methods are tailored specifically for unconstrained finite-sum objectives. The high-probability convergence guarantee is also a theoretically stronger result than the in-expectation guarantees that are common in this area.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound. The analysis, which relies on an inductive argument over epochs and a martingale concentration inequality to bound the stochastic terms, is clearly presented and appears correct. The construction of the mixed gradient and its use within a shrinking ball is an elegant way to control variance and drive the linear convergence.

However, the quality is somewhat diminished by a significant weakness in the resulting complexity. The number of stochastic gradients required per epoch scales with `O(κ²)`. This dependence is substantially worse than the `O(κ)` dependence achieved by other state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods such as SVRG and SDCA. In the finite-sum setting, the total complexity of EMGD would be roughly `O((n + κ²) log(1/ε))`, which for moderate to large κ, is uncompetitive with SDCA's `O((n + κ) log(1/ε))`. While the authors' focus is on the number of full gradients, the overall computational cost cannot be ignored, and this `κ²` factor is a major drawback.

Another practical weakness is the requirement that the condition number κ be known beforehand to set the number of inner iterations `T`. While the authors correctly identify this as a limitation and suggest avenues for future work, it hinders the algorithm's immediate applicability.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the algorithm is presented cleanly, and the theoretical analysis, though technical, is well-structured. The comparison with existing work is thorough and honestly highlights both the advantages (generality, memory, stronger convergence guarantee) and disadvantages (computational cost in some regimes) of the proposed method.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty:** The paper introduces a genuinely new and bold idea—making full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire a new line of research.
2.  **Significant Goal:** Reducing the reliance on expensive full gradients in ill-conditioned problems is a highly important and practical goal. This paper provides the first proof-of-concept that this is achievable.
3.  **Theoretical Merit:** The work is theoretically solid, providing a high-probability linear convergence guarantee for a general class of constrained optimization problems, which is a strong result.
4.  **Opens New Questions:** The suboptimal `κ²` rate is not a fatal flaw but rather an invitation for future work. This paper lays the foundation, and it is highly likely that others will build upon this novel framework to improve the rates, for example by incorporating acceleration or more sophisticated variance reduction schemes.

### Arguments Against Rejection:

1.  **Suboptimal Complexity:** The `O(κ²)` dependence on stochastic gradients makes the algorithm's total runtime potentially worse than existing methods in many scenarios. A reviewer focused solely on state-of-the-art complexity might see this as a reason for rejection.
2.  **Practicality Concerns:** The need to know κ in advance limits its use as an off-the-shelf solver.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While the algorithm's computational complexity in terms of stochastic gradients is not state-of-the-art, this weakness is overshadowed by the outstanding novelty and significance of its core idea. This paper is not an incremental improvement; it is a bold proposal that challenges a long-held barrier in optimization by creatively changing the rules. It successfully demonstrates that the number of expensive full gradient computations can be made independent of problem conditioning, a powerful and inspiring result. The work is likely to stimulate new research on designing cost-aware hybrid algorithms. NIPS should be a venue for such ambitious and thought-provoking research, even if the first instantiation of the idea is not yet methodologically perfect. The weaknesses identified are clear targets for follow-up work, which this paper is sure to generate.
