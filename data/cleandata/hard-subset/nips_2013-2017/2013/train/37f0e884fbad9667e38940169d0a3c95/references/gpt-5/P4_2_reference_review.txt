Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies first-order optimization for smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives F over a convex set W, under an oracle model that provides (i) a full gradient oracle Og(w) = ∇F(w), and (ii) a function oracle Of returning random smooth functions f with F(w) = Ef[f(w)]. The goal is to reduce reliance on expensive full gradients while achieving linear convergence.

The proposed algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch k, it computes one full gradient at a reference point w̄k. Within the epoch, it performs T iterations using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), together with a proximal-gradient step constrained to a shrinking Euclidean ball B(w̄k; Δk) (with Δk+1 = Δk/√2). The variance of the stochastic component is controlled by smoothness and the shrinking radius. The main theorem shows a high-probability linear decrease in suboptimality and distance to the optimum, with m epochs yielding an O(2−m) reduction. Consequently, the number of full gradient evaluations is O(log(1/ε)), independent of the condition number κ = L/λ, while the number of stochastic-gradient evaluations is O(κ^2 log(1/ε)).

Related work is discussed for deterministic methods (e.g., Nesterov’s accelerated gradient with O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations) and stochastic methods (SGD variants with polynomial-in-1/ε rates; methods with linear convergence for finite sums such as SAG and SDCA). The paper positions EMGD as trading most full gradient calls for stochastic ones while retaining a linear rate and requiring only O(d) memory and working with constraints.

Quality
Soundness: The high-level proof strategy—variance reduction via control variates (difference-of-gradients at wt and w̄k plus a full gradient snapshot), and domain shrinkage to bound the stochastic term—follows established principles. The Azuma–Hoeffding bound on a martingale difference sequence and smoothness are used to derive a per-epoch contraction. However, there are technical issues to address:

- Strong convexity is incorrectly stated in equation (4) as a lower Lipschitz bound on the gradient norm, ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖. The standard condition is (∇F(w) − ∇F(w′))⊤(w − w′) ≥ λ‖w − w′‖^2. Several subsequent inequalities (e.g., (13)) appear to use a −λ‖wt − w*‖^2 term instead of the standard −(λ/2)‖wt − w*‖^2 that follows from strong convexity; constants should be checked and corrected.
- The analysis requires knowing κ to set T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) and the step size η = 1/(L√T); the paper acknowledges this limitation.
- The projection in each iteration is onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk). While projecting onto a ball is easy and many W are simple, the intersection may not always admit an inexpensive projection; the paper notes feasibility “provided” step 8 can be solved efficiently.

Completeness: The theoretical result is self-contained at a high level, but constants are loose (e.g., T ≥ 1152(L/λ)^2 ln(1/δ)) and no empirical validation is provided to corroborate practical gains or the behavior of the radius-shrink schedule.

Clarity
The algorithmic description is generally understandable, and the proof outline is presented with intermediate lemmas. However, the exposition suffers from:

- Misstatements of strong convexity and some factor inconsistencies, duplicate equation numbering, and several typographical errors (e.g., “epoches”).
- Parameter choices and their dependence on κ and δ could be summarized more cleanly; guidance on Δ1 and practical tuning is limited.

Originality
The mixed gradient estimator g̃t = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄) is essentially the control-variate estimator used by variance-reduced stochastic methods for finite-sum problems (e.g., SAG; SDCA in dual; and importantly SVRG). The paper cites SAG and SDCA, but omits discussion of SVRG-style methods that compute a full gradient at a snapshot point and then use difference-of-gradients within an epoch to achieve linear convergence with O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) total gradient computations and O(d) memory, and without the shrinking-ball constraint. Given this close similarity, the novelty of EMGD’s core idea is limited. The use of an explicit shrinking ball to bound variance, and the condition-number–independent count of full gradients, are not unique relative to SVRG-like methods, which also use periodic full gradients with O(log(1/ε)) snapshots.

Significance
The objective—reducing full-gradient usage to O(log(1/ε))—is relevant, and a high-probability linear rate with O(d) memory and support for constraints is useful. However, the κ^2 dependence in the number of stochastic gradients is substantially worse than the κ dependence achieved by SVRG/SAGA/S2GD and the (n + κ) scaling of SDCA for finite sums. Without empirical evidence or tighter theory, the incremental benefit over existing variance-reduced methods appears modest.

Pros
- Clear goal of minimizing full-gradient calls while retaining a linear convergence rate.
- High-probability linear convergence guarantee with a simple per-epoch structure.
- O(d) memory and applicability to constrained problems (subject to efficient projection onto W ∩ ball).
- Discussion of distributed computation for the full gradient step.

Cons
- Core estimator and epoch structure are very close to established variance-reduced methods; missing citations and comparisons to SVRG/S2GD reduce novelty.
- Worse dependence on κ: O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients vs. O(κ log(1/ε)) in state-of-the-art methods.
- Technical inaccuracies in the statement/use of strong convexity; several clarity/notation issues.
- Requires prior knowledge of κ and an initial radius Δ1; no empirical validation.
- The mixed-oracle model assumes access to a true full gradient oracle Og even when F is given only via Of (outside finite-sum), which may be unrealistic.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the strong convexity statements and all constants that depend on them; ensure consistency of 1/2 factors.
- Provide a thorough comparison with SVRG/S2GD/SAGA, both theoretically (oracle complexity and memory) and experimentally. Clarify what is novel relative to those methods.
- Investigate whether the shrinking-ball constraint can be removed while retaining high-probability guarantees and improving the κ dependence to O(κ).
- Clarify the oracle model: in the general expectation setting, discuss how Og is implemented; for finite sums, relate Og to a pass over data and discuss practical costs.
- Add experiments on standard strongly convex problems to validate the claimed trade-offs and the benefit of condition-number–independent full gradients.
- Provide guidance for choosing Δ1, T, η without oracle knowledge of κ, e.g., via adaptive schemes.

Recommendation
Given the close overlap with existing variance-reduction methods, the weaker κ dependence, and technical clarity issues, I do not recommend acceptance in its current form. Strengthening the theoretical dependence, correcting technical inaccuracies, and positioning the contribution clearly relative to SVRG/S2GD/SAGA with empirical evidence would substantially improve the case for publication.
