This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm for smooth, strongly convex problems aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations by strategically combining them with stochastic gradients. The core idea, building upon epoch gradient descent, involves performing a mixed gradient descent step with one full gradient and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, achieving a linear convergence rate and a full gradient complexity of O(log(1/ε)) independent of the condition number κ.

The problem addressed is highly relevant to NIPS, given the prevalence of large-scale convex optimization in machine learning and engineering. The motivation – reducing the computational cost of full gradient evaluations – is well-articulated and directly addresses a practical challenge. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost, and attempt to mitigate the former without significantly increasing the latter. The paper’s connection to prior work, including discussions of deterministic, stochastic, and semi-stochastic optimization methods, as well as related algorithms like SAG and SDCA, is reasonable and demonstrates awareness of the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the problem and motivates the need for an algorithm that reduces the dependence on the condition number for full gradient methods.
*   **Novel Algorithm:** EMGD presents a novel combination of full and stochastic gradients, and the epoch-based approach offers a structured way to balance these evaluations.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a convergence analysis demonstrating a linear convergence rate and a logarithmic dependence on 1/ε for the number of full gradients, independent of κ. This is a significant result if proven correctly.
*   **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The comparison to existing algorithms (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) is insightful and helps position EMGD within the broader landscape of optimization methods. Identifying conditions where EMGD is competitive is a valuable contribution.
*   **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The acknowledgment of the need to estimate the condition number κ in practice, and suggestions for how to approach this (sampling, regularization), adds to the paper's practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of the Algorithm:** While the algorithm is described, the detailed steps within each epoch (specifically, Step 8 in Algorithm 1 involving the constrained minimization) could benefit from further clarification. The choice of projection and its implications are not fully discussed. The reliance on solving a constrained optimization problem each iteration is a potential bottleneck.
*   **Proof Details:** The convergence proof (Section 4) is very high-level and lacks sufficient detail.  Crucial steps, like bounding AT and BT using the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality, are stated but not fully elaborated. Providing more details would significantly strengthen the analysis and build confidence in the results. The jump from the bounds to the overall convergence rate is not fully clear.
*   **Condition Number Estimation:** The discussion of condition number estimation is brief. A more thorough exploration of practical estimation techniques, including their limitations and potential impact on performance, would be valuable. The cost of estimation itself isn't considered.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The complete absence of any experimental evaluation is a major weakness.  While theoretical results are important, demonstrating the performance of EMGD on real-world datasets or synthetic problems would substantially strengthen the paper’s impact. Even a simple comparison with standard methods would be beneficial.
*   **Constant Factors:** While asymptotic complexities are provided, the presence of potentially large constant factors (e.g., in the bounds on AT and BT) is not addressed. These constants could significantly impact practical performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The algorithm is novel and addresses a relevant problem in optimization.
*   The theoretical analysis, if correct, provides strong guarantees on convergence and gradient complexity.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation and main results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the algorithm.
*   The detailed proof is missing and lacks sufficient rigor.
*   The algorithm relies on solving a constrained optimization problem in each iteration, which may be computationally expensive.
*   The discussion of practical considerations, such as condition number estimation, is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising algorithm with strong theoretical foundations. However, the lack of empirical validation and the insufficient detail in the proof significantly weaken its contribution. While the ideas are interesting and the motivation is sound, I recommend **rejection** at this time.  

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   **Provide a complete and detailed proof of all theorems and lemmas.** This is crucial for establishing the validity of the claims.
*   **Include a comprehensive experimental evaluation** on a range of datasets and compare EMGD to existing methods.
*   **Elaborate on the implementation details** of the constrained optimization step in Algorithm 1 and discuss its computational cost.
*   **Provide a more in-depth discussion of condition number estimation techniques** and their practical implications.
*   **Analyze the impact of constant factors** on the overall performance of the algorithm.
*   **Clarify the assumptions** made throughout the paper, particularly regarding the function oracle and the smoothness of the objective function.
