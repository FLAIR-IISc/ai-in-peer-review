### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of optimizing a smooth and strongly convex function. The authors propose a new first-order algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, especially for ill-conditioned problems (i.e., large condition number κ). The key idea is to combine a small number of full gradient evaluations with a large number of cheaper stochastic gradient evaluations. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, one full gradient is computed at a reference point. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" estimator, which combines this fixed full gradient with a stochastic term that corrects for the deviation from the reference point. This technique is a form of variance reduction. The updates are performed within a projection onto a shrinking domain.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate. Specifically, to reach an ε-optimal solution, the algorithm requires O(log(1/ε)) full gradient computations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient computations. The significant claim is that the number of full gradient evaluations is independent of the condition number κ, unlike standard accelerated methods which require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients. The paper compares this complexity to other linear-convergent methods for finite-sum problems, such as SAG and SDCA, and argues that EMGD has advantages in its applicability to general constrained optimization problems, its low storage requirements, and its high-probability convergence guarantee.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**

*   **Strengths**: The theoretical analysis presented in the paper appears to be technically sound. The proof strategy, based on induction over epochs and leveraging the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality to bound the stochastic terms, is a valid approach. The derivation leads to a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is a stronger theoretical result than the in-expectation guarantees provided by some related methods.

*   **Weaknesses**:
    1.  The most significant weakness is the complete lack of experimental validation. For a paper proposing a new optimization algorithm, empirical results are crucial to demonstrate its practical performance, validate the tightness of the theoretical bounds, and compare its efficiency against existing state-of-the-art methods. Without experiments, the claims about the algorithm's practical utility remain speculative.
    2.  The algorithm requires the condition number κ to be known in advance to set the number of iterations per epoch, T. This is a strong assumption that is often not met in practice. While the authors briefly mention potential remedies in the conclusion, this remains a practical limitation of the current work.
    3.  The theoretical complexity for stochastic gradients, O(κ² log(1/ε)), has a quadratic dependence on the condition number. This is a substantial cost and may make the algorithm less competitive than other variance-reduced methods that achieve a linear dependence on κ.

**Clarity:**

*   **Strengths**: The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for reducing full gradient calls is well-established, and the algorithm is presented in a clear, step-by-step format. The related work section provides good context for the problem.

*   **Weaknesses**: The analysis section (Section 4.2) is quite dense. While the steps are laid out, more high-level intuition between the mathematical manipulations would improve readability for a broader audience.

**Originality:**

*   **Strengths**: The goal of designing an algorithm where the number of full gradient evaluations is independent of the condition number is a novel and interesting research direction. The proposed combination of an epoch-based scheme, a variance-reduced gradient, and a shrinking projection domain represents a novel composition of techniques.

*   **Weaknesses**:
    1.  The "mixed gradient" estimator `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is nearly identical to the gradient estimator used in the Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) algorithm (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). SVRG is a highly influential and directly relevant work that is not cited or discussed in this paper. This omission is critical, as SVRG also achieves linear convergence for finite-sum problems with a complexity of O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, which is superior to the O((n + κ²) log(1/ε)) complexity of EMGD.
    2.  Given the strong similarity to SVRG, the novelty of the paper is reduced to the specific epoch structure with the shrinking domain constraint and the resulting high-probability analysis. The paper does not sufficiently argue why this specific combination is superior to SVRG or proximal SVRG, which can also handle certain constraints.

**Significance:**

*   **Strengths**: The paper addresses an important and practical problem in large-scale machine learning and optimization. If the proposed method were shown to be efficient in practice, it would be a valuable contribution. The highlighted advantages, such as handling general constraints, low memory overhead (O(d) vs. O(nd) for SAG), and high-probability guarantees, are potentially significant differentiators from other variance-reduced methods.

*   **Weaknesses**: The significance of the contribution is substantially undermined by the comparison to existing work and the lack of empirical evidence. The unfavorable theoretical complexity compared to SVRG and the absence of experiments make it difficult to ascertain whether the proposed algorithm would be used by practitioners or researchers. While the potential advantages are noted, they are not demonstrated, and it is unclear if they outweigh the higher computational complexity.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  The paper tackles a well-motivated problem: reducing the dependency on expensive full gradients in first-order optimization.
2.  The theoretical goal of achieving a number of full gradient calls that is independent of the condition number κ is novel and interesting.
3.  The analysis provides a strong high-probability convergence guarantee, which is theoretically more robust than in-expectation results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

1.  **Critical Omission of Prior Work**: The paper fails to cite or compare itself to SVRG, a seminal work that uses a very similar variance reduction technique and achieves a better theoretical complexity. This calls the novelty and contribution of the paper into question.
2.  **Lack of Experiments**: There are no empirical results to validate the algorithm's performance. It is standard for papers introducing new optimization algorithms to include experiments comparing them to relevant baselines.
3.  **Unfavorable Complexity**: The O(κ²) dependence on the number of stochastic gradients is worse than the O(κ) dependence achieved by SVRG, suggesting the proposed algorithm may be less efficient in practice.
4.  **Practical Limitation**: The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ, which limits its practical applicability.
