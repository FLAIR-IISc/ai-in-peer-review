This paper introduces a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods in strongly convex optimization by strategically interleaving full and stochastic gradient evaluations. The core idea – dividing the optimization into epochs and performing a mixed gradient descent within each – is intriguing and potentially impactful. The authors convincingly argue that this approach can achieve a condition-number independent iteration complexity for full gradients, a significant improvement over standard methods. This addresses a crucial bottleneck in large-scale optimization where full gradient computation is expensive. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the comparison to existing methods like SAG and SDCA is well-presented.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The EMGD algorithm is a genuinely new approach to tackling the condition number dependence in first-order methods. The combination of epoch-based optimization with a mixed gradient strategy is a clever idea.
*   **Significance:** Reducing the number of full gradient evaluations to O(log 1/ǫ) independent of the condition number is a potentially significant result, especially for large-scale problems.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes a clear theoretical analysis and a well-defined convergence theorem.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner, and the key concepts are explained adequately. The comparison to related work is also well-structured.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need to estimate the condition number) and suggest avenues for future work to address these.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Validation:** The paper lacks empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is strong, it would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating the performance of EMGD on real-world datasets or synthetic problems, and comparing it to state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Condition Number Estimation:** The dependence on knowing or estimating the condition number κ is a practical hurdle. While the authors mention possible approaches, a more detailed discussion of practical estimation techniques would be valuable.
*   **Constant Factors:** The analysis focuses on asymptotic complexity. The constant factors hidden within the O() notation could be substantial and might impact the practical performance of the algorithm.
*   **Domain Constraint:** The gradient mapping in Step 8 of Algorithm 1, constrained to a shrinking domain, may be computationally expensive to implement, depending on the specific problem. This aspect could benefit from further discussion.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel algorithm with the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of optimization for large-scale problems.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and supports the claims made by the authors.
*   The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution is a creative and promising approach.
*   The authors are aware of the limitations of their work and suggest avenues for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness. It’s hard to gauge the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm without experimental results.
*   The practical challenges of condition number estimation could limit the applicability of the algorithm.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of empirical validation, I believe this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of optimization. The core idea is innovative, the theoretical analysis is strong, and the potential impact is substantial. I am willing to overlook the absence of experiments given the novelty and potential significance of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with the strong encouragement to include experimental results in a future version. The authors should prioritize demonstrating the practical performance of EMGD and addressing the challenges of condition number estimation. I also suggest they provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the domain constraint in Algorithm 1.
