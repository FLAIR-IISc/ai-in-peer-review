- Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) combining full and stochastic gradients for smooth, strongly convex problems.
- Main theoretical claim: linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations independent of condition number κ.
- Requires O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, trading off full-gradient cost for many stochastic updates.
- Positions EMGD between full-gradient (e.g., Nesterov) and stochastic methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA) and builds on epoch gradient descent.
- Strength: significant theoretical contribution reducing full-gradient dependence from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)).
- Strength: clear, rigorous convergence analysis with an accessible induction-based proof for Theorem 1.
- Strength: comprehensive comparisons showing EMGD can be more efficient when κ ≤ n^(2/3).
- Strength: discusses practical aspects like storage requirements, constrained optimization applicability, and distributed implementation.
- Strength: well-written, well-structured presentation with clear pseudocode and literature context.
- Weakness: no experimental validation to demonstrate practical performance or confirm theoretical gains.
- Weakness: algorithm requires knowing κ to set parameters (e.g., iterations per epoch), with only vague practical suggestions.
- Weakness: high stochastic gradient complexity O(κ² log(1/ε)) may be prohibitive for large κ and lacks guidance on when trade-off is beneficial.
- Weakness: limited discussion on practical implementation details and cost trade-offs between stochastic and full gradients.
- Weakness: insufficient engagement with more recent accelerated methods beyond Nesterov that could affect comparisons.
- Overall: novel and sound theoretical contribution but practical applicability is unclear without experiments and κ-estimation methods.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; add experiments, provide concrete κ estimation or robust parameter choices, and clarify practical scenarios favoring EMGD.
