Summary
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization and asks whether one can reduce the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations below the O(√κ log(1/ε)) barrier of accelerated methods by mixing in cheaper stochastic gradients. It proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at a “snapshot” point w̄k, then runs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k) while constraining updates to a shrinking Euclidean ball around w̄k. With T ≈ c(L/λ)² log(1/δ), EMGD contracts the error by a constant per epoch and thus achieves linear convergence: O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients with high probability. The authors argue this makes the number of full gradients independent of κ and discuss comparisons to SAG/SDCA and accelerated full-gradient methods.

Relation to prior work
The “mixed gradient” is the variance-reduced estimator popularized by SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which also computes one full gradient per epoch at a snapshot and then uses ∇f(wt)−∇f(w̄)+∇F(w̄). SVRG achieves linear convergence with O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations and O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, without the shrinking-ball device and with better κ dependence. SAGA (Roux et al., NIPS 2012; Defazio et al., 2014) and SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013) are also closely related variance-reduction methods. The paper cites SAG/SDCA but omits SVRG, which appears to be the closest match to EMGD’s core idea and guarantees. Proximal/SVRG variants also handle constraints/composite objectives.

Strengths
- Clear goal: minimizing the number of full gradient evaluations by mixing stochastic gradients is an important and practical question.
- Simple algorithm and analysis: the snapshot-plus-corrected gradient and epoch structure are easy to implement and reason about. The proof provides a clean high-probability linear rate.
- High-probability guarantees: most related analyses are in expectation; this is a (modest) analytical advantage.
- Applicability to constrained problems via projection, and low memory footprint (O(d)).
- Emphasis on the resource trade-off (“passes over data” vs. mini-batch iterations) is helpful to practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Originality: The core estimator and epochic use of one full gradient per epoch is essentially SVRG. EMGD’s main new element seems to be projecting into shrinking balls to control variance, but this comes at the cost of a worse dependence on κ and the need to know κ to set T and the radii. The headline claim—O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations independent of κ—already holds for SVRG/Prox-SVRG.
- Suboptimal complexity: EMGD requires O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, which is significantly worse than O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) achieved by SVRG/SDCA/SAGA in the finite-sum case and O(κ log(1/ε)) in some oracle models. The comparison section acknowledges EMGD is often not best computationally, but understates how unfavorable κ² is in ill-conditioned regimes.
- Missing and inaccurate related work: SVRG is not cited; several statements (e.g., linear convergence of SAG/SDCA only in expectation, unclear distributed behavior) are either out of date or too strong. High-probability variants and distributed/parallel extensions exist. The “semi-stochastic approximation” references are quite old and not the most relevant to current variance-reduction literature.
- Assumptions/practicality: The function-oracle model (returning a full random function f) is unusual outside finite-sum settings; the algorithm requires knowing κ and performing projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) with shrinking radii, which can be impractical. The constants in the bound are large (e.g., 1152 L²/λ² ln(1/δ)), and the chosen step size η = 1/(L√T) is conservative.
- Validation and clarity: No empirical results to demonstrate the claimed trade-offs or utility in constrained settings. Writing has multiple typos/format issues (“epoches,” “interstage”), and some steps (e.g., footnote about SDCA assumptions) interrupt flow.

Quality: The theory appears technically sound for the stated oracle model and produces a clear high-probability linear-rate bound. However, the κ² dependence is weak relative to the state of the art, and the missing comparison to SVRG is a substantial issue.

Clarity: Generally understandable but needs editing for grammar, notation consistency, and a cleaner algorithmic presentation. A dedicated related-work section that accurately situates EMGD among SVRG/SAGA/SDCA is essential.

Originality: Limited. The key idea matches SVRG; the shrinking-domain twist is not compelling enough given the resulting rates.

Significance: Moderate to low as currently written. The focus on “κ-independent number of full gradients” is valuable, but the same property exists in prior work with better total complexity and broader adoption.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and practical question.
- Simple algorithm with high-probability linear convergence.
- Works with constraints and low memory.

Cons
- Overlaps heavily with SVRG; missing citation.
- Weaker κ dependence (κ²) and conservative constants.
- Requires κ and shrinking-ball projections.
- No experiments; some comparison claims are inaccurate.

Suggestions for improvement
- Cite and carefully compare with SVRG (NIPS 2013) and Prox-SVRG; clarify what is new beyond those methods. If the contribution is high-probability bounds under constraints, emphasize that and tighten κ dependence.
- Attempt to improve the inner-epoch analysis to achieve O(κ) rather than O(κ²) iterations, potentially eliminating the shrinking-ball device and using standard variance-reduction lemmas.
- Provide experiments on finite-sum problems and constrained settings to validate the computational trade-offs and the benefit of using fewer full gradients.
- Correct and update the related work and comparison claims; temper statements about SAG/SDCA expectations and distributed variants.
- Improve presentation and fix typos; clarify the oracle model and practical parameter selection (remove need to know κ if possible).

Recommendation: Reject (originality concerns and suboptimal complexity vs. well-known variance-reduction methods). Confidence: High.
