This paper addresses the important problem of reducing the computational cost of first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex functions, particularly in ill-conditioned settings (large κ). The main contribution is a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which aims to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations to be independent of the condition number κ, achieving a linear convergence rate.

The core idea of EMGD is to blend a small number of full gradient evaluations with a large number of cheaper stochastic gradient evaluations. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch `k`, a single full gradient `∇F(w̄k)` is computed at the current solution `w̄k`. This full gradient then serves as an anchor for `T` inner iterations. Within the epoch, updates are performed using a "mixed gradient" `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)`. This can be interpreted as a variance-reduced stochastic gradient estimator, where the full gradient provides a stable baseline and the stochastic term `∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` provides a correction based on the progress within the epoch. The optimization is restricted to a shrinking ball around `w̄k`, and the solutions are averaged at the end of each epoch. The main theoretical result is that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution with `O(log(1/ε))` full gradient calls and `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))` stochastic gradient calls.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Problem and Novel Goal:** The paper targets a highly relevant problem. The cost of full gradient evaluations is a major bottleneck in large-scale machine learning. The goal of making the number of full gradient calls independent of the condition number κ is ambitious and, if achieved efficiently, would be a significant contribution.

2.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the algorithm is presented clearly. The theoretical analysis, while condensed, follows a logical structure based on an inductive argument over epochs, which is easy to follow.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** The paper provides a high-probability linear convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation results common for many stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA. The achieved full-gradient complexity of `O(log(1/ε))` is indeed independent of κ and improves upon the `O(√κ log(1/ε))` complexity of accelerated gradient descent.

4.  **Broader Applicability:** The authors correctly point out several potential advantages over finite-sum methods like SAG and SDCA. EMGD is applicable to constrained optimization problems (provided the projection is efficient) and has lower memory requirements (`O(d)` vs. `O(nd)` for SAG), making it potentially suitable for different problem classes.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Crucial Omission of Related Work:** The paper's most significant weakness is the failure to connect the "mixed gradient" to the well-established literature on variance reduction techniques. The gradient estimator `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the estimator used in SVRG (Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient, Johnson & Zhang, 2013). The entire family of algorithms including SVRG, SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014), and MISO (Mairal, 2015) is built on this principle and achieves linear convergence for finite-sum problems. By not citing or comparing to this seminal work, the paper incorrectly frames its core technical idea as novel. The novelty of EMGD is not the gradient estimator itself, but rather its use within a specific epoch-based framework with a shrinking domain. The paper must be revised to properly position itself within this context.

2.  **Unfavorable Complexity in the Finite-Sum Setting:** The comparison in Table 2 is incomplete and potentially misleading. The total complexity of EMGD in the finite-sum setting (`F(w) = (1/n) Σ f_i(w)`) is `O((n + κ^2) log(1/ε))` stochastic gradient-equivalent operations. In contrast, the complexity of SVRG and SDCA is `O((n + κ) log(1/ε))`. This means EMGD is only competitive with SVRG if `κ^2` is on the order of `κ`, which is only true for well-conditioned problems (`κ ≈ 1`). For the ill-conditioned problems the paper claims to target, the `κ^2` dependency makes EMGD significantly less efficient than existing variance-reduced methods. The paper's own analysis suggests it is better than Nesterov's method only when `κ ≤ n^(2/3)`, but SVRG and SDCA are superior in a much broader and more practical regime. This severely undermines the practical significance of the proposed algorithm for the finite-sum case.

3.  **Dependence on Unknown Parameters:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ (or `L` and `λ`) to set the number of inner iterations `T`. This is a common but major practical limitation. The discussion in the conclusion about estimating κ is brief and speculative. While this issue plagues many theoretical algorithms, it is a significant barrier to practical use that should be discussed more upfront.

4.  **Analysis Concerns:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the true optimum `w*` remains within the shrinking ball `B(w̄k; Δk)` at each epoch. This is justified by the inductive proof (i.e., if `‖w̄k - w*‖^2 ≤ Δk^2`, then the next iterate `w̄k+1` will satisfy `‖w̄k+1 - w*‖^2 ≤ Δk^2 / 2 = Δk+1^2`). While likely correct, this crucial assumption for the update step in Eq. (12) should be stated and justified more explicitly in the proof sketch.

### Summary and Recommendation

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important problem with a clear and ambitious goal.
*   Presents a well-defined algorithm with a strong, high-probability linear convergence guarantee.
*   Highlights potential advantages (constrained optimization, low memory) over some existing methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Critically fails to cite or compare against the SVRG family of algorithms, which use the exact same variance reduction technique. This misrepresents the novelty of the core method.
*   The resulting stochastic gradient complexity of `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))` is significantly worse than the `O(κ log(1/ε))` achieved by SVRG and related methods, making the algorithm less practical for the key finite-sum setting it is compared against.
*   Relies on prior knowledge of the condition number κ, a major practical limitation.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the goal of the paper is commendable, the failure to acknowledge the deep and direct connection to SVRG is a fatal flaw in its current form. Furthermore, the resulting algorithm's complexity appears inferior to these established methods in the most common settings. For the paper to be reconsidered, it would require a major revision that: 1) thoroughly rewrites the related work to properly situate EMGD relative to SVRG, SAGA, etc.; and 2) provides a compelling argument for a setting where the `O(n+κ^2)` complexity is genuinely advantageous over the `O(n+κ)` complexity of its direct competitors. Without this, the contribution is not significant enough for NIPS.
