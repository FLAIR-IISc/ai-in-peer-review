This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations in strongly convex and smooth optimization by leveraging stochastic gradients. The core idea of interleaving full and stochastic gradients within epochs, and achieving a linear convergence rate independent of the condition number, is intriguing. The authors position their work within the context of existing first-order methods, providing a table summarizing iteration complexities.

However, despite the novelty of the approach, a multitude of minor—yet cumulatively substantial—flaws significantly detract from the paper’s overall quality. My assessment is that these flaws, while individually small, indicate a lack of meticulousness that is critical for a NIPS publication.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of EMGD, blending full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition number independence, is a worthwhile exploration.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of expensive full gradient evaluations and the trade-off with stochastic gradients.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors attempt a theoretical analysis, providing a convergence theorem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation & Clarity:** Several notational inconsistencies and ambiguities exist. For example, the function oracle *Of* returns a *function* f(·), but this function is then used as if it were a stochastic gradient itself. The gradient within the mixed gradient update (g̃kt) is confusingly constructed. The distinction between *fkt* (a function) and its gradient is often blurred.
*   **Algorithm Presentation:** Algorithm 1 is poorly presented. The indentation is inconsistent, and the explanations within comments are unnecessarily verbose. Line 8, "Update the solution by...", uses an ambiguous and unhelpful description. The role of the ℓ2 ball constraint in step 8 is not adequately justified.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The proof sketch provided in Section 4 is excessively brief and lacks crucial details. Several steps are simply asserted without proper justification. The claim that "AT" can be bounded as stated in (18) feels overly optimistic without a more detailed explanation of how the smoothness constant influences the iterative updates. The application of Lemma 2 feels somewhat superficial; the conditions for its use should be explicitly verified.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The complete absence of experimental results is a major drawback. While the authors acknowledge the need for further work, the lack of even basic simulations to demonstrate the algorithm’s performance severely limits the paper's impact. The claim of computational improvement needs to be substantiated.
*   **Comparison Table (Table 2):** The comparisons to SAG and SDCA are not entirely fair. The assumption of 'n times more expensive' for full gradients is arbitrary and not justified. The discussion lacks nuance regarding the constraints under which each algorithm performs best. The statement that EMGD "may not be the best one, but has advantages in other aspects" is weak and non-committal.
*   **Minor Errors & Typos:** Numerous minor errors and typos exist throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts/superscripts, grammatical errors). These suggest a lack of careful proofreading. A particular example is the inconsistent use of parentheses around subscripts.

**Specific Points Requiring Clarification/Correction:**

*   **Line 6:** What is meant by a “random function fkt(·)”? Is this a sampled function from a distribution? This needs to be clarified.
*   **Line 8:** The minimization problem is not standard and requires explanation. Why this specific formulation?
*   **Equation (2):** The smoothness condition should apply to *fkt*, not *f*.
*   **Section 3.4:** The claim about EMGD working for both constrained and unconstrained problems needs to be supported.
*   **Section 4.2:** The derivation of inequality (16) is not fully clear.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The abundance of minor flaws collectively indicate a lack of thoroughness.
*   The absence of experimental validation is a serious omission.
*   The theoretical analysis is incomplete and lacks sufficient detail.
*   The clarity of the presentation is hampered by notational issues and poor organization.

**For:**

*   The core idea of EMGD is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The paper addresses an important problem in optimization.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the core idea, the paper suffers from too many weaknesses. The lack of experimental validation, combined with the numerous minor flaws in the presentation and analysis, make it unsuitable for NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the points raised in this review, provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis, and include comprehensive experimental results before resubmitting. A significant rewrite to improve clarity and consistency is also necessary.
