

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems that aims to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations needed, which is computationally expensive for large datasets. The authors observe that standard first-order methods with full gradients have an iteration complexity of O(√κ log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. Their key contribution is to achieve linear convergence (O(log(1/ε))) by mixing full and stochastic gradients, requiring only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the algorithm and compares it with existing methods such as Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA. The authors show that for optimization problems that are sums of n smooth functions (F(w) = (1/n)∑ᵢ=1ⁿ fᵢ(w)), EMGD has lower computational complexity than full gradient methods when κ ≤ n^(2/3).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors provide a detailed convergence proof using standard techniques from convex optimization (strongly convex functions properties, martingale difference sequences, and Hoeffding-Azuma inequality). The induction proof of Theorem 1 is thorough and carefully constructed, with all intermediate steps properly justified.

The paper acknowledges limitations of their approach, particularly the need to know the condition number κ in advance, and discusses potential future work to address this. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, highlighting both advantages and disadvantages of EMGD.

However, there are some quality concerns:
- The paper lacks any experimental results to validate the theoretical claims. Without empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed method.
- The requirement to know κ in advance is a major practical limitation that isn't adequately addressed in the paper. The authors briefly mention that they'll explore estimation methods in future work, but this remains a significant barrier to practical application.
- The number of stochastic gradients required (O(κ² log(1/ε))) seems high, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that this bound is tight or that it's practical for real-world problems.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The related work section provides a comprehensive review of relevant literature, situating the work within the broader context of convex optimization. The algorithm is described in pseudocode (Algorithm 1), and the convergence analysis is presented with sufficient detail for an expert reader to follow.

The writing is clear, concise, and accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization. The paper provides adequate mathematical notation and definitions, making it easy to follow the technical arguments. I would only suggest that the connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation could be made clearer, particularly regarding how to estimate κ.

## Originality

The paper presents a novel approach to mixing full and stochastic gradients for convex optimization. The key insight that the number of full gradients can be made condition number independent (O(log(1/ε)) instead of O(√κ log(1/ε))) is original and represents a meaningful contribution to the literature.

The authors provide a thorough comparison with existing methods, clearly identifying how EMGD differs from Nesterov's accelerated gradient, SAG, and SDCA. The paper's distinction between high-probability convergence (for EMGD) versus convergence in expectation (for SAG and SDCA) is particularly valuable.

However, the paper's novelty is somewhat limited by its similarity to existing "semi-stochastic" methods (cited as [12] and [13] in the paper). While the specific epoch-based approach with fixed stochastic gradient evaluations per epoch is novel, the overall concept of mixing full and stochastic gradients isn't entirely new.

## Significance

The problem addressed is significant in the context of large-scale machine learning, where computing full gradients is computationally expensive. The potential for reducing the number of full gradient evaluations could lead to substantial computational savings in practice.

The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by establishing a new method for achieving linear convergence with condition number independent access to full gradients. The advantages of EMGD (constrained optimization, lower storage requirements, high-probability convergence) are compelling in specific scenarios.

However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by:
1. The requirement to know κ in advance, which is often not feasible in practice
2. The high number of stochastic gradients required (O(κ² log(1/ε))), which might make the approach less practical than other methods for large condition numbers
3. The lack of empirical validation to support the theoretical claims

## Final Recommendation

I recommend a **weak acceptance** for this paper. The theoretical contribution is solid and represents a meaningful advancement in understanding how to mix full and stochastic gradients for convex optimization. The paper is well-written and technically sound, but it would be significantly strengthened by addressing the practical limitations noted above.

### Strengths:
- Clear and thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm
- Comprehensive comparison with existing methods
- Identification of important advantages over existing approaches (constrained optimization, lower storage requirements, high-probability convergence)
- Well-organized and clearly written

### Weaknesses:
- Requires knowledge of condition number κ, a major practical limitation
- Lacks experimental validation to support theoretical claims
- The number of stochastic gradients required (O(κ² log(1/ε))) might be too high for practical use
- The novelty is somewhat incremental compared to existing "semi-stochastic" methods

### Suggestions for Improvement:
1. Provide more detailed discussion on how to estimate κ in practice (e.g., using sampling as briefly mentioned in Section 5)
2. Include a small set of experiments to validate the theoretical claims, even on synthetic problems
3. More clearly distinguish the proposed approach from existing "semi-stochastic" methods
4. Consider whether the O(κ²) bound on stochastic gradients can be improved

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that would be a good fit for NIPS, but it would benefit from addressing the practical limitations before publication. The theoretical insights are valuable, and with minor improvements, this paper could be a significant contribution to the field.
