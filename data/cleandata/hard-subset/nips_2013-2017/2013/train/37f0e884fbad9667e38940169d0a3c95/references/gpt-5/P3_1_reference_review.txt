Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization with access to both (i) a full gradient oracle Og and (ii) a stochastic “function” oracle Of that returns random smooth functions f with F(w) = Ef[f(w)]. The main contribution is Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, EMGD computes one full gradient at a snapshot point w̄k; within the epoch it uses a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k), and performs projected gradient steps constrained to a shrinking ball around w̄k. With T = Ω(κ2 log(1/δ)) inner iterations and step size η = 1/(L√T), the analysis shows a constant-factor contraction in suboptimality per epoch with high probability, yielding linear convergence overall: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.

The mixed gradient estimator is identical to the control variate used by semi-stochastic variance-reduced methods such as SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which perform periodic full gradients at a snapshot and then use ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) for inner iterations. The paper compares to SAG and SDCA and discusses storage/constraints differences, but does not cite or compare to SVRG/SAGA or more recent proximal VR methods. The analysis differs in controlling variance via a shrinking projection ball and high-probability martingale bounds, at the price of κ2 inner-loop complexity.

Quality
- The core analysis is sound: the proof leverages smoothness, strong convexity, a projection to keep iterates close to the snapshot (which bounds the variance of the mixed gradient), and an Azuma–Hoeffding bound to obtain high-probability guarantees. The epoch-wise contraction is clearly derived.
- However, the iteration complexity in stochastic gradients, O(κ2 log(1/ε)), is substantially weaker than known variance-reduced methods (e.g., SVRG, SAGA, SDCA), which achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) in the finite-sum case or O(κ log(1/ε)) dependence on the condition number without projections. The analysis appears conservative; tighter variance bounds (e.g., bounding E‖∇f(w)−∇f(w̄) − (∇F(w)−∇F(w̄))‖2 by function suboptimality) typically remove the need for shrinking domains and reduce κ2 to κ.
- The algorithm requires knowing κ to set T and the shrinking schedule, and repeatedly projects onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk), which can be nontrivial unless projections are easy.
- There are no experiments to validate practical performance, the cost trade-offs, or the claimed advantages on constrained problems.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and the proof is detailed. The high-level idea is well explained.
- There are several issues/typos that hinder clarity: in Algorithm 1, the update objective in Step 8 appears malformed (“1 / ‖w − wkt ‖2” instead of (1/2)‖w − wkt ‖2). A footnote about SDCA appears in an odd place. The strong convexity condition is stated as ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖; while equivalent to λ-strong convexity under differentiability, it would be clearer to use the standard inequality on function values/gradients and state equivalence conditions.
- The oracle model deserves more discussion: when does one have both a full gradient oracle and a function oracle in non-finite-sum settings? In many stochastic settings a full gradient is not accessible.

Originality
- The mixed-gradient estimator and the epoch structure with a snapshot full gradient are not new; they coincide with SVRG’s core idea. The paper does not cite or compare to SVRG (NIPS 2013) or SAGA (NIPS 2014). The projection onto shrinking balls and the specific high-probability proof technique are incremental twists, but they lead to a worse κ dependence than prior work. Claims such as “condition-number independent access of full gradients” are also achieved by SVRG (one full gradient per epoch, O(log(1/ε)) epochs).
- The discussion contrasting storage and constrained optimization support is partially accurate (SAG/SDCA need O(n) memory; EMGD uses O(d)), but SVRG also uses O(d) memory and extends to proximal/constrained settings without the shrinking-ball projection.

Significance
- Reducing the number of full gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)) is valuable, but this has already been achieved with stronger overall complexity bounds and without projections by SVRG/SDCA/SAGA in the finite-sum setting. EMGD’s κ2 dependence and need for projections make it less competitive theoretically and likely practically.
- If the contribution were a general oracle result beyond finite-sum (Ef model) with only Og and Of access, that would be interesting; however, the reliance on Og each epoch limits applicability, and the rates are not state-of-the-art.

Pros
- Clear, self-contained high-probability analysis.
- Simple algorithmic template; O(d) memory.
- Emphasis on constrained settings is welcome.

Cons
- Overlaps substantially with prior variance-reduction methods; missing key citations (SVRG/SAGA) and comparisons.
- Weaker stochastic gradient complexity O(κ2 log(1/ε)) compared to O(κ log(1/ε)) or O(n + κ).
- Requires knowledge of κ, shrinking ball projections, and full gradient oracle; no experiments.
- Some clarity/typo issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add and discuss related variance-reduction literature (SVRG, proximal SVRG, SAGA, SDCA), and provide a thorough theoretical and empirical comparison. In particular, clarify how EMGD differs from SVRG beyond the projection trick, and why the κ2 factor is unavoidable (or tighten the analysis to κ with standard variance bounds tied to suboptimality).
- Remove the shrinking-ball constraint by bounding the variance in terms of F(w)−F(w*) and F(w̄)−F(w*); this typically yields O(κ) inner-loop length and eliminates the need to know κ precisely (epoch-doubling can be used).
- Clarify the oracle model’s applicability and discuss finite-sum vs general expectation settings. For finite-sum, quantify total component-gradient evaluations and compare fairly to SAG/SAGA/SVRG/SDCA.
- Fix notation/typos (especially the proximal update), and streamline the SDCA footnote and strong convexity statements.
- Include experiments on standard datasets (logistic/squared loss) with constraints and measure wall-clock vs gradient counts.

Recommendation
Given the significant overlap with established variance-reduced methods, inferior κ dependence, lack of empirical validation, and missing key related work, I do not recommend acceptance in its current form. The work could be valuable if the analysis is strengthened to O(κ log(1/ε)), the novelty over SVRG is clearly articulated, and thorough comparisons (theoretical and empirical) are added. My confidence is high.
