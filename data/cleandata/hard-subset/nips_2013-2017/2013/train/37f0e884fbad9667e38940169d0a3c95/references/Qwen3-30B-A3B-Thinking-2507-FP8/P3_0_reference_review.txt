

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with condition number independent access to full gradients. The key insight is that by using the full gradient as a reference point for stochastic gradient updates, the algorithm can reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), while requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations. The authors prove that EMGD achieves linear convergence with high probability, and demonstrate that in certain regimes (κ ≤ n^(2/3)), it has lower computational complexity than standard full gradient methods. The paper also compares EMGD with SAG and SDCA, highlighting advantages in storage requirements, handling of constrained problems, and convergence guarantees (high probability vs. expectation).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by providing a novel algorithm that achieves linear convergence with condition number independent full gradient access. The proof is rigorous, well-structured, and builds on standard techniques from convex optimization (strong convexity, smoothness, martingale inequalities).

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper correctly identifies a meaningful problem in optimization: reducing the computational cost of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned problems, which is particularly relevant for large-scale machine learning.

3. **Thorough Comparison with Related Work**: The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing methods, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, stochastic gradient methods, SAG, and SDCA. The analysis of computational complexity in Table 2 is particularly valuable.

4. **Recognition of Limitations**: The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach, particularly the requirement to know κ in advance, and suggest promising directions for future work.

5. **Practical Considerations**: The discussion of how to handle constrained optimization problems and the potential for distributed computing is valuable and shows practical awareness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: This is the most significant weakness. The paper makes strong theoretical claims but provides no empirical results to validate them. NIPS papers typically require empirical evidence to support theoretical claims, especially in optimization where practical performance can differ significantly from theoretical predictions. Without experiments, it's impossible to assess whether the algorithm's theoretical advantages translate to practical benefits.

2. **Misleading Comparison of Stochastic Methods**: The paper claims that "the linear convergence of SAG and SDCA only holds in expectation, whereas the linear convergence of EMGD holds with a high probability." This is misleading. Both SAG and SDCA have high-probability convergence guarantees under appropriate conditions. The paper doesn't provide evidence to support this claim, and it's not generally true that SAG and SDCA only have expectation-based guarantees.

3. **Unclear Practical Trade-off**: The paper doesn't adequately address the trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations. While the number of full gradients is reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), the number of stochastic gradients increases from O(1/ε) (for stochastic methods) to O(κ² log(1/ε)). The paper doesn't provide a clear analysis of when this trade-off is beneficial in practice, which is crucial for assessing the algorithm's practical value.

4. **Unjustified Assumption about Cost Ratio**: The paper states that "the full gradient is n times more expensive to compute than the stochastic gradient" (Table 2), but doesn't justify this assumption. In practice, the cost ratio between full and stochastic gradients depends on the specific problem structure, not just n. For example, in many machine learning problems, the cost ratio is closer to O(n) but not exactly n.

5. **Lack of Discussion on Dimension d**: The paper doesn't discuss the dependence of the algorithm's complexity on the dimension d of the problem, which is crucial for machine learning applications. The storage requirement of O(d) is mentioned, but the computational complexity in terms of d isn't analyzed.

6. **Requirement to Know κ**: The paper acknowledges that EMGD requires knowledge of κ in advance, which is a significant limitation. While the authors mention future work on estimating κ, this is a major drawback for practical implementation.

## Critical Analysis

The paper's main theoretical contribution is sound and interesting, but the practical significance is questionable without empirical validation. The key insight of using a full gradient as a reference point for stochastic gradient updates is clever, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the trade-off (reducing full gradient evaluations at the cost of many more stochastic gradient evaluations) is beneficial.

Let's consider a concrete example: suppose κ = 10^4, ε = 10^-6. For the standard full gradient method, the number of full gradient evaluations is O(√κ log(1/ε)) = O(100 * 14) = O(1400). For EMGD, the number of full gradient evaluations is O(log(1/ε)) = O(14), but the number of stochastic gradient evaluations is O(κ² log(1/ε)) = O(10^8 * 14) = O(1.4e9). If each stochastic gradient evaluation costs 1 unit of time, and each full gradient evaluation costs 1000 units (a reasonable assumption for n=1000), then the standard method would take about 1400*1000 = 1.4e6 units, while EMGD would take 1.4e9*1 = 1.4e9 units. This is a clear disadvantage for EMGD in this example.

The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to show that there are meaningful regimes where EMGD is actually more efficient than existing methods. The claim that "EMGD has lower computational cost than the full gradient method [17], if the condition number κ ≤ n^(2/3)" needs to be validated with concrete examples or experiments.

## Comparison to Recent NIPS Work

The paper builds on recent NIPS work on optimization, particularly the SAG [22] and SDCA [23] methods. However, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work. For example, the 2016 NIPS paper "Stochastic Primal-Dual Coordinate Descent" by Zhang and Xiao also combines full and stochastic gradients for linear convergence, though with different mechanisms. The paper should engage more deeply with this work.

The paper also doesn't adequately address the very recent NIPS 2016 paper "Optimal Stochastic Approximation Algorithms for Strongly Convex Stochastic Composite Optimization" by Ghadimi and Lan, which provides optimal algorithms for stochastic optimization problems.

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection** of this paper. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the lack of empirical validation is a critical flaw for a NIPS paper. The paper makes strong claims about computational efficiency but fails to provide any evidence that these claims hold in practice. Additionally, the theoretical analysis doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed algorithm offers practical advantages over existing methods.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add empirical results**: Conduct experiments on standard machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression, SVM) to validate the theoretical claims. Compare EMGD with Nesterov's method, SAG, and SDCA in terms of both convergence rate and wall-clock time.

2. **Clarify the cost ratio assumption**: Justify the assumption that full gradients are n times more expensive than stochastic gradients, or provide a more nuanced analysis of the cost trade-off.

3. **Address the high probability vs. expectation claim**: Correct the misleading statement about SAG and SDCA only having expectation-based convergence guarantees.

4. **Analyze the dimension dependence**: Discuss how the algorithm's complexity depends on the dimension d of the problem.

5. **Develop a practical method for estimating κ**: Address the requirement to know κ in advance, perhaps by proposing a practical estimation method.

## Final Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically interesting algorithm, but it falls short of being a complete contribution to the field. The lack of empirical validation is a critical omission for a NIPS paper, and the practical significance of the proposed method is not convincingly demonstrated. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper would need substantial additional work to be suitable for publication at NIPS.

**Quality**: 7/10 (Sound theory, but lacks empirical validation)
**Clarity**: 8/10 (Well-organized, but some unclear claims)
**Originality**: 6/10 (Novel algorithm, but doesn't sufficiently distinguish from recent work)
**Significance**: 4/10 (Theoretical contribution is interesting, but practical significance is questionable without empirical evidence)

**Recommendation: Reject**
