Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies first-order optimization for smooth, strongly convex objectives F over a convex domain, with access to both a full-gradient oracle Og(w) = ∇F(w) and a “function oracle” Of that returns a random smooth function f with F(w) = E[f(w)]. The goal is to reduce the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations required to reach ε-accuracy. The proposed algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), proceeds in epochs: at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at a reference point w̄k, and within the epoch performs T iterations using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), together with projected gradient steps on a shrinking ball around w̄k. The analysis shows a linear convergence rate with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, where κ = L/λ is the condition number. The guarantees hold with high probability.

The mixed gradient estimator is a variance-reduced estimator of the form used in semi-stochastic/variance-reduction methods; in the finite-sum setting F(w) = (1/n)∑ fi(w), it coincides with the SVRG estimator, and is closely related to SAG/SAGA/SDCA families which achieve linear rates. The paper positions itself relative to full-gradient methods (e.g., Nesterov’s accelerated methods) and to SAG/SDCA, emphasizing the independence of the number of full gradients from κ and the ability to handle constraints, but it does not discuss SVRG/SAGA and related variance-reduction literature explicitly.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The high-level proof strategy (variance reduction via control variates around a snapshot point, decreasing domain to control stochastic variation, Azuma/Hoeffding martingale bounds) is standard and appears correct at a high level. The mixed gradient is unbiased for ∇F(wt), and the shrinking ball yields a bound on the stochastic term via smoothness. However, the resulting epoch length T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) is conservative and suboptimal compared to known O(κ) epoch lengths in variance-reduction methods.
- Assumptions and model: The function-oracle model Of returning a random f for which one can compute gradients at both wt and w̄k is stronger than the common stochastic-gradient oracle that returns ∇f(wt) at a single point. It is appropriate for finite-sum problems (where f is an index i), but outside that setting the assumption can be restrictive; this should be discussed more explicitly.
- Completeness: The paper is purely theoretical; there are no experiments to validate practical performance, constants, or the overhead of projections onto shrinking balls W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk). Given the algorithm’s closeness to existing VR methods and its worse κ-dependence, empirical evidence is important to motivate its practical merits.
- Carefulness: There are several presentation/notation issues (e.g., some duplicated equation labels, and minor typos). The comparison to SDCA/SAG includes claims on high-probability vs expectation that should be nuanced, as modern analyses also provide high-probability guarantees in many cases.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and the main idea is conveyed. The algorithm is clearly stated. However:
- The relation to SVRG/SAGA and the broader variance-reduction literature is missing; this makes it difficult to assess novelty without that context.
- The computational model assumptions for Of should be clarified in both finite-sum and general expectation settings.
- The cost of projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) and the need to know κ to set T and η should be highlighted earlier as practical constraints.

Originality:
The mixed gradient estimator and epoch structure are essentially those of SVRG-like methods. The distinctive elements here are (i) the focus on counting full-gradient calls independent of κ and (ii) the use of shrinking-domain projections to control variance and obtain high-probability bounds. However, in the finite-sum setting, existing methods (SVRG/SAGA/SDCA) already achieve linear convergence with better κ dependence (O(n + κ) log(1/ε)) and comparable or stronger theory, with simpler updates (no shrinking balls) and often without needing κ. Without a tighter analysis or a new algorithmic ingredient improving the κ^2 factor or enabling a genuinely broader oracle model, the originality is limited.

Significance:
The idea of reducing full-gradient evaluations is sensible, but the total complexity is dominated by the O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls, which is not competitive with state-of-the-art methods in common finite-sum regimes. The claimed advantages (works with constraints, low memory, high-probability bounds) are useful but not compelling absent empirical evaluation and a stronger theoretical complexity. As written, the likely impact is limited.

Pros:
- Clear goal: minimize expensive full-gradient calls; linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients.
- Simple algorithm; low memory footprint; supports constraints via projections.
- High-probability convergence analysis.

Cons:
- Close overlap with existing variance-reduction methods; missing discussion/citations (e.g., SVRG and successors).
- Suboptimal stochastic complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) vs O(κ log(1/ε)) known results; requires knowledge of κ.
- No experiments; unclear practical benefit vs SVRG/SAGA/SDCA.
- Function-oracle assumption is strong outside finite-sum; projection onto shrinking balls may be costly.
- Presentation issues and some overstatements in comparisons.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Please position EMGD relative to SVRG/SAGA and related VR methods, and explain what is genuinely new. Can your analysis be tightened to O(κ) per epoch without shrinking balls?
- Clarify the oracle model: can EMGD operate with a standard stochastic-gradient oracle that does not expose f(·) for evaluation at multiple points?
- Provide empirical comparisons on standard L2-regularized logistic regression/SVM tasks, including constrained variants if relevant, to quantify the practical trade-offs.
- Discuss how to set T, η, and Δk without knowing κ, or provide adaptive strategies.
- Clarify the computational cost of projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) and when this is efficient.

Recommendation:
I lean towards rejection in its current form due to limited novelty over variance-reduction methods, suboptimal κ dependence, and lack of empirical validation. With a tighter complexity bound, proper positioning relative to SVRG/SAGA/SDCA, and experiments, the work could be strengthened.
