

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations needed for smooth strongly convex optimization from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)). The key idea is to use a sequence of epochs where each epoch evaluates one full gradient and O(κ²) stochastic gradients, achieving a constant error reduction per epoch. The theoretical analysis claims linear convergence with respect to the number of epochs, making the algorithm more efficient than standard full-gradient methods when κ is large.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the optimization literature by exploring the trade-off between full and stochastic gradients. The algorithm design is novel in its approach to combining both gradient types, and the analysis provides a clear convergence guarantee. The comparison with SAG and SDCA is well-motivated, highlighting important advantages such as applicability to constrained problems and reduced storage requirements.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Flaws
1. **Incorrect asymptotic complexity**: The paper claims the number of stochastic gradients is O(κ² log(1/ε)), but the actual complexity is O(κ² log(1/ε) log(1/δ)). The proof (equation 20) shows that the error bound depends on √(T log(1/δ)), and T = Ω(κ² log(1/δ)). To achieve high probability of success (1-δ), δ must be set to O(ε/m), making the total stochastic gradient count O(κ² log(1/ε) log(1/ε)), not O(κ² log(1/ε)) as claimed. This is a fundamental error in the main result.

2. **Weak probability guarantee**: The condition δ ≤ e^(-1/2) (approximately 0.606) in Theorem 1 is extremely weak. Typically, we want δ to be small (e.g., 0.01 or 0.05) for practical applications. With this condition, the probability of success 1-mδ could be less than 0.4 when m=2, which is unacceptable for most applications.

3. **Incorrect notation and formatting**: The paper uses inconsistent and confusing notation throughout:
   - "O( √ κ log 1ǫ )" should be "O(√κ log(1/ε))"
   - "log 1ǫ" should be "log(1/ε)" or "log(1/ε)"
   - "κ 2" should be "κ²"
   - "2m+1" in the error bound should be "2^(m+1)"
   - Inconsistent use of "ǫ" vs "ε"

4. **Missing practical details**: The paper fails to explain how to estimate the condition number κ, which is required by the algorithm. While the authors mention future work on this, the algorithm as presented is unusable without knowing κ in advance.

### Theoretical and Presentation Issues
5. **Mischaracterization of convergence**: The paper claims "linear convergence," but the error decreases by a constant factor per epoch, not per iteration. In optimization literature, "linear convergence" typically means the error decreases exponentially with the number of iterations, not epochs.

6. **Missing table reference**: The introduction states "Table 1" summarizes optimal iteration complexities, but no such table appears in the paper.

7. **Inconsistent definition of condition number**: The condition number κ = L/λ is defined in section 3.1, but the paper should define it earlier in the introduction for clarity.

8. **Overstated computational comparison**: The paper claims EMGD has lower computational cost than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but doesn't properly account for the fact that the full gradient is n times more expensive to compute than a stochastic gradient. The computational cost analysis is incomplete and potentially misleading.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical approach to combining full and stochastic gradients, the technical flaws are substantial. The incorrect asymptotic complexity claim (O(κ² log(1/ε)) vs actual O(κ² log²(1/ε))) is particularly concerning, as it fundamentally misrepresents the algorithm's performance. The weak probability guarantee (δ ≤ 0.606) makes the theoretical result practically unusable, and the numerous notation inconsistencies undermine the paper's credibility.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Correcting the asymptotic complexity claim
2. Providing a proper high-probability guarantee with δ small
3. Fixing all notation inconsistencies
4. Adding a practical method for estimating κ

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper has a novel algorithmic idea, the technical errors and inconsistencies in the main result are too substantial to overlook. The incorrect complexity claim fundamentally undermines the paper's contribution, and the weak probability guarantee makes the theoretical result practically useless. These issues are not merely minor formatting errors but represent fundamental misunderstandings of the algorithm's complexity and probability guarantees.

The paper would be suitable for acceptance if these major technical issues were addressed and the asymptotic complexity corrected. As it stands, the paper does not meet NeurIPS's standards for technical rigor.
