## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the computational cost associated with full gradient evaluations in strongly convex and smooth optimization problems. The core idea is to interleave full gradient evaluations with stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, achieving a linear convergence rate while requiring only O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient evaluations – independent of the condition number κ.  The authors position their work within the landscape of first-order methods, contrasting it with full-gradient, stochastic-gradient, and semi-stochastic approaches. They provide theoretical analysis guaranteeing the convergence rate and discuss potential applications and improvements.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant problem:** The computational cost of full gradient evaluations is a significant bottleneck in large-scale optimization, especially with ill-conditioned problems (large κ).  Reducing this cost without sacrificing convergence speed is a valuable contribution.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of epoch-based descent with a carefully designed mixing of full and stochastic gradients is a reasonable and interesting approach.  The theoretical analysis suggesting independence from κ is particularly noteworthy.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper includes a formal convergence analysis, providing a guarantee of linear convergence under certain conditions. The use of lemmas and a clear inductive proof structure strengthens the theoretical arguments.
* **Comparison to existing methods:** The paper places EMGD in context by comparing its computational complexity to existing methods like Nesterov’s acceleration, SAG, and SDCA, highlighting potential advantages.
* **Discussion of practical considerations:** The authors acknowledge the need for estimating the condition number in practice and suggest potential avenues for doing so, showing an awareness of real-world implementation challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The algorithm relies on the availability of *both* full gradients and stochastic gradients, which may not always be realistic.  The benefits of EMGD diminish if obtaining stochastic gradients is as costly as obtaining full gradients. This assumption is acknowledged, but its implications for applicability aren't fully explored.
* **Constant Factors:** While the algorithm achieves O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient complexity, the constant factors hidden within the big-O notation aren't explicitly analyzed. The T ≥ 1152L²/λ² ln(δ) condition in Theorem 1 suggests that the number of iterations *per epoch* (T) could be quite large, potentially offsetting the reduction in full gradient evaluations.
* **Clarity of the Mixed Gradient:** The motivation behind the specific form of the mixed gradient (g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) +∇fkt(wkt )−∇fkt(w̄k)) isn’t fully clear. A more intuitive explanation of why this particular combination is effective would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical evaluation.  While theoretical guarantees are valuable, demonstrating the practical performance of EMGD on real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the work. Even a simple comparison against standard SGD or a semi-stochastic method would provide compelling evidence.
* **Repetitive discussion of related work:** The related work section feels somewhat repetitive and could be streamlined.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The inductive proof in the appendix, while lengthy, appears correct. However, the lack of empirical validation weakens the overall assessment. **Score: 7/10**
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the explanation of the mixed gradient could be improved. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is clearly presented. **Score: 8/10**
* **Originality:** The combination of epoch-based descent with a mixed gradient approach appears novel. The claim of condition-number-independent full gradient complexity is a significant contribution. **Score: 8/10**
* **Significance:** If the theoretical guarantees translate to practical performance, this algorithm could be a valuable tool for large-scale optimization problems. However, the significance is currently limited by the lack of empirical validation. **Score: 6/10**



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for reducing the cost of full gradient evaluations.
* Addresses a practically important problem in large-scale optimization.
* Provides a rigorous analysis guaranteeing a linear convergence rate independent of the condition number.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks empirical evaluation to validate the theoretical results.
* Relies on strong assumptions regarding the availability of both full and stochastic gradients.
* Potentially large constant factors hidden in the asymptotic analysis could limit practical performance.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with a request for a thorough empirical evaluation. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating its practical performance on real-world datasets.  The authors should also address the concerns regarding the constant factors in the analysis and provide a more intuitive explanation of the mixed gradient. If the authors can address these concerns, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
