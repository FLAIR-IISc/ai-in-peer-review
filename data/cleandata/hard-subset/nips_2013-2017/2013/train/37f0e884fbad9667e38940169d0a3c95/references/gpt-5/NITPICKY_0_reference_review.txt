Summary
The paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a first-order method for smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization that mixes occasional full gradients with frequent stochastic gradients. Each epoch computes one full gradient ∇F(v) at the epoch anchor v and then runs T proximal steps using a “mixed” gradient estimator ∇f(w) − ∇f(v) + ∇F(v); the domain is restricted to a shrinking Euclidean ball around v to control variance. The main theoretical claim is linear convergence: O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) calls to the stochastic oracle, with high-probability guarantees. The authors argue this decouples the number of full gradients from κ (condition number), and discuss comparisons to Nesterov’s accelerated method, SAG, and SDCA.

Relation to prior work
The “mixed gradient” estimator is algebraically identical to the variance-reduced estimator used by SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013) and closely related to SAGA/SAG. SDCA is also a variance-reduction method with linear convergence. The paper cites SAG and SDCA, but omits SVRG/SAGA and other variance-reduced methods and does not acknowledge that the one-full-gradient-per-epoch paradigm (and the resulting O(log(1/ε)) full gradients) is already standard in this literature. Moreover, the analysis here yields a κ^2 dependence in stochastic gradients, whereas SVRG/SAGA/SDCA achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) or similar. The ball-projection device to control variance is a nonstandard twist but seems unnecessary relative to known analyses that avoid projections while achieving better κ dependence.

Quality
- Correctness: The high-level approach is plausible and the main proof line follows a standard martingale difference argument. However, there are several technical inaccuracies that undermine confidence:
  - Strong convexity is misstated in (4) as a norm inequality ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖; the correct form is strong monotonicity ⟨∇F(w) − ∇F(w′), w − w′⟩ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖^2 (or the function-value inequality). The paper occasionally uses the incorrect (4) later.
  - Algorithm 1’s update omits the standard factor 1/(2η) in the proximal term; yet the analysis uses terms like ‖·‖^2/(2η). This is a material discrepancy.
  - In Theorem 1, T is required to satisfy T ≥ 1152 L^2/λ^2 ln(1/δ). Earlier it is written as ln δ (sign error). There is also an unexplained “max” in the condition on Δ1 and a missing 1/2 inside the square root suggested by strong convexity.
  - The Hoeffding–Azuma application specifies Vi ∈ [Ai, Ai + ci], but the proof only bounds |Vt| ≤ c and does not reconcile the interval condition (constants may be off).
  - Equation numbering is inconsistent (e.g., (2) reused), and some references to equations are incorrect.
- Assumptions/oracle model: The function oracle returns a random function f with L-smoothness, enabling access to both ∇f(wt) and ∇f(v). This is realistic in finite-sum settings but not for general stochastic oracles that only return unbiased gradients at a single point. The paper claims generality but the algorithm fundamentally requires the finite-sum (or equivalent) structure.
- Completeness: No experiments are provided to complement theory or to exhibit practical trade-offs against SVRG/SAG/SDCA (memory, constraints, distributed setting).

Clarity
The paper is difficult to follow due to numerous typos, inconsistent notation, and formatting issues (misspelled “epoaches,” broken footnotes, mid-proof digressions, missing citation details). The algorithm and proof would benefit from tighter exposition, correct definitions (strong convexity), consistent constants, and a clean separation of assumptions for finite-sum vs. expectation settings.

Originality
The estimator and epoch structure are essentially SVRG; the shrinking-ball projection is the main distinctive idea. However, this design choice degrades the stochastic gradient complexity to O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) compared to O(κ log(1/ε)) in standard variance-reduced methods, and adds a potentially costly projection step. Claims about “condition-number independent access of full gradients” are already realized by SVRG/SDCA (one full gradient per epoch/pass).

Significance
Given stronger existing results (tighter κ dependence, simpler updates, broader scope, and well-established), the contribution is limited. The constraints-support and low-memory claims are interesting, but again are addressed by proximal SVRG variants and low-memory variance-reduced methods. Without empirical evidence demonstrating advantages in constrained or distributed regimes, significance is modest.

Pros
- Simple algorithmic template; high-probability linear convergence proof.
- Emphasizes reducing full-gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)).
- Discusses constrained settings and memory footprint.

Cons
- Novelty limited; core idea overlaps with SVRG/SAGA; key related work omitted.
- Weaker stochastic gradient complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) vs. O(κ log(1/ε)) in prior art.
- Oracle model unrealistic beyond finite-sum; scope not clearly delineated.
- Multiple technical and typographical errors (definitions, constants, equation references).
- Requires knowledge of L, λ, and κ; no adaptive strategy.
- No experiments; comparisons rely on debatable conditions (e.g., SAG needing n ≥ 8κ).

Questions for the authors
1. How does EMGD compare theoretically to SVRG/SAGA (complexity, memory, constraints) when analyzed under the same oracle/model? Can you tighten your analysis to O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch?
2. Can the ball-projection be removed while maintaining high-probability linear convergence?
3. Please clarify the intended setting: finite-sum vs. general stochastic expectations. If the latter, how is ∇f(v) obtained?
4. Can step sizes and T be made adaptive without knowing κ?

Typos and minor issues (non-exhaustive)
- Title grammar (“Access of” → “Access to”).
- Algorithm 1 missing 1/(2η) in quadratic term; inconsistent T vs. T+1 averaging.
- Incorrect strong convexity statement (4).
- Theorem 1: ln δ should be ln(1/δ); Δ1 condition malformed; equation numbering duplicated.
- Many citation entries incomplete; numerous spelling mistakes (“epoaches,” “interstage,” etc.).

Recommendation
Reject. The paper’s main idea is not sufficiently novel relative to established variance-reduced methods, the analysis yields weaker κ dependence, and the manuscript contains multiple technical and presentation errors that require substantial revision. Empirical evidence and a tighter, clearer analysis benchmarked against SVRG/SAGA/SDCA would be necessary to reconsider. Confidence: high.
