Summary and contributions:
The paper considers smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization where both a full-gradient oracle Og and a stochastic function oracle Of (hence stochastic gradients) are available. It proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which runs in epochs; at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at the snapshot point w̄k, and then performs T projected proximal-gradient steps using a “mixed” control-variate estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k). The feasible region is intersected with a shrinking Euclidean ball around w̄k to control variance. With step size η = 1/(L√T) and T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)), each epoch reduces the suboptimality by a constant factor with high probability. Consequently, EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations to reach ε accuracy. The paper positions EMGD relative to full-gradient accelerated methods (Nesterov), stochastic methods with polynomial dependence on 1/ε, and finite-sum methods like SAG/SDCA.

Relation to prior work:
- The mixed gradient estimator is essentially the standard control-variate estimator used in variance-reduced methods (g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄)). In the finite-sum setting, SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013) and SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014) exploit this idea to obtain linear convergence with complexity O((n + κ) log(1/ε)), using one full gradient per epoch and O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch, without shrinking-ball projections and with low memory (SVRG). These works, and subsequent proximal/constraint variants, are not discussed.
- SAG/SDCA are cited; however, the discussion of their limitations (e.g., storage, constraints, distributed computing) is dated/incomplete given later variants (e.g., Prox-SVRG/SAGA, distributed VR methods) that mitigate these issues and provide high-probability guarantees.
- The paper references older “semi-stochastic” work, but does not position its oracle model or complexity against the now-standard variance-reduction literature, leaving the novelty unclear.

Strengths:
- Clear high-level goal: reduce the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)) independent of κ.
- Simple algorithmic structure; the estimator is unbiased and its variance is controlled by smoothness and the shrinking trust region.
- Theoretical guarantee with a high-probability linear convergence bound.
- Discussion of constrained settings and low memory footprint is useful in principle.

Weaknesses:
- Suboptimal stochastic-gradient complexity: O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) versus O(κ log(1/ε)) available in SVRG/SAGA/SDCA. The κ^2 dependence appears to be an artifact of the analysis choices (η = 1/(L√T), ball projections, and a crude bound ∑‖∇g_t‖^2 ≤ TL^2Δ^2). Existing analyses achieve O(κ) inner-loop lengths without shrinking balls.
- Missing and outdated related work: closely related variance-reduced methods (SVRG/SAGA and their proximal/constraint/distributed/high-probability variants) are not cited or compared, and they already achieve the headline property “O(log(1/ε)) full gradients” with better stochastic complexity. Claims that EMGD uniquely handles constraints or uses low memory are not accurate relative to Prox-SVRG and memoryless SVRG.
- Oracle model practicality: assuming an exact full-gradient oracle Og alongside a stochastic function oracle is unusual outside finite-sum problems; in general expectation settings, Og may be unavailable. In the finite-sum case, the method is dominated by established VR methods.
- Algorithmic practicality: requires knowing κ to set T and uses a shrinking-ball projection, which can complicate projections for nontrivial constraints. The requirement on Δ1 and the condition δ ≤ e−1/2 are awkward. Constants are large (e.g., T ≥ 1152(L/λ)^2 log(1/δ)).
- No empirical evaluation to substantiate claimed computational advantages.

Technical comments:
- The mixed gradient is unbiased for ∇F(wt). The variance control via shrinking balls is intuitive, but a more refined analysis (e.g., standard VR arguments bounding E‖∇f(wt)−∇f(w̄)−(∇F(wt)−∇F(w̄))‖^2) could likely improve κ dependence and avoid ball constraints.
- Some notational/typographical issues: repeated equation labels (2), “epoches,” and the proximal update in Algorithm 1 appears to miss the factor 1/(2η) on the quadratic term. Please check the constants in the update and the proof.
- The inequality ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖ is a consequence of strong convexity via strong monotonicity and Cauchy–Schwarz; it would help to state the standard strong convexity definition first.
- The comparison table and distributed-computing claims should be revisited in light of distributed SVRG/SAGA and more recent literature.

Significance and originality:
- The goal of decoupling the number of full-gradient evaluations from κ is valuable, but it has already been achieved with better stochastic complexity and broader scope by variance-reduction methods. As presented, EMGD does not advance the state-of-the-art in theory or practice, and the novelty over existing VR schemes is not established.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and the high-level idea is clear. However, the related work is incomplete, some assumptions and constants are unusual, and the algorithm/proof presentation would benefit from careful editing.

Recommendation: Reject.
Pros:
- Clear objective and simple algorithm.
- High-probability linear convergence with a transparent proof.
- Discussion of constrained settings and memory footprint.

Cons:
- Suboptimal κ^2 dependence; weaker than standard VR methods.
- Missing key related work (SVRG/SAGA and variants) and outdated comparisons.
- Questionable practicality of the oracle model; requires κ and ball projections.
- No experiments; several presentation issues.

Suggestions to improve:
- Tighten the analysis to achieve O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient complexity and remove the shrinking-ball constraint if possible.
- Properly position the work relative to SVRG/SAGA/SDCA and proximal/constraint/distributed variants; add empirical comparisons.
- Provide adaptive strategies that do not require κ or Δ1.
- Clarify the intended setting (finite-sum vs. general expectation) and the feasibility of Og. Fix typos and algorithm constants.
