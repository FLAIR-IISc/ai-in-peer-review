This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to reduce the computational cost associated with full gradient evaluations in strongly convex and smooth optimization problems. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient steps within epochs, achieving a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number. While the problem addressed is relevant and potentially impactful, and the idea of mixing gradients is intuitively appealing, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet distracting, flaws in presentation, analysis, and justification.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Reducing the cost of full gradient computations is a significant challenge in large-scale optimization.
*   **Novelty (Partial):** The specific combination of epoch-based descent with mixed gradients is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper provides a theoretical convergence analysis demonstrating linear convergence and a reduction in full gradient complexity.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The writing is frequently imprecise and lacks sufficient detail. Several statements are made without adequate justification or explanation. The notation is sometimes inconsistent.
*   **Technical Rigor:** The analysis, while claiming rigor, contains several gaps and hand-waving arguments. The connection between the theoretical guarantees and practical performance is not fully established.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison to related work, while present, is often superficial. The authors claim advantages over existing methods but lack a detailed complexity analysis that clearly demonstrates these advantages *under realistic conditions*. The discussion of how EMGD builds upon existing epoch-based methods feels underdeveloped.
*   **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper detract from its credibility.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claims regarding the advantages of EMGD, particularly concerning practical applicability, are somewhat overstated given the current presentation.

**Detailed Critique:**

1.  **Introduction:** The introduction lacks a strong motivating example. While stating the problem, it doesn’t clearly illustrate *where* this computational bottleneck arises in real-world scenarios beyond vague references to large datasets. The transition from discussing stochastic vs. full gradients to introducing EMGD feels abrupt.

2.  **Related Work:** The related work section is overly brief. A more thorough discussion of existing methods for reducing full gradient cost, such as variance reduction techniques (SVRG, SAGA), would be beneficial. The claim that EMGD achieves better results than SDCA in Table 2 relies on the assumption that full gradients are *n* times more expensive than stochastic gradients, which is not always true and should be explicitly stated as a constraint. The discussion of SAG and SDCA's storage requirements is irrelevant unless memory is a significant constraint.

3.  **Algorithm & Analysis:**
    *   **Oracle Definitions:** The definitions of the gradient oracle (Og) and function oracle (Of) are vague. The connection between Of and stochastic gradients isn't made clearly. Is `fkt(w)` assumed to be unbiased?
    *   **Algorithm Description:** The algorithm description in Algorithm 1 is unclear in several places. The meaning of `wkt+1` being the solution to a constrained optimization problem is not fully explained – how is this solved in practice? The shrinking domain size (∆k) is not sufficiently motivated; why is a shrinking domain beneficial?
    *   **Theorem 1 & Proof:** The conditions on T in Theorem 1 (δ ≤ e−1/2 and T ≥ 1152L2/λ2 ln(δ)) are highly restrictive and not justified. Why this specific threshold? The proof in Section 4 is exceptionally condensed and relies on several unsubstantiated claims (e.g., the bounds on AT and BT). The use of Lemma 2 without fully defining the martingale difference sequence is problematic. The convergence rate claimed in Theorem 1 is only theoretical and its impact on practical runtimes needs further investigation.
    * **Step-size:** The choice of step-size η = 1/[L √ T ] is suboptimal and does not exploit the strong convexity property effectively.

4.  **Minor Errors:**
    *   Equation (2) is redundant given the definition of the function oracle Of.
    *   In equation (3), the inequality direction is reversed.
    *   Numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper (e.g. "e.g." used incorrectly, missing articles, awkward phrasing).

5.  **Conclusion:** The conclusion reiterates the benefits of EMGD but lacks a balanced assessment of its limitations. The discussion of future work is underdeveloped. The suggested methods for estimating κ are themselves non-trivial and require further elaboration.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant problem in optimization.
*   The proposed algorithm has a theoretical guarantee of linear convergence.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from numerous technical flaws and lacks sufficient rigor.
*   The presentation is unclear and contains numerous minor errors.
*   The comparison to related work is superficial.
*   The claims of practical advantage are not convincingly supported.
*   The restrictive conditions imposed on T diminish the theoretical result's practical applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its potentially interesting idea, the paper's numerous flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of clarity, rigor, and detailed analysis significantly undermines the contribution. The paper requires substantial revisions to address these issues before it can be considered a solid scientific contribution. I recommend **rejection** at this time. A major revision addressing the points outlined above might warrant reconsideration.
