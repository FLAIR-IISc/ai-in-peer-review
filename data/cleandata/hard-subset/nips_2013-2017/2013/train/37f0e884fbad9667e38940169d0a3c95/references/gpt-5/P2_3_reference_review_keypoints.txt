- Paper studies EMGD: epoch-based mixed-gradient method using one full gradient per epoch and variance-reduced stochastic updates inside epochs.  
- EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls (high probability).  
- The mixed gradient estimator matches SVRG in finite-sum settings and is related to SAG/SAGA/SDCA variance-reduction families.  
- The paper emphasizes independence of full-gradient count from κ and ability to handle constraints, but omits explicit discussion of SVRG/SAGA literature.  
- High-level proof (control variates, shrinking-domain, martingale bounds) is standard and appears correct, but conservative.  
- Epoch length T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) is suboptimal vs known O(κ) epoch lengths in variance-reduction methods.  
- The function-oracle model Of returning f for multiple-point gradients is stronger than standard stochastic-gradient oracles and may be restrictive outside finite-sum problems.  
- No experiments are provided to validate practical performance, constants, or projection overheads.  
- Presentation has minor issues (duplicated labels, typos) and some overstated comparisons to SDCA/SAG regarding high-probability vs expectation results.  
- Relation to SVRG/SAGA and broader variance-reduction literature is missing, making novelty assessment difficult.  
- The need to know κ to set T and η, and the cost of projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk), should be highlighted earlier.  
- Originality is limited: algorithm and estimator are essentially SVRG-like; novelty mainly in focusing on full-gradient counts and using shrinking-domain projections.  
- Without improving the κ^2 factor or generalizing the oracle model, the contribution is modest relative to existing methods.  
- Significance is limited because total complexity (O(κ^2 log(1/ε))) is not competitive with state-of-the-art finite-sum methods.  
- Claimed advantages (constraints, low memory, high-probability bounds) are useful but unconvincing without experiments or stronger theory.  
- Pros: clear goal, simple algorithm, low memory, supports constraints, high-probability analysis.  
- Cons: overlap with existing VR methods, missing citations, suboptimal stochastic complexity, requirement of κ, no experiments, strong oracle assumption, projection costs, presentation issues.  
- Ask authors to position EMGD relative to SVRG/SAGA, tighten analysis to O(κ) per epoch if possible, and clarify what is genuinely new.  
- Request clarification if EMGD can work with a standard stochastic-gradient oracle that only returns ∇f(wt) at one point.  
- Suggest empirical comparisons on L2-regularized logistic regression/SVM and constrained variants to demonstrate practical trade-offs.  
- Ask for strategies to set T, η, and Δk without knowing κ or adaptive alternatives.  
- Ask authors to clarify computational cost and practicality of projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk).  
- Recommendation: lean toward rejection due to limited novelty, suboptimal κ dependence, and lack of empirical validation; suggest revision with tighter bounds, proper positioning, and experiments.
