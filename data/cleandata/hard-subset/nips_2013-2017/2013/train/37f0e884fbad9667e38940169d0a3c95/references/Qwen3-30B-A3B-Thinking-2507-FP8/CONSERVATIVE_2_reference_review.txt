

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm for smooth strongly convex optimization that claims to achieve linear convergence with condition number independent access to full gradients. The authors argue that by mixing full and stochastic gradients, their method can reduce the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), while requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations.

## Summary and Context

The paper builds on well-established results in convex optimization, particularly the standard lower bound of O(√κ log(1/ε)) for first-order methods with full gradients. The authors' approach is to introduce a "mixed" gradient that combines full and stochastic gradients, with the goal of reducing the number of full gradient evaluations. The paper compares EMGD with standard methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA, claiming computational advantages when κ ≤ n^(2/3).

## Strengths

The paper presents a technically sound theoretical analysis with a clear proof of the convergence rate. The algorithm design is well-motivated, and the authors carefully address the relationship between the condition number and computational complexity. The proof of Theorem 1 is thorough, with detailed technical arguments that appear correct. The paper also provides a useful comparison with existing methods in Table 2, highlighting specific advantages such as applicability to constrained problems and lower storage requirements compared to SAG and SDCA.

## Weaknesses

The main weakness of this paper is that the claimed advantage of "condition number independent access" is misleading. The paper's key contribution appears to be a reduction in the number of full gradient evaluations, but the number of stochastic gradient evaluations increases as O(κ²), which is problematic when κ is large. In many practical machine learning applications, κ can be much larger than n^(2/3), making the claimed computational advantage irrelevant. For instance, in typical regularized problems where the strong convexity parameter λ is proportional to the regularization parameter (e.g., λ = τ), and the smoothness parameter L is proportional to the maximum norm of the data, κ = L/λ can be as large as O(n) or larger.

The paper fails to provide convincing evidence that κ ≤ n^(2/3) is a common or practical scenario. The authors note that "as long as τ > Ω(n^(-2/3)), which is a reasonable scenario," but this claim is not supported by empirical evidence or references to real-world problems where this condition holds. In fact, for many practical machine learning problems, τ is often set to be small (e.g., τ = 1/n) to achieve good generalization, which would make κ = O(n) or larger.

Furthermore, the paper does not provide any experimental results to validate the theoretical claims. In the era of deep learning, where empirical validation is crucial, the lack of experiments is a significant drawback. The paper claims that "the computational complexity of EMGD is lower than Nesterov's algorithm [17] as long as the condition number κ ≤ n^(2/3)," but without experiments showing actual runtime comparisons, this claim remains unverified.

The assumption that κ must be known beforehand is also a major practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion but do not provide a convincing solution for estimating κ in practice. While they mention sampling and concentration inequalities, these are not developed into a practical algorithm.

## Comparison with Existing Work

The paper's most significant failure is its lack of clear distinction from existing "semi-stochastic" methods. The authors mention that their approach is similar to "semi-stochastic" approximation methods [12, 13], but they do not provide a detailed comparison with these methods. In particular, the paper does not address whether EMGD offers any advantage over the methods described in [12, 13] for the same problem settings. The paper's claim of "linear convergence" is also misleading, as this is standard for methods like SAG and SDCA when applied to finite-sum problems.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution, the claimed practical advantages are questionable. The condition number dependence of the stochastic gradient evaluations (O(κ²)) undermines the main claim of "condition number independent access" to full gradients. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence that the regime κ ≤ n^(2/3) is common in practice, and it lacks experimental validation. The assumption that κ must be known beforehand is also a significant practical limitation.

The paper makes a modest theoretical contribution to the literature on convex optimization, but it does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way for practical applications. The authors have not provided sufficient evidence to support their claims about computational efficiency, and the theoretical results do not translate into clear practical advantages for most machine learning problems.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper fails to demonstrate meaningful practical advantages over existing methods. The claimed "condition number independent access" to full gradients is misleading due to the κ² dependence on stochastic gradients, and the paper does not provide convincing evidence that the claimed computational advantage holds in practical scenarios. The lack of experimental validation is particularly problematic for a machine learning conference like NIPS.

The paper would be significantly improved if the authors could:
1. Provide empirical evidence showing the claimed computational advantage in practical settings
2. Demonstrate that κ ≤ n^(2/3) is a common scenario in machine learning problems
3. Address the issue of estimating κ without prior knowledge
4. Clearly differentiate their method from existing semi-stochastic approaches

Without these improvements, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims and would not be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
