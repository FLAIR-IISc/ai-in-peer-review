## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods for strongly convex smooth optimization, particularly when the condition number is large. The core idea of interleaving full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with a number of full gradient accesses independent of the condition number is interesting and potentially valuable. However, while the paper presents a solid theoretical analysis and a clear algorithm, the practical implications and comparisons to existing methods are somewhat limited and require further elaboration. I recommend **weak accept** with a request for more detailed experimental evaluation and a stronger discussion of practical limitations.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in optimization: the trade-off between the faster convergence of full-gradient methods and the computational expense of evaluating full gradients, especially for large-scale problems with ill-conditioning (high κ). Existing first-order methods exhibit iteration complexities that scale with √κ for full gradients. This work proposes EMGD, which cleverly combines full and stochastic gradients within epochs to achieve linear convergence (O(log 1/ε)) with only O(log 1/ε) full gradient evaluations, thus becoming independent of κ. 

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of optimization algorithms. It highlights the iteration complexity results for deterministic (gradient descent, Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent) and stochastic optimization (SGD, SAG, SDCA).  The connection to epoch gradient descent [9] is well-explained as a source of inspiration for the epoch-based approach.  The authors appropriately identify gaps in existing literature – the desire to reduce full gradient evaluations without sacrificing linear convergence – and clearly articulate how EMGD addresses this.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of strategically interleaving full and stochastic gradients within epochs to bypass the √κ dependence is a genuinely novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving the linear convergence rate and establishing the bounds on the number of full and stochastic gradient evaluations. The proof appears sound, leveraging strong convexity and smoothness properties effectively.
* **Clarity:** The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented clearly and concisely. The notation is well-defined, and the explanation of each step is easy to follow.
* **Well-Motivated Problem:**  The paper clearly articulates the motivation for the work – reducing the computational burden of full gradients in ill-conditioned problems – and emphasizes the practical relevance of this challenge.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge limitations, such as needing to know the condition number *a priori* and briefly discuss potential estimation strategies. The comparison to SAG and SDCA, highlighting advantages in storage and applicability to constrained problems, is useful.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Evaluation is Limited:** The paper lacks a comprehensive experimental evaluation. While the theoretical results are compelling, it’s crucial to demonstrate the practical benefits of EMGD. The authors mention potential application to machine learning, but no experiments on standard datasets (e.g., logistic regression, SVM) are presented.  Without empirical validation, it's difficult to assess the algorithm's performance in realistic scenarios.
* **Condition Number Estimation:** The paper acknowledges the need for knowing κ beforehand but only briefly mentions potential estimation strategies. A more detailed discussion of practical methods for estimating κ, including their own trade-offs (accuracy vs. computational cost), would be valuable.  The dependence on a good κ estimate could be a significant limitation in practice.
* **Computational Complexity of Mixed Gradient:** The calculation of the "mixed gradient" (g̃kt) requires both a full gradient and a stochastic gradient per iteration within an epoch.  The paper doesn't analyze the constant factors in the overall computation compared to simply using stochastic gradient methods. Is the overhead of computing the full gradient in each epoch outweighed by the reduced number of epochs?
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the comparison to SAG and SDCA is good, the paper doesn't compare EMGD to other relevant approaches for reducing full-gradient evaluations (e.g., methods that use low-rank approximations of the Hessian). A more thorough comparative analysis would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Discussion of Domain Size Shrinkage:** The domain size shrinkage (∆k+1 = ∆k/√2) is a crucial part of the algorithm, but its impact on the solution's accuracy and robustness isn’t fully explored. Is this shrinkage strategy optimal, or could other strategies be more effective?

**4. Originality & Significance:**

The algorithm's core idea is demonstrably original. While the concept of combining full and stochastic gradients isn’t entirely new, the specific epoch-based approach with a guaranteed linear convergence rate and condition number independence is a significant contribution. 

The significance of this work lies in its potential to make full-gradient methods more practical for large-scale, ill-conditioned problems.  If the empirical results support the theoretical claims, EMGD could be a valuable addition to the optimization toolbox, particularly in domains where full gradient information is crucial (e.g., certain machine learning tasks with specific regularization requirements).

**5. Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and theoretically sound algorithm.
* Addresses a practical challenge in optimization.
* Clear presentation and rigorous analysis.
* Potential for significant impact if empirically validated.

**Con:**

* Lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation.
* Dependence on knowing the condition number a priori.
* Limited discussion of practical implementation details and potential overhead.
* Comparisons to a broader range of relevant methods would be beneficial.



**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.** I believe the paper has merit and presents a valuable theoretical contribution. However, the lack of experimental validation raises concerns about its practical applicability. I strongly recommend that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by including a more thorough experimental evaluation on relevant datasets and providing a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's practical limitations and computational cost.  A clear discussion of strategies for estimating the condition number would also be beneficial. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
