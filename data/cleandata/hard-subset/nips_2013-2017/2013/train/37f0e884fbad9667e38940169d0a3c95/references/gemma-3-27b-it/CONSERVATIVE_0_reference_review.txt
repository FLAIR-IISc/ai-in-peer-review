This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations in strongly convex smooth optimization by strategically interleaving them with stochastic gradients. The core idea – dividing the process into epochs and performing a mixed gradient descent – builds upon existing epoch gradient descent methods. The authors claim to achieve a convergence rate independent of the condition number, reducing the full gradient complexity from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)).

While the problem addressed—reducing the cost of full gradients—is certainly relevant, and the motivation is clear, I find the paper’s contribution incremental and lacking in sufficiently compelling evidence. The leap from observing a tradeoff between iteration count and per-iteration cost to a new algorithm with a claimed condition number independent complexity feels overstated. Specifically, the assumption of access to *both* full and stochastic gradients is a critical point that, while stated, isn’t fully justified. Why would a problem both necessitate full gradients (ill-conditioning) *and* allow efficient stochastic gradient computation? This limits the immediate applicability of the method.

The technical soundness appears adequate, though the proof relies on a standard inductive argument building on existing lemmas. However, the analysis doesn’t fully convince regarding the practical implications. The constant factors hidden within the logarithmic terms are not discussed, and it’s unclear how they compare to existing methods *in practice*. The comparison to SAG and SDCA (Table 2) feels somewhat cherry-picked, focusing on a specific computational cost assumption (full gradient is n times more expensive than a stochastic gradient) and overlooking potential implementation complexities. Moreover, the claim of a stronger convergence guarantee for EMGD (linear convergence holds with high probability, unlike the expectation-based guarantees for SAG/SDCA) is not strongly supported and feels potentially misleading; Hoeffding-Azuma inequalities are routinely used to obtain high-probability bounds in stochastic optimization.

Clarity is acceptable, though the notation could be cleaner, and the connection between the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and the subsequent analysis is sometimes difficult to follow. The explanation of the mixed gradient and its connection to the shrinking domain requires careful reading.

Originality is the primary concern. The algorithm feels like a natural extension of existing techniques (epoch gradient descent, semi-stochastic methods), and the contribution doesn't represent a significant paradigm shift. The core idea of mixing gradients isn’t entirely novel. The value truly added is questionable.

Significance is limited. While reducing full gradient computations is valuable, the specific scenario where this method shines – requiring both full and stochastic gradients, and where κ is favorable compared to n – feels rather restricted. The practical impact, even within that restricted scenario, is not convincingly demonstrated. The discussion of practical considerations (estimating κ) is brief and hints at further work rather than offering a concrete solution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant problem in optimization.
* Technically sound analysis, building on established results.
* Presents a clearly defined algorithm.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Incremental contribution; lacks substantial novelty.
* Restricted applicability due to the requirement of both full and stochastic gradients.
* Limited discussion of practical considerations and constant factors.
* Weak comparison to existing methods.
* Overstated claims regarding condition number independence and convergence guarantees.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. The paper presents a technically correct but ultimately incremental contribution. The assumptions limiting its applicability and the lack of compelling evidence demonstrating significant practical improvements outweigh the marginal benefits. The claims regarding the improvement in complexity and convergence require stronger justification and more thorough empirical validation. I would be more receptive to a revised version with a more nuanced discussion of the algorithm’s limitations and a more comprehensive comparison to established methods.
