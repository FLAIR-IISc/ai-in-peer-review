

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence for smooth strongly convex problems with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number κ. The authors address a theoretically interesting question: can we reduce the number of full gradient computations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by leveraging stochastic gradients?

**Summary of Main Ideas:**
The paper builds on the standard convex optimization framework where the objective function F(w) is both smooth and strongly convex. The authors recognize that while full gradient methods achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity, stochastic gradient methods typically have slower convergence (O(1/ε) or O(1/λε)). They propose EMGD, which uses one full gradient per epoch and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, achieving linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This differs from existing methods like SAG and SDCA, which require O(n) storage and are only applicable to specific problem structures (sum of functions).

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by demonstrating how to achieve condition-number independent full gradient access. The convergence analysis is rigorous and provides high-probability guarantees, which is stronger than the expectation-based guarantees of SAG and SDCA.

2. **Novel Algorithmic Insight:** The mixed gradient approach (combining full and stochastic gradients) is a clever way to bypass the standard lower bounds for first-order methods. The paper correctly identifies that this is a new direction distinct from existing work.

3. **Thorough Comparison:** The paper provides a detailed comparison with existing methods in Table 2, showing the tradeoffs between different approaches. The discussion of advantages over SAG/SDCA (constrained optimization support, lower storage requirements, distributed computing potential) is particularly valuable.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written with clear notation and logical flow. The algorithm description and convergence proof are accessible to optimization researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Experimental Validation:** The most significant weakness is the absence of empirical results. The paper claims "computational complexity" advantages but provides no evidence that these translate to practical speedups. Without experiments, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical improvements.

2. **Practical Limitation - Known κ:** The algorithm requires knowing κ in advance, which is a major practical limitation. While the authors briefly mention future work on estimating κ, this is a significant barrier for real-world applications where κ is rarely known.

3. **Marginal Improvement in Many Cases:** Table 2 shows that EMGD's computational advantage over Nesterov's method only holds when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which may not be common in practice. For many machine learning problems, κ is typically much larger than n^(2/3).

4. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper doesn't adequately address very recent methods like those in [26] or [27] that also achieve linear convergence for smooth strongly convex problems with a different approach.

**Quality Assessment:**
The theoretical analysis is sound and the proof is technically correct. However, the paper lacks experimental validation to support the claims about computational efficiency. The authors are honest about the limitations (e.g., needing to know κ), but the practical significance is not sufficiently demonstrated.

**Significance Assessment:**
The theoretical insight is novel and valuable for understanding the tradeoffs between full and stochastic gradients. However, the practical significance is limited by the need to know κ and the lack of empirical validation. The paper advances theoretical understanding but doesn't clearly demonstrate how this translates to better practical optimization methods.

**Clarity Assessment:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear exposition of the algorithm and its analysis. The mathematical notation is consistent and the proofs are well-structured.

**Originality Assessment:**
The paper is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS work. While the combination of full and stochastic gradients has been explored in other contexts (e.g., [12]), the specific approach of using mixed gradients to achieve condition-number independent full gradient access is novel.

**Recommendation:**

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to optimization theory but lacks the empirical validation needed to demonstrate practical significance. The requirement to know κ in advance is a substantial practical limitation that needs to be addressed for this to be a compelling contribution for NeurIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Add experimental results comparing EMGD with SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's method on standard machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression, SVMs) with varying condition numbers.

2. Develop and test a practical method for estimating κ (e.g., using the approach mentioned in the conclusion but with concrete implementation details).

3. Expand the comparison to include more recent methods that achieve linear convergence for smooth strongly convex problems.

4. Provide a more nuanced discussion of when EMGD would be preferable to existing methods, perhaps by showing a range of κ values where it provides clear advantages.

While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the lack of empirical validation and the practical limitation of requiring known κ prevent this from being a strong NeurIPS submission. With the suggested improvements, particularly the addition of experiments, this could become a valuable contribution to the field.
