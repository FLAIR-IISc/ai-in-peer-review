- Paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth λ-strongly convex optimization using a full-gradient oracle Og and a stochastic function oracle Of.  
- EMGD computes a full gradient at a snapshot each epoch and uses the mixed estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) with projected steps in a shrinking ball.  
- Analysis yields linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls (T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) per epoch, η = 1/(L√T)).  
- The mixed-gradient estimator is identical to the control variate used by SVRG/SAGA; the paper omits citations and comparisons to SVRG, SAGA, and recent proximal VR methods.  
- Proof technique is sound: uses smoothness, strong convexity, shrinking projections to bound variance, and Azuma–Hoeffding for high-probability guarantees.  
- The O(κ^2) dependence on the condition number is significantly worse than known VR methods (SVRG/SAGA/SDCA achieve O(κ log(1/ε)) or O(n+κ)).  
- The analysis appears conservative; standard variance bounds tied to function suboptimality could likely reduce κ^2 to κ and remove shrinking domains.  
- Algorithm requires prior knowledge of κ to set epoch length and shrinking schedule, and needs repeated projections onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk), which can be costly.  
- The oracle model (access to both Og and Of) is not well-motivated for non-finite-sum settings and deserves more discussion.  
- No experiments are provided to validate practical performance, trade-offs, or advantages on constrained problems.  
- Paper is generally readable with detailed proofs, but contains typos and notation issues (malformed update in Algorithm 1, odd footnote placement, nonstandard strong-convexity statement).  
- Novelty is limited: core estimator and epoch structure mirror SVRG; shrinking-ball projection and high-probability proof are incremental and worsen κ dependence.  
- Claims about condition-number-independent full-gradient access are already achieved by SVRG; EMGD’s O(d) memory advantage over SAG/SDCA is overstated because SVRG also uses O(d).  
- Significance is limited since same full-gradient reduction is known with better overall complexity and without projections; applicability beyond finite-sum is unclear due to Og reliance.  
- Pros: clear, self-contained high-probability analysis; simple algorithmic template; O(d) memory; attention to constrained settings.  
- Cons: substantial overlap with prior VR methods, missing key citations/comparisons, worse κ dependence, requires κ knowledge and projections, no experiments, clarity/typo issues.  
- Suggested improvements: add and compare to SVRG/SAGA/proximal VR methods theoretically and empirically; tighten analysis to O(κ) or justify κ^2; remove shrinking-ball via variance bounds tied to suboptimality; clarify oracle model and finite-sum vs expectation settings; fix notation/typos; add experiments.  
- Recommendation: reject in current form due to overlap with existing variance-reduction methods, inferior theoretical rates, missing related work, and lack of empirical validation.
