This paper introduces a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods in strongly convex and smooth optimization by strategically incorporating stochastic gradients. The core idea – interleaving full and stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs – is a compelling and potentially impactful direction, especially given the increasing scale of datasets where full gradient computation becomes prohibitive. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost, and EMGD attempts to navigate this balance. The connection to Epoch Gradient Descent is also well-motivated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The algorithm represents a genuine attempt to circumvent the limitations imposed by the condition number in full-gradient methods. The combination of epoch-based updates with mixed gradients is a fresh approach.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a theoretical convergence analysis demonstrating linear convergence and, crucially, a reduction in the number of full gradient evaluations to O(log 1/ǫ), independent of the condition number. This is the paper's strongest asset.
*   **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The algorithm is presented clearly (Algorithm 1), and the main theorem is stated concisely.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of comparisons to existing methods like SAG and SDCA, including storage requirements and potential for distributed computing, showcases an awareness of practical implications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper *entirely* lacks experimental validation. While theoretical guarantees are valuable, the absence of experiments leaves the practical efficacy of EMGD unproven. It is unclear how the constant factors hidden in the big-O notation would affect performance, and whether the overhead of managing epochs and mixed gradients would offset the benefits.
*   **Dependence on Condition Number Knowledge:** The algorithm currently requires knowing the condition number *a priori*. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and briefly mention potential estimation techniques in the conclusion, this is a significant practical hurdle. Real-world applications often struggle to accurately estimate the condition number.
*   **Clarity of the Mixed Gradient:** The rationale behind the specific form of the "mixed gradient" (g̃kt) isn't entirely intuitive. A more detailed explanation of why this particular combination of full and stochastic gradients leads to improved convergence would be beneficial.
*   **Proof Details:** The proof sketch in Section 4 feels rushed and lacks detail. While the core ideas are mentioned, a more comprehensive presentation would increase confidence in the results. The references to lemmas without detailed application are problematic.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and addresses a significant challenge in large-scale optimization.
*   The theoretical result—achieving condition number-independent full gradient complexity—is a substantial contribution.
*   The paper's clear articulation of the problem and the proposed solution is commendable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The complete absence of experimental validation is a major weakness. Without experiments, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the algorithm.
*   The dependence on *a priori* knowledge of the condition number limits the applicability of the method.
*   The lack of detail in the proof makes it harder to verify the correctness of the analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a compelling theoretical contribution with a potentially significant impact on the field of optimization. The novelty of the approach and the strong theoretical guarantees are highly attractive. However, the complete lack of experimental validation is a serious concern. While I generally prioritize novelty over methodological perfection, the absence of *any* empirical results is too significant to ignore.

Despite my reservations, I lean towards **acceptance** with a strong recommendation for future work to include extensive experimental validation. The theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant publication, but the paper’s ultimate impact will depend on demonstrating its practical benefits. The authors should be strongly encouraged to address the limitations regarding condition number estimation and provide a more detailed and rigorous proof in a follow-up work.



**Rating:** 7/10 (Strong Accept, contingent on future experimental work)
