Summary
The paper studies first-order optimization for smooth, λ-strongly convex objectives F over a convex domain W, under access to two oracles: (i) a full gradient oracle Og returning ∇F(w), and (ii) a function oracle Of that samples a random smooth function f with F(w) = Ef[f(w)], enabling unbiased stochastic gradients. The main contribution is Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which operates in epochs. Each epoch computes one full gradient at a snapshot point w̄k and then performs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k). The iterates are constrained to a shrinking Euclidean ball around w̄k. With stepsize η = 1/(L√T) and T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)), the analysis shows a constant-factor decrease per epoch, yielding linear convergence: to reach ε-suboptimality, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient evaluations (independent of κ) and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations with high probability.

Relation to prior work
- Deterministic first-order methods: Nesterov’s accelerated gradient achieves O(√κ log(1/ε)) full-gradient iterations for smooth, strongly convex objectives; this is optimal for first-order full-gradient methods.
- Stochastic methods: For general stochastic optimization, rates are typically polynomial in 1/ε; when F is a finite sum F = (1/n)∑ fi, variance-reduced methods achieve linear convergence. SAG (NIPS 2012) and SDCA (NIPS/ICML 2012–2013) obtain O(n log(1/ε)) and O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) component-gradient complexity, respectively.
- Very closely related is SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and its proximal variants, which use exactly the same control-variate gradient estimator ∇F(w̄)+∇f(w)−∇f(w̄), perform one full gradient per epoch, and achieve linear convergence with total complexity O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) in the finite-sum setting and O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients per epoch. The paper does not cite or compare to SVRG/SAGA, which is a significant omission.

Strengths
- Objective: The goal of decoupling the number of full-gradient evaluations from κ is clear and well-motivated for large-scale problems where a full gradient is expensive.
- Convergence guarantee: A high-probability linear convergence result is established, with explicit parameter choices and a clean epoch-wise contraction argument. The proof technique—bounding the variance via smoothness and a shrinking trust region and using Azuma–Hoeffding—is sound in structure.
- Memory and constraints: The method requires O(d) memory and readily accommodates constraints via projection onto W∩B(·;·), which is an advantage over some finite-sum methods that store per-component information.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to variance-reduction: The mixed-gradient estimator is the same as in SVRG; EMGD’s structure (one full gradient per epoch, inner stochastic updates around a snapshot) is also highly similar. The main distinct elements are (i) a shrinking ball constraint and (ii) a high-probability analysis. The paper does not acknowledge or compare to SVRG/SAGA/Prox-SVRG, which already achieve condition-number-independent numbers of full gradients with better stochastic-gradient complexity. This substantially diminishes novelty.
- Suboptimal stochastic-gradient complexity: EMGD requires O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, which is worse than the O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) complexity of SVRG/SDCA in finite-sum problems and worse than O(κ log(1/ε)) dependence that can be achieved without the shrinking-ball device in variance-reduced analyses.
- Oracle model and practicality: The algorithm assumes access to an exact full gradient oracle Og, which is a strong requirement outside finite-sum settings. The need to know κ and to choose T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)) is also limiting; the paper acknowledges this but does not provide an adaptive strategy. The shrinking-domain projection may be impractical in constrained problems where projection is already costly, and it adds algorithmic complexity.
- Clarity and presentation: There are typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., “epoches,” ln δ vs ln(1/δ), incomplete references). Equation (4) states ‖∇F(w)−∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w−w′‖, which is stronger than the standard definition of strong convexity; later inequalities use the standard definition, so (4) should be corrected for clarity. The requirement on Δ1 references F(w*) which is unknown; guidance for setting Δ1 without access to F(w*) would improve practicality.

Quality
- The main theorem and supporting lemmas follow a standard template for variance-reduced analyses. The argument appears technically sound under the stated assumptions. However, the rate is conservative (κ^2) compared to existing variance-reduced methods, suggesting the analysis is non-tight. Some constants are large (e.g., 1152 L^2/λ^2).
- The comparison with SAG/SDCA is fair in some respects (memory, constraints) but omits SVRG/SAGA and proximal variants that share EMGD’s low memory and apply to constrained/prox settings.

Clarity
- The high-level idea and algorithm are understandable. Proof steps are mostly clear, but the paper would benefit from correcting the definition of strong convexity used, fixing typos, and tightening notation. A more precise complexity accounting (distinguishing component vs full gradients) would help.

Originality
- Limited, due to strong overlap with SVRG/SAGA ideas and estimator; the main differences (shrinking ball and high-probability bound) do not appear to improve complexity and may hinder practicality.

Significance
- The guarantee of O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls is valuable, but this property is already achieved by established methods with better stochastic-gradient dependence and broader applicability. Without empirical results or tighter theory, the contribution is incremental.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear objective and a high-probability linear convergence result.
- Low memory footprint and an argument for constrained problems.
- Simple algorithmic structure.

Cons for acceptance
- Overlaps heavily with existing variance-reduction methods; missing key related work (SVRG/SAGA).
- Weaker stochastic-gradient complexity (κ^2) than the state of the art.
- Practical limitations (need for κ, exact full gradients, shrinking-domain projections).
- Presentation issues and minor technical inaccuracies/typos.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate EMGD within the variance-reduction literature (SVRG, SAGA, Prox-SVRG, Katyusha/KatyushaX), clarifying similarities/differences and providing theoretical and empirical comparisons.
- Aim to tighten the analysis to O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients per epoch, possibly removing the shrinking-ball constraint and adopting standard VR proofs.
- Provide an adaptive schedule for epoch length and stepsize that does not require κ, e.g., doubling/tripling epoch length or using line-search/variance tests.
- Clarify the oracle model and discuss feasibility outside finite-sum settings; consider mini-batching and distributed variants.
- Fix definitions (strong convexity), typos, and give a practical way to set Δ1 without knowing F(w*).
- Add experiments on standard benchmarks comparing with AGD, SVRG/SAGA, SAG, and SDCA.

Overall recommendation
- Given the overlap with established variance-reduction methods and weaker complexity, the contribution appears incremental. Strengthening novelty, tightening the rate, and adding thorough comparisons would be needed for a strong case.
