- Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that mixes one full gradient per epoch with many stochastic gradients for smooth, strongly convex problems.
- Claims linear convergence with only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, making full-gradient access independent of the condition number κ.
- Theoretical analysis shows EMGD needs O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to reach ε-optimality.
- Convergence proof (Theorem 1) is rigorous, uses standard convex tools and martingale inequalities, and provides probabilistic guarantees.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear problem framing, precise algorithm description, consistent notation, and reproducible proofs.
- Originality lies in the specific epoch-based combination of one full gradient per epoch with stochastic updates to decouple full-gradient count from κ.
- Practical advantages include low storage O(d) (vs O(n) for SAG/SDCA) and applicability to constrained optimization.
- Significance: reduces costly full-gradient evaluations for ill-conditioned problems and gives practical guidelines (EMGD preferable when κ ≤ n^(2/3)).
- Major weakness: no empirical validation — absence of experiments leaves practical performance and constant-factor effects untested.
- Requires prior knowledge of κ to set parameters (e.g., epoch length T), which is a significant practical limitation not resolved in the paper.
- Complexity comparisons show EMGD is not always best (e.g., worse than SAG/SDCA when κ > n^(1/2)), and more discussion on regimes of superiority is needed.
- Potential overstatement: full-gradient count is κ-independent, but stochastic gradient count still depends heavily on κ (O(κ² log(1/ε))), which can be problematic.
- Recommendation: accept — strong theoretical contribution and clear presentation, but would be improved by adding simple experiments and methods to estimate κ.
