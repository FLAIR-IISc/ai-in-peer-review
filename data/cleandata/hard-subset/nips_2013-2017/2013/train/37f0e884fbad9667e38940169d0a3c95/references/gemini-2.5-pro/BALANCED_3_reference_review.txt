### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex functions. The primary goal is to design an algorithm that reduces the dependency on expensive full gradient computations, particularly for ill-conditioned problems where the condition number `κ` is large. Standard accelerated methods require `O(sqrt(κ) log(1/ε))` full gradient evaluations. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a cheaper stochastic gradient oracle.

EMGD operates in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at the current solution estimate. Then, within the epoch, it performs a fixed number of iterations using a "mixed gradient," which is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient estimator constructed using the full gradient from the start of the epoch. The algorithm also confines the updates to a shrinking `l2`-ball around the epoch's starting point. The main theoretical result is that EMGD achieves an `ε`-optimal solution by computing `O(log(1/ε))` full gradients and `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))` stochastic gradients. This makes the number of expensive full gradient calls independent of the condition number `κ`, which is the paper's central contribution. The authors compare their method theoretically to full gradient, stochastic gradient, SAG, and SDCA methods, highlighting advantages in its applicability to constrained optimization and its low memory footprint.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Interesting Problem:** The goal of decoupling the number of full gradient evaluations from the condition number `κ` is highly relevant and important. In many large-scale machine learning settings, computing a full gradient is the main bottleneck, and this work provides a principled approach to minimizing this cost.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a rigorous convergence analysis, culminating in a high-probability linear convergence rate (Theorem 1). The proof, based on an inductive argument over epochs, appears to be technically sound. Obtaining a high-probability bound, rather than a bound in expectation, is a notable strength compared to some related work (e.g., SAG, SDCA, SVRG).

3.  **Generality and Practical Advantages:** The proposed algorithm is presented for a general stochastic optimization framework (`F(w) = E[f(w)]`) and is not limited to the finite-sum setting. Furthermore, its ability to handle constrained problems (via the projection step in line 8 of Algorithm 1) is a significant advantage over methods like SAG and SDCA, which are typically designed for unconstrained problems. The authors also correctly point out the low memory requirement (`O(d)`) as a benefit over methods that need to store past gradients.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Crucial Missing Comparison to SVRG:** The paper's most significant weakness is the omission of any reference or comparison to Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) [Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013]. The "mixed gradient" estimator used in EMGD, `g̃_kt = ∇F(w̄^k) + ∇f_kt(w^k_t) - ∇f_kt(w̄^k)`, is *identical* to the gradient estimator used in SVRG. The epoch-based structure of computing one full gradient and then performing multiple inner updates is also the core idea of SVRG. While EMGD has differences (e.g., the shrinking domain constraint, the averaging scheme, and the high-probability analysis vs. expectation in SVRG), the fundamental algorithmic primitive is not novel. This omission significantly overstates the originality of the proposed algorithm. A thorough discussion and comparison of the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees with SVRG is essential.

2.  **High Dependence on `κ` for Stochastic Gradients:** While the number of full gradients is independent of `κ`, the number of stochastic gradients per epoch (`T`) scales with `O(κ^2)`. This `κ^2` dependence is quite severe and may limit the practical applicability of the method for ill-conditioned problems. For the finite-sum case, the total complexity of EMGD (in units of stochastic gradients, assuming a full gradient costs `n`) is `O((n + κ^2) log(1/ε))`. This compares unfavorably with SVRG's `O((n + κ) log(1/ε))` complexity. The comparison in Table 2 is therefore incomplete, as it does not fully account for this `κ^2` factor when comparing total computational cost. The claim that EMGD is advantageous for `κ ≤ n^(2/3)` seems plausible but needs to be benchmarked against state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods like SVRG or SAGA.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While theory papers are valuable, the claims about computational efficiency would be much more convincing if supported by experiments. An empirical comparison against AGD, SVRG, and SAG/SDCA on a standard problem like regularized logistic regression would help validate the theory and demonstrate the practical trade-offs of the `κ^2` stochastic gradient complexity versus the `κ`-independent full gradient complexity.

4.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the smoothness `L` and strong convexity `λ` parameters (and thus `κ`) to set the number of inner iterations `T`. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but it is a significant practical limitation. The use of a shrinking domain `B(w̄^k; Δ_k)` also introduces a hyperparameter `Δ_1` that must be set based on properties of the initial point, which can be difficult in practice.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Integrate SVRG:** The authors should revise the paper to thoroughly acknowledge SVRG. The related work section should position EMGD relative to SVRG, highlighting the identical gradient estimator. The contribution should be framed more carefully, emphasizing the differences: the high-probability analysis, the extension to general stochastic/constrained settings, and the analysis technique using a shrinking domain, rather than the algorithm's core structure.
*   **Refine Complexity Comparison:** The complexity analysis in Section 3.4 should be expanded to provide a direct comparison of the *total* computational cost (in stochastic gradient units) with SVRG, SDCA, and SAG. This will provide a more nuanced understanding of the regimes where EMGD is superior.
*   **Add Experiments:** I strongly encourage the authors to include an experimental section. Even simple synthetic experiments could demonstrate the performance profile of EMGD as `κ` and `n` vary, and compare it directly to its closest competitors. This would be invaluable for assessing the practical impact of the work.
*   **Justify the Shrinking Domain:** It would be helpful to add a sentence or two in the algorithm description to motivate the use of the shrinking domain `B(w̄^k; Δ_k)`. Is it essential for controlling the variance and achieving the high-probability bound? Clarifying its role would improve the paper's clarity.

### **Recommendation**

**Reject.**

The paper presents an interesting theoretical result around a well-motivated problem. However, the failure to cite and compare with SVRG constitutes a major flaw, as the core algorithmic idea is substantially overlapping with this highly influential prior work. This omission prevents an accurate assessment of the paper's originality and contribution. Furthermore, the steep `O(κ^2)` dependency in the stochastic gradient complexity, combined with the lack of any empirical evidence, raises questions about the algorithm's practical significance compared to existing methods. While the work has several strengths, particularly its generality and high-probability analysis, these contributions are not enough to outweigh the major weaknesses in its current form. I believe the paper could be a strong submission to a future conference after a major revision that addresses the points raised above, especially the positioning relative to SVRG.
