

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm that achieves linear convergence for smooth strongly convex problems while reducing the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log 1/ε) to O(log 1/ε). The key insight is combining one full gradient per epoch with O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, thereby making the full gradient access independent of the condition number κ.

The paper builds on the well-known gap in optimization literature: full gradient methods suffer from O(√κ) dependence on condition number, while stochastic methods have better iteration complexity but slower convergence in terms of 1/ε. The authors cleverly bridge this gap by introducing a "mixed gradient" that combines deterministic and stochastic components. This approach is distinct from existing methods like SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, which either depend on κ or require different computational assumptions.

The main theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which proves that EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log 1/ε) full gradient evaluations and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradient evaluations. The proof is technically sound, with careful handling of the stochastic component through martingale analysis. The comparison with related work in Section 3.4 is particularly strong, showing that EMGD has advantages in terms of applicability to constrained problems, storage requirements, and convergence guarantees (high probability vs. expectation).

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty:** The mixed gradient approach represents a genuinely new perspective on combining full and stochastic gradients, addressing a fundamental limitation in optimization theory. This is not just an incremental improvement but a new conceptual direction.

2. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a clean theoretical analysis showing condition-number-independent access to full gradients, which is a significant theoretical advance in convex optimization.

3. **Practical Implications:** The authors clearly articulate when their approach would be beneficial (κ ≤ n^{2/3}), making it relevant to real machine learning problems where the condition number is often large but manageable.

4. **Comparative Analysis:** The comparison with SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's method is thorough and highlights both theoretical and practical advantages of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical Limitation:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ in advance, which may be difficult to obtain in practice. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a compelling solution for estimating κ.

2. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical with no experiments to validate the practical performance of EMGD. While theoretical novelty is valued, some empirical evidence would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Computational Assumption:** The analysis assumes that a full gradient is n times more expensive than a stochastic gradient, which might not hold in all scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to a fundamental problem in optimization
- The theoretical contribution is significant and addresses a known limitation in the literature
- The analysis is technically sound and well-justified
- The approach could have broad applicability in machine learning where full gradient evaluations are expensive

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Lack of empirical validation for the theoretical claims
- Requires knowledge of κ in advance, which is a practical limitation
- The computational complexity comparison with SAG/SDCA might not always favor EMGD

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance in convex optimization. The core idea of combining full and stochastic gradients in a novel way to achieve condition-number-independent full gradient access is bold and original. While the paper has some minor weaknesses (particularly the lack of empirical validation and the need for known κ), these do not undermine the substantial theoretical contribution. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and conceptual advances over minor methodological imperfections, I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field.

The authors successfully identify a gap in the literature and provide a theoretically sound approach to address it. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for the novelty of their approach. The comparison with related work is thorough, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous.

I believe this paper should be accepted at NIPS. The novelty of the approach, the clean theoretical contribution, and the clear articulation of the problem and solution outweigh the minor limitations. This work opens up a new direction for optimization research that could influence future algorithms and applications in machine learning. The paper's contribution to understanding the tradeoffs between full and stochastic gradients is significant and represents a meaningful advance in the field.
