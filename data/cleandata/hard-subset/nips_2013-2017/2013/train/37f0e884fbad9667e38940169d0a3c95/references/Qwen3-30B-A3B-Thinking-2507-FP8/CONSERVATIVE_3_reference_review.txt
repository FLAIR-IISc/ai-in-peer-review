

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), claiming to achieve linear convergence for smooth strongly convex optimization by reducing the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log 1/ε) to O(log 1/ε), independent of the condition number κ. The key innovation is using a "mixed gradient" that combines one full gradient with O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch.

## Strengths
The paper presents a theoretically sound algorithm with a clear convergence proof (Theorem 1) that correctly demonstrates the reduced full gradient complexity. The analysis properly addresses the relationship between the condition number, stochastic gradients, and convergence rate. The related work section adequately covers relevant literature, including SAG, SDCA, and Nesterov's methods. The paper also correctly identifies the practical limitations of existing approaches.

## Major Weaknesses

### 1. Lack of Empirical Validation
The most significant weakness is the complete absence of experimental results. The paper makes strong claims about computational efficiency ("the computational complexity of EMGD is lower than Nesterov's algorithm [17] as long as the condition number κ ≤ n²/³"), but provides no empirical evidence to support this. Without experiments showing actual runtime comparisons on real datasets, these claims remain theoretical and unverified. This is particularly concerning for a paper making computational efficiency claims, as the constant factors in the O-notation are crucial for practical relevance.

### 2. Overstated Practical Relevance
The paper's main claim relies on the assumption that "stochastic gradients can be computed efficiently," but fails to demonstrate that the increased number of stochastic gradient evaluations (O(κ² log 1/ε)) actually leads to computational savings in practice. For many machine learning problems, κ can be much larger than n²/³ (the threshold mentioned), making the claimed advantage irrelevant. The paper doesn't provide any examples where κ ≤ n²/³ in realistic machine learning applications.

### 3. Unaddressed Critical Limitation
The paper acknowledges that "a drawback of EMGD is that it requires the condition number κ is known beforehand," but fails to provide a practical solution for this. The authors briefly mention "how to find a good estimation of κ" as future work without addressing whether this is feasible in practice. In real-world applications, κ is rarely known a priori, making this a significant limitation for practical adoption.

### 4. Superficial Comparison with Baselines
The comparison with SAG and SDCA in Table 2 is purely theoretical and lacks empirical validation. The paper claims advantages in storage requirements and convergence probability, but doesn't provide concrete examples or data showing these advantages translate to real-world benefits. The paper doesn't compare against the most relevant baseline, SVRG, which is the standard method for this problem.

### 5. Overstated Significance
The paper claims EMGD "may not be the best one [in terms of computational cost], but it has advantages in other aspects." However, these claimed advantages (constrained optimization, storage requirements, high-probability convergence) are not substantial enough to justify the theoretical novelty without empirical evidence. The paper fails to demonstrate that these advantages outweigh the computational cost increase.

## Specific Technical Concerns

1. The paper claims "the number of full gradients required is reduced from O(√κ log 1/ε) to O(log 1/ε)," but doesn't address how the O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients compare to the O(√κ log 1/ε) stochastic gradients used by standard methods like SVRG.

2. The condition T ≥ 1152L²/λ² ln(1/δ) in Theorem 1 suggests that the number of stochastic gradients per epoch could be prohibitively large for large condition numbers, which contradicts the paper's claim of practical efficiency.

3. The paper assumes F(w) = (1/n)∑fi(w), but doesn't address how this assumption affects the practical applicability of the method to more general convex optimization problems.

## Conclusion

While the theoretical contribution is technically sound, the paper lacks the empirical validation required to support its claims about computational efficiency. As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes established methods and rigorous validation, I find the paper's claims unsubstantiated. The absence of experimental results is particularly concerning for a paper making computational efficiency claims. The paper would need extensive empirical validation on standard machine learning datasets to demonstrate that the theoretical reduction in full gradient evaluations translates to practical runtime improvements.

The paper makes a modest theoretical contribution but fails to demonstrate its practical relevance. Without empirical evidence showing that EMGD outperforms existing methods in practice, the paper does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper makes strong claims without sufficient experimental validation to support them. The theoretical results are interesting but lack practical significance without empirical demonstration. The paper would benefit from extensive experiments comparing EMGD to state-of-the-art methods like SVRG, SAG, and SDCA on standard machine learning datasets before being considered for publication.
