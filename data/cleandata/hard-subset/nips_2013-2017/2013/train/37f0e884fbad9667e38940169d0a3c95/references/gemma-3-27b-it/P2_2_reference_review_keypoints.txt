- Proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) that interleaves full and stochastic gradients to get linear convergence while reducing expensive full gradient evaluations.  
- Claims full-gradient accesses drop from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), independent of condition number κ, with theoretical support.  
- Addresses the important problem of reducing full-gradient computation cost in large-scale, ill-conditioned problems.  
- Introduces a novel epoch-based mixed gradient strategy combining benefits of full and stochastic gradients.  
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees and formal convergence analysis quantifying full and stochastic gradient counts.  
- Compares method to Nesterov, SAG, SDCA and highlights potential advantages like broader applicability and stronger guarantees.  
- Paper is generally clear, well-structured, and uses consistent notation with a straightforward algorithm presentation.  
- Practical implications are underexplored: hidden constants and per-epoch iteration counts (T) may significantly affect performance.  
- Reliance on knowing or estimating κ is a practical limitation; proposed solutions are underdeveloped.  
- Implementation may be more complex than simpler alternatives (e.g., SGD) due to epoch management and mixed-gradient overhead.  
- Lacks empirical evaluation and comparisons to state-of-the-art solvers on standard benchmark datasets.  
- Connection to existing epoch-based methods is not fully clarified beyond brief mentions.  
- Assumes access to both gradient and function oracles, which may be unrealistic in some applications.  
- Quality: technically sound and rigorous theory, but weakened by absence of experiments.  
- Originality: promising novel combination, but differentiation from related epoch methods could be strengthened.  
- Significance: potentially impactful if theory translates to practice, but practical significance remains uncertain without experiments.  
- Pros: tackles an important challenge, presents a novel algorithm with strong theory, thorough analysis, and clear presentation.  
- Cons: missing experiments, practical implementation concerns (hidden constants, κ estimation), limited discussion of drawbacks, and weaker ties to related work.  
- Overall: promising theoretical contribution that requires substantial empirical validation to assess practical impact.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept, contingent on adding substantial experimental results and sensitivity analyses for κ estimation and hidden constants.
