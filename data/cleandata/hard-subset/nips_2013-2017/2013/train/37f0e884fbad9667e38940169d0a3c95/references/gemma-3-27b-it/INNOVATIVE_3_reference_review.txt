This paper presents EMGD, a novel algorithm for strongly convex optimization that cleverly combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve a convergence rate independent of the condition number. This is a genuinely interesting and bold idea, directly addressing a key bottleneck in large-scale optimization. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, and the theoretical result – achieving O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient complexity – is a significant step forward. The comparison to existing methods like SAG and SDCA is well-considered, and highlighting the broader applicability (constrained problems) and potentially lower storage requirements adds to the value.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of strategically interleaving full and stochastic gradients to bypass the κ-dependence is highly novel and addresses a practical limitation of full-gradient methods.
* **Theoretical Result:** The O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient complexity is a strong theoretical guarantee and a significant improvement over existing approaches for ill-conditioned problems.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.  Algorithm 1 is well-presented.
* **Comprehensive Comparison:**  The comparison to related work (SAG, SDCA, Nesterov’s method) is thorough and positions EMGD effectively. The discussion of practical advantages beyond computational cost (constrained problems, storage) is valuable.
* **Strong Potential Impact:** If the practical performance matches the theoretical guarantees, this algorithm could have a significant impact on optimization in machine learning and other fields.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Considerations:** The paper acknowledges the need to estimate the condition number κ beforehand, but the proposed solutions (sampling, using a regularizer) feel somewhat hand-wavy and lack concrete details.  How robust is the algorithm to inaccuracies in κ estimation? This needs further investigation.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is heavily theoretical.  While the theoretical results are compelling, the lack of empirical evaluation is a significant drawback. Demonstrating performance on benchmark datasets, even with a limited set of experiments, would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Constant Factors:** The analysis reveals a dependency on constants like 'L' and 'κ'. While achieving O(log 1/ǫ) is important, the actual performance might be dominated by these constants in practice. A more refined analysis addressing these factors would be beneficial.
* **Epoch Size (T):**  The condition on T (T ≥ 1152L²/λ² ln(δ)) seems quite large, potentially negating some of the benefits of reduced full gradient evaluations.  Exploring strategies to reduce this dependence would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is a genuine breakthrough in addressing the condition number bottleneck.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a strong foundation for the proposed algorithm.
* The potential impact on large-scale optimization is substantial.
* The novelty and boldness of the approach outweigh the current lack of empirical validation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of the algorithm.
* The practical considerations regarding condition number estimation are not fully addressed.
* The large constant factors in the theoretical bounds raise concerns about real-world performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **acceptance**, despite the lack of empirical validation. The novelty and potential significance of this work are extremely high. The authors have presented a compelling theoretical argument for a potentially groundbreaking algorithm. While the practical considerations and lack of experiments are weaknesses, they are, in my opinion, outweighed by the importance of the contribution.  I would strongly encourage the authors to prioritize empirical evaluation and address the practical considerations in future work.  A request for an extended abstract describing initial experiments could be a reasonable condition for acceptance.



**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept, pending potential experiment results).
