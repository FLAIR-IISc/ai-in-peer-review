

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm for smooth strongly convex optimization that reduces the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) by mixing full and stochastic gradients. The authors claim this achieves linear convergence independent of the condition number κ.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel algorithmic approach that effectively combines full and stochastic gradients. The theoretical analysis demonstrates a clear reduction in full gradient accesses, and the algorithm appears to handle both constrained and unconstrained optimization problems. The comparison with SAG and SDCA is well-structured, highlighting advantages in storage requirements, applicability to constrained problems, and high-probability convergence guarantees.

## Weaknesses

1. **Notational inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. In multiple places, it writes "O(log 1ǫ )" with a space between "log" and "1", which is non-standard. It should be "O(log(1/ε))" or "O(log(1/ε))". Similarly, "ǫ" is sometimes written as "ε", creating confusion. The abstract contains "O( √ κ log 1ǫ )" which should be "O(√κ log(1/ε))" with proper spacing.

2. **Theoretical constants**: The condition T ≥ 1152L²/λ² ln(1/δ) in Theorem 1 appears unnecessarily large. The constant 1152 (2×24²) lacks justification, and the paper doesn't provide a lower bound showing this constant is necessary. This makes the practical applicability of the algorithm questionable.

3. **Probability bound issues**: The probability bound 1-mδ in Theorem 1 is problematic for large m. If m = O(log(1/ε)) and δ = O(1/log(1/ε)), the probability approaches 0, contradicting the claim of high probability convergence. The paper should specify how δ should be chosen to maintain a constant probability bound as m increases.

4. **Algorithmic details**: The description of Algorithm 1 is ambiguous about how the gradient oracle Og is implemented. If F(w) = (1/n)∑fi(w), computing ∇F(w̄k) requires O(n) work, yet the paper claims this can be distributed to O(n/k) with k computers without explaining how this is integrated into the algorithm. This disconnect between theory and practical implementation needs clarification.

5. **Comparison with prior work**: The paper claims EMGD has lower computational complexity than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), but doesn't specify whether this is in terms of function evaluations, gradient evaluations, or actual computational time. The paper should clarify this distinction, as the cost of full vs. stochastic gradients differs significantly.

6. **Practical limitations**: While the paper mentions that EMGD requires knowing κ beforehand, it provides no practical method to estimate κ in real applications. The brief mention of estimating κ via sampling from the Hessian is insufficient, as it doesn't address how to handle the sampling error or how this affects the convergence guarantee.

7. **Minor formatting errors**: In the related work section, the paper cites "the two very recent works" as [22, 23] but doesn't provide the full references in the text. The references section has incomplete information (e.g., "IEEE Transactions on Information Theory,, , , " with multiple commas).

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, the numerous minor but significant technical inconsistencies, notational errors, and unresolved practical concerns prevent it from being accepted. The paper's claims about computational complexity would be much stronger with a more precise analysis of the constant factors and a clearer explanation of how the algorithm would be implemented in practice. The notational inconsistencies alone are problematic for readers attempting to understand or reproduce the results.

The paper makes a novel contribution to the field, but the current presentation contains too many technical and formatting issues that undermine its credibility. I recommend rejection due to these unresolved issues, though the core idea has merit.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address the notational inconsistencies, justify the constant factors, clarify the practical implementation details, and provide a more precise comparison of computational complexity.
