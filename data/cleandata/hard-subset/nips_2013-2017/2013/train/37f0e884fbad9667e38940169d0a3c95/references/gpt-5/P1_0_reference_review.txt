Summary
The paper studies first-order optimization for smooth, strongly convex objectives and proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD). The setting assumes two oracles: (i) a full gradient oracle Og, and (ii) a “function” oracle Of that samples a random function f such that F(w) = E[f(w)] with each f L-smooth, enabling stochastic gradients. EMGD runs in epochs: at the start of epoch k it computes one full gradient ∇F(w̄k); then performs T inner iterations using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k) and updates via a proximal projection over a shrinking ball around w̄k. The main result shows a linear convergence rate with high probability: after m epochs (m calls to Og and mT calls to Of), F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ λΔ1^2 / 2^{m+1}, provided T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) and a stepsize η = 1/(L√T). Hence EMGD attains an ε-accurate solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (independent of κ) and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper positions this as reducing expensive full-gradient evaluations by leveraging cheaper stochastic gradients, and compares qualitatively against Nesterov’s accelerated methods, SAG, and SDCA.

Relation to prior work
- Full-gradient methods: For smooth strongly convex F, accelerated gradient descent attains O(√κ log(1/ε)) iterations with full gradients (Nesterov). EMGD reduces full gradient calls to O(log(1/ε)).
- Stochastic/incremental methods: SAG and SDCA achieve linear rates for finite-sum structure F = (1/n)∑ fi, with total gradient complexity roughly O((n + κ) log(1/ε)). The proposed mixed estimator ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄) is essentially the same variance-reduced estimator used by SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013), which also computes a full gradient once per epoch and uses inner stochastic updates with linear convergence. Unlike SAG/SAGA, EMGD uses O(d) memory. The paper does not cite SVRG, which is highly relevant and similar in spirit.

Strengths
- Clear goal: minimize the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations while retaining linear convergence.
- Simple algorithmic structure: periodic full gradients plus a variance-reduced direction that combines a fresh stochastic gradient with a snapshot subtraction.
- High-probability linear convergence guarantee (via Azuma-Hoeffding), not just in expectation.
- Memory efficiency: requires O(d) storage, an advantage over SAG/SAGA-style methods that store past gradients/parameters.
- Applicability to constrained problems (assuming prox over intersection with a ball is computable).

Weaknesses and limitations
- κ^2 dependence in stochastic gradient complexity: The inner epoch length T must be Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)), leading to O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients overall. This is substantially worse than SVRG/Prox-SVRG and SDCA, which achieve O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) or O(κ log(1/ε)) dependence in stochastic steps in regimes where full gradients cost n. As a result, EMGD is likely dominated computationally except in narrow regimes.
- Missing key related work: The estimator and epoch strategy are essentially SVRG’s; the absence of Johnson & Zhang (2013) and subsequent variance-reduction methods (e.g., SAGA) reduces the perceived originality and makes the novelty unclear. The claimed advantages over SAG/SDCA (e.g., high-probability vs expectation) could likely be obtained for those methods as well.
- Assumptions and oracle model: The algorithm requires access to both a full gradient oracle and a function oracle that allows multiple gradient evaluations for the same sampled f at different points. This is natural for finite-sum problems but less standard in generic stochastic optimization where only unbiased gradients at a single point are available. The paper’s claims outside the finite-sum setting thus rely on a fairly strong oracle model.
- Technical correctness issue: Strong convexity is incorrectly stated as ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖ (Eq. (4)). The standard strong convexity condition is (∇F(w) − ∇F(w′))^T (w − w′) ≥ λ‖w − w′‖^2, or equivalently F(u) ≥ F(v) + ⟨∇F(v), u − v⟩ + (λ/2)‖u − v‖^2. While the subsequent inequality used in (13) is consistent with strong convexity, the statement in (4) should be corrected.
- Practicality: The method requires knowledge of κ to set T and η; the authors note this as a drawback. The projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) may be nontrivial for general W. There are no empirical results to assess constants, robustness, and practical performance against baselines.
- Comparisons: Some claims (e.g., distributed implementability or strength of guarantees relative to SAG/SDCA) are asserted but not rigorously justified; distributed and asynchronous variants exist in the literature. The quantitative comparisons assume specific cost models; more careful empirical or theoretical accounting would help.

Quality
- The core analysis is standard variance-reduction with a shrinking trust region and martingale concentration, and appears broadly sound, aside from the incorrect strong convexity statement. The κ^2 dependence emerges from bounding variance via smoothness times the shrinking radius; it is conservative compared to state-of-the-art analyses that achieve κ. No experiments are provided.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but there are several typos/formatting issues, and the strong convexity definition is wrong. The algorithm and theorem statements are clear; the proof sketch is detailed enough for reproduction by an expert. The related work omits crucial references (SVRG), and some comparisons are not fully precise.

Originality
- The mixed gradient estimator and epoch structure closely mirror SVRG. The shrinking ball constraint and the specific high-probability analysis are variations, but the core idea is not novel relative to known variance-reduction methods. Positioning the contribution as making full-gradient calls independent of κ is also achieved by SVRG for finite-sum objectives.

Significance
- The theoretical point that O(log(1/ε)) full gradients suffice is interesting, but not new in the finite-sum regime. Given the κ^2 factor and lack of experiments, the practical impact is limited. The memory advantage and constrained-setting applicability are pluses, but similar benefits have been achieved by other methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Cite and compare thoroughly to SVRG (and related SAGA/MISO/Finito), including complexity in full and stochastic gradients, memory, and convergence mode (expectation vs high probability).
- Fix the strong convexity definition and carefully check all inequalities depending on it.
- Investigate whether the κ^2 dependence can be improved to κ via tighter variance bounds (as in SVRG analyses) without the shrinking ball, or by adaptive epoch lengths/stepsizes.
- Provide experiments on standard datasets comparing to accelerated gradient, SVRG/Prox-SVRG, SAG/SAGA, and SDCA under matched cost models; report wall-clock and gradient counts.
- Discuss practicality of the projection step and oracle assumptions; clarify how Of is instantiated in finite-sum problems and in general stochastic settings.

Recommendation
- Leaning toward reject in current form due to limited novelty relative to existing variance-reduced methods (notably SVRG), weaker stochastic complexity (κ^2), missing key related work, and lack of experiments. The paper could be strengthened substantially by tightening the analysis to κ, situating the contribution vis-à-vis SVRG/SAGA/SDCA, correcting technical statements, and adding empirical validation.

Confidence: High.
