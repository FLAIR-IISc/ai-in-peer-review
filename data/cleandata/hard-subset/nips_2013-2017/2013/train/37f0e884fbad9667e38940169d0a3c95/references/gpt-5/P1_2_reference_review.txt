Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies first-order optimization of smooth and strongly convex objectives F over a convex domain W, under access to two oracles: (i) a full gradient oracle Og(w) = ∇F(w) and (ii) a function oracle Of returning random f with F(w) = E[f(w)] and each f L-smooth, enabling stochastic gradients. The main contribution is Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an epoch-based projected method that, in each epoch k, computes a single full gradient at the epoch anchor w̄k and then performs T projected steps using a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k). By shrinking an ℓ2 ball constraint across epochs and averaging, the paper proves a per-epoch constant error reduction with high probability. The stated complexity to reach ε-suboptimality is O(log(1/ε)) accesses to Og and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) accesses to Of, where κ = L/λ.

The idea of using control variates to reduce the variance of stochastic gradients around a snapshot full gradient is closely related to subsequent variance-reduction methods for finite-sum problems F = (1/n)∑ fi: SAG (Le Roux et al. 2012/2013), SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang 2013), SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), SAGA (Defazio et al. 2014), and later developments (e.g., Katyusha). Among these, SVRG uses essentially the same gradient estimator ∇fi(wt) − ∇fi(w̃) + ∇F(w̃), needs one full gradient per epoch, obtains a linear rate with O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, and requires only O(d) memory. The present work differs in adding a shrinking-ball constraint and proving a high-probability bound, but suffers a κ^2 dependence rather than κ.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Analysis: The high-level proof strategy is standard: decompose the error over an epoch, bound the variance via smoothness and a bounded domain, invoke a martingale concentration inequality, and choose η = 1/(L√T) to ensure a constant reduction. The derivations are generally coherent.
- However, there are technical issues that should be corrected:
  - The paper states as Eq. (4) that ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖ for λ-strongly convex F. This inequality is not generally true; strong convexity implies (∇F(w) − ∇F(w′))⊤(w − w′) ≥ λ‖w − w′‖^2, but not the norm inequality. Fortunately, the subsequent inequality used in (13) is the correct strong convexity inequality on function values, so the proof likely goes through without Eq. (4). Still, Eq. (4) must be fixed throughout.
  - In (5), the condition “T ≥ (1152 L^2/λ^2) ln δ” has a sign error; it should be ln(1/δ), which the paper later uses.
  - The dependence T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) seems suboptimal compared to known results (e.g., SVRG yields T = O(κ) for step-sizes of order 1/L without shrinking radii). It would be helpful to discuss whether the κ^2 is an analysis artifact (from using η = 1/(L√T) and Azuma with bounded ranges) and whether a refined analysis (e.g., variance recursion or smoothness-based self-bounding) can improve it to O(κ).
- Assumptions: The Of oracle assumption is stronger than pure unbiased stochastic gradients but matches the finite-sum case. The method requires knowledge of κ to set T and the shrinkage schedule; while the authors note this as a drawback, no adaptive remedy is provided.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but there are numerous typographical and grammatical issues (“epoches,” “interstage,” formatting of Algorithm 1, missing symbols in the proximal update term, inconsistent use of ln δ vs ln(1/δ)). Tightening notation and fixing these would improve clarity.
- The constrained update “argmin over W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk)” relies on efficient projection; a brief discussion of when this is tractable would help practitioners.
- A clear comparison to closely related SVRG/SAGA should be added, both conceptually (same estimator) and in complexity.

Originality
- The mixed-gradient estimator and one-full-gradient-per-epoch template are essentially those of SVRG. EMGD’s distinctive elements are: the shrinking-ball constraint, a high-probability analysis, and a focus on the number of full gradients being independent of κ. However, SVRG already achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients without shrinking, with O(d) memory. As such, the novelty is limited unless the authors can show a substantive advantage (e.g., handling general constraints that SVRG cannot, or provably better constants/robustness).

Significance
- Reducing the number of full gradients to O(log(1/ε)) is useful in distributed or expensive-gradient settings. However, the κ^2 dependence on stochastic gradients is not competitive with state-of-the-art VR methods, and there are no experiments to support practical benefits or to demonstrate advantages in constrained problems or distributed settings. This weakens practical significance for NeurIPS.
- Claims in the comparison section are somewhat dated or incomplete. For example, SVRG is not cited; modern SDCA/SAGA analyses and proximal variants handle a range of settings, and high-probability bounds can often be obtained.

Pros
- Clear goal: decouple full-gradient calls from κ while retaining linear convergence.
- High-probability linear convergence proof.
- Works with constraints (if projection is tractable) and uses only O(d) memory.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to SVRG/SAGA; key idea and estimator are well known.
- Suboptimal κ^2 dependence and requirement of shrinking ball and known κ.
- Technical inaccuracies (Eq. (4), ln δ sign).
- No empirical evaluation; unclear practical benefits versus established VR methods.
- Comparisons omit closely related SVRG and more recent literature; some comparison claims are debatable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a thorough comparison to SVRG/SAGA/acceleration (Katyusha), both theoretically and empirically. Clarify what EMGD offers that these do not (e.g., constraints, distributed full gradients).
- Fix technical errors (replace Eq. (4) by the correct strong convexity inequality; correct log terms; clean up Algorithm 1).
- Investigate whether the κ^2 term can be reduced to κ with a refined analysis or parameter choice, removing the shrinking ball if possible.
- Provide experiments on standard finite-sum problems (logistic regression, SVM) and a constrained example, comparing wall-clock and oracle counts.
- Propose an adaptive scheme that does not require κ (e.g., doubling epoch length or using observed variance).

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper contains a clear and interesting goal and a sound high-probability analysis, but its core idea duplicates known SVRG-style variance reduction with weaker complexity, lacks crucial related work and empirical validation, and contains technical inaccuracies. Addressing the above issues could make it suitable for a future submission.
