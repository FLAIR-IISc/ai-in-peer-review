This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm aiming to reduce the computational cost of full-gradient methods for strongly convex and smooth optimization by strategically incorporating stochastic gradients. The core idea – dividing the optimization process into epochs and utilizing a mixed gradient consisting of a full gradient and stochastic gradients – is conceptually sound and addresses a practical concern regarding the scalability of full-gradient approaches. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost and attempt to mitigate the former by leveraging the latter. The connection to epoch gradient descent and related work (SAG, SDCA) is appropriately discussed.

However, despite the promising premise, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet significant, flaws that collectively diminish its overall quality and warrant rejection. My assessment is based on a meticulous examination of the technical details, clarity, and presentation.

**Strengths:**

*   The motivation for the work is well-defined and relevant. Reducing the dependence on full gradients is a valuable goal.
*   The theoretical analysis demonstrates a logarithmic iteration complexity with respect to accuracy, independent of the condition number, which is a notable result *if* the assumptions hold.
*   The comparison with existing methods (Nesterov, SAG, SDCA) provides context and highlights potential advantages.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Notational Inconsistencies & Clarity Issues:** The notation is frequently inconsistent and lacks clarity. For example, `fkt` is used to denote a *random function* within epoch k and iteration t, yet it is then used in the gradient calculation `∇fkt (wkt )−∇fkt (w̄k)`. This strongly implies a function *evaluation* at different points, not a function itself. This is a critical ambiguity. Furthermore, the subscripting convention (k for epoch, t for iteration) is sometimes applied inconsistently.
2.  **Algorithm 1 – Ambiguity in Update Rule:** Step 8 of Algorithm 1 defines the update `wkt+1 = argmin w∈W∩B(w̄k;∆k) η〈w −wkt , g̃kt 〉+ 1/‖w −wkt ‖2`. This is a proximal gradient step, but the quadratic term `1/‖w −wkt ‖2` is highly unusual and unexplained. Is this a typo? If intentional, its purpose and justification are entirely missing. This casts doubt on the correctness of the algorithm.
3.  **Theorem 1 – Unjustified Assumptions & Lack of Rigor:** The conditions imposed in Theorem 1, specifically `δ ≤ e−1/2` and `∆1 ≥ max √ 2 λ (F (0)− F (w∗))`, are not well motivated. Why is such a stringent constraint on δ necessary? The initial domain size `∆1` being linked to `F(0) - F(w*)` is an odd constraint; typically, the Lipschitz constant would be more relevant. The lack of justification severely weakens the theorem's significance.
4.  **Analysis – Hand-waving & Missing Details:** The convergence proof (Section 4) is extremely terse and relies on numerous unsubstantiated claims. The bounding of `AT` and `BT` using the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality is presented without sufficient detail, making it difficult to verify its correctness. Many steps are simply stated without explanation. The use of Lemma 2 is correct, but the preceding steps are not explained with sufficient detail, leading to concerns regarding the validity of the proof.
5.  **Comparison – Superficial & Limited:** The comparison with SAG and SDCA in Section 3.4 is limited to computational complexity and overlooks crucial differences in applicability and implementation challenges. The statement that EMGD "works for both constrained and unconstrained problems" is misleading; the constrained problem in step 8 *must* be solvable efficiently, which isn't always the case.
6.  **References – Formatting Error:** The references are not formatted consistently. Some have volume numbers while others do not.
7.  **Minor Errors:** Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor grammatical errors and typos (e.g., “epoch gradient descent [9]. It divides…” should be “epoch gradient descent [9], which divides…”). These small issues detract from the overall polish of the paper.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The algorithm addresses a relevant problem and achieves a desirable theoretical result (logarithmic convergence).
*   The paper presents a clear motivation and comparison to related work.

**Con:**

*   Significant clarity issues in the algorithm description and notation.
*   Lack of rigor in the theoretical analysis and proof.
*   Unjustified assumptions and missing details in the convergence theorem.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the *idea* behind EMGD is potentially interesting, the paper is marred by a significant number of technical flaws and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. The ambiguities in the algorithm, the incomplete proof, and the numerous minor errors raise serious concerns about its correctness and reliability. The lack of a thorough and rigorous presentation makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the clarity issues, provide a more detailed and rigorous analysis, and carefully review the entire manuscript for errors before resubmitting.
