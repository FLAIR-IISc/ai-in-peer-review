## Peer Review for "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aimed at reducing the computational cost associated with full gradient evaluations in strongly convex and smooth optimization. The core idea of strategically mixing full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with a number of full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number is compelling. While the paper is clearly written and theoretically sound, its practical significance and comparison to existing methods could be strengthened. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses a key challenge in large-scale optimization: the cost of computing full gradients when the problem is ill-conditioned (high condition number κ). Standard first-order methods require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations to reach an ε-optimal solution. EMGD proposes a hybrid approach, leveraging the efficiency of stochastic gradients to reduce this dependency to O(log(1/ε)). This builds upon the idea of epoch-based gradient descent [9] and seeks to bridge the gap between full-gradient methods (faster convergence) and stochastic gradient methods (cheaper per iteration). 

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of convex optimization, highlighting the trade-offs between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost. They provide a good overview of related work, including deterministic, stochastic, and semi-stochastic methods, referencing key papers like Nesterov’s accelerated gradient descent [16,18], SAG [22], and SDCA [23]. The paper accurately points out the limitations of existing methods, particularly regarding computational complexity and storage requirements.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of epoch-based descent with a strategic mixture of full and stochastic gradients is a novel approach that addresses a significant practical concern.  The theoretical guarantee of condition number independent full gradient access is a strong contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of EMGD, demonstrating its linear convergence rate and bounding the number of full and stochastic gradient evaluations. The proof, while dense, appears logically sound and follows a standard inductive approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized.  The algorithm is presented clearly (Algorithm 1), and the notation is consistent throughout.
* **Potential for Efficiency:** Reducing the dependence on the condition number κ for full gradient evaluations has the potential to significantly improve performance on ill-conditioned problems, especially in large-scale machine learning applications.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge the limitation of requiring knowledge of κ and suggest avenues for estimating it in future work (distributed computing, leveraging regularization).

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any experimental results. While the theoretical analysis is strong, it's crucial to demonstrate the practical benefits of EMGD on real-world datasets. A comparison to existing methods (e.g., SDCA, stochastic gradient descent with adaptive step sizes) would greatly strengthen the paper.  Showing how the performance scales with κ would be particularly valuable.
* **Comparison with Existing Methods:** While the paper mentions comparisons in Table 2, it feels somewhat superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the practical trade-offs between EMGD, SAG, SDCA, and full gradient methods is needed, considering factors beyond computational complexity.
* **Practicality of the Domain Constraint:** The algorithm relies on projecting the solution onto a shrinking ball around the current iterate (Step 8 of Algorithm 1). The computational cost of this projection step, especially for high-dimensional problems, is not discussed. The authors should address the feasibility and efficiency of this step in practice.
* **Assumptions:** The algorithm's performance relies on knowing the smoothness constant L and strong convexity parameter λ. While the authors acknowledge this and discuss estimation strategies, the robustness of the algorithm to inaccurate estimates should be briefly discussed.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete theoretical analysis. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the proofs appear rigorous. However, the lack of empirical validation detracts from its overall quality.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The algorithm is easy to understand, and the notation is consistent.
* **Originality (8/10):** The idea of strategically mixing full and stochastic gradients in an epoch-based framework is novel. The authors effectively build upon existing concepts to create a new approach.
* **Significance (7/10):** The potential impact of EMGD is significant, particularly for large-scale optimization problems with ill-conditioning. However, the lack of experimental results makes it difficult to assess its practical significance.




**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Novel and theoretically sound algorithm addressing a practical problem.
*  Clear and well-written presentation.
*  Potential for significant performance improvements in specific scenarios.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Lack of empirical evaluation.
*  Limited discussion of practical implementation details.
*  Superficial comparison with existing methods.



**6. Recommendation and Suggestions for Revision:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should:

* **Include experimental results:**  Evaluate EMGD on a few benchmark datasets and compare its performance to existing methods. Focus on scenarios where the condition number is high.
* **Elaborate on the practical implications of the domain constraint:** Discuss the computational cost of the projection step and potential strategies for efficient implementation.
* **Provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods:**  Analyze the trade-offs between EMGD, SAG, SDCA, and full gradient methods in terms of computational cost, memory requirements, and convergence rate.
* **Briefly discuss the robustness of the algorithm to inaccurate estimates of L and λ.**




I believe that addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the research community.
