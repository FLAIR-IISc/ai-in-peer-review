### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for minimizing smooth and strongly convex functions. The algorithm operates in a setting where both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle are available. The primary goal is to achieve a linear convergence rate while minimizing the number of expensive full gradient computations, particularly making this number independent of the problem's condition number, κ.

The proposed EMGD algorithm proceeds in epochs. At the start of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at the current solution anchor. Then, for a fixed number of inner iterations, it updates the solution using a "mixed gradient" estimator, which combines the initial full gradient with a variance-reduction term constructed from stochastic gradients. The authors prove that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This result demonstrates that the number of full gradient calls is independent of κ, trading this improvement for a larger number of stochastic gradient calls that depends quadratically on κ. The paper compares this complexity to standard full gradient methods and other linear-convergent stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA, arguing for advantages in certain regimes and for constrained problems.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in large-scale optimization: the high computational cost of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned settings. The goal of making the number of full gradient calls independent of the condition number κ is a valuable research direction.

2.  **Generality of the Framework:** Unlike several competing methods such as SAG and SDCA that are designed specifically for finite-sum objective functions and unconstrained problems, the proposed EMGD algorithm is presented for a more general stochastic optimization framework (F(w) = E[f(w)]) and can handle convex constraints, provided the projection subproblem is tractable.

3.  **Stronger Convergence Guarantee:** The theoretical analysis provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is a stronger result than the in-expectation guarantees provided by many stochastic optimization algorithms, including SAG and SDCA.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the main ideas behind the analysis are communicated effectively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical Omission of Related Work and Originality:** The most significant weakness of this paper is its failure to cite and compare with highly relevant, contemporaneous work on variance-reduced stochastic gradient methods. The core idea of EMGD—using a full gradient at the start of an epoch to construct a variance-reduced stochastic estimator for subsequent iterations—is the central mechanism of Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) [1] and Semi-Stochastic Gradient Descent (S2GD) [2]. The "mixed gradient" `g̃_kt = ∇F(w̄^k) + ∇f_kt(w_kt) - ∇f_kt(w̄^k)` is precisely the unbiased gradient estimator used in SVRG. Given that SVRG was published at NIPS 2013, its omission from a paper citing other 2013 works is a critical flaw. This oversight significantly undermines the claimed originality of the proposed algorithm and misrepresents the state of the art.

2.  **Sub-optimal Complexity:** The analysis shows that the number of inner stochastic gradient steps, T, must be O(κ²). This leads to a total stochastic gradient complexity of O(κ² log(1/ε)). In contrast, the standard analysis of SVRG [1] requires only O(κ) inner iterations, leading to a total complexity of O((n+κ)log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient calls for finite-sum problems. The O(κ²) dependency in EMGD is substantially worse and would make the algorithm less practical for problems with even moderately large condition numbers. The paper does not provide any justification for why its analysis leads to this worse dependency compared to SVRG.

3.  **Practicality Concerns:** The algorithm's parameters, particularly the number of inner iterations T, depend on the condition number κ, which is often unknown in practice. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, it remains a significant barrier to practical application. More advanced methods in this family often employ adaptive strategies to circumvent this requirement.

4.  **Incomplete Comparison:** The comparison in Section 3.4 and Table 2 is incomplete and potentially misleading due to the omission of SVRG. A proper comparison of total computational work (e.g., in terms of equivalent stochastic gradient evaluations) against SVRG is necessary to fairly evaluate EMGD's contribution. For the finite-sum case, the total work for EMGD is O((n + κ²)log(1/ε)), while for SVRG it is O((n+κ)log(1/ε)). This shows that SVRG is strictly better in terms of dependence on κ.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   The paper tackles an important and well-motivated problem.
*   The proposed framework is more general than some competing methods (e.g., applicable to constrained optimization).
*   The high-probability convergence analysis is a technical strength.

**Arguments against:**
*   **Fatal Flaw in Citing Prior Art:** The algorithm is nearly identical to SVRG [1], a seminal paper in this area, which is not cited or discussed. This fundamentally questions the novelty of the work.
*   **Worse Theoretical Guarantees:** The algorithm's O(κ²) dependence on the number of stochastic gradients is significantly worse than the O(κ) dependence achieved by SVRG and related methods.
*   **Limited Practicality:** The requirement to know the condition number κ to set hyperparameters is a major practical limitation.

### Decision

I recommend **rejection** for this paper.

The failure to acknowledge and compare against SVRG [1] is a critical oversight that makes it impossible to evaluate the paper's contribution in the context of the field. The proposed algorithm is not novel in its core mechanism. Furthermore, the theoretical results obtained are weaker (in terms of dependence on κ) than those already established for SVRG. While the high-probability analysis and extension to constrained problems are potentially valuable, they do not outweigh the fundamental issues of originality and the suboptimal convergence rate.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

For the authors to improve this work, I strongly recommend the following:

1.  **Thorough Literature Review:** Conduct a comprehensive review of variance-reduced methods, starting with SVRG [1], S2GD [2], SAGA [3], and their extensions.
2.  **Reframe the Contribution:** The paper must be repositioned in light of this prior work. If the core contribution is, for instance, a novel analysis that provides high-probability bounds for SVRG-type methods under constraints, then the paper should be framed as such. The current framing as a novel algorithm is not tenable.
3.  **Improve the Analysis:** Investigate why the analysis results in an O(κ²) dependency for the inner loop length. Standard SVRG analysis techniques should allow for an O(κ) dependency. The use of a shrinking ball `B(w̄^k;∆k)` in the projection step is non-standard and might be the cause; clarifying its role or removing it in favor of a more standard analysis would be beneficial.
4.  **Address Practicality:** To make the work more impactful, consider an adaptive variant of the algorithm that does not require prior knowledge of L and λ.
5.  **Empirical Evaluation:** A revised version should include a thorough empirical comparison against relevant baselines, most importantly SVRG, on a range of datasets.
