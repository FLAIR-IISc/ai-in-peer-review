This paper proposes a novel first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex objective functions. The primary goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradient computations, particularly for ill-conditioned problems where the condition number κ is large. The standard complexity for accelerated methods is O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient calls. The proposed EMGD algorithm leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. It operates in epochs, computing one full gradient at the beginning of each epoch and then performing O(κ²) stochastic gradient-based updates within the epoch. The main theoretical result is that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution using O(log(1/ε)) full gradient calls and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient calls. This effectively makes the number of full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ, which is the paper's central claim. The authors also highlight advantages over methods like SAG and SDCA, such as applicability to constrained problems, lower memory requirements, and a high-probability convergence guarantee.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Goal:** The problem of reducing the dependency on the condition number κ for expensive gradient computations is well-motivated and highly relevant to large-scale machine learning. Achieving a number of full gradient calls that is logarithmic in 1/ε and independent of κ would be a significant theoretical advance.

2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous proof of linear convergence for the proposed algorithm. The result holds with high probability, which is a stronger guarantee than the convergence in expectation offered by many related stochastic methods. The analysis, leveraging standard tools like martingale concentration inequalities, appears to be sound.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the motivation for the mixed-gradient approach and the epoch-based structure is well-explained.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the complete absence of any experimental results. For a paper proposing a new optimization algorithm, this is a major deficiency. The theoretical complexity hides constants and may not reflect real-world performance. Without empirical evidence, it is impossible to assess whether EMGD provides any practical benefit over established methods. How does it compare in wall-clock time against Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, or modern stochastic variance-reduced methods like SVRG, SAG, and SDCA? A thorough empirical study on both synthetic and real-world datasets is necessary to validate the claims and demonstrate the algorithm's utility.

2.  **High Stochastic Gradient Complexity:** The claimed benefit of κ-independent full gradient calls comes at the cost of O(κ²) stochastic gradient calls per epoch. This dependence on κ² is very steep. For the ill-conditioned problems that this method targets (i.e., large κ), the number of required stochastic gradients could become prohibitively large, potentially making the algorithm slower in practice than methods with a better dependence on κ (e.g., O(κ) in SVRG or SDCA). The paper's own comparison in Table 2 shows that EMGD is only competitive with SAG/SDCA in a narrow regime (κ ≤ n¹/²), which severely limits its practical applicability. The authors should be more forthright about this significant trade-off.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison to Related Work:** The related work section omits several highly relevant variance-reduced stochastic gradient methods. Most notably, Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) is missing. SVRG also uses an epoch-based structure, computes a full gradient periodically, and achieves linear convergence. The mixed gradient `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the variance-reduced gradient used in SVRG and related methods. The paper needs to be situated properly within this literature and a clear comparison of the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees against SVRG is required. This omission undermines the claimed novelty of the approach.

4.  **Practicality of Hyperparameters:** The algorithm requires setting the number of inner iterations T based on the condition number κ (`T ≥ 1152(L²/λ²) log(1/δ)`). In practice, L and λ (and thus κ) are often unknown and difficult to estimate. The paper defers this crucial practical issue to future work. Furthermore, the constant `1152` is very large, suggesting that T would need to be massive, casting further doubt on the algorithm's practical efficiency.

**Summary of arguments:**

**Pros:**
*   The paper addresses an important and well-known bottleneck in convex optimization.
*   The theoretical goal of making full gradient calls independent of κ is novel and appealing.
*   The analysis provides a strong high-probability linear convergence guarantee.

**Cons:**
*   The work is entirely theoretical, with no experiments to support the claims or demonstrate practical utility. This makes the contribution feel incomplete and unproven.
*   The O(κ²) dependency for stochastic gradients is a major drawback that likely negates the benefits of the method for the very problems (large κ) it aims to solve.
*   The paper fails to compare against key, highly similar prior work, particularly SVRG.
*   The method relies on knowledge of the condition number κ, a common but significant practical limitation that is not addressed.

In its current state, the paper presents an interesting theoretical idea but fails to provide the rigorous validation required for a top-tier conference like NeurIPS. The claims of superiority are not backed by evidence, and the comparison to the state of the art is incomplete. While the theoretical direction is promising, the work is not mature enough for publication. A comprehensive empirical evaluation and a more thorough engagement with the literature on variance reduction are essential.
