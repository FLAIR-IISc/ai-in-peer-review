This paper addresses the high cost of full gradient evaluations in first-order methods for smooth and strongly convex optimization, where the complexity typically scales with `O(sqrt(kappa))`, with `kappa` being the condition number. The central and highly novel idea is to develop an algorithm whose required number of full gradient evaluations is independent of `kappa`. To achieve this, the authors propose a mixed-oracle model where the algorithm has access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle.

The proposed algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), cleverly combines these two oracles. It operates in epochs, where at the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed and stored. Within the epoch, a series of `O(kappa^2)` updates are performed using a "mixed gradient." This mixed gradient is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient, using the stored full gradient as an anchor point, similar in spirit to techniques like SVRG. The algorithm guarantees a constant factor reduction in error at each epoch, leading to an overall linear convergence rate. The main theoretical result is that EMGD requires `O(log(1/epsilon))` full gradient calls and `O(kappa^2 * log(1/epsilon))` stochastic gradient calls to reach an `epsilon`-optimal solution. This successfully shifts the dependency on the condition number from the expensive full gradient calls to the cheaper stochastic ones.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its bold and original core contribution. The goal of making the full gradient complexity independent of the condition number is ambitious and, to my knowledge, novel. By modifying the oracle model to be more realistic for many large-scale settings (where both full passes and mini-batches are possible), the paper bypasses a well-known lower bound. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a conceptual leap that could open a new and fruitful direction of research in optimization. It encourages the community to think differently about existing complexity barriers and how they might be circumvented by pragmatic changes to the computational model.

*   **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and establishes a high-probability linear convergence rate. This is a strong type of guarantee, more desirable than the in-expectation results often found for related stochastic algorithms like SAG or SDCA. The proof structure, based on induction over epochs, is clear and logical.

*   **Generality:** A key advantage of EMGD over many competing linear-rate stochastic methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA) is its generality. The analysis does not assume the finite-sum structure required by those methods and is applicable to general stochastic objectives. Furthermore, the algorithm naturally handles constrained optimization problems, which is a significant practical advantage. The authors also fairly discuss other benefits like lower memory requirements.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **High Cost of Stochastic Gradients:** The primary weakness is the `O(kappa^2)` dependency on the number of stochastic gradients per epoch. This is a steep price to pay for removing the `kappa` dependency from the full gradient calls and may be worse than the `O(kappa)` dependency seen in other variance-reduced methods. However, given the novelty of the framework, I view this as a first result in a new direction. This is a foundational paper, and follow-up work will almost certainly focus on improving this rate. The conceptual contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance despite this high cost.

*   **Knowledge of Problem Parameters:** The algorithm requires knowledge of `L` and `lambda` (and thus `kappa`) to set the number of inner-loop iterations `T`. While this is a common requirement in theoretical analyses, it is a practical limitation. The authors honestly acknowledge this and propose reasonable heuristics in the conclusion, but it remains a weakness of the current formulation.

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The submission is entirely theoretical. While the strength of the theoretical idea is sufficient for a NIPS paper, the inclusion of even a simple synthetic experiment to demonstrate the trade-off between full and stochastic gradients as a function of `kappa` would have made the paper's claims more concrete and compelling.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The core idea of achieving full-gradient complexity independent of the condition number is highly novel and significant.
2.  The work introduces a new conceptual approach to optimization that could inspire a new line of research.
3.  The algorithm is more general than many competing methods, as it handles general stochastic objectives and constraints.
4.  The analysis provides strong, high-probability convergence bounds.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The `O(kappa^2)` dependency for stochastic gradients is high and may limit practical performance.
2.  The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number.
3.  The paper lacks any experimental validation of its theoretical claims.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The conceptual novelty and significance of making the full gradient complexity independent of the condition number far outweigh the weaknesses. This is precisely the kind of bold, thought-provoking work that NIPS should showcase. While the practical efficiency of this specific algorithm (EMGD) might be debated due to the `kappa^2` term, the paper's main value lies in opening a new door. The limitations are not fundamental flaws but rather opportunities for future research to build upon this exciting foundation. This is a beautiful theoretical result that fundamentally alters our understanding of the trade-offs in large-scale optimization.
