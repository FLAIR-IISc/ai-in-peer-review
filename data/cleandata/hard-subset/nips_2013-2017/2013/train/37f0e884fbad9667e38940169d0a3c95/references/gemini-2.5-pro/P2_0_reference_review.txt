### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the optimization of smooth and strongly convex functions, a fundamental problem in machine learning. The authors identify a key bottleneck in first-order methods: the number of expensive full gradient computations often depends on the problem's condition number, κ. The main contribution is a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which aims to make the number of full gradient evaluations independent of κ. EMGD is a hybrid method that uses both full and stochastic gradients. It operates in epochs, where each epoch begins with a single full gradient calculation. Subsequent iterations within the epoch use a variance-reduced stochastic gradient estimator, constructed from the initial full gradient and stochastic gradients at the current and initial points of the epoch. The authors prove that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate, requiring O(log(1/ε)) full gradient computations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient computations to reach an ε-optimal solution. They argue this provides a significant advantage over standard accelerated gradient descent when κ is large, and also highlight benefits over other variance-reduced methods like SAG and SDCA, such as applicability to constrained problems, lower memory usage, and a high-probability convergence guarantee.

### Review

This is a well-written and theoretically interesting paper that proposes a novel trade-off in first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex problems. The goal of removing the condition number dependence from the number of full gradient calls is well-motivated and significant. The proposed EMGD algorithm and its analysis are presented clearly. However, the practical competitiveness of the method is questionable due to a strong dependence on κ in its stochastic complexity, and the paper misses a crucial comparison to highly related prior work.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significant Goal and Strong Theoretical Result:** The primary motivation—reducing the full gradient complexity from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε))—is compelling. For ill-conditioned problems where full gradients are the main bottleneck, this is a very desirable property. The paper provides a rigorous proof that EMGD achieves this, establishing linear convergence with a high-probability guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation guarantees common for stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA.

2.  **Clear Presentation and Algorithm:** The paper is very clearly written. The EMGD algorithm is easy to understand, and its components (epochs, mixed gradient, shrinking domain) are well-explained. The analysis, while technical, is laid out in a logical, step-by-step manner, making it relatively easy to follow the high-level argument.

3.  **Well-Articulated Advantages over Related Methods:** The authors do a good job of positioning their work. In Section 3.4, they clearly articulate potential advantages over SAG and SDCA, which are state-of-the-art for the finite-sum problem structure. These include the ability to handle general convex constraints (provided the projection is efficient), a low memory footprint (O(d) vs. O(nd)), and amenability to simple distributed computation for the full gradient step. These are important practical considerations.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection):**

1.  **Severe Dependence on κ for Stochastic Gradients:** The most significant weakness is the O(κ² log(1/ε)) complexity for stochastic gradients. While the paper successfully shifts the κ-dependence away from full gradients, it reappears quadratically in the stochastic part. For many problems, κ can be large. In the finite-sum setting (F(w) = (1/n)∑fᵢ(w)), state-of-the-art methods like SVRG, SAGA, and SDCA have complexities like O((n+κ)log(1/ε)). EMGD's total cost (assuming full gradient is n times costlier) is O((n + κ²)log(1/ε)). This means EMGD is only competitive with these methods when κ is very small (roughly κ < √n), a regime where the condition number is not a major issue to begin with. The paper's own comparison in Table 2 shows it is worse than SDCA unless κ ≤ n. The quadratic dependence significantly limits the algorithm's practical applicability compared to existing hybrid methods.

2.  **Missing Key Citations and Comparison (Originality):** The "mixed gradient" estimator, `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)`, is the core of the variance reduction technique used here. This is precisely the estimator used in the SVRG algorithm (Johnson & Zhang, "Accelerating Stochastic Gradient Descent using Predictive Variance Reduction", NIPS 2013). SVRG is a seminal paper in this area and its omission is a major oversight. While EMGD's overall structure with shrinking domains and a different epoch-based analysis is novel, the gradient estimator itself is not. This weakens the paper's claim to originality. A thorough comparison with SVRG is necessary, both in terms of the algorithm's structure and the resulting complexity bounds.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable, the claims about practical trade-offs and advantages cry out for empirical validation. An experimental section could demonstrate:
    *   In what regimes of n and κ does EMGD actually outperform AGD, SDCA, and SVRG?
    *   How sensitive is the algorithm to the choice of parameters, particularly T, which depends on a (usually unknown) κ?
    *   Does the high-probability bound translate into more stable performance in practice compared to methods with in-expectation guarantees?
    Without experiments, the significance of the proposed trade-off remains purely conceptual.

4.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The algorithm requires knowledge of the condition number κ (or L and λ) to set the number of inner iterations T. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation and suggest it as future work, but it is a significant practical barrier. While they suggest methods for estimating it, this adds another layer of complexity and potential for suboptimal performance if the estimate is poor.

**Final Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and elegant result on an important problem. The idea of trading a κ-dependence in full gradients for a stronger dependence in stochastic gradients is an interesting contribution to the optimization landscape. However, the quadratic dependence on κ in the stochastic complexity, combined with the lack of comparison to the highly relevant SVRG algorithm, significantly undermines the paper's claims of novelty and practical significance. While the identified advantages (handling constraints, low memory) are valid, they may not be enough to compensate for the potentially much higher computational cost in many common scenarios. The lack of experiments further makes it difficult to judge the real-world value of the proposed algorithm.

**Recommendation:** Borderline, leaning towards rejection. The paper has a solid theoretical core but needs to be significantly revised to properly position itself with respect to prior work (especially SVRG) and to provide a more convincing argument for its practical utility, ideally with empirical evidence.
