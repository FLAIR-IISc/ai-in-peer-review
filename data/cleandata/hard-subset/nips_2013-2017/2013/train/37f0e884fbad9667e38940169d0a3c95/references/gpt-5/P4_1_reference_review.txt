Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization using first-order methods with access to both (i) a full gradient oracle Og that returns ∇F(w) and (ii) a function oracle Of that samples a random function f with F(w) = E[f(w)] and L-smooth f. The goal is to reduce the number of expensive full gradients needed to reach ε-accuracy. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) proceeds in epochs: at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at a “reference” point w̄k, and within the epoch it performs T mixed-gradient steps using the estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k). Each step solves a projected proximal subproblem over a shrinking ball around w̄k. The analysis shows linear convergence: after m epochs, F(w̄m+1) − F(w*) ≤ λΔ1^2/2^{m+1} with high probability, using m calls to Og and mT calls to Of. Choosing T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)) yields O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

The mixed gradient estimator is the same variance-reduced gradient used in semi-stochastic/variance-reduction methods such as SVRG/Prox-SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and related methods (e.g., SAGA, Defazio et al., 2014), which also achieve linear convergence by computing one full gradient per epoch and then many low-cost variance-reduced steps. The paper compares to SAG and SDCA but does not discuss SVRG-style methods, which are highly relevant because they share the same estimator and memory footprint and typically yield O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) total gradient computations in finite-sum problems, i.e., O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient passes and O(κ) (not κ^2) stochastic steps per epoch. The projection onto a shrinking ball and the high-probability analysis are distinctive elements of this paper.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus: reducing dependence on the condition number for full-gradient evaluations in smooth, strongly convex optimization.
- Simple algorithmic structure: one full gradient per epoch; within-epoch steps use a variance-reduced estimator and a proximal update. Memory footprint is O(d).
- Handles constrained domains (via projection onto W ∩ ball), which is less explicit in some prior finite-sum methods.
- Provides a high-probability linear-convergence guarantee, not only in expectation.
- The analysis is self-contained and uses standard tools (smoothness, strong convexity, martingale concentration) and explains the role of domain shrinkage in variance control.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: The core idea—using g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄)—is identical to SVRG’s variance-reduced gradient. The paper does not cite or compare to SVRG/Prox-SVRG and closely related VR methods, where the number of full gradients is also O(log(1/ε)) and the number of stochastic gradients per epoch is O(κ), not O(κ^2). This omission significantly weakens the originality and positioning.
- Suboptimal complexity: The requirement T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) is substantially worse than the O(κ) inner-iteration complexity in SVRG/SAGA/SDCA (in various regimes), making EMGD less competitive in both theory and practice.
- Assumptions and definitions: The paper defines λ-strong convexity via the gradient difference norm lower bound ||∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)|| ≥ λ||w − w′||, which is stronger than the standard definition and not generally equivalent. In the proof, the inequality F(w) − F(w*) ≤ ⟨∇F(w), w − w*⟩ − λ||w − w*||^2 appears to miss the standard factor 1/2. These points should be corrected or justified carefully.
- Practicality: The algorithm requires knowing κ (to set T) and an initial radius Δ1 ≥ √(2/λ)(F(0) − F(w*)). Both are typically unknown. The paper mentions this as a drawback but does not provide an adaptive scheme (e.g., doubling tricks, adaptive epoch lengths, or line-search) to remove these requirements.
- Clarity and presentation: Some notation and equation numbering are inconsistent (reuse of label (2); “epoches” vs “epochs”; missing constants like 1/2 in the proximal term). These issues impede readability.

Quality
- The high-level proof technique is standard and plausible (variance control via shrinking radius, Azuma for martingale differences, epoch averaging). However, the nonstandard strong convexity assumption and the factor mismatch in key inequalities should be rectified. As stated, the correctness of constants and some steps is unclear.
- The complexity bound is weaker than current best-known results for the same estimator, suggesting either a loose analysis or the shrinkage/projection device introduces unnecessary κ factors.

Clarity
- The paper is generally structured well (algorithm, theorem, comparison, proof sketch). However, multiple presentation issues (typos, equation labels, and assumptions) should be addressed. A clearer statement of the oracle and finite-sum models and a unified comparison to SVRG/SAGA/SDCA would improve clarity.

Originality
- Limited. The mixed gradient estimator and one-full-gradient-per-epoch strategy are well known from SVRG-style methods. The main differences are the shrinking-ball projection and a high-probability bound, but these do not compensate for the missing comparisons and suboptimal rates.

Significance
- As presented, significance is limited due to suboptimal iteration complexity and missing positioning relative to SVRG/SAGA. The constrained setting and high-probability guarantee could be useful if accompanied by competitive complexity and stronger empirical or theoretical advantages.

Detailed suggestions
- Cite and compare to variance-reduction methods (SVRG/Prox-SVRG, SAGA) and clarify similarities/differences: estimator, memory, constraints/prox, rates (O(κ) vs O(κ^2)).
- Rework the analysis to match O(κ) inner complexity if possible (e.g., bounding the variance by function suboptimality without shrinking the domain).
- Use the standard strong convexity definition and correct constants (e.g., F(w) − F(w*) ≤ ⟨∇F(w), w − w*⟩ − (λ/2)||w − w*||^2).
- Provide an adaptive scheme for epoch lengths and step sizes that does not require knowing κ or Δ1, and discuss how to choose δ across epochs.
- Clarify the cost model for Of vs Og and provide empirical comparisons in finite-sum settings, including constrained problems, against SVRG/SAGA/SDCA.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important efficiency question: reducing the number of full gradients while retaining linear convergence.
- Handles constrained problems and uses only O(d) memory.
- High-probability convergence guarantee.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overlaps substantially with established variance-reduction methods; key related work is missing.
- Provides weaker complexity (O(κ^2) stochastic steps per epoch) than state-of-the-art O(κ).
- Technical assumptions and some inequalities are nonstandard or possibly incorrect.
- Requires prior knowledge of κ and Δ1; lacks adaptivity.

Recommendation
Given the novelty concerns, suboptimal complexity, and issues in assumptions/presentation, I do not recommend acceptance in its current form. Substantial revision is needed to correctly position the work relative to SVRG/SAGA, tighten the analysis to O(κ) inner iterations, and fix technical and clarity issues.
