Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies smooth, strongly convex optimization when both full gradients and stochastic gradients are available. It proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): at the start of each epoch, compute a full gradient at a snapshot point w̄, and in the epoch use a “mixed” gradient estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄), with updates constrained to a shrinking ball around w̄. The analysis shows that each epoch contracts the suboptimality by a constant factor with high probability, yielding linear convergence. Crucially, the number of full gradient evaluations is O(log 1/ε), independent of the condition number κ, while the number of stochastic gradient evaluations is O(κ^2 log 1/ε). The paper compares qualitatively with accelerated gradient methods (Nesterov), SAG, and SDCA.

The mixed gradient estimator and epoch structure are essentially the same as variance-reduced methods such as SVRG and Prox-SVRG (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013), which the paper does not cite. In the finite-sum setting F(w)= (1/n)∑ f_i(w), SVRG achieves linear convergence with total complexity O((n+κ) log 1/ε), uses O(d) memory, and requires O(log 1/ε) full gradient computations—also independent of κ. Compared to SAG/SDCA, SVRG likewise provides a clean memory footprint and works in proximal/constrained variants. The present analysis differs mainly in its use of shrinking trust regions and yields a weaker κ^2 dependence in the number of stochastic-gradient calls.

Strengths:
- Clear goal: reduce the dependence on κ in the number of full gradient evaluations, achieving linear convergence with O(log 1/ε) full gradients.
- The estimator g̃ centered at ∇F(w̄) is intuitive and known to reduce variance as w stays in a neighborhood of w̄.
- High-probability guarantees are presented (as opposed to only in expectation), using a martingale difference argument.
- The method requires only O(d) memory and, in principle, can handle constraints via projections (if projections and the shrinking-ball subproblems are efficient).

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is limited relative to existing variance-reduced stochastic methods. The estimator, epoch design, and O(log 1/ε) number of full gradient evaluations mirror SVRG. The paper does not cite or compare against SVRG, which is central prior work at NIPS.
- Suboptimal dependence on κ. The analysis requires T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)) stochastic steps per epoch, whereas SVRG-type analyses attain T = Θ(κ) (and constant step sizes) without the shrinking-ball constraint.
- Practicality and modeling: the general “function oracle” model together with a “full gradient oracle” is uncommon outside the finite-sum case. If F is given only via sampling (expectation), exact full gradients are generally unavailable. The paper’s main application is therefore the finite-sum case, where stronger results already exist.
- The algorithm requires prior knowledge of κ (and an initial radius ∆1), and uses a shrinking-domain projection, which complicates implementation. There are known adaptive strategies (e.g., doubling, restarts, or adaptive epoch length) in the variance-reduction literature that avoid this.
- Clarity and presentation: several typos/formatting issues; key complexities and assumptions could be more crisply stated; empirical validation is missing; some comparative claims (e.g., “much stronger” high-probability vs. expectation) are overstated without quantitative context.

Quality:
The proofs follow standard techniques (strong convexity, smoothness, gradient mapping, martingale bounds) but deliver weaker κ dependence than the state of the art and impose additional constraints (shrinking ball). The high-probability analysis is sound but could be sharpened using variance bounds standard in SVRG analyses to avoid the κ^2 factor.

Clarity:
Overall readable, but the exposition would benefit from:
- A clear statement up front of the per-epoch costs (1 full gradient + T stochastic gradients) and the final total complexity.
- A dedicated section positioning the method relative to SVRG/Prox-SVRG (NIPS 2013) and subsequent SAGA/SVRG+ variants, including differences in memory, constraints, and rates.
- Cleaning up notation and typos, and justifying the practicality of the shrinking-ball constraint and choice of η = 1/(L√T).

Originality:
Limited. The core estimator and epoch scheme are known; the claimed contribution (condition-number-independent number of full gradients) is already achieved by SVRG. The analysis with shrinking domains and high-probability bounds is not new enough to offset the overlap, especially given the κ^2 dependence.

Significance:
In its current form, the contribution is incremental and dominated in both theory and practice by SVRG-like methods. Without improved κ dependence, broader modeling scope, or new insights (e.g., non-Euclidean geometry, adaptive oracles, distributed guarantees with preserved rates), impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Cite and carefully compare with SVRG/Prox-SVRG (NIPS 2013) and SAGA, including complexities: O((n+κ) log 1/ε) vs. your O((n+κ^2) log 1/ε). Explain whether the shrinking-ball approach yields any advantages (e.g., stronger high-prob bounds), and if not, consider adopting the standard variance-reduction analysis to obtain T = O(κ) and a constant step size η = Θ(1/L), eliminating the κ^2 factor and the shrinking-ball constraint.
- Provide an adaptive scheme that avoids knowing κ, such as increasing epoch length or online tuning; analyze its convergence.
- Extend to composite/proximal settings and constraints with rigorous guarantees, or clarify the scope and computational cost of the constrained subproblem in Step 8.
- Discuss the practical availability of the full gradient oracle; in finite-sum problems, report total gradient computation counts and wall-clock time comparisons against SVRG, SAG, SDCA, and accelerated full-gradient methods on benchmarks.
- Strengthen the high-probability analysis using standard variance bounds for g̃ to achieve tighter constants and κ dependence.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear objective; simple algorithm; high-probability linear convergence proof; handles constraints; low memory.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overlaps substantially with SVRG; misses key citations; weaker κ dependence (κ^2 vs. κ); extra implementation burdens (shrinking ball, known κ); no experiments; modest practical impact.

Recommendation: Reject. With a thorough positioning relative to SVRG/SAGA, improved κ dependence, and empirical evidence, a revised version could be competitive.
