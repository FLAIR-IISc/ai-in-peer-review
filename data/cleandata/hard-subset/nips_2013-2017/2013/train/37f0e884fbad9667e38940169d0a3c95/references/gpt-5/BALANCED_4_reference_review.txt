Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies first-order methods for smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization, aiming to reduce the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations when stochastic gradients are also available. The setting assumes access to two oracles: a full-gradient oracle Og(w) = ∇F(w) and a “function” oracle Of that returns a random smooth function f with F(w) = E[f(w)], so stochastic gradients ∇f(w) are available. The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) runs in epochs; at the start of each epoch it computes one full gradient at a snapshot point w̄k, and within the epoch it performs projected updates using a “mixed” gradient g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k), i.e., a control-variate estimator whose variance depends on ∥w − w̄k∥. The algorithm shrinks a trust region radius per epoch and averages iterates. The main theorem shows that with T = Θ(κ2 log(1/δ)) stochastic steps per epoch (κ = L/λ), each epoch halves the error with high probability, yielding overall linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

Conceptually, EMGD is very close to variance-reduction methods now standard in stochastic optimization. In the finite-sum case F = (1/n)∑ fi, the mixed gradient is exactly the SVRG estimator (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013), which also computes one full gradient per epoch and achieves linear convergence. Related work includes SAG (Roux et al., NIPS 2012/13), SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014), SDCA (Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2013), MISO/Finito, and proximal/accelerated variants (e.g., Prox-SVRG, Katyusha). Compared to these, EMGD differs in the use of a shrinking ball constraint and a high-probability analysis under a general stochastic-function oracle (not necessarily finite-sum).

Strengths
- Clear goal: reduce dependence of the number of full gradients on κ; EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, independent of κ.
- Simple algorithmic structure: snapshot full gradient + control-variated stochastic gradients; easy to implement when both oracles exist.
- High-probability linear rate under smooth/strongly-convex assumptions; proof is self-contained and uses standard tools (martingale concentration, trust-region argument).
- Discussion of constrained problems and low memory footprint versus SAG/SDCA is useful; the high-probability (vs. in-expectation) claim is a potential advantage.
- The oracle model clarifies when occasional full gradients can be useful in expectation settings.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Complexity is suboptimal: the analysis yields O(κ2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient evaluations. SVRG/SAGA/SDCA analyses give O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) in finite-sum settings and O(κ log(1/ε)) dependence on the condition number, i.e., linear rather than quadratic in κ. The shrinkage-and-concentration proof here seems conservative; variance-reduction analyses typically avoid the extra κ factor without shrinking balls.
- The projection step is onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk). For general W, projection onto the intersection may be nontrivial; the paper defers this with a caveat (“provided Step 8 can be solved efficiently”). This is a significant practical limitation, especially compared to proximal/VR methods that need only prox/projection onto W.
- Assumes κ known to set T and step sizes. This is acknowledged as a drawback; no adaptive scheme (doubling trick, line-search, or online variance diagnostics) is provided.
- No experiments. Given many competing methods with strong practical performance, empirical evidence would help assess the proposed trade-offs (e.g., fewer full gradients vs. more stochastic steps, constrained settings, high-probability behavior).

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, but there are typographical issues, and some claims about related work are dated or too strong (e.g., “linear convergence only in expectation” for SAG/SDCA; high-probability variants exist in later literature).
- The computational model comparison would benefit from a precise accounting (wall-clock cost per iteration, projection cost onto the intersection, distributed full-gradient computation).

Originality
- The mixed gradient is essentially identical to SVRG’s estimator. The key novelty claimed—O(log(1/ε)) full gradients—is already achieved by SVRG in the finite-sum setting and, more broadly, by control-variate VR methods. The shrinking-domain device and high-probability analysis under the Of-oracle are the paper’s distinctive elements, but these do not appear to yield better complexity and may complicate implementation.

Significance
- As stated, the theoretical contribution is incremental relative to variance-reduction literature and has worse κ dependence. Without empirical validation or substantially improved theory (e.g., removing κ2), the impact is limited.
- The constrained-setting angle could be significant if the intersection projection is tractable and if EMGD demonstrably outperforms VR methods with proximal/projection steps.

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten the analysis to O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients per epoch. You likely can remove the shrinking ball by directly bounding the variance of the control-variate estimator and using strong convexity to ensure contraction, as in SVRG analyses.
- Provide an adaptive scheme that does not require κ, e.g., epoch lengths that grow geometrically, or a doubling schedule with statistical tests to detect sufficient decrease, or a line-search on the step size.
- Replace projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) with a proximal regularizer to w̄k (trust-region via a quadratic penalty) so each step reduces to a standard prox onto W.
- Add experiments against accelerated gradient, SVRG/SAGA/SDCA (and proximal variants) on both unconstrained and constrained problems, measuring full-gradient calls, stochastic-gradient calls, and wall-clock time.
- Update and nuance related-work discussion: include SVRG, SAGA, proximal/accelerated VR, and note available high-probability results; clarify where EMGD offers advantages (e.g., general stochastic-oracle setting with occasional full gradients, constrained problems).

Pros
- Simple algorithm; clear high-probability linear convergence.
- Reduces the number of full-gradient computations to O(log(1/ε)).
- Potential applicability to constrained problems and distributed/full-gradient scenarios.
- Low memory requirement.

Cons
- κ2 dependence in stochastic-gradient complexity is suboptimal.
- Not novel relative to SVRG-style variance reduction; mixed gradient is standard.
- Requires κ and a potentially difficult projection onto an intersection set.
- No empirical results; some comparative claims are incomplete.

Recommendation: Reject
Rationale: The core idea overlaps strongly with established variance-reduction methods, yet yields weaker complexity (κ2 vs. κ), adds a nontrivial projection requirement, and lacks empirical validation. With a tighter analysis (removing κ2), an adaptive scheme, clearer handling of constraints, and thorough experiments situating EMGD against modern VR baselines, the work could become competitive.
