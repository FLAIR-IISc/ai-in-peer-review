

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that reduces the number of full gradient evaluations required to achieve linear convergence. The authors address the limitation of standard first-order methods, which require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations for smooth strongly convex problems, by combining full and stochastic gradients. EMGD achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations (independent of condition number κ) and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient evaluations, providing a computational advantage when κ is large.

The paper relates to established work in convex optimization, including Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent (O(√κ log(1/ε)) for full gradients [17]), stochastic gradient descent methods (O(1/λε) for stochastic gradients [9,10,21]), and finite-sum optimization methods like SAG [22] and SDCA [23]. The key distinction is that EMGD leverages both full and stochastic gradients to bypass the general lower bound for first-order methods, which typically requires Ω(√κ) full gradient evaluations.

**Quality:** The paper provides a technically sound theoretical analysis. The proof of Theorem 1 is thorough and carefully constructed, with appropriate use of lemmas (including Lemma 1 on strongly convex functions) and inequalities. The authors carefully establish conditions for convergence (including the requirement T ≥ 1152L²/λ² ln(1/δ)) and provide a detailed proof of the convergence rate. The analysis correctly identifies the trade-off between full and stochastic gradient evaluations. However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims, which would strengthen the quality assessment.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to theoretical analysis. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented with sufficient detail, and the notation is consistent throughout. The comparison with existing methods (Table 2) effectively highlights the computational advantages of EMGD. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the mixed gradient approach enables the condition-number-independent convergence, but the technical presentation is clear and accessible to optimization researchers.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by proposing a method that combines full and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations. This is distinct from previous work that relies solely on full gradients (e.g., Nesterov [17]) or solely on stochastic gradients (e.g., SAG [22], SDCA [23]). The authors clearly articulate how EMGD differs from related work, particularly in its ability to work for both constrained and unconstrained problems, with lower storage requirements (O(d) vs. O(n) for SAG/SDCA), and with high-probability convergence guarantees. The approach is novel in the context of NIPS proceedings (recent NIPS papers on optimization include [22,23,26], but none with this specific combination of full/stochastic gradients).

**Significance:** The significance of the work lies in the potential computational savings for large-scale optimization problems where full gradient evaluations are expensive. The paper provides a clear analysis of when EMGD is advantageous compared to existing methods (when κ ≤ n^(2/3)), which is a meaningful contribution to the field. The method's applicability to both constrained and unconstrained problems, along with its lower storage requirements, adds practical value beyond the theoretical contribution. However, the paper's significance would be strengthened by empirical results demonstrating the practical advantages.

**Strengths:**
- Solid theoretical contribution with rigorous proof of convergence rate
- Clear distinction from related work, particularly in the context of finite-sum optimization
- Practical advantages over SAG/SDCA (storage requirements, applicability to constrained problems)
- Detailed computational complexity analysis with clear conditions for advantage
- High-probability convergence guarantee (superior to SAG/SDCA's expectation-based guarantee)

**Weaknesses:**
- Assumes condition number κ is known in advance, which is a practical limitation
- Lacks empirical validation to support theoretical claims
- Computational advantage (κ ≤ n^(2/3)) may not hold in all practical scenarios
- Does not address how to handle non-smooth problems or non-strongly convex cases

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
- Novel algorithm that addresses a practical problem in large-scale optimization
- Theoretical analysis is sound and well-executed
- Clear comparison with related work highlighting specific advantages
- Provides meaningful conditions under which the method is advantageous
- Addresses important limitations of existing approaches (storage, constrained problems)

**Con:**
- No empirical results to validate the theoretical claims
- Practical limitation of requiring known condition number
- Computational advantage is limited to specific regimes (κ ≤ n^(2/3))
- Lacks discussion of how to handle cases where the condition number is large

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to reducing the computational cost of optimization for smooth strongly convex problems by combining full and stochastic gradients. The main contribution is the development of EMGD, which achieves linear convergence with condition-number-independent full gradient access. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the lack of empirical validation and the practical limitation of requiring knowledge of the condition number are notable weaknesses. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding empirical results demonstrating the computational advantages in practice. Given the solid theoretical foundation and clear distinction from related work, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the empirical validation gap.
