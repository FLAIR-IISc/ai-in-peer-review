### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex functions, a setting where the optimal full-gradient methods have an iteration complexity of O(√κ log(1/ε)), with κ being the condition number. The authors identify that the dependence on κ can be computationally expensive for ill-conditioned problems, as it requires many full gradient evaluations. The central idea is to reduce the number of required full gradient computations by leveraging access to a stochastic gradient oracle.

The proposed algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), combines elements of epoch-based methods and variance-reduced stochastic gradients. The optimization is divided into epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed at the current solution iterate. Then, a fixed number of inner iterations are performed using a "mixed gradient," which is a variance-reduced estimator constructed from the initial full gradient and stochastic gradients at the current and initial iterates. This procedure is performed within a shrinking search domain. The authors prove that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate, requiring only O(log(1/ε)) full gradient computations and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient computations. This key result makes the number of expensive full gradient calls independent of the condition number κ. The paper compares this complexity to standard full-gradient, stochastic, and other variance-reduced methods like SAG and SDCA, and discusses qualitative advantages such as applicability to constrained problems and lower memory requirements.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem. Reducing the computational bottleneck of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned settings is highly relevant for large-scale machine learning. The goal of making the number of full gradient calls independent of κ is a strong and appealing motivation.

2.  **Sound Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a novel algorithmic structure and provides a rigorous theoretical analysis to back its claims. The proof, based on induction over epochs and using the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality to bound the stochastic terms, is clear and appears technically sound. The result—linear convergence with a κ-independent number of full gradient calls—is a strong theoretical statement.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, the related work is appropriately summarized, the algorithm is presented cleanly, and the analysis section outlines the proof strategy before diving into the details. This high level of clarity makes the paper's contribution accessible.

4.  **Thoughtful Comparison:** The authors provide a good discussion of how their method compares to related linear-convergent stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA. The identified advantages—handling constrained optimization, lower memory footprint (O(d) vs O(n)), and high-probability convergence guarantees (vs. in expectation)—are valid and important distinctions that add value to the contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Originality and Relation to SVRG:** The main weakness of the paper is its failure to cite and compare against the highly related Stochastic Variance Reduced Gradient (SVRG) method (Johnson and Zhang, NIPS 2013). The "mixed gradient" estimator `g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)` is precisely the gradient estimator used in SVRG, where `w̄k` serves as the "snapshot" point for the epoch. SVRG also achieves linear convergence and its analysis is quite similar in spirit. The novelty of EMGD therefore lies not in the gradient estimator itself, but in the specific algorithmic structure: the fixed number of inner-loop iterations `T` (dependent on κ²), the shrinking ball constraint, and the averaging step. The authors need to explicitly position their work relative to SVRG and clarify what advantages their specific epoch structure offers over the standard SVRG algorithm. Without this comparison, the originality of the core technique is overstated.

2.  **Practicality and Dependence on κ:** The algorithm's parameters, particularly the number of inner iterations `T`, depend directly on the condition number κ (specifically, `T` must be O(κ²)). In many practical applications, κ is unknown and can be difficult to estimate. The paper acknowledges this limitation in the conclusion but it is a significant practical barrier. While suggestions for estimating κ are mentioned, they are brief and may not apply generally. This makes the algorithm difficult to deploy "out-of-the-box" compared to methods that are less sensitive to such parameters.

3.  **Stochastic Gradient Complexity:** The stochastic gradient complexity of O(κ² log(1/ε)) is quite high. Other variance-reduced methods like SVRG and SAGA typically have complexities that scale with (n + κ) passes over the data (in the finite-sum setting), which is often much better than a κ² dependence, especially for large κ. The paper’s comparison in Table 2 shows EMGD is competitive only in a limited regime (e.g., κ ≤ n¹/² compared to SAG/SDCA). For moderately or highly ill-conditioned problems, the κ² term could make the algorithm prohibitively slow, negating the benefit of fewer full gradients.

4.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While this can be acceptable, the practical trade-offs discussed (fewer full gradients vs. more stochastic gradients, dependence on κ) would be much clearer with empirical validation. Even a simple experiment on a synthetic dataset with tunable κ would significantly strengthen the paper by demonstrating if and when the proposed algorithm outperforms baselines like AGD and SVRG in wall-clock time.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Addresses an important problem with a strong theoretical motivation.
*   The main result—achieving a κ-independent number of full gradient evaluations for linear convergence—is novel and significant.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and the theoretical analysis is clear and appears correct.
*   It highlights valuable advantages over some existing methods, such as its applicability to constrained problems.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   A critical piece of related work, SVRG, which uses the identical gradient estimator, is not cited or discussed. This significantly obscures the paper's true novelty.
*   The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ, which is a major practical limitation.
*   The complexity in terms of stochastic gradients (O(κ²)) is high and likely worse than state-of-the-art variance-reduced methods in many practical regimes.
*   The lack of any experimental results makes it hard to assess the practical value and trade-offs of the proposed method.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically sound and well-written contribution to an important area of optimization. The core result of achieving linear convergence with a number of full gradient evaluations that is independent of the condition number is compelling. However, the paper's contribution is significantly weakened by the omission of a comparison to SVRG, which uses the same core gradient estimator. Furthermore, the practical utility of the algorithm is questionable due to its dependence on knowing the condition number κ and its high O(κ²) stochastic gradient complexity.

While the theoretical idea is interesting, the current manuscript overstates its originality and does not adequately address its practical limitations. I would recommend rejection in its current form, but I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper to (1) thoroughly discuss and differentiate their work from SVRG, (2) provide a more convincing story about the parameter tuning (dependence on κ), and (3) ideally include some empirical results to validate their claims and compare against relevant baselines. With these changes, this could be a strong paper.
