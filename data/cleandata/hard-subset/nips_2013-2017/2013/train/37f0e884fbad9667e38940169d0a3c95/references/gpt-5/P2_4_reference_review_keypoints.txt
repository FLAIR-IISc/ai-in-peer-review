The paper proposes EMGD: epochal mixed-gradient descent using a control-variate estimator (∇F(w̄) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄)) with shrinking-ball projections to reduce full-gradient calls.

EMGD claims linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (high probability).

The mixed-gradient estimator and epoch structure are essentially the same as SVRG/SAGA control-variate methods, but SVRG/SAGA are not cited.

The analysis is technically sound (unbiased estimator, variance controlled by distance to anchor, uses smoothness/strong convexity/Azuma) but conservative.

The κ^2 dependence on stochastic gradients stems from the shrinking-ball contraction argument and is substantially worse than existing variance-reduction bounds (e.g., O(κ log(1/ε)) or O((n+κ) log(1/ε))).

No empirical evaluation is provided, which is a major omission given practical claims about memory, constraints, and runtime.

Implementation details are under-specified: cost of projecting onto W ∩ B(w̄; Δk), choice of initial radius Δ1, and reliance on knowing κ.

Writing has stylistic/typo issues and some equations/algorithm steps (projection) need clearer explanation and computational implications.

The paper mixes oracle and finite-sum cost models without clearly separating assumptions (e.g., full gradient cost vs stochastic).

Originality is limited: the estimator matches SVRG’s snapshot/control-variate; the only new element is the shrinking-ball projection proof yielding high-probability bounds.

Practical significance is limited by κ^2 stochastic complexity, requirement for shrinking projections, and lack of experiments to show advantages over SAG/SDCA/SVRG/SAGA.

Pros: clear goal of reducing full gradients, high-probability linear convergence, works with constraints, O(d) memory, simple algorithm.

Cons: missing key related work (SVRG/SAGA), worse κ dependence, no experiments, need for projections and known κ, and unclear Δ1 choice.

Suggestions: cite and compare to SVRG/SAGA, clarify differences, attempt to tighten κ dependence, add experiments (including constrained problems), discuss projection costs and alternatives, and remove dependence on knowing κ.

Recommendation: Weak reject — correct and interesting idea but overshadowed by prior variance-reduction methods, missing citations/experiments, and weaker stochastic complexity.
