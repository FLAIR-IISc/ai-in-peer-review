- Overall assessment: EMGD is a novel, theoretically sound algorithm that mixes full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with full-gradient calls independent of the condition number; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Core idea: Epoch-based mixing of full and stochastic gradients reduces full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)).  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on epoch-based methods and positions itself relative to deterministic, stochastic, and semi-stochastic approaches (e.g., Nesterov, SAG, SDCA).  
- Novelty: Combining epoch descent with strategic full/stochastic gradient mixing is a new approach addressing expensive full gradient evaluations.  
- Theoretical soundness: Rigorous analysis shows linear convergence and bounds on full and stochastic gradient evaluations; proofs appear correct.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, organized, and presents the algorithm and notation clearly.  
- Potential efficiency: Reducing κ-dependence for full gradients can improve performance on ill-conditioned large-scale problems.  
- Practical considerations: Authors note need to know κ and suggest estimation strategies and distributed approaches as future work.  
- Lack of empirical evaluation: No experiments are provided to demonstrate practical benefits or scaling with κ.  
- Insufficient comparisons: Table 2 and comparisons to SAG, SDCA, and other methods are superficial; more in-depth trade-off analysis needed.  
- Projection practicality: The shrinking-ball projection step may be costly in high dimensions and its implementation/efficiency is not discussed.  
- Parameter assumptions: Algorithm requires knowledge of L and λ; robustness to estimation errors is not analyzed.  
- Quality score: Paper is technically strong but weakened by lack of experiments (8/10).  
- Clarity score: Exceptionally clear and well-presented (9/10).  
- Originality score: Novel idea with solid grounding in existing concepts (8/10).  
- Significance score: Potentially impactful for ill-conditioned problems but practical impact unclear without experiments (7/10).  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, clear presentation, and potential for significant improvements in specific settings.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Missing empirical validation, limited implementation details, and superficial method comparisons.  
- Mandatory revisions suggested: Add experiments on benchmarks (especially high-κ), analyze projection cost and implementation, deepen comparisons with related methods, and discuss robustness to inaccurate L and λ estimates.
