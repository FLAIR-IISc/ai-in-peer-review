## Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm aiming to achieve linear convergence for strongly convex and smooth optimization problems by strategically combining full and stochastic gradient evaluations. The core idea is to reduce the dependency on the condition number κ typically present in full-gradient methods, a significant concern for large-scale datasets. The authors claim to achieve O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient complexity, independent of κ, while still leveraging the efficiency of stochastic gradients.

The motivation is sound. The trade-off between iteration complexity and per-iteration cost is a well-established consideration in optimization, and diminishing the dependence on κ is a desirable goal. The presented approach, building on epoch-based methods, is a reasonable direction to explore. However, the paper falls short of convincing me of the practical benefits and substantial novelty claimed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of reducing full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned problems.
*   **Logical Algorithm Design:** The EMGD algorithm, combining full and stochastic gradients within epochs, appears logically constructed. The connection to existing epoch-based methods is explicitly stated.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a theoretical convergence analysis, claiming linear convergence and a reduction in full gradient complexity.
*   **Comparison to Existing Work:** The related work section provides a reasonable overview of relevant literature, including SAG and SDCA.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** This is the most significant weakness. The paper *completely lacks* empirical validation. While a theoretical analysis is presented, it is insufficient to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of EMGD. No experiments are conducted to compare EMGD against standard methods (e.g., accelerated gradient descent, stochastic gradient descent with appropriate step size schedules) on benchmark datasets. Without empirical results, the claimed benefits remain purely hypothetical.
*   **Overstated Novelty:** The core idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients isn't entirely new. Semi-stochastic methods and similar hybrid approaches exist, as briefly mentioned in the related work. The authors need to more convincingly highlight the specific *distinctive* contribution of EMGD compared to these related approaches. The claim of independence from κ needs very strong justification, and I remain skeptical without demonstrating a clear advantage over existing techniques.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The analysis relies on knowing the condition number κ *a priori*.  As acknowledged by the authors, estimating κ can be a non-trivial task. The paper doesn’t address how to handle situations where κ is unknown or poorly estimated, significantly limiting its practical applicability.
*   **Algorithm 1 Clarity:** The notation within Algorithm 1 is dense and could benefit from clearer explanations. The purpose of the domain constraint in Step 8 is not immediately obvious and could be elaborated upon.
*   **Theorem 1 Conditions:** The conditions for Theorem 1, specifically regarding δ and ∆1, seem quite restrictive and might limit the algorithm's applicability in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically interesting idea, but its lack of empirical validation is a critical flaw. The theoretical claims, while potentially correct, are not supported by any experimental evidence. The novelty of the approach is also not sufficiently emphasized. Furthermore, the dependence on knowing the condition number beforehand raises practical concerns.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound analysis (though requiring validation).
*   Addresses a relevant problem in optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** This is a showstopper.
*   Overstated novelty.
*   Practical limitations regarding the condition number.
*   Restrictive theoretical conditions.



**Recommendation:** **Reject**.  

The paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. I would encourage the authors to conduct thorough experimental evaluation, compare EMGD against established baselines, and clearly demonstrate its advantages in practical scenarios.  A more detailed discussion of the algorithm's sensitivity to the condition number estimate would also be beneficial. Without these changes, the paper remains a theoretical exploration with limited practical impact.
