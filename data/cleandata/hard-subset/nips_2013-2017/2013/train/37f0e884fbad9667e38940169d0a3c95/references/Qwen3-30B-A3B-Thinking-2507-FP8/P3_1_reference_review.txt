

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The key insight is that by combining full gradients and stochastic gradients, the number of full gradient evaluations required for convergence can be reduced from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), making it condition number independent. The algorithm divides optimization into epochs, where each epoch uses one full gradient and O(κ²) stochastic gradients. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis proving linear convergence with these complexity bounds, and compare their approach with existing methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, and SDCA.

The paper fits well within the NIPS literature on optimization, building upon recent works on stochastic optimization (e.g., [22, 23] for SAG and SDCA) and the well-established theory of convex optimization [17]. It addresses a practical concern in large-scale machine learning where full gradient computation is expensive for large datasets.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The proof of Theorem 1 is detailed and well-structured, using standard techniques from convex optimization theory (e.g., strong convexity properties, martingale analysis for bounding stochastic terms). The authors carefully establish the convergence guarantees and provide explicit conditions for the parameters (T, η, etc.).

However, there are some notable limitations in the quality of the work:

1. **Lack of empirical validation**: The paper contains no experiments to validate the theoretical claims. Without empirical results, it's difficult to assess the practical performance of EMGD compared to other methods. In machine learning, theoretical guarantees need to be complemented with empirical evidence to demonstrate real-world utility.

2. **Condition number knowledge requirement**: The algorithm requires knowing the condition number κ in advance, which is a significant practical limitation. The authors acknowledge this as a drawback but do not provide a meaningful solution or analysis of how to handle this in practice. In many real-world scenarios, the condition number is unknown and must be estimated, which adds complexity.

3. **Computational cost analysis**: The paper compares computational complexity in terms of gradient evaluations but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the actual computational cost (e.g., time complexity) of stochastic gradient versus full gradient evaluations. The assumption that stochastic gradients are "significantly more efficient" needs more justification.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to theoretical analysis. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The algorithm pseudocode is clear and easy to follow.

However, some sections could benefit from improved clarity:

1. **Algorithm explanation**: The intuition behind the "mixed gradient" (g̃kt = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇fkt(wkt) - ∇fkt(w̄k)) could be better explained. Why does this specific combination work better than simply using stochastic gradients?

2. **Theoretical results**: While the proofs are technically sound, the paper could provide more intuition for why the convergence rate achieves the condition number independence. A high-level explanation of the mechanism behind the reduction in full gradient evaluations would enhance understanding.

3. **Comparison section**: The comparison with SAG and SDCA in Table 2 is useful but lacks context. The paper should clarify why the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3) is considered reasonable (as mentioned in Section 4.1), and provide more concrete examples of practical machine learning problems where this condition holds.

## Originality

The paper presents a novel approach to combining full and stochastic gradients for smooth strongly convex optimization. The specific algorithm (EMGD) and its theoretical analysis appear to be original contributions. The key insight of reducing full gradient evaluations to a condition number independent quantity is new and addresses a gap in the literature.

The paper properly situates itself within the existing literature, citing relevant works on stochastic optimization (e.g., [9, 10, 21, 22, 23, 26]) and convex optimization (e.g., [17, 18, 19]). The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous methods, particularly in the number of full gradient evaluations required.

However, the paper could better distinguish its contribution from other "semi-stochastic" methods [12, 13] that also combine full and stochastic gradients. The paper mentions these works in the related work section but doesn't clearly explain why EMGD is different or superior.

## Significance

The significance of the paper lies in the potential practical impact of reducing the number of full gradient evaluations, which is particularly valuable for large-scale machine learning problems where full gradient computation is expensive. The theoretical result that the number of full gradients can be made condition number independent is an important theoretical contribution.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by:

1. **Practical constraints**: The requirement to know κ in advance is a major practical limitation that may prevent real-world adoption.

2. **Limited empirical validation**: Without experiments, it's difficult to assess whether the theoretical improvements translate to practical speedups.

3. **Comparison with existing methods**: The paper claims EMGD has advantages over SAG and SDCA (e.g., handling constrained optimization, lower storage requirements), but these claims are theoretical. It would be valuable to see whether these advantages hold in practice.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees
- Novel insight about reducing full gradient evaluations to a condition number independent quantity
- Well-written and well-organized paper
- Clear comparison with relevant existing methods
- Properly contextualized within the convex optimization literature

**Weaknesses:**
- No empirical validation of the theoretical claims
- Requires knowing the condition number κ in advance, which is a significant practical limitation
- Limited discussion of how to estimate κ in practice
- Lacks detailed comparison of actual computational cost (not just gradient evaluations)
- The "mixed gradient" approach could be better motivated with more intuition

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following caveats:

1. The paper presents a theoretically sound contribution that advances the understanding of convex optimization. The reduction of full gradient evaluations to a condition number independent quantity is an important theoretical result.

2. However, the lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness for a machine learning conference like NIPS. Theoretical results need to be complemented with empirical evidence to demonstrate practical utility.

3. The requirement to know κ in advance is a major practical limitation that should be addressed or at least discussed in more depth. The authors acknowledge this as a drawback but don't provide a meaningful solution.

4. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding a small set of experiments comparing EMGD with other methods on standard machine learning problems (e.g., logistic regression, SVMs) to validate the theoretical claims.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add empirical results**: Include experiments on standard machine learning datasets comparing EMGD with Nesterov's method, SAG, and SDCA. Measure both the number of full gradient evaluations and the actual wall-clock time to reach a given accuracy.

2. **Address the κ estimation problem**: Expand Section 5 to discuss practical approaches for estimating κ. The paper mentions using the Hessian from a subset of functions, but this needs more detail and analysis of the trade-offs involved.

3. **Improve the intuition for the mixed gradient**: Add a paragraph explaining why the specific combination of full and stochastic gradients in the mixed gradient leads to the condition number independence.

4. **Clarify the computational cost analysis**: Provide a more detailed comparison of the actual computational cost (time complexity) of stochastic gradient versus full gradient evaluations, not just the number of evaluations.

5. **Expand the comparison with semi-stochastic methods**: Better distinguish EMGD from existing semi-stochastic approaches [12, 13] and explain why EMGD's approach is superior for the problem at hand.

## Conclusion

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the field of convex optimization by showing how to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations for smooth strongly convex problems to a condition number independent quantity. The technical analysis is sound, and the algorithm is well-designed. However, the lack of empirical validation and the requirement to know the condition number in advance significantly limit the paper's practical impact. With the suggested improvements, particularly adding empirical results and addressing the κ estimation problem, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, it's a solid theoretical paper that would benefit from more practical validation.
