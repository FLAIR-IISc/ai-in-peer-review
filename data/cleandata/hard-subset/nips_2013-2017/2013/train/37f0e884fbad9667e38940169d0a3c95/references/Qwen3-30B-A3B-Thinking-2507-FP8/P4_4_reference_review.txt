

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that reduces the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. The key insight is to combine full gradients with stochastic gradients in a way that maintains linear convergence while significantly reducing the number of expensive full gradient computations. The authors relate their work to previous NIPS contributions such as Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent [17], SAG [22], and SDCA [23], noting that EMGD differs by using a combination of full and stochastic gradients rather than relying solely on one or the other.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound theoretical analysis. The authors provide a rigorous proof of the convergence rate (Theorem 1) showing that EMGD achieves linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The analysis carefully handles the stochastic component using martingale difference sequences and Hoeffding-Azuma inequality. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the need for prior knowledge of κ, and suggests future work on estimating κ. However, the absence of experimental results to validate the theoretical claims is a notable weakness, as the practical benefits of the method remain untested.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is described with sufficient detail, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The authors effectively relate their approach to prior work, making it easy to understand the novelty. The convergence rate analysis is presented logically, with clear definitions and lemmas. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why the domain size shrinks by a factor of √2 each epoch, but the overall presentation is of high quality.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by introducing a novel algorithm that combines full and stochastic gradients in a way that achieves condition number independent full gradient access. This differs from previous work that either uses only full gradients (e.g., Nesterov's method) or only stochastic gradients (e.g., SAG, SDCA). The authors properly contextualize their work within the NIPS literature, citing relevant prior contributions and clearly articulating how EMGD advances the state of the art. The paper's novelty lies in the specific mechanism for combining gradient types to achieve the desired complexity.

**Significance**: The results have significant practical implications for large-scale optimization problems where full gradient evaluations are computationally expensive. The paper demonstrates that EMGD can be more computationally efficient than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is a reasonable condition in many machine learning applications. The authors also highlight important practical advantages: EMGD works for both constrained and unconstrained problems, requires only O(d) storage space (compared to Ω(n) for SAG/SDCA), and achieves high-probability convergence (unlike SAG/SDCA which only guarantee convergence in expectation). These aspects make the work potentially valuable for practitioners.

**Strengths**:
1. Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees
2. Novel approach combining full and stochastic gradients
3. Comprehensive comparison with relevant prior work
4. Practical advantages over existing methods (constraints, storage, convergence probability)
5. Clear acknowledgment of limitations and future work

**Weaknesses**:
1. Requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ, which is often unknown in practice
2. Lacks experimental validation to support theoretical claims
3. Computational complexity analysis shows EMGD may not be optimal for large κ (κ > n^(1/2))
4. Limited comparison with recent methods like SVRG and SAGA that also aim to reduce full gradient evaluations

**Pro/Con for Acceptance**:

Pro:
- Introduces a novel algorithm that achieves significant theoretical improvements in full gradient access complexity
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with clear convergence guarantees
- Offers practical advantages over existing methods in several important aspects
- Well-written and clearly contextualized within the literature

Con:
- Requires knowledge of κ, which is a significant practical limitation
- No experimental results to validate theoretical claims
- Computational complexity analysis suggests EMGD may not be optimal for many practical scenarios

**Conclusion**: This paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the field of convex optimization with clear advantages over existing methods in specific scenarios. While the requirement for prior knowledge of κ is a notable limitation, the paper's theoretical rigor and the practical benefits it highlights (constraints, storage, convergence probability) make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The lack of experimental results is a drawback, but the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance, especially given the importance of the problem domain. The paper addresses a meaningful challenge in optimization that has practical implications for large-scale machine learning applications.
