Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies first-order methods for minimizing a smooth, λ-strongly convex function F over a convex set W, with access to two oracles: (i) a full-gradient oracle Og(w)=∇F(w) and (ii) a function oracle Of that samples a smooth function f with F(w)=E[f(w)]. The goal is to reduce the number of expensive full-gradient evaluations while retaining linear convergence.

The proposed Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) proceeds in epochs. At the start of epoch k, it computes a full gradient at a reference point w̄k. Within the epoch, at each iteration it samples f and forms a “mixed” gradient g̃=∇F(w̄k)+∇f(wt)−∇f(w̄k), then performs a proximal gradient step constrained to a shrinking Euclidean ball around w̄k. With T=Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) iterations per epoch (κ=L/λ), the method halves the error per epoch with high probability, yielding O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls to reach ε-suboptimality.

This mixed gradient estimator is identical to the one used in variance-reduced methods such as SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013). That literature (SAG, SDCA, SVRG, and later SAGA, SARAH/SPIDER, Catalyst) achieves linear convergence on finite sums F=1/n∑fi with complexities typically O((n+κ)log(1/ε)), requiring O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient computations (one per epoch) and O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient steps. The paper cites SAG and SDCA but does not discuss SVRG/SAGA, which is a significant omission because EMGD’s estimator and epoch structure closely mirror SVRG while having a worse κ dependence and an additional shrinking-ball constraint.

Strengths
- Clear objective: reduce the number of full-gradient evaluations to O(log(1/ε)) for smooth, strongly convex problems.
- Simple algorithmic structure; the analysis is based on standard smooth/strongly convex inequalities and a martingale concentration bound, resulting in a high-probability linear rate.
- Works with constraints (via projection onto W∩B(w̄k;Δk)) and requires only O(d) memory, which can be advantageous relative to methods that store per-sample gradients.
- Discussion of distributed computation for full gradients is sensible.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: The mixed gradient g̃=∇F(w̄)+∇f(wt)−∇f(w̄) is the SVRG estimator. EMGD’s epoch design with one full gradient per epoch parallels SVRG; however, EMGD imposes a shrinking trust region and requires T=Ω(κ^2), yielding O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients, which is weaker than SVRG/SAGA’s O(κ log(1/ε)) for similar assumptions. The paper neither cites nor compares to SVRG/SAGA.
- Assumptions and scope: The two-oracle model allows an exact full gradient at chosen points. In finite-sum problems this is a full pass over data; the paper’s complexity discussion partly abstracts away that cost and thus may be optimistic relative to realistic implementations.
- Practicality: The shrinking-ball projection can be burdensome for general W and introduces an extra parameter Δk that depends on κ; the method requires prior knowledge (or estimation) of κ to set T and step sizes.
- Clarity and rigor: Several typographical inconsistencies (e.g., ln δ vs ln(1/δ), missing 1/2 in the proximal term in the update), and some steps in the proof would benefit from clearer exposition (e.g., feasibility of w* in the shrinking ball, conditions ensuring this across epochs). Comparisons with SAG/SDCA contain statements that are too categorical (e.g., high-probability guarantees exist for some variance-reduced methods).

Quality
- The high-level proof strategy (variance control via smoothness and domain shrinkage; Azuma for the noise term; epoch-wise contraction) is standard and appears sound, though some constants and conditions should be corrected and clarified. The requirement T=Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)) is conservative relative to known results.
- No experiments are provided to validate practical performance or the impact of the ball constraint.

Clarity
- The paper is readable but hampered by notational/typographical issues and missing references. The algorithm would benefit from a precise statement of the proximal update (including the 1/2 factor), explicit assumptions ensuring w* remains in W∩B(w̄k;Δk), and a clean presentation of parameter choices (η, T, Δ1) without sign errors in logs.

Originality
- Limited. The core estimator and epoch structure coincide with SVRG; the main differentiator is the shrinking trust region and a high-probability analysis, but these lead to worse κ dependence and added constraints. The absence of a comparison to SVRG/SAGA is a significant gap.

Significance
- The contribution is incremental and, in its current form, weaker than established variance-reduced methods in both theory (κ^2 vs κ) and practicality (extra projections, κ knowledge). The claimed advantage of κ-independent full-gradient calls already holds for SVRG/SAGA.

Pros for acceptance
- High-probability linear convergence analysis with a simple method.
- Applicability to constrained settings and low memory footprint.

Cons for acceptance
- Overlaps strongly with SVRG’s estimator and epoch design; missing key references.
- Worse dependence on κ and additional algorithmic burden (shrinking-ball projections, κ knowledge).
- No empirical validation; clarity issues and minor technical inaccuracies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate EMGD within the variance-reduction literature (SVRG, SAGA, SARAH, Catalyst), and provide theoretical and empirical comparisons.
- Strengthen the analysis to reduce κ^2 to κ without the shrinking-ball constraint (as in SVRG), or justify when the ball constraint yields a practical advantage (e.g., constrained problems where projection onto W∩ball is easy).
- Remove or reduce dependence on prior κ knowledge (e.g., adaptive epoch lengths, line search, or restarting schemes).
- Clarify and correct technical details (log signs, proximal update, feasibility of w* across epochs), and add experiments on standard benchmarks.

Recommendation
- Reject. The methodological overlap with known variance-reduced techniques, weaker theoretical dependence on κ, and missing comparisons limit novelty and significance for NeurIPS at this time.

Confidence
- High.
