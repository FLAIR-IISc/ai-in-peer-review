Summary
The paper studies first-order optimization for smooth and strongly convex objectives and proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an algorithm that interleaves infrequent full-gradient computations with many stochastic-gradient steps per epoch. At each epoch k, EMGD computes a snapshot full gradient at w̄k and then uses a variance-reduced “mixed gradient” g̃ = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(w) − ∇f(w̄k) while constraining iterates to a shrinking ball around w̄k. With a carefully chosen step size and number of inner iterations T, the method reduces suboptimality by a constant factor per epoch, yielding a linear convergence rate. The main claimed benefit is that the number of full-gradient evaluations is O(log(1/ε)), independent of the condition number κ = L/λ, while the number of stochastic gradients is O(κ^2 log(1/ε)). The analysis provides high-probability guarantees using martingale bounds.

Relation to prior work
- Deterministic smooth/strongly convex optimization: Nesterov’s accelerated methods achieve O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients.
- Purely stochastic methods: for strongly convex objectives, O(1/(λ ε)) iteration complexity; typically polynomial in 1/ε, not linear.
- Finite-sum variance-reduction methods: SAG and SDCA achieve linear convergence with complexities proportional to (n + κ) log(1/ε), with trade-offs in storage and duality.
Crucially, the mixed gradient g̃ used here is essentially the SVRG estimator (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013): ∇f_i(w) − ∇f_i(ŵ) + ∇F(ŵ), with one full gradient per epoch and many stochastic steps, leading to O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O((n + κ) log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. The paper does not cite or compare to SVRG (or proximal variants), which is a major omission. Relative to SVRG, the proposed method imposes a shrinking-ball projection and yields a worse dependence on κ (κ^2 vs. κ) for the number of stochastic gradients per epoch.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant goal: reducing dependence on κ for costly full-gradient evaluations by mixing in stochastic steps.
- High-probability linear convergence guarantee, with explicit constants.
- Discussion of practical aspects: low memory footprint compared to SAG, applicability to constrained domains, and potential for distributed full-gradient computation.
- Proof structure is transparent: variance control via bounded domain, martingale difference bounding, and epoch-wise contraction.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Missing comparison to SVRG, which uses the same gradient estimator and already achieves O(log(1/ε)) full gradients with O(κ) stochastic gradients per epoch. The presented κ^2 dependence is strictly worse than the state of the art.
- No empirical evaluation. Claims about practical superiority (memory, constrained settings, distributed computation) are not substantiated experimentally.
- Technical assumptions are imprecise in places. The paper defines strong convexity via the nonstandard inequality ‖∇F(w) − ∇F(w′)‖ ≥ λ‖w − w′‖, which is stronger than standard strong convexity and not equivalent in general. Yet later parts of the proof use the standard strong convexity inequality on function values; the mismatch should be corrected.
- The shrinkage-by-√2 ball constraint each epoch is unusual and may be unnecessary; modern analyses obtain variance reduction without such constraints and with better κ dependence.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but there are several notational inconsistencies and equation mislabels (e.g., reference to (4) where a different inequality is used), and occasional typos.
- Algorithm description omits practical guidance on solving the constrained subproblem efficiently for general W, and how to set/estimate κ (acknowledged as a limitation).

Originality
- Limited. The core idea and estimator mirror SVRG. The novelty appears to be the shrinking-ball projection and a high-probability analysis, but these come with worse κ dependence and are close in spirit to existing epoch-based analyses. The paper also reiterates known advantages (low memory, constrained applicability) that Prox-SVRG and related methods already address.

Significance
- The stated contribution (“condition number independent access of full gradients”) is already achieved by SVRG and related methods. Given the inferior stochastic-gradient complexity and lack of experiments, the impact is limited. High-probability guarantees can be valuable, but should be paired with competitive complexity and proper comparisons.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Cite and compare to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) and its proximal variants. Discuss similarities/differences explicitly, including storage, applicability to constraints/composite objectives, and convergence in expectation vs. with high probability.
- Improve κ dependence. Consider adopting SVRG-style analysis with constant step size and T = Θ(κ) inner iterations per epoch to remove the κ^2 factor. The shrinking-ball device might be unnecessary; bounding variance via smoothness and snapshot variance reduction often suffices.
- Correct the definition of strong convexity and ensure all inequalities are used consistently. Use the standard inequality F(y) ≥ F(x) + ⟨∇F(x), y − x⟩ + (λ/2)‖y − x‖^2, or strong monotonicity ⟨∇F(x) − ∇F(y), x − y⟩ ≥ λ‖x − y‖^2.
- Provide experiments on standard finite-sum problems (logistic regression, ridge regression) comparing EMGD to Nesterov, SAG, SDCA, SVRG/Prox-SVRG. Report wall-clock time, passes over data, number of full gradients, and memory.
- On practicalities: discuss how to implement the projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) for typical W, and whether the ball constraint hampers progress in ill-conditioned directions. Consider replacing it with standard proximal steps.
- The dependence on knowing κ is restrictive. Explore adaptive step-size and inner-loop length selection (e.g., doubling strategies) and whether your high-probability analysis can accommodate them.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem (variance-reduced stochastic methods).
- Provides a high-probability linear rate with simple analysis.
- Highlights practical aspects (low memory, constrained settings).

Cons for acceptance
- Overlaps strongly with SVRG without citation; key novelty claim is not new.
- Inferior complexity in κ (κ^2 vs. κ) and lacks empirical validation.
- Some technical imprecision and clarity issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the goal is important, the main idea is essentially known (SVRG), the complexity is worse than existing methods, and the paper lacks critical comparisons and experiments. With a revised analysis achieving κ-level inner complexity, proper positioning relative to SVRG/Prox-SVRG, corrected assumptions, and empirical support, the work could be competitive. Confidence: high.
