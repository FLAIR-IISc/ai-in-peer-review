This paper introduces a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), designed to reduce the computational cost of full gradient evaluations in strongly convex and smooth optimization problems. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, aiming to achieve a convergence rate independent of the condition number κ. The authors demonstrate a linear convergence rate and provide a theoretical analysis supporting their claims.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant problem:** Reducing the cost of full gradient evaluations is crucial for large-scale optimization, particularly in machine learning where data is abundant. The motivation is clearly articulated.
* **Novel approach:** The combination of epoch-based learning with mixed gradient descent (full and stochastic) presents a genuinely novel approach. It’s not merely a straightforward application of existing techniques.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, culminating in a convergence theorem demonstrating a logarithmic dependence on 1/ǫ, independent of the condition number. This is a significant contribution.
* **Clear presentation of algorithm:** Algorithm 1 is presented in a clear and concise manner, making it easy to understand the proposed method.
* **Comparison to existing methods:** The paper includes a reasonable comparison to related work (SAG, SDCA, Nesterov’s method), highlighting the trade-offs and potential benefits of EMGD.
* **Discussion of practical considerations:** The acknowledgement of the need to estimate the condition number κ in practice, and suggestions for how to do so, demonstrates an awareness of real-world applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Condition number assumption:** The proposed method relies on the assumption that stochastic gradients are efficiently computable. While stated, a deeper discussion of scenarios where this assumption *doesn't* hold, and the resulting performance implications, would be beneficial.
* **Constant factors:** While the logarithmic dependence on 1/ǫ is established, the constant factors hidden within the O(log 1/ǫ) notation are not discussed. These constants can significantly impact practical performance, and a more detailed analysis or empirical evaluation might be warranted.
* **Limited empirical validation:** The paper lacks any empirical results. While the theoretical analysis is strong, it's essential to demonstrate the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets and compare it to existing methods in practice. This is a major omission.
* **Complexity comparison caveats:**  The comparison in Table 2 relies on assumptions about the relative cost of full vs. stochastic gradients. A more nuanced discussion about how the cost comparison impacts the practical advantages of EMGD would be valuable.  The claim that EMGD isn’t necessarily the *best* but has advantages is a bit weak without supporting data.
* **Oracle definitions:** While the oracle definitions are standard, clarifying the computational cost associated with each oracle call would strengthen the analysis. Specifically, how does the cost of `Of` relate to the cost of a single component of the gradient in SAG/SDCA?

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and the algorithm is well-defined. However, the lack of empirical validation weakens the overall assessment.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly establishes the motivation and contributions. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the theoretical derivations are reasonably well-explained.

**Originality:** The approach of combining epoch-based learning with mixed gradient descent is novel and represents a genuine contribution to the field.  It’s a thoughtful combination of existing ideas.

**Significance:** If the theoretical results translate to practical performance improvements, this work could be significant. Reducing the dependence on full gradients is highly desirable in many applications. However, the lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the contribution.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound algorithm for a relevant problem.
* Strong theoretical guarantees with a linear convergence rate independent of the condition number.
* Clear and concise presentation of the algorithm and analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* Dependence on assumptions (efficient stochastic gradients) without extensive discussion of limitations.
* Constant factors in the convergence rate are not analyzed.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection** at this time. While the paper presents a promising theoretical contribution, the lack of empirical validation is a significant drawback. NIPS papers are expected to demonstrate not only theoretical soundness but also practical relevance, and this paper falls short on that front. 

**Suggestions for improvement:**

* **Prioritize empirical evaluation:** Conduct experiments on relevant datasets to demonstrate the performance of EMGD and compare it to state-of-the-art methods.
* **Analyze constant factors:** Investigate the constant factors hidden within the O(log 1/ǫ) notation and discuss their impact on practical performance.
* **Address limitations:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the algorithm, particularly in scenarios where the stochastic gradient assumption doesn't hold.
* **Refine complexity comparison:** Provide a more thorough and nuanced comparison of EMGD to other methods, considering different cost models and problem settings.
* **Consider a discussion on hyperparameter tuning:** How sensitive is the performance to the choice of step size, number of epochs, etc.?
