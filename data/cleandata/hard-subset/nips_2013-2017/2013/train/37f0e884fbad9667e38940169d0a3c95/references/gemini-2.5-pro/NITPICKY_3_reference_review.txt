**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing smooth and strongly convex functions, where first-order methods typically require a number of full gradient evaluations dependent on the condition number κ. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle. The core idea is to structure the optimization into epochs. At the start of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed. Within the epoch, a series of updates are performed using a variance-reduced stochastic gradient estimator, which combines the initial full gradient with stochastic gradient information. The main theoretical result is that EMGD can find an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients. This makes the number of expensive full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ, a significant theoretical departure from classical methods like Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent. The paper also discusses advantages over related methods like SAG and SDCA, such as applicability to constrained problems and lower memory requirements.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Important Goal:** The objective of reducing the dependency on the condition number κ for the number of full gradient calls is well-motivated and significant. For large-scale problems where κ is large and full gradients are computationally prohibitive, such an approach could, in principle, offer substantial practical benefits.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** The paper provides a non-asymptotic linear convergence guarantee that holds with high probability, which is a stronger result than guarantees in expectation that are common in the stochastic optimization literature (e.g., for SAG and SDCA). The analysis, based on an inductive argument over epochs, appears sound.

3.  **Generality:** Unlike many recent methods that achieve linear convergence (e.g., SAG, SDCA, SVRG), the proposed algorithm is not restricted to the finite-sum setting of Equation (1). It operates under the more general expectation setting F(w) = E[f(w)]. Furthermore, its formulation naturally handles constrained optimization problems, provided the projection in Step 8 is efficient.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a conceptually interesting idea, it suffers from a substantial number of flaws, ranging from significant methodological issues to minor but numerous presentational inconsistencies.

1.  **Poor Positioning and Missing Relation to Variance Reduction:** The "mixed gradient" defined in Step 7, `g̃kt = ∇F (w̄k) +∇fkt (wkt )−∇fkt (w̄k)`, is precisely the gradient estimator used in the SVRG algorithm [Johnson and Zhang, "Accelerating Stochastic Gradient Descent using Predictive Variance Reduction", NIPS 2013]. The authors fail to cite or discuss this seminal work. This is a critical omission. The proposed EMGD algorithm can be viewed as a variant of SVRG, where each epoch of EMGD corresponds to an outer loop of SVRG. This lack of connection to the established variance reduction literature significantly misrepresents the originality of the proposed technique.

2.  **Severe Dependence on the Condition Number:** The primary contribution is reducing the number of full gradients to be independent of κ. However, this comes at the cost of requiring `T = O(κ²) ` stochastic gradients per epoch. This dependence is extremely severe. For comparison, SVRG for finite-sum problems requires O(κ) or O(κ log κ) inner iterations, and standard SGD requires O(1/ε) iterations total. An O(κ²) complexity for the inner loop makes the algorithm impractical for anything but mildly ill-conditioned problems. The very large constant factor (`1152` in the analysis for T) further compounds this issue.

3.  **Impracticality of Hyperparameters and Assumptions:**
    *   The algorithm requires knowledge of L and λ (and thus κ) to set the number of inner iterations `T`. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but it remains a major practical barrier.
    *   The assumption on the initial domain size, `∆1 ≥ max(√2/λ * (F(0) - F(w*)))`, requires knowledge of the optimal function value `F(w*)`, which is unknown. This makes the theoretical guarantee difficult to ground in practice.

4.  **Clarity and Methodological Details:**
    *   The averaging in Step 10, `w̄k+1 = 1/(T+1) * Σ_{t=1}^{T+1} w_t^k`, is unusual as it includes the initial point of the epoch `w_1^k = w̄^k`. The rationale for this specific averaging scheme is not provided, making it seem arbitrary. Standard analyses of similar methods typically average only the iterates `w_t` for `t > 1` or simply take the final iterate.
    *   In Footnote 1, the claim that SDCA requires each `fi` to be strongly convex seems to be an oversimplification. The original SDCA analysis handles the case where only the sum `F(w)` is strongly convex. This should be stated more carefully.

5.  **Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
    *   The notation `1ǫ` is used throughout the paper instead of the standard `1/ε`. This is unconventional and reduces readability.
    *   The use of superscripts for the epoch index and subscripts for the iteration index is standard, but the notation `wkt` is ambiguous. It should be `w_t^k` for clarity.
    *   The provided reference list is incomplete. Many citations in the text (e.g., [1], [2], [4], [6], [7], [8], [25], [26], etc.) are missing from the bibliography. This reflects a significant lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
    *   The abstract states `O(κ^2)` stochastic gradients are evaluated *at each epoch*, but the final complexity is stated as `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))` total. This is correct, but could be phrased more clearly to avoid momentary confusion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces the valuable goal of decoupling the number of full gradient evaluations from the condition number in a general stochastic setting.
*   It provides a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is theoretically stronger than guarantees in expectation.
*   The method is applicable to constrained problems, unlike many competing variance-reduced methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The failure to position the work relative to SVRG is a major scientific oversight.
*   The O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients is severe and likely renders the algorithm impractical.
*   Key parameters of the algorithm depend on unknown problem constants (κ, F(w*)).
*   The manuscript contains numerous presentational and formatting errors, including an incomplete reference list, which suggests a rushed preparation.

**Decision**

I recommend **rejection**. While the high-level goal is commendable, the paper is undermined by a critical failure to acknowledge and build upon the closely related SVRG literature. This misattributes the novelty of the core gradient estimation technique. Furthermore, the severe O(κ²) dependence on stochastic gradients, combined with the reliance on unknown problem parameters, calls the practical significance of the proposed algorithm into serious question. The accumulation of these major weaknesses, along with numerous minor clarity and formatting issues, prevents me from recommending acceptance. The authors have identified an interesting direction, but the work requires a fundamental reframing and a more realistic analysis of its computational trade-offs before it meets the standards of NIPS.
