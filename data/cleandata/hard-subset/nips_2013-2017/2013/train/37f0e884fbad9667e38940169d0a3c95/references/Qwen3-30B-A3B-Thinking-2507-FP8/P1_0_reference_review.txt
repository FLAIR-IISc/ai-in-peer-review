

# Peer Review: "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth and strongly convex optimization problems. The key insight is that by combining full gradients and stochastic gradients, the algorithm can achieve linear convergence with a number of full gradient accesses that is independent of the condition number κ, specifically O(log(1/ε)) instead of the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)).

The authors build upon the epoch gradient descent framework and introduce a "mixed gradient" that combines a full gradient (computed once per epoch) with stochastic gradients. This mixed gradient approach allows them to achieve a constant reduction in optimization error per epoch, leading to linear convergence. The algorithm is theoretically analyzed and shown to require O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

The paper relates to previous work in several ways:
- It extends the epoch gradient descent method [9]
- It builds upon the SAG and SDCA methods for optimization of finite sums [22, 23]
- It addresses the gap between deterministic and stochastic optimization methods

The authors provide a comprehensive comparison with existing methods, showing that EMGD has advantages in terms of storage requirements, applicability to constrained optimization, and high-probability convergence guarantees, though it may not always have the lowest computational complexity.

## Quality Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution. The algorithm design is well-motivated, and the convergence proof is rigorous. The authors carefully establish the necessary conditions (smoothness, strong convexity) and provide a detailed proof of the convergence rate using standard techniques from convex optimization (martingale inequalities, strong convexity properties).

The main theoretical claim (Theorem 1) is well-supported by the analysis. The proof follows a standard induction approach and carefully bounds the key terms in the convergence analysis. The authors also properly acknowledge limitations, particularly the requirement to know κ in advance.

However, the paper has a significant weakness: it lacks any experimental validation. While theoretical contributions are valuable, machine learning conferences like NIPS typically expect empirical results to support theoretical claims, especially when discussing computational complexity and practical advantages. Without experiments, it's difficult to assess how the algorithm performs in practice compared to existing methods.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-structured and generally clear. The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivation. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise, and the convergence analysis is logically presented. The notation is consistent throughout the paper.

The related work section is comprehensive and properly contextualizes the paper within the literature. The comparison with existing methods (Table 2) is particularly helpful for understanding the trade-offs.

One minor clarity issue: The paper states "the condition number κ is known beforehand" but doesn't elaborate on how this is typically handled in practice. A brief discussion of how to estimate κ would improve clarity.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a novel contribution by proposing a method that achieves condition number-independent full gradient access. The mixed gradient approach is a clever combination of full and stochastic gradients that hasn't been explored in this way before.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work. It's not merely a combination of existing techniques but a new algorithm with a rigorous theoretical analysis that establishes a new convergence rate. The authors correctly identify the gap between previous methods (which either use only full gradients or only stochastic gradients) and propose a solution that bridges this gap.

The paper properly cites the relevant literature, including key papers on SAG [22], SDCA [23], and Nesterov's accelerated methods [17].

## Significance Assessment

The results are significant for large-scale optimization problems where computing full gradients is computationally expensive. Reducing the number of full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) could lead to substantial computational savings for ill-conditioned problems.

However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the paper's limitations. The computational complexity comparison (Table 2) shows that EMGD's computational complexity is only better than Nesterov's method when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which may not be the typical case in practice. The paper also acknowledges that SAG and SDCA have better computational complexity in many cases.

The paper's advantages (storage requirements, applicability to constrained problems, high-probability convergence) are significant, but without experimental validation, it's difficult to assess their practical impact.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Novel algorithm design that achieves condition number-independent full gradient access
2. Rigorous theoretical analysis with high-probability convergence guarantees
3. Comprehensive comparison with existing methods (SAG, SDCA, Nesterov's method)
4. Clear identification of advantages over existing methods (storage requirements, constrained optimization, high-probability convergence)
5. Proper acknowledgment of limitations and suggestions for future work

**Weaknesses:**
1. Lack of empirical validation (no experiments to support theoretical claims)
2. Requires knowledge of the condition number κ in advance (a practical limitation)
3. Computational complexity comparison shows EMGD may not be the most efficient method in many cases
4. The paper doesn't provide guidance on how to estimate κ when it's unknown
5. The theoretical analysis is complex but lacks practical implementation details

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add experimental results:** Include experiments on standard machine learning datasets (e.g., logistic regression, SVM) to validate the theoretical claims. Compare running time, convergence speed, and number of full gradient evaluations with baseline methods.

2. **Address the κ estimation problem:** Expand the discussion in the conclusion to include practical approaches for estimating κ when it's unknown. Mention how the Hessian estimation approach suggested in the paper could be implemented.

3. **Clarify practical relevance:** Provide more concrete examples of when κ ≤ n^(2/3) is a reasonable assumption in practice, especially in machine learning contexts.

4. **Simplify the algorithm description:** The current description of the algorithm (Algorithm 1) is precise but somewhat dense. Consider adding a brief intuitive explanation of why the mixed gradient approach works.

5. **Clarify the computational complexity comparison:** In Table 2, clarify what "computational complexity" means (e.g., total number of gradient evaluations, total time, etc.) and provide more context for the condition κ ≤ n^(2/3).

## Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to convex optimization. The algorithm is novel and the analysis is sound. However, the lack of experimental validation is a significant drawback for a machine learning conference like NIPS. Without empirical results, it's difficult to assess the practical value of the theoretical claims.

The paper would be significantly stronger with experiments showing the practical benefits of EMGD compared to existing methods. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but in the machine learning community, theoretical advances are often expected to be supported by empirical evidence.

Given the quality of the theoretical contribution and the potential significance, I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the lack of experimental results in a revised version. The paper would be a stronger contribution with empirical validation.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept

**Justification:** The paper makes a novel theoretical contribution that addresses a practical problem in optimization. The analysis is rigorous, and the paper is well-written. However, the lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness for a machine learning conference. If the authors can provide experimental results demonstrating the practical advantages of EMGD, this would substantially strengthen the paper. As it stands, the paper has theoretical merit but lacks the practical validation expected at NIPS.
