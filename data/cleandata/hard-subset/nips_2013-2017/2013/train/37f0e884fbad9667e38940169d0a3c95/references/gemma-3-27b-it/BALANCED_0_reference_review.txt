This paper introduces the Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) algorithm for strongly convex and smooth optimization, aiming to reduce the dependence on the condition number κ when using full gradients. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradient evaluations within epochs, achieving a linear convergence rate with O(log 1/ǫ) full gradient evaluations – independent of κ. The authors position their work between full-gradient methods (suffering from κ-dependence) and stochastic gradient methods (with slower convergence rates).

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Reducing the computational cost of full gradient evaluations in ill-conditioned problems is a practical and relevant challenge, especially in large-scale machine learning.
* **Novel Algorithm:** EMGD presents a novel combination of full and stochastic gradient descent, building on the concept of epoch-based methods.
* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis demonstrating a linear convergence rate and the claimed reduction in full gradient complexity to O(log 1/ǫ).
* **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm is clearly described, and the theoretical analysis, while dense, is generally well-structured.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing landscape of optimization algorithms, referencing relevant literature (e.g., Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent, SAG, SDCA).
* **Detailed Comparisons:** The comparison to other algorithms in Table 2, considering computational complexity, provides useful context. Discussion of practical considerations (e.g. estimating κ) is appreciated.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality Concerns:** The algorithm's performance relies heavily on accurate estimation of the condition number κ, which is acknowledged as a drawback. The proposed methods for estimation (sampling, leveraging regularizers) are mentioned briefly but lack detailed discussion or analysis. This is a crucial aspect for real-world applicability.
* **Constant Factors:** While the asymptotic complexity is improved, the paper doesn’t deeply analyze the constant factors hidden within the O() notation. In practice, these constants can significantly impact performance. Specifically, the factor of κ² in the number of stochastic gradients (O(κ² log 1/ǫ)) could be substantial for large κ, potentially negating some of the benefits.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any experimental evaluation. While theoretical guarantees are valuable, demonstrating the algorithm's performance on benchmark datasets is crucial to validate its practical effectiveness and compare it to existing methods.
* **Domain Constraint:** The algorithm involves solving a constrained optimization problem (minimization over the intersection of W and a ball) in each iteration, which could introduce computational overhead. The paper doesn’t elaborate on the efficient solution of this constrained problem.
* **Clarity in Notation:** While generally clear, certain notations (e.g., the meaning of 'fkt' in Algorithm 1) could benefit from more precise definitions or explanations.
* **Lemma 1 Assumption:** Lemma 1 (strong convexity property) is stated without a citation, although it is standard.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in optimization with a novel algorithmic approach.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong convergence guarantees.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the algorithm and its analysis.
* The work contributes to the ongoing research in developing efficient optimization methods for large-scale problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation weakens the claims of practical improvement.
* The dependence on accurate condition number estimation is a significant practical limitation.
* The large constant factor (κ²) in the stochastic gradient complexity might limit the practical benefits.
* The constrained optimization step within each iteration requires further analysis regarding efficiency.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and well-motivated paper that proposes a novel algorithm with promising theoretical properties. However, the lack of experimental validation and the practical concerns regarding condition number estimation and constant factors raise significant questions about its real-world impact. The paper currently falls short of demonstrating sufficient practical significance to warrant strong acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to address the weaknesses before resubmission. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Include a comprehensive experimental evaluation:** Implement the EMGD algorithm and compare its performance to state-of-the-art methods on relevant benchmark datasets.
2. **Investigate practical methods for condition number estimation:** Provide a more detailed analysis of practical techniques for estimating κ and assess their accuracy and computational cost.
3. **Analyze constant factors:** Discuss the impact of constant factors on the algorithm's performance and explore potential optimizations.
4. **Address the constrained optimization step:** Discuss efficient methods for solving the constrained optimization problem in each iteration.
5. **Consider a discussion on potential modifications:** Explore ways to mitigate the impact of the κ² factor in the stochastic gradient complexity, perhaps through adaptive techniques.
