### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of minimizing smooth and strongly convex functions, a central task in machine learning. The authors aim to reduce the dependency on the condition number κ in the number of full gradient evaluations required by first-order methods. Standard accelerated methods require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient computations. The paper proposes a new algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), that leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle to reduce this to O(log(1/ε)), making the number of expensive full gradient calls independent of κ.

The EMGD algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes a single full gradient at a reference point. It then performs a fixed number of inner iterations using a "mixed gradient", which is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient estimator constructed from the initial full gradient and stochastic gradients at the current and reference points. The iterates are projected onto a ball centered at the reference point, and the radius of this ball shrinks from one epoch to the next. The main theoretical result shows that EMGD achieves a linear convergence rate, requiring O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients to find an ε-optimal solution. The authors compare this complexity to standard full gradient methods and other linearly convergent stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA, arguing that EMGD can be more efficient in certain regimes and offers advantages such as applicability to constrained problems, low memory requirements, and a high-probability convergence guarantee.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The convergence analysis, which relies on an inductive argument over the epochs and uses a martingale concentration inequality (Hoeffding-Azuma) to establish a high-probability bound, appears correct. The proof sketch is provided and the steps are logical.

However, the work has some weaknesses regarding its completeness and evaluation.
1.  **High Complexity:** The number of stochastic gradients required per epoch is O(κ²), leading to a total stochastic gradient complexity of O(κ² log(1/ε)). This is significantly higher than other variance-reduction methods like SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013) or SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS 2014), which achieve a complexity of O((n+κ)log(1/ε)). This high dependence on κ may limit the practical applicability of the proposed method, especially for ill-conditioned problems where κ is large.
2.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. There are no experiments to validate the performance of EMGD. Claims about its computational efficiency compared to other methods (e.g., in Table 2) are based solely on theoretical complexity bounds. Without empirical results, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed algorithm provides a practical advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods, especially given its less favorable dependence on κ.
3.  **Parameter Dependency:** The algorithm's parameters, such as the step size η and the number of inner iterations T, depend on the smoothness constant L and strong convexity constant λ (and thus κ). The paper briefly mentions strategies for estimating κ in future work, but as it stands, this requirement can be a practical limitation.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-established, the algorithm is presented in a clear pseudocode format, and the main theoretical result is stated precisely. The analysis section provides a helpful sketch of the proof, highlighting the key steps and inequalities.

One area for improvement is the comparison in Section 3.4 and Table 2. The table of computational complexities could be made clearer by explicitly stating the assumptions and defining the units of complexity (e.g., number of stochastic gradient equivalent operations). For EMGD, the total complexity should be articulated as O((n + κ²)log(1/ε)) to make the comparison with SAG and SDCA more direct.

### Originality

The central idea of reducing variance in stochastic gradients has been a major focus of recent research. The "mixed gradient" `g̃_kt = ∇F(w̄^k) + ∇f_kt(w^k_t) - ∇f_kt(w̄^k)` is identical to the variance-reduced gradient estimator used in SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS 2013). This is a critical piece of related work that is not cited or discussed. The failure to acknowledge SVRG significantly reduces the perceived novelty of the core algorithmic component.

The primary novelty of this paper therefore lies not in the gradient estimator itself, but in its specific use within an epoch-based framework with a shrinking projection domain and the corresponding high-probability convergence analysis. This combination of techniques is original. The high-probability guarantee is a strength, as many related methods (including SVRG and SAG) provide guarantees only in expectation. The extension to constrained domains is also a valuable contribution. However, the contribution would be much stronger if it were properly contextualized with respect to SVRG and other closely related variance-reduction methods.

### Significance

The problem of designing optimization algorithms whose performance is less sensitive to the condition number is of high importance. The paper's goal of making the number of full gradient evaluations independent of κ is significant.

The potential impact of the work is tempered by several factors. First, the unaddressed relationship to SVRG and the less favorable theoretical complexity `O(κ²) vs. O(κ)` for stochastic gradients raises questions about its competitiveness. Second, the lack of empirical evidence makes it difficult for practitioners or other researchers to gauge its real-world utility. While the paper highlights potential advantages for constrained optimization and distributed computing, these claims remain speculative without experimental support. The paper presents an interesting theoretical idea, but its significance for the field is unclear until it is demonstrated to be competitive with or superior to existing, highly-optimized variance-reduction techniques.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  The paper addresses the important problem of reducing the dependence on the condition number κ for full gradient methods.
2.  It presents a novel combination of an epoch-based scheme, a variance-reduction technique, and a shrinking projection domain.
3.  It provides a strong theoretical result with a high-probability linear convergence guarantee, which is an improvement over the in-expectation guarantees of some related methods.
4.  The proposed method is applicable to constrained optimization problems, extending beyond the typical unconstrained setting of many variance-reduction algorithms.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  A critical piece of prior work, SVRG, which uses the identical gradient estimator, is not cited or discussed. This significantly misrepresents the novelty of the proposed method.
2.  The theoretical complexity for stochastic gradients, O(κ² log(1/ε)), is worse than that of state-of-the-art methods like SVRG and SAGA, which achieve O(κ) dependence.
3.  The paper lacks any experimental validation, making it impossible to assess the practical performance of the algorithm and verify the claimed benefits.
4.  The algorithm requires knowledge of problem parameters (L, λ), which can be a significant practical drawback.
