Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies smooth, λ-strongly convex optimization and proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), which interleaves occasional full gradient evaluations with many stochastic gradient evaluations. Each epoch computes one full gradient at a snapshot point w̄k and then performs T iterations using a “mixed” (control variate) estimator g̃t = ∇F(w̄k) + ∇f(wt) − ∇f(w̄k), updating within a shrinking ball around w̄k and averaging iterates. The analysis shows linear convergence: to reach ε-suboptimality, EMGD uses O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients (where κ = L/λ). The paper argues that this makes the access to full gradients independent of κ, potentially reducing wall-clock time when full gradients are expensive.

The proposed estimator is closely related to variance-reduced gradient methods developed for finite-sum problems F(w) = (1/n)∑i fi(w), notably SAG (NIPS’12), SDCA (2013), and especially the “snapshot-gradient + control variate” approach of SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS’13) and later SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS’14) and Katyusha (NIPS’16). Those works establish linear convergence with gradient complexities O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) and O(d) memory, using the same mixed-gradient idea but without a shrinking-ball constraint. The paper positions EMGD relative to SAG/SDCA but does not discuss SVRG/SAGA and more recent variance-reduction literature, which are highly pertinent given the estimator and goals.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-probability analysis via a martingale bound and the projection into a shrinking ball appears internally consistent. The control variate reduces variance, and the ball ensures bounded deviations to enable Azuma–Hoeffding. The main rate follows from choosing T = Ω(κ^2 log(1/δ)). Assumptions and steps are mostly clear, though there are typographical issues in Step 8 (missing 1/2 factor) and a few re-used equation tags.
- Completeness: There are no experiments. Given the wealth of related algorithms with overlapping goals, empirical evidence is crucial to assess practical utility.
- Comparisons: Theoretical comparisons focus on SAG/SDCA and Nesterov’s AGD but omit SVRG/SAGA/Katyusha and other modern VR methods that would sharpen the context.

Clarity
The paper is readable, with a clearly stated problem, algorithm, and proof outline. However:
- Several typos and grammar issues (e.g., “epoches,” “interstage”), and some notational inconsistencies (e.g., the quadratic term in Step 8) detract from polish.
- The cost model is informal; the claim “independent number of full gradients” would benefit from a more precise total time model across different regimes of n and κ.
- The assumption of a function oracle Of and a full-gradient oracle Og is abstract; in practice, full gradients arise from finite sums, which should be made explicit earlier to ground the discussion.

Originality
The mixed-gradient estimator and epoch structure are not new relative to the control-variate variance reduction line. EMGD’s distinctive elements are: (i) the use of a shrinking ball constraint and averaging within epochs, and (ii) framing the result as “κ-independent access to full gradients.” However, SVRG already achieves O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls with O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) total gradient complexity and O(d) memory, without requiring a shrinking domain. EMGD’s κ^2 dependence for stochastic gradients is weaker than the O(κ) dependence achievable in SVRG/SAGA/Katyusha. Thus, relative novelty is limited.

Significance
As stated, the main guarantee (O(log(1/ε)) full gradients; O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients; high probability) is theoretically clean but likely dominated in practice by SVRG/SAGA-type methods that (a) need the same O(log(1/ε)) full gradients, (b) achieve better κ dependence, (c) avoid ball projections, and (d) have extensive empirical validation. The paper’s arguments about constraints, memory, and distributed computation are valid advantages relative to SAG/SDCA, but they also apply to SVRG/SAGA. Without experiments or improved theory (e.g., reducing κ^2 to κ), the overall impact seems limited.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, self-contained high-probability analysis using a standard control variate estimator.
- Linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradients and O(d) memory.
- Works for constrained problems (if the projection onto W ∩ ball is tractable).
- Discussion of distributed computation for full gradients is reasonable.

Cons:
- Key idea and estimator coincide with SVRG-style variance reduction; related work is incomplete (missing SVRG/SAGA etc.).
- Stochastic gradient complexity O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) is suboptimal vs. O(κ log(1/ε)).
- No experiments; practical behavior and constants are unknown.
- Requires knowing κ, with only a sketch for estimation.
- Shrinking-ball projection may complicate implementation vs. simpler VR methods.
- Some overstatements: e.g., “much stronger” than SAG/SDCA due to high-probability vs. in-expectation guarantees, while high-probability variants exist.

Suggestions for improvement
1) Positioning and citations: Compare explicitly with SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, NIPS’13), SAGA (Defazio et al., NIPS’14), and later accelerated variants (Katyusha). Clarify what EMGD offers beyond those (e.g., constrained setting, high-probability with simple assumptions), and avoid overstated claims.
2) Theory: Strengthen the bound to O(κ log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients per epoch, aligning with standard VR analyses. The shrinking ball appears to drive the κ^2 dependence; consider removing it and controlling variance via smoothness and strong convexity as in SVRG/SAGA analyses. Provide a proximal/constraint-friendly version with tractable projections or proximal maps.
3) Adaptivity: Remove dependence on κ by adaptive epoch lengths (e.g., doubling tricks), or data-driven step sizes. Provide stopping criteria that do not require κ.
4) Experiments: Include benchmarks (logistic regression, ridge regression) versus AGD, SVRG, SAGA, SAG, SDCA across varying n, κ, constrained/unconstrained, and report wall-clock time and memory. Test distributed full-gradient steps if claimed as an advantage.
5) Clarity and polish: Fix typos and Step 8’s quadratic term. Provide a precise computational model (cost per Og vs. Of), discuss when each method dominates in practice, and state assumptions about accessing Og in expectation-only settings.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper’s main idea closely matches established variance-reduction methods and achieves weaker κ dependence without empirical validation. With an updated theoretical bound, proper positioning, and experiments, the contribution could be reconsidered.
