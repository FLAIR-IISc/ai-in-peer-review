

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm for smooth strongly convex problems that combines full gradients and stochastic gradients to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations needed from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), where κ is the condition number. The key insight is that by using one full gradient per epoch and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, the algorithm achieves linear convergence with condition number-independent full gradient access. The authors compare EMGD to Nesterov's accelerated gradient descent (which requires O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradients), SAG (O(n log(1/ε))), and SDCA (O((n+κ) log(1/ε))), showing that EMGD has advantages in terms of storage requirements (Ω(d) vs. Ω(n)), applicability to constrained problems, and high-probability convergence guarantees.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to optimization, with a rigorous convergence proof that carefully handles the interplay between full and stochastic gradients. The induction argument and use of appropriate lemmas (e.g., Lemma 1 for strongly convex functions) demonstrate strong technical quality.
2. The algorithm design is novel in its approach to combining full and stochastic gradients within an epoch-based framework, offering a different perspective than existing "semi-stochastic" methods.
3. The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with relevant NIPS papers (e.g., [9, 10, 21, 22, 23]) and clearly articulates EMGD's advantages over existing methods in terms of storage requirements, applicability to constrained problems, and convergence guarantees.
4. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, particularly the need to know κ in advance, and suggest promising future directions for estimating κ.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The O(κ²) dependence on the condition number for stochastic gradients appears high and is not adequately justified. The paper doesn't explain why this dependence is necessary or whether it can be improved, which is a significant concern for practical implementation on ill-conditioned problems.
2. The computational complexity comparison in Table 2 shows EMGD only outperforms other methods when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which may be restrictive in many practical machine learning scenarios. The paper fails to provide empirical validation of this claim or discuss how common this condition is in practice.
3. The paper lacks empirical results to support the theoretical claims. Without experiments comparing EMGD to existing methods on real optimization problems, it's difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
4. The paper doesn't adequately address the practical challenge of estimating κ, which is a significant limitation for real-world application. While the authors mention this in the conclusion, they don't propose a concrete solution for this critical issue.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound with a rigorous proof. However, the O(κ²) dependence on the condition number for stochastic gradients raises questions about the practical utility of the algorithm. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of this term's necessity and potential improvements.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear notation and a logically structured presentation. The algorithm description and convergence proof are detailed. However, the computational cost analysis could be clearer, particularly regarding the practical implications of the κ dependence.

**Originality:** The core idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients in an epoch-based framework is novel. While semi-stochastic methods exist (e.g., [12, 13]), EMGD's specific approach to combining these gradients to achieve condition number-independent full gradient access appears to be a new contribution. The comparison with SAG and SDCA is thorough and well-justified.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is important for large-scale optimization, particularly in machine learning. However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the restrictive condition κ ≤ n^(2/3) for computational advantage and the lack of empirical validation. The high-probability convergence guarantee is a notable theoretical contribution, but without practical validation, its significance is diminished.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to optimization theory, the practical limitations and lack of empirical validation significantly reduce its impact for NIPS. The O(κ²) dependence on the condition number for stochastic gradients is a major concern that isn't adequately addressed, and the paper doesn't demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Provide empirical results comparing EMGD to existing methods on standard machine learning optimization problems, including both synthetic and real datasets.
2. Investigate whether the O(κ²) dependence can be reduced or eliminated through a more sophisticated algorithm design.
3. Develop a practical method for estimating κ in real-world applications, not just a theoretical suggestion.
4. Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical conditions under which EMGD would be preferred over SAG or SDCA, including how common the κ ≤ n^(2/3) condition is in typical machine learning problems.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to optimization, but without addressing the practical limitations and providing empirical validation, it falls short of the high standard expected at NIPS.
