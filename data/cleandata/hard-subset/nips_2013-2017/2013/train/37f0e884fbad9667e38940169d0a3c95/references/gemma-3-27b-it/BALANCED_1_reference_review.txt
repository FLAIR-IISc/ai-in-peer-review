This paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), an optimization algorithm aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations for strongly convex, smooth optimization problems. The core idea is to interleave full and stochastic gradients within epochs to achieve a linear convergence rate with a number of full gradients independent of the condition number κ. The authors present a detailed algorithm, theoretical analysis, and comparisons to related work.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of strategically mixing full and stochastic gradients within epochs to achieve condition number independence in the number of full gradient evaluations is a valuable contribution. This addresses a practical bottleneck in large-scale optimization where full gradient computation is expensive.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a clear and relatively comprehensive theoretical analysis, including a convergence theorem with a well-defined rate. The proof appears reasonably sound, building upon established results from stochastic optimization.
* **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented clearly, and the notation is generally consistent. The paper is well-structured, making it easier to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate good awareness of the related literature, referencing key papers in deterministic, stochastic, and semi-stochastic optimization. They position their work appropriately within the existing landscape.
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The comparative analysis (Table 2) provides a useful context for understanding the trade-offs of EMGD versus other algorithms like SAG and SDCA. The discussion of practical considerations, such as the assumption of known κ, is also helpful.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality of Condition Number Estimation:** The paper acknowledges that knowing the condition number κ beforehand is a limitation. While they briefly mention potential estimation strategies (sampling the Hessian, exploiting regularizers), these are not fleshed out sufficiently. A more detailed discussion of practical estimation techniques would significantly enhance the paper's relevance. How robust is the algorithm to *inaccurate* estimation of κ?
* **Tightness of the Convergence Rate:** While a linear convergence rate is achieved, the constant factors involved may be significant in practice. A more detailed discussion of the constant factors, or perhaps empirical results demonstrating their impact, would be beneficial. Is the linear convergence rate tight, or could it be improved with a different approach?
* **Computational Cost per Epoch:** The algorithm performs O(κ<sup>2</sup>) stochastic gradient evaluations per epoch. This could be computationally prohibitive for very large condition numbers, potentially negating the benefit of reducing the *number* of full gradients. This trade-off needs more careful consideration.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of L-smoothness and strong convexity is standard, but it's worth highlighting the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions. A brief discussion of robustness to noise or slight deviations from smoothness would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical results. While theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the algorithm's performance on real-world datasets would greatly strengthen the claims. Comparison to baselines (e.g., standard gradient descent, SGD, SAG) would be crucial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in optimization – reducing the computational cost of full gradient evaluations.
* The proposed algorithm is novel and theoretically well-supported.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
* The work contributes to the growing body of research on hybrid optimization algorithms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The practical applicability of the algorithm is limited by the need to know the condition number.
* The lack of empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm’s performance in practice.
* The potentially high computational cost per epoch for large condition numbers warrants further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of optimization. The idea of strategically mixing full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition number independence is promising. However, the lack of empirical validation and the practical challenges associated with condition number estimation raise concerns about the algorithm’s real-world applicability. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize:

1.  **Adding a comprehensive empirical evaluation** on a variety of datasets, comparing EMGD to standard optimization algorithms.
2.  **Providing a more detailed discussion of practical techniques for estimating the condition number κ**, and analyzing the algorithm’s sensitivity to estimation errors.
3.  **Analyzing the computational cost per epoch** more carefully, and considering strategies for mitigating the impact of large condition numbers.
4.  **Discussing the tightness of the convergence rate** and potential avenues for improvement.
