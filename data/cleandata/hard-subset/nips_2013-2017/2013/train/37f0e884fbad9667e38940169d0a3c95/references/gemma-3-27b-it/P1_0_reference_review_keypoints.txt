- Introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) to interleave full and stochastic gradients for linear convergence with fewer full-gradient accesses.  
- Claims reduction of full gradient computations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)).  
- Provides theoretical analysis and a convergence theorem (Theorem 1) supporting the claimed complexity.  
- Positions the work clearly within first-order optimization literature and compares to Nesterov, SAG, SDCA, and epoch methods.  
- Novelty: mixing full and stochastic gradients within epochs to decouple full-gradient access from the condition number.  
- Theoretical guarantees: a well-defined linear convergence proof sketch and complexity analysis.  
- Comparison strengths: thoughtful complexity comparisons, memory considerations, and applicability to constrained problems.  
- Practical considerations: discusses need to estimate κ and suggests sampling or regularizers as remedies.  
- Writing quality: clear presentation, consistent notation, and well-organized algorithm description.  
- Implementation practicality: sparse details on choosing epoch size and step size when κ is unknown; assumes both gradient and function oracles.  
- Constant factors: asymptotic focus may hide large constants in O(log(1/ε)) that could reduce practical benefits.  
- Empirical evaluation: no experiments provided; lack of benchmarks undermines practical claims.  
- Assumptions: relies on L-smoothness and λ-strong convexity without discussing robustness to violations.  
- Theorem 1 conditions: requirements on δ and ∆1 appear strict, potentially limiting applicability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, rigorous analysis, relevant comparisons, and addresses an important large-scale optimization challenge.  
- Arguments against acceptance: absent empirical validation, strict theorem conditions, potential large constants, and limited implementation guidance.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically sound and potentially significant, but practical impact is unclear without experiments.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, with strong encouragement to add empirical results and address constant factors and implementation details in revision.
