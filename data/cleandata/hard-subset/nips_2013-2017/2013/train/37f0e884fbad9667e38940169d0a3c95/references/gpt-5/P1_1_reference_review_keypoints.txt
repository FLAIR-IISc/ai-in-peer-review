- Paper introduces Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD): epochal method using one full gradient per epoch and a mixed estimator g̃ = ∇F(w̄k)+∇f(w)−∇f(w̄k), achieving linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full-gradient calls and O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic-gradient calls.

- The mixed gradient estimator is identical to SVRG’s estimator; the connection to SVRG/SAGA/SAG variance-reduction literature must be explicitly discussed.

- Theoretical analysis is clean: high-probability linear convergence via a potential/induction argument and Hoeffding–Azuma, using shrinking trust-region balls to bound variance.

- The algorithm requires knowledge of κ (to set epoch length T) and L (to set step size), and assumes a function oracle that returns ∇f at two points for the same random f (stronger than standard streaming SG oracle).

- Complexity-wise achieving O(log(1/ε)) full gradients is interesting, but the inner-loop length T = Θ(κ^2 log(1/δ)) leads to overall O(κ^2 log(1/ε)) stochastic cost, worse than SVRG/SAGA/SDCA’s O((n+κ) log(1/ε)) in finite-sum ERM.

- In the finite-sum regime EMGD is dominated in stochastic-gradient complexity by established variance-reduced methods despite fewer full-gradient passes.

- No empirical results are provided; experiments comparing against AGD, SAG, SVRG/SAGA/SDCA across varying n and κ are needed to validate practical advantages (especially for constrained or distributed settings).

- Clarity is generally acceptable and proofs are self-contained, but numerous typos, notation glitches, and minor errors (e.g., incorrect ln δ) hinder readability.

- The function oracle and projection onto W ∩ B(w̄k; Δk) need clearer definitions and discussion of computational cost and practical implementation.

- Originality is limited: the mixed estimator is not new (SVRG), though the shrinking-ball trust-region idea for high-probability bounds is a novel analytic twist.

- Significance: theoretically clarifies trade-off between full-gradient and stochastic-gradient calls and gives high-probability guarantees; practical impact is unclear due to poor κ dependence and lack of experiments.

- Pros: clean analysis, simple algorithm, constraint support via projection, O(d) memory, and conceptual decoupling of full-gradient count from κ.

- Cons: pessimistic O(κ^2) inner-loop complexity, missing comparison to close related work (SVRG/SAGA), no experiments, requires known κ and L, and presentation issues.

- Suggested improvements: add thorough comparison to SVRG/SAGA (and explain κ^2 vs κ), provide experiments on convex ERM varying n and κ, remove need to know κ via adaptive/doubling schemes, clarify oracle/projection costs, and fix typos and constants.

- Recommendation: Weak reject — interesting conceptual point and clean analysis but dominated by existing variance-reduced methods in the key finite-sum setting and lacking empirical validation.
