This paper proposes a novel optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), aiming to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations in smooth, strongly convex optimization by strategically combining them with stochastic gradients. The core idea of dividing the optimization process into epochs and performing mixed gradient descent within each epoch is inspired by epoch gradient descent, but with a crucial difference: the algorithm’s iteration complexity is designed to be independent of the condition number κ. The authors claim to achieve a linear convergence rate with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, a significant improvement over the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity.

While the *idea* is compelling, and the problem addressed is relevant (reducing the cost of full gradient computations is a practical concern), the paper suffers from a surprisingly high number of minor flaws and inconsistencies that, cumulatively, detract from its overall quality. These issues range from notational ambiguities to incomplete explanations and a somewhat inflated sense of novelty. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The motivation for reducing full gradient evaluations is well-established and important.
*   **Clear Goal:** The paper clearly defines its objective: to achieve condition number independent iteration complexity.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a theoretical analysis and convergence rate for their algorithm (Theorem 1).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The repeated use of both 'w' and 'x' as solution/iteration variables creates confusion. Using consistent notation throughout is crucial for clarity.
*   **Oracle Definitions:** The definition of the function oracle *Of* feels awkwardly phrased. It states it *returns a random function* f, such that F(w) = E[f(w)]. This is not standard terminology and requires more precise explanation. It’s more common to speak of stochastic gradients *being* unbiased estimates of the full gradient.
*   **Step Size Selection:** The paper simply states η = 1/[L√T] without justification. Why is this a suitable step size? What guarantees does this choice provide? A more thorough discussion of step size selection is needed.
*   **Algorithm 1 Clarity:** The nested loop structure in Algorithm 1 is slightly confusing. A more visually clear presentation could improve readability. The comment on line 8 regarding the updating being similar to standard gradient descent is insufficient - it requires expansion on the domain constraint and its impact.
*   **Theorem 1 Assumptions:** Assumption (5) regarding δ and ∆1 feels contrived and not well-motivated. Why these specific conditions? Also, the practical implications of these assumptions are not discussed.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** The comparison in Section 3.4 is somewhat superficial.  The statement that EMGD is “more efficient” than algorithms in [9,10,21] simply because of linear convergence is an oversimplification. Those algorithms also have their advantages, and a more nuanced comparison, perhaps considering the constant factors involved, is needed. The discussion regarding the cost of the gradient computation in Table 2, and its impact on comparing EMGD, SAG, and SDCA seems very sensitive to the assumption that a full gradient is 'n' times more expensive than a stochastic gradient, and doesn't adequately consider the constant factors.
*   **Proof Details:** The proof sketch in Section 4 is extremely high-level and relies heavily on hand-waving. While a complete proof isn’t always necessary in a conference paper, more details and justifications are needed to convince the reader of the correctness of the analysis. The use of Hoeffding-Azuma inequality without proper context or a clear explanation of the martingale difference sequence is problematic.
*   **Future Work:** The discussion of estimating the condition number κ in future work feels tacked on and lacks concrete ideas.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The idea of combining full and stochastic gradients to reduce the cost of optimization is promising. The problem addressed is important.

**Con:** The paper is riddled with minor flaws – notational inconsistencies, unclear explanations, unsubstantiated claims, and a superficial comparison to related work. The theoretical analysis lacks sufficient detail, and the proof sketch is inadequate. The assumptions made in Theorem 1 are not well-motivated. While individually minor, these issues accumulate to significantly detract from the paper’s overall quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea behind EMGD is interesting, the paper suffers from a lack of polish and rigor. The numerous minor flaws, coupled with a somewhat inflated sense of novelty, lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper requires significant revision to address the issues outlined above before it would be suitable for NIPS. Specifically, the authors must address the notational inconsistencies, clarify the oracle definitions, provide a more detailed and justified analysis of the algorithm, and provide a more nuanced comparison to related work. A more complete proof sketch, or a reference to a more detailed proof in an appendix, is also essential.
