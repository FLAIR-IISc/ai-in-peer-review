

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel algorithm for smooth strongly convex optimization that claims to achieve linear convergence with condition number independent access to full gradients. The authors argue that by mixing full and stochastic gradients, they can reduce the number of full gradient computations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), while requiring O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient computations. The paper provides a theoretically sound analysis of the algorithm, proving that EMGD achieves the claimed convergence rates with high probability.

The theoretical analysis is technically rigorous, with careful proof of the convergence properties. The authors properly cite relevant related work, including SAG, SDCA, and other semi-stochastic methods, and provide a detailed comparison of EMGD with existing approaches. They highlight several theoretical advantages of EMGD over SAG and SDCA, including the ability to handle constrained optimization problems, lower storage requirements (Ω(d) vs. Ω(n)), and high-probability convergence guarantees rather than convergence in expectation.

However, the paper suffers from a critical deficiency: it lacks any empirical validation of the proposed algorithm. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the practical significance of the claimed improvements cannot be assessed without experimental results. The authors claim that "the computational complexity of EMGD is lower than Nesterov's algorithm [17] as long as the condition number κ ≤ n^(2/3)," but provide no evidence to support this claim in practice. Without experiments on real optimization problems, it's impossible to determine whether the theoretical advantages translate to practical benefits.

Furthermore, the paper does not adequately address the practicality of the O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient computations. For large condition numbers (which are common in ill-conditioned problems), this could be prohibitively expensive, potentially negating any benefit from reducing the full gradient computations. The authors acknowledge that "a drawback of EMGD is that it requires the condition number κ is known beforehand," but they don't provide a convincing argument for how this limitation is overcome in practice.

The paper's theoretical claim that EMGD can achieve linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient computations relies on the assumption that stochastic gradients can be computed efficiently. This assumption is not justified with empirical evidence, and it's unclear whether this holds in the contexts where full gradients are expensive to compute. The paper also fails to provide any discussion of the computational cost of evaluating stochastic gradients relative to full gradients in practical scenarios.

The comparison with SAG and SDCA is primarily theoretical, but the authors claim that "the linear convergence of SAG and SDCA only holds in expectation, whereas the linear convergence of EMGD holds with a high probability." This is a significant claim, but it's not supported by any empirical evidence. A proper comparison would require experiments demonstrating that EMGD indeed achieves better convergence rates with higher probability than SAG and SDCA in practical settings.

Given NIPS's emphasis on rigorous experimental validation and practical significance, this paper falls short of the expected standards. While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the lack of empirical results makes it difficult to evaluate the practical relevance of the proposed method. The paper presents a novel theoretical approach but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims in a practical context.

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound and rigorously presented
- Clear description of the algorithm and its differences from existing methods
- Proper citation of relevant related work
- Detailed theoretical comparison with existing methods

**Weaknesses:**
- No empirical validation of the proposed algorithm
- Does not address practical limitations of O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradient computations
- Assumes knowledge of κ, which is often unknown in practice
- Theoretical claims lack experimental support
- Fails to provide computational cost analysis in practical settings

**Recommendation:** Reject

The paper's theoretical contribution is interesting but insufficiently validated. Without experimental results demonstrating the practical advantages of EMGD over established baselines, it fails to meet the quality standards expected at NIPS. The authors should provide comprehensive experimental results comparing EMGD with state-of-the-art methods on standard optimization problems before reconsidering for publication.
