

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper proposes a novel approach to convex optimization for smooth and strongly convex problems by combining full and stochastic gradients. The key insight is that by using a hybrid algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), the number of full gradient evaluations needed to reach ε-optimality can be reduced from the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)), independent of the condition number κ. This is achieved by using one full gradient per epoch and O(κ²) stochastic gradients per epoch, with the algorithm dividing the optimization process into epochs that progressively reduce the domain size.

The paper's main novelty lies in its bold approach to bypassing the well-known lower bound for first-order methods with only full gradients. While standard methods must pay the O(√κ) factor in iteration complexity, this work cleverly leverages the availability of stochastic gradients to circumvent this limitation. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that EMGD achieves linear convergence with condition number independent full gradient access, a significant conceptual advance.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) a novel algorithm design that blends full and stochastic gradients in a way that ensures the stochastic component remains bounded; (2) a rigorous convergence analysis showing O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations; (3) a comparison showing computational advantages over existing methods for certain ranges of κ (specifically, when κ ≤ n^(2/3)); and (4) practical advantages over SAG and SDCA, including applicability to constrained problems, lower storage requirements (Ω(d) vs Ω(n)), and convergence with high probability rather than in expectation.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The mathematical analysis is thorough, with careful attention to the stochastic component's variance and a well-constructed induction proof for convergence. The assumptions are clearly stated and reasonable for the problem setting. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, such as the need for knowledge of κ in advance, and discusses potential future work for addressing this.

The significance of this work is substantial. While the paper doesn't claim to outperform all existing methods in every scenario, it introduces a fundamentally new direction in optimization that could have broad implications for large-scale machine learning and signal processing. The key insight that condition number dependence can be eliminated by combining full and stochastic gradients is both elegant and potentially transformative for practical applications where full gradient computation is expensive.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the complex technical content accessible. The comparison with related work is comprehensive and appropriately contextualized.

Strengths:
- Novel conceptual approach to bypassing the standard O(√κ) lower bound
- Clear theoretical contribution showing condition number independent full gradient access
- Practical advantages over existing methods in constrained settings and storage requirements
- Well-structured and technically sound analysis
- Strong theoretical guarantees with high probability convergence

Weaknesses:
- Requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ, which may be a limitation in some applications
- The computational advantage over SAG/SDCA is limited to specific ranges of κ (κ ≤ n^(2/3))
- The O(κ²) stochastic gradient evaluations might be high for large κ
- The paper doesn't provide empirical results to validate the theoretical claims in practice

Despite these limitations, the paper's core contribution is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance. The conceptual breakthrough of making full gradient access condition number independent is bold and valuable, even if the computational advantages are conditional. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

The paper does not seem to be out of scope for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in optimization with clear relevance to machine learning applications. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by introducing a new conceptual direction rather than merely improving upon existing methods.

I would recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to better address the limitation of requiring knowledge of κ in advance, perhaps by discussing potential approaches for estimating κ in practice (as briefly mentioned in the conclusion). The paper's novelty and conceptual significance outweigh the minor limitations in the current form.
