This paper proposes a new optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for smooth and strongly convex functions. The primary goal is to reduce the number of full gradient evaluations to be independent of the function's condition number, κ. The algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, it computes one full gradient. Then, within the epoch, it performs a series of updates using a variance-reduced stochastic gradient, constructed from the initial full gradient and stochastic gradients at the current iterate. The iterates are projected onto a shrinking domain. The main theoretical result shows that EMGD achieves an ε-optimal solution by computing O(log 1/ε) full gradients and O(κ² log 1/ε) stochastic gradients. This work relates to the literature on variance-reduced stochastic methods like SAG and SDCA, and epoch-based methods, but extends the ideas to a more general stochastic oracle setting beyond finite sums and provides a high-probability convergence guarantee.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel Goal and Contribution:** The objective of making the number of expensive full gradient computations independent of the condition number κ is significant and well-motivated, especially for ill-conditioned problems. The paper successfully achieves this, reducing the complexity from O(√κ log 1/ε) to O(log 1/ε) full gradient calls.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous proof for the linear convergence of EMGD. The analysis, based on an inductive argument over epochs, is clear and leads to a high-probability convergence guarantee, which is stronger than the in-expectation results common for similar algorithms like SAG and SDCA.
3.  **Generality:** The algorithm is presented for a general setting with a stochastic oracle and is applicable to constrained optimization problems, provided the projection step is efficient. This gives it potential advantages over methods like SAG and SVRG that are primarily designed for unconstrained finite-sum problems. The low memory requirement (O(d)) is also a notable practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while presenting an interesting theoretical result, suffers from a substantial number of methodological and presentational flaws that undermine its contribution.

1.  **Technical and Methodological Issues:**
    *   **Severe Dependence on κ:** The main claim of κ-independence is misleading. While the number of *full* gradients is independent of κ, the number of *stochastic* gradients per epoch is `T = O(κ²)`. This results in a total stochastic gradient complexity of O(κ² log 1/ε). This `κ²` dependence is extremely severe and is significantly worse than the `O(κ)` dependence of related methods like SDCA and SVRG. For even moderately ill-conditioned problems, this cost would be prohibitive, a crucial point that is not sufficiently emphasized.
    *   **Omission of Key Related Work:** The paper surprisingly omits any comparison to SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013) and SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014). SVRG, in particular, uses a very similar epoch-based structure with a full gradient anchor and variance-reduced stochastic updates. Its complexity for finite-sum problems is O((n+κ)log 1/ε), which is superior to EMGD's effective O(n log 1/ε + κ² log 1/ε) total work. The lack of discussion and comparison with SVRG is a major oversight.
    *   **Unrealistic Assumptions:** The algorithm and its analysis rely on several impractical assumptions.
        *   Knowledge of κ: The number of inner iterations T depends directly on κ, but κ is rarely known in practice. The suggestion in the conclusion to estimate it via sampling is hand-wavy and unsubstantiated; this is a non-trivial problem in its own right.
        *   Knowledge of `F(w*)`: The initial domain size `∆1` depends on the optimal function value `F(w*)`, which is unknown. This limits the direct applicability of the theorem.
    *   **Understated Projection Cost:** The update in Step 8 requires a projection onto `W ∩ B(w̄k; ∆k)`. The paper claims this is an advantage for constrained problems, but the cost of projecting onto an intersection of an arbitrary convex set `W` and a ball can be very high, potentially dominating the cost of gradient computations. This practical challenge is glossed over.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the polish expected of a top-tier publication.
    *   **Inconsistent Formatting:** There are numerous minor formatting errors. The term "epoches" is used instead of "epochs". The sentence "We will interstage how to find..." in the conclusion contains a typo ("interstage" for "investigate"). Reference formatting in the text is inconsistent (e.g., details provided for [22] but not [23]).
    *   **Ambiguous Notation and Definitions:** The use of `wkt` for `w_t^k` is acceptable but dense. The definition of strong convexity in Eq. (4) is non-standard (it is strong monotonicity of the gradient) and should be properly introduced as a consequence of the standard definition to avoid confusion. In Algorithm 1, `w̄k+1` is defined as an average over `T+1` points (`w_t^k` for `t=1...T+1`), which is correct as the loop produces `w_{T+1}^k`, but the notation could be clearer.
    *   **Footnote Usage:** Footnote 1 disrupts the reading flow in the Related Work section. Footnote 2 makes a claim about a "reasonable scenario" (`τ > Ω(n−2/3)`) with minimal justification, which weakens the argument.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core theoretical idea of achieving κ-independent full gradient complexity is novel and elegant.
*   The high-probability convergence bound is a strong theoretical result.
*   The algorithm's applicability to general stochastic oracles and constrained settings is a valuable extension over finite-sum methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The catastrophic `O(κ²)` dependence in the stochastic gradient complexity severely limits the algorithm's practical relevance and is a major weakness compared to state-of-the-art methods.
*   The paper fails to compare against SVRG, a highly relevant and superior baseline, which constitutes a significant gap in the evaluation of the proposed method.
*   The algorithm relies on impractical prior knowledge (κ, F(w*)), and potential computational bottlenecks (the projection step) are not adequately addressed.
*   The manuscript contains a distracting number of minor typos, formatting inconsistencies, and clarity issues.

**Recommendation:** Rejection.

While the paper is founded on a clever and theoretically interesting idea, its significant flaws outweigh its contributions. The `O(κ²)` complexity is a critical drawback that is not given due prominence, and the omission of a comparison to SVRG is a fatal flaw in positioning this work. Combined with the reliance on impractical assumptions and numerous presentational issues, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication. A major revision would be required to address the comparison to related work, provide a more honest assessment of the practical limitations, and polish the manuscript to an acceptable standard.
