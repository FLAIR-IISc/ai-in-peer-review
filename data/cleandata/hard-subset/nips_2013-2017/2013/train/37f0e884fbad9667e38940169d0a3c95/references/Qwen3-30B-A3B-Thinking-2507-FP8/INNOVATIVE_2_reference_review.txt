

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

This paper introduces a novel algorithm called Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD) for smooth and strongly convex optimization, with the key insight that by mixing full and stochastic gradients, one can achieve linear convergence with a number of full gradient evaluations that is independent of the condition number κ. The paper demonstrates that EMGD requires only O(log(1/ε)) full gradients (compared to the standard O(√κ log(1/ε)) for full gradient methods) while using O(κ² log(1/ε)) stochastic gradients.

The novelty of this work is significant. The authors recognize that the O(√κ log(1/ε)) complexity for full gradient methods is generally not improvable, but they bypass this lower bound by introducing a new oracle model that allows access to both full and stochastic gradients. This is a bold conceptual shift rather than an incremental improvement to existing methods. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work on full gradient methods (like Nesterov's accelerated methods) and stochastic methods (like SAG, SDCA), highlighting that EMGD achieves linear convergence with condition number independent full gradient access.

The paper's significance is substantial. For large-scale optimization problems where full gradient computation is expensive (e.g., when F(w) = (1/n)∑f_i(w)), EMGD can be more computationally efficient than full gradient methods when κ ≤ n^(2/3), which is a reasonable condition in many machine learning applications. The authors also demonstrate that EMGD has practical advantages over SAG and SDCA: it works for both constrained and unconstrained problems, requires only O(d) storage (compared to Ω(n) for SAG/SDCA), and achieves convergence with high probability rather than in expectation.

The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The authors provide a clear convergence rate proof using induction and carefully bound the stochastic terms using the Hoeffding-Azuma inequality. The proof is technically sound, though some parts could benefit from more intuitive explanations for readers less familiar with the technical details.

The paper has a few minor limitations that don't detract from its novelty:
1. The need to know κ in advance is a practical constraint, though the authors provide a reasonable approach to estimate κ for specific problems (e.g., with regularizers).
2. The computational complexity comparison with SAG/SDCA shows EMGD is not always superior, but the paper correctly frames this as a trade-off rather than a claim of universal superiority.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in optimization theory. While the idea of mixing full and stochastic gradients has been explored before (e.g., in semi-stochastic methods), the authors' specific approach of using a fixed number of full gradients per epoch to achieve condition number independent access is novel. The paper doesn't just improve upon existing methods—it introduces a new paradigm for considering optimization oracles.

In terms of NIPS contribution, this work advances the theoretical understanding of optimization algorithms and provides a new direction for algorithm design that could inspire practical applications in machine learning. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a strong case for its novelty and significance.

The paper's main strength is its bold conceptual contribution, which aligns perfectly with NIPS's interest in novel theoretical approaches. The minor limitations (like the need to know κ) are acceptable given the paper's significant theoretical contribution, and the authors appropriately acknowledge these limitations.

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. It's not just an incremental improvement but introduces a new concept that changes how we think about the relationship between full and stochastic gradient access in optimization. The paper demonstrates how to achieve linear convergence with condition number independent full gradient access—a fundamentally new approach that could inspire future work.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Strengths**:
- Novel conceptual approach (mixing full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition number independent full gradient access)
- Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis
- Demonstrates practical advantages over existing methods in specific settings
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Weaknesses**:
- Requires knowledge of κ in advance (though the authors provide reasonable estimation approaches)
- Not universally superior to SAG/SDCA (but this is appropriately acknowledged)

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance that should be of interest to the NIPS community. The novelty and boldness of the approach outweigh the minor limitations.
