

# Review of "Linear Convergence with Condition Number Independent Access of Full Gradients"

## Summary
This paper proposes Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), a novel optimization algorithm for smooth and strongly convex problems that combines full and stochastic gradients to achieve linear convergence with condition number independent access to full gradients. The authors address the limitation of standard first-order methods, which require O(√κ log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations for ε-optimality, by reducing this to O(log(1/ε)) through strategic use of stochastic gradients. The algorithm operates by dividing optimization into epochs, where each epoch uses one full gradient evaluation and O(κ²) stochastic gradient evaluations. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that EMGD achieves linear convergence with high probability, with the number of full gradients independent of the condition number κ. The paper relates this work to Nesterov's accelerated methods [17], stochastic gradient methods [9,10,21], and the SAG [22] and SDCA [23] algorithms, highlighting EMGD's advantages in storage requirements, constraint handling, and convergence guarantees.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of EMGD's convergence properties. The proof of Theorem 1 is comprehensive, using standard convex optimization tools (strong convexity, smoothness, martingale inequalities) appropriately. The authors carefully bound all error terms and provide precise conditions for the convergence rate (e.g., T ≥ 1152L²/λ² log(1/δ)).

2. **Novelty**: The core contribution of combining full and stochastic gradients to achieve condition-number-independent full gradient access is novel. While previous work has explored hybrid approaches [12,13], this paper specifically demonstrates how to achieve linear convergence with O(log(1/ε)) full gradient evaluations, which differs from SAG and SDCA that require O(1/ε) iterations (though with different computational complexity).

3. **Comparative Analysis**: The paper provides a thorough comparison with existing methods in Table 2, showing EMGD's computational complexity advantages when κ ≤ n^(2/3) compared to Nesterov's method [17], and its advantages over SAG and SDCA in terms of storage requirements (O(d) vs O(n)), constraint handling, and high-probability convergence guarantees.

4. **Practical Considerations**: The authors acknowledge the limitation of requiring knowledge of κ and provide concrete suggestions for estimating it in machine learning contexts (e.g., using regularizers or sampling-based Hessian estimation), making the approach more practical.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical with no experimental results. While theoretical analysis is valuable, the absence of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical efficiency of EMGD compared to existing methods, particularly regarding the claimed computational complexity advantages.

2. **Condition Number Knowledge Requirement**: The algorithm requires prior knowledge of the condition number κ, which is often not available in practice. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest estimation methods, these are not fully developed, and the paper does not discuss how estimation errors affect performance.

3. **Computational Complexity Assumption**: The paper assumes that a full gradient is n times more expensive to compute than a stochastic gradient, which may not hold in all practical scenarios. This assumption is critical to the claimed complexity advantage but is not thoroughly justified.

4. **Limited Scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on smooth and strongly convex problems. While this is a well-motivated setting, the approach does not address non-convex optimization, which is increasingly important in machine learning applications.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to convex optimization with a novel algorithm that achieves linear convergence with condition number independent full gradient access. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the comparison with existing methods is thorough. The main limitations are the lack of empirical validation and the requirement for prior knowledge of κ, though the authors appropriately acknowledge these.

The significance of this work lies in addressing a practical computational bottleneck in large-scale optimization problems. For ill-conditioned problems where full gradient evaluation is expensive, the ability to reduce full gradient evaluations from O(√κ log(1/ε)) to O(log(1/ε)) represents a meaningful theoretical advance. The storage efficiency (O(d) vs O(n)) and ability to handle constrained problems provide additional practical advantages over SAG and SDCA.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization. The theoretical contribution is significant enough to advance the state of the art in first-order optimization methods.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of convex optimization with a novel algorithm that addresses a meaningful practical limitation. The technical analysis is sound, the novelty is clear, and the significance is demonstrated through thorough comparisons with existing methods. While the lack of empirical validation is a limitation, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

**Pro**: Rigorous theoretical analysis, novel algorithm design, clear comparison with existing methods, practical considerations regarding κ estimation.

**Con**: No empirical validation, requires knowledge of κ, computational complexity assumption may not hold in all settings.

The paper meets all four review criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) at a high level, with minor weaknesses that do not undermine the overall contribution. I recommend acceptance.
