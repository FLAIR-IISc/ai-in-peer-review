**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a new first-order optimization algorithm, Epoch Mixed Gradient Descent (EMGD), for minimizing functions that are both smooth and strongly convex. The primary motivation is to reduce the number of computationally expensive full gradient evaluations required by standard accelerated methods, whose complexity depends on the problem's condition number, `κ`. Standard accelerated gradient descent (AGD) requires `O(√κ log(1/ε))` full gradient calls to find an `ε`-optimal solution. The proposed EMGD algorithm leverages access to both a full gradient oracle and a stochastic gradient oracle to reduce this dependency.

The algorithm operates in epochs. At the beginning of each epoch, a single full gradient is computed at an anchor point. Within the epoch, a series of updates are performed using a "mixed gradient," which is a variance-reduced estimator constructed from the initial full gradient and stochastic gradients at the current and anchor points. This is combined with a search domain that shrinks in each epoch. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that EMGD achieves an `ε`-optimal solution with high probability, requiring `O(log(1/ε))` full gradient evaluations and `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))` stochastic gradient evaluations. This makes the number of full gradient computations independent of the condition number `κ`. The paper compares this complexity to AGD and other linearly convergent methods for finite-sum problems like SAG and SDCA, highlighting advantages in its applicability to constrained problems and its low memory footprint.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper addresses the significant practical problem of reducing the computational cost of optimization for ill-conditioned problems, where full gradient evaluations are a bottleneck. The goal of making the number of full gradient calls independent of the condition number is a strong and novel contribution.

2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The main result (Theorem 1) provides a high-probability linear convergence guarantee (`O(log(1/ε))` dependence on precision `ε`) where the number of full gradient calls is independent of `κ`. This is a theoretically interesting result that formally demonstrates a trade-off between a small number of expensive full gradient calls and a larger number of cheaper stochastic gradient calls.

3.  **Broader Applicability:** Unlike several related variance-reduced methods (e.g., SAG, SDCA) that are designed specifically for unconstrained finite-sum problems, the proposed framework for EMGD can handle general convex constraints, as long as the projection step (Step 8 in Algorithm 1) is efficient. This broadens its potential applicability.

4.  **Low Memory Requirement:** The algorithm only needs to store the current iterates and the anchor point, resulting in an `O(d)` memory footprint. This is a practical advantage over methods like SAG, which require `O(nd)` storage to store past gradients for each data point in a finite-sum setting.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Omission of Key Related Work:** The "mixed gradient" estimator, `g̃_kt = ∇F(w̄_k) + ∇f_kt(w_kt) - ∇f_kt(w̄_k)`, is identical to the variance-reduced gradient estimator introduced in the SVRG algorithm (Johnson and Zhang, "Accelerating Stochastic Gradient Descent using Predictive Variance Reduction", NIPS 2013). SVRG is a seminal work in this area and is highly relevant. The proposed algorithm can be interpreted as an SVRG variant applied in an epoch-based scheme with a shrinking domain constraint. The lack of citation and comparison to SVRG significantly impacts the assessment of the paper's originality.

2.  **High Stochastic Gradient Complexity:** The theoretical analysis shows that the number of stochastic gradients required is `O(κ^2 log(1/ε))`. The dependence on `κ^2` is very high and compares unfavorably to other variance-reduced methods. For example, in the finite-sum setting, SVRG's complexity is `O((n+κ)log(1/ε))`, which typically has a much better dependence on `κ`. For ill-conditioned problems (large `κ`), the `κ^2` factor could make EMGD prohibitively slow in practice, potentially overwhelming the savings from fewer full gradient calls.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. No experiments are provided to validate the proposed algorithm's performance. For an optimization paper, empirical evaluation is crucial to demonstrate that the theoretical improvements translate into practical gains. An experimental comparison against AGD, SVRG, and SAG/SDCA would be necessary to understand the practical trade-offs of EMGD, especially given the high `κ^2` factor in its complexity.

4.  **Dependence on Known Parameters:** The setting of the algorithm's hyperparameters, specifically the step size `η` and the number of inner iterations `T`, requires knowledge of the smoothness constant `L` and the strong convexity constant `λ` (and thus `κ`). While this is a common requirement in theoretical analyses, it is a practical limitation, as these constants are often unknown. The authors acknowledge this and suggest future work, but it remains a weakness of the current proposal.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel theoretical goal: making full gradient evaluations independent of the condition number.
*   It provides a complete high-probability convergence analysis for a non-trivial algorithm that combines ideas from epoch-based schemes and variance reduction.
*   The algorithm's design for constrained problems and its low memory usage are valuable practical features.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper fails to acknowledge or compare its core gradient estimation technique with SVRG, a highly influential and directly related prior work from NIPS. This is a major omission that overstates the work's novelty.
*   The `O(κ^2)` dependence on the condition number for the stochastic gradient complexity is severe and likely renders the algorithm impractical for the very ill-conditioned problems it aims to solve.
*   The complete absence of experiments makes it impossible to assess the practical value and performance trade-offs of the proposed method.

In summary, while the paper presents a theoretically interesting idea, its novelty is diminished by the omission of key related work, and its practical significance is questionable due to the high `κ^2` complexity and the lack of empirical validation.
