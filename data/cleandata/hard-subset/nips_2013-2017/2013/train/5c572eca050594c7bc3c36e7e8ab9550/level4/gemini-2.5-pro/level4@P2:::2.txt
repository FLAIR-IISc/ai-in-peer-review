This paper introduces a new framework for estimating the density ratio q/p, a problem central to importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The core idea is to reframe the estimation problem as a classical inverse problem by solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose two main formulations: Type I, derived from the importance sampling identity, which is suitable when samples from both distributions p and q are available; and Type II, based on the property of local kernels (e.g., Gaussian), which is applicable when q is a known function. These integral equations are solved using Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm penalty, leading to a family of simple and efficient algorithms named FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel case, and introduces a novel, fully unsupervised method for model selection called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Framework:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and provides a principled connection between this machine learning problem and classical methods in functional analysis. This approach is novel and separates the underlying integral equation from the regularization method, offering a flexible framework that can accommodate different norms and regularizers.

2.  **Unsupervised Model Selection:** A major contribution of this work is the development of the CD-CV method for hyperparameter selection (e.g., kernel width and regularization). The ability to perform cross-validation in a completely unsupervised setting is a significant advance. This is a key advantage over related methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM). In KMM, the objective function is based on an RKHS norm, making its value dependent on the choice of kernel. Consequently, comparing objective values across different kernel parameters is not meaningful, which complicates model selection. The L2 norm-based objective function proposed here provides a consistent performance measure that makes cross-validation a viable and effective strategy.

3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, deriving concentration bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean spaces and manifold settings. This adds significant rigor to the paper and supports the soundness of the proposed method.

4.  **Practicality and Performance:** The resulting FIRE algorithms have closed-form solutions that are simple to implement. The experimental results demonstrate strong performance on several datasets, showing that the method is competitive with or superior to existing approaches like LSIF and a thresholded kernel density estimator baseline.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Discussion on Theoretical Rates:** The theoretical analysis could be strengthened with a discussion on achieving faster convergence rates. The current results seem to require the kernel width `t` to decrease with the sample size to ensure convergence. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss whether polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size `n` can be achieved for a *fixed* kernel width `t`, perhaps under stronger yet reasonable assumptions. For example, in the related area of kernel ridge regression, works such as Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) establish optimal rates by making assumptions on the spectral decay of the integral operator. A similar analysis for the operator `K_p` in this context would significantly enhance the theoretical contribution.

2.  **Implications of Assumptions:** The method relies on the assumption that the target ratio `q/p` belongs to a well-behaved function class, such as a Sobolev space. While this is a common assumption in the field, its practical implications merit further discussion. In particular, this assumption can be quite strong, requiring knowledge about the tail behaviors of both `p` and `q`. Furthermore, if `q/p` is smooth, the inverse ratio `p/q` may not be, which would limit the method's applicability if the roles of the two distributions were swapped. A brief discussion of these limitations would provide valuable context for practitioners.

3.  **Clarity of Supplementary Material:** The main paper is well-written, but the supplementary material requires substantial revision. It contains numerous typos and errors which make it very difficult to follow the proofs and verify the correctness of the theoretical claims presented in the main text. For instance, there appear to be inconsistencies in notation (e.g., `W2^2` vs `Ws^2` in Lemma 5) and incorrect intermediate expressions. Given that the theoretical results are a key contribution, it is crucial that the supplementary material is thoroughly proofread and corrected to ensure the work is fully supported and reproducible.

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel and well-founded approach to density ratio estimation. Its main strengths are the elegant formulation as an inverse problem and, most importantly, the development of a practical and effective method for unsupervised cross-validation. The experimental results are encouraging. The primary areas for improvement are in strengthening the theoretical analysis by exploring conditions for faster convergence rates, providing more discussion on the limitations of the underlying assumptions, and thoroughly revising the supplementary material for clarity and correctness. Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field.
