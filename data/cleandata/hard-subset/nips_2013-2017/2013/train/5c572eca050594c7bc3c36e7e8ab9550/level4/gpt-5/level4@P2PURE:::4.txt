The authors propose two equalities to construct estimators of the Radon–Nikodym derivative f = p/q: (i) Ep[k(x, .) f] = Eq[k(x, .)] and (ii) Ep[k(x, .) f] ≈ q(x). They replace the corresponding integral operators with empirical counterparts. The family {k(x, .)} serves as a set of test functions over which the discrepancy between the two sides is minimized. Choosing {k(x, .)} has the advantage that standard kernel methods can be used for optimization.

They provide convergence guarantees for both setups under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. As expected for density estimation, the rates depend critically on the ambient dimension.

Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution.

Comments:
- Convergence rates: Optimizing over the set {k(x, .)} is sufficient to ensure convergence to the density ratio. For (ii) this feels intuitive due to the bandwidth dependence. It would be helpful to include a brief intuitive explanation for why the same reasoning carries over to (i).
- On page 2 you optimize over L2,p, but in (9) the objective mixes L2,p and L2,q. Some motivation for this choice of cost function would be useful. Ultimately, such choices should be tied to the target downstream tasks; it would be good to connect particular cost functions to representative applications.
- Applications: One example is importance sampling, where q/p is relevant for transporting Eq to Ep. However, in your empirical objectives you are already estimating Ep and Eq to build the loss. Why not directly use the Eq you have there? Can Ep(q/p) be superior to directly using Eq? I am asking for motivation for the overall setup, since you need to estimate Ep and Eq anyway in order to estimate q/p. You briefly mention on p. 5 that sampling from Eq can be difficult. Is this the primary application you have in mind?
- Regularization: You enforce smoothness of the ratio q/p, whereas the theorem later assumes smoothness of q and p (which seems the natural place to impose it). Is there a principled relationship between smoothness of q and p and smoothness of their ratio? One can construct many different q and p with the same ratio, potentially with irregular behavior by modifying them on countably or uncountably many points. So perhaps one direction is p, q smooth (and possibly bounded away from zero) ⇒ ratio smooth (?), or conversely ratio smooth ⇒ there exist smooth p and q producing that ratio.
- Please discuss the dependence of your rates on the dimension d. For what ranges of d do you expect the method to remain practical?
- Is there a strong motivation for your particular family of test functions U?
- A more general question: In q/p, covariate shift, and related settings, a central challenge is selecting a generic cost function. One route is to express the problem via expectation operators and probe them with suitable test functions to recover the quantities of interest. Do you believe {k(x, .)} is “the right” choice here? There are similarities with [1] in terms of using transformations to access the target quantities. Your approach to estimating the Radon–Nikodym derivative is more elegant by working with test functions, but the overall strategy appears related.

[1] Smooth Operators; Grünwald, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor

I think this is a timely and strong paper on a relevant problem, with solid convergence analysis and informative experiments.
