## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission: [Paper Title - Please Fill In]

This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating the ratio of two probability density functions. The authors propose a solution based on a kernel method and framing the problem as a Fredholm integral equation, leading to an inverse problem formulation. While the problem itself is undeniably important, and the proposed approach offers a novel perspective, several significant concerns regarding the theoretical foundations and clarity of assumptions warrant careful consideration.

From a machine learning perspective, estimating probability density functions is inherently difficult. It is well-established that within any epsilon neighborhood of a probability measure *P* possessing a density with respect to a measure *ν*, there exist probability measures that do *not* have a density with respect to *ν*. Consequently, the estimation of a density ratio – and the reliance on the existence of such densities – is fundamentally problematic. The authors do not adequately address this foundational challenge. 

A crucial omission is a clear specification of the dominating measure *ν* used throughout the analysis. Is it the Lebesgue measure? A counting measure? Or something else entirely? This is not merely a detail; the choice of *ν* dramatically impacts the validity of the proposed method and its theoretical guarantees. Furthermore, without this specification, it’s difficult to assess the rigor of the results, particularly in cases where sets of *ν*-measure zero may play a critical role.  The sensitivity of the results to such sets needs to be explicitly addressed.

Beyond the choice of *ν*, the paper lacks a precise statement of its core assumptions.  For instance, the space in which *x* resides is undefined. Does *x* belong to *R<sup>d</sup>*, or is it defined on a more general topological space?  Similarly, is the density *p* assumed to be strictly positive everywhere, or are zero-density regions permitted? Clarifying these assumptions is essential for understanding the scope and limitations of the proposed method. The absence of these fundamental details hinders a proper evaluation of the contribution.

Specifically, the formulation of the Fredholm integral equation feels somewhat disconnected from practical application without a clear understanding of the underlying measure space and the properties of the kernel employed.  More detail on the kernel's properties (e.g., positive definiteness, smoothness) and its impact on the solution would be beneficial.

In summary, while the approach presented is intriguing, the lack of clarity regarding fundamental assumptions, the dominating measure *ν*, and potential issues arising from sets of zero measure raise serious concerns. Addressing these points is critical to establishing the theoretical soundness and practical relevance of the proposed method.  Without these clarifications, it’s difficult to assess the overall contribution and recommend acceptance.
