The paper proposes a method for estimating the ratio q/p of two probability density functions by reformulating the task as a Fredholm integral equation and solving the resulting inverse problem using kernels. 

While the idea is interesting, I have several concerns about the foundational assumptions and their implications for machine learning practice and for the rigor of the analysis.

- Assumption of densities. From a machine learning perspective, the assumption that distributions admit densities with respect to a fixed dominating measure is quite strong. It is well known that in any ε-neighborhood of a probability measure P that has a ν-density, there exist probability measures that do not have ν-densities. Consequently, estimating the ratio of two densities can be problematic in ML settings, where small perturbations of the data-generating distribution may take us outside the class of absolutely continuous measures. It would help to discuss how the proposed approach behaves under such perturbations, and whether any robustness guarantees are available when densities fail to exist or are only approximately defined.

- Dominating measure. As far as I can tell, the paper does not specify the dominating measure ν with respect to which p and q are defined (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure, or another measure on a structured space). Without this, it is unclear how to interpret statements about densities, and whether the results are intended to be invariant to the choice of ν. Moreover, some arguments may hinge on properties on sets of ν-measure zero; please clarify how these are handled to ensure the results are rigorous.

- Statement of assumptions. I am missing a clear and consolidated statement of the assumptions under which the results hold. For example:
  - What is the domain of x? Does x lie in R^d, a subset thereof, a manifold, or a more general topological/measurable space?
  - Is the density p strictly positive everywhere on its support, or only almost everywhere? Are lower/upper bounds on p or q assumed?
  - Which regularity properties (e.g., smoothness, support conditions) are required of p, q, and the ratio q/p?
  - Which properties of the kernel are essential (e.g., Gaussian, δ-family behavior), and which are optional?

Clarifying these points would make it much easier to assess the scope and rigor of the theoretical results and to understand their applicability in typical ML scenarios where absolute continuity may fail or boundaries/singularities arise.
