## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission

This paper presents a compelling and well-executed approach to estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative, *f = p/q*, using two distinct estimation strategies derived from fundamental equalities relating expectations under *p* and *q*. The core idea of replacing integral operators with empirical versions and optimizing over a family of test functions, {*k(x,.)*}, is elegantly implemented and allows for the application of standard kernel methods. The inclusion of convergence guarantees, under reasonable smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, significantly strengthens the contribution. Overall, this is a timely and strong paper addressing a relevant problem, supported by a convergence study and experimental results.

The authors derive two estimators: (i) *E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] = E<sub>q</sub>[k(x,.)]* and (ii) *E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] ≈ q(x)*. The choice of {*k(x,.)*} as test functions is particularly insightful, facilitating optimization using established kernel methods. The convergence rates, appropriately dependent on the dimensionality of the underlying space, are as expected for density estimation problems. 

I have a few suggestions that would further enhance the paper.

Regarding the convergence rates, the optimization over {*k(x,.)*} demonstrably guarantees convergence to the ratio. The intuition for this result is clear in the context of estimator (ii), with its inherent bandwidth dependence. However, a brief, intuitive explanation of why this also holds for estimator (i) would be beneficial for readers.

The optimization on page 2 utilizes an L<sub>2,p</sub> norm, while equation (9) employs a combination of L<sub>2,p</sub> and L<sub>2,q</sub> norms. Further motivation for this specific cost function choice would be valuable. Linking the cost function selection to practical applications – illustrating *why* these norms are preferred for specific tasks – would provide stronger justification.

Speaking of applications, the paper highlights importance sampling, where *q/p* is crucial for transitioning from *E<sub>q</sub>* to *E<sub>p</sub>*. Given that the method estimates both *E<sub>p</sub>* and *E<sub>q</sub>* to determine the empirical cost function, I’m curious about the advantage of this approach over directly utilizing the estimated *E<sub>q</sub>*. Could *E<sub>p</sub>(q/p)* provide a substantial improvement over simply using *E<sub>q</sub>*? A clearer articulation of the motivation behind this setup – particularly concerning the need to estimate both expectations to estimate *q/p* – would be helpful. The mention on page 5 regarding the difficulty of sampling from *E<sub>q</sub>* touches upon this, and clarifying whether this difficulty is the primary driving application would be useful.

The paper imposes a smoothness constraint on the ratio *q/p* via regularization, and subsequently states smoothness assumptions on *q* and *p* in the theorem. Exploring the relationship between the smoothness of *q* and *p* and the smoothness of their ratio would be interesting. While it's true that numerous *q* and *p* distributions can yield the same ratio, potentially exhibiting irregularity, a statement like "smooth *q* and *p* (possibly bounded away from 0) implies a smooth ratio" or "a smooth ratio implies the existence of smooth *p* and *q* distributions that produce it" might be insightful.

A discussion of how the dimensionality (*d*) affects the convergence rates would be valuable. For what dimensions can this method be expected to perform well? Providing guidance on the practical applicability based on the problem's dimensionality would strengthen the paper.

Similarly, elaborating on the motivation for the chosen family of test functions, *U*, would be beneficial. What properties of *U* are particularly advantageous for this estimation problem?

Finally, a more fundamental question arises: in the context of covariate shift and similar *q/p* settings, defining a generic cost function often proves challenging. The approach of leveraging expectation operators and probing them with diverse test functions to extract underlying densities is a common strategy. Do the authors believe this approach, utilizing the set {*k(x,.)*}, represents an optimal solution? There are parallels to the work in [1], which also employs transformations to estimate quantities of interest. While the proposed method offers a more elegant approach to the Radon-Nikodym derivative through the use of test functions, the overall strategy shares similarities.

[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor



In conclusion, this is a strong contribution that addresses a relevant problem with a theoretically sound and well-executed method. Addressing the points raised above would further enhance the paper's clarity and impact.
