- Proposes estimating density ratios q/p by recasting the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.  
- Develops two formulations: Type I (importance-sampling identity with general kernel operator Kp, L2(p) discrepancy) and Type II (local kernel Kt,p approximating q as t→0).  
- Both formulations yield closed-form, easily implemented algorithms (FIRE) with concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and smooth submanifolds.  
- Introduces CD-CV, a fully unsupervised cross-validation procedure that leverages the L2 formulation to select hyperparameters using samples from both p and q.  
- Relates to KMM and LSIF: unlike KMM’s RKHS-mean matching and LSIF’s parametric bases, the approach places the regression target in L2 and regularizes in an RKHS, avoiding basis design and enabling spectral analysis and out-of-sample extension.  
- Strength: elegant inverse-problem formulation connecting operator theory to ML practice, producing stable estimators with straightforward evaluation.  
- Strength: CD-CV is a practical, compelling method for unsupervised model selection, filling a notable gap.  
- Strength: nontrivial theoretical analysis (concentration bounds and rates) for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean and manifold domains.  
- Strength: experiments show competitive or superior accuracy and improved stability over KMM in some regimes, with lower computational cost.  
- Weakness: stated convergence rates appear slow for fixed kernel width t; paper lacks discussion of additional assumptions (source conditions, spectral decay) that could yield polynomial rates.  
- Weakness: strong smoothness/regularity assumptions on q/p are required; paper needs discussion of implications for tails, support mismatch, and cases where p and q supports differ.  
- Weakness: supplementary material contains numerous typos and errors that hinder verification of theoretical claims and must be carefully revised.  
- Weakness: limited practical guidance on choosing between Type I and Type II and on how t affects bias–variance trade-offs.  
- Additional remark: should explicitly note why RKHS-norm objectives (as in KMM) are ill-suited for kernel selection via CV, and how the L2 criterion avoids this.  
- Additional remark: recommend discussing robustness to model misspecification (large q/p in low-p regions) and practical safeguards like thresholding/clipping.  
- Evaluation: technically sound and original with significant potential impact, but clarity issues in notation and the supplement need correction.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled inverse-problem view, practical unsupervised model selection, theoretical guarantees, and competitive empirical performance.  
- Cons for acceptance: slow-looking rates without stronger-assumption discussion, strong q/p assumptions, and errors in the supplement.  
- Recommendation: strong, well-motivated contribution; accept after supplement corrections and added discussion on conditions for faster rates and limitations of q/p assumptions.
