- Summary: Formulates density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and proposes kernel-regularized inverse solutions from samples.  
- Strength: Elegant operator-theoretic Fredholm formulation linking density-ratio estimation to classical inverse problems.  
- Strength: Potential to unify and extend existing density-ratio methods via standard regularization tools.  
- Strength: May yield theoretical insights on existence, uniqueness, and stability if assumptions are clarified.  
- Major weakness: Ambiguous measure-theoretic setup—paper refers to “densities” without specifying a dominating measure ν, causing technical issues.  
- Major weakness: Should target the Radon–Nikodym derivative r = dP/dQ under explicit P ≪ Q instead of unspecified ν-densities.  
- Weakness: Domain and function-space assumptions are missing—unclear sample space X, kernel properties, and the space where r lives (L2(Q), RKHS, etc.).  
- Weakness: Identifiability/support assumptions (P ≪ Q, nonnegativity of r, normalization) are not articulated or enforced.  
- Weakness: Ill-posedness and regularization are not sufficiently developed—choice of regularizer, parameter selection, and sample-to-population operator approximations are unclear.  
- Weakness: No finite-sample guarantees, consistency proofs, or rates tied to operator spectral properties and source conditions.  
- Weakness: Relation to prior work is underdeveloped—connections and differences with KLIEP, uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF, KMM, and logistic methods are not clarified or empirically compared.  
- Weakness: Practical aspects like computational complexity, scalability (Nyström/random features), sensitivity to kernel/λ, and constraint handling (nonnegativity/normalization) are not addressed.  
- Technical suggestion: Reframe target as r = dP/dQ on (X, 𝒳) with explicit P ≪ Q and avoid unspecified ν except for concrete examples.  
- Technical suggestion: Specify X (e.g., separable metric or subset of R^d), kernel assumptions (bounded, p.d., measurable), and define the RKHS H and Q-integral operator T_Q and P-mean g_P.  
- Technical suggestion: State the core operator equation T_Q r = g_P and the precise regularized objective and its empirical representer-form solution.  
- Technical suggestion: Explain how λ and kernel parameters are chosen (e.g., cross-validation on surrogate risk) and how empirical operators concentrate around population operators.  
- Technical suggestion: State source conditions (e.g., r in H or r = T_Q^β h), derive consistency and rates based on eigenvalue decay and provide high-probability bounds.  
- Technical suggestion: Address constraints—discuss enforcing nonnegativity and normalization or justify unconstrained estimators and propose projection/constraint incorporation.  
- Experimental suggestion: Compare empirically to KLIEP, uLSIF/KuLSIF/RuLSIF, KMM, and logistic-ratio methods on synthetic and real covariate-shift tasks with relevant metrics (importance-weighted risk, test risk, direct r-error).  
- Experimental suggestion: Report sensitivity analyses for λ and kernel bandwidth, runtime/scalability (e.g., Nyström), and ablations showing regularization impact on stability.  
- Clarity suggestion: List all assumptions in one place, be explicit about a.e. statements and Q-null sets, and provide intuition/derivation linking r = dP/dQ to the operator equation.  
- Clarity suggestion: Improve notation consistency and define all operators, norms, and inner products where used.  
- Originality/significance: Operator framing is promising and could unify methods, but impact depends on precise measure-theoretic setup, rigorous analysis, and thorough empirical comparison.  
- Overall evaluation: Promising idea with appealing connection to Fredholm equations, but currently lacks essential assumptions, rigorous theory, and solid empirical baselines—authors should address (i) reframing target, (ii) functional-analytic setup and regularization, (iii) theoretical guarantees, and (iv) stronger experiments.
