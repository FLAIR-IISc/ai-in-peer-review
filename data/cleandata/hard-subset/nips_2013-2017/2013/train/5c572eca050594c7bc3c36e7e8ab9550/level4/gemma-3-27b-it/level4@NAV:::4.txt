The authors present a method for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative, f = p/q, by deriving and analyzing two estimators based on the equalities: (i) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] = E<sub>q</sub>[k(x,.)] and (ii) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] ≈ q(x). They effectively replace integral operators with their empirical counterparts, framing the problem as minimizing the difference between the left- and right-hand sides of these equations using a set of test functions {k(x,.)}.  Importantly, the selection of {k(x,.)} allows for the application of standard kernel methods during optimization.

The paper provides convergence guarantees for both estimators under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, with convergence rates appropriately dependent on the dimensionality of the underlying space. Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution. 

However, a few points could be clarified:

*   Regarding the convergence rates: the optimization over {k(x,.)} is shown to guarantee convergence to the ratio. While the bandwidth dependence explains the result for (ii), a brief intuitive explanation of why this also holds for (i) would be beneficial.
*   The cost function transitions from an L<sub>2,p</sub> norm on page 2 to a combination of L<sub>2,p</sub> and L<sub>2,q</sub> in equation (9). More motivation for this choice would strengthen the paper. Connecting the cost function specifically to performance in key applications would be particularly helpful.
*   The authors cite importance sampling (moving from E<sub>q</sub> to E<sub>p</sub> via q/p) as an application. Given that the method estimates both E<sub>p</sub> and E<sub>q</sub> to construct the empirical cost function, it's unclear why directly utilizing the estimated E<sub>q</sub> wouldn't suffice. A justification for estimating both expectations to estimate q/p would be appreciated. The sentence on page 5 regarding the difficulty of sampling from E<sub>q</sub> hints at this motivation, and explicitly stating this as the primary application would be helpful.
*   The regularizer enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p. The theorem later includes smoothness assumptions on q and p themselves, which is a natural condition. Exploring the relationship between the smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of their ratio would be insightful.  While many q and p could yield the same ratio, perhaps a connection exists such as smooth (and bounded away from 0) q and p implying a smooth ratio, or a smooth ratio implying the existence of smooth q and p that produce it.
*   Discussion of how the convergence rates scale with dimension (d) is lacking. Specifying the dimensions for which the method is expected to be effective would be beneficial.
*   Further justification for the choice of the test function family U would be valuable.
*   A broader, more fundamental question: in covariate shift scenarios, defining a generic cost function is a central challenge. The authors' approach of leveraging expectation operators and employing various test functions to estimate densities appears promising. It would be interesting to consider whether {k(x,.)} represents an optimal approach in this context. The work shares similarities with [1] in its manipulation of transformations to estimate quantities of interest; however, the use of test functions provides a more elegant solution for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative.

[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor

In conclusion, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem with a thorough convergence study and experimental validation.
