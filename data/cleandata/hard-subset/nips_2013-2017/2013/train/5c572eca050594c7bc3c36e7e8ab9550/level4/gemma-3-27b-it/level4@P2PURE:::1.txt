This paper introduces a novel method for density ratio estimation, a problem for which several approaches already exist. The key contribution lies in reformulating this estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

The paper’s strengths include its clear and engaging writing style, and the inherent importance and interest of the density ratio estimation problem itself.

However, there are several points that require attention:

(i) The paper omits certain details requiring justification. For instance, the claim that \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t) lacks either a proof within the paper or its supplementary material, or a supporting reference. This is needed for all such statements, even those that may appear straightforward.

(ii) The method extends to density estimation as a special case when q=1. Interestingly, this approach avoids the need for bandwidth convergence to zero, a limitation of standard kernel density estimation which is not consistent in such cases. Expanding the discussion to highlight the distinct roles of the bandwidth parameters here compared to those in KDE would be beneficial for readers.

(iii) A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would be helpful, as this technique may not be widely familiar to the NIPS audience.

(iv) All proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, and I have not verified their correctness.

(v) It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion outlining why a naive plug-in approach – separately estimating densities p and q – is suboptimal compared to the proposed method.

(vi) The paper lacks a clear comparison of its performance to existing techniques. For example, [12] also derives minimax bounds, and it is unclear how the upper bounds presented in Corollaries 2 and 4 compare to the optimal minimax rate.

(vii) Please provide references for the datasets used in the experimental evaluation (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) Demonstrating the method’s behavior on simplified, toy problems with known p and q distributions is currently missing. For example, a plot of the true and estimated density ratio for 1-dimensional p and q could visually illustrate the quality of the estimation.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on density ratio estimation. However, its relative performance compared to other algorithms (e.g., [12]) remains unclear, and the inclusion of illustrative examples using simple 1-dimensional densities would further strengthen the work.
