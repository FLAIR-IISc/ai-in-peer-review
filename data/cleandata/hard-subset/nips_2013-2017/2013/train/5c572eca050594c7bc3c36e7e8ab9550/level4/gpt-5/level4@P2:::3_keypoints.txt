- Paper reframes density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and solves it via Tikhonov-Phillips regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form FIRE estimators.
- Introduces CD-CV, an unsupervised model-selection method based on validating the importance-sampling identity with random test functions.
- Provides nonasymptotic concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth compact manifolds.
- Connects to prior work (LSIF, KLIEP, KMM) by framing density-ratio estimation as an inverse problem and using spectral/operator-theoretic analysis.
- Conceptual strength: principled unification of importance sampling, kernel integral operators, and inverse problems.
- Algorithmic strength: simple closed-form solutions with natural out-of-sample extensions via kernel matrix operations.
- Flexibility: supports multiple loss norms and both Type I (q samples) and Type II (pointwise q evaluations) data scenarios.
- Theoretical strength: provides relatively uncommon nonasymptotic bounds and rates for this literature.
- Practical strength: CD-CV is simple to implement; empirical results show competitive performance and computational efficiency.
- Major weakness: assumptions about measurable space, dominating measure, and absolute continuity (where q/p is defined) are not clearly and early stated.
- Major weakness: domain and smoothness assumptions are scattered; a consolidated assumptions subsection is needed (domain, boundaries, boundedness, positivity, smoothness).
- Major weakness: ambiguity between the integral-operator kernel and the RKHS kernel (k vs k_H) and their effects on bias, stability, and rates; practitioner guidance missing.
- Major weakness: identifiability and nonnegativity/normalization constraints for q/p (especially in Type II) are not discussed.
- Major weakness: CD-CV lacks discussion of statistical properties (bias, dependence on test-function family, robustness to sample imbalance).
- Major weakness: experiments need more details on computational complexity, numerical stability (conditioning with small λ), sensitivity analyses, and comparisons to KMM with out-of-sample extension.
- Quality: technical development appears sound but would benefit from clearer assumption statements alongside theorems.
- Clarity: paper is notation-dense; recommend an early assumptions section, clearer distinction of kernels, explicit handling of p=0, and a short complexity discussion.
- Originality: inverse-problem/Fredholm framing and spectral analysis are a fresh angle; CD-CV is a practical, novel validation idea.
- Significance: a stable, regularized, out-of-sample-capable density-ratio estimator with theory and unsupervised model selection is useful for covariate shift and related tasks.
- Pros for acceptance: principled formulation, solid theory, simple practical algorithms, unsupervised model selection, competitive empirical results.
- Cons for acceptance: unclear assumptions about measures/support, ambiguous kernel choices, limited CD-CV theory and experimental ablations.
- Minor comments: fix typos (e.g., “TiknonovPhilips”), be consistent with norm/operator notation, and avoid symbol overloading.
- Recommendation: borderline accept pending clarifications on dominating measure, domain/support/positivity assumptions, kernel-choice notation, and CD-CV and computational discussions.
