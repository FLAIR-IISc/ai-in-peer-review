This paper proposes two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative, f = p/q, derived from the following two equalities:
(i) E_p[k(x,・)f] = E_q[k(x,・)]
(ii) E_p[k(x,・)f] ∝ q(x)

In practice, the expectation operators are replaced with their empirical estimates based on samples from p and q. The set of functions {k(x,・)}, parameterized by x, serves as a family of test functions. The goal is to find an f that minimizes the discrepancy between the left- and right-hand sides of these equalities. A key advantage of using a reproducing kernel k is that the optimization can be performed efficiently using standard kernel methods.

The paper provides convergence guarantees for both estimators, derived under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities p and q. As is typical for density estimation problems, the convergence rates exhibit a strong dependence on the dimensionality of the input space.

Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable piece of work that addresses a relevant problem. I have a few comments and questions for the authors:

*   **Intuition for Convergence:** The paper shows that optimizing over the function class {k(x,・)} is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the true density ratio. For estimator (ii), this is quite intuitive, especially considering the role of the kernel bandwidth. However, it would be helpful if the authors could provide a similar brief, intuitive argument for why this sufficiency also holds for estimator (i).

*   **Choice of Cost Function:** The choice of cost function warrants further discussion. On page 2, the optimization is performed with respect to the L_2(p) norm, whereas the objective in Equation (9) involves a combination of L_2(p) and L_2(q) norms. It would be beneficial to motivate this choice. Presumably, the ideal cost function depends on the downstream task. Could the authors connect the choice of these specific norms to important applications where they are particularly suitable?

*   **Motivation for Importance Sampling Application:** The paper discusses importance sampling as a key application, where the goal is to estimate an expectation under one distribution (p) using samples from another (q). This requires estimating the density ratio f = p/q. However, the proposed method requires samples from *both* p and q to construct the empirical objective function. This raises a question: if samples from p are already available (for training the estimator), why not use them to compute the target expectation E_p[h(x)] directly via a standard Monte Carlo average? What is the advantage of the more indirect route of first estimating f = p/q and then computing an importance-weighted average using samples from q? A sentence on page 5 suggests that direct sampling might be difficult, but it would be helpful to elaborate on this. Clarifying the motivation for this setup would be valuable.

*   **Regularization and Smoothness Assumptions:** The proposed method uses a regularizer that enforces smoothness on the estimated ratio p/q. In contrast, the theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities p and q. It would be insightful for the authors to briefly discuss the relationship between these two notions of smoothness. For instance, if p and q are smooth (and q is bounded away from zero), their ratio should also be smooth. Does this justify imposing a smoothness regularizer on the ratio? Conversely, a single ratio can be generated by infinitely many pairs of (p, q), some of which could be highly irregular. Does the smoothness of the ratio imply the existence of at least one pair of smooth densities (p, q) that could generate it?

*   **Dimensionality Dependence:** The paper shows that the convergence rates depend on the data dimensionality, d. It would be helpful to discuss the practical implications of this dependence. For what range of dimensions do the authors expect their method to be practically useful?

*   **Choice of Test Functions:** Could the authors provide more motivation for choosing the family of test functions U (the RKHS associated with the kernel k)? While the computational advantages are clear, is there a deeper justification for why this is a particularly suitable choice for density ratio estimation compared to other function classes?

*   **Relation to Broader Literature:** On a more principled level, a central challenge in problems like density ratio estimation and covariate shift is defining an appropriate objective function. The general approach of matching expectations over a class of test functions is powerful. Do the authors see their choice of {k(x,・)} as the most natural class of test functions for this problem, or is it primarily one of convenience? The overall strategy bears some resemblance to the work of Grünewald et al. [1] on "smooth operators," which also manipulates operators to estimate quantities of interest. While the present paper's use of test functions is arguably more direct, a brief comment on the relationship to this broader line of research would be welcome.

In summary, this is a strong and timely paper. The inclusion of a theoretical convergence analysis and empirical validation makes for a solid contribution to the field.
