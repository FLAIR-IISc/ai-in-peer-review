The paper proposes a new method to estimate the density ratio p/q, a problem that has been addressed by several existing methods. The key contribution of this paper lies in its reformulation of the density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

The paper has several strong points. It is well-written and enjoyable to read, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The density ratio estimation problem itself is important and interesting, warranting further research.

However, there are several weak points and areas that require clarification. Firstly, some details are missing from the paper. For instance, the statement that \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t) is not adequately justified. Either a proof (in the paper or in the supplementary material) or a reference is needed to support such claims, even if they seem straightforward to prove.

Secondly, the proposed method can be applied to density estimation as a special case (q=1). It is noteworthy that this approach does not require the bandwidth to converge to zero, unlike standard kernel density estimation (KDE), which is not consistent in that case. A more detailed discussion on this aspect would be beneficial, as it would help readers understand the distinct roles of the bandwidth parameters in this paper compared to KDE.

Thirdly, a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would be helpful, as this concept may be unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS community.

Fourthly, all the proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which I have not verified for correctness.

Fifthly, it would be informative to include a few sentences explaining why a naive plug-in approach, which involves separately estimating densities p and q, is inferior to the proposed method.

Sixthly, the paper does not provide a clear comparison of the proposed method's performance with that of other existing methods. For instance, [12] also derives minimax bounds. While Corollary 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate, it is unclear how far these bounds are from the optimal minimax rate.

Seventhly, the authors should provide references for the datasets used in the paper, namely CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS.

Lastly, I found it lacking that the paper does not demonstrate the proposed method's performance on toy problems with fixed and known p and q. For example, a plot of the true p/q and the estimated p/q for simple 1-dimensional p and q densities would help readers assess the quality of the estimation.

In conclusion, the paper presents a nice approach to density ratio estimation. However, its performance compared to other algorithms (e.g., [12]) is unclear, and a demonstration of its effectiveness on toy problems would be beneficial.
