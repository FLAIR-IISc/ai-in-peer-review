This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques. The authors leverage Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop a family of algorithms, termed FIRE, with associated theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The work establishes a connection between density ratio estimation and classical operator theory, offering a principled framework for analysis and algorithm design.

The core idea – reformulating the problem as solving a Fredholm equation – is insightful and well-motivated. The connection to existing kernel methods is clearly articulated, and the resulting algorithms are presented in a straightforward manner. The theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in various settings (Euclidean space and manifolds), is a significant contribution. The introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is also a valuable addition. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  The Fredholm equation perspective provides a fresh and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The convergence rates and concentration bounds, particularly for the Gaussian kernel, are a strong selling point.  These results provide valuable insight into the performance of the algorithms.
* **Algorithmic Simplicity:** The FIRE algorithms are relatively easy to implement due to their reliance on standard kernel methods.
* **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The CD-CV method addresses a crucial practical challenge in unsupervised learning.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively relates its approach to relevant literature, including Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF).
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance across various datasets and tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Intuition for Convergence (Eq. 1 vs Eq. 2):**  While the theoretical results are solid, a more intuitive explanation of why optimizing over the kernel family {k(x,.)} guarantees convergence to the ratio, particularly relating to the initial equalities (1) and (2), would be beneficial.
* **Cost Function Motivation:** The rationale for using a combination of L2,p and L2,q norms in equation (9) could be strengthened. Connecting the choice of the cost function more directly to specific applications would enhance understanding.  The paper mentions importance sampling as a key application; elaborating on why estimating q/p via estimating Ep and Eq is advantageous in this context – beyond the stated difficulty in sampling from q – would be helpful.
* **Smoothness Assumptions:** The interplay between smoothness assumptions on the densities (p and q) and the smoothness of their ratio is not fully explored. A discussion of whether smoothness of p and q necessarily implies smoothness of q/p, and vice versa, would be insightful.
* **Dimensionality Dependence:** A more detailed discussion of the dependence of the convergence rates on the dimensionality (d) of the data would be valuable.  Specifically, highlighting for which dimensions the proposed method is expected to perform well would be useful for potential users.
* **Test Function Family Justification:**  The choice of the test function family U for the CD-CV method could benefit from further justification.  Why are random linear and half-space functions particularly suitable for this purpose?
* **Broader Context:** While the paper highlights connections to related work, a more in-depth discussion of alternative approaches to tackling the generic cost function challenge in covariate shift adaptation would be valuable. Considering work such as [1] (Smooth Operators; Grünewälder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor) and contrasting its approach with the proposed method would strengthen the contribution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in machine learning.
* The convergence guarantees and experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.
* The work fills a gap in the literature by connecting density ratio estimation to classical operator theory.
* The code is available, facilitating reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Some aspects of the theoretical justification and cost function motivation could be further elaborated.
* A more thorough discussion of the dimensionality dependence and the choice of test functions would enhance the paper's impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation, theoretical analysis, and empirical validation are all commendable. While some aspects could be further refined, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
