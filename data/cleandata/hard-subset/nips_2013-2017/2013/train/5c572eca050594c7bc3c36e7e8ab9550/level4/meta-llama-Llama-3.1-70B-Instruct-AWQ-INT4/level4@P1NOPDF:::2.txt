This paper presents two novel methods for estimating the density ratio of two probabilities. The first method leverages the importance sampling equation and formulates it as a regularization problem, incorporating the L^2 norm of the deviation from equality and a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) regularization term, resulting in a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that must approach zero for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem, yielding a Type II Fredholm equation. The paper provides theoretical results on convergence and demonstrates the practical performance of these methods experimentally.

While the proposed methods share similarities with existing approaches, such as kernel mean matching (KMM), a significant advantage of the proposed methods is that the L^2 formulation enables the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. Unlike supervised learning problems, where CV is a dominant method for choosing parameters, there is a lack of general methods for unsupervised learning. The paper addresses this issue in density ratio estimation by cleverly utilizing the L^2 norm.

It is worth noting that the objective function derived from RKHS, as in KMM, is not suitable for kernel selection via CV, since the values of the objective function depend on the kernel, rendering them incomparable.

Theorem 1 derives a convergence rate with a (-t/log λ) factor, resulting in a slow convergence rate for fixed t. In contrast, many kernel method literatures have shown that, with fixed t, a polynomial rate can be achieved with respect to the sample size after fixing the rate of λ. It would be interesting to explore whether reasonable additional assumptions can lead to a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) make assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to derive the optimal rate for kernel ridge regression. A stronger assumption on q/p may also yield a polynomial rate. The authors may consider discussing such polynomial rates under stronger assumptions with fixed t.

In density ratio estimation, it is common to assume that q/p belongs to a nice function class, such as Sobolev. However, this assumption is quite strong, requiring prior knowledge on the ratio or tails of p and q. Furthermore, under such an assumption on q/p, the opposite ratio p/q often exhibits poor behavior. It is essential to acknowledge and discuss this limitation of density ratio estimation.

Although not a requirement, the supplementary materials need improvement. Typos and minor mistakes are prevalent, making it challenging to verify the correctness of the theoretical results in the main paper. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be ||ξ||^2 ≥. Since the theoretical results on convergence are a primary contribution of this paper, I advise the authors to revise the supplementary material.
