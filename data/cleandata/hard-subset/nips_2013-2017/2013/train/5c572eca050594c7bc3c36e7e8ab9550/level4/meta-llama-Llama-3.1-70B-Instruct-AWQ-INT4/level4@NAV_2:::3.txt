The authors propose a novel approach to estimating the ratio of two probability density functions, leveraging a kernel and a Fredholm integral equation to formulate an inverse problem. This method is particularly relevant in machine learning, where density estimation is a crucial task. However, it is well-known that estimating densities can be challenging, as any epsilon neighborhood of a probability measure P with a ν-density contains probability measures without ν-densities. Therefore, estimating the ratio of two densities is inherently problematic.

The authors' approach relies on the assumption of densities, which may be considered a strong assumption in the context of machine learning. A clear statement of the assumptions made in this work, such as the choice of dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure), the domain of the variable x (e.g., Rd, a general topological space), and the properties of the density p (e.g., positivity for all x), would be beneficial.

The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel in various settings. The authors also propose a simple and efficient algorithm, FIRE, for estimating the density ratio, which is based on the Fredholm inverse regularized estimator. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in comparison to existing alternatives.

Strengths of the paper include its novel formulation of the density ratio estimation problem as an inverse problem, its comprehensive theoretical analysis, and its simple and efficient algorithm. The paper also provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the material.

Weaknesses of the paper include the lack of clarity on the assumptions made, particularly with regards to the choice of dominating measure ν and the properties of the density p. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the proposed method in the context of machine learning.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to estimating the ratio of two probability density functions, which is a significant contribution to the field. The use of a Fredholm integral equation to formulate an inverse problem is a unique aspect of the paper.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the accuracy of density ratio estimation in various applications, including importance sampling and covariate shift. The proposed method is also simple and efficient, making it a practical solution for many problems.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning, with a novel approach to estimating the ratio of two probability density functions. While there are some areas for improvement, particularly with regards to clarity on assumptions and implications, the paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis.
