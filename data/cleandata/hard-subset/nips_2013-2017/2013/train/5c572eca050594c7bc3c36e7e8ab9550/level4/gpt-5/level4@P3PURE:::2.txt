This paper proposes two new methods for estimating the density ratio q/p. The first derives from the importance-sampling identity and casts estimation as a regularized problem that minimizes the L^2 deviation from the equality with an RKHS penalty; this corresponds to a Type I Fredholm equation. The second introduces a kernel whose locality parameter must shrink to zero for consistency and also formulates estimation via regularization; this corresponds to a Type II Fredholm equation. The paper provides convergence results and empirical evaluations.

Strengths
- The L^2-based formulation is a notable advantage over existing approaches such as KMM because it enables principled cross-validation (CV) for hyperparameter selection. In contrast to supervised learning—where CV is the standard practice—there is no generally accepted CV methodology in unsupervised settings; this paper offers a compelling solution for density-ratio estimation by leveraging the L^2 loss.
- The empirical demonstrations and the theoretical convergence analysis support the practicality of the approach.

Suggestions and questions
- Please explicitly remark that RKHS objective functions used by methods like KMM are not suitable for kernel selection via CV, since the numerical objective values depend on the choice of kernel and are therefore not directly comparable across kernels. This helps clarify the benefit of the proposed L^2 formulation for model selection.
- Convergence rate in Theorem 1: Because of the (-t / log λ) factor, the rate is slow for fixed t. In much of the kernel-methods literature, one can obtain polynomial rates in the sample size under fixed t after fixing the decay of λ. Are there reasonable additional assumptions under which the proposed estimator achieves a polynomial rate with fixed t? For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression by imposing assumptions on the spectrum of the relevant operator (here, K_{p,t}). I suspect that stronger smoothness or source-condition assumptions on q/p would yield polynomial rates. It would strengthen the paper to state such results or at least discuss them under stronger assumptions with fixed t.
- Assumptions on q/p: Density-ratio estimation typically requires that q/p belong to a “nice” function class, e.g., a Sobolev space. This is a strong assumption that effectively presumes knowledge about the ratio or about the tails of p and q. Moreover, when q/p is well-behaved under such assumptions, the inverse ratio p/q often behaves poorly. Please include a discussion of this limitation and its practical implications, as it is important for framing where density-ratio estimation is appropriate.

Comments on the supplementary material
- The supplementary materials contain numerous typos and minor errors that make it difficult to verify the main theoretical results. Since the convergence theory is a central contribution, I strongly recommend a careful revision. Specific issues include:
  - In Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should read D1 (t / -log λ)^2 + λ^{1−α} D2 ||f||_2^2.
  - The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq. (31) is correct.
  - In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be ||ξ||^2 ≥ [appropriate bound], not as currently written.

Reference
- Caponnetto, A., and De Vito, E. Optimal Rates for Regularized Least-Squares Algorithm. Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 7:331–368 (2007).

Overall assessment
- The paper offers a promising approach to density-ratio estimation, with the clear practical advantage that CV can be effectively applied for hyperparameter selection. Addressing the convergence-rate question under stronger assumptions, discussing the limitations imposed by function-class assumptions on q/p (and the poor behavior of p/q), and thoroughly correcting the supplementary material would significantly strengthen the work.
