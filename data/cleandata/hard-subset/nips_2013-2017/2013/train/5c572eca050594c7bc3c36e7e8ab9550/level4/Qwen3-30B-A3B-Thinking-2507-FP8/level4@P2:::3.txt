

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors introduce the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms, which they theoretically analyze for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and compact sub-manifolds. The paper also introduces CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a completely unsupervised model selection method that addresses a key challenge in unsupervised learning.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a principled connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, providing a new theoretical perspective that bridges statistical learning and operator theory. Second, the authors develop a flexible family of algorithms within the kernel learning framework that are theoretically grounded and computationally efficient. Third, they provide detailed theoretical analysis including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel in both Euclidean and manifold settings. Finally, the CD-CV method offers a practical solution to model selection in unsupervised density ratio estimation, which is often a challenging problem.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-executed, providing concrete error bounds and convergence rates that advance our understanding of density ratio estimation. The connection to classical operator theory is insightful and provides a solid foundation for the proposed methods. The experimental results demonstrate strong performance compared to existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE), particularly in computational efficiency. The CD-CV method is particularly novel and addresses a critical gap in unsupervised learning literature.

However, the paper has several important weaknesses that need addressing. The most significant issue is the lack of clarity regarding the mathematical foundations. The authors do not specify the dominating measure ν used in their density ratio formulation (Lebesgue, counting, or another measure), which is essential for rigor. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether their theoretical results hold generally or only under specific measure-theoretic assumptions. 

The paper also fails to clearly state fundamental assumptions about the domain and density properties. It's unclear whether the authors assume the data resides in R^d or a more general topological space, and it's ambiguous whether the density p is positive everywhere (a critical assumption for density ratio estimation). The abstract states "p is a density function" but doesn't clarify if this implies p(x) > 0 for all x in the support, which would be necessary for the ratio q/p to be well-defined.

Additionally, while the authors reference related work including Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), they could better articulate how their theoretical framework differs from these methods. For instance, while noting that KMM can be viewed within their Type I setting, they don't sufficiently explain how their regularization approach provides advantages over KMM's optimization procedure.

The paper's clarity would also benefit from more precise mathematical notation. For example, the distinction between the kernel used for the integral operator (k) and the RKHS kernel (k_H) is sometimes ambiguous, which could confuse readers. The authors should clarify these distinctions throughout the paper.

From a significance perspective, the paper addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning (density ratio estimation) that has important applications in covariate shift, transfer learning, and importance sampling. The proposed methods could be valuable to practitioners, particularly with the introduction of CD-CV for model selection. However, the paper would strengthen its significance claim by more clearly articulating the practical impact of their theoretical advances.

Regarding quality, the experimental design is sound and the results are convincing, but the paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with recent baselines. While they compare with KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE, they don't discuss how their method performs on more recent approaches like the ones mentioned in the related work section.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically grounded and practically useful approach to density ratio estimation that makes several important contributions to the field. The main weaknesses are the lack of clarity regarding mathematical assumptions and foundational details, which are critical for evaluating the paper's theoretical claims. With appropriate revisions to clarify these aspects, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

The paper addresses important criteria: it provides quality through detailed theoretical analysis, has clear presentation of the main ideas (though with some mathematical ambiguities), demonstrates originality through the Fredholm equation formulation and CD-CV method, and shows significance through both theoretical advances and practical applications. With the suggested clarifications, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the field.
