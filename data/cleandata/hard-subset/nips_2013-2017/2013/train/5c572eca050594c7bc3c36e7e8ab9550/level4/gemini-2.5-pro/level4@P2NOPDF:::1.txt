This paper introduces a novel method for estimating the density ratio p(x)/q(x) by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This is an elegant approach that leverages the well-established theory of integral equations to derive a new estimator. The paper is well-written, and the problem it addresses is both important and of significant interest to the machine learning community.

While the core idea is promising, the paper could be substantially improved by addressing several points related to clarity, theoretical context, and empirical evaluation.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Context and Comparison with Alternative Methods:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other methods, which makes it difficult to assess the practical and theoretical advantages of the proposed technique.
    *   **Theoretical Comparison:** Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate of the proposed estimator. However, these bounds are presented in isolation. To establish the theoretical merit of the method, it is crucial to compare these rates with the optimal minimax rates for this problem, such as those derived in [12]. This comparison would clarify whether the proposed estimator is statistically optimal or how close it comes to the fundamental limits of the problem.
    *   **Naive Plug-in Approach:** The authors should include a brief discussion on why a direct estimation method, like the one proposed, is preferable to a naive "plug-in" approach. Specifically, why is solving the integral equation superior to separately estimating p(x) and q(x) (e.g., with kernel density estimators) and then taking their quotient? Highlighting the potential pitfalls of the plug-in method (e.g., error amplification in low-density regions) would strengthen the motivation for this work.
    *   **Empirical Comparison:** The experimental section should include a more direct comparison against state-of-the-art density ratio estimation algorithms. Without this, it is difficult to judge the practical performance of the proposed method.

2.  **Illustrative Examples and Intuition:** The evaluation is based on real-world datasets, which is valuable, but the paper would greatly benefit from a simple, low-dimensional illustrative example.
    *   For instance, using one-dimensional Gaussian densities for p and q, a plot showing the true ratio p(x)/q(x) alongside the estimate from the proposed method would provide invaluable intuition. Such a "toy problem" demonstration would help readers visually assess the quality of the estimation and better understand the behavior of the algorithm.

3.  **Clarity on Theoretical Details and Assumptions:**
    *   **Missing Justifications:** Several technical statements are made without proof or citation. For example, the key identity `∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)` should be justified with either a proof (in the main text or appendix) or a reference to a standard result. Even if such claims are considered standard in some fields, providing support is essential for rigor and accessibility.
    *   **Role of the Bandwidth Parameter:** The paper notes that as a special case (q=1), the method can perform density estimation, and interestingly, consistency does not require the bandwidth `t` to converge to zero. This is a significant departure from standard kernel density estimation (KDE), where such a condition is necessary. The authors should expand on this point, explicitly contrasting the role and behavior of the bandwidth parameter in their framework versus its role in KDE. This would prevent potential confusion and highlight a unique theoretical aspect of their approach.
    *   **Explanation of Tikhonov Regularization:** Tikhonov regularization is central to the proposed solver but may be unfamiliar to a portion of the NIPS audience. A few sentences providing an intuitive explanation of why this form of regularization is necessary (i.e., to handle the ill-posed nature of the Fredholm equation) and how it works would make the paper more self-contained and accessible.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Dataset References:** Please provide citations or sources for the datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).
*   **Location of Proofs:** I note that all proofs have been deferred to the supplementary material and have not been checked as part of this review.

**Conclusion:**

This is a well-written paper that introduces a novel and interesting reformulation of the density ratio estimation problem. However, its primary weaknesses are the lack of thorough comparison to existing methods (both theoretically and empirically) and the absence of simple illustrative examples to build intuition. By addressing the points above—particularly by benchmarking against minimax rates and state-of-the-art algorithms, and by demonstrating the method on a toy problem—the authors could significantly strengthen their contribution and make a more compelling case for its adoption.
