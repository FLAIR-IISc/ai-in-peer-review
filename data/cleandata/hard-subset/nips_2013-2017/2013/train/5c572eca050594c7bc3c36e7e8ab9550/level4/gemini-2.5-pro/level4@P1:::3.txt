This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability density functions, q/p. The core idea is to reframe this estimation task as a classical inverse problem by formulating a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This inverse problem is then solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, leading to a family of algorithms the authors call FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The main contributions include this novel formulation connecting density ratio estimation to classical operator theory, a detailed theoretical analysis providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, and a practical unsupervised model selection method called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). The authors provide experiments comparing their method to alternatives like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF).

**Strengths:**

*   The primary strength of the paper is the elegant reformulation of density ratio estimation as a well-studied inverse problem. This provides a principled and flexible framework that connects the problem to the rich literature on regularization and operator theory.
*   The resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, derived directly from the optimization problems, and appear easy to implement, with explicit formulas provided for the solutions.
*   The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is thorough, providing convergence rates for distributions on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds, which adds significant weight to the proposed method.
*   The CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is a valuable practical contribution, addressing what is often a major challenge in unsupervised and semi-supervised learning tasks.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The main concerns relate to the foundational assumptions of the method, which could be stated more clearly and rigorously.

1.  The approach is fundamentally based on the assumption that both p and q have densities. From a statistical viewpoint, this can be a strong assumption. It is well-known that in any epsilon-neighborhood of a probability measure P that has a density with respect to a measure ν, there exist other probability measures that do not have ν-densities. This raises questions about the robustness of an estimation procedure that relies on the existence of a density ratio. Could the authors comment on how sensitive their method might be to small perturbations of the measures that could violate the density assumption?

2.  The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more formal and explicit statement of its underlying assumptions. For instance:
    *   What is the assumed dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure)? This is not explicitly stated but is crucial for defining a density.
    *   How do the theoretical results handle sets of ν-measure zero? This is an important detail for ensuring the rigor of the analysis.
    *   What are the assumptions on the support of the densities? For the ratio q(x)/p(x) to be well-defined, p(x) must be non-zero where q(x) is non-zero. Is p assumed to be strictly positive on its domain? A clear statement of the assumptions on p and q would improve the paper's clarity.
    *   While Section 3 specifies the domain for the theoretical analysis (R^d or a manifold), the general problem setup could be more precise about the space to which x belongs.
