Summary
The paper tackles the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability densities by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation and solving the resulting inverse problem using kernel methods. Intuitively, if P and Q are two probability measures and r = dP/dQ is the density ratio, then the integral operator associated with a kernel and Q can map r to a function determined by P. The authors propose to regularize and solve this inverse problem from samples.

Strengths
- The Fredholm formulation is elegant and connects density-ratio estimation to classical inverse problems and kernel integral operators.
- The methodology could unify and potentially extend existing density-ratio estimators by offering a principled operator-theoretic perspective and standard regularization tools.
- The approach may yield theoretical insights (e.g., existence/uniqueness and stability) and practical algorithms if the assumptions and analysis are clearly stated.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The paper is not precise about the underlying measure-theoretic setup. As currently written, it refers to “densities” without specifying the dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue, counting, or a general σ-finite measure). This ambiguity leads to technical issues:
  - If the goal is the ratio of ν-densities p/q, the existence of densities is a strong assumption and is unstable under perturbations: in any ε-neighborhood of a measure with a ν-density, there exist measures without ν-densities.
  - The object that is naturally identifiable from data is r = dP/dQ (Radon–Nikodym derivative), which exists Q-almost everywhere under P ≪ Q, independently of ν. The theory and algorithm should be formulated in terms of r = dP/dQ rather than densities with respect to an unspecified ν.
- The paper does not clearly state the domain and function spaces:
  - What is the sample space X? Is it a subset of R^d with Lebesgue structure, a Polish space, or a general measurable space?
  - What are the assumptions on the kernel k (boundedness, continuity, universality, Mercer property) and on the associated integral operator under Q?
  - In what space does r live (L2(Q), RKHS, or an interpolation space)? Without this, well-posedness, regularization, and rates are unclear.
- Identifiability and support assumptions are not articulated:
  - The key condition P ≪ Q (no P-mass where q = 0) should be explicit; otherwise r is not defined on sets where q = 0.
  - Is r assumed nonnegative Q-a.e.? Does the estimator enforce nonnegativity and normalization ∫ r dQ = 1?
- The inverse problem and regularization:
  - Ill-posedness requires careful treatment (e.g., Tikhonov regularization in the RKHS/L2(Q)), including how the regularization parameter is chosen and how sample-based operators approximate population operators.
  - Finite-sample guarantees, consistency, and rates depend on spectral properties of the Q-operator and source conditions on r. None of this is clearly stated.
- Relation to prior work is underdeveloped:
  - There is extensive literature on direct density-ratio estimation: KLIEP, uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF, kernel mean matching, and logistic-regression-based estimators. The proposed Fredholm approach seems closely related to KuLSIF and moment-matching estimators via operator equations. The paper should carefully position its contribution, clarify equivalences/differences, and compare empirically.
- Practical aspects are unclear:
  - Computational complexity (naively O(n^3)) and scalability are not discussed; are there Nyström or random-feature approximations?
  - Sensitivity to kernel choice and regularization is not evaluated.
  - How are constraints (nonnegativity, normalization) handled in practice?

Technical suggestions and requests for clarification
- Reframe the target as the Radon–Nikodym derivative r = dP/dQ on a measurable space (X, 𝒳), with the standing assumption P ≪ Q. Avoid referring to ν unless needed for specific examples; if ν is used, specify it explicitly and address issues on ν-null sets.
- Specify the domain X and kernel assumptions:
  - Assume X is a separable metric space (or X ⊂ R^d), k is bounded, symmetric, positive definite, and measurable, and define the RKHS H.
  - Define the Q-integral operator T_Q f(·) = ∫ k(·, x) f(x) dQ(x) and the P-mean g_P(·) = ∫ k(·, x) dP(x).
  - State explicitly the operator equation T_Q r = g_P that underlies the method.
- State regularization and solution:
  - Provide the objective (e.g., minimize ||T_Q f − g_P||_H^2 + λ ||f||_H^2 or the appropriate L2(Q)/H norm), the empirical counterparts using samples from P and Q, and the closed-form solution via the representer theorem.
  - Describe how λ and kernel parameters are selected (e.g., cross-validation on a surrogate risk under Q).
- Assumptions on r and rates:
  - Clarify whether r ∈ H or satisfies a source condition relative to T_Q (e.g., r = T_Q^β h with h ∈ H); provide consistency and, if possible, convergence rates depending on eigenvalue decay of T_Q and β.
  - Include high-probability bounds via concentration for empirical operators.
- Constraints and identifiability:
  - Discuss nonnegativity and normalization. If unconstrained, explain why the estimator remains meaningful; optionally propose a projection step or incorporate constraints into the optimization.
  - Explicitly assume support overlap (P ≪ Q) and discuss behavior when this fails.
- Experiments:
  - Compare against KLIEP, uLSIF/KuLSIF/RuLSIF, KMM, and logistic-regression-based ratios across synthetic and real covariate-shift tasks.
  - Report metrics relevant to downstream use (importance-weighted risk, test risk under shift), as well as direct error metrics for r where possible.
  - Analyze sensitivity to λ and kernel bandwidth; report runtime and scalability; test positivity/normalization handling.
  - Include ablations showing the impact of regularization strength on stability.

Clarity and presentation
- Clearly list all assumptions in one place: domain X, kernel properties, P ≪ Q, boundedness conditions, and whether r is assumed positive and normalized.
- Be explicit about almost-everywhere statements and how sets of Q-measure zero are handled; avoid ambiguity over ν-null sets.
- Provide intuition for the operator equation and its relation to existing estimators; a short derivation starting from r = dP/dQ and the reproducing property would help.
- Improve notation consistency and ensure all operators, norms, and inner products are defined where used.

Originality and significance
The operator-theoretic framing has merit and could yield a unified view and potentially stronger theory. However, without a precise measure-theoretic setup, clear assumptions, and a thorough comparison to established density-ratio methods, the current contribution is difficult to assess. The paper’s potential impact depends on delivering rigorous analysis (existence/uniqueness, consistency/rates) and demonstrating empirical advantages or distinctive properties (e.g., stability under ill-posedness, principled selection of λ, or improved performance).

Overall evaluation
Promising idea with an appealing connection to Fredholm integral equations, but the paper in its current form lacks essential assumptions, rigor regarding dominating measures and null sets, and a solid empirical comparison to standard baselines. I encourage the authors to (i) reframe the target as r = dP/dQ with P ≪ Q, (ii) clearly specify the functional-analytic setting and regularization scheme, (iii) provide theoretical guarantees, and (iv) substantially strengthen the experimental evaluation and related work discussion. With these improvements, the paper could make a solid and well-founded contribution to density-ratio estimation.
