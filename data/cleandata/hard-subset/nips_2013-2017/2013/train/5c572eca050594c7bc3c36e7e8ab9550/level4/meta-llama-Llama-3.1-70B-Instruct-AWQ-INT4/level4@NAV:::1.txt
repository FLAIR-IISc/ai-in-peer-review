The paper introduces a novel approach to estimating the density ratio p/q by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation, distinguishing it from existing methods.

The paper's strong points include its clear and engaging writing style, making it a pleasure to read. The topic of density ratio estimation is both important and intriguing.

However, there are several areas that require improvement:
(i) Certain details are not provided in the paper. For instance, the derivation of \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t) is not explicitly shown. Either an in-paper proof, supplementary material proof, or a relevant reference is necessary to support such statements, even if they appear straightforward.
(ii) Notably, this method can be applied to density estimation as a special case (q=1), offering an alternative to traditional kernel density estimation (KDE) methods. Unlike KDE, this approach does not necessitate bandwidth convergence to zero. Further discussion on this aspect would be beneficial, highlighting the distinct roles of bandwidth parameters in this paper compared to KDE.
(iii) A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would be helpful, as it may be unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS community.
(iv) Although all proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, their accuracy remains unchecked.
(v) A comparison with the naive plug-in approach, where densities p and q are separately estimated, would be insightful. Explaining why this method is superior would enhance the paper's clarity.
(vi) The performance of this method relative to other approaches, such as [12], which also derives minimax bounds, is unclear. While Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on convergence rates, their proximity to the optimal minimax rate is unknown.
(vii) References for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be provided.
(viii) A demonstration of the proposed method's efficacy on toy problems with fixed, known p and q would be valuable. For example, a plot of the true p/q and estimated p/q for 1-dimensional p and q could facilitate assessment of the estimation quality.

In summary, the paper presents an innovative approach to density ratio estimation. Nevertheless, its performance compared to other algorithms, such as [12], and its application to toy problems, remain unclear, warranting further investigation.
