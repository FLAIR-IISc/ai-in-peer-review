

This paper proposes two novel methods for estimating the density ratio of two probability distributions. The first method leverages the importance sampling equation, formulating it as a regularization problem with an L² norm penalty for deviation from equality and an RKHS regularization term (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method employs a kernel whose locality parameter must vanish for consistency, similarly expressed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees and validate practical performance through experiments.  

A key advantage over existing approaches like KMM is the use of an L² formulation, which enables cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. Unlike supervised learning, where CV is standard for hyperparameter tuning, unsupervised learning lacks general parameter selection methods. This paper addresses that gap for density ratio estimation via a clever application of the L² norm. The paper should explicitly note that RKHS-based objective functions (e.g., in KMM) are unsuitable for CV, as their values depend on the kernel and thus are incomparable across different kernels.  

In Theorem 1, the convergence rate includes a factor of $-t / \log \lambda$, resulting in a slow rate for fixed $t$. In kernel method literature (e.g., Caponnetto and De Vito, 2007), polynomial convergence rates in sample size are achievable for fixed $t$ by fixing the decay rate of $\lambda$. The authors should discuss whether reasonable additional assumptions (e.g., on the spectrum of $K_{p,t}$ or stronger conditions on $q/p$) could yield a polynomial rate. Addressing this would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.  

The paper assumes $q/p$ belongs to a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev), which is a strong requirement demanding prior knowledge of the ratio or tail behavior of $p$ and $q$. Crucially, under this assumption, the reciprocal ratio $p/q$ typically behaves poorly. The authors should discuss this limitation of density ratio estimation in the main text.  

The supplementary material requires substantial revision due to numerous typos and minor errors that impede verification of theoretical results. For instance:  
- Lemma 5: $W_2^2$ should be $W_s^2$, and the bound should be $D_1 (t / -\log \lambda)^2 + \lambda^{1-\alpha} D_2 \|f\|_2^2$.  
- Expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, though Equation (31) is correct.  
- In Lemma 5’s proof, the integral domain at line 1147 must be $\|\xi\|^2 \geq \ldots$.  
As the convergence results constitute a core contribution, the authors must correct these issues before publication.  

References:  
Caponnetto, A., & De Vito, E. (2007). Optimal Rates for Regularized Least-Squares Algorithm. *Foundations of Computational Mathematics*, 7, 331–368.  

The paper presents a valuable approach to density ratio estimation, with the key strength being the effective application of cross-validation for parameter selection.
