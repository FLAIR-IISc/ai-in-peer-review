

This paper addresses the important problem of density ratio estimation (q/p) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors develop a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) that can handle both Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when q values are known at sample points from p) settings. The work builds upon and extends previous approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), while providing a theoretically grounded framework with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a novel connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems in operator theory, which provides a principled framework for algorithm design and theoretical analysis. Second, it develops a flexible family of algorithms (FIRE) with simple implementations that can be applied to various settings. Third, the authors provide detailed theoretical analysis including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel in both Euclidean and manifold settings. Finally, they introduce a completely unsupervised model selection method called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) that addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making a complex technical subject accessible. The theoretical results are substantial and address an important gap in understanding density ratio estimation. The experimental results on multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to existing methods like KMM and LSIF.

Strengths:
- The connection to Fredholm equations provides a fresh perspective on density ratio estimation, bridging classical operator theory with modern machine learning.
- The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings.
- The CD-CV model selection technique is a valuable contribution that addresses a significant practical challenge.
- The paper is well-structured with clear exposition of the mathematical framework.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper makes several claims about approximation properties (e.g., ∫k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)) without providing proofs or references. The authors should either include these in the main text or clearly reference them in the supplementary material.

2. The relationship between the bandwidth parameter t in this approach and the bandwidth in traditional KDE needs more explicit discussion. As noted in the draft review, this method can be used for density estimation (q=1) without requiring the bandwidth to converge to zero, which is a significant difference from standard KDE. This point deserves elaboration to help readers understand the distinct roles of bandwidth parameters in different approaches.

3. The paper would benefit from a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization for readers unfamiliar with this classical technique, as it's central to the proposed framework. A sentence or two explaining its role in solving ill-posed inverse problems would be helpful.

4. The comparison to related work could be strengthened by discussing why a naive plug-in approach (estimating p and q separately via KDE and taking the ratio) is inferior to the proposed method. This would clarify the theoretical advantage of the FIRE framework.

5. While the paper provides upper bounds (Corollary 2 and 4), it would be valuable to discuss how these bounds compare to the minimax optimal rates established in works like [12]. The paper mentions [12] but doesn't explicitly address the gap between their upper bounds and the known minimax rates.

6. The datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be properly referenced to allow reproducibility and context for readers.

7. A simple 1D demonstration showing the true ratio q/p and the estimated ratio would significantly help readers assess the quality of the estimation. This would be particularly valuable for illustrating the behavior of the method on a known problem.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical framework is sound, the algorithms are well-motivated and practical, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. The main weaknesses are primarily in the exposition and comparison to related work, which could be addressed with minor revisions. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), introduces a novel perspective on an important problem (Originality), and provides results with clear significance for practitioners and researchers (Significance). I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the minor points raised above.
