- Paper reframes density-ratio estimation w(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation (linear inverse problem) and solves it via RKHS regularization.
- Two formulations: Type I uses Ep[k(x,·)w(·)] = Eq[k(x,·)] (exact operator equation); Type II uses local kernel kt to approximate q(x) from Ep[kt(x,·)w(·)].
- Both formulations yield closed-form representer-theorem solutions (FIRE) and allow out-of-sample evaluation.
- The authors provide nonasymptotic concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Rd and on smooth manifolds.
- A practical unsupervised model-selection method (CD-CV) is proposed, matching expectations of random test functions under p and q using learned weights.
- Empirical comparisons include LSIF/KLIEP, KMM discussion, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, with applications to covariate shift (regression/classification).
- Connections drawn to prior density-ratio work (KMM, LSIF/KLIEP) and to inverse problems/spectral RKHS regularization literature.
- Strength: Clear operator-theoretic reformulation that unifies density-ratio estimation with classical inverse problems, enabling principled regularization and analysis.
- Strength: Simple, explicit algorithms with flexibility in kernels and norms and efficient representer-theorem solutions.
- Strength: Transparent separation of approximation and estimation errors and manifold-inclusive theoretical results.
- Strength: CD-CV is a practical, fully unsupervised model-selection mechanism leveraging samples from both p and q.
- Strength: Encouraging empirical results showing improvements and good calibration under covariate shift.
- Weakness/question: Need more intuition and identifiability conditions for Type I (when Kp is injective on chosen RKHS; relation to characteristic kernels/KMM).
- Weakness/question: Clarify motivation for choosing losses (L2,p vs L2,q vs mixed) and implications for downstream tasks (classification vs regression).
- Weakness/question: Explain when CD-CV and the approach are preferable to directly using Eq when q-samples are available; expand Type II use cases (e.g., AIS, MCMC).
- Weakness/question: Discuss how smoothness/boundedness assumptions on p and q translate to smoothness of w, and whether theory requires p bounded away from zero.
- Weakness/question: Address dimensional dependence of rates and practical regimes where FIRE is effective (manifold structure, Gaussian smoothing).
- Weakness/question: Justify choice of test function families for CD-CV, and analyze sensitivity/stability to number and distribution of test functions.
- Weakness/question: Consider constraints on w (nonnegativity/clipping) for importance weighting in practice.
- Weakness/question: Report computational complexity, scaling of n×n matrix inversions, and use of approximations (Nyström/iterative solvers) compared to LSIF/KMM.
- Weakness/question: Discuss empirical impact of differing operator vs RKHS kernel choices and practical recommendations.
- Pros: Elegant inverse-problem framing, practical unsupervised model selection, manifold-theoretic bounds, and strong empirical performance.
- Cons: Limited intuition/identifiability for Type I, sparse discussion of loss choices, dimensional and computational scaling concerns, and lack of explicit positivity guidance.
- Minor suggestions: State identifiability conditions (e.g., characteristic kernels), advise when to favor L2,p/L2,q, clarify p bounded-away-from-zero assumption, report complexity/approximations, and relate to “Smooth Operators” literature.
- Overall assessment: Strong, timely paper with principled theory and practical contributions; recommend acceptance pending clarifications on identifiability, loss choice, and computational aspects.
