This paper presents a novel method for estimating the density ratio p/q. While several methods exist for this problem, the primary contribution of this work is the reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

**Strong Points:**
The paper is well-written and addresses the important and interesting problem of density ratio estimation.

**Weak Points, Comments, and Questions:**

(i) Several technical statements lack formal justification. For instance, the claim that ∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) should be supported by either a proof (in the main text or supplementary material) or a citation, even if the derivation is straightforward. This applies to other similar statements throughout the paper.

(ii) In the special case where q=1, the proposed method can be used for density estimation. An interesting aspect of this approach is that it does not require the bandwidth to converge to zero for consistency, unlike standard kernel density estimation (KDE). The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of this point, clarifying how the role of the bandwidth parameter in this framework differs from its role in traditional KDE.

(iii) To improve the paper's accessibility for the broader NIPS audience, I recommend including a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization, as some readers may not be familiar with it.

(iv) All proofs are provided in the supplementary material. I have not verified their correctness.

(v) The paper should discuss why the proposed direct estimation method is preferable to a naive plug-in approach, where one would first estimate the densities p and q separately and then compute their ratio. A brief explanation of the drawbacks of the plug-in method would provide valuable context.

(vi) The paper's comparison to existing methods is insufficient, both theoretically and empirically. The theoretical analysis, particularly Corollaries 2 and 4, provides upper bounds on the convergence rate. However, it remains unclear how these rates compare to the optimal minimax rates for this problem. A comparison with the minimax bounds derived in [12], for example, would be highly informative.

(vii) Please provide citations for the datasets used in the experiments (e.g., CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) The empirical evaluation would benefit from a demonstration on a simple toy problem with known p and q. For example, a plot showing the true density ratio versus the estimated ratio for 1-dimensional distributions would provide a clear and intuitive assessment of the estimation quality.

**Summary:**
Overall, this is a solid paper on an important topic. The primary weaknesses are the lack of a clear comparison to the performance of other state-of-the-art algorithms (such as [12]) and the absence of a simple illustrative example on a toy problem to help visualize the method's effectiveness.
