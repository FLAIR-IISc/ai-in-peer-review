

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, leading to a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The authors derive two primary formulations: Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when function values of q are known at sample points from p). By applying Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norms, they develop theoretically grounded algorithms that naturally incorporate cross-validation for parameter selection—a significant advantage in unsupervised learning settings where standard validation techniques are typically unavailable.

The paper's most compelling contribution is the introduction of CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a clever unsupervised method for model selection that leverages the L² structure of the problem. Unlike supervised learning where cross-validation is standard practice, unsupervised parameter selection remains a challenging open problem. The authors demonstrate that their L²-based approach enables effective parameter tuning without requiring labeled data, which is particularly valuable for density ratio estimation applications. This method addresses a critical limitation in existing approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), where the RKHS-based objective function is not suitable for cross-validation due to kernel-dependent scaling issues. The paper effectively contrasts their method with KMM and LSIF, highlighting how their formulation provides both theoretical guarantees and practical advantages.

The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on manifolds. The authors establish meaningful error bounds for both Type I and Type II settings, demonstrating how the estimation error depends on sample size, kernel width, and regularization parameters. While the convergence rates in Theorem 1 (Type I setting) involve a (-t/log λ) factor that leads to relatively slow convergence for fixed t, the authors provide a clear justification for their approach. However, it would be valuable to explore whether stronger assumptions on the spectral properties of the integral operator (as in Caponnetto and De Vito, 2007) could yield polynomial rates with fixed t, which would make the theoretical analysis even more compelling.

A notable limitation concerns the assumption that q/p belongs to a Sobolev space W²₂(Ω). While mathematically convenient for the theoretical analysis, this assumption is quite strong in practice—it requires significant prior knowledge about the density ratio or the tail behavior of p and q. The authors should acknowledge that when this assumption holds, the reciprocal ratio p/q often exhibits poor behavior, which may limit the applicability of their method in certain practical scenarios. A brief discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper's contribution by providing clearer boundaries for when their approach is most effective.

The experimental results are well-designed and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods. The authors compare FIRE with KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE across regression and classification tasks, showing consistent improvements in performance and computational efficiency. The parameter selection via CD-CV proves particularly effective, as evidenced by the experiments using different test function families (linear, half-space, kernel-based). The thorough evaluation across multiple datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) and resampling strategies (PCA-based and label-based) provides strong empirical support for their claims.

Regarding technical execution, the theoretical results are a major strength of the paper, but the supplementary material requires significant improvement. Several typos and minor errors in the proofs (such as the incorrect expression in Lemma 5, where W²₂ should be Wˢ₂, and the domain specification at line 1147) undermine confidence in the theoretical results. The authors should carefully revise the supplementary material to ensure all technical details are correctly presented, as the theoretical contributions form a core part of the paper's novelty and significance.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution by framing density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem with well-established theoretical machinery, while simultaneously providing practical algorithms with effective unsupervised parameter selection. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are convincing, and the CD-CV method addresses an important gap in unsupervised learning. The main limitations involve the strong Sobolev space assumption and the supplementary material's technical errors, which could be addressed in a revision. The paper's theoretical grounding, combined with its practical utility, makes it a strong candidate for NIPS.
