### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio `q(x)/p(x)`, a quantity crucial for applications like importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation in transfer learning. The authors propose a novel and principled approach by reformulating this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind.

Two main settings are derived from this formulation:
1.  **Type I:** Based on the equality `E_p[k(x,·) * (q/p)] = E_q[k(x,·)]`, this setting is applicable when samples from both distributions `p` and `q` are available.
2.  **Type II:** Based on the approximation `E_p[k_t(x,·) * (q/p)] ≈ q(x)` using a local delta-like kernel `k_t`, this setting is useful when `q` is a known function and only samples from `p` are available.

To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the authors employ Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to a family of simple and flexible algorithms named Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). The paper provides closed-form solutions for the resulting optimization problems.

The key contributions are:
*   A novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem, connecting it to classical operator theory.
*   A family of kernel-based algorithms (FIRE) derived from this framework using RKHS regularization.
*   A detailed theoretical analysis providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for the estimators, considering data on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
*   A practical, unsupervised method for model selection called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).
*   Encouraging experimental results comparing FIRE to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF).

### Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is novel and conceptually elegant. It connects a modern machine learning problem to a well-established area of classical analysis, providing a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed methods.

2.  **Flexible and Practical Algorithms:** The use of RKHS-based regularization is a natural fit for the problem and leads to a family of algorithms (FIRE) that are simple to implement. The derivation via the representer theorem results in clean, closed-form solutions involving standard kernel matrix operations. The framework is flexible, accommodating different loss functions (L2,p, a mix of L2,p and L2,q, and the RKHS norm).

3.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis with convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings. The analysis covers distributions in Rd as well as on manifolds, which is a significant strength and adds to the paper's completeness and technical depth.

4.  **Novel Solution for Model Selection:** Unsupervised model selection is a notoriously difficult problem. The proposed Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method is an important practical contribution. It provides a data-driven way to select hyperparameters (like kernel width and regularization) without access to labels, which is highly relevant for the problem setting.

5.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing FIRE against state-of-the-art methods on several datasets and tasks (regression and classification under covariate shift). The results demonstrate the competitive performance and utility of the proposed approach.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are several points that could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve its impact.

1.  **Intuition for Convergence in Type I Setting:** The convergence guarantee for the Type II setting is intuitive, as the integral operator with a delta-like kernel approximates the identity operator. However, the intuition for the Type I setting is less direct. A brief discussion on why matching the expectations over the family of kernel functions, `E_p[k(x,·)f] = E_q[k(x,·)]`, is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the true ratio `f = q/p` would be beneficial for the reader.

2.  **Motivation for Loss Function Choices:** The paper presents several possible loss functions, including `L2,p` and a convex combination of `L2,p` and `L2,q`. The motivation for choosing one over another could be more explicitly linked to specific applications. For instance, is one norm preferable for importance sampling tasks and another for covariate shift adaptation? A short discussion on this would guide practitioners in applying the method.

3.  **Relationship Between Smoothness Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities `p` and `q`, while the regularization term imposes a smoothness penalty on their ratio `q/p`. It would be insightful to include a discussion on the relationship between these assumptions. For example, under what conditions does the smoothness of `p` and `q` (and `p` being bounded away from zero) imply the smoothness of the ratio?

4.  **Practical Implications of Dimensionality:** The derived convergence rates exhibit a strong dependence on the data's dimensionality `d`, which is common for non-parametric estimation. It would be valuable for the authors to briefly discuss the practical implications of this. For what range of dimensions can the method be expected to perform well? This would help set realistic expectations for practitioners.

5.  **Justification for CD-CV Test Functions:** The CD-CV method is a nice contribution. However, the paper could provide more justification for the choice of the test function families `U` (random linear functions and half-space indicators). A brief explanation of why these are suitable choices and a discussion of the method's sensitivity to this choice would strengthen this part of the paper.

### Overall Assessment

This is a timely and high-quality paper that makes significant contributions to the important problem of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation based on Fredholm integral equations is a strong conceptual advance, and the resulting FIRE algorithms are both practical and theoretically well-founded. The inclusion of a rigorous convergence analysis and a novel method for unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) further elevates the work.

The identified weaknesses are minor and primarily concern areas where additional clarification and discussion could enhance the paper's clarity and practical guidance. They do not detract from the core strengths of the work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of clear relevance to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
