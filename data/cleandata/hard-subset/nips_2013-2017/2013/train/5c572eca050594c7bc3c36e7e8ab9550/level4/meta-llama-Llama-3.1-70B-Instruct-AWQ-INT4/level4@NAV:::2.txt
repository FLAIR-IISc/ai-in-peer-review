This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio of two probabilities. The first approach utilizes importance sampling and formulates it as a regularization problem, incorporating the L^2 norm of the deviation from equality and a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) regularization term, resulting in a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that must converge to zero for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem in the form of a Type II Fredholm equation. The paper presents theoretical convergence results and demonstrates the practical performance of the proposed methods through experimental evaluation.

While sharing similarities with existing methods such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), the proposed approach offers a significant advantage by enabling the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection, thanks to the L^2 formulation. Unlike supervised learning problems, where CV is a dominant method for parameter selection, the proposed method addresses the lack of general methods for unsupervised learning in density ratio estimation through the innovative use of the L^2 norm.

It is worth noting that the objective function defined by RKHS, as seen in KMM, is not suitable for kernel selection via CV, as the objective function values depend on the kernel and are therefore not comparable.

The convergence rate derived in Theorem 1, influenced by the (-t/log λ) factor, is slow for fixed t. In contrast, many kernel method literatures have shown that, with fixed t, a polynomial rate can be achieved with respect to the sample size after fixing the rate of λ. The authors may consider exploring additional assumptions that could lead to a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) made assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to derive the optimal rate for kernel ridge regression. A stronger assumption on q/p may also yield a polynomial rate. Discussing such polynomial rates under stronger assumptions with fixed t would be a valuable addition.

In density ratio estimation, it is common to assume that q/p belongs to a nice function class, such as Sobolev. However, this assumption is quite strong, requiring prior knowledge on the ratio or tails of p and q. Under this assumption, the opposite ratio p/q often behaves poorly. It would be beneficial to include a discussion on this limitation of density ratio estimation.

Although not a requirement, the supplementary materials would benefit from improvement. Given the significance of the convergence results in this paper, revising the supplementary material is advisable.
