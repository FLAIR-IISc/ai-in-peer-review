This paper addresses the important problem of estimating the density ratio `q/p`, a quantity central to applications in importance sampling, transfer learning, and particularly, covariate shift adaptation. The authors' primary contribution is to reframe this estimation task as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This novel perspective is derived from two fundamental integral equalities which relate the unknown ratio `q/p` to integral operators defined by a kernel function.

The two resulting formulations are:
1.  **Type I:** Based on the equality `Ep[k(x, ·) * (q/p)] = Eq[k(x, ·)]`, suitable when samples from both distributions `p` and `q` are available.
2.  **Type II:** Based on the approximation `Ep[k(x, ·) * (q/p)] ≈ q(x)` for a localizing kernel `k`, applicable when function values of `q` are known on a sample from `p`.

To solve these ill-posed inverse problems, the authors employ Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), a standard and powerful tool in machine learning. This principled approach leads to a family of simple and flexible algorithms, which the authors name FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper further provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing convergence rates for the proposed estimators using Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. On the practical side, the paper introduces a fully unsupervised method for model selection, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), and presents experimental results that demonstrate the method's strong performance against established baselines.

### Strengths

*   **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The connection drawn between density ratio estimation and Fredholm integral equations is elegant and insightful. It provides a strong theoretical foundation for the problem by linking it to the classical theory of integral operators and regularization, moving beyond heuristic approaches.
*   **Technical Soundness and Practicality:** The work is very well-executed. By leveraging the RKHS framework, the proposed regularization schemes result in simple, closed-form solutions that are straightforward to implement using standard kernel method libraries.
*   **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis:** A major strength of this paper is its detailed theoretical analysis. The authors provide explicit error bounds and convergence rates for both the Type I and Type II settings, considering different data domains (Euclidean and manifolds). This adds significant rigor and value to the proposed methods.
*   **Valuable Method for Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter tuning is a significant practical contribution. Model selection is a notoriously difficult challenge in unsupervised learning, and the proposed technique provides a well-motivated and effective solution for this specific problem setting.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper includes a convincing set of experiments that validate the FIRE algorithms and the CD-CV procedure. The comparisons to relevant methods like LSIF and a kernel density-based baseline effectively showcase the merits of the proposed approach.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

The paper is strong, but its clarity and impact could be further enhanced by addressing the following points:

1.  **Motivation and Intuition:**
    *   For the Type I setting, an intuitive argument for why optimizing the integral equality over the family of test functions `{k(x, ·)}` is sufficient to recover the true ratio would be helpful. This would parallel the clear intuition provided for the Type II setting, where `k` is a delta-like kernel.
    *   The paper proposes using different loss functions (e.g., the `L2,p` norm in Eq. 5 and a linear combination of `L2,p` and `L2,q` norms in Eq. 9). A more detailed discussion motivating the choice of a specific loss function for different applications would be beneficial. For instance, when is one norm preferable over the other?

2.  **Problem Framing and Applications:**
    *   The application to importance sampling is a key motivation, yet the FIRE algorithm requires samples from both `p` and `q` to construct its empirical objective function. The paper could be strengthened by elaborating on the specific scenarios where this setup is advantageous. The introduction mentions cases where sampling from `q` is computationally expensive; a more detailed discussion of such applications would clarify the method's primary use cases and differentiate it from direct empirical estimation.

3.  **Assumptions and Practical Limits:**
    *   The regularizer imposes a smoothness constraint on the estimated ratio `q/p`, while the theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities `p` and `q`. The relationship between these assumptions could be explored further. For example, do smooth densities `p` and `q` (perhaps bounded away from zero) guarantee a smooth ratio?
    *   The derived convergence rates show a clear dependence on the data dimensionality `d`. A discussion on the practical limitations of the method with respect to `d` would be highly valuable for practitioners seeking to apply this method.

4.  **Clarification of Experimental Choices:**
    *   The CD-CV method utilizes specific families of test functions `U` (random linear and half-space indicator functions). A brief justification for these choices over other potential function families would be helpful.

5.  **Context with Related Ideas:**
    *   The general approach of using integral operators to extract properties of underlying distributions shares some high-level similarity with work such as [1]. While the formulation presented here for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is novel and direct, briefly acknowledging this broader context could help further situate the paper's contribution within the field.

[1] Grünewälder, S., Gretton, A., & Shawe-Taylor, J. (2012). Smooth operators. *ICML*.

### Conclusion

This is a timely and strong paper that makes excellent contributions to an important and relevant problem. The novel formulation is theoretically sound and leads to practical algorithms, supported by both a convergence study and solid experimental results. Addressing the minor points suggested above would further improve the clarity and impact of this very good work.
