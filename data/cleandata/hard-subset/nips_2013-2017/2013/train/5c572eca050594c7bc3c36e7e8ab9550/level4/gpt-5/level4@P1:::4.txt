Summary
The paper tackles estimation of the density ratio f(x) = q(x)/p(x) by casting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two complementary formulations underlie the approach:
- Type I: Ep[k(x, ·) f(·)] = Eq[k(x, ·)], which holds exactly for any kernel k.
- Type II: Ep[k_t(x, ·) f(·)] ≈ q(·) for localization kernels k_t (e.g., Gaussian with bandwidth t), yielding an approximation that becomes exact as t → 0 under smoothness assumptions.

Replacing population operators and norms with their empirical versions leads to simple algorithms solvable by the representer theorem; the authors call these FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimators). They provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels when p and q are supported on Rd or on smooth compact submanifolds, and propose an unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, based on cross-density validation with generic test functions. Experiments compare favorably to LSIF and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and they discuss connections to KMM and related kernel operator methods.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to KMM, which exploits Eq[Φ(x)] = Ep[(q/p) Φ(x)] in an RKHS, but differs by directly solving a regularized inverse problem with a natural out-of-sample extension and stability via Tikhonov regularization. It also relates to LSIF/KLIEP, which minimize L2,p or KL divergences in parametric families, while FIRE leverages RKHS operators and yields explicit solutions. The paper connects to RKHS-based inverse problems and kernel density/operator literature, and is thematically similar to “Smooth Operators” in its use of operator identities to access latent quantities.

Quality
- The methodology is technically sound and grounded in classical inverse problem theory and RKHS regularization.
- The theoretical analysis provides finite-sample bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings. The dependence on dimension aligns with expectations for density-related problems.
- The empirical section illustrates the approach and introduces CD-CV for unsupervised model selection; comparisons are appropriate, though broader benchmarks would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The operator identities and the two FIRE variants are explained well, and the empirical algorithms are derived explicitly.
- A short, intuitive explanation for why optimizing over the test set {k(x, ·)} suffices in Type I (beyond the Type II δ-kernel intuition) would help. In effect, the RKHS feature map spans a rich test function class; clarifying the conditions under which this is “large enough” to identify q/p would be useful.

Originality
- Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem and solving it via RKHS regularization is a neat synthesis. The CD-CV model selection mechanism is also appealing and practical.
- The connection to KMM as a particular operator identity in H is insightful; the regularized formulation and out-of-sample property differentiate FIRE from KMM.

Significance
- Density ratio estimation is central to covariate shift and importance sampling, so improved theory and algorithms are valuable.
- The results give concrete rates and a practical algorithmic framework; CD-CV addresses a key challenge in unsupervised model selection.

Detailed comments and questions
- Convergence via {k(x, ·)} in Type I: For Type II, the δ-kernel limit provides intuition. For Type I, a brief argument linking identifiability to the richness of the RKHS and properties of Kp as an operator (e.g., injectivity over the relevant function class) would make the guarantees more transparent.
- Loss choice and application linkage: The paper optimizes over L2,p initially and later considers γ L2,p + (1 − γ) L2,q. Please motivate these choices in terms of downstream tasks: when is minimizing error under p preferable to q, or to a mixture? Concrete application scenarios (e.g., training-vs-test risk weighting) would guide practitioners.
- Importance sampling motivation: Since the empirical objectives already require estimating expectations under both p and q, why not directly use Eq where available? Under what scenarios is Ep-weighted estimation of q/p preferable to directly estimating Eq? The text notes settings where sampling from q is hard; clarifying whether this is the main intended use case (e.g., MCMC, unnormalized models) would be helpful.
- Regularization vs. smoothness assumptions: The theory assumes smooth q and p (and boundedness), while the algorithm regularizes the ratio q/p. Can you clarify relationships between smoothness of q, p, and q/p (e.g., under p bounded away from zero)? Even if pathologies exist, stating typical sufficient conditions under which smooth q and p imply smooth q/p would align assumptions with the regularizer.
- Dimension dependence: Please discuss the d dependence in your rates and the practical dimensional regimes where FIRE is effective. For high d, do you recommend manifold-supported settings, or specific kernel choices/priors?
- CD-CV test function families U: What motivates the particular choices (random linear and half-space indicators)? Are there guidelines for selecting U to match downstream tasks (e.g., linear predictors vs. kernel machines), and how sensitive is CD-CV to this choice?
- Broader perspective: The general strategy is to enforce agreement of expectations under a rich test function class. Do you view {k(x, ·)} as the “right” or preferred class for this problem, or one principled option among many? A brief comparison to alternative test families (including those used in Smooth Operators) would contextualize the design choice.

Pros
- Elegant formulation as a Fredholm inverse problem and clean RKHS/Tikhonov solution.
- Theoretical guarantees with explicit rates in Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Simple, stable algorithms with out-of-sample predictions.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection via CD-CV.
- Relevance to covariate shift and importance sampling.

Cons
- Limited intuition in Type I for why the chosen test class suffices for identifiability.
- Motivation for the specific loss choices (L2,p vs. mixture) could be stronger and more application-driven.
- The dependence on dimension warrants more practical guidance.
- The choice of CD-CV test functions could be better justified.

Overall evaluation
This is timely, solid work with a principled formulation, nontrivial theory, and useful algorithms. With strengthened motivation for loss functions, clearer intuition for Type I identifiability, and guidance on dimensionality and test function selection, it would be a strong contribution. I lean positive.
