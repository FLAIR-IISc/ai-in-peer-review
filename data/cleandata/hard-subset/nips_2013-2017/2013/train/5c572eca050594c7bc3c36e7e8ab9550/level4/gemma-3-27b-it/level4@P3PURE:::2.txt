This paper introduces two novel methods for density ratio estimation. The first approach leverages the importance sampling equation and reformulates it as a regularization problem incorporating both an L<sup>2</sup> norm penalizing deviation from equality and a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) regularization term, effectively framing it as a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method utilizes a kernel with a locality parameter designed to approach zero for consistency and is similarly expressed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). The authors support their methods with theoretical convergence results and empirical demonstrations of practical performance.

A key advantage of the proposed methods, particularly the L<sup>2</sup> formulation, over existing techniques such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) is the ability to employ cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. While CV is a standard practice in supervised learning, general methods for parameter tuning in unsupervised learning are less common. This work addresses this gap for density ratio estimation through the insightful application of the L<sup>2</sup> norm. It is worth highlighting that objective functions based on RKHS norms, as found in KMM, are less suitable for CV-based kernel selection due to the dependence of objective function values on the chosen kernel, rendering direct comparisons problematic.

Regarding the convergence rate presented in Theorem 1, the presence of the (-t/log λ) factor indicates a slow rate for fixed *t*. In much of the kernel methods literature, a polynomial rate with respect to the sample size can be established for fixed *t* after determining the rate of λ. The authors should consider whether stronger assumptions could yield such a polynomial rate for their estimator. For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression by imposing assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K<sub>p,t</sub>. I hypothesize that strengthening assumptions on q/p could similarly lead to a polynomial rate. Discussing such a rate under stronger assumptions would enhance the paper’s contribution.

A common requirement in density ratio estimation is the assumption that q/p resides in a well-behaved function class, such as a Sobolev space. However, this assumption is strong, necessitating prior knowledge of the ratio or the tails of *p* and *q*. Furthermore, under such an assumption on q/p, the inverse ratio p/q often exhibits undesirable behavior. The authors should include a discussion of this inherent limitation of density ratio estimation. 

While not essential, the supplementary materials require significant improvement. Numerous typos and minor errors hinder verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. For instance, in Lemma 5, W<sub>2</sub><sup>2</sup> should be W<sub>s</sub><sup>2</sup>, and the bound should be D<sub>1</sub>(t/-log λ)<sup>2</sup> + λ<sup>1-α</sup> D<sub>2</sub> ||f||<sub>2</sub><sup>2</sup>. The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq.(31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 should be restricted to ||ξ||<sup>2</sup> ≥ 0. Given the centrality of the convergence results to this paper’s contributions, a thorough revision of the supplementary material is strongly advised. 

References: 
Caponnetto A., De Vito E. Optimal Rates for Regularized Least-Squares Algorithm. Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 7 331-368 (2007). 

Overall, the paper proposes a compelling new approach to density ratio estimation, with the effective application of CV for parameter selection being a notable strength.
