This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability density functions. The proposed method formulates this task as an inverse problem by leveraging a Fredholm integral equation with a specified kernel. While the approach is novel, the paper suffers from a lack of theoretical rigor and clarity on its fundamental assumptions.

A central concern is the strong assumption that the underlying probability measures possess densities. From a machine learning perspective, this is a restrictive premise. It is a well-established result in measure theory that for any probability measure P that has a density with respect to a dominating measure ν, there exist other probability measures arbitrarily close to P (i.e., within any epsilon-neighborhood) that are not absolutely continuous with respect to ν and therefore lack a density. This makes the very concept of estimating a density ratio sensitive to small perturbations and potentially ill-posed in practical scenarios where the existence of densities cannot be guaranteed. The authors do not acknowledge or address this foundational issue.

Furthermore, the paper's theoretical framework lacks clarity on several key assumptions, which makes it difficult to assess the rigor and scope of the presented results. I would ask the authors to clarify the following points:

1.  **Dominating Measure:** The dominating measure ν, with respect to which the densities are defined, is never explicitly stated. It is unclear whether the authors assume the standard Lebesgue measure on R^d, a counting measure for discrete domains, or some other measure. This ambiguity is critical, as the choice of dominating measure fundamentally affects the problem formulation and the interpretation of the results.

2.  **Input Space:** The domain of the input variable x is not formally defined. Is it assumed to be R^d, a specific subset thereof, or a more general topological space? The properties of the input space are crucial for the validity of the kernel methods and integral equations employed.

3.  **Support of the Densities and Sets of Measure Zero:** The paper does not state its assumptions regarding the support of the densities p(x) and q(x). For the ratio r(x) = p(x)/q(x) to be well-defined, it is typically necessary to assume that the support of p is contained within the support of q. The manuscript does not discuss the behavior of the proposed estimator when q(x) is zero or approaches zero. The treatment of sets of ν-measure zero is not addressed, which is a potential source of theoretical complications.

In summary, while the idea of using a Fredholm integral equation is interesting, the paper in its current form is not sufficiently rigorous. The authors must clearly state their assumptions and address the fundamental theoretical challenges associated with density ratio estimation to make a convincing case for their method.
