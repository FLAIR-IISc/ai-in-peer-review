This paper addresses the important problem of density ratio estimation, specifically estimating the ratio q(x)/p(x), relevant to importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The core novelty lies in reformulating this estimation as a Fredholm integral equation, leveraging tools from operator theory and regularization techniques. 

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation and methodology. The connection to classical integral equation theory provides a principled framework for developing and analyzing algorithms – the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family. The theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, is a significant contribution. The introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is also a valuable addition.

However, several points could be strengthened. While the paper clearly states the problem and proposes a solution, some details regarding supporting claims are missing. For instance, the statement regarding the approximation  \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) requires either a proof (perhaps in the supplementary material) or a clear citation.  Similarly, a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would benefit readers less familiar with the technique.  

The paper highlights a potential advantage over standard kernel density estimation (KDE) by avoiding the need for bandwidths to converge to zero. Expanding on this point, clarifying the distinct role of the bandwidth parameter 't' in FIRE compared to KDE bandwidth selection, would enhance understanding.

The theoretical results (Corollaries 2 and 4) provide convergence rate bounds, but their relationship to known minimax optimality results (e.g., [24]) is unclear. Discussing how these bounds compare to theoretical lower bounds would strengthen the theoretical contribution. While the experimental results are encouraging, including demonstrations on simple, well-defined 1D examples with known p and q would visually illustrate the method’s performance and aid reader comprehension.  A discussion comparing performance to alternatives beyond those mentioned in the paper, such as [12], would be helpful.

Furthermore, the paper lacks references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS). A short justification explaining why a naive plug-in density estimation approach is inferior to the proposed method would also be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a novel and potentially impactful approach.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence rates and concentration bounds.
*   **Practicality:** The FIRE algorithms are presented as simple and easily implementable.
*   **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The CD-CV method addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Missing Details:** Several statements lack sufficient justification or citations.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to existing methods could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding theoretical optimality.
*   **Lack of Simple Examples:** The absence of demonstrations on toy problems hinders intuitive understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel approach and strong theoretical backing. Addressing the minor weaknesses identified above—providing supporting details, clarifying comparisons, and including illustrative examples—would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
