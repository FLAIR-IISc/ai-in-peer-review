The paper proposes a method for estimating the ratio of two probability densities using a kernel-based formulation of a Fredholm integral equation, which turns the task into an inverse problem.

From a machine learning perspective, the assumption that both distributions admit densities with respect to a common dominating measure is very strong. It is well known that, in any epsilon-neighborhood of a probability measure P that has a ν-density, there exist probability measures that do not have ν-densities. This makes density-ratio estimation potentially fragile and raises concerns about robustness and applicability.

The manuscript does not specify the dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue, counting, or another measure), which leaves key definitions ambiguous. Without this, it is unclear how the authors handle sets of ν-measure zero, whether the ratio is defined only ν-almost everywhere, and what implications this has for identifiability, uniqueness, and the rigor of the results.

I also miss a clear and complete statement of assumptions. For example:
- What is the domain of x? Is x in R^d or in a more general measurable/topological space?
- Are the densities strictly positive everywhere, or only on their support? If positivity is assumed, is this uniform?
- What regularity conditions are imposed on the densities and kernel (e.g., continuity, boundedness, integrability)?
- Do the two distributions share the same support, and how are boundary or support-mismatch cases handled?

Since the approach leads to an inverse problem, please specify the conditions under which the Fredholm equation is solvable and the solution is unique, and describe how issues of ill-posedness are addressed (e.g., via regularization) with accompanying guarantees. Clear statements of these assumptions and their role in the proofs are necessary to assess rigor and to understand the scope of applicability.
