This paper proposes two estimators of the Radon–Nikodym derivative f = p/q based on the identities (i) E_p[k(x, ·) f] = E_q[k(x, ·)] and (ii) E_p[k(x, ·) f] ≈ q(x). The integral operators are replaced with their empirical counterparts, and the family {k(x, ·)} is treated as a set of test functions over which the discrepancy between the two sides is minimized. This choice enables the use of standard kernel methods for optimization. The paper provides convergence guarantees for both estimators under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, with convergence rates that (as expected for density-related problems) depend critically on the ambient dimension.

Overall, I find this to be a well-executed and timely contribution. The problem is important, and the paper offers both a convergence analysis and supporting experiments.

Comments and questions:

- Convergence intuition:
  - Optimizing over the class {k(x, ·)} appears sufficient to guarantee consistency of the ratio estimator. For estimator (ii), this is intuitive due to the bandwidth dependence. It would be helpful to give a short, high-level intuition for why the same holds for (i), beyond the formal argument.

- Choice of objective and norms:
  - On page 2 you optimize in L2,p, whereas in equation (9) you combine L2,p and L2,q. Some motivation for this specific choice of cost function would be useful. In particular, it would help to connect these norms to target tasks: which applications motivate weighting errors under p versus under q (or mixtures of the two)?

- Applications and motivation of the setup:
  - One application discussed is importance sampling, where the ratio q/p is used to move from E_q to E_p. In your empirical objectives, however, you already estimate E_p and E_q to build the loss. Why not directly use the E_q estimate you already have? Can E_p(q/p) be preferable to directly using E_q in practice? I am seeking more motivation for estimating the ratio in this setup, given that both E_p and E_q must be estimated anyway. You note on p. 5 that sampling is difficult from E_q; is this the primary application scenario you have in mind?

- Regularization and smoothness assumptions:
  - You regularize to enforce smoothness of the ratio q/p, while your theorems assume smoothness of p and q (which seems the natural assumption). Is there a principled relationship between smoothness of p and q and smoothness of their ratio? For instance, under what conditions (e.g., p bounded away from zero) does smoothness of p and q imply smoothness of the ratio? Conversely, given a smooth ratio, do there exist smooth p and q realizing it? I realize the ratio is not unique (many different p and q can yield the same ratio, and one can make either density arbitrarily irregular on sets of measure zero or more), but a brief discussion of when smoothness transfers would be valuable.

- Dimensional dependence:
  - Please discuss the dependence on dimension d in your rates. For which ranges of d (and sample sizes) do you expect the proposed methods to be practically useful?

- Choice of test function family U:
  - Is there a principled motivation for the particular family U = {k(x, ·)} beyond computational convenience? For example, are there approximation or identifiability considerations that inform this choice?

- Broader perspective:
  - In covariate shift and related q/p settings, a core challenge is selecting a generic, task-relevant cost function. One approach—adopted here—is to work through expectation operators with suitable test functions to recover the target quantity. Do you view the choice {k(x, ·)} as “the right” or canonical approach for this problem class? There seems to be some conceptual similarity to [1], which also leverages transformations to access the quantities of interest; I find your approach for the Radon–Nikodym derivative appealing because it directly operates via test functions, but the broader strategy feels related.

Reference:
[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor

Overall assessment: This is a strong and timely paper addressing an important problem, with clear methodological contributions, a convergence study, and empirical results.
