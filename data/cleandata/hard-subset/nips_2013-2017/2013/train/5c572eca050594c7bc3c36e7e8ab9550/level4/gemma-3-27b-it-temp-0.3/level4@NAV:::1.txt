This paper introduces a novel approach to density ratio estimation, specifically targeting the problem of estimating p/q. While several methods already exist, this work distinguishes itself by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation.

The paper is well-written and presents an important and interesting problem. 

However, several points require further attention. 

(i) The paper lacks detail in certain areas. For instance, the claim that \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t) requires justification, either through a proof included in the paper or supplementary materials, or a relevant citation. This applies to similar statements throughout the manuscript.

(ii) The method’s applicability to density estimation as a special case (when q=1) is noteworthy, particularly its independence from the typical bandwidth-to-zero convergence requirement seen in kernel density estimation. Expanding the discussion to clearly delineate the role of bandwidth parameters in this work versus standard KDE would be beneficial for readers.

(iii) A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization is recommended, as its familiarity may vary within the NIPS community.

(iv) All proofs are currently relegated to the supplementary material, and their correctness has not been verified.

(v) A discussion of why a naive plug-in density estimation approach (separate estimation of p and q) is inferior to the proposed method would strengthen the paper.

(vi) The paper would benefit from a comparative analysis of its performance against existing methods, such as the minimax bounds derived in [12]. While Corollaries 2 and 4 provide convergence rate bounds, their proximity to the optimal minimax rate remains unclear.

(vii) Please include references for the datasets utilized in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) Demonstrating the method’s behavior on simple, controlled toy problems with known p and q would be valuable. For example, a plot comparing the true and estimated p/q for 1-dimensional densities could provide a clear visual assessment of the estimation quality.

Overall, this is a promising paper on density ratio estimation. However, clarifying its performance relative to existing algorithms (e.g., [12]) and providing illustrative examples with toy problems would significantly enhance its impact.
