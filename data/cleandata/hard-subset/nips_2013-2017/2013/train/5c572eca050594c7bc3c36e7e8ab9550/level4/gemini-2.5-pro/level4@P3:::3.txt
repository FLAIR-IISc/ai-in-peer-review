This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q/p, a quantity of significant interest in areas such as importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation in transfer learning. The central contribution is a novel formulation of this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. By viewing the density ratio as the unknown function in this equation, the authors leverage the well-established framework of Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) to derive a family of algorithms, which they name Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE).

The authors propose two primary settings: Type I, where samples from both distributions p and q are available, and Type II, where samples from p are available along with the ability to evaluate q at those sample points. This formulation leads to simple, closed-form solutions for the density ratio estimate, expressed as linear combinations of kernel functions centered on the data points. The paper further contributes a detailed theoretical analysis, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for the FIRE estimators using Gaussian kernels, covering cases where data lies in R^d or on a smooth manifold. A significant practical contribution is a completely unsupervised model selection technique called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which provides a principled way to tune the algorithm's hyperparameters (kernel width and regularization strength). The paper's approach is situated within a rich body of related work, drawing clear connections and distinctions to methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF), noting that their framework can be seen as a regularized extension of ideas similar to those underlying KMM, while offering more stability and a natural out-of-sample extension.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of the paper is its elegant reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem. This approach connects a core machine learning problem to a classical field of applied mathematics, providing a solid theoretical foundation. It separates the core integral equation from the regularization scheme, offering a flexible framework where different norms and regularizers could be employed.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates for the proposed estimators. The analysis covers both Euclidean and manifold settings, which is a non-trivial extension and adds significant value. This theoretical backing is a substantial contribution compared to more heuristic approaches.

3.  **Practicality and Simplicity:** Despite the sophisticated theoretical underpinnings, the resulting FIRE algorithms are simple to implement, involving standard kernel matrix operations with closed-form solutions. The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection addresses a critical and often challenging aspect of unsupervised learning, making the overall method highly practical.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly motivates the problem, derives the algorithms, presents the theoretical results, and validates the approach with comprehensive experiments.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Clarity on Foundational Assumptions:** The theoretical framework rests on several implicit assumptions that should be stated more explicitly and discussed.
    *   **Dominating Measure:** The paper does not explicitly state the dominating measure with respect to which the densities `p` and `q` are defined. While context implies the Lebesgue measure, explicitly stating this is crucial for mathematical rigor.
    *   **Support of the Densities:** For the ratio `q(x)/p(x)` to be well-defined and meaningful, the support of `q` should be contained within the support of `p`. More critically, the method's behavior when `p(x)` is close to zero is a key concern. The use of a regularizer `λ` likely prevents division by zero in the empirical problem, but the theoretical and practical implications of near-zero density values in `p` are not discussed. A discussion on how the method handles or is affected by violations of this support condition would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Assumption of Density Existence:** The framework fundamentally assumes that the probability measures P and Q have densities. From a measure-theoretic standpoint, this is a strong assumption, as measures without densities can exist arbitrarily close to those with densities. While this is a common assumption in the literature, a brief acknowledgment of this limitation, especially in the context of high-dimensional or complex data, would be appropriate.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   In Section 4, the CD-CV method relies on a collection of test functions `U`. The performance of the model selection could be sensitive to the choice of this family. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of CD-CV to the choice and number of test functions used?
*   The theoretical analysis provides convergence rates for the proposed algorithm. It would be insightful to compare these rates, even if only qualitatively, to the known minimax optimal rates for density ratio estimation (e.g., as in [14]) to better contextualize the theoretical contribution.
*   The experiments demonstrate strong empirical performance. A brief discussion on the computational complexity of the FIRE algorithm compared to LSIF and KMM would be a valuable addition. The paper claims better computational efficiency, but this is not explicitly quantified.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, theoretically sound, and practical approach to density ratio estimation. The connection to Fredholm integral equations is insightful and provides a principled foundation. The primary weaknesses relate to a lack of explicit discussion around some of the foundational mathematical assumptions. These are relatively minor and could be addressed in a revised version. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS.
