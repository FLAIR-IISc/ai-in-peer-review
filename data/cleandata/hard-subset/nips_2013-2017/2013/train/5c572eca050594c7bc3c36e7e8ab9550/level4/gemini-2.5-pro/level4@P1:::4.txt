This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio `q/p`, also known as the importance function. This is a crucial task in areas like importance sampling, transfer learning under covariate shift, and selection bias correction. The authors' core contribution is to reframe this estimation problem as a classical Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This is achieved through two main formulations:
1.  **Type I:** Based on the equality `E_p[k(x,·) * (q/p)] = E_q[k(x,·)]`.
2.  **Type II:** Based on the approximation `E_p[k_t(x,·) * (q/p)] ≈ q(x)` for a localizing kernel `k_t`.

By treating the ratio `q/p` as the unknown function, these equations become inverse problems. The authors propose solving them using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This principled approach leads to a family of simple and efficient algorithms, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel case on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. A key practical contribution is an unsupervised model selection procedure called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). The effectiveness of the FIRE algorithm and the CD-CV method is demonstrated through experiments on several datasets, showing strong performance compared to existing methods like KMM and LSIF.

This is a well-executed and valuable contribution to the literature on density ratio estimation.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Formulation:** Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is an elegant and powerful idea. It connects the problem to a rich body of classical analysis and provides a principled foundation for algorithm development.
*   **Algorithmic Simplicity:** The resulting FIRE algorithms are derived directly from the regularization framework and have simple, closed-form solutions within the standard kernel methods paradigm.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates that explicitly characterize the dependency on the sample size, regularization parameter, and data dimensionality.
*   **Practical Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method addresses the critical and often difficult problem of hyperparameter tuning in an unsupervised setting, which is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing FIRE to relevant baselines across different tasks and demonstrating its competitive performance and computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**
The paper is strong, but the following points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve it.

1.  **Intuition for Convergence (Type I):** The convergence guarantees rely on minimizing the estimation error over the function set `{k(x,·)}`. For the Type II setting, the intuition is clear due to the localizing nature of the kernel `k_t`. However, for the Type I setting, it would be helpful to include a short, intuitive argument explaining why this particular set of test functions is sufficient to ensure convergence to the true density ratio.

2.  **Motivation for Loss Functions:** On page 2, the `L2,p` norm is used, while Equation (9) introduces a convex combination of `L2,p` and `L2,q` norms. The motivation for choosing one loss function over another could be strengthened. It would be beneficial to link the choice of the cost function more directly to specific applications or task objectives.

3.  **Application Scenarios:** One of the main applications mentioned is importance sampling, where `q/p` is used to transform an expectation under `p` to one under `q`. However, the empirical cost functions for FIRE themselves require samples from both `p` and `q` to estimate the respective expectations. Could the authors elaborate on scenarios where estimating the ratio `q/p` is more advantageous than estimating `E_q` directly from the sample from `q`? The paper notes that sampling from `q` can be difficult; expanding on this and other relevant scenarios (e.g., when the sample from `q` is small or biased) as the primary motivation would be very helpful.

4.  **Smoothness Assumptions:** The regularizer enforces smoothness on the estimated ratio `q/p`. The theorems, in turn, rely on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities `q` and `p`. It would be insightful if the authors could discuss the relationship between these assumptions. For example, do smooth densities `p` and `q` (perhaps bounded away from zero) necessarily imply a smooth ratio? Conversely, if the ratio is known to be smooth, does this imply the existence of smooth densities `p` and `q` that produce it?

5.  **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The derived convergence rates show a clear dependence on the dimension `d`. A discussion of the practical implications of this dependency would be valuable. Based on these rates, for what range of dimensions would the authors expect their method to be most useful in practice?

6.  **CD-CV Test Functions:** For the CD-CV model selection method, could the authors provide more motivation for the specific choice of test function families `U` (i.e., random linear functions and half-spaces)?

7.  **Relation to Broader Methods:** This work fits into a broader class of methods that estimate density ratios by matching expectations over a set of test functions. It would be interesting to see a discussion on why the reproducing kernel basis `{k(x,·)}` is a particularly suitable choice. The overall approach also bears some conceptual similarity to the "smooth operators" framework [7], which uses integral operators to study properties of distributions. While the goals and techniques differ, a brief comment on the relationship could help place this work in a wider context.

Overall, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem. It provides a novel perspective, solid theoretical backing, and convincing experimental results. I recommend acceptance.
