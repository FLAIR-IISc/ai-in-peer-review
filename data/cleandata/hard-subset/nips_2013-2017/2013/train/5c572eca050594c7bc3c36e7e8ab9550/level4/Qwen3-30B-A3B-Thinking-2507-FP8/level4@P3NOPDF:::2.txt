

This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating density ratios between two probability distributions, addressing a key challenge in unsupervised learning. The first approach formulates density ratio estimation as a regularized Type I Fredholm equation using the L² norm of the deviation from the importance sampling identity, while the second method employs a kernel with a vanishing locality parameter, expressed as a Type II Fredholm equation. The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate practical efficacy through experiments. The primary strength lies in enabling cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection—a significant advancement, as CV is rarely applicable in unsupervised settings. Unlike kernel-based methods such as KMM, where the objective function depends on the kernel and precludes meaningful CV comparisons, the L² formulation here provides a principled basis for CV, making it a compelling practical contribution.

However, several theoretical and technical points warrant clarification. First, Theorem 1 derives a convergence rate containing the factor $-t / \log \lambda$, which is suboptimal under fixed $t$. In kernel methods literature (e.g., Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007), polynomial rates in sample size are standard when $\lambda$ decays appropriately with $n$ under spectral assumptions on the kernel operator. The authors should discuss whether additional assumptions (e.g., on the spectrum of $K_{p,t}$ or the smoothness of $q/p$) could yield a polynomial rate for fixed $t$. This would strengthen the theoretical contribution and align with established kernel method analysis.

Second, the paper relies on the assumption that $q/p$ belongs to a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev space), which is restrictive. This assumption necessitates prior knowledge of the density ratio’s behavior or the tails of $p$ and $q$, potentially limiting applicability. Crucially, under such an assumption, the reciprocal ratio $p/q$ often exhibits poor behavior (e.g., unbounded derivatives or singularities), undermining downstream tasks like covariate shift adaptation where $p/q$ is used. The authors should explicitly address this limitation, discussing its implications for real-world applications.

The supplementary material requires significant revision. Several typographical and mathematical errors impede verification of the theoretical results. Specifically:  
- Lemma 5: $W_2^2$ should be $W_s^2$; the bound should read $D_1 (t / -\log \lambda)^2 + \lambda^{1-\alpha} D_2 \|f\|_2^2$.  
- Lines 675 and 686: Incorrect expressions (Eq. (31) is correct).  
- Proof of Lemma 5: The integral domain at line 1147 must be $\|\xi\|^2 \geq \cdots$.  
These errors, particularly in the core convergence analysis, undermine confidence in the main theorems. The authors should correct all discrepancies before publication.

While the CV-enabled parameter selection is a strong point, the paper could further clarify why the L² norm overcomes the CV incompatibility inherent in RKHS-based objectives (e.g., KMM). A brief remark contrasting the L² objective’s CV-friendliness with RKHS objectives’ kernel-dependent values would strengthen the methodological justification.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable practical advance by enabling CV for density ratio estimation. The core theoretical contributions are promising but require refinement: (1) a deeper discussion of achievable convergence rates under standard assumptions, (2) acknowledgment of the $q/p$ smoothness assumption’s limitations, and (3) correction of technical errors in the supplementary material. Addressing these points would significantly elevate the work’s impact.
