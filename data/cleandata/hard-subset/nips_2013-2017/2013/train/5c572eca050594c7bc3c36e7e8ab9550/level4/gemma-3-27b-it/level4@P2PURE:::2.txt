This paper introduces two novel methods for density ratio estimation. The first leverages the importance sampling equation and reformulates it as a regularization problem, incorporating both an L^2 norm penalty for deviation from unity and a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) regularization term—effectively a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that, for consistency, must approach zero, and is similarly expressed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). The authors support their approach with theoretical convergence results and experimental validation.

A key advantage of the proposed methods, particularly the L^2 formulation, over existing techniques like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) is the ability to utilize cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. While CV is a standard practice in supervised learning, general methods for unsupervised parameter tuning are lacking; this work addresses that gap for density ratio estimation through the clever application of the L^2 norm. It is worth noting that the objective functions derived from RKHS regularization, as commonly used in methods like KMM, are not well-suited for CV-based kernel selection due to the kernel-dependence of the objective function values, rendering comparisons problematic.

Regarding the convergence analysis presented in Theorem 1, the derived rate, incorporating the (-t/log λ) factor, appears slow for a fixed value of *t*. Kernel method literature frequently demonstrates polynomial convergence rates with respect to sample size when *t* is fixed, after establishing a rate for λ. It would be valuable to investigate whether additional, reasonable assumptions could yield a polynomial convergence rate for the proposed estimator. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) utilized assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to achieve optimal rates in kernel ridge regression. Exploring stronger assumptions on the ratio *q/p* might similarly lead to a polynomial rate. A discussion of such possibilities, along with corresponding rates under strengthened assumptions with fixed *t*, would significantly enhance the paper. 

It is standard in density ratio estimation to assume that *q/p* belongs to a well-behaved function class, such as a Sobolev space. However, it’s crucial to acknowledge the strength of this assumption, as it requires prior knowledge regarding either the ratio itself or the tails of the underlying distributions *p* and *q*. Under such an assumption, the inverse ratio *p/q* often exhibits poor behavior. A discussion of this inherent limitation of density ratio estimation is necessary. 

While not essential, the supplementary materials require substantial improvement. Numerous typos and minor errors hinder verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. Specifically, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-\alpha} D2 \|f\|_2^2. The expressions on lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, whereas Eq.(31) is correct. Furthermore, in the proof of Lemma 5, the integral’s domain on line 1147 should be \| ξ\|^2 ≥. Given the centrality of these theoretical contributions to the paper, a thorough revision of the supplementary material is strongly recommended.
