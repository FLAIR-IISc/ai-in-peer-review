This paper discusses two new methods for estimating the density ratio of two probabilities. The first method uses the equation of importance sampling and formulates it as a regularization problem, where the L^2 norm of the deviation from the equality and an RKHS regularization term are used (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method considers a kernel with a locality parameter that should go to zero for consistency, also expressed in the form of regularization (Type II Fredholm equation). The paper shows some theoretical results on convergence and demonstrates the practical performance experimentally.

While the method has similarities to existing methods such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), a strong advantage of the proposed one is that the L^2 formulation enables the application of cross-validation (CV) approaches to choose the parameters in the algorithm. Unlike supervised problems, where CV is dominant as a method for choosing parameters in practice, there are no general methods for unsupervised learning. The paper solves this issue for density ratio estimation with a clever idea of using the L^2 norm.

It is worth noting that the objective function given by RKHS, such as in KMM, is not appropriate for choosing a kernel with CV, since the values of the objective function depend on the kernel and are thus not comparable.

The theoretical analysis in Theorem 1 shows a convergence rate that is slow with fixed t, due to the (-t/log λ) factor. In many literatures of kernel methods, with fixed t, one can derive a polynomial rate with respect to the sample size after fixing the rate of λ. It would be interesting to explore whether there are any reasonable additional assumptions that give a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator. For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) make assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to derive the optimal rate for kernel ridge regression. Similarly, making a stronger assumption on q/p may also derive a polynomial rate.

In estimating a density ratio, it is usually necessary to make an assumption that q/p is in a nice function class, such as Sobolev. However, this assumption is very strong, requiring some knowledge on the ratio or tails of p and q. Under such an assumption on q/p, the opposite ratio p/q usually behaves badly. It would be beneficial to include some discussions on this limitation of density ratio estimation in the paper.

Finally, while not a requirement, the supplementary materials could be improved. There are many typos and minor mistakes, which make it hard to check the correctness of the theoretical results in the main paper. For instance, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-\alpha} D2 \|f\|_2^2. The expressions at line 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq.(31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be \| \xi\|^2 \geq. Since the theoretical results of convergence consist of main contributions of this paper, it is advised that the authors revise the supplementary material.

The paper proposes a new approach to density ratio estimation, with a strong point being that CV can be effectively applied to choose parameters.

Arguments For Acceptance:

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation using the Fredholm equation and regularization.
2. The method has a strong advantage over existing methods, such as KMM, in that it allows for the application of cross-validation (CV) approaches to choose parameters.
3. The paper provides some theoretical results on convergence and demonstrates the practical performance experimentally.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

1. The theoretical analysis in Theorem 1 shows a slow convergence rate with fixed t.
2. The assumption that q/p is in a nice function class, such as Sobolev, is very strong and may not always hold in practice.
3. The supplementary materials contain many typos and minor mistakes, which make it hard to check the correctness of the theoretical results in the main paper.
