This paper discusses two new methods for estimating the density ratio of two probabilities. The first method uses the importance sampling equation and formulates it as a regularization problem using the L^2 norm of the deviation from the mean and the RKHS regularization term (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method considers a kernel where the locality parameter must go to zero for stability. The latter is also expressed as regularization (Type II Fredholm equation). This paper shows some theoretical results on convergence and demonstrates practical performance experimentally.

While the method has similarities with existing methods like KMM, a strong advantage of the proposed method is that the L^2 formulation enables us to apply a cross-validation (CV) approach to choosing parameters in the algorithm. Unlike supervised problems, where CV is effective as a method for choosing parameters in practice, there is no general method for unsupervised learning. This paper addresses this issue with a clever idea of using the L^2 norm for density ratio estimation.

It is better to note that the objective function given by RKHS, such as in KMM, is not suitable for choosing a kernel with CV, because the values of the objective function depend on the kernel and are thus not comparable.

In Proposition 1, the derivative convergence rate is slower with constant t, by the factor (-t/log \lambda). In much of the literature on kernel methods, a polynomial rate can be obtained with respect to the sample size after fixing the rate of \lambda with constant t. Are there any reasonable additional assumptions that give a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator? For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) make assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to obtain the optimal rate for kernel ridge regression. I would guess that making a strong assumption on q/p would also yield a polynomial rate. It would be more interesting if the authors discussed such a polynomial rate under strong assumptions with constant t.

When estimating the density ratio, we usually need to make the assumption that q/p is in a good function class such as Sobolev. It is important to note that this assumption is very strong, requiring some knowledge of the ratio or tail of p and q. Under such an assumption on q/p, the inverse ratio p/q usually behaves poorly. It is necessary to include some discussion somewhere on this limitation of density ratio estimation.

This is not a requirement, but the supplementary material should be improved. It contains many typos and minor errors, which make it difficult to verify the correctness of the theoretical results in the main paper. For example, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound D1 (t/-log \lambda)^2 + \lambda^{1-\alpha} D2 \|f\|_2^2. Expressions expressed on lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while equation (31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral on line 1147 should be \| \xi\|^2 \geq. Since the theoretical results of convergence are made up of the main contribution of this paper, I advise the authors to revise the supplementary material.
