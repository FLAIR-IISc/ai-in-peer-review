This paper introduces two novel methods for density ratio estimation, framing both within a regularization framework reminiscent of Fredholm equations (Type I via importance sampling with L^2 deviation and RKHS regularization, and Type II with a locality-parameter-dependent kernel). The authors provide theoretical convergence results and demonstrate empirical performance.

A key strength of the proposed methods lies in the L^2 formulation, which uniquely enables the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection – a significant advantage over existing techniques like KMM, and a valuable contribution given the scarcity of general parameter selection methods for unsupervised learning problems. It would be beneficial to explicitly highlight that objective functions based on RKHS norms, as used in KMM, are less suited for CV-based kernel selection due to the kernel-dependence of the objective values, rendering direct comparison problematic.

Regarding the convergence analysis, the convergence rate presented in Theorem 1, incorporating the (-t/log λ) factor, appears relatively slow for fixed *t*. Kernel method literature often demonstrates polynomial convergence rates with respect to sample size when *t* is fixed, achieved by carefully controlling λ. Investigating potential additional assumptions – such as those concerning the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} (as in Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007) or stronger constraints on q/p – to achieve a polynomial rate would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution. Exploring such rates under more restrictive assumptions would be of considerable interest.

A crucial consideration in density ratio estimation is the typical assumption that q/p resides in a well-behaved function class (e.g., Sobolev space). The authors should acknowledge the inherent strength of this assumption, as it necessitates prior knowledge about the ratio or the tails of the underlying distributions *p* and *q*. Furthermore, they should discuss the potential for ill-behaved behavior of the inverse ratio, p/q, under such conditions, highlighting a fundamental limitation of the density ratio estimation problem itself.

While not essential, a thorough revision of the supplementary materials is strongly recommended. Numerous typos and minor errors currently impede verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. Specific examples include inconsistencies in Lemma 5 (Ws^2 instead of W2^2, an incorrect bound, and errors at lines 675, 686, and a discrepancy between lines 675/686 and Eq. (31)), and an incorrect domain of integration in the proof of Lemma 5. Given the centrality of these theoretical results to the paper’s contributions, ensuring the accuracy of the supplementary material is paramount.

**References:**
Caponnetto A., De Vito E. Optimal Rates for Regularized Least-Squares Algorithm. Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 7 331-368 (2007).

Overall, this paper presents a promising new approach to density ratio estimation, with the effective application of CV for parameter tuning representing a notable strength.
