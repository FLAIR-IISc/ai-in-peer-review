

The paper introduces a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation, offering a fresh perspective on an important and well-studied topic. The method is well-motivated, and the exposition is clear and accessible, making it a promising contribution to the field. However, several critical gaps must be addressed before the work can be considered for publication.

The primary strength lies in the elegant reformulation of density ratio estimation, which enables a new theoretical framework. The paper is well-structured and technically sound in its core development. Nevertheless, the following concerns require clarification and expansion:

1.  **Missing Justifications:** The paper states without proof that $\int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)$ (and similar approximations). Given the centrality of this step to the method, a concise proof (in the main text or supplementary) or a clear reference is essential. Many readers will need this detail to assess the validity of the approach.

2.  **Connection to Density Estimation:** The special case where $q=1$ (yielding density estimation) is intriguing. The paper notes that bandwidths need not converge to zero for consistency, contrasting with standard KDE. This is a significant point but requires deeper discussion. The paper should explicitly contrast the role of bandwidth parameters here with those in KDE to clarify this key advantage for readers unfamiliar with the nuances.

3.  **Tikhonov Regularization:** As the paper relies on Tikhonov regularization, a brief explanation of its purpose and relevance within the context of solving the integral equation (e.g., why it addresses ill-posedness) would be valuable for the broader NIPS audience, many of whom may not be familiar with this technique.

4.  **Proof Verification:** All proofs are relegated to the supplementary material. While this is acceptable, the reviewer notes they have not verified the correctness of these proofs. The authors should ensure the supplementary proofs are rigorous and complete, or explicitly state that the main text's claims are supported by the provided proofs.

5.  **Comparison to Plug-in Estimation:** A brief discussion contrasting the proposed method with the naive plug-in approach (estimating $p$ and $q$ separately) is necessary. The paper should explain *why* the direct ratio estimation approach is superior in terms of bias, variance, or practical performance, especially given the challenges of density estimation.

6.  **Performance Benchmarking:** The paper provides convergence rate upper bounds (Corollaries 2 and 4), but it is unclear how these compare to the known minimax optimal rates. For instance, reference [12] establishes minimax bounds; the authors should explicitly discuss the gap between their upper bounds and the minimax rate to contextualize the method's theoretical efficiency. A comparative analysis of empirical performance against state-of-the-art methods (including [12]) on standard benchmarks is also crucial for assessing practical utility.

7.  **Dataset References:** The datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) require proper citation. The paper must provide references for these datasets to enable reproducibility and allow readers to understand their standard usage in the literature.

8.  **Toy Problem Demonstration:** A critical omission is the lack of a simple, illustrative example on known densities. For instance, a 1D plot showing true $p/q$ versus the estimated ratio for explicitly defined $p$ and $q$ (e.g., Gaussians) would significantly aid readers in intuitively assessing the method's quality and behavior. This is particularly important given the theoretical claims.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically interesting and well-written reformulation of density ratio estimation. The core idea is promising, but the current version lacks sufficient detail to fully evaluate its novelty, theoretical guarantees, and practical advantages over existing methods. Addressing the points above—especially providing the missing proofs, clarifying the bandwidth role in density estimation, benchmarking against minimax rates and competitors, and including a toy example—is essential for the paper to make a strong contribution. The method's potential is evident, but the current presentation does not fully convey its comparative strengths or enable a thorough assessment of its practical performance. The authors should revise the manuscript to incorporate these elements before the work can be recommended for publication.
