The paper proposes a novel approach to estimate the density ratio p/q by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. This is a well-studied problem with existing methods, but the authors' reformulation brings a fresh perspective.

Strong points of the paper include its clear writing style and the importance of the density ratio estimation problem. The authors have done a great job in presenting their ideas and making the paper enjoyable to read.

However, there are some weak points and areas that require clarification. Firstly, some technical details are missing, such as the proof or reference for the statement ∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t). Providing these details would strengthen the paper and make it more self-contained.

Secondly, it is worth noting that this method can also be used for density estimation as a special case when q=1. A discussion on how this approach differs from standard kernel density estimation (KDE) methods, particularly in terms of bandwidth parameters, would be beneficial. The authors should highlight that the bandwidth parameters in their method have different roles than those in KDE.

Additionally, a brief introduction to Tikhonov regularization would be helpful, as this may not be familiar to the majority of the NIPS community.

The proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, and while I have not checked them, it is essential to ensure their correctness.

A comparison with a naive plug-in approach, where densities p and q are separately estimated, would be valuable. The authors should explain why their method is superior to this approach.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear comparison with other methods, such as [12], which also derives minimax bounds. The authors should discuss how their upper bounds on convergence rates (Corollary 2 and 4) relate to the optimal minimax rate.

References for the datasets used in the paper (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) are also missing.

Finally, it would be beneficial to include a demonstration of the proposed method on toy problems with fixed and known p and q. For example, a plot of the true p/q and the estimated p/q for 1-dimensional p and q densities would help readers assess the quality of the estimation.

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation, but it requires some clarifications and additions to strengthen its contribution and impact.
