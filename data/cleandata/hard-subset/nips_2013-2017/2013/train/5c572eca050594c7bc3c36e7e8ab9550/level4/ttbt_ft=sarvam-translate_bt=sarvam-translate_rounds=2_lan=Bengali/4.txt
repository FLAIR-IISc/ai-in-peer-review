The authors used two equations to find two approximate values of the Radon-Nicodime derivative f= p/q:
(i) Ep ( k(x,.) f) = E_q k(x,.) and (ii) Ep (k(x,.) f ) ~ q(x)
The integral operators have been replaced with empirical versions. {k(x,.)} can be seen as a set of testing functions where the difference between the right and left sides is minimized. The advantage of choosing {k(x,.)} as a family of testing functions is that the standard kernel method can be used for optimization.

Convergence is guaranteed in both cases under the assumption of smooth and finite limits of density. The rate of convergence mainly depends on the dimension of the underlying space – which is expected for density assumptions.

- Regularizer: You apply smoothness to the q/p ratio. Later in the theorem, you get a smoothness estimate on q and p (which I would say is normal). So I'm wondering if there is a relationship between the smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of the ratio? I think there is already an answer here that you have many different q and p that make the same ratio; that is, it becomes extremely irregular when q and p are changed by a countable or uncountable number of points. So it could be smooth p,q (possibly limited to away from 0) => the ratio is smooth (?) or the ratio is smooth => there is a smooth p and q that makes the ratio.
