

The authors propose a novel approach for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative \(f = p/q\) using two key equalities: (i) \(E_p[k(x,\cdot)f] = E_q[k(x,\cdot)]\) and (ii) \(E_p[k(x,\cdot)f] \approx q(x)\). They replace integral operators with empirical counterparts, interpreting \(\{k(x,\cdot)\}\) as a family of test functions over which the discrepancy between the sides of each equality is minimized. This choice leverages standard kernel methods for optimization, providing a computationally tractable framework. Convergence guarantees are established under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on \(p\) and \(q\), with the convergence rate explicitly dependent on the ambient dimension—consistent with expectations for density estimation problems.

The work is well-executed and addresses a relevant problem with rigorous analysis. Below are specific comments to enhance clarity and impact:

1.  **Convergence Rate Intuition (Equality i):** For equality (i), optimizing over \(\{k(x,\cdot)\}\) suffices for convergence to \(f\). While the bandwidth dependence in (ii) provides intuitive justification, a brief argument explaining why this holds for (i) would strengthen the presentation. Specifically, why does minimizing the discrepancy over the kernel family ensure convergence under the given assumptions?

2.  **Cost Function Justification:** The optimization on page 2 uses the \(L_{2,p}\) norm, but equation (9) combines \(L_{2,p}\) and \(L_{2,q}\). A clear motivation for this specific cost function is needed. Since the choice likely stems from target applications, linking it explicitly to concrete tasks (e.g., importance sampling, covariate shift) would improve the paper’s practical relevance.

3.  **Application Motivation (Importance Sampling):** The paper mentions importance sampling as an application (\(q/p\) enables moving from \(E_q\) to \(E_p\)). However, the method requires estimating both \(E_p\) and \(E_q\) to compute the empirical cost for \(q/p\). Why not directly use the estimated \(E_q\) for the target expectation? The brief mention on page 5 that "sampling from \(E_q\) is difficult" seems key—please clarify if this is the primary motivating scenario for the proposed setup.

4.  **Smoothness Assumptions:** The regularization enforces smoothness on \(f = q/p\), yet Theorem 1 assumes smoothness on \(p\) and \(q\) (a natural requirement). It would be valuable to discuss the relationship between smoothness of \(p\), \(q\), and \(f\). For instance: Does \(p, q\) smooth (and bounded away from zero) imply \(f\) is smooth? Conversely, if \(f\) is smooth, do there exist smooth \(p, q\) yielding it? The current discussion hints at this via pathological examples, but a concise theoretical link would be beneficial.

5.  **Dimensional Dependence:** The paper notes the dimension-dependent convergence rate but does not discuss practical implications. For which dimensions (e.g., \(d \leq 5\), \(d \leq 10\)) would this method be effective? A brief discussion on the feasibility threshold for real-world applications would be helpful.

6.  **Test Function Family Motivation:** The choice of the kernel family \(\{k(x,\cdot)\}\) as test functions is central. A more principled justification for why this family is suitable (beyond computational convenience) would strengthen the methodological contribution. Is it uniquely optimal under certain criteria?

7.  **Broader Methodological Context:** The paper’s approach—using test functions to extract density ratios—shares conceptual similarities with methods in [1] (e.g., "Smooth Operators" by Grunewalder et al.). While the authors’ formulation for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is elegant, it would be insightful to discuss how this fits into the broader landscape of density ratio estimation. Specifically, does this approach represent a "natural" or "optimal" path compared to alternatives that also manipulate expectation operators with test functions?

Overall, this is a timely and strong contribution. The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis, addresses a critical problem in machine learning (density ratio estimation), and includes experimental validation. The core methodology is sound, and the technical execution is thorough. Addressing the points above—particularly the cost function motivation, application context, and relationship between smoothness assumptions—would significantly elevate the paper’s impact and clarity. The work merits acceptance.
