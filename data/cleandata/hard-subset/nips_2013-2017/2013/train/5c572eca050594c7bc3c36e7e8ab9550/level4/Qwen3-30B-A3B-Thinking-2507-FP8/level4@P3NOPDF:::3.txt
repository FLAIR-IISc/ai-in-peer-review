

The paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability density functions using kernel methods and Fredholm integral equations. While the technical approach is mathematically sophisticated, the work suffers from critical ambiguities that undermine its theoretical rigor and practical relevance.  

A primary concern is the absence of a specified dominating measure $\nu$. The paper discusses densities relative to $\nu$ but fails to identify $\nu$ (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure, or another reference measure). This omission is severe: the definition of a density and the validity of the ratio $p/q$ depend entirely on $\nu$. For instance, if $\nu$ is Lebesgue measure, the ratio is undefined on sets where $q$ vanishes; if $\nu$ is counting measure, the problem reduces to discrete probability spaces. Crucially, the paper does not clarify whether $\nu$-null sets (e.g., measure-zero sets where $q(x)=0$) are excluded from consideration. This lack of specification renders the problem statement ambiguous and risks invalidating the proposed solution.  

Furthermore, the paper lacks explicit assumptions about the domain and density properties. It is unclear whether the support $X$ is $\mathbb{R}^d$ (a standard assumption in ML) or a general topological space. This matters significantly: the Fredholm equation framework and kernel methods typically assume Euclidean structure, but the paper does not state this. More critically, the positivity of the densities is unstated. For the ratio $p/q$ to be well-defined, $q(x) > 0$ must hold almost everywhere with respect to $\nu$. The paper does not assume or verify this, nor does it address cases where $q$ vanishes on a set of positive $\nu$-measure—a common issue in practice (e.g., with Gaussian mixtures). Without this, the ratio estimator may be undefined or unstable.  

The theoretical foundation also appears questionable. The authors frame the problem as an inverse problem via a Fredholm equation, but they overlook a fundamental ML challenge: the assumption that both $p$ and $q$ admit $\nu$-densities is fragile. As is well-known in measure theory, any $\varepsilon$-neighborhood of a probability measure $P$ with a $\nu$-density contains measures without $\nu$-densities (e.g., via the Cantor distribution). This implies that density ratios are inherently unstable under small perturbations—a critical issue for ML applications where data is noisy. The paper does not engage with this instability, nor does it provide error bounds that account for it. Consequently, the proposed method’s robustness remains unaddressed.  

The lack of clarity extends to the paper’s contribution. While the Fredholm approach is novel, the absence of assumptions about $\nu$, $X$, and density positivity prevents a rigorous analysis of the estimator’s consistency or convergence. For example, without specifying $\nu$, it is impossible to assess whether the kernel-based solution converges in the $\nu$-sense. Similarly, without domain constraints, the paper cannot justify the use of Euclidean kernels or the invertibility of the integral operator. These gaps make it difficult to evaluate the method’s theoretical guarantees or compare it to existing density-ratio estimators (e.g., KLIEP or logistic regression).  

In summary, the paper’s core issue is a lack of mathematical precision. To be viable, the work must: (1) explicitly define $\nu$ and justify its choice; (2) state all domain assumptions (e.g., $X \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$); (3) assume $q(x) > 0$ $\nu$-a.e. and address cases where this fails; and (4) discuss the instability of density ratios under perturbations. Without these, the theoretical claims are unsubstantiated, and the method’s practical utility is unclear. The current draft reads as a technical sketch rather than a rigorous contribution. While the Fredholm approach merits exploration, the present work falls short of NIPS’s standards for clarity and completeness. I recommend rejection unless the authors address these foundational gaps in a revised submission.
