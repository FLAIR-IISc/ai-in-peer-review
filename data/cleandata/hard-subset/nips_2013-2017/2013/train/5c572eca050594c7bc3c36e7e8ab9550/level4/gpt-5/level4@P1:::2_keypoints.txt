- Proposes a kernel-based framework that formulates density-ratio estimation q/p as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind solved by Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.  
- Develops two variants: Type I (uses importance sampling identity with samples from p and q) and Type II (uses local kernel Kt and requires q known/evaluable at p samples).  
- Derives closed-form, out-of-sample estimators (FIRE) via the representer theorem.  
- Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth submanifolds.  
- Introduces an unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) based on validating estimated ratios via importance sampling identities.  
- Empirically compares FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and thresholded inverse KDE, showing competitive performance and computational advantages under covariate shift.  
- Relation to KMM: similar operator identity but differs by direct RKHS regularization of the weight function enabling stability and out-of-sample prediction.  
- Relation to LSIF/KLIEP: differs by inverse-problem formulation and nonparametric kernel regularization with theoretical guarantees.  
- Strength: elegant conceptual contribution tying inverse problems, operator theory, and kernel learning.  
- Strength: practical, simple closed-form estimators with natural out-of-sample extension, unlike KMM’s sample-weight optimization.  
- Strength: CD-CV is a useful unsupervised validation mechanism addressing model selection in unsupervised/semi-supervised settings.  
- Strength: clear theoretical statements (concentration bounds/rates) under stated assumptions.  
- Strength: solid empirical results with favorable comparisons to baselines.  
- Weakness/question: Theorem 1 yields slow rates with fixed bandwidth t due to a (−t/log λ) factor; polynomial rates in n might be possible under additional spectral/source conditions.  
- Suggestion: Discuss stronger standard assumptions (eigenvalue decay, source conditions) à la Caponnetto & De Vito (2007) to obtain sharper polynomial rates with fixed t.  
- Weakness/question: Theory assumes q/p lies in a “nice” class (e.g., Sobolev), which is strong and raises concerns about tail behavior, unbounded ratios, and poor behavior of p/q; robustness and clipping strategies should be discussed.  
- Cross-validation point: Explicitly note RKHS-norm objectives (as in KMM) are unsuitable for kernel selection via CV because their scale depends on the kernel; L2-based CD-CV avoids this.  
- Supplement issues: Numerous typos/mistakes in the supplement impede verification; specific corrections requested (e.g., Lemma 5 notation and bounds, errors at lines 675/686, integral domain at line 1147).  
- Pros for acceptance: principled formulation, stable closed-form estimators with out-of-sample prediction, novel unsupervised model selection, and strong empirical performance.  
- Cons for acceptance: suboptimal fixed-bandwidth rates without discussion of stronger assumptions, strong smoothness/boundedness assumptions on q/p limiting applicability, and significant supplementary-material corrections needed.  
- Overall: Technically interesting and well-motivated work that advances density-ratio estimation; addressing rate issues under stronger assumptions and fixing the supplement would substantially strengthen the paper.
