The paper derives two estimators of the Radon–Nikodym derivative f = p/q from two identities: (i) E_p[k(x, .) f] = E_q[k(x, .)] and (ii) E_p[k(x, .) f] ≈ q(x). The integral operators are replaced by their empirical counterparts. The collection {k(x, .)} serves as a set of test functions over which the discrepancy between the two sides is minimized. Using {k(x, .)} has the benefit that standard kernel machinery can be used for optimization. The authors provide convergence guarantees for both formulations under smoothness and boundedness conditions on the densities. The convergence rates depend critically on the ambient dimension, as is typical for density estimation. I find this to be a neat and well-executed piece of work.

A few comments:
- On the convergence rates: Optimizing over {k(x, .)} appears sufficient to ensure convergence to the ratio. For (ii) this is intuitive via the bandwidth dependence. It would be helpful to include a brief intuitive explanation of why this also holds for (i).
- You optimize on page 2 over L2,p. Later, in (9), the objective mixes L2,p and L2,q. Some motivation for this choice of loss would be welcome. Ultimately, I imagine these choices should be guided by the downstream tasks; linking the objective to key applications would clarify this.
- On applications: one example is importance sampling, where q/p is used to move from E_q to E_p. Since you empirically estimate E_p and E_q to build your objective, why not directly use the E_q you already compute? Can E_p(q/p) be better than using E_q directly? I am essentially asking for motivation of the setup, given that you estimate both E_p and E_q to estimate q/p. You mention on p. 5 that sampling from E_q can be difficult—is this the primary application you have in mind?
- Regularization: You regularize the smoothness of q/p, whereas the theorem assumes smoothness of q and p (which seems the natural assumption). Is there a relationship between smoothness of q and p and smoothness of their ratio? One might expect something like “p, q smooth (and bounded away from 0) ⇒ ratio smooth,” or conversely, “ratio smooth ⇒ there exist smooth p and q producing that ratio,” though many distinct p, q can yield the same ratio by modifying them on sets of points. Clarifying this connection would be useful.
- It would be valuable to discuss the dimension dependence of your rates—for which ranges of d do you expect the method to be practically useful?
- Is there a principled rationale for your choice of the test-function family U?
- A broader question: In covariate shift/ratio estimation, a central challenge is defining a generic objective. One strategy is to operate via expectations and probe with test functions. Do you view the {k(x, .)} approach as “the right” choice here? There is also some resemblance to [1] in the use of transformations to access the target quantities. Your approach to the Radon–Nikodym derivative is cleaner via test functions, but the overarching strategy shares similarities.

[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor

Overall, this is a timely and strong paper on a relevant problem, with convergence analysis and empirical results.
