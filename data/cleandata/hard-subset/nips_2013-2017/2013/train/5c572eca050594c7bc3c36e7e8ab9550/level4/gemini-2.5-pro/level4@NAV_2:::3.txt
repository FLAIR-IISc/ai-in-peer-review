This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q/p, a quantity crucial for applications like importance sampling and covariate shift correction. The central idea is to reframe this estimation task as a classical inverse problem. Specifically, the authors formulate it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, where the density ratio is the unknown function to be solved for. This elegant formulation connects the problem to the well-established theory of integral operators and regularization.

The authors propose solving this inverse problem using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach leads to a family of algorithms, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). Two primary settings are considered: Type I, where samples from both p and q are available, and Type II, where samples are drawn from p and the values of q are known at these sample points. The paper connects its methodology to prior work, noting similarities and differences with methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), arguing that the proposed regularized approach offers improved stability and a more principled optimization procedure. Key contributions include not only the algorithms themselves but also a detailed theoretical analysis providing convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and a novel unsupervised method for model selection called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is novel and powerful. It provides a new perspective by connecting a common machine learning problem to classical operator theory, which may inspire further research.
*   **Quality (Theoretical Soundness):** The paper is technically strong. It provides a principled framework for deriving a family of algorithms and backs them with a rigorous theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates. This level of theoretical support is a significant strength.
*   **Practical Contribution:** The proposed unsupervised model selection procedure, CD-CV, addresses a critical and often difficult aspect of applying such methods in practice. The development of a data-driven way to select hyperparameters without labeled data is a highly valuable contribution.
*   **Clarity and Completeness:** The resulting FIRE algorithms are presented with explicit, simple-to-implement formulas. The experimental section appears thorough, comparing the proposed methods against relevant state-of-the-art approaches on several datasets and tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity on Assumptions:** The problem setup in the introduction could be more precise. The paper implicitly assumes the existence of densities with respect to the Lebesgue measure (indicated by integrals with `dx`). The domains of interest (Rd, manifolds) and other technical assumptions are formally stated later in the theoretical section. Clarifying these foundational assumptions at the beginning would improve the paper's readability.
*   **Handling of Support Mismatch:** The paper does not explicitly discuss the practical and theoretical challenge that arises when the support of p is not a superset of the support of q, or more generally, when p(x) can be zero or very small for some x where q(x) > 0. The density ratio q(x)/p(x) is ill-defined or can explode in such regions. While regularization may implicitly mitigate this, a direct discussion of this well-known issue in importance estimation would strengthen the work.
*   **Robustness of Density Assumption:** The method hinges on the assumption that both p and q possess well-behaved densities. From a measure-theoretic standpoint, this is a strong assumption, as many probability measures are not absolutely continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure. A brief discussion on the potential robustness or limitations of the method when this assumption is violated would be beneficial.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and theoretically well-grounded approach to density ratio estimation. The connection to Fredholm integral equations is insightful, the theoretical analysis is comprehensive, and the proposed model selection technique is of significant practical importance. The main weaknesses relate to a lack of clarity on some foundational assumptions and a need for discussion on the practical challenges of support mismatch, which could be addressed in a revision to further improve the manuscript.
