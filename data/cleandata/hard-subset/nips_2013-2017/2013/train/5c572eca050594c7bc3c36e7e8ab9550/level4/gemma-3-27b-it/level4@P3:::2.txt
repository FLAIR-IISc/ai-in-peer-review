This paper introduces a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as an inverse problem based on Fredholm integral equations. The authors propose two algorithms, FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), utilizing Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The core contribution lies in enabling parameter selection via cross-validation – a significant advantage in unsupervised settings where standard model selection techniques are often lacking. The paper provides theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrates promising empirical results.

The work builds upon existing methods for density ratio estimation, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [Gretton et al., 2009] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [Kanamori et al., 2010]. However, it distinguishes itself by explicitly framing the problem as an inverse problem, allowing for the application of tools from classical analysis and providing a principled approach to regularization. The ability to leverage cross-validation for parameter tuning, facilitated by the L2 formulation, is a notable strength. While KMM utilizes an RKHS norm, the proposed method's direct optimization over the RKHS avoids the limitations of KMM regarding out-of-sample extension and stability.

The theoretical analysis, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, is a valuable contribution. However, the derived convergence rate in Theorem 1, involving a factor of (-t/log λ), appears slow. Many kernel methods achieve polynomial rates with respect to sample size when the regularization parameter is appropriately tuned, assuming a fixed kernel width *t*. It would strengthen the paper to discuss potential additional assumptions – perhaps on the smoothness of q/p, inspired by works like Caponnetto and De Vito [2007] – that would lead to such polynomial rates with a fixed *t*. Exploring conditions under which a faster convergence rate can be achieved would significantly enhance the theoretical results.

A crucial discussion is missing regarding the assumptions inherent in density ratio estimation. Typically, such methods require assumptions about the regularity of q/p, often in Sobolev spaces. These assumptions can be quite strong, demanding prior knowledge about the ratio or the tails of the underlying distributions *p* and *q*. The resulting behavior of p/q under these conditions is often less well-behaved, representing a limitation of the approach. Addressing this point would provide a more nuanced understanding of the method's applicability.

The supplementary material requires significant improvement. Numerous typos and minor errors hinder the verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. For example, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^(1-α) D2 ||f||_2^2. The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq. (31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 should specify  ||ξ||^2 ≥. Given the central role of the theoretical results in this paper, a careful revision of the supplementary material is strongly recommended.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Principled framework for regularization and parameter selection.
*   Effective use of cross-validation in an unsupervised setting.
*   Theoretical analysis providing convergence guarantees.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating competitive performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Convergence rate in Theorem 1 appears slow; exploration of stronger assumptions for polynomial rates is needed.
*   Lack of discussion regarding the strong assumptions required for density ratio estimation (e.g., smoothness of q/p).
*   Supplementary material contains numerous errors hindering verification of theoretical results.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The proposed framework is theoretically sound and demonstrates practical benefits through effective parameter selection via cross-validation. Addressing the concerns regarding the convergence rate, the discussion of assumptions, and the correction of errors in the supplementary material would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a compelling addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
