This paper proposes a new method for estimating the density ratio p/q. Several methods have already been proposed for this problem. The novelty of this paper lies in the fact that the authors have reformulated the method for estimating the density ratio as a Fredholm integral equation.

**Strong points of the paper:**

The paper is well-written; I enjoyed reading it. The density ratio estimation problem is important and interesting.

**Weak points, comments, and questions of the paper:**

(i) Some details are missing in the paper. For example, why is
\int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t)? All such statements require a proof (in the paper or supplementary materials) or a reference, even if they are easy to prove.
(ii) As a special case (q=1), this method can also be used for density estimation. It is interesting that this method does not require bandwidth to converge to zero. Standard KDE density estimation is not compatible in that case. It would be good to see further discussion on this topic, so that readers can clearly see that the bandwidth parameters of the paper have a different role from KDE.
(iii) I would suggest adding a few sentences about Tikhonov regularization. Most members of the NIPS community may not be familiar with it.
(iv) All proofs are in the supplementary materials. I have not checked whether they are correct.
(v) It would be good to read a few sentences about why a simple plug-in method, which separately estimates the density p and q, is worse than the method proposed in this paper.
(vi) It is also not clear how this method works compared to other methods. For example, if I remember correctly [12], a minimax bound also arises. Assumptions 2 and 4 provide an upper bound on the rate of convergence, but it is not clear how they deviate from the optimal minimax rate.
(vii) Please provide references for the datasets used in the paper (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).
(viii) I missed the demonstration of how the proposed method would work in toy problems for specific and known p and q values. For example, for 1-dimensional p and q, a plot of the actual p/q and the estimated p/q can help readers evaluate the quality of the estimation.

This is a nice paper on density ratio estimation. It is not clear to me how this method will work compared to other algorithms (such as [12]), and I missed the demonstration of how this method would work in toy problems, such as using a simple 1-dimensional p and q density.
