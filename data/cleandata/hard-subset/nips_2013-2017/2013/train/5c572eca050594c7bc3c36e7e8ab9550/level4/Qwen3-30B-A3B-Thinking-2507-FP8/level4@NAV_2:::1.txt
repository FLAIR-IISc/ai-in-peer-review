

The paper introduces a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), provides a principled framework that connects density ratio estimation to classical operator theory and spectral analysis. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for the Gaussian kernel in Euclidean spaces and on sub-manifolds, and propose a novel unsupervised model selection technique called CD-CV. The work builds upon and extends previous contributions to density ratio estimation, particularly relating to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10], while offering a more flexible framework with explicit theoretical analysis. The connection to classical analysis is particularly noteworthy as it provides a different perspective from the more common machine learning approaches to this problem.

Strengths of the paper include: (1) The clear and well-organized presentation of a technically sound approach that bridges classical operator theory with modern machine learning; (2) The comprehensive theoretical analysis providing error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings, which is rare for this class of problems; (3) The development of CD-CV, a completely unsupervised model selection method that addresses a significant practical challenge in density ratio estimation; (4) The detailed experimental evaluation comparing FIRE with several state-of-the-art methods across multiple datasets and tasks.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement: (i) The paper states that ∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) without providing a proof or sufficient reference, which is a critical approximation for the Type II formulation. The authors should provide a reference or proof (in the main paper or supplementary material) to support this claim, as it is central to their approach. (ii) The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the bandwidth parameter t in their method differs from the bandwidth parameter in standard kernel density estimation (KDE). Specifically, the paper claims that their approach doesn't require t to converge to zero for consistency, which contrasts with KDE, but this distinction needs clarification to help readers understand the different roles of parameters in the two approaches. (iii) While the paper mentions Tikhonov regularization, it would be helpful to include a brief explanation of this technique for readers who may not be familiar with it, as it is a key component of their framework. (iv) The theoretical results in Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on convergence rates, but it would be valuable to compare these rates with the minimax optimal rates established in [14] to better understand the theoretical performance of the method. (v) The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a simple 1-dimensional toy example demonstrating the density ratio estimation with known p and q, where the true ratio and estimated ratio can be visualized. This would help readers assess the quality of the estimation in a concrete setting. (vi) The paper references datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) without providing standard references for these benchmark datasets, which would be helpful for readers to locate the data sources. (vii) While the paper compares FIRE with several methods, a more detailed discussion of why a naive plug-in approach (estimating p and q separately) would perform worse than the proposed method would provide additional context for the value of their approach.
