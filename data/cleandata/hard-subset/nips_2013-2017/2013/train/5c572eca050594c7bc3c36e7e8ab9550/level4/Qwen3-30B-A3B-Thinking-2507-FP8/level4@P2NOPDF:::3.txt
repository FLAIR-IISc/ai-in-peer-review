

The authors propose a method for estimating the ratio of two probability density functions using kernel-based approaches and Fredholm integral equations, framing the problem as an inverse problem. However, the theoretical foundation of this approach requires significant clarification to establish rigor.  

A critical gap lies in the unspecified dominating measure $\nu$. The paper fails to identify $\nu$ (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure, or another reference measure), which is essential for defining densities and ensuring the mathematical validity of the ratio. In measure-theoretic terms, the existence of a $\nu$-density for a probability measure $P$ does not guarantee such densities for all nearby measures under the total variation topology—a standard result in probability theory. Consequently, the authors' framework risks being ill-posed if $\nu$ is not explicitly chosen and justified.  

Furthermore, the paper does not address sets of $\nu$-measure zero, which are pivotal in density ratio estimation. For instance, if the ratio $p(x)/q(x)$ is undefined on a $\nu$-null set (e.g., where $q(x)=0$), the solution to the integral equation may lack uniqueness or fail to represent the true ratio almost everywhere. The absence of a rigorous treatment of these sets undermines the claimed theoretical guarantees.  

The work also omits explicit assumptions necessary for the problem to be well-defined. Key questions remain unanswered:  
1. Is the domain $\mathcal{X}$ restricted to $\mathbb{R}^d$, or is it a general topological space?  
2. Are the densities $p$ and $q$ required to be strictly positive everywhere on $\mathcal{X}$? If not, the ratio may be undefined on subsets of $\mathcal{X}$, and the integral equation approach may not recover the ratio on such sets.  
3. What regularity conditions (e.g., continuity, boundedness) are imposed on $p$ and $q$? Without these, the Fredholm equation may not admit a solution.  

For example, if $\mathcal{X}$ is $\mathbb{R}^d$ and $q$ vanishes on a non-empty open set, the ratio $p/q$ is undefined there, yet the paper does not acknowledge this limitation. Similarly, if $\mathcal{X}$ is discrete (e.g., a countable space), the choice of $\nu$ as counting measure would be natural, but the paper does not specify this. The lack of clarity on these foundational aspects prevents a rigorous assessment of the method’s applicability and correctness.  

In machine learning contexts, density ratio estimation typically assumes a common dominating measure (e.g., Lebesgue for continuous data, counting for discrete), but the authors provide no justification for their choice or its implications. Without this, the theoretical results cannot be generalized to standard ML settings, and the claimed "rigorous" solution appears incomplete. The paper’s failure to state assumptions explicitly renders the analysis incomplete, as the validity of the approach hinges entirely on these unstated conditions.  

To strengthen the contribution, the authors should:  
- Explicitly define $\nu$ and justify its choice for the problem context.  
- Specify the domain $\mathcal{X}$ and required properties of $p$ and $q$ (e.g., strict positivity, continuity).  
- Address the treatment of $\nu$-null sets and their impact on the ratio’s definition.  
- Clarify whether the solution holds almost everywhere with respect to $\nu$.  

Without these clarifications, the theoretical claims lack grounding, and the method’s practical utility remains questionable. The current draft does not sufficiently resolve the inherent challenges of density ratio estimation in measure-theoretic terms, particularly regarding the role of the dominating measure and the necessity of well-defined densities. The work would benefit from a more precise mathematical foundation before its application to machine learning problems can be reasonably evaluated.
