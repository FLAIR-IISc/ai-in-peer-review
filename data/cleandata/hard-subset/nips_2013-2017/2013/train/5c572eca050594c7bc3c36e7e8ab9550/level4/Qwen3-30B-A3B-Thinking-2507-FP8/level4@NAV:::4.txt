

The authors employ two equalities to derive estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative \(f = p/q\):  
(i) \(\mathbb{E}_p[k(x,\cdot)f] = \mathbb{E}_q[k(x,\cdot)]\) and (ii) \(\mathbb{E}_p[k(x,\cdot)f] \approx q(x)\).  
Integral operators are replaced by empirical counterparts. The family \(\{k(x,\cdot)\}\) functions as a set of test functions minimizing the discrepancy between left- and right-hand sides. This choice enables standard kernel methods for optimization.  
Convergence guarantees are provided for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, with convergence rates dependent on the underlying dimension—consistent with density estimation expectations.  
The work is well-executed and timely.  

Key comments:  
- For convergence rates: Optimizing over \(\{k(x,\cdot)\}\) ensures convergence to the ratio. For (ii), bandwidth dependence makes this intuitive; a brief justification for (i) would strengthen the argument.  
- The optimization on page 2 uses \(\mathcal{L}_{2,p}\), while Equation (9) combines \(\mathcal{L}_{2,p}\) and \(\mathcal{L}_{2,q}\). Motivation for this cost function choice is needed, ideally linking it to specific applications.  
- Regarding applications: Importance sampling uses \(q/p\) to transition from \(\mathbb{E}_q\) to \(\mathbb{E}_p\). Since the method estimates \(\mathbb{E}_p\) and \(\mathbb{E}_q\) to construct the empirical cost, why not use \(\mathbb{E}_q\) directly? Clarify the motivation—e.g., whether sampling from \(\mathbb{E}_q\) is infeasible (as noted on p.5).  
- The regularizer enforces smoothness on \(q/p\), yet Theorem 1 assumes smoothness on \(q\) and \(p\). Discuss the relationship between smoothness of \(q,p\) and their ratio (e.g., whether \(q,p\) smooth \(\implies\) ratio smooth, or vice versa).  
- Elaborate on the dimension dependence of rates: For which dimensions is the method practically useful?  
- Justify the choice of test function family \(\mathcal{U}\).  
- Address the broader question: Given the challenge of defining generic cost functions in covariate shift settings, does the approach with \(\{k(x,\cdot)\}\) represent the "right" methodology? Note similarities to [1] (e.g., "Smooth Operators"), though the test-function approach for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is preferable.  

This is a strong, timely contribution with rigorous convergence analysis and experimental validation.
