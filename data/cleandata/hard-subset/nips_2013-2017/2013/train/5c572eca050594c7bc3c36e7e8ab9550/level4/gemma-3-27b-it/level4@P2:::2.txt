This paper introduces a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as an inverse problem based on Fredholm integral equations. The authors propose two algorithms, termed FIRE, leveraging regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. One approach (Type I) directly estimates the density ratio, while the other (Type II) relies on a localized kernel and is suitable when the target density doesn’t need to be explicitly known. The paper provides theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, and demonstrates promising experimental results, including a novel unsupervised model selection method, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).

The core strength of this work lies in its principled formulation and the ability to apply cross-validation for parameter selection – a significant advantage over existing methods. While cross-validation is standard in supervised learning, effective unsupervised parameter tuning remains a challenge. This paper addresses this gap specifically within the context of density ratio estimation by cleverly utilizing the L2 norm formulation.  The observation that objective functions based on RKHS norms (like those used in Kernel Mean Matching, KMM) are less suitable for cross-validation due to their dependence on the kernel is insightful and well-articulated.

However, the derived convergence rates in Theorem 1 appear somewhat slow due to the (-t/log λ) factor. In many kernel method analyses, polynomial rates with respect to sample size are achievable with fixed kernel width *t*. It would strengthen the paper to explore whether stronger assumptions—for example, on the smoothness of q/p or the spectral properties of the kernel operator, as explored in Caponnetto and De Vito (2007)—could yield such polynomial rates.  Investigating this would highlight the trade-offs between assumptions and achievable performance.

A critical point often overlooked in density ratio estimation is the inherent assumption that q/p belongs to a well-behaved function class, such as a Sobolev space.  This assumption can be quite strong, requiring prior knowledge of the ratio or the tails of the underlying distributions *p* and *q*.  Consequently, the converse ratio, p/q, may exhibit poor behavior. A discussion of this limitation would provide a more nuanced understanding of the applicability of this approach.

Finally, while not critical for acceptance, the supplementary material requires substantial improvement. Numerous typos and minor errors hinder verification of the theoretical results. For example, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^(1-α) D2 ||f||_2^2. The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq.(31) is correct. The inequality domain in the proof of Lemma 5 at line 1147 is also inaccurate (should be  ||ξ||^2 ≥ ). Given the importance of the theoretical contributions, a careful revision of the supplementary material is strongly recommended.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem.
*   Principled approach leveraging RKHS and regularization techniques.
*   Development of a practical and effective unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV).
*   Theoretical analysis providing concentration bounds and convergence rates.
*   Demonstrated competitive performance on benchmark datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Convergence rates in Theorem 1 are potentially slow.
*   Lack of discussion regarding the strong assumptions required for the smoothness of q/p.
*   Numerous errors in the supplementary material impede verification of theoretical results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The proposed framework is theoretically sound, and the practical benefits of CD-CV are significant.  While the convergence rates could be improved and the supplementary material requires attention, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the concerns raised regarding the supplementary material and potentially exploring stronger assumptions to achieve faster convergence rates.
