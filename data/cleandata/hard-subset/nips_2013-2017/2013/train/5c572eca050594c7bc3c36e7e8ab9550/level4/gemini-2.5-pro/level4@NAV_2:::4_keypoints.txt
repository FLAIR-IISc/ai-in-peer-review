- Problem: Estimates density ratio q/p, useful for importance sampling and covariate shift correction.
- Main idea: Reformulates density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, yielding two settings (Type I and Type II).
- Method: Solves the ill-posed inverse problems via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, producing the FIRE family of algorithms.
- Theory: Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates that depend on data-manifold dimensionality under standard smoothness and boundedness assumptions.
- Model selection: Introduces an unsupervised Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) procedure for tuning.
- Empirical results: Demonstrates strong performance against LSIF and a kernel density-based baseline.
- Strength — Originality: Elegant, principled framing connecting density-ratio estimation to classical inverse problems and operator theory.
- Strength — Theoretical rigor: Solid analysis with guarantees that increase confidence in the method’s behavior.
- Strength — Practicality: Algorithms are simple, explicit, kernel-friendly, and CD-CV addresses a key practical issue.
- Strength — Clarity and execution: Well-written paper with clear derivations and comprehensive experiments.
- Weakness — Intuition (Type I): Need a short intuitive argument why test functions {k(x,·)} suffice for convergence in Type I, analogous to Type II intuition.
- Weakness — Loss motivation: More explanation linking L2,p and combined γL2,p+(1-γ)L2,q losses to downstream tasks and when to prefer each.
- Weakness — Setup motivation: Clarify scenarios motivating estimating q/p from both p and q samples (e.g., q known up to constant or q sampling costly) versus directly using q samples for Eq[h].
- Weakness — Smoothness assumptions: Discuss relation between smoothness enforced by RKHS regularizer on q/p and assumed smoothness of p and q (and p bounded away from zero).
- Weakness — Dimensional dependence: Briefly discuss practical implications of convergence rates’ dependence on dimension and feasible dimensional ranges for effectiveness.
- Weakness — CD-CV test functions: Motivate choice of random linear functions and half-space indicators and characterize desirable properties for the test-function family.
- Weakness — Related work: Situate the approach within broader integral-operator-based distribution-learning literature (e.g., Smooth Operators) and clarify distinctions.
