This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, where one is a probability density, motivated by applications in importance sampling and covariate shift. The core idea is to reformulate this ratio estimation as an inverse problem using a Fredholm integral equation, leveraging tools from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors propose a family of algorithms, FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), and provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in various settings. They also introduce a model selection technique, CD-CV, and demonstrate performance through experiments.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in density ratio estimation and transfer learning, referencing key papers like [6, 9, 10, 19, 22, 27]. The connection to Fredholm integral equations and operator theory provides a novel perspective, potentially offering a more principled framework for analysis and algorithm design. The theoretical results, particularly convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, are a significant contribution. The unsupervised model selection method CD-CV is also a valuable addition, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While acknowledging the general difficulty of density estimation, the authors do not explicitly address the fundamental mathematical challenges related to the existence of densities and the choice of dominating measure (e.g., Lebesgue measure) underlying their analysis. A clearer statement of assumptions regarding the domain (e.g., $R^d$ or a more general topological space) and the properties of the density *p* (e.g., positivity) would strengthen the rigor. The lack of discussion around these foundational issues detracts from the clarity of the technical contribution. 

The experimental section is a bit dense and the connections between the different experiments (parameter selection with different test functions, regression, classification) could be made clearer. While comparisons to existing methods like TIKDE and LSIF are included, a more in-depth discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results would be beneficial.  The paper would also benefit from a more concise presentation of the mathematical derivations, potentially moving some of the detailed proofs to the supplementary material.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Formulation:** Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem offers a fresh perspective and leverages tools from classical analysis.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The provided concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels are valuable contributions.
*   **Unsupervised Model Selection:** CD-CV provides a practical approach to parameter selection in unsupervised settings.
*   **Clear Connections to Related Work:** The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on density ratio estimation and transfer learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Discussion on Density Existence:** The paper does not adequately address the fundamental mathematical challenges of working with densities and the underlying dominating measure.
*   **Assumptions Not Explicitly Stated:** Key assumptions about the domain and properties of the densities are not clearly defined.
*   **Dense Experimental Section:** The experimental results could be presented more clearly and with a greater focus on practical implications.
*   **Mathematical Detail:**  Some mathematical derivations could be streamlined.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical analysis is a significant contribution to the field.
*   The CD-CV method offers a practical solution for unsupervised model selection.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of discussion surrounding fundamental mathematical issues related to density estimation weakens the rigor of the work.
*   The experimental section could be improved for clarity and impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field, but some aspects of the technical presentation and foundational discussion need improvement.  I believe the paper is a strong submission, but would recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the points raised above, specifically clarifying the assumptions and discussing the limitations of their approach regarding the existence of densities. I would rate this a **weak accept**.
