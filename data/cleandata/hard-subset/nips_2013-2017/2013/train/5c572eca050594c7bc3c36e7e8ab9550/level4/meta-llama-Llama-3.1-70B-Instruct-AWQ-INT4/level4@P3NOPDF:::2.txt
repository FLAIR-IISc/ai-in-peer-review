This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio of two probabilities. The first method leverages the equation of importance sampling, formulating it as a regularization problem that incorporates the L^2 norm of the deviation from the equality and a regularization term based on a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), resulting in a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method employs a kernel where the locality parameter must approach zero for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem, yielding a Type II Fredholm equation. The authors provide theoretical results on convergence and demonstrate the practical performance of their methods through experimental evaluations.

While the proposed methods share similarities with existing approaches, such as kernel mean matching (KMM), a significant advantage of the proposed methods is the use of the L^2 formulation, which enables the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. Unlike supervised learning, where CV is a dominant method for choosing parameters, there is a lack of general methods for unsupervised learning. The authors address this issue in density ratio estimation by cleverly utilizing the L^2 norm, allowing for the effective application of CV.

It is worth noting that the objective function derived from RKHS, as seen in KMM, is not suitable for kernel selection via CV, as the values of the objective function depend on the kernel, rendering them incomparable. In contrast, the proposed L^2 formulation provides a more suitable framework for CV-based parameter selection.

The authors' theoretical analysis, as presented in Theorem 1, reveals a slow convergence rate due to the (-t/log λ) factor, given a fixed t. In many kernel method literatures, a polynomial rate with respect to the sample size can be derived after fixing the rate of λ. The question arises whether reasonable additional assumptions can lead to a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) make assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to derive the optimal rate for kernel ridge regression. It is plausible that stronger assumptions on q/p could also yield a polynomial rate. A discussion on the potential for achieving a polynomial rate under stronger assumptions with fixed t would be a valuable addition to the paper.

In density ratio estimation, it is common to assume that q/p belongs to a specific function class, such as Sobolev. However, this assumption is quite strong, requiring prior knowledge on the ratio or tails of p and q. Furthermore, under this assumption, the opposite ratio p/q often exhibits poor behavior. It is essential to acknowledge and discuss this limitation of density ratio estimation in the paper.

Although not a requirement, the supplementary materials would benefit from improvement. Given the significance of the convergence results to the paper's main contributions, it is crucial for the authors to revise the supplementary material to ensure accuracy and clarity.
