This paper proposes two new methods for estimating the density ratio between two distributions. The first leverages the importance-sampling identity and casts it as a regularized problem that minimizes the L^2 residual of the equality with an RKHS penalty (a Type-I Fredholm equation). The second uses a kernel with a locality parameter that must shrink to zero for consistency, and it is also formulated via regularization (a Type-II Fredholm equation). The paper provides convergence results and demonstrates empirical performance.

Although the approach is related to existing methods such as KMM, a key advantage is that the L^2 formulation enables principled cross-validation (CV) for tuning algorithmic parameters. Unlike in supervised learning, where CV is standard, there is no generally accepted parameter-selection method for unsupervised problems. This paper addresses that gap for density-ratio estimation by exploiting the L^2 objective.

It would also be helpful to note explicitly that RKHS-mismatch objectives such as in KMM are not suitable for CV-based kernel selection: because the objective value depends on the kernel, objectives across kernels are not comparable.

Regarding theory, Theorem 1 yields a slow rate with fixed t due to the (-t / log λ) factor. In much of the kernel literature, one can obtain polynomial rates in the sample size with fixed t by choosing λ appropriately. Are there reasonable additional assumptions under which the proposed estimator achieves polynomial rates? For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) assume spectral conditions on the operator K_{p,t} to derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression. I suspect that stronger regularity/source assumptions on q/p would also yield polynomial rates. It would strengthen the paper to discuss such polynomial rates under stronger assumptions with fixed t.

More broadly, density-ratio estimation typically assumes that q/p belongs to a regular function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This is a strong assumption that implicitly requires knowledge about the ratio or about the tail behavior of p and q. Moreover, when q/p is well behaved under such assumptions, the inverse ratio p/q often behaves poorly. Please include a discussion of this limitation of density-ratio methods.

While not strictly required, the supplementary material needs improvement. Numerous typos and minor errors make it difficult to verify the main paper’s theoretical claims. For example, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t / -log λ)^2 + λ^{1−α} D2 ||f||_2^2. The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, whereas Eq. (31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be ||ξ||^2 ≥. Since the convergence theory is a principal contribution of the paper, I strongly recommend revising the supplement for accuracy and clarity.

Reference:
Caponnetto A., De Vito E. Optimal Rates for Regularized Least-Squares Algorithm. Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 7:331–368 (2007).

Overall, this is a promising approach to density-ratio estimation, with the notable strength that cross-validation can be used effectively to select parameters.
