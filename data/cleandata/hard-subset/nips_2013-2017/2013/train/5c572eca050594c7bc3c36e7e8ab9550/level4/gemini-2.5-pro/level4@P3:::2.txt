This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the density ratio q/p, a fundamental problem in importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and other areas of machine learning. The core idea is to reframe the estimation problem as a classical Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This is achieved by leveraging the integral operator associated with a kernel function. The authors propose two distinct formulations: a Type I problem, derived from the importance sampling identity, which is suitable when samples from both densities p and q are available; and a Type II problem, based on the property of delta-like kernels, which applies when values of q are known at points sampled from p. Both formulations lead to Tikhonov regularization problems within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), resulting in a family of simple and efficient algorithms named FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator).

The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, establishing concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators using Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. A key practical contribution is a fully unsupervised model selection procedure called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to create a validation metric. The effectiveness of the FIRE algorithms and the CD-CV method is demonstrated through experiments on several datasets, comparing favorably to existing methods like LSIF and a baseline kernel density-based approach.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and well-motivated. It connects a modern machine learning problem to a classical area of analysis, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms and their analysis.

2.  **Unsupervised Model Selection:** A major strength of this work is the proposed CD-CV method for hyperparameter selection (e.g., kernel width and regularization parameter). Choosing hyperparameters is a notoriously difficult problem in unsupervised learning. The paper's L2-based formulation allows for a clever and effective cross-validation scheme that does not require any labeled data. This is a significant practical advantage over methods where parameter tuning is less straightforward.

3.  **Flexibility and Simplicity:** The FIRE framework is flexible, accommodating different loss functions (e.g., L2,p, L2,q, or their combination) and regularization norms. The resulting algorithms are simple to implement, leading to closed-form solutions involving standard kernel matrix operations.

4.  **Thorough Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical treatment, deriving convergence rates for their estimators. This analysis lends strong support to the proposed methodology.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Convergence Rates:** The derived convergence rates in Theorem 1 appear to be slow for a fixed kernel width `t`. In much of the kernel methods literature, it is common to achieve polynomial rates in the sample size `n` under additional assumptions. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether faster, polynomial rates can be obtained under stronger, yet reasonable, assumptions on the smoothness of the target ratio `q/p` or on the spectral decay of the integral operator `Kp`. An analysis similar to that in Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) for kernel ridge regression could provide valuable insights here.

2.  **Assumptions on the Density Ratio:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the true ratio `q/p` is sufficiently smooth (e.g., belongs to a Sobolev space). This is a strong assumption that has important practical implications, as it constrains the relationship between the tails of the distributions `p` and `q`. The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation. For instance, if `q/p` is well-behaved, the inverse ratio `p/q` may not be, which could be problematic in applications where both ratios are of interest. A brief discussion of this inherent challenge in density ratio estimation would add valuable context.

3.  **Clarity of Supplementary Material:** The supplementary material is essential for verifying the paper's theoretical claims, but in its current state, it contains numerous typos and minor errors that hinder comprehension and verification. For example:
    *   In Lemma 5, `W2^2` appears to be a typo for `Ws^2`, and the bound presented seems incorrect.
    *   The expressions at lines 675 and 686 seem inconsistent with the correct form in Eq. (31).
    *   In the proof of Lemma 5 (line 1147), the domain of integration seems to be specified incorrectly.
    Given that the theoretical results are a cornerstone of this paper, I strongly advise the authors to carefully revise and proofread the supplementary material to ensure its clarity and correctness.

4.  **Comparison with KMM:** To further emphasize the novelty and utility of the proposed CV method, it would be beneficial to add a remark clarifying why objective functions based directly on the RKHS norm, such as in KMM, are not well-suited for kernel selection via cross-validation. The key issue is that the scale of the RKHS norm is kernel-dependent, making objective values non-comparable across different kernel choices. Explicitly stating this would better motivate the L2-based approach.

**Summary:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field. The proposed framework for density ratio estimation is novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful. The development of an effective unsupervised cross-validation procedure is a particularly significant achievement. While there is room for improvement in the discussion of theoretical rates and the presentation of the supplementary material, these weaknesses do not detract from the overall merit of the work. The paper is well-written, the ideas are clearly communicated, and the experimental results are encouraging. It advances the state of the art and is likely to be influential.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem.
*   A major practical contribution in the form of an unsupervised cross-validation method (CD-CV).
*   Thorough theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
*   Simple, flexible, and efficient algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The supplementary material contains errors that make verification of the theory difficult.
*   The discussion of the derived convergence rates and the underlying assumptions could be more complete.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are significantly stronger. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the issues raised, particularly the clarity and correctness of the supplementary material.
