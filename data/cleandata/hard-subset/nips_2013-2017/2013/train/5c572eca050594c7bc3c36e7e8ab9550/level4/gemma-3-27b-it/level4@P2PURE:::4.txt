The authors derive two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative f = p/q based on the equalities (i) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] = E<sub>q</sub>[k(x,.)] and (ii) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] ≈ q(x). They replace the integral operators with empirical counterparts, framing {k(x,.)} as a set of test functions chosen to minimize the difference between the two sides of these equations. A key advantage of selecting {k(x,.)} as the test function family is the applicability of standard kernel methods for optimization. 

Convergence guarantees are established for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, with convergence rates dependent on the dimensionality of the underlying space – a predictable characteristic of density estimation. Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution.

However, a few points could be clarified:

- Regarding the convergence rates: optimizing over {k(x,.)} is sufficient for convergence to the ratio. While this is intuitive for (ii) given the bandwidth dependence, a brief, intuitive explanation of why this holds for (i) would be helpful.
- The optimization on page 2 utilizes the L<sub>2,p</sub> norm, while equation (9) employs a combination of L<sub>2,p</sub> and L<sub>2,q</sub>. Some justification for this cost function choice would be beneficial, particularly linking it to the specific tasks or applications it is intended to serve.
- Concerning applications, importance sampling – where q/p is relevant for transitioning from E<sub>q</sub> to E<sub>p</sub> – is mentioned as an example. Since the method estimates both E<sub>p</sub> and E<sub>q</sub> to construct the empirical cost function, it's unclear why not directly utilize the E<sub>q</sub> estimate. A justification for estimating Ep(q/p) rather than directly using Eq would be appreciated, providing further motivation for the overall setup. The paper briefly notes the difficulty of sampling from E<sub>q</sub> on page 5; is this the primary application scenario?
- The regularizer enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p, while the theorem's assumptions later introduce smoothness constraints on q and p – a natural requirement. It is worth exploring the relationship between smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of their ratio. While multiple q and p can produce the same ratio, potentially exhibiting irregularity, a connection such as “smooth and bounded q and p imply a smooth ratio” or “a smooth ratio implies the existence of smooth q and p that produce it” could be insightful.
- Discussing the dimension dependence of the convergence rates and identifying the problem dimensionality for which the method remains effective would be beneficial.
- What motivates the specific choice of the test function family U?
- A broader question: a major challenge in covariate shift and related settings is defining a suitable cost function. Your approach leverages expectation operators and employs various test functions to extract the underlying densities. Do you believe the selection of {k(x,.)} represents an optimal strategy in this context? There are similarities to the work in [1], which also explores transformations to estimate quantities of interest. While your approach using test functions to address the Radon-Nikodym derivative is more elegant, the underlying methodology shares conceptual similarities.
[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor

In conclusion, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem with a theoretical analysis and experimental validation.
