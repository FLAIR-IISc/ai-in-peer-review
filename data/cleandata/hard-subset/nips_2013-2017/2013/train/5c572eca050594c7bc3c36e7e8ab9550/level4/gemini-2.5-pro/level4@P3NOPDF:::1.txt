This paper introduces a novel method for estimating the density ratio p(x)/q(x). The core contribution is the reformulation of this problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using Tikhonov regularization.

### Strengths

*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and a pleasure to read.
*   The work addresses the important and widely applicable problem of density ratio estimation.
*   The proposed approach of casting the problem as an integral equation is elegant and theoretically principled.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper presents a promising direction, there are several areas where it could be substantially improved in terms of clarity, theoretical context, and experimental validation.

**1. Clarity and Justification:**

*   **Missing Derivations:** Several key statements are presented without sufficient justification. For example, the assertion that `∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)` is central to the formulation but is not proven. Even if straightforward, such statements require either a brief proof (in the appendix) or a citation to a standard result.
*   **Explanation of Tikhonov Regularization:** A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would be beneficial. While familiar to specialists in inverse problems, a concise overview would improve the paper's accessibility to the broader machine learning community at NIPS.
*   **Comparison to a Naive Baseline:** The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of why a direct estimation of the ratio is preferable to a naive "plug-in" approach (i.e., separately estimating p and q via density estimation and then taking their ratio). This comparison is crucial for motivating the proposed method over simpler, more intuitive baselines.

**2. Theoretical Contributions:**

*   **Connection to Density Estimation:** The paper notes the connection to density estimation when q is uniform. A particularly interesting aspect is that the bandwidth parameter does not need to converge to zero, unlike in standard kernel density estimation (KDE) where this is a requirement for consistency. Expanding on this point would be valuable. A more detailed discussion could clarify the distinct roles of the bandwidth parameters in this framework versus traditional KDE, highlighting why standard consistency arguments do not apply and why this method succeeds.
*   **Comparison to Optimal Rates:** The analysis provides convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4. However, the paper does not compare these upper bounds to known minimax lower bounds for this problem, such as those discussed in reference [12]. Situating the theoretical results within the context of established optimal rates would provide a clearer picture of the method's theoretical performance and its potential for optimality.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**

*   **Lack of Illustrative Toy Problem:** The experimental evaluation could be significantly enhanced by including a simple, illustrative toy problem. For instance, using known one-dimensional densities for p and q, a plot showing the true ratio p(x)/q(x) alongside the estimated ratio would provide a clear and intuitive assessment of the method's performance. Such a demonstration would build reader confidence and provide valuable insight into the method's behavior in a controlled setting.
*   **Dataset References:** For reproducibility, please provide citations or sources for the datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

**4. Proofs:**

*   I acknowledge that the proofs are provided in the supplementary material; due to time constraints, I have not verified their correctness in detail.

### Conclusion

In summary, this is a well-written paper that presents an elegant and interesting new approach to density ratio estimation. However, its current form leaves open important questions regarding its practical and theoretical comparison to existing methods. The lack of a simple illustrative example and a comparison to a naive baseline makes it difficult to fully appreciate the method's advantages. Similarly, a comparison of the derived convergence rates to minimax optimal rates is needed to contextualize the theoretical contribution. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify its contributions to the field.
