- Problem & contribution: Proposes estimating density ratio r(x)=q(x)/p(x) by casting it as a Fredholm integral equation and solving via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.

- Type I estimator: For two-sample setting, uses Kp r = Kq 1 and solves an empirical regularized L2 problem with representer-theorem closed-form linear-system solutions (FIRE).

- Type II estimator: For settings with p samples and evaluable q, approximates δ-kernels to obtain Kt,p r ≈ q and a regularized least-squares estimator.

- Theoretical analysis: Provides spectral/operator-based concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and smooth compact manifolds.

- Model selection: Introduces CD-CV, an unsupervised cross-density cross-validation that evaluates agreement of importance-weighted expectations using random test functions.

- Experiments: Compares FIRE to LSIF, KMM (selectively), and thresholded inverse KDE, showing competitive performance and computational simplicity.

- Relation to prior work: Connects to KMM, LSIF/KLIEP, and operator-theoretic kernel methods, clarifying similarities and differences.

- Strength — formulation: The Fredholm inverse-problem view is elegant and unifies several existing techniques under principled regularization.

- Strength — algorithms: FIRE algorithms are simple, implementable, yield out-of-sample predictions, and reduce to well-structured linear systems.

- Strength — theory: Nonasymptotic error bounds and manifold results strengthen the theoretical contribution.

- Strength — model selection: CD-CV is a practical unsupervised selection criterion useful when labeled validation is unavailable.

- Strength — related work discussion: Paper offers useful, clarifying discussion of connections to KMM and LSIF.

- Weakness — measure-theoretic clarity: The paper should state the dominating measure ν and make absolute continuity assumptions explicit early.

- Weakness — support/positivity: Must define support and positivity assumptions (p>0 where q>0) and clarify estimator behavior where p is small.

- Weakness — collect assumptions: Recommend collecting domain, smoothness, and boundedness assumptions in a single early subsection and echoing them in algorithm sections.

- Weakness — kernel notation: Clarify and unify kernel notation (k vs kH), discuss bias/conditioning implications, and guide choosing t relative to n for Type II.

- Weakness — scalability: Methods require solving n×n systems; include complexity/memory costs and discuss Nyström or random feature scaling strategies.

- Weakness — CD-CV evaluation: Need ablations showing CD-CV’s correlation with downstream task loss and sensitivity to test function family and number F; compare to IWCV.

- Weakness — experimental scope: Benchmarks are limited; add real domain adaptation datasets, higher-dimensional synthetic tests, and broader comparisons to KMM and recent estimators.

- Weakness — low-density regions: Discuss regularization or constraints (positivity/upper bounds) to handle ratio blow-ups where p is small.

- Quality comment: Theory is solid but intricate; provide recommended scaling rules for (t,λ) with n and clarify dependence of constants on manifold geometry.

- Clarity suggestions: Tighten notation, move key assumptions earlier, fix minor editorial issues, and add pseudocode with inputs/outputs and complexity for each FIRE variant.

- Originality: The Fredholm inverse-problem framing and the Type II setting and manifold analysis appear novel and insightful despite related prior work.

- Significance: FIRE is a practical, theoretically-backed addition to density-ratio estimation with increased impact pending clearer assumptions and broader experiments.

- Acceptance arguments: Elegant unifying formulation, simple out-of-sample algorithms, nonasymptotic bounds including manifold cases, and practical unsupervised model selection.

- Rejection arguments: Early lack of measure-theoretic clarity, limited experimental breadth and scaling studies, and insufficient tuning/low-density guidance.

- Overall evaluation: Strong and promising submission that needs clearer assumptions, expanded experiments, and more tuning/scalability guidance to be a solid NIPS paper.
