Review- The paper introduces a new approach for estimating the density ratio p/q. While several methods exist for this task, the key novelty here is casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

Strong points of the paper:
The manuscript is clearly written; I found it enjoyable to read. The density ratio estimation problem is both significant and engaging.

Weak points of the paper, comments, and questions:
(i) Some technical details are missing. For instance, why does
∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t)?
Please provide either a proof (in the main text or supplementary) or a citation for statements of this sort, even if they are straightforward.

(ii) As a special case (q = 1), the method yields a density estimator. It is noteworthy that this framework does not require the bandwidth to vanish, whereas standard KDE is inconsistent under a fixed bandwidth. A clearer discussion would help readers understand that the bandwidths here play roles different from those in KDE.

(iii) I suggest adding a brief overview of Tikhonov regularization, as many in the NIPS community may not be familiar with it.

(iv) All proofs are deferred to the supplement. I have not verified their correctness.

(v) Please include a discussion of why a naive plug-in strategy—estimating p and q separately and taking their ratio—is inferior to the proposed method.

(vi) It is not clear how this method compares with existing approaches. For example, if memory serves, [12] also derives minimax bounds. Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on convergence rates, but it is unclear how close these are to optimal minimax rates.

(vii) Please add references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) I missed a toy demonstration with known p and q. For instance, in one dimension, plotting the true p/q alongside the estimate would help readers gauge estimation quality.

Overall, this is a nice paper on density ratio estimation. However, I remain uncertain about its performance relative to competing methods (e.g., [12]), and I would have liked to see a toy example, such as simple one-dimensional p and q, to illustrate the approach.
