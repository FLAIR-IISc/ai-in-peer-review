

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, leading to a principled regularization approach called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The authors connect density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, providing a fresh perspective that bridges operator theory and kernel methods in machine learning. This formulation naturally accommodates both Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when values of q are known at p-sampled points) settings, with the Type I approach being particularly relevant for covariate shift scenarios.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a theoretical framework that establishes density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem; (2) a flexible FIRE algorithm family within the kernel learning framework; (3) detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds; and (4) a novel unsupervised model selection technique called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) that addresses a significant challenge in unsupervised learning.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings across different domains (Euclidean space, sub-manifolds). While the theoretical results are impressive, I have one concern: the convergence rate in Theorem 1 (which contains a (-t/log λ) factor) appears slow with fixed t. In kernel methods literature, polynomial rates with respect to sample size are typically achievable with fixed t by making stronger assumptions about the operator spectrum. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression under spectral assumptions. It would be valuable if the authors could discuss whether similar assumptions on q/p would yield polynomial rates for their estimator, as this would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.

The paper's most compelling strength is its CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection. Unlike supervised learning where cross-validation is standard practice, unsupervised learning lacks general parameter selection methods. The authors cleverly leverage the L² formulation to develop CD-CV, which is a significant contribution to the field. This addresses a critical practical challenge in density ratio estimation and distinguishes the paper from related work like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), where the RKHS-based objective function is not comparable across different kernels, making cross-validation inapplicable.

The paper compares FIRE against several baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) on multiple datasets (USPS, CPUsmall, Kin8nm) across classification and regression tasks. The results consistently demonstrate FIRE's superior performance, particularly in computational efficiency and robustness to parameter selection. The authors appropriately acknowledge that while their approach requires the assumption that q/p belongs to a nice function class (e.g., Sobolev space), this is standard in density ratio estimation literature. However, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitation that this assumption often requires knowledge about the tails of p and q, which may not be available in practice.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear exposition of the mathematical framework and algorithms. The connection to previous work is thorough, particularly the comparison with KMM (which can be viewed as a special case of Type I with different optimization) and LSIF. The authors correctly note that while KMM provides a natural out-of-sample extension, FIRE's L² formulation enables effective cross-validation.

I have one minor concern about the supplementary material, which contains several typos and minor technical errors. For instance, Lemma 5 contains an incorrect expression (W²₂ should be Wˢ₂) and an erroneous bound. The supplementary material contains additional errors at lines 675 and 686, while Equation (31) is correct. Given the centrality of the theoretical results to the paper's contribution, I strongly recommend the authors revise the supplementary material to ensure the correctness of their proofs.

In terms of significance, this work makes important contributions to a fundamental problem with applications in transfer learning, covariate shift, and importance sampling. The FIRE framework provides a unified perspective that connects classical analysis with modern machine learning, while CD-CV solves a key practical challenge in unsupervised learning. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, particularly in parameter selection and computational efficiency.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with strong theoretical foundations, a novel contribution to unsupervised model selection, and compelling empirical results. The main weakness is the theoretical convergence rate with fixed t, which could be addressed with stronger assumptions. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a significant contribution to a core problem in machine learning, with both theoretical and practical impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to the supplementary material and a more detailed discussion of the theoretical rate under stronger assumptions.
