This paper introduces two new techniques for estimating the density ratio of two probability measures. The first leverages the importance sampling identity and recasts it as a regularization problem that penalizes the L^2 deviation from the equality together with an RKHS regularizer (Type I Fredholm equation). The second employs a kernel whose locality parameter must vanish for consistency, and is likewise posed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). The authors provide convergence guarantees and demonstrate empirical performance.

While the approach is related to methods such as KMM, a key advantage is that the L^2 formulation permits the use of cross-validation (CV) to choose algorithmic parameters. Unlike supervised learning—where CV is the prevailing strategy for model selection—unsupervised settings generally lack such tools. The paper addresses this gap for density-ratio estimation via the L^2 criterion.

It would be helpful to note that RKHS objective functions, as in KMM, are not suitable for kernel selection via CV, because the objective values depend on the kernel and are therefore not comparable.

In Theorem 1, the presence of the (-t/log λ) factor yields a slow convergence rate when t is fixed. In much of the kernel-methods literature, with fixed t one can obtain a polynomial rate in the sample size after fixing the rate of λ. Are there reasonable additional assumptions that would yield a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator? For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) impose spectral assumptions on the operator K_{p,t} to derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression. I suspect that stronger assumptions on q/p would also lead to a polynomial rate. It would be more informative if the authors discussed such polynomial rates under stronger assumptions with fixed t.

In density-ratio estimation, one typically assumes that q/p lies in a regular function class such as a Sobolev space. This is quite strong, as it presumes knowledge of the ratio or of the tails of p and q. Under such an assumption on q/p, the reverse ratio p/q often behaves poorly. It is important to include some discussion of this limitation of density-ratio estimation.
