This paper proposes two novel methods for estimating the density ratio between two probability distributions. The first method leverages the fundamental equation of importance sampling, formulating it as a regularization problem. This formulation minimizes the L2-norm of the deviation from the equality, subject to a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) regularization term, which corresponds to a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method introduces a kernel-based approach where the locality parameter is designed to converge to zero to ensure consistency, and is also framed as a regularization problem (a Type II Fredholm equation). The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate the empirical performance of their methods through experiments.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its novel L2-norm-based formulation for density ratio estimation. While related to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), the proposed approach has a significant practical advantage: it enables the use of standard cross-validation (CV) for hyperparameter selection (e.g., the regularization parameter and kernel parameters). This is a noteworthy contribution, as robust and principled hyperparameter tuning is a persistent challenge in unsupervised learning settings, unlike in supervised learning where CV is a standard tool. The paper's formulation provides a well-justified objective function for this purpose.

**Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Motivation for CV-based Kernel Selection:** To further highlight the novelty of the proposed CV-based approach, it would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly discuss why conventional RKHS-based objective functions (as seen in methods like KMM) are often unsuitable for kernel selection via CV. Specifically, since the objective function's value is scale-dependent on the choice of kernel, comparing objective values across different kernels is not meaningful. Explicitly stating this would better motivate the authors' L2 formulation.

2.  **Convergence Rate Analysis:** The convergence rate presented in Theorem 1, which includes a `(-t/log λ)` factor, appears to be quite slow for a fixed `t`. In much of the kernel methods literature, it is common to derive polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size by assuming `λ` decays at a specific rate. Could the authors discuss whether a polynomial rate can be achieved under stronger, yet reasonable, assumptions? For instance, following the work of Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) for kernel ridge regression, one might impose assumptions on the spectrum of the integral operator. Alternatively, stronger smoothness or regularity assumptions on the true density ratio `q/p` might also yield a faster, polynomial rate. A discussion of these possibilities would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

3.  **Discussion of Inherent Assumptions:** A broader point of discussion that would improve the paper is the nature of the assumptions on the density ratio itself. Density ratio estimation methods typically require the true ratio `q/p` to belong to a well-behaved function class (e.g., an RKHS or a Sobolev space). This can be a strong assumption, as it implies certain knowledge about the relationship between `p` and `q`, particularly their tail behaviors. Furthermore, if `q/p` is well-behaved, the inverse ratio `p/q` may not be, limiting the applicability in scenarios where the direction is reversed. A brief discussion of these inherent limitations of the density ratio estimation framework would provide valuable context for the reader.

4.  **Corrections to Supplementary Material:** The supplementary material requires significant revision. It currently contains numerous typos and errors that impede the verification of the theoretical results. For example:
    *   In Lemma 5, `W2^2` appears to be a typo for `Ws^2`, and the bound seems to be missing terms (e.g., it should likely be of the form `D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^(1-α) D2 ||f||_2^2`).
    *   The expressions in lines 675 and 686 appear incorrect, though Eq. (31) is correct.
    *   In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be `||ξ||^2 ≥ ...`.
    Given that the convergence guarantees are a central contribution of this paper, it is crucial that the proofs are presented clearly and correctly. The authors are strongly encouraged to carefully proofread and revise the supplement.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper introduces a promising new framework for density ratio estimation. The ability to use cross-validation for hyperparameter selection is a significant practical advantage over existing methods and constitutes the main contribution of the work. However, the paper could be substantially improved by addressing the questions regarding the theoretical convergence rates, discussing the inherent assumptions of the problem, and thoroughly correcting the supplementary material to ensure the validity of its theoretical claims.
