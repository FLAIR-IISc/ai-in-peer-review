

The paper introduces a novel approach for density ratio estimation \(p/q\) by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation, distinct from existing methods.  

Strong points:  
The paper is well-written and engaging. The density ratio estimation problem is both significant and compelling.  

Weak points, comments, and questions:  
(i) Key asymptotic claims lack justification; for instance, the assertion \(\int k_t(x,y)f(x)\,dx = f(y) + O(t)\) requires a proof (in the main text or supplementary material) or a reference, regardless of simplicity.  
(ii) The special case \(q=1\) enables density estimation without requiring bandwidth convergence to zero (unlike standard KDE, which fails here). A clearer discussion of bandwidth parameter roles versus KDE would help readers.  
(iii) A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization is recommended, as most NIPS attendees may be unfamiliar.  
(iv) All proofs reside in the supplementary material; their correctness remains unverified by this reviewer.  
(v) A comparison to the naive plug-in approach (estimating \(p\) and \(q\) separately) is needed to highlight advantages of the proposed method.  
(vi) The method’s relative performance against existing algorithms (e.g., [12], which establishes minimax bounds) is unclear. While Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds, their proximity to the optimal minimax rate requires elaboration.  
(vii) References for datasets (CPU_small, Kin8nm, USPS) must be provided.  
(viii) A toy example with known 1-dimensional \(p\) and \(q\) (e.g., plots of true vs. estimated \(p/q\)) would demonstrate estimation quality effectively.  

The paper presents a promising contribution to density ratio estimation. However, its comparative performance against methods like [12] and practical validation via toy examples remain insufficiently addressed.
