This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio of two probabilities. The first method leverages the equation of importance sampling, formulating it as a regularization problem that incorporates the L^2 norm of the deviation from equality and an RKHS regularization term, resulting in a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method employs a kernel where the locality parameter must approach zero for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem, yielding a Type II Fredholm equation. The authors provide theoretical results on convergence and demonstrate the practical performance of their methods through experiments.

The proposed methods share similarities with existing approaches, such as KMM. However, a significant advantage of the proposed methods is that the L^2 formulation enables the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. Unlike supervised learning, where CV is a dominant method for choosing parameters, there is a lack of general methods for unsupervised learning. The paper addresses this issue in density ratio estimation by cleverly utilizing the L^2 norm.

It is worth noting that the objective function derived from RKHS, as in KMM, is not suitable for kernel selection via CV, as the objective function values depend on the kernel and are therefore not comparable.

Theorem 1 derives a convergence rate that is slow with a fixed t due to the (-t/log λ) factor. In contrast, many kernel method literatures achieve polynomial rates with respect to the sample size after fixing the rate of λ. The authors may consider exploring reasonable additional assumptions that could lead to polynomial rates for the proposed estimator. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) make assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to derive the optimal rate for kernel ridge regression. Similarly, stronger assumptions on q/p may also yield polynomial rates. Discussing such polynomial rates under stronger assumptions with fixed t would be more interesting.

In density ratio estimation, it is common to assume that q/p belongs to a nice function class, such as Sobolev. However, this assumption is strong, requiring knowledge of the ratio or tails of p and q. Notably, under this assumption, the opposite ratio p/q often behaves poorly. The authors should include a discussion on this limitation of density ratio estimation.

While not required, the supplementary materials need improvement. There are numerous typos and minor mistakes that make it challenging to verify the correctness of the theoretical results in the main paper. For example, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 ||f||_2^2. The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, whereas Eq.(31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be ||ξ||^2 ≥. Given that the theoretical results on convergence are a primary contribution of this paper, the authors are advised to revise the supplementary material.
