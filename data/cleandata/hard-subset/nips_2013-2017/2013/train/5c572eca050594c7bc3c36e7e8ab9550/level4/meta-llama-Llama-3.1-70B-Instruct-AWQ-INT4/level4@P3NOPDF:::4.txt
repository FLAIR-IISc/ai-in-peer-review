This paper presents a novel approach to estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative, f = p/q, using two equalities and replacing integral operators with empirical versions. The authors employ a set of test functions, {k(x,.)}, to minimize the difference between the right and left sides of the equalities. This choice of test functions allows for the use of standard kernel methods for optimization.

The paper provides convergence guarantees for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. The rate of convergence is shown to depend crucially on the dimension of the underlying space, which is expected for density estimates.

Overall, this is a well-executed work that addresses a relevant problem in the field. The authors provide a thorough convergence study and experimental results to support their approach.

A few comments and suggestions for improvement are in order. Firstly, the convergence rates are shown to be dependent on the dimension of the underlying space. While this is intuitive for the second equality, a short intuitive argument for why this also works out for the first equality would be helpful.

Secondly, the choice of cost function is not fully motivated. The authors optimize over L2,p on page 2, but later use a combination of L2,p and L2,q in equation (9). It would be beneficial to provide a clear motivation for the choice of cost function and link it to specific applications.

In this regard, the authors mention importance sampling as an application, where q/p is relevant for moving from Eq to Ep. However, it is not entirely clear why estimating Ep and Eq to get the empirical cost function is necessary, when Eq could be used directly. The authors mention that sampling from Eq is difficult, but it would be helpful to provide more context and motivation for this setup.

Furthermore, the regularizer enforces smoothness for the ratio q/p, but the theorem assumes smoothness on q and p. It would be interesting to explore the relationship between smoothness on q and p and smoothness of the ratio. While it is true that many different q and p can produce the same ratio, it is possible that smoothness on q and p implies smoothness of the ratio, or that smoothness of the ratio implies the existence of smooth q and p that produce the ratio.

Additionally, it would be beneficial to discuss the dimension dependence of the rates and for what dimensions the method is expected to be useful.

The choice of the family of test functions U could also be better motivated. While the authors mention that standard kernel methods can be used for optimization, it is not clear why this particular choice of test functions is optimal.

On a more principled note, the approach taken in this paper is reminiscent of other works, such as [1], which also use transformations to extract the underlying densities. While the authors' approach is distinct and has its advantages, it would be interesting to discuss the relationship between these approaches and whether the authors believe they have found the "right" approach for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that addresses a relevant problem and provides a thorough convergence study and experimental results. With some minor revisions to address the above comments, it has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
