The authors propose a novel approach to estimating the ratio of two probability density functions, leveraging a kernel and a Fredholm integral equation to formulate an inverse problem. This framework is innovative and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying mathematical concepts.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The introduction effectively sets the stage for the problem, and the subsequent sections provide a logical progression of ideas, from the formulation of the inverse problem to the development of the FIRE algorithm and its theoretical analysis.

The authors demonstrate a good grasp of the relevant literature, citing key papers and positioning their work within the broader context of machine learning and statistical inference. The connections to kernel methods, regularization, and spectral analysis are particularly well-explored.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is thorough and provides valuable insights into the properties of the FIRE algorithm. The use of concentration inequalities and spectral theory to establish bounds and convergence rates is impressive and demonstrates a high level of mathematical sophistication.

The experimental evaluation in Section 4 is comprehensive and well-designed, with a clear description of the datasets, resampling methods, and performance measures used. The comparison with other methods, such as KMM and LSIF, is informative and helps to contextualize the results.

However, I do have some concerns regarding the assumptions made in the paper. Specifically, the authors assume that the densities p and q are well-defined and positive for all x. While this assumption is common in machine learning, it is not always realistic, and it would be beneficial to discuss the implications of relaxing this assumption.

Additionally, the authors could provide more clarity on the choice of dominating measure ν. As the reviewer notes, the estimation of a ratio of two densities is problematic from the viewpoint of machine learning, and it is unclear whether the results are rigorous due to sets of ν-measure zero.

Minor suggestions for improvement include providing more detail on the computational complexity of the FIRE algorithm and discussing potential extensions to more general settings, such as non-Euclidean spaces or non-parametric densities.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and statistical inference, and the authors are to be commended for their innovative approach and thorough analysis.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to estimating the ratio of two probability density functions
* Thorough theoretical analysis and experimental evaluation
* Clear connections to kernel methods, regularization, and spectral analysis

Weaknesses:

* Assumptions regarding the densities p and q could be more clearly stated and discussed
* Choice of dominating measure ν could be more explicitly justified
* Computational complexity of the FIRE algorithm could be more thoroughly analyzed
* Potential extensions to more general settings could be explored

Acceptance:

* Yes, with minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.

Rating: 8/10
