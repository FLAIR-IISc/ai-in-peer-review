This paper proposes a new approach to estimating the density ratio p/q by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. Several methods for density ratio estimation already exist, but this reformulation is the main novelty.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written and engaging.
- The density ratio estimation problem is important and timely.

Weaknesses, comments, and questions:
1) Some technical details are missing. For example, the claim ∫ k_t(x, y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) requires a proof or a precise reference. Please provide full justifications (in the paper or supplement) for such statements, even if they are standard.

2) When q = 1, the method reduces to density estimation. It is notable that your approach does not require the bandwidth to shrink to zero, whereas standard KDE would be inconsistent in that regime. Please expand the discussion to clarify the distinct roles of bandwidth in your method versus KDE, and explain the consistency guarantees under fixed bandwidth.

3) Consider adding a brief, self-contained overview of Tikhonov regularization, since many NIPS readers may be unfamiliar with it.

4) All proofs are placed in the supplementary material; I have not verified them.

5) Please discuss why a naive plug-in strategy—estimating p and q separately and taking their ratio—is inferior to your approach. For example, highlight error amplification in the denominator, bias-variance trade-offs, or regularization differences.

6) The empirical and theoretical comparisons to prior work are not fully clear. For instance, if memory serves, [12] provides minimax bounds. Your Corollaries 2 and 4 give upper bounds, but it is unclear how they compare to known minimax-optimal rates. Please position your rates relative to optimality and to [12], and, if possible, include direct empirical comparisons.

7) Provide references for all datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

8) A toy example would help readers build intuition. For known one-dimensional p and q, plotting the true ratio p/q alongside your estimate would make accuracy and bias visually clear.

Overall, this is a solid and well-presented contribution to density ratio estimation. However, the paper would be substantially strengthened by clarifying the technical claims, adding background on the regularization, providing theoretical positioning against prior minimax results (e.g., [12]), including direct empirical comparisons, and illustrating performance on simple toy problems with known ground truth.
