The paper proposes a novel method for estimating the density ratio p/q, a problem for which several other approaches have already been suggested. The key contribution of this work lies in its reformulation of the density ratio estimation problem as a Fredholm integral equation.

Strong points of the paper:

The paper is well-written and engaging to read. The problem of density ratio estimation is both important and interesting.

Weak points of the paper, comments, and questions:

(i) Certain details are omitted from the paper. For instance, the statement \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t) lacks a proof or reference, even though the proof may be straightforward. Including proofs or references for such statements would enhance the paper's clarity.

(ii) As a special case (q=1), this method can also be applied to density estimation. Notably, this approach does not require the bandwidth to converge to zero, unlike standard kernel density estimation (KDE), which is inconsistent in this case. A more in-depth discussion on this topic would help readers understand the distinct roles of the bandwidth parameters in this paper compared to KDE.

(iii) Adding a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would be beneficial, as this concept may be unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS community.

(iv) All proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which I have not verified for correctness.

(v) A few sentences explaining why a naive plug-in approach, which involves separately estimating densities p and q, is inferior to the proposed method would be a welcome addition.

(vi) The paper does not provide a clear comparison of the proposed method's performance to that of other existing methods. For example, [12] also derives minimax bounds. While Corollary 2 and 4 offer upper bounds on the convergence rate, it is unclear how close these bounds are to the optimal minimax rate.

(vii) References for the datasets used in the paper (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be provided.

(viii) I would have liked to see a demonstration of the proposed method's performance on toy problems with fixed and known p and q. For instance, a plot of the true p/q and the estimated p/q for 1-dimensional p and q would help readers assess the quality of the estimation.

Overall, this is a nice paper on density ratio estimation. However, its performance compared to other algorithms (e.g., [12]) is unclear, and a demonstration of the approach on toy problems would have been a valuable addition.
