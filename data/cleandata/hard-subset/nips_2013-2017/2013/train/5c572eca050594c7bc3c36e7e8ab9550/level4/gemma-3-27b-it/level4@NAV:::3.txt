The authors tackle the challenging problem of estimating the ratio of two probability density functions, employing a kernel-based method that leads to a Fredholm integral equation and, consequently, an inverse problem. While framed as a density estimation task, this approach faces fundamental difficulties from a machine learning perspective. It is established that within any neighborhood of a probability measure possessing a density, there exist measures lacking one, raising concerns about the inherent identifiability of density ratios. 

My primary concern is the lack of clarity regarding the underlying dominating measure, ν, used in the analysis – whether it is Lebesgue, counting, or another. This omission, coupled with potential issues arising from sets of zero ν-measure, leaves the rigor of the results questionable. Furthermore, the manuscript lacks explicit statements of key assumptions. For instance, the space in which *x* resides (ℝ<sup>d</sup> or a more general topological space) is not specified, nor is it stated whether the density *p* is assumed to be positive everywhere.
