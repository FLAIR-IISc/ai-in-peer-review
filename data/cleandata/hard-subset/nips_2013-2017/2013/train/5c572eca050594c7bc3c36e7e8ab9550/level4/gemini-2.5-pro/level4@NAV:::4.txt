This paper proposes two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative, f = p/q, derived from the equalities: (i) E_p[k(x,·)f] = E_q[k(x,·)] and (ii) E_p[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x). The estimation is performed by replacing the integral operators with their empirical counterparts and minimizing the discrepancy between the two sides of the equalities over a set of test functions {k(x,·)}. A key advantage of selecting this family of test functions is that it enables the use of standard kernel-based optimization techniques. The authors provide convergence guarantees for both estimators under standard smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. As expected for density estimation, the convergence rates exhibit a crucial dependence on the dimensionality of the underlying space.

Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable piece of work.

I have a few comments for the authors to consider:
-   **Convergence Rate Intuition:** The paper establishes that optimizing over the set {k(x,·)} is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the density ratio. For setting (ii), this is quite intuitive given the dependence on the kernel bandwidth. However, the paper would benefit from a short, intuitive argument clarifying why this optimization is also sufficient for convergence in setting (i).
-   **Choice of Cost Function:** The motivation behind the choice of cost function could be clarified. The optimization is framed over L2,p on page 2, but the objective in Equation (9) later employs a combination of L2,p and L2,q norms. It would be helpful to justify this specific formulation and, ideally, link the choice of cost function to the requirements of important downstream applications.
-   **Application to Importance Sampling:** The paper mentions importance sampling as a key application, where estimating f = q/p is necessary to transform an expectation under q to one under p. The proposed method requires empirical estimates of both E_p and E_q to construct its cost function. This raises a question: if an empirical estimate of E_q is already available, what is the benefit of this indirect approach over using the direct empirical estimate of E_q? The paper briefly notes on page 5 that sampling from q may be difficult. It would be beneficial to expand on this point and clarify if this scenario is the primary intended use case.
-   **Regularization and Smoothness Assumptions:** There is a subtle disconnect between the regularization and the theoretical assumptions. The regularizer imposes smoothness on the ratio f = q/p, whereas the convergence theorems rely on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities p and q. The relationship between these two concepts of smoothness should be discussed. For instance, do smooth densities p and q (bounded away from zero) necessarily imply a smooth ratio? A brief exploration of this relationship would strengthen the paper's theoretical grounding.
-   **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The paper should include a discussion on the practical implications of the convergence rates' dependence on dimensionality (d). For what range of dimensions do the authors anticipate their method will be most useful?
-   **Motivation for Test Functions:** Could the authors provide a more detailed motivation for their specific choice of the family of test functions, U?
-   **Broader Context:** On a more fundamental level, a central challenge in density ratio estimation is formulating a generic cost function. The approach of using expectation operators and test functions is one powerful strategy. It would be valuable to position this work in relation to other high-level approaches. For example, there are conceptual similarities to methods like those in [1], which also leverage transformations to estimate properties of distributions. While the test function approach here seems more direct for the Radon-Nikodym derivative, a brief comparison of the philosophies would be insightful. Do the authors see this kernel-based test function approach as a particularly "correct" or uniquely advantageous formulation for this class of problems?

In conclusion, this is a strong and timely paper addressing a relevant problem. The combination of a novel methodology, a formal convergence analysis, and supporting experimental results makes it a solid contribution to the field.
