The paper proposes a method for estimating the ratio of two probability density functions using a kernel-based formulation and a Fredholm integral equation, which leads to an inverse problem.

From a machine learning perspective, the assumption that the distributions admit densities is very strong. It is well known that in any ε-neighborhood of a probability measure P that has a ν-density, there exist probability measures that have no ν-density. Consequently, estimating a ratio of two densities is problematic for ML, as the assumption of absolute continuity is fragile under small perturbations. This fragility raises concerns about the robustness and applicability of the proposed approach.

As far as I can tell, the paper does not specify the dominating measure ν with respect to which densities are defined (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure, or something else). Without this, it is unclear whether the statements and proofs are rigorous, especially regarding sets of ν-measure zero. The treatment of such null sets is crucial for defining densities, handling ratios, and ensuring that the integral equation is well defined.

I also miss a clear and explicit statement of the assumptions. In particular:
- What is the sample space for x? Does x lie in R^d, or in a more general measurable or topological space?
- Which dominating measure ν is used?
- Are the densities strictly positive for all x (or at least on their supports)? How are zero-density regions handled when forming a ratio?
- What measurability and regularity assumptions are imposed on the kernel and on the densities?
- Are there assumptions on support overlap to ensure the ratio is well defined where needed?

Without a precise specification of the underlying measurable space, the dominating measure, the positivity/absolute continuity conditions, and how ν-null sets are handled, it is difficult to assess the rigor and practical relevance of the results. I recommend adding a formal problem setup that clearly states:
- The measurable space (X, B, ν);
- The conditions under which the two distributions are absolutely continuous with respect to ν;
- Any positivity or support-overlap assumptions required for the ratio;
- The domain of x (e.g., R^d) and any structural assumptions needed for the kernel construction.

Clarifying these points would substantially strengthen the theoretical foundation and make the scope and limitations of the method clear.
