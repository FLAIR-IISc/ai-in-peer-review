This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability densities, or more generally, the ratio of two functions. The authors formulate this problem as an inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm equation of the first kind, and propose a family of algorithms (FIRE – Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) based on regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. They provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, and demonstrate promising experimental results, including applications to classification within a covariate shift setting.

The approach is well-motivated, building on the connection between density ratio estimation and importance sampling. A key contribution is the development of a completely unsupervised method for model selection, termed CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), which leverages the L2 formulation of the problem to enable cross-validation – a crucial capability often lacking in unsupervised learning. This is a significant strength, as standard cross-validation techniques are widely used and well-understood in supervised learning contexts. The authors correctly point out the difficulty of applying cross-validation directly to methods based on RKHS norms, like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), where the objective function's value is inherently tied to the chosen kernel.

While the method shares similarities with existing techniques such as KMM and LSIF, the FIRE algorithm offers a flexible framework with various norms and regularization techniques. The theoretical analysis, providing concentration and error bounds, is a valuable contribution. The experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

However, the convergence rates presented in Theorem 1 appear relatively slow, with a dependence on `t` (the kernel width) that could be limiting in practice. Many kernel methods achieve polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size when `t` is fixed. It would be beneficial if the authors explored stronger assumptions—perhaps on the smoothness of `q/p`—that would allow them to derive such polynomial rates. Existing work, such as Caponnetto and De Vito (2007), leverages spectral properties of the kernel operator to achieve optimal rates in kernel ridge regression; a similar analysis could be worthwhile here. 

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations inherent in density ratio estimation. Estimating `q/p` often requires strong assumptions about its regularity (e.g., belonging to a Sobolev space), which can be difficult to justify in practice and may lead to poor performance when `p/q` is poorly behaved. Acknowledging this trade-off and its implications would strengthen the work.

Finally, while not critical, the supplementary material contains numerous typos and minor errors that hinder verification of the theoretical results. Addressing these inaccuracies is highly recommended. For example, in Lemma 5, “W2^2” should be “Ws^2,” and the bound should be “D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^(1-α) D2 ||f||_2^2.” The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are also incorrect, although Eq.(31) is correct. The domain of the integral at line 1147 in the proof of Lemma 5 should be “||ξ||^2 ≥”. Given the importance of the theoretical results to the paper’s contributions, a careful revision of the supplementary material is strongly advised.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem.
*   Development of the FIRE algorithm, a flexible and principled approach based on regularization.
*   Introduction of CD-CV, a practical and effective unsupervised model selection method.
*   Theoretical analysis providing concentration bounds and convergence rates.
*   Competitive experimental performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Slow convergence rates in Theorem 1, potentially limiting practical applicability.
*   Lack of discussion regarding the strong assumptions often required for density ratio estimation.
*   Numerous errors in the supplementary material.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The proposed method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically promising. While the convergence rates could be improved and the discussion of limitations expanded, the novel formulation and the development of CD-CV are significant achievements. Addressing the errors in the supplementary material is essential. I recommend acceptance, pending minor revisions to address the points raised above.
