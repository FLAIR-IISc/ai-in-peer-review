

This paper introduces a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem in operator theory. The authors propose a principled approach using regularization within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), resulting in a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The approach is theoretically grounded, with detailed analysis of convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and introduces a novel unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) for parameter tuning.

The work connects to recent literature on density ratio estimation in transfer learning, particularly relating to covariate shift problems. It builds upon and extends methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), but provides a more unified theoretical framework by establishing the problem as a classical inverse problem. This perspective allows the authors to leverage spectral theory for analysis and provides a natural way to incorporate regularization, addressing a key limitation in previous approaches where regularization was not systematically handled.

The paper demonstrates several significant strengths. First, the theoretical analysis is thorough, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for both Type I (using samples from both distributions) and Type II (using known function values at sample points) settings. The authors establish rates for both Euclidean domains and sub-manifolds, which is particularly valuable for real-world applications. Second, the introduction of CD-CV for unsupervised model selection is a major contribution, solving a critical problem in density ratio estimation where traditional cross-validation is not applicable. Third, the framework is flexible, allowing for various loss functions and norms, while maintaining computational simplicity through the representer theorem.

The paper excels in quality, with sound theoretical foundations and well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, and the experimental results demonstrate strong performance compared to state-of-the-art methods like KMM and LSIF. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with kernel methods. The significance of the work is substantial, as density ratio estimation is fundamental to many applications in transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift adaptation.

However, the paper has some limitations that should be addressed. The theoretical analysis in Theorem 1 shows a slow convergence rate (with a (-t/log λ) factor) when t is fixed, which may be suboptimal compared to standard kernel methods where polynomial rates can be achieved with fixed bandwidths. The authors should discuss whether stronger assumptions on the problem structure (such as those on the spectrum of the integral operator, as in Caponnetto and De Vito, 2007) would yield better rates. Additionally, the paper relies on strong assumptions about the density ratio (q/p being in a Sobolev space), which requires significant prior knowledge about the distributions. It would be valuable to discuss the practical implications of this assumption, particularly since the inverse ratio (p/q) often behaves poorly under such conditions.

The supplementary material contains several technical errors that need correction, including typos in Lemma 5 (W2^2 should be Ws^2) and incorrect expressions at lines 675 and 686. The proof of Lemma 5 also contains a domain error in the integral bounds. Since the theoretical results form a core contribution of the paper, these issues should be addressed to ensure the correctness of the analysis.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel framework that unifies density ratio estimation under a classical inverse problem perspective, enabling the application of cross-validation for parameter selection - a significant advancement in unsupervised learning. This is particularly valuable as most unsupervised learning methods lack reliable model selection procedures. The comparison with KMM is insightful, highlighting how the L2 formulation enables effective cross-validation where RKHS-based objectives do not.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation by providing a theoretically grounded framework with practical implementation advantages. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in unsupervised learning methodology. The theoretical analysis is strong, though could be strengthened by addressing the convergence rate concerns and discussing the limitations of the Sobolev space assumption. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.

Pros:
- Novel framework unifying density ratio estimation under classical inverse problem theory
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates for multiple settings
- Introduces CD-CV, a valuable unsupervised model selection method
- Clear connection to and extension of existing work (KMM, LSIF)
- Strong experimental results demonstrating practical advantages

Cons:
- Theoretical convergence rate could be improved with stronger assumptions
- Strong Sobolev space assumption for q/p requires significant prior knowledge
- Supplementary material contains technical errors requiring correction
- Limited discussion of the limitations of the density ratio estimation approach

This paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical application of density ratio estimation. With the suggested revisions to the theoretical analysis and supplementary material, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
