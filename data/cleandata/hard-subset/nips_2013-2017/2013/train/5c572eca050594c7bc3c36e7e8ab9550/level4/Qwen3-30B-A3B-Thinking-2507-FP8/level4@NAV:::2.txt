

This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio of two probability distributions. The first method leverages the importance sampling equation, reformulated as a regularization problem incorporating an L^2 norm penalty for deviation from equality and an RKHS regularization term (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that must vanish for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). Theoretical convergence results and experimental validation of practical performance are provided.  

The approach shares similarities with existing methods like KMM, but its key strength is the L^2 formulation, enabling the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection—a significant advantage. Unlike supervised learning, where CV is standard for hyperparameter tuning, unsupervised methods lack general parameter selection frameworks; this paper effectively addresses that gap for density ratio estimation. It would be beneficial to include a remark noting that RKHS-based objective functions (e.g., in KMM) are unsuitable for CV, as their values depend on the kernel and thus are non-comparable.  

In Theorem 1, the convergence rate derived with the factor $(-t/\log \lambda)$ is slow for fixed $t$. In kernel method literature (e.g., Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007), fixed $t$ typically yields polynomial rates in sample size by fixing $\lambda$'s decay. Reasonable additional assumptions (e.g., spectral conditions on $K_{p,t}$ or stronger assumptions on $q/p$) might yield a polynomial rate. Discussing such a rate under stronger assumptions would enhance the theoretical contribution.  

Density ratio estimation typically requires $q/p$ to reside in a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev), a strong assumption demanding prior knowledge of the ratio or tail behavior. Crucially, under this assumption, $p/q$ often behaves poorly. The paper should address this limitation of density ratio estimation in the discussion.  

The supplementary material requires improvement: numerous typos and minor errors impede verification of theoretical results. For example:  
- Lemma 5: $W_2^2$ should be $W_s^2$; the bound is $D_1 (t/(-\log \lambda))^2 + \lambda^{1-\alpha} D_2 \|f\|_2^2$.  
- Lines 675 and 686 contain incorrect expressions (Eq. (31) is correct).  
- In Lemma 5’s proof, line 1147 requires the integral domain $\|\xi\|^2 \geq \ldots$.  
Given the centrality of the convergence results to the paper’s contribution, the supplementary material must be rigorously revised.  

The paper’s core strength lies in enabling effective parameter selection via CV for density ratio estimation.
