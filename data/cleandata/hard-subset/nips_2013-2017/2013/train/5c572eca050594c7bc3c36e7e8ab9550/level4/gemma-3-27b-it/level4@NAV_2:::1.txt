The paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, p/q, with applications to importance sampling and covariate shift. The core idea is to reformulate this estimation problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leverage regularization techniques from operator theory to develop a family of algorithms, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The authors provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates, and demonstrate promising experimental results.

This paper presents a well-written and motivated approach to a significant problem in machine learning and statistics. The connection to classical integral equation theory provides a novel perspective and a principled framework for algorithm design and analysis. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and its relevance to covariate shift and related areas, effectively situating the work within the existing literature.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. First, while the paper states that  \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t), a brief justification or citation would be helpful, even if the result is well-known.  Similarly, a concise explanation of Tikhonov regularization would be beneficial for readers less familiar with the technique. Currently, the paper assumes a certain level of expertise in functional analysis.

Second, the paper briefly mentions that the approach avoids the bandwidth convergence issues common in standard kernel density estimation. Expanding on this point – clearly delineating the role of the kernel parameter *t* in FIRE versus the bandwidth selection in KDE – would highlight a key advantage of the proposed method. 

Third, while the theoretical analysis provides convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4), it would be valuable to contextualize these rates relative to known minimax bounds, such as those derived in [12]. Discussing the gap (if any) between the achieved rates and the theoretical optimum would further strengthen the contribution. 

Fourth, the experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, would benefit from illustrative examples on simple, known distributions (e.g., 1D Gaussian mixtures for p and q). Visualizing the estimated density ratio alongside the true ratio on these toy problems would enhance the reader’s understanding and build confidence in the method’s performance. The datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should also be more fully referenced.

Finally, a brief discussion of why a naive plug-in density estimation approach is suboptimal compared to the proposed method would be informative.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Principled framework based on regularization techniques with solid theoretical foundations (concentration bounds, convergence rates).
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating competitive performance.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Some theoretical statements lack sufficient justification or references.
*   Missing context regarding the optimality of the derived convergence rates compared to existing minimax bounds.
*   Absence of illustrative examples on toy problems to demonstrate performance on known distributions.
*   Limited discussion comparing the method to a naive plug-in density estimation approach.



Overall, this is a valuable contribution with the potential to advance the field of density ratio estimation. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would significantly enhance the paper's clarity and impact.
