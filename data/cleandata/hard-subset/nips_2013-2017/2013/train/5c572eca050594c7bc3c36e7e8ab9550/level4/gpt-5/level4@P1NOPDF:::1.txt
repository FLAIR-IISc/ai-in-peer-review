This paper proposes a new method for estimating the density ratio p/q by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. Several methods already exist for density ratio estimation; the contribution here is this alternative formulation.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written and engaging. 
- The density ratio estimation problem is important and timely.

Weaknesses, comments, and questions:
(i) Some technical details are missing. For example, why does ∫ k_t(x, y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t)? Please provide a proof (in the main text or supplementary material) or a precise reference for this and similar statements, even if they are straightforward.

(ii) As a special case (q = 1), the method yields a density estimator. It is striking that the approach does not require the kernel bandwidth to shrink to zero; standard KDE is inconsistent under a fixed bandwidth. Please add discussion clarifying why the bandwidth plays a different role here than in KDE, and under what conditions consistency holds without bandwidth decay.

(iii) Consider adding a brief primer on Tikhonov regularization. Many readers in the NIPS community may be unfamiliar with it; a short explanation of the objective, the role of the regularization parameter, and its interplay with the kernel and sample size would be helpful.

(iv) All proofs are in the supplementary material. I have not verified them; a brief sketch of the main proof ideas in the paper would improve accessibility.

(v) Please discuss why a naive plug-in approach—separately estimating p and q and then taking their ratio—is inferior in this setting. For example, is the proposed method more stable in regions where q is small, does it achieve better bias–variance trade-offs, or does it avoid compounding errors from two separate estimators?

(vi) The comparative performance against existing methods is unclear. For instance, if I recall correctly, [12] also derives minimax bounds. Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate, but it is not clear how close these are to optimal minimax rates under your assumed smoothness and margin conditions. Please position your rates relative to known lower bounds (e.g., from [12] or related work), or explicitly state the gap if it is unknown.

(vii) Please provide references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS), and specify any preprocessing.

(viii) I would have liked to see simple toy experiments with known p and q. In one dimension, plotting the true ratio p/q alongside the estimate would let readers visually assess bias, variance, and behavior near regions where q is small.

Overall assessment:
This is a nice paper with a clear presentation and an interesting reformulation of density ratio estimation via a Fredholm integral equation. However, I am uncertain how the method compares—both theoretically and empirically—to strong baselines (e.g., [12]). Additional theoretical positioning (minimax optimality or explicit gaps), practical guidance on bandwidth and regularization, and illustrative toy examples would substantially strengthen the paper.
