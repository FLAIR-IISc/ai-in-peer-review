Summary and relation to prior work
This paper considers estimating the density ratio r(x) = q(x)/p(x), a quantity central to importance sampling and covariate shift correction. The key idea is to rewrite the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind involving kernel integral operators, and to solve the resulting inverse problem via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are developed:

- Type I (two-sample): Given samples from p and q, use the identity Kp r = Kq 1, where Kp and Kq are kernel integral operators, and estimate r by minimizing an empirical L2 loss (or mixtures of L2,p and L2,q, or an RKHS norm) with an RKHS penalty. The representer theorem yields closed-form linear system solvers (FIRE).

- Type II (semi-parametric q): When one has samples from p and can evaluate q at those sample locations (e.g., in MCMC or unnormalized models), approximate δ-kernels yield Kt,p r ≈ q, leading to a regularized least-squares estimator for r.

The authors analyze FIRE using spectral/operator techniques, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels when p and q are supported on Rd or on smooth compact manifolds. They also propose an unsupervised model selection criterion, cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), which evaluates agreement of importance-weighted expectations across samples from p and q using random test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (selectively), and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and demonstrate competitive performance and computational simplicity.

This work connects to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which matches RKHS means Eq[Φ(x)] and Ep[r(x)Φ(x)] but optimizes weights at sample points without an explicit functional out-of-sample extension; to LSIF/KLIEP, which minimize L2,p or KL divergence over a parametric function class; and to operator-theoretic approaches to inverse problems and kernel methods.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation: Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and unifies several existing techniques within a principled regularization framework.

- Simple, implementable algorithms: The FIRE estimators reduce to solving well-structured linear systems with standard kernel matrices, with a natural out-of-sample representation.

- Theoretical guarantees: The paper provides nonasymptotic error bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels under specified smoothness assumptions, including manifold-supported distributions.

- Unsupervised model selection: CD-CV is a practical criterion in unsupervised/semi-supervised settings where labeled validation is unavailable.

- Useful discussion of related work: The connections to KMM and LSIF are informative, clarifying similarities and differences in objectives and solutions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Assumptions and measure-theoretic clarity
- The paper should state upfront the dominating measure ν with respect to which p and q have densities (e.g., Lebesgue measure on Ω = Rd or the Riemannian volume measure on a manifold). Sections 1–2 read as though p and q are generic “densities,” but the rigorous development (Section 3) assumes absolute continuity and smoothness with respect to such a ν.

- Define support and positivity assumptions explicitly: to make q/p well-defined, assume p(x) > 0 on the support of q, or state that the ratio is defined only on {x: p(x) > 0} and clarify how the estimators handle regions where p is small. This is especially important for the empirical losses weighted by p and for numerical stability.

- Collect all assumptions in a single early subsection (domain Ω, smoothness classes, boundedness p(x), q(x) ≤ Γ, Sobolev membership of q/p, etc.) and echo them in the algorithm sections so readers can relate them to practical choices.

Algorithmic and modeling details
- Kernel choices and notation: In several places k and kH are different; in others they coincide. Clarify when they are identical and discuss the implications for bias and conditioning. For Type II, emphasize the approximation error term introduced by δ-kernels and provide guidance for choosing t relative to n.

- Complexity and scalability: The methods require solving O(n) by O(n) systems; please report computational cost and memory, and consider adding experiments or discussion with Nyström/randNystrom or random features to scale beyond a few thousand points.

- Model selection via CD-CV: Although CD-CV is appealing, its correlation with downstream task loss (e.g., weighted risk in classification/regression) is not fully characterized. An ablation quantifying the sensitivity to the choice of test function families U (linear vs half-space vs kernelized) and to the number F would strengthen the case. A comparison to importance-weighted cross-validation (IWCV) would also be informative.

- Experimental scope: The benchmarks are useful but limited in dimension and difficulty. It would help to include (i) additional covariate shift settings (e.g., real domain adaptation datasets), (ii) higher-dimensional synthetic tests probing the bias-variance trade-offs of t and λ, and (iii) a broader comparison to KMM (even if only on test points via a simple out-of-sample scheme) and to more recent ratio estimators if applicable.

- Practicalities near low density regions: Since ratios can blow up where p is small, discuss whether the regularizer and/or an explicit constraint (e.g., positivity, upper bounds) is used. The Type I RKHS-loss variant could naturally incorporate constraints; exploring this would be valuable.

Quality
- Theoretical analysis is solid and uses appropriate operator tools. Rates are sensible, though somewhat intricate. It would be helpful to provide recommended scaling of (t, λ) with n that nearly optimize the bounds, and to interpret the dimensional dependence. Clarify whether the constants depend on manifold curvature/injectivity radius.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized but would benefit from tightening notation and moving key assumptions earlier. Minor editorial issues: unify kernel notation, fix a/an (“a unsupervised”), and provide pseudocode for each FIRE variant with input/output and complexity.

Originality
- The Fredholm inverse-problem framing is a novel and insightful way to view density ratio estimation. While related ideas exist (KMM, LSIF, inverse problems in RKHS), the integration and analysis here appear original, especially the Type II setting and manifold results.

Significance
- Estimating q/p is broadly useful. FIRE’s simplicity, theoretical backing, and out-of-sample form make it a practical addition to the toolbox. The impact would increase with clearer assumptions and stronger empirical breadth.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant unifying formulation with principled regularization.
- Concrete, simple algorithms with out-of-sample predictions.
- Nonasymptotic bounds and convergence rates, including manifold cases.
- Practical unsupervised model selection.

Arguments against acceptance
- Assumptions and measure-theoretic foundations are not stated early and clearly; support/positivity issues are under-discussed.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and omits stronger comparisons and scaling studies.
- Limited guidance on tuning (t, λ) beyond CD-CV and on behavior in low-density regions.

Overall evaluation
A strong and promising submission that contributes a clear operator-theoretic perspective and practical algorithms with theory. With improvements to the clarity of assumptions, expanded experiments, and more detailed guidance on parameter selection and scalability, this paper would be a solid addition to NIPS.
