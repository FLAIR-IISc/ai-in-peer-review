This paper proposes a method for estimating the ratio of two probability density functions using a kernel-based formulation that leads to a Fredholm integral equation, thereby casting the task as an inverse problem.

From a machine learning perspective, the central modeling assumption—that the relevant probability measures admit densities with respect to a common dominating measure—is very strong. It is well known that for any ε-neighborhood of a probability measure P that has a ν-density, there exist probability measures that do not admit ν-densities. Consequently, the problem of estimating the ratio of two densities is inherently problematic in ML settings, where such density assumptions may fail even under small perturbations of the data-generating distribution.

In addition, the paper does not specify the dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue, counting, or otherwise), and it is unclear whether the theoretical results are fully rigorous given potential issues on sets of ν-measure zero. A precise treatment of these sets is essential for results involving densities and their ratios.

More broadly, I miss a clear and explicit statement of the assumptions underpinning the analysis. For example:
- What is the domain of x? Does x lie in R^d, or in a more general topological space?
- Is the density p strictly positive for all x?

Clarifying the choice of ν, the handling of ν-null sets, and the exact assumptions on the sample space and densities would substantially strengthen the rigor and interpretability of the results.
