Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a principled framework for estimating density ratios r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by recasting the task as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are treated:

- Type I: Use the identity Eq(k(x,·)) = Ep(r(x) k(x,·)) and estimate both sides from samples drawn from q and p, respectively. This yields a regularized least-squares estimate of r in L2(p), with RKHS penalty, and has variants that use L2(q) or mixed norms, or an RKHS loss.
- Type II: Use a δ-kernel (e.g., Gaussian with bandwidth t) and the approximation Kt,p r ≈ q, yielding a regularized least-squares fit to q in L2(p). This accommodates the case where q is not a density but is observed at sample points from p.

The approach leads to simple closed-form solutions via the representer theorem (the proposed FIRE estimators), and a theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on R^d and on smooth manifolds. The paper also introduces an unsupervised model selection method, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which selects kernel and regularization parameters by checking whether Ep[u(x) r̂(x)] ≈ Eq[u(x)] over random test functions u. Experiments compare FIRE with LSIF, KMM (discussed), and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, including covariate shift classification.

This work is closely related to KMM (Gretton et al., 2009), LSIF/KLIEP (Kanamori et al., 2009; Sugiyama et al., 2007), and the broader theory of learning as inverse problems (e.g., Smola & Schölkopf, 1998; De Vito et al., 2005). The Fredholm formulation unifies these perspectives and supports spectral analysis of regularization.

Strengths
- Quality: The formulation is technically sound and leverages well-established tools from operator theory and RKHS regularization. Theoretical results provide finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings. The algorithms are explicit, stable (due to Tikhonov regularization), and have natural out-of-sample evaluation.
- Originality: Casting density ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem and developing a family of regularized estimators (FIRE) is a clean and unifying idea. The CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is practical and well-motivated by the importance sampling identity.
- Significance: Density ratio estimation is central to covariate shift, domain adaptation, and importance sampling. A stable, analyzable method with an unsupervised model selection mechanism can have real impact for practitioners. The manifold case is also of interest.
- Clarity: The main ideas are well conveyed, and algorithmic forms are explicit. The discussion situates the work relative to KMM and LSIF and clarifies differences (e.g., out-of-sample extension and stability).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Rates and assumptions: The stated rates depend intricately on t and λ. As written (and as further detailed in the supplement), the rate with fixed t can be slow (e.g., expressions involving factors like −t/log λ appear in the supplemental derivations). Many kernel learning results obtain polynomial rates for fixed kernels under source/spectral conditions on the integral operator (e.g., Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007). It would strengthen the paper to:
  - State explicit source/spectral assumptions on K_{p,t} and smoothness assumptions on r = q/p, and derive polynomial rates with fixed t by balancing approximation and estimation errors.
  - Clarify the minimax status relative to results such as Nguyen et al. (2008) on likelihood ratio estimation.
- Scope of assumptions: The analysis assumes bounded densities and that r and related quantities lie in Sobolev spaces. This is a strong requirement. In practice, assuming r ∈ W^s can implicitly enforce tail conditions on p and q. Moreover, if r is smooth, the inverse ratio p/q often behaves poorly. Please discuss:
  - Practical diagnostics for when r is estimable (e.g., overlap and tail behavior).
  - How FIRE behaves under covariate shift violations (e.g., small p-mass in regions where q has support).
  - Safeguards such as clipping or localized regularization to control variance in low-p regions.
- Clarifying the comparison to KMM/LSIF: The remark that L2-based formulations enable CV is important. It is worth emphasizing that RKHS-distance objectives, as in KMM, are not directly comparable across kernels, so kernel selection by CV is ill-posed for KMM. Conversely, FIRE’s L2(p) or mixed norms make CV meaningful. A small empirical ablation that compares parameter selection across kernels would be helpful.
- Bias in Type II: The Type II method introduces O(t)-bias due to the δ-kernel approximation. The paper mentions this; it would help to give guidance on choosing t and λ jointly (e.g., scaling rules), and to report sensitivity plots in the experiments.
- Supplementary material: The supplement contains numerous typos and small errors that make verification difficult. Examples include:
  - Lemma 5: W2^2 should be W_s^2, and the bound should read D1 (t/−log λ)^2 + λ^{1−α} D2 ||f||_2^2.
  - Expressions on lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq. (31) is correct.
  - In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of integration at line 1147 should be ||ξ||^2 ≥ ... (missing inequality).
Given that the convergence analysis is a main contribution, I strongly recommend a thorough pass to correct these issues and align the main text with the corrected statements.

Pro/con arguments for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually clean inverse-problem formulation leading to simple, stable algorithms with closed-form solutions.
- Unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) is practical and well-justified.
- Solid theoretical development for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
- Empirical results indicate competitive or improved performance and efficiency.

Cons:
- Convergence rate discussion could be strengthened; conditions for polynomial rates with fixed kernels are not explored.
- Strong smoothness and boundedness assumptions on r = q/p may limit applicability; practical guidance on violations is limited.
- Supplementary material requires significant editing to ensure correctness and readability.
- Some comparative aspects (e.g., kernel selection issues for KMM; sensitivity to t in Type II) could be demonstrated more thoroughly.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance (brief ratings with rationale)
- Quality: Good, contingent on correcting the supplement. The analysis and algorithms are sound; more complete rate results would improve it.
- Clarity: Generally good in the main text; needs improved exposition of assumptions and a cleaned supplement.
- Originality: Moderate to high. The Fredholm framing and CD-CV selection provide a novel and useful perspective beyond KMM/LSIF.
- Significance: High. Robust density ratio estimation with unsupervised model selection is important for covariate shift and importance sampling.

Additional minor comments
- Provide computational complexity and memory costs for each FIRE variant, and brief guidance on choosing between Type I and Type II in practice.
- Unify notation early (e.g., when K and K_H coincide) and keep a concise notation table.
- Consider reporting robustness to clipping of r̂ in regions with small p-density.

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution that unifies density ratio estimation with inverse problem theory, provides practical algorithms and unsupervised model selection, and offers nontrivial analysis. I am inclined toward acceptance after revision, particularly addressing the supplement’s correctness and, if possible, adding discussion (or results) on polynomial rates under stronger, standard spectral/source assumptions.
