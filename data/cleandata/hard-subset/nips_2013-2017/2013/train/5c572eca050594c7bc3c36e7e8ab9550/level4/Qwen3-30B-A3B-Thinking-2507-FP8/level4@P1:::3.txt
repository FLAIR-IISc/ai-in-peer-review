

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it using regularization techniques. The authors develop a principled framework for constructing algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which leverages Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) theory and provides theoretical guarantees for convergence rates. The work is well-motivated by the importance of density ratio estimation in covariate shift adaptation, transfer learning, and importance sampling.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes a connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, offering a fresh perspective on this well-studied problem. Second, it develops a flexible family of algorithms that can be applied in both Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when only values of q are known at sample points) settings. Third, it provides detailed theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds, establishing concentration bounds and convergence rates. Finally, it introduces CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a completely unsupervised model selection method that addresses a significant challenge in unsupervised learning.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by clear mathematical derivations. The authors carefully distinguish between Type I and Type II settings and provide appropriate theoretical bounds for each. The experimental results show that FIRE outperforms existing methods like KMM and LSIF on various datasets, including regression and classification tasks under covariate shift. The CD-CV method for parameter selection is particularly valuable as it avoids the need for labeled data.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The authors effectively relate their work to previous contributions, particularly highlighting connections to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). The theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds is a notable strength, providing concrete convergence rates that advance our understanding of density ratio estimation.

The significance of this work is evident in its practical applications to transfer learning and covariate shift adaptation. The FIRE framework provides a principled approach to density ratio estimation that is both theoretically grounded and computationally efficient. The CD-CV method for model selection is especially valuable for practitioners who often struggle with parameter tuning in unsupervised settings.

I have a few concerns that could be addressed in revision. The paper does not explicitly state which dominating measure is used for the densities (e.g., Lebesgue measure on R^d), which is standard in this literature but would strengthen the theoretical foundations. The manuscript could also more clearly specify the domain assumptions (e.g., whether x belongs to R^d or a more general space) and the positivity requirements for the density p.

The draft review's concern about "the assumption of densities being problematic" is based on a misunderstanding. Density ratio estimation typically assumes both distributions have densities with respect to the same measure (e.g., Lebesgue measure), which is a standard assumption in the literature on covariate shift and importance sampling. The authors' approach is well-defined under these standard assumptions, and their theoretical analysis carefully addresses the required conditions (e.g., p(x) < Γ, q(x) < Γ, and qp in Sobolev space).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The theoretical framework is novel and well-developed, the empirical results are compelling, and the practical applications are well-motivated. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with rigorous analysis (Quality), well-written and clearly organized (Clarity), presents a fresh perspective on density ratio estimation (Originality), and addresses an important problem with practical significance (Significance).

Strengths:
- Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm problem
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates
- Practical unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV)
- Strong empirical performance across multiple datasets
- Clear connections to existing work

Weaknesses:
- Slightly unclear specification of the dominating measure and domain assumptions
- Could more explicitly state the positivity requirement for density p

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning with significant theoretical and practical merits. The authors have addressed an important problem with a principled framework that advances our understanding of density ratio estimation.
