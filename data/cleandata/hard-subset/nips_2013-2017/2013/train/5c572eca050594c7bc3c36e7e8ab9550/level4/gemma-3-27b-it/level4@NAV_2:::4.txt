The authors address the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, p/q, motivated by applications in importance sampling and covariate shift. They recast this problem as estimating a solution to a Fredholm integral equation, leveraging tools from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). This leads to a family of algorithms, FIRE, which are presented with detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in various settings. A novel unsupervised model selection technique, CD-CV, is also introduced and empirically validated.

This is a well-executed and valuable contribution. The formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem provides a strong theoretical foundation and connects the problem to classical analysis tools. The resulting algorithms are simple and flexible, and the detailed convergence analysis is a significant strength. The empirical evaluation demonstrates strong performance compared to existing methods.

Several points could be clarified. Regarding the convergence rates, while the bandwidth dependence in the (ii) setting is intuitive, a brief explanation of why convergence is guaranteed in the (i) setting – minimizing over the kernel functions – would be beneficial. The motivation for using a combined L2,p and L2,q norm in equation (9) could be strengthened by explicitly linking the choice to specific applications where each norm is particularly relevant. The authors briefly mention that sampling from q can be difficult, motivating their approach, but a more thorough discussion of the primary use cases where this limitation is critical would be helpful. 

The assumption of smoothness for q/p and its relationship to the smoothness of q and p themselves is an interesting point. Exploring whether smoothness of q and p guarantees smoothness of the ratio (or vice versa) would provide further insight.  A discussion of how the dimension ‘d’ affects the practical applicability of the method, given the convergence rate dependence on ‘d’, would also be valuable.  Finally, a more in-depth discussion of the choice of test functions (U) and a justification of why the selected family is appropriate would strengthen the paper. 

The overall approach of framing the problem through expectation operators and utilizing test functions shares some similarities with work like [1]. While the authors’ formulation with respect to the Radon-Nikodym derivative and test functions is more elegant, acknowledging this connection and discussing the advantages of their approach would be beneficial.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Strong theoretical foundation and detailed convergence analysis.
* Novel formulation of a relevant problem with connections to classical analysis.
* Simple and flexible algorithms with clear implementation details.
* Strong empirical performance compared to existing methods.
* Introduction of a novel unsupervised model selection technique.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Some aspects of the motivation for choices (e.g., combined norm, test functions) could be further strengthened.
* The impact of dimensionality on practical applicability could be discussed in more detail.
* A clearer articulation of the specific scenarios where the method offers a significant advantage over existing approaches would be helpful.

Overall, this is a timely and strong paper addressing a relevant problem, providing a convergence study and experimental results. I recommend acceptance.
