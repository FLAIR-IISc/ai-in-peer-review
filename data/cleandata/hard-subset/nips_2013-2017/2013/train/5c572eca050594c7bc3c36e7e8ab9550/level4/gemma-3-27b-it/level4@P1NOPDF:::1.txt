The paper proposes a novel method for density ratio estimation, p/q. While several approaches to this problem already exist, the key contribution of this work is the reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read. The density ratio estimation problem is both important and intrinsically interesting.

**Weaknesses, Comments, and Questions:**

(i) The paper lacks detail in certain areas. For instance, the statement \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t) requires justification. Either a proof (within the paper or supplementary material) or a citation is needed for statements of this kind, even if intuitively obvious. 

(ii) As a special case where q=1, the method effectively performs density estimation. Notably, this approach does not require the bandwidth parameter to converge to zero, a limitation of standard kernel density estimation (KDE) which can be inconsistent in such cases. A more thorough discussion of this distinction would be beneficial, clearly articulating the differing roles of the bandwidth parameters in this paper versus those in KDE.

(iii) I recommend briefly explaining Tikhonov regularization, as it may be unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS community. 

(iv) All proofs are relegated to the supplementary material. I have not verified their correctness.

(v) It would be helpful to include a few sentences explaining why a naive plug-in approach – separately estimating densities p and q – is expected to perform worse than the proposed method.

(vi) The paper’s performance relative to existing methods is unclear. For example, [12] also derives minimax bounds. While Corollary 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate, it is unclear how these bounds compare to the optimal minimax rate.

(vii) Please provide references for the datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) A demonstration of the method’s behavior on toy problems with fixed, known distributions for p and q would be valuable. For instance, a plot comparing the true and estimated p/q for simple 1-dimensional distributions could help readers assess the quality of the estimation.

In summary, this is a strong paper addressing an important problem in density ratio estimation. However, the paper would benefit from a clearer comparison to existing algorithms (e.g., [12]) and a demonstration of its performance on illustrative toy problems, such as those using simple 1-dimensional densities for p and q.
