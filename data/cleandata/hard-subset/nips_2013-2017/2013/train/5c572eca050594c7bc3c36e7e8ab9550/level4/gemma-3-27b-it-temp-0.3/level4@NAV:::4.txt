The authors present a method for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative, f = p/q, by deriving and analyzing two estimators based on the equalities: (i) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] = E<sub>q</sub>[k(x,.)] and (ii) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] ≈ q(x). Integral operators are empirically approximated, and the set {k(x,.)} functions as test functions, chosen to minimize the difference between the two sides of the equations, allowing for optimization via standard kernel methods.

The paper provides convergence guarantees for both estimators under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities p and q, with convergence rates appropriately dependent on the dimensionality of the underlying space. Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution.

I have a few suggestions for improvement:

- Regarding the convergence rates: The optimization over {k(x,.)} is sufficient for convergence to the ratio f. While the bandwidth dependence in (ii) provides intuition for this, a brief explanation of why this holds for (i) would be beneficial.
- The cost function: The paper initially optimizes over L<sub>2,p</sub>, then utilizes a combination of L<sub>2,p</sub> and L<sub>2,q</sub> in equation (9).  Further motivation for this choice of cost function would strengthen the work. Connecting the cost function to specific, important applications would be particularly helpful.
- Application motivation: The authors mention importance sampling as an application, where q/p is relevant for transitioning between E<sub>q</sub> and E<sub>p</sub>. Given that the method estimates both E<sub>p</sub> and E<sub>q</sub> to construct the empirical cost function, it’s worth clarifying why estimating E<sub>p</sub>(q/p) is preferable to directly using the estimated E<sub>q</sub>. A more detailed discussion of the setup’s motivation, potentially expanding on the statement on page 5 regarding the difficulty of sampling from E<sub>q</sub>, would be valuable.
- Regularization and smoothness: The method enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p, and the theorem includes smoothness assumptions on q and p themselves. Exploring the relationship between the smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of their ratio would be insightful. While many different q and p can produce the same ratio, potentially exhibiting irregularity, clarifying whether smooth q and p necessarily imply a smooth ratio (or vice versa) would be useful.
- Dimensionality of rates: A discussion of how the dimension (d) affects the applicability of the method, and for what values of d it remains useful, would be beneficial.
- Test function family: Providing further justification for the choice of the family of test functions, U, would strengthen the paper.
- Broader context: A central challenge in covariate shift problems is defining a suitable cost function. The authors’ approach of utilizing expectation operators and test functions to extract densities is a reasonable strategy. It would be interesting to discuss whether the chosen {k(x,.)} represents an optimal approach, and to acknowledge the similarities to related work, such as [1], which also employs transformations to estimate quantities of interest. While the current approach for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is more elegant through the use of test functions, the overall strategy shares conceptual similarities.

[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor

In conclusion, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem, provides a rigorous convergence analysis, and includes experimental validation.
