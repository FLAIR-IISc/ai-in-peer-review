This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q/p, a quantity crucial for tasks like importance sampling and covariate shift correction. The authors propose a novel and principled approach by reformulating this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two distinct settings are derived from two integral equalities: [Type I] Ep[k(x,·)f] = Eq[k(x,·)] and [Type II] Ep[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x), where f represents the ratio q/p. To solve these ill-posed problems, the authors apply Tikhonov regularization in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), leading to a family of simple and flexible algorithms termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). This framework connects the problem to classical operator theory and allows for a rigorous theoretical analysis. The paper provides convergence guarantees for both settings, with rates that depend on the dimensionality of the data manifold, under standard smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. Furthermore, the authors introduce a fully unsupervised model selection procedure, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), and demonstrate strong empirical performance against existing methods like LSIF and a kernel density-based baseline.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Principled Formulation:** The core contribution of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and novel. It provides a clear, principled framework that separates the underlying integral equation from the choice of regularization, connecting a common machine learning problem to classical methods of analysis.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators. This is a significant contribution that provides confidence in the method's behavior.
*   **Practicality:** The resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, explicit, and easy to implement within the standard kernel methods framework. The proposed unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, addresses a critical and often difficult aspect of applying such methods in practice.
*   **Clarity and Execution:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed ideas are well-executed, with a clear derivation of the algorithms and comprehensive experimental validation. The work represents a strong and timely contribution to the literature on density ratio estimation and transfer learning.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is strong, but the following points could be clarified to further improve the manuscript:
1.  **Intuition for Convergence (Type I):** The paper provides formal convergence guarantees. For the Type II setting, convergence is intuitive due to the use of a δ-kernel with a bandwidth parameter. However, it would be beneficial to add a short, intuitive argument for why using the set of test functions {k(x,·)} is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the true ratio in the Type I setting as well.
2.  **Motivation for Loss Functions:** The paper introduces an L2,p loss (Eq. 5) and later a combined γL2,p + (1-γ)L2,q loss (Eq. 9). The motivation for these choices could be expanded. It would be helpful to explicitly link the choice of loss function to specific downstream applications or desired properties of the estimator. For instance, under what circumstances would one be preferable over the other?
3.  **Motivation for the General Setup:** In the context of importance sampling, the goal is often to compute Eq[h(x)] given samples from p. The proposed method requires estimating quantities based on samples from both p and q to find the ratio. A potential question arises: if samples from q are available to construct the empirical cost function, why not use them to estimate Eq[h(x)] directly? The paper briefly mentions that sampling from q can be difficult (page 5). Expanding on this motivation and clarifying the primary application scenarios (e.g., where q is known only up to a constant, or sampling is computationally prohibitive) would strengthen the paper's positioning.
4.  **Smoothness Assumptions:** The regularizer enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p by penalizing its RKHS norm. The theorems, however, rely on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities p and q. Could the authors comment on the relationship between these two notions of smoothness? For instance, does the smoothness of p and q (and p being bounded away from zero) imply the smoothness of the ratio? Discussing this connection would provide a more complete picture.
5.  **Dependence on Dimensionality:** As expected for a density-related estimation problem, the derived convergence rates depend on the dimensionality 'd'. It would be useful for the authors to include a brief discussion on the practical implications of this dependence. For what range of dimensions do the authors expect their method to be most effective?
6.  **Choice of Test Functions for CD-CV:** The CD-CV method for model selection relies on a collection of test functions U. Could the authors provide more rationale for their choice of random linear functions and half-space indicators? While these are reasonable choices, some discussion on what properties make a family of test functions suitable for this validation procedure would be valuable.
7.  **Relation to Broader Approaches:** The general approach of using integral operators and a set of test functions to identify properties of distributions has similarities with other recent work, such as [1]. While the paper's focus on directly estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative via a Fredholm equation is distinct and elegant, a brief discussion situating the work within this broader philosophical approach to learning on distributions could be insightful.

[1] Grünewälder et al., Smooth Operators, ICML 2012.
