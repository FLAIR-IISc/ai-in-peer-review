

The authors derive two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative $f = p/q$ using two key identities:  
(i) $E_p[k(x,\cdot)f] = E_q[k(x,\cdot)]$ and (ii) $E_p[k(x,\cdot)f] \approx q(x)$. Empirical versions replace the integral operators, with $\{k(x,\cdot)\}$ acting as test functions minimizing the discrepancy between sides. This choice enables standard kernel methods for optimization. Convergence guarantees are provided under smoothness and boundedness assumptions, with rates depending critically on the ambient dimension $d$—as expected for density estimation.  

This is a well-executed and timely contribution addressing a relevant problem with rigorous convergence analysis and empirical validation. Key comments:  

1. **Convergence rates**: While optimization over $\{k(x,\cdot)\}$ suffices for convergence in (ii) (intuitively tied to bandwidth dependence), a brief justification for its efficacy in (i) would strengthen the argument.  

2. **Cost function choice**: The paper optimizes over $L_{2,p}$ on page 2 but uses a mix of $L_{2,p}$ and $L_{2,q}$ in (9). Motivation for this specific cost function is needed. Since cost functions often stem from downstream tasks, linking the choice to concrete applications would be valuable.  

3. **Application motivation**: The importance sampling example (where $q/p$ enables transforming $E_q$ to $E_p$) raises a question: the method estimates $E_p$ and $E_q$ to compute the cost function, yet $E_q[1/p]$ could be used directly. Why is $E_p[q/p]$ preferable? The brief mention on page 5 that sampling from $q$ is difficult suggests this is the primary motivation—please clarify if this is the intended use case.  

4. **Smoothness assumptions**: The regularizer enforces smoothness on $f = q/p$, yet Theorem 1 assumes smoothness on $p$ and $q$ (which is natural). Is there a relation between smoothness of $p,q$ and $f$? For instance, smooth $p,q$ (bounded away from zero) typically imply smooth $f$, but the converse may not hold (e.g., $p,q$ can be irregular while $f$ remains smooth). Clarifying this connection would be useful.  

5. **Dimensionality dependence**: Discussing the practical range of $d$ for which the method remains effective would help assess scalability.  

6. **Test function family**: The choice of $\{k(x,\cdot)\}$ as test functions merits deeper justification—why is this family particularly suited for the problem?  

7. **Broader perspective**: For covariate shift settings, a core challenge is designing a generic cost function. The approach of using test functions to extract density ratios is elegant and avoids pitfalls of direct density estimation. While similar in spirit to [1] (which explores transformations to estimate quantities of interest), the authors’ focus on test functions for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is a clear strength. Is this the most principled approach, or could alternative test families yield better guarantees?  

Overall, the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation and compelling experimental results. Addressing the points above would further enhance its impact.
