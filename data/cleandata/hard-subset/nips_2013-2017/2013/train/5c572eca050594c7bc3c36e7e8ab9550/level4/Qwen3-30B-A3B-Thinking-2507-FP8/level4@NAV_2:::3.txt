

The authors propose a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which they solve using regularization techniques within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach leads to a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which generalizes previous methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). The paper establishes theoretical guarantees for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and smooth d-dimensional sub-manifolds, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates. The authors also introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a completely unsupervised model selection method for parameter tuning.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical connections to classical inverse problems in mathematical analysis, which is a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, extending previous work on density ratio estimation (e.g., [9, 10, 23]) by providing convergence rates for distributions supported on manifolds. The CD-CV method addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning and is well-motivated by the authors' theoretical framework. The experimental results show promising performance compared to existing methods like KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE across multiple datasets.

However, the paper would benefit from greater clarity regarding foundational assumptions. The authors do not specify which dominating measure ν is used for density estimation (Lebesgue, counting, or another measure), which is critical for the mathematical rigor of their results. The paper also does not clearly state whether the domain of the random variable x is restricted to R^d or a more general topological space, nor does it explicitly address whether the density p is assumed to be positive everywhere (a necessary condition for the ratio q/p to be well-defined). These omissions make it difficult to assess the generality of their theoretical results, particularly regarding sets of measure zero. While the paper references related work on density estimation (e.g., [12, 4, 11]), it would strengthen its contribution by more explicitly differentiating from previous NIPS papers on similar topics, such as the theoretical analysis of KMM in [26].

In terms of quality, the technical approach is sound and well-justified, though the missing assumptions weaken the theoretical claims. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, though the paper would benefit from a more precise statement of assumptions in the introduction and theoretical sections. The originality is strong, as the Fredholm equation perspective provides a new framework for density ratio estimation that connects machine learning to classical analysis. The significance is high, as density ratio estimation is crucial for covariate shift adaptation and importance sampling, with clear applications in transfer learning.

Strengths: 
- Novel connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm integral equations
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels
- Practical CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection
- Strong experimental validation across multiple datasets

Weaknesses:
- Ambiguity regarding the dominating measure ν
- Unclear domain assumptions (R^d vs. general topological space)
- Missing explicit statement about positivity of density p
- Insufficient differentiation from previous NIPS work on similar topics

Overall, this paper presents a principled and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation with clear practical implications. With clarification of foundational assumptions, it would make a significant contribution to the field.
