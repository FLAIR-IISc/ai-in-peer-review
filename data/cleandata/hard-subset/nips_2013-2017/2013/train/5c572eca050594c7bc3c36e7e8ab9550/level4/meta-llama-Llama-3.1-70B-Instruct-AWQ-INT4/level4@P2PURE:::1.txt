The paper proposes a novel method for estimating the density ratio p/q, a problem that has been addressed by several existing approaches. The key contribution of this work lies in reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

The paper has several strong points. It is well-written and enjoyable to read. The problem of density ratio estimation is important and interesting, making the paper a valuable contribution to the field.

However, there are several areas that require improvement. Some details are missing from the paper, such as the derivation of the equation ∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t). The authors should provide either a proof, a reference, or include this derivation in the supplementary material, even if it is straightforward.

The proposed method can also be applied to density estimation as a special case (q=1). It is noteworthy that this approach does not require the bandwidth to converge to zero, in contrast to standard kernel density estimation (KDE), which is not consistent in this case. A more in-depth discussion of this aspect would be beneficial, as it would help readers understand the distinct roles of the bandwidth parameters in this paper compared to KDE.

A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would be helpful, as this concept may be unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS community.

While the proofs are included in the supplementary material, I have not verified their correctness.

It would be beneficial to include a discussion on why a naive plug-in approach, which involves separately estimating densities p and q, is inferior to the proposed method.

The performance of the proposed method compared to other approaches is unclear. For instance, [12] also derives minimax bounds. Although Corollary 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate, it is uncertain how close these bounds are to the optimal minimax rate.

References for the datasets used in the paper (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be provided.

A demonstration of the proposed method on toy problems with fixed and known p and q would be helpful. For example, a plot of the true p/q and the estimated p/q for 1-dimensional p and q would enable readers to assess the quality of the estimation.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to density ratio estimation. However, its performance compared to other algorithms, such as [12], and its demonstration on toy problems, are unclear.
