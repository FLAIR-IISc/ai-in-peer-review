This paper introduces two novel methods for density ratio estimation, framing both within a regularization framework inspired by Fredholm equations (Type I via importance sampling and L^2 deviation, Type II via a limiting kernel parameter). The authors provide theoretical convergence results alongside experimental validation of their methods' performance.

A key advantage of the proposed approach, differentiating it from existing techniques like KMM, lies in the formulation using an L^2 norm. This allows for the practical and beneficial application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection – a significant contribution given the scarcity of general parameter tuning methods for unsupervised learning problems. It's worth noting that standard objective functions based on Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), as commonly used in KMM, are less suited for CV-based kernel selection due to kernel-dependent objective values hindering comparability. 

Regarding theoretical convergence, the rate presented in Theorem 1, incorporating the (-t/log λ) factor, appears relatively slow for fixed *t*.  Many kernel method analyses achieve polynomial convergence rates with respect to sample size when *t* is fixed, following appropriate scaling of λ. Investigating whether stronger assumptions – potentially regarding the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t}, similar to the work of Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) – could yield such polynomial rates would strengthen the paper.  Exploring the impact of assumptions on q/p in this context is also warranted.

A crucial consideration within density ratio estimation is the typical assumption that q/p resides within a well-behaved function class (e.g., Sobolev space). The authors should acknowledge the inherent strength of this assumption, as it necessitates prior knowledge of the ratio or the tails of the underlying densities *p* and *q*. Furthermore, they should discuss the potential for ill-behaved behavior of p/q when assuming smoothness on q/p.

Finally, while not essential for acceptance, substantial improvements are needed within the supplementary materials. Numerous typos and minor errors currently impede verification of the paper's core theoretical results. For example, Lemma 5 contains errors – W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-\alpha} D2 \|f\|_2^2. Lines 675 and 686 contain incorrect expressions, although Eq.(31) appears correct. Additionally, the integral domain in the Lemma 5 proof at line 1147 requires clarification (\| \xi\|^2 \geq). Given the centrality of these convergence results, a thorough revision of the supplementary materials is highly recommended.

**Reference:**
Caponnetto A., De Vito E. Optimal Rates for Regularized Least-Squares Algorithm. Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 7 331-368 (2007).

In summary, this paper presents a promising new approach to density ratio estimation with the significant benefit of enabling effective parameter selection via cross-validation.
