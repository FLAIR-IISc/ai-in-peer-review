

This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio between two probability distributions. The first approach leverages the importance sampling equation, reformulating it as a regularization problem incorporating an L² norm penalty for deviation from equality and an RKHS regularization term (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that must vanish for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate practical efficacy through experiments.

A key strength of the proposed framework is its compatibility with cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection—a significant advantage over existing methods like KMM. While CV is standard practice in supervised learning, its application to unsupervised problems like density ratio estimation lacks general solutions. The L² formulation enables a principled CV approach, addressing this gap effectively. It would be helpful to explicitly note that objective functions derived from RKHS frameworks (e.g., KMM) are unsuitable for CV, as their values depend on kernel choice and thus are incomparable across different kernels.

The convergence rate in Theorem 1, derived with a fixed parameter $t$, is suboptimal due to the $-t / \log \lambda$ factor. In kernel methods literature (e.g., Caponnetto and De Vito, 2007), polynomial rates in sample size are achievable under standard assumptions on the operator spectrum (e.g., for kernel ridge regression). For the proposed estimator, could similar assumptions on the spectral properties of $K_{p,t}$ or stronger regularity conditions on $q/p$ yield a polynomial rate with fixed $t$? A discussion of such scenarios would strengthen the theoretical contribution, as the current rate appears conservative.

The authors assume $q/p$ belongs to a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev space), which is a strong requirement demanding prior knowledge of the ratio or tail behavior of $p$ and $q$. Crucially, this assumption often implies that the reciprocal ratio $p/q$ behaves poorly. The paper should acknowledge this limitation in density ratio estimation, as it significantly impacts the applicability of the method to real-world scenarios where $p/q$ may lack regularity.

The supplementary material requires substantial revision. Several typographical errors and minor technical inaccuracies undermine confidence in the theoretical results. For instance:
- Lemma 5: $W_2^2$ should be $W_s^2$, and the bound should read $D_1 (t / -\log \lambda)^2 + \lambda^{1-\alpha} D_2 \|f\|_2^2$.
- Expressions at lines 675 and 686 contain errors (while Equation (31) is correct).
- In Lemma 5’s proof, the integral domain at line 1147 must satisfy $\|\xi\|^2 \geq$ [missing bound].

Given that the convergence analysis constitutes a core contribution, the authors must correct these issues before publication. The current supplementary material hinders verification of the main paper’s theoretical claims.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable method for density ratio estimation by enabling CV through an L² formulation. The technical contributions are sound, but addressing the convergence rate discussion, the limitation of smoothness assumptions, and the supplementary material’s errors would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. The proposed approach is a meaningful step toward practical parameter selection in unsupervised density estimation.
