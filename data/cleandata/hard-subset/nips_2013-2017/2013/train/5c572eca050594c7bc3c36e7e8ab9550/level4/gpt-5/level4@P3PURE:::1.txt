This paper proposes a new method for estimating the density ratio p/q. While there are several existing approaches to this problem, the novelty here is to reformulate density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

Strong points:
- The paper is clearly written and engaging.
- The density-ratio estimation problem is important and timely.

Weak points, comments, and questions:
(i) Several technical details are missing. For example, why does ∫ k_t(x, y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t)? Please provide a proof (in the main text or supplementary material) or a citation for this and similar claims, even if they are straightforward.

(ii) As a special case (q = 1), the method can be used for density estimation. It is notable that this approach does not require the bandwidth to converge to zero, whereas standard KDE is not consistent in that regime. It would be helpful to add a discussion clarifying this distinction, so readers understand that the bandwidth parameters here play roles different from those in KDE.

(iii) Please include a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization, as many in the NIPS community may not be familiar with it.

(iv) All proofs are in the supplementary material; I have not verified their correctness.

(v) Please add a discussion of why a naive plug-in approach—separately estimating p and q and then taking their ratio—is inferior to the proposed method.

(vi) The comparative performance against other methods is unclear. For instance, if I recall correctly, [12] also derives minimax bounds. Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate, but it is not clear how close these are to the optimal minimax rates. A comparison or discussion would strengthen the paper.

(vii) Please provide references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) A simple toy demonstration would be valuable. For example, in one dimension with known p and q, plotting the true p/q alongside the estimate would allow readers to visually assess estimation quality.

Overall assessment:
This is a nice paper on density-ratio estimation with an interesting Fredholm integral equation formulation. However, it remains unclear how the method performs relative to existing algorithms (e.g., [12]), and I missed a clear demonstration on toy problems (e.g., simple one-dimensional p and q). Addressing the points above—especially technical justifications, comparative rates, and illustrative experiments—would significantly strengthen the paper.
