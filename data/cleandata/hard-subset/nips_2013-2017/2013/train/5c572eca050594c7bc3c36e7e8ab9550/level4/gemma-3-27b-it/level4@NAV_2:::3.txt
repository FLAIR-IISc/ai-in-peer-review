The authors address the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, where one is a probability density and the other can be an arbitrary function, framing it as an inverse problem using Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques. This approach leads to a family of algorithms, FIRE, for density ratio estimation, accompanied by theoretical analysis and a novel unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV). The work builds on a significant body of research in density ratio estimation, covariate shift adaptation, and kernel methods, citing relevant papers such as [6, 10, 9, 22, 2] and [19, 27].

The core idea of reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is a novel and potentially powerful approach, offering connections to classical operator theory and facilitating theoretical analysis. The resulting FIRE algorithms, leveraging Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), appear flexible and well-suited for implementation. The theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, is a substantial contribution, extending the applicability of these methods to densities defined on R and smooth sub-manifolds.  Furthermore, the CD-CV method provides a practical solution for model selection in an unsupervised setting, a challenging problem in general.

However, the paper lacks clarity regarding fundamental assumptions. The choice of the dominating measure ν for defining densities is not explicitly stated, and this omission raises concerns about the rigor of the results, particularly considering the existence of probability measures without densities with respect to certain measures. A more precise statement of the underlying assumptions—for instance, specifying the domain of x (Rd or a more general space) and the positivity of the density p—is crucial. While the authors acknowledge the connection to Kernel Mean Matching [9], the nuances of the differences and the advantages of FIRE beyond the theoretical analysis are not fully elucidated. Additionally, the experimental evaluation, while extensive, could benefit from a more detailed ablation study focusing on the impact of different design choices within the FIRE framework.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and convergence rates.
*   Practical unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV).
*   Well-defined algorithms (FIRE) with clear implementation details.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity regarding the underlying measure (ν) used for defining densities.
*   Insufficient discussion of the assumptions made regarding the problem setup.
*   Limited exploration of the advantages of FIRE over closely related methods like KMM.
*   Experimental evaluation could benefit from a more focused ablation study.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a technically sound and original contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. While the lack of clarity regarding certain assumptions and a more in-depth comparison to related work are weaknesses, the novel approach, strong theoretical foundation, and practical algorithm make this a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised regarding the choice of measure and assumptions.
