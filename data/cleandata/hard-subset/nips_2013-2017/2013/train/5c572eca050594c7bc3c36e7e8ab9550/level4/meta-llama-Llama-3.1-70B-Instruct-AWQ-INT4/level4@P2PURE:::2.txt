This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio of two probabilities. The first method leverages the importance sampling equation, formulating it as a regularization problem that incorporates the L^2 norm of the deviation from equality and a regularization term based on a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method considers a kernel with a locality parameter that must approach zero for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). The paper presents theoretical results on convergence and demonstrates the practical performance of the proposed methods through experiments.

While the proposed methods share similarities with existing approaches, such as kernel mean matching (KMM), a significant advantage of the proposed methods is that the L^2 formulation enables the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. Unlike supervised learning, where CV is a dominant method for choosing parameters, there is a lack of general methods for unsupervised learning. The paper addresses this issue in the context of density ratio estimation by cleverly utilizing the L^2 norm. Notably, the objective function defined by RKHS, as in KMM, is not suitable for kernel selection via CV, as the objective function values depend on the kernel and are therefore not comparable.

Theorem 1 derives a convergence rate that is slow due to the (-t/log λ) factor, with fixed t. In contrast, many kernel method literatures have shown that, with fixed t, a polynomial rate can be achieved with respect to the sample size after fixing the rate of λ. It would be beneficial to explore reasonable additional assumptions that could lead to a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) made assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to derive the optimal rate for kernel ridge regression. A stronger assumption on q/p may also yield a polynomial rate. A discussion on the polynomial rate under stronger assumptions with fixed t would be more interesting.

In density ratio estimation, it is common to assume that q/p belongs to a nice function class, such as Sobolev. However, this assumption is quite strong, requiring some knowledge of the ratio or tails of p and q. Notably, under such an assumption on q/p, the opposite ratio p/q often behaves poorly. It is essential to include a discussion on this limitation of density ratio estimation.

Although not a requirement, the supplementary materials need improvement. There are numerous typos and minor errors, making it challenging to verify the correctness of the theoretical results in the main paper. For example, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 \|f\|_2^2. The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq.(31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be \|ξ\|^2 ≥. Since the theoretical results on convergence are a primary contribution of this paper, I advise the authors to revise the supplementary material.
