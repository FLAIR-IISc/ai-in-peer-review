- Proposes two new density-ratio estimators: a Type I L^2-RKHS regularized estimator from an importance-sampling identity and a Type II estimator using a shrinking-locality kernel and regularization.  
- Provides theoretical convergence results and empirical evaluations supporting practicality.  
- L^2-based formulation advantage: enables principled cross-validation for kernel/hyperparameter selection, unlike many RKHS objectives.  
- Empirical demonstrations and theory reasonably support the approach’s effectiveness.  
- Request to explicitly state that RKHS objectives like KMM are unsuitable for kernel selection via CV because objective values are not comparable across kernels.  
- Question on Theorem 1’s convergence rate: the (-t / log λ) factor yields slow rates for fixed t; ask whether polynomial rates in sample size can be obtained under additional assumptions.  
- Suggestion to discuss obtaining polynomial rates under stronger assumptions (e.g., spectral assumptions or source conditions on K_{p,t}, as in Caponnetto & De Vito 2007).  
- Need to discuss assumptions on q/p: requiring q/p to lie in a “nice” function class (e.g., Sobolev) is strong and presumes knowledge of tails; also note that p/q may behave poorly under these assumptions.  
- Recommend adding a discussion of practical implications and limitations of function-class assumptions for density-ratio estimation.  
- Supplementary material contains numerous typos/errors that hinder verification of theoretical results and needs careful revision.  
- Specific supplementary corrections: in Lemma 5, W2^2 → Ws^2 and the bound should be D1 (t / -log λ)^2 + λ^{1−α} D2 ||f||_2^2.  
- Specific supplementary corrections: expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect (Eq. (31) is correct).  
- Specific supplementary corrections: in proof of Lemma 5, the integral domain at line 1147 must be ||ξ||^2 ≥ [appropriate bound], not as currently written.  
- Overall assessment: promising approach with clear CV advantage; strengthen the paper by addressing convergence-rate under stronger assumptions, discussing q/p assumption limitations, and thoroughly correcting the supplementary material.
