This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the density ratio q/p, a fundamental task in machine learning with applications in importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The authors' main contribution is to reformulate this estimation problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This principled approach connects the problem to the classical theory of inverse problems and allows for the development of a flexible family of algorithms.

The core idea is to use a kernel function to define an integral operator that relates the unknown density ratio to quantities that can be estimated from data. The paper explores two main settings based on this idea:
1.  **Type I:** Assumes samples from both distributions p and q are available. The problem is formulated as solving the integral equation K_p(f) = K_q(1) for the ratio f = q/p.
2.  **Type II:** Assumes samples from p are available, and the function q can be evaluated at these sample points. This leads to the approximate integral equation K_{t,p}(f) ≈ q.

To solve these ill-posed inverse problems, the authors apply Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), a standard and powerful technique in machine learning. This leads to a family of algorithms named Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), which yield simple, closed-form solutions. The paper also provides a rigorous theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case, deriving concentration bounds and convergence rates on both Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. A significant practical contribution is the proposal of Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), an unsupervised method for model selection. Experiments on several datasets demonstrate the method's effectiveness compared to existing approaches like KMM and LSIF.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Principled Formulation:** The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and novel. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed methods, connecting a common machine learning problem to a well-studied area of classical analysis.
*   **Algorithmic Simplicity:** The resulting FIRE algorithms are straightforward and easy to implement, with explicit solutions derived from the representer theorem. The framework is also flexible, accommodating various norms and loss functions.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is supported by a detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for the proposed estimators. (Note: I have not checked the proofs in the supplementary material but assume they are correct for this review).
*   **Practical Contribution:** The CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is a valuable contribution, as it addresses a challenging and critical aspect of applying such methods in practice.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making the core ideas and their development easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Justification of Key Assumptions:** The clarity of the technical exposition could be improved by providing justification or references for key statements. For example, the approximation `∫ k_t(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)` is foundational to the Type II setting but is stated without proof or citation. Making the paper more self-contained in this regard would strengthen it.

2.  **Comparison to Baselines:** The experimental evaluation would be more compelling if it included a comparison to a naive "plug-in" baseline. This would involve estimating p and q separately using a standard method like Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) and then taking their ratio. Demonstrating the superiority of the proposed direct estimation approach over this simple alternative is crucial.

3.  **Lack of Illustrative Experiments:** While the experiments on real-world datasets are valuable, the paper would greatly benefit from an experiment on a simple, low-dimensional synthetic problem where the ground truth is known. For instance, a plot comparing the true q/p with the estimated ratio for 1D Gaussian distributions would provide a clear and intuitive visualization of the method's performance.

4.  **Relation to Kernel Density Estimation (KDE):** The paper could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how this method relates to classical density estimation, particularly in the special case where q is a uniform distribution (making the problem one of estimating 1/p). The role of the kernel bandwidth `t` is especially interesting, as it appears not to require `t → 0` for consistency, unlike in standard KDE. Clarifying this distinction would offer valuable insight.

5.  **Context for Theoretical Rates:** The paper provides upper bounds on the estimator's convergence rate. To better situate these theoretical results, it would be valuable to compare them to the known minimax optimal rates for this problem (e.g., as discussed in [14]). This would clarify how close the FIRE estimator's performance is to the theoretical optimum.

6.  **Accessibility and Reproducibility:**
    *   A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would make the paper more accessible to the broader NIPS audience, who may not be familiar with inverse problem theory.
    *   For reproducibility, please provide citations or sources for the datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and well-founded approach to density ratio estimation. The connection to Fredholm integral equations is insightful and leads to a family of practical algorithms with theoretical guarantees. Addressing the points above, particularly by adding comparisons to baselines and including illustrative toy experiments, would significantly enhance the paper's impact and completeness.
