

The paper presents a well-motivated approach for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative \(f = p/q\) using kernel-based integral equations. The authors derive two estimators via the equalities \(\mathbb{E}_p[k(x,\cdot)f] = \mathbb{E}_q[k(x,\cdot)]\) and \(\mathbb{E}_p[k(x,\cdot)f] \approx q(x)\), replacing integral operators with empirical counterparts. The choice of \(\{k(x,\cdot)\}\) as test functions enables standard kernel methods for optimization, leveraging the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) framework. Convergence guarantees are established under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on \(p\) and \(q\), with rates scaling with the ambient dimension—a natural dependency for density estimation. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and addresses a relevant problem in covariate shift and importance sampling.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing clear convergence rates that align with established density estimation principles. The empirical validation demonstrates practical utility, and the connection to kernel methods ensures computational feasibility. The focus on minimization over test functions is a clean, principled alternative to ad-hoc approaches.

**Key Comments for Improvement:**  

1. **Convergence Intuition for Estimator (i):**  
   While the convergence of estimator (ii) is intuitive due to bandwidth dependence, the justification for estimator (i) lacks a concise argument. A brief explanation—e.g., how the equality \(\mathbb{E}_p[k(x,\cdot)f] = \mathbb{E}_q[k(x,\cdot)]\) inherently aligns with the target ratio—would strengthen the theoretical foundation.  

2. **Cost Function Justification:**  
   The optimization in Section 2 uses an \(L^2_p\)-norm, but Equation (9) combines \(L^2_p\) and \(L^2_q\). The authors should clarify the rationale for this choice. Since cost functions often depend on downstream tasks (e.g., importance sampling weights), linking the norm selection to specific applications (e.g., minimizing variance in Monte Carlo estimators) would add significant value.  

3. **Application Motivation (Importance Sampling):**  
   The paper cites importance sampling as an application, noting that \(q/p\) enables moving from \(\mathbb{E}_q\) to \(\mathbb{E}_p\). However, the setup requires estimating \(\mathbb{E}_p\) and \(\mathbb{E}_q\) to compute the empirical cost, raising the question: *Why not use \(\mathbb{E}_q\) directly?* The authors mention "sampling from \(\mathbb{E}_q\) is difficult" (p. 5), but this should be explicitly framed as the primary motivation for the indirect approach. Clarifying this trade-off (e.g., "When direct \(\mathbb{E}_q\) sampling is infeasible, the ratio estimate becomes essential") would resolve ambiguity.  

4. **Smoothness Assumptions:**  
   Theorems assume smoothness on \(p\) and \(q\), not the ratio \(f\). It is unclear whether \(p, q\) smoothness implies \(f\) smoothness (e.g., if \(p, q > 0\) and smooth, is \(f\) smooth?). Conversely, does \(f\) smoothness *require* smooth \(p, q\)? A short discussion of this relationship would address potential concerns about the assumptions' necessity.  

5. **Dimensionality and Practical Utility:**  
   The convergence rates depend on dimension \(d\). The manuscript should explicitly state for which \(d\) the method remains practical (e.g., "Effective for \(d \lesssim 10\) under typical bandwidth choices"). This helps readers gauge applicability.  

6. **Test Function Family \(\mathcal{U}\):**  
   The choice of \(\{k(x,\cdot)\}\) as test functions is central but lacks motivation. Why is this family optimal? A brief justification—e.g., "RKHS test functions minimize bias in integral operator estimation under smoothness" —would solidify the approach.  

7. **Broader Context and Related Work:**  
   The method shares conceptual similarities with [1] (Grunewalder et al., *Smooth Operators*), which also uses test functions for density estimation. While the authors note this, they should explicitly contrast their framework: "Unlike [1], which focuses on operator smoothing, our work directly targets the RN derivative via test function minimization, yielding a more direct estimator." This clarifies novelty without overstating.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a timely, strong contribution that advances the theory and practice of RN derivative estimation. The convergence analysis is thorough, and the kernel-based approach is elegant. Addressing the above points—particularly the cost function motivation, application rationale, and smoothness discussion—would elevate the paper from excellent to outstanding. The experimental results support the theory, and the writing is generally clear. Minor polishing of the technical arguments will make the work more accessible to the NIPS audience.  

*Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions addressing the above points).*
