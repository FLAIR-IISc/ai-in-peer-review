This paper proposes a principled framework for estimating density ratios q/p by recasting the task as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are developed:

- Type I: uses the basic importance sampling identity with a general kernel operator Kp and minimizes an L2(p) discrepancy to match Kp(q/p) to Kq1, with an RKHS penalty.
- Type II: uses a “local” kernel (e.g., Gaussian) so that Kt,p(q/p) approximates q as t → 0, again coupled with RKHS regularization.

Both lead to closed-form, easily implemented algorithms (FIRE), with concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and smooth submanifolds. The authors also propose a simple, fully unsupervised model selection procedure, CD-CV, that leverages the L2-based formulation to enable cross-validation using samples from both p and q. Experiments compare FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and simple baselines under covariate shift and related settings.

Relation to prior work: The approach is closely related to KMM, which matches RKHS means Eq[Φ(x)] = Ep[(q/p)Φ(x)], but differs by placing the regression target in L2 and regularizing the function in an RKHS, yielding stability and a natural out-of-sample extension. It is also related to LSIF/KLIEP, which minimize L2(p) or KL divergences using parametric bases; here the inverse-problem view with RKHS regularization avoids basis design and enables spectral analysis.

Strengths:
- Clear and elegant reformulation of density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem, connecting machine learning practice with operator theory.
- Regularized L2 formulations lead to stable estimators with closed-form solutions and straightforward out-of-sample evaluation.
- CD-CV is a practical and compelling contribution: it enables truly unsupervised hyperparameter selection by exploiting samples from both densities, a notable gap in unsupervised/semi-supervised settings.
- Nontrivial theoretical analysis (concentration bounds and rates) for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and manifolds.
- Experiments suggest competitive or superior accuracy and better stability than KMM in some regimes, with lower computational burden.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Convergence rates. As stated (e.g., the factor involving −t/log λ), rates appear slow for fixed kernel width t. In many kernel settings one can obtain polynomial rates in n by appropriately scaling λ (and sometimes t) under standard assumptions. Are there natural additional assumptions (e.g., source conditions on q/p and spectral decay conditions on Kp,t) that yield polynomial rates with fixed t? Results in Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) for kernel ridge regression might provide a template; stronger smoothness assumptions on q/p or capacity assumptions on the operator could clarify minimax-optimal behavior.
- Assumptions on q/p. Practical density ratio estimation often assumes q/p lies in a smooth class (e.g., Sobolev). This is strong: it implicitly requires knowledge of the tails and relative support of p and q, and typically implies that the inverse ratio p/q may be poorly behaved. A brief discussion of this limitation, when it matters in practice, and how FIRE behaves under support mismatch or heavy tails, would be helpful.
- Clarity and correctness of the supplement. The supplementary material contains many typos and minor errors that make verification difficult. Examples include: in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2; the bound should read D1 (t/−log λ)^2 + λ^(1−α) D2 ||f||_2^2; expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect (Eq. (31) is correct); and in the proof of Lemma 5 the domain of integration at line 1147 should be ||ξ||^2 ≥ … Since the theory is a core contribution, a careful revision is important.
- Guidance on choosing Type I vs Type II. The paper notes trade-offs (e.g., Type II does not require sampling from q but introduces kernel approximation error). A short prescriptive section on when to prefer each, and the effect of t on bias-variance, would improve practical usability.

Additional remarks:
- It would be useful to explicitly note why RKHS-norm objectives (as in KMM) are ill-suited for CV-based kernel selection: the objective value itself depends on the kernel defining the norm, making comparisons across kernels meaningless. The L2-based criterion avoids this pitfall.
- A brief discussion of robustness to model misspecification (e.g., finite-sample instability when q/p is large on low-probability regions under p) and practical safeguards (thresholding, clipping) would be valuable.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The methodology is technically sound and well-justified; empirical results are encouraging. Some theoretical statements and proofs need clearer exposition and correction in the supplement.
- Clarity: The main paper is generally clear; however, notation is heavy and the supplement needs significant editing to ensure correctness.
- Originality: Recasting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem with RKHS regularization, together with CD-CV for unsupervised model selection, is novel and insightful relative to KMM/LSIF.
- Significance: Density ratio estimation is central to covariate shift and importance sampling. The proposed framework and CD-CV are likely to influence practice and follow-on work.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled inverse-problem formulation with clear algorithmic instantiations.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) addressing a real gap.
- Theoretical guarantees on Euclidean and manifold domains.
- Competitive empirical performance and computational simplicity.

Cons for acceptance:
- Convergence rates appear slow for fixed t; lack of discussion of stronger assumptions yielding polynomial rates.
- Strong smoothness/regularity assumptions on q/p and limited discussion of their practical implications.
- Numerous errors in the supplementary material hinder verification.

Recommendation: A strong, well-motivated contribution. With corrections to the supplement and a discussion of conditions for faster rates and limitations of the q/p assumption, this work would be a valuable addition to the literature on density ratio estimation and covariate shift.

Reference:
