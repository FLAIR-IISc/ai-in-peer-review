This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q/p, a quantity crucial for applications such as importance sampling and covariate shift correction in transfer learning. The central and novel contribution is to reframe this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. By representing quantities like E_q[k(x,y)] as integral operators acting on the unknown ratio function, the authors derive two formulations (Type I and Type II) depending on whether samples from q or function values of q are available. Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) is then applied to solve these ill-posed integral equations, leading to a family of algorithms named Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). This approach connects the problem to classical operator theory and allows for a detailed theoretical analysis of convergence rates. The paper also introduces a practical and fully unsupervised method for model selection, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). The proposed methods are evaluated empirically and compared to related work such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Funtion (LSIF).

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and provides a new perspective that connects this machine learning problem to a rich body of work in classical analysis. This principled framework is a significant contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The derivation from the initial integral equations to the final, implementable kernel-based algorithms is clear and easy to follow.
*   **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators under different assumptions on the data domain (Euclidean space and manifolds).
*   **Practical Utility:** The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection addresses a critical and often difficult aspect of applying such methods in practice. The experimental results demonstrate strong performance compared to existing techniques.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** Some technical statements are presented without sufficient justification or reference, which could hinder reproducibility and reader understanding. For instance:
    *   The claim that ∫ k_t(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) for δ-kernels should be accompanied by a brief proof sketch or a precise citation (e.g., to [24] in the paper's references, but with a specific chapter/theorem).
    *   A brief, high-level explanation of Tikhonov regularization could benefit readers in the NIPS community who may not be familiar with its use for inverse problems.
    *   References for the datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be provided.

2.  **Contextual Comparison:** The paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of its relationship to simpler or more standard statistical approaches.
    *   A discussion on why directly estimating p and q via kernel density estimation (KDE) and then taking their ratio (the naive "plug-in" approach) is suboptimal compared to the proposed direct estimation method would be very insightful.
    *   In the special case where q is a uniform distribution (or q=1 in the Type II setting), the method can be viewed as a form of density estimation for p. It would be helpful to discuss how the role of the kernel bandwidth `t` in this framework differs from its role in standard KDE, where the bandwidth must converge to zero for consistency.

3.  **Scope of Evaluation:**
    *   The theoretical analysis provides convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4), but it is not clear how these rates compare to the optimal minimax rates for this problem (e.g., as discussed in [14]). Placing the theoretical results in this broader context would better establish the method's significance.
    *   The empirical evaluation is solid, but it would benefit greatly from a simple, illustrative toy example. For instance, a plot showing the true ratio and the estimated ratio for known one-dimensional densities p and q would provide valuable intuition about the quality of the estimation and the behavior of the algorithm.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and principled framework for density ratio estimation. It establishes a valuable connection to classical inverse problem theory, derives simple and flexible algorithms, provides a strong theoretical analysis, and includes a practical solution for unsupervised model selection.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The evaluation, both theoretical and empirical, could be better contextualized. A comparison to minimax optimal rates is missing, and the lack of a simple illustrative experiment makes it harder to build intuition for the method's performance. Several minor but important details regarding technical claims and experimental setup need clarification.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting an interesting and significant contribution to an important problem. The weaknesses identified are largely related to presentation and the scope of the evaluation, and I believe they can be addressed in a revision.
