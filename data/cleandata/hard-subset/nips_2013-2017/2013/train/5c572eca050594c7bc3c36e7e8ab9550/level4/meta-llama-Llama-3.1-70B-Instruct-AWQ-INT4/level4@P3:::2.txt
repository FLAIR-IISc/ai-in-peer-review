This paper introduces a novel approach to estimating the density ratio of two probabilities, leveraging the Fredholm equation of the first kind. The authors propose two methods, Type I and Type II, which formulate the problem as a regularization issue. The Type I method uses the importance sampling equation, while the Type II method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that approaches zero for consistency. Both methods are expressed in the form of regularization problems.

The paper presents theoretical results on convergence and demonstrates the practical performance of the proposed methods experimentally. The approach has similarities to existing methods, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), but offers a significant advantage in that the L^2 formulation enables the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. This is particularly valuable in unsupervised learning, where general methods for choosing parameters are limited.

A notable aspect of the paper is the use of the L^2 norm, which allows for the application of CV. In contrast, objective functions given by Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), such as in KMM, are not suitable for CV due to their dependence on the kernel.

The authors provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel. However, the convergence rate derived in Theorem 1 is slow with fixed t. It would be interesting to explore additional assumptions that could lead to a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator. For instance, making stronger assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} or on the ratio q/p might yield a polynomial rate.

The paper assumes that the ratio q/p belongs to a nice function class, such as Sobolev. While this assumption is common in density ratio estimation, it is essential to acknowledge its limitations. Specifically, this assumption requires knowledge of the ratio or tails of p and q, and the opposite ratio p/q may behave poorly. A discussion of these limitations would be a valuable addition to the paper.

The supplementary materials require improvement, as they contain typos and minor mistakes that make it challenging to verify the correctness of the theoretical results. The authors should revise the supplementary material to ensure the accuracy and clarity of the theoretical contributions.

In conclusion, the paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation, offering a significant advantage in the application of cross-validation for parameter selection. While the theoretical analysis is thorough, additional assumptions could lead to improved convergence rates. The authors should also acknowledge the limitations of the assumptions made in the paper and revise the supplementary materials to ensure their accuracy.
