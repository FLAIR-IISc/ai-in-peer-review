The paper proposes a novel method for estimating the density ratio p/q, reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. This approach distinguishes itself from existing methods, which also tackle the same problem.

The paper has several strong points. It is well-written and enjoyable to read. The density ratio estimation problem is both important and interesting, making it a compelling topic.

However, there are some areas that require improvement. Firstly, some details are missing from the paper, such as a proof or reference for the statement \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t). Including either a proof in the paper or supplementary material, or providing a reference, would strengthen the argument.

Another point worth discussing is the special case where q=1, which allows this method to be used for density estimation. Notably, this approach does not require the bandwidth to converge to zero, unlike standard kernel density estimation (KDE), which is inconsistent in this case. A more in-depth discussion on the role of bandwidth parameters in this paper, compared to KDE, would provide clarity for readers.

A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would also be beneficial, as it may be unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS community.

All proofs are provided in the supplementary material, but their correctness has not been verified.

It would be helpful to include a few sentences explaining why a naive plug-in approach, which involves separately estimating densities p and q, is inferior to the proposed method.

Furthermore, the paper does not provide a clear comparison of the proposed method's performance to other existing methods. For instance, [12] also derives minimax bounds. While Corollary 2 and 4 offer upper bounds on the convergence rate, it is unclear how close these bounds are to the optimal minimax rate.

References for the datasets used in the paper (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be provided.

A demonstration of the proposed method's performance on toy problems with fixed and known p and q would be beneficial. For example, a plot of the true p/q and estimated p/q for 1-dimensional p and q could help readers assess the quality of the estimation.

In conclusion, the paper presents a nice approach to density ratio estimation. However, its performance compared to other algorithms, such as [12], is unclear, and a demonstration of its effectiveness on toy problems is lacking.
