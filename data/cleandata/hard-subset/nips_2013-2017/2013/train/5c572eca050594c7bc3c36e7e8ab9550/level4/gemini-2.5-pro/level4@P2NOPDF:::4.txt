This is a well-executed and timely paper that addresses the important problem of density ratio estimation. The proposed kernel-based approach is elegant, and the work is supported by both a theoretical convergence analysis and compelling experimental results.

### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative (or density ratio) f = p/q. The core idea is to frame the estimation problem as finding a function f that satisfies one of two equalities: (i) E_p[k(x,·)f] = E_q[k(x,·)] or (ii) E_p[k(x,·)f] = q(x). The authors leverage the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) framework by using the family of kernel functions {k(x,·)} as a set of test functions. The objective is to minimize the discrepancy between the left and right sides of these equalities, where the expectations are replaced by their empirical estimates. This formulation has the distinct advantage of allowing the use of standard kernel-based optimization techniques.

The paper provides a theoretical analysis, establishing convergence guarantees for both estimators under standard smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities p and q. As expected for a non-parametric estimation problem, the derived convergence rates exhibit a strong dependence on the dimensionality of the data space.

### Comments and Questions for the Authors

The work is strong, but the following points could be clarified to further improve the paper:

1.  **Intuition for Convergence in Setting (i):** The paper demonstrates that optimizing over the set of test functions {k(x,·)} is sufficient to guarantee convergence. While this is intuitive for estimator (ii), perhaps due to its connection to kernel density estimation, a brief, intuitive explanation for why this also holds for estimator (i) would strengthen the paper's clarity.

2.  **Motivation for the Choice of Cost Function:** The authors initially optimize the objective in the L2,p norm, but later, in equation (9), the analysis involves a combination of L2,p and L2,q norms. A clearer motivation for this choice of cost function would be beneficial. Since the optimal choice of a loss function is often task-dependent, it would be valuable to connect these specific norm choices to the requirements of key applications like importance sampling or covariate shift.

3.  **Clarification on the Importance Sampling Application:** Regarding the application of importance sampling (estimating an expectation under p using samples from q), a potential conceptual question arises. The proposed method requires samples from both p and q to construct the empirical cost function and estimate the ratio f = p/q. However, if samples from p are readily available to build the empirical estimate E_p, what is the advantage of first estimating p/q to then compute an importance-weighted estimate of the same expectation? The paper briefly mentions on page 5 that sampling from p might be difficult. It would be helpful to expand on this motivation. Is the primary use case one where we have a small, fixed set of samples from p, but can generate many more from q?

4.  **Relationship Between Smoothness of Densities and Their Ratio:** There appears to be a disconnect between the regularization strategy and the theoretical assumptions. The regularizer enforces smoothness on the ratio f = p/q itself. However, the convergence theorems rely on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities p and q. It would be insightful to discuss the relationship between these two notions of smoothness. For instance, do smooth p and q (bounded away from zero) guarantee a smooth ratio? A brief discussion on this point would better connect the practical algorithm design with its theoretical underpinnings.

5.  **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The derived convergence rates have a clear dependence on the data dimensionality, d. Given the challenges of the curse of dimensionality in non-parametric estimation, it would be valuable for the authors to discuss the practical implications of this dependence. For what range of dimensions do the authors expect their method to be most effective?

6.  **Motivation for the Family of Test Functions U:** Could the authors provide additional motivation for the specific choice of the family of test functions, U? While the RKHS framework is well-justified, the reasoning behind the specific construction of U could be elaborated upon.

7.  **Positioning the Approach in a Broader Context:** On a more general note, a central challenge in problems like covariate shift is defining a suitable, generic cost function. The approach of using integral probability metrics, which test the equality of expectations over a class of functions, is a powerful paradigm. In this context, is there a principled reason to believe that the RKHS functions {k(x,·)} constitute the "right" or optimal class of test functions for density ratio estimation? The overall strategy bears some resemblance to the work of Grünewälder et al. [1], which also manipulates expectation operators. While the authors' use of test functions is arguably more direct for this specific problem, a brief comparison or positioning of this work within that broader context would be welcome.

In summary, this is a strong contribution to an important area. Addressing the points above would further enhance its impact and clarity.
