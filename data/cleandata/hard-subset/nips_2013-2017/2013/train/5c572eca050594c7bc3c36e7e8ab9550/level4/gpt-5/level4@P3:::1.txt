Summary
This paper addresses density ratio estimation, i.e., estimating r(x) = q(x)/p(x), by recasting the task as an inverse problem for an integral operator (a Fredholm equation of the first kind). The authors develop a family of regularized RKHS-based estimators (FIRE), with two main formulations: Type I uses the identity Kp(r) = Kq(1) and Type II uses approximate δ-kernels to obtain Kt,p(r) ≈ q, then solves the ill-posed inverse problem via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. The framework yields closed-form solutions via the representer theorem and allows a spectral analysis of error, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and smooth manifolds. The paper also proposes a practical, unsupervised model selection strategy (CD-CV) that uses importance-sampling identities with random test functions, and reports experiments under covariate shift, comparing FIRE to LSIF, KMM (where applicable), and a KDE-based baseline.

Relation to prior work: The formulation connects kernel mean matching (KMM) to a Fredholm equation in H (showing KMM as a special case when optimizing an RKHS discrepancy without explicit regularization) and contrasts with LSIF/KLIEP, which fit parametric function classes by minimizing L2,p or KL. The paper also situates the analysis within operator-theoretic approaches to inverse problems and kernel regularization.

Strengths
- Clear, principled reformulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, with a unified RKHS-regularized treatment that yields simple, closed-form algorithms.
- Theoretical guarantees include concentration inequalities and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds; this is valuable and relatively rare in density ratio work.
- CD-CV is a practical, unsupervised model selection mechanism that leverages the availability of samples from both p and q and is easy to implement.
- The presentation is generally well organized and the connection to KMM is elucidating.

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) Justification of approximate identity: The statement ∫ kt(x, y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) for δ-kernels (e.g., Gaussian) should be stated precisely with assumptions on f and the domain (e.g., f ∈ W2 2(Ω) or C2 with bounded derivatives), and the order term specified. While the text cites Tsybakov (Ch. 1), please add a short lemma with conditions, a brief proof sketch, and a precise reference (e.g., standard results on mollifiers/approximate identities or heat semigroup expansions).

2) Special case q ≡ 1 (density estimation): Clarify how FIRE reduces to density estimation, and how the kernel bandwidth plays a different role than in KDE. In Type II, an O(t) (or O(t1−ε) on manifolds) approximation error appears, suggesting t must shrink with n; in Type I, the kernel in Kp need not be a δ-kernel at all. A brief discussion contrasting bias/variance control via regularization in FIRE versus bandwidth in KDE would help, including whether consistency requires t → 0 in each setting.

3) Brief primer on Tikhonov regularization: Many readers may benefit from a short paragraph giving intuition for Tikhonov regularization in inverse problems (ill-posedness, stability, bias-variance tradeoff), and how it interacts with RKHS norms in this context.

4) Proofs and assumptions: All proofs are in the supplement; please ensure the main paper states the key assumptions (smoothness classes, boundedness of p and q, manifold regularity, boundary conditions) alongside each theorem, and briefly explain the role of each term in the rates (approximation, regularization bias, stochastic error).

5) Why not plug-in KDE? Include a short analysis or experiment showing why r̂ = q̂/p̂ is inferior (e.g., high variance where p is small, instability of division, difficulty enforcing positivity/normalization), and how direct estimation with regularization improves stability. A toy 1D example would be persuasive.

6) Optimality of rates: Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds. Please relate these to known minimax lower bounds for likelihood/density ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen, Wainwright, Jordan, NIPS 2007) to position FIRE’s rates relative to optimal rates under comparable smoothness/dimension assumptions.

7) Experimental baselines and scope: The empirical comparison to LSIF and KMM could be strengthened. Where KMM lacks out-of-sample extension, consider reporting in-sample results or including regularized KMM variants. Report computational complexity and wall-clock time for each method, since FIRE involves solving linear systems with kernel matrices.

8) Positivity and normalization: Since r(x) is nonnegative, discuss whether FIRE enforces r ≥ 0 (it appears unconstrained). If not, comment on the practical impact and whether simple post-processing (e.g., clipping) or constrained variants are feasible.

9) Datasets and references: Please provide citations for CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS, and give brief dataset descriptions (size, dimensionality) in the main text.

10) Toy demonstrations: Add simple 1D (or low-dimensional) examples with known p and q, plotting the true r and r̂ from FIRE, LSIF, and KDE plug-in, to help readers visually assess bias/variance and boundary behavior.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears solid, with an appropriate regularization framework and clear operator-theoretic grounding. The main missing pieces are more explicit assumptions in the main text and discussion of positivity/constraints. The experimental validation is encouraging but could be broader and include toy ground-truth cases and stronger baselines.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. It would benefit from: a concise Tikhonov primer; a sharper statement of the approximate-identity lemma; explicit assumptions in theorem statements; and clearer guidance on the roles of t and λ in Type I vs. Type II.

Originality
- Recasting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem and unifying several methods under an RKHS-Tikhonov lens is novel and insightful, even if related ideas exist in KMM/LSIF and inverse problems literature.

Significance
- The approach is broadly applicable (importance sampling, covariate shift), theoretically grounded, and practically implementable, with a useful unsupervised model selection strategy.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled inverse-problem formulation with simple algorithms and explicit solutions.
- Nontrivial theoretical analysis on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection (CD-CV).
- Clear connections to and extensions of existing kernel methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical comparisons and absence of toy ground-truth demonstrations.
- Unclear optimality of rates relative to minimax bounds.
- Missing details on positivity constraints, assumptions, and the role of bandwidth vs. regularization.
- Some statements (approximate identity) need firmer justification in the main text.

Overall, this is a strong and interesting submission on density ratio estimation. Addressing the points above—especially (i)–(ii), (5)–(7), and (10)—would considerably strengthen the paper.
