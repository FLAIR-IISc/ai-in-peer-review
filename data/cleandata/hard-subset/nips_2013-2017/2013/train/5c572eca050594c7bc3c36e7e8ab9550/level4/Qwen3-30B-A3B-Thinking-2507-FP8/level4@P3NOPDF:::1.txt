

The paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. This perspective offers a fresh theoretical foundation for an important and well-motivated problem. The manuscript is well-written and clearly structured, making it a pleasure to read. The core idea—leveraging integral equations for density ratio estimation—holds promise, particularly given the method's unique behavior in density estimation as a special case (when $q=1$).

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical contribution is significant, and the exposition is generally clear and accessible. The connection to density estimation (without requiring bandwidth convergence to zero) is noteworthy and merits further exploration, as it contrasts sharply with standard KDE consistency requirements.

**Major Weaknesses and Required Revisions:**  
1.  **Missing Justifications:** Key technical claims lack supporting details. Specifically, the assertion that $\int k_t(x,y)f(x)\,dx = f(y) + O(t)$ requires either a proof (in main text or supplementary) or a precise reference. Such foundational steps are essential for reproducibility and reader confidence.  
2.  **Clarification on Bandwidth Role:** The special case $q=1$ (density estimation) is a compelling point. However, the manuscript does not sufficiently explain how the bandwidth parameters in the proposed method differ from those in standard KDE. A concise discussion clarifying this distinction—especially why the method avoids the bandwidth convergence requirement that breaks KDE consistency—is critical for readers to appreciate the novelty.  
3.  **Tikhonov Regularization:** A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization (e.g., its purpose and relevance to the solution of the integral equation) would greatly benefit the NIPS audience, many of whom may not be familiar with this technique in this context.  
4.  **Comparative Analysis:** The paper lacks a meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art methods. While Corollary 2 provides an upper bound on convergence rate, it is unclear how this compares to the minimax optimal rate established in [12] (or others). The authors should explicitly address:  
    - How their rate compares to known minimax lower bounds.  
    - Why their approach outperforms naive plug-in density estimation (separately estimating $p$ and $q$), which is known to be suboptimal for ratio estimation. A short theoretical or empirical justification is needed.  
5.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper omits a crucial demonstration on simple, known densities (e.g., 1D Gaussian mixtures). A plot showing the true $p(x)/q(x)$ versus the estimated ratio for a toy example would significantly aid readers in assessing the method’s practical efficacy. The absence of such a visualization is a notable gap.  
6.  **Dataset References:** Citations for datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) are missing. Providing standard references for these benchmark datasets is necessary for reproducibility.  
7.  **Proof Verification:** All proofs are confined to the supplementary material. While this is acceptable, the reviewer notes that the absence of main-text proofs (and the lack of verification of supplementary proofs) may hinder assessment of the technical soundness.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a promising contribution to density ratio estimation with a novel theoretical framework. The core idea is compelling, and the potential implications for density estimation are intriguing. However, the current manuscript requires substantial revisions to address the gaps outlined above. The most urgent needs are: (a) clarifying the bandwidth behavior in the density estimation special case, (b) providing a comparative analysis against existing methods (including minimax rates and naive plug-in), and (c) including a toy example demonstrating the method’s performance on simple, known distributions. Without these additions, the significance and practical utility of the proposed method remain unclear to the NIPS audience.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by integrating these elements. As it stands, the technical contribution is interesting but its full impact is obscured by insufficient detail and comparison. With the requested revisions, this work could become a valuable addition to the literature.
