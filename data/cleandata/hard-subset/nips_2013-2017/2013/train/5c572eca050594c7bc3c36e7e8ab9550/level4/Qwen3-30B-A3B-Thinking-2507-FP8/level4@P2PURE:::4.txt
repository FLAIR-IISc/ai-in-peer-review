

The authors derive two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative $f = p/q$ using the equalities: (i) $E_p[k(x, \cdot) f] = E_q[k(x, \cdot)]$ and (ii) $E_p[k(x, \cdot) f] \approx q(x)$. These integral operators are replaced by empirical counterparts, where $\{k(x, \cdot)\}$ serves as a family of test functions minimizing the discrepancy between the two sides. This choice enables the use of standard kernel methods for optimization. Convergence guarantees are provided for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, with the convergence rate critically dependent on the underlying space dimension—consistent with expectations for density estimation.  

The work is well-executed and addresses a relevant problem. Key comments:  
- For convergence rates: Optimizing over $\{k(x, \cdot)\}$ suffices to ensure convergence to the ratio. While intuitive for (ii) due to bandwidth dependence, a concise argument for (i) would strengthen the presentation.  
- Cost function motivation: The optimization on page 2 uses $L^2_p$, but Equation (9) combines $L^2_p$ and $L^2_q$. A justification linking the cost function to specific applications (e.g., task-dependent objectives) would be valuable.  
- Application context: The importance sampling example (where $q/p$ enables moving from $E_q$ to $E_p$) raises a question: Since the method estimates $E_p$ and $E_q$ to construct the empirical cost, why not directly use $E_q$? The rationale on page 5—that sampling from $E_q$ is difficult—appears central; elaborating on this as the primary application context would clarify the setup.  
- Smoothness relation: The regularizer enforces smoothness on $q/p$, yet Theorem 1 assumes smoothness on $q$ and $p$ (natural). It is unclear whether smoothness of $q/p$ implies smoothness of $p$ and $q$ (or vice versa), given that many $p,q$ pairs yield the same ratio (e.g., via modifications on countable/uncountable sets).  
- Dimension dependence: A discussion of the practical dimensionality regime for which the method remains effective would be beneficial.  
- Test function family $\mathcal{U}$: The rationale for selecting $\{k(x, \cdot)\}$ as the test function family warrants further motivation.  
- Methodological perspective: For covariate shift and related settings, a key challenge is designing generic cost functions. The authors’ approach via test functions aligns with [1] (e.g., in transforming expectation operators to extract densities), though their treatment of the Radon-Nikodym derivative is more direct. It would be insightful to discuss whether $\{k(x, \cdot)\}$ represents the "optimal" choice, given this similarity.  

[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor.  

The paper is timely, strong, and provides a convergence analysis alongside experimental validation.
