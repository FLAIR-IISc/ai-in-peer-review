This paper proposes two novel methods for density ratio estimation. The first method formulates the importance sampling identity as a regularization problem combining the L2 norm of the deviation with an RKHS regularization term, resulting in a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method utilizes a kernel with a locality parameter that must converge to zero to ensure consistency, and is also cast as a regularization problem (a Type II Fredholm equation). The authors provide theoretical convergence results and experimental validation for their proposed methods.

A key advantage of this work, despite its similarities to existing methods such as KMM, is that the L2 formulation enables the use of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. While CV is a standard tool in supervised learning, principled and general methods for parameter tuning in unsupervised settings are scarce. The paper's clever use of the L2 norm provides a valuable solution to this problem for density ratio estimation.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

1.  It would strengthen the paper to explicitly state that objective functions based on an RKHS norm, such as in KMM, are ill-suited for model selection via CV. This is because the objective function's value is dependent on the choice of kernel, making comparisons between different kernels meaningless.

2.  The convergence rate derived in Theorem 1, which includes a (-t/log λ) factor, is logarithmic and thus slow for a fixed t. In the kernel methods literature, it is common to derive polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size (for a fixed t and an appropriately chosen λ). Could the authors establish a polynomial rate for their estimator under reasonable additional assumptions? For instance, following Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) who made assumptions on the spectrum of the kernel operator to derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression, it seems likely that stronger regularity assumptions on the true density ratio q/p could yield a polynomial rate. A discussion of this possibility would significantly enhance the paper's theoretical contribution.

3.  Density ratio estimation methods typically require the assumption that the true ratio q/p belongs to a well-behaved function class, such as a Sobolev space. It is important to acknowledge that this can be a strong assumption, implying specific knowledge about the tail behavior of p and q. Furthermore, under this assumption, the inverse ratio p/q is often ill-behaved. The paper should include a discussion of these inherent limitations of the density ratio estimation problem.

4.  The supplementary material requires significant revision. It contains numerous typos and minor errors that hinder the verification of the main paper's theoretical results. For example:
    *   In Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound appears to be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^(1-α) D2 ||f||_2^2.
    *   The expressions on lines 675 and 686 are incorrect (though Eq. 31 is correct).
    *   In the proof of Lemma 5, the integration domain on line 1147 should be ||ξ||^2 ≥ T.
    Given that the convergence analysis is a central contribution of this work, I strongly advise the authors to thoroughly proofread and correct the supplementary material.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel and valuable approach to density ratio estimation. Its primary strength lies in proposing a framework where cross-validation can be effectively applied for parameter selection.
