This paper introduces a new approach to estimating the density ratio p/q. While several methods have been proposed for this task, the central novelty here is to cast density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and solve it via regularization in RKHS.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written and engaging.
- The density-ratio estimation problem is both important and interesting.

Weaknesses, comments, and questions:
(i) Some technical steps lack detail. For instance, why does ∫ k_t(x, y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) hold? Even if straightforward, please provide a proof (in the paper or supplement) or a precise citation for such claims.

(ii) As a special case (q = 1), the method applies to density estimation. It is noteworthy that this approach does not require the bandwidth to shrink to zero, whereas standard KDE is inconsistent under a fixed bandwidth. Please expand the discussion to clarify how the bandwidths in this framework play a role different from KDE.

(iii) Consider adding a brief primer on Tikhonov regularization, as many in the NIPS audience may not be familiar with it.

(iv) All proofs are deferred to the supplement; I have not verified their correctness.

(v) Please explain why a naive plug-in strategy—separately estimating p and q and then taking their ratio—performs worse than the proposed method.

(vi) The comparative performance against alternative methods is unclear. If I recall correctly, [12] derives minimax rates. Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds, but it is not clear how close these are to the optimal minimax rate.

(vii) Please add references for the datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) I missed a toy demonstration with known p and q. For example, in one dimension, plotting the true p/q alongside the estimate would help readers gauge estimation quality.

Overall, this is a solid paper on density-ratio estimation. However, I am uncertain how it compares empirically and theoretically with existing algorithms (e.g., [12]), and I would have liked to see simple 1D toy examples with known p and q to illustrate the method.
