The authors develop two estimators of the Radon–Nikodym derivative f = p/q using the identities:
(i) E_p[k(x, ·) f(·)] = E_q[k(x, ·)]
(ii) E_p[k(x, ·) f(·)] ≈ q(x) (via the kernel density estimation limit as bandwidth shrinks).
They replace expectations with empirical averages and view {k(x, ·)} as a family of test functions over which they minimize the discrepancy between the two sides. Choosing kernel sections as test functions enables the use of standard kernel machinery for optimization. The paper provides convergence guarantees under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, with rates that—unsurprisingly for density estimation—deteriorate with dimension. Overall, this is a well-executed and timely contribution.

Comments and questions:
- Convergence intuition for (i). Consistency for (ii) is intuitive through the bandwidth dependence and the kernel density estimation limit. For (i), a brief intuition for why optimizing over the family {k(x, ·)} suffices for recovering the ratio would be helpful. For instance, does this hinge on the kernel being characteristic/universal so that matching k(x, ·)-moments implies matching against a dense set of test functions in L2,p?

- Choice of objective. On page 2, the optimization is posed in L2,p, while equation (9) later mixes L2,p and L2,q. Please motivate this cost: why this particular combination, and how does it relate to downstream tasks? It would strengthen the paper to explicitly connect each loss to common applications (e.g., covariate shift correction, off-policy evaluation), explaining which risk is most appropriate in each case.

- Applications and importance sampling. Importance sampling requires either p/q or q/p depending on direction (to estimate E_p from q-samples, one needs p/q; to estimate E_q from p-samples, one needs q/p). Since your empirical objective estimates expectations under p and q to construct the estimator, why not use the directly available expectation (e.g., E_q) in those steps? Under what conditions is E_p[(q/p)·g] preferable to E_q[g] computed from the same samples? Page 5 mentions difficulty sampling under E_q—if this is the main use case, it would be helpful to state it clearly upfront, and to delineate when each estimator is advantageous.

- Regularization vs. assumptions. You regularize the ratio to enforce smoothness, but your theorems assume smoothness of p and q themselves. What is the relationship? For example, under what conditions (e.g., q bounded away from zero) does smoothness of p and q imply smoothness of p/q, and vice versa? Clarifying this would help reconcile the regularizer with the stated assumptions.

- Dimensional dependence. Please discuss the dependence on ambient dimension d more concretely: for what ranges of d and sample sizes do you expect the method to be practical? Any guidance (theoretical or empirical) on kernel bandwidth/regularization choices as d grows would be useful.

- Test function family U. Beyond computational convenience, is there a conceptual motivation for U = {k(x, ·)} (e.g., characteristic kernels yielding identification; control of integral probability metrics; RKHS density in L2)? Are there scenarios where alternative test function classes might be preferable?

- Broader perspective. The approach fits within a general “match expectations over test functions to identify latent objects” paradigm. Do you view {k(x, ·)} as the “right” choice for estimating Radon–Nikodym derivatives in this setting, or one among several viable options (e.g., via Stein operators, other integral transforms)? It would be useful to articulate why the kernel test-function route is preferable here. There also seems to be conceptual overlap with “Smooth Operators” (Grünewälder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor), where transformations are used to access target quantities; your method is arguably cleaner by working directly with test functions. A short discussion of similarities and differences would provide helpful context.

In sum, the paper addresses a relevant problem with a clear formulation, solid theory, and convincing experiments. With additional intuition for the (i) estimator, clearer motivation for the objectives and applications, and a discussion of dimensional scaling and test-function design, it would be even stronger.
