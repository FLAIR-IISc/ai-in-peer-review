This paper proposes a kernel-based framework for estimating the density ratio q/p by reformulating the task as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two variants are developed. Type I leverages the importance sampling identity Eq[k(x,·)] = Ep[(q/p)(x) k(x,·)], yielding an inverse problem for q/p when samples from both p and q are available. Type II uses a “local” kernel kt so that Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q, giving an approximate inverse problem when q is known (or can be evaluated) at sample points from p. Both lead to closed-form, out-of-sample estimators (FIRE) via the representer theorem. The authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth submanifolds, and introduce an unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, based on validating the estimated ratio through importance sampling identities with test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, including under covariate shift.

Relation to prior work: The approach is closely related to KMM, which enforces Eq(Φ) = Ep[(q/p)Φ] in an RKHS, but differs by (i) directly regularizing the weight function in an RKHS (stabilizing the problem and enabling out-of-sample prediction), and (ii) adopting an L2-type loss that supports unsupervised cross-validation. It is also related to LSIF/KLIEP, which minimize L2,p or KL criteria for parametric function classes, but here the inverse problem formulation and regularization yield nonparametric kernel estimators with theoretical guarantees.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem with RKHS regularization is elegant and unifies several ideas from operator theory and kernel learning.
- Practicality: The resulting FIRE estimators are simple, closed-form, and have a natural out-of-sample extension, unlike KMM’s empirical optimization over sample weights.
- Model selection: CD-CV is a thoughtful and useful unsupervised validation mechanism derived from the importance sampling identity, addressing a core challenge in unsupervised/semi-supervised settings.
- Theory: The paper provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, with clear statements and assumptions.
- Empirical results: Competitive performance across density shift scenarios, with computational advantages over alternatives.

Weaknesses and questions
- Convergence rates with fixed bandwidth: In Theorem 1, due to the (-t/log λ) factor, the derived rate is slow for fixed t. In much of the kernel literature, polynomial rates in n can be established with fixed t by coupling λ to n under spectral/source conditions. Are there reasonable additional assumptions (e.g., on the spectrum of Kp,t or source smoothness conditions on q/p) under which the proposed estimators achieve polynomial rates? Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression by assuming eigenvalue decay and source conditions; analogous assumptions here, possibly with stronger regularity on q/p, may yield sharper rates. It would substantially strengthen the paper to discuss such rates under standard stronger assumptions with fixed t.
- Assumptions on q/p: The theory assumes q/p lies in a “nice” function class (e.g., Sobolev). This requirement is strong in practice, as it implies delicate control of the ratio and of the tails of p and q. Often, under such conditions on q/p, the reciprocal p/q behaves poorly. It would be important to discuss this limitation, including conditions ensuring boundedness or integrability of the ratio, robustness when the ratio is unbounded or heavy-tailed, and practical safeguards (e.g., clipping).
- Cross-validation criterion: It would help to explicitly note that an RKHS-norm objective (as in KMM) is not suitable for choosing kernels via cross-validation, because the scale of the objective depends on the kernel and thus values are not comparable across kernels. This highlights a concrete advantage of the proposed L2-based validation.
- Supplementary material: The supplement contains numerous typos and minor mistakes that impede verification of the main results. Since the convergence analysis is a key contribution, I strongly recommend revision. Specific issues:
  - Lemma 5: W2^2 should be Ws^2. The bound should read D1 (t/−log λ)^2 + λ^{1−α} D2 ||f||_2^2.
  - The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect; Eq. (31) appears correct.
  - In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be ||ξ||^2 ≥ (threshold).
Please proofread and correct these and related derivations.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation connecting density ratio estimation to inverse problems.
- Practical, stable, closed-form kernel estimators with out-of-sample predictions.
- Novel and useful unsupervised model selection via CD-CV.
- Solid empirical performance and favorable comparison to baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Convergence rates with fixed bandwidth appear suboptimal; discussion of stronger assumptions yielding polynomial rates is missing.
- Strong smoothness/boundedness assumptions on q/p limit applicability; the paper should more fully discuss this limitation and robustness.
- Supplemental material needs significant correction to ensure reproducibility and clarity of the theoretical results.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated and technically interesting paper that advances density ratio estimation by framing it as an inverse problem and by providing a practical unsupervised validation approach. Addressing the rate issue under stronger but standard assumptions and revising the supplement would further strengthen the contribution.

Reference
Caponnetto A., De Vito E. Optimal Rates for Regularized Least-Squares Algorithm. Foundations of Computational Mathematics, 7:331–368 (2007).
