- Paper casts density-ratio estimation q/p as a Fredholm integral equation and solves it with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS (FIRE).
- Two formulations: Type I (exact identity Ep[k(x,·) f(·)] = Eq[k(x,·)]) and Type II (localized kernel approximation Ep[k_t(x,·) f(·)] ≈ q(·) as t→0).
- Empirical versions yield simple representer-theorem-solvable algorithms with out-of-sample predictions.
- Authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth compact submanifolds.
- Propose CD-CV, an unsupervised cross-density validation method for model selection using generic test function families.
- Empirical comparisons show FIRE performs favorably versus LSIF and thresholded inverse KDE baselines.
- Approach closely relates to KMM but differs by solving a regularized inverse problem with explicit RKHS operator formulation.
- Also related to LSIF/KLIEP and RKHS inverse-operator literature; connects thematically to “Smooth Operators.”
- Methodology is technically sound and grounded in classical inverse problem and RKHS regularization theory.
- Theoretical analysis provides finite-sample bounds with dimension dependence consistent with density estimation expectations.
- Empirical section is illustrative and introduces practical model selection, but broader benchmarks would strengthen claims.
- Paper is mostly clear and well organized, though Type I identifiability intuition could be expanded.
- Casting and CD-CV are original and practical; the connection to KMM and the out-of-sample/stability advantages are notable.
- Significance: improves tools for covariate shift and importance sampling with concrete rates and practical algorithms.
- Questions/suggestions: clarify Type I identifiability, motivate loss choices (L2,p vs. mixtures) with downstream tasks, and explain when Ep-weighted estimation is preferred over direct Eq.
- Need clarification on regularization vs. smoothness assumptions for q, p, and q/p, and on practical dimensionality regimes and kernel choices.
- CD-CV test-function choices (random linear, half-space indicators) need motivation and guidance on selecting U for downstream tasks.
- Pros: elegant Fredholm/RKHS framing, theoretical guarantees, stable algorithms, practical model selection, relevance to key applications.
- Cons: limited intuition for Type I, insufficient motivation for loss choices, unclear guidance on dimension dependence, and weak justification for CD-CV test functions.
- Overall: timely, solid contribution with principled formulation and nontrivial theory; positive recommendation pending clearer motivation and practical guidance.
