

This paper addresses the important problem of density ratio estimation (q/p) using a novel mathematical framework that reformulates it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose a principled approach using regularization within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), leading to a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The work provides theoretical guarantees including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and d-dimensional sub-manifolds, and introduces CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for unsupervised model selection.

The paper makes significant contributions to the field by connecting density ratio estimation to classical operator theory, which provides a more unified theoretical foundation compared to previous approaches. This connection to Fredholm equations is particularly noteworthy as it allows the application of spectral analysis techniques to analyze the problem. The authors establish clear theoretical relationships between their approach and existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), while highlighting key differences in their optimization procedures and theoretical properties.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors provide detailed convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings, demonstrating how the error depends on dimensionality, sample size, and kernel parameters. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing that FIRE outperforms alternatives including KMM and LSIF in various settings. The paper adequately addresses the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly regarding the practical implications of the different theoretical settings.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some sections could benefit from more explicit connections between the mathematical formulations and their practical implementation. The theoretical results are well-motivated and presented in a logically structured manner, with sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results. The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the broader literature on covariate shift and importance sampling, citing relevant NIPS papers from recent years.

The originality of the work is strong, as it provides a fundamentally different perspective on density ratio estimation by framing it as an inverse problem. This approach distinguishes itself from previous work by establishing connections to classical analysis techniques, rather than treating the problem as a black-box machine learning task. The authors thoroughly review related work, including KMM, LSIF, and other kernel-based methods, and clearly articulate how their approach differs and improves upon these.

The significance of the paper is substantial, as density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with applications in importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and transfer learning. The authors demonstrate practical utility through experiments on regression and classification tasks, showing that their method provides better computational efficiency and performance compared to existing alternatives. The introduction of CD-CV for unsupervised model selection addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning, making the work particularly valuable for practitioners.

Strengths:
- Novel theoretical framework connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates for multiple settings
- Practical algorithm (FIRE) with strong experimental performance
- Introduction of CD-CV for unsupervised model selection
- Clear connections to related work and theoretical foundations

Weaknesses:
- The motivation for choosing specific cost functions (L2,p, L2,q, combinations) could be strengthened with more explicit connections to practical applications
- The relationship between smoothness assumptions on p, q and their ratio requires more detailed discussion
- The practical implications of the dimensionality dependence in convergence rates could be expanded
- Some applications could be more specifically linked to the theoretical results

The paper's theoretical contributions are particularly impressive, as they provide a more unified framework for understanding density ratio estimation. The convergence rates clearly demonstrate how the error depends on the dimensionality of the data, which is crucial for understanding the practical limitations of the approach. The authors' observation that the Type II setting provides a natural way to handle cases where q(x) is known explicitly but sampling from q is difficult (e.g., in MCMC applications) is particularly valuable.

The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the trade-offs between the different settings (Type I vs. Type II) and their suitability for specific applications. While the authors mention that Type II is useful when sampling from q is difficult, a more detailed exploration of these scenarios would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field that addresses an important problem with a novel theoretical perspective. The work is well-executed, provides comprehensive theoretical guarantees, and demonstrates practical utility through thorough experiments. The authors have made a significant contribution to the literature on density ratio estimation, with clear connections to both classical analysis and modern machine learning techniques.

I recommend acceptance as it meets all the NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The paper advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation and provides a principled framework that should be valuable to researchers in machine learning and statistical inference.
