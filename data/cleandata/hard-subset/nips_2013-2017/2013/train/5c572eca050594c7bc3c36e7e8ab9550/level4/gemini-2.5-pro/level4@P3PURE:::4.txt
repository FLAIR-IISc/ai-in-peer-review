This paper proposes two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative f = p/q, derived from the equalities (i) E_p[k(x,·)f] = E_q[k(x,·)] and (ii) E_p[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x). The method replaces the integral operators with their empirical versions and minimizes the discrepancy between the left and right-hand sides over a set of test functions {k(x,·)}. By choosing this set to be the unit ball in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), the optimization can be solved using standard kernel methods. The authors provide convergence guarantees for both estimators under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. As expected for a density estimation problem, the convergence rate depends on the dimensionality of the underlying space.

This is well-executed and valuable work. The following comments and questions are intended to help improve the final version of the paper.

**Comments:**

1.  **Convergence Rate Intuition:** The paper provides convergence rates for both proposed methods. While the intuition for convergence in case (ii) is clear due to its dependence on the kernel bandwidth, a similar intuitive explanation for why minimizing over the set {k(x,·)} is sufficient for convergence in case (i) would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Choice of Cost Function:** The paper initially describes optimization with respect to the L2,p norm (page 2), but the cost function in equation (9) is a combination of L2,p and L2,q norms. Some motivation for this specific choice of cost function would be beneficial. It would be particularly helpful to connect this choice to the requirements of key applications.

3.  **Application Scenarios:** One of the primary applications mentioned is importance sampling, where the goal is to estimate expectations with respect to p using samples from q (i.e., E_p[g] = E_q[(p/q)g]). However, the proposed method for learning the ratio p/q requires access to samples from *both* p and q to construct the empirical cost function. This raises a question about the practical setup: if samples from p are available for training, why not use them to directly compute an empirical estimate of E_p[g]? The paper briefly mentions on page 5 that sampling from p might be difficult. It would be helpful to expand on this motivation and clarify the target application scenarios where samples from both distributions are available for training the density ratio, but not necessarily for the final estimation task.

4.  **Smoothness of Densities vs. Ratio:** The proposed regularizer enforces smoothness on the density ratio p/q. The convergence theorems, however, rely on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities p and q. Could the authors elaborate on the relationship between these two notions of smoothness? For instance, do smooth densities p and q (with q bounded away from zero) imply a smooth ratio? Conversely, does a smooth ratio imply the existence of smooth p and q that produce it? Clarifying this connection would be valuable.

5.  **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The paper provides convergence rates that depend on the data's dimensionality, `d`. A discussion of this dependence would be welcome. Specifically, for what range of dimensions do the authors expect their method to be practically useful?

6.  **Motivation for Test Functions:** Could the authors provide a more detailed motivation for choosing the specific family of test functions U, namely the unit ball in an RKHS?

7.  **General Framework and Relation to Prior Work:** On a more general note, a significant challenge in settings like covariate shift is the formulation of a generic and well-motivated cost function. The paper's approach of using expectation operators and a family of test functions is one compelling strategy. It would be interesting to hear the authors' perspective on whether the RKHS-based family {k(x,·)} is "the right" or most natural choice of test functions for this problem. The overall strategy bears some resemblance to that of [1], which also manipulates operators to estimate quantities of interest. While the proposed approach for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is arguably more direct, a brief comparison situating this work in the context of such related operator-based methods could be insightful.

[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem. The proposed methods are well-motivated, and the inclusion of a convergence analysis and experimental results makes for a solid contribution.
