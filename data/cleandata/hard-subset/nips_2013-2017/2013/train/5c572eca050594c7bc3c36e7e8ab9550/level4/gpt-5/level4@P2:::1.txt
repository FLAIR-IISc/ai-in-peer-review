Summary
This paper addresses density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x), a central component of importance sampling and covariate-shift adaptation. The key contribution is a reformulation of density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The ratio is recovered by solving an inverse problem with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding a family of kernel methods (FIRE). Two settings are considered: (Type I) when samples from both p and q are available, and (Type II) when q is known (or evaluated) at the sample points from p. The authors derive closed-form solutions via representer theorems, propose an unsupervised model-selection scheme (CD-CV) based on cross-density validation, and provide concentration and convergence-rate results (notably for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and smooth manifolds). Experiments compare FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded plug-in baseline (TIKDE), with applications to covariate shift in classification and regression.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects density-ratio estimation to operator-theoretic inverse problems and RKHS regularization, relating to KMM and LSIF/KLIEP but with a different formulation (Fredholm equation) and an explicit regularization term. The theoretical analysis leverages spectral/operator tools common in inverse problems and kernel methods.

Strengths
- Clear, generally well-written manuscript on an important problem.
- Elegant inverse-problem perspective that unifies algorithm design and analysis within the RKHS framework.
- Simple, closed-form kernel algorithms with natural out-of-sample evaluations.
- Theoretical bounds and convergence rates under Gaussian kernels, including manifold settings.
- Practical, fully unsupervised model selection via CD-CV.

Weaknesses, questions, and suggestions
1) Missing technical details/citations. Statements such as ∫ k_t(x, y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) for Gaussian δ-kernels should include precise conditions and references (or proofs). For example, cite standard approximate-identity results (e.g., in nonparametric texts) and specify smoothness and domain assumptions for the O(t) rate.

2) Special case q ≡ 1 (density estimation). In this case, FIRE becomes a density estimator. Please clarify the role of kernel bandwidth in this setting: the paper seems to suggest that bandwidth need not tend to zero for consistency due to regularization, which contrasts with standard KDE. A short discussion would help readers understand how bandwidth and regularization trade off, and how this differs from KDE behavior.

3) Brief primer on Tikhonov regularization. Since many NIPS readers may not be familiar with inverse-problem terminology, a concise overview of Tikhonov–Phillips regularization (objective, stability/ill-posedness motivation, role of regularization parameter) would improve accessibility.

4) Proofs only in the supplement. I did not verify the supplementary proofs. Consider moving key lemmas or proof sketches into the main text to highlight the crux of the analysis.

5) Comparison to plug-in estimators. Please discuss why directly estimating p and q (e.g., via KDE) and forming q̂/p̂ is inferior in theory or practice. For instance, quantify variance amplification in regions where p is small, sensitivity to bandwidth mismatch, or show empirical comparisons on the same benchmarks.

6) Convergence rates vs. minimax optimality. Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds, but it is unclear how these compare to known lower bounds or optimal rates (e.g., results such as those in [12]). A discussion of rate suboptimality (if any), dependencies on d and smoothness, and whether the rates match known minimax benchmarks would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

7) Dataset references. Please provide citations for CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS, and basic dataset statistics to aid reproducibility.

8) Toy demonstrations. A 1D or low-dimensional example with known p and q, plotting the true r(x) and the FIRE estimate, would greatly aid intuition and qualitative assessment (including sensitivity to λ and kernel bandwidth).

Overall assessment
This is a thoughtful and promising contribution that reframes density-ratio estimation as a regularized inverse problem, leading to simple algorithms with theoretical guarantees and a practical, unsupervised model-selection procedure. The paper would be strengthened by tightening technical justifications, clarifying the bandwidth/regularization roles (especially in the q ≡ 1 case), situating convergence rates against minimax results, adding dataset citations, and including an illustrative toy example.

Arguments for acceptance
- Important problem; principled and novel inverse-problem formulation.
- Clean RKHS-based algorithms with out-of-sample extension.
- Theoretical error bounds and convergence rates in realistic settings.
- Useful unsupervised model selection (CD-CV).
- Generally clear exposition.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some technical claims lack precise conditions/citations in the main text.
- Limited discussion of optimality of rates relative to known minimax results.
- Empirical section would benefit from stronger baselines/analyses (including plug-in KDE ratios) and simple toy illustrations.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary proofs without main-text highlights.
