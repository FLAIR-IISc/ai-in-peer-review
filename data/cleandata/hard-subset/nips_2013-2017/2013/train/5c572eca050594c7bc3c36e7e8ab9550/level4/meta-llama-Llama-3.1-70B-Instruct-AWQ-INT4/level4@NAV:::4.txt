The authors propose a method for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative f = p/q using two equalities:
(i) Ep (k(x,.) f) = E_q k(x,.) and (ii) Ep (k(x,.) f ) ~ q(x). 
The integral operators are approximated with empirical versions, where {k(x,.)} is a set of test functions that minimises the difference between the left and right sides. The choice of {k(x,.)} allows for the application of standard kernel methods for optimisation.

Convergence guarantees are provided for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. The convergence rate is significantly affected by the dimension of the underlying space, as expected for density estimation.

Overall, this is a well-executed work. A few comments for further improvement:

- Intuitive explanations for convergence rates would be beneficial, particularly for understanding why optimising over {k(x,.)} is sufficient for convergence in both settings.

- A clear motivation for the choice of cost function, specifically the combination of L2,p and L_2,q, would be helpful. It would be interesting to link this choice to relevant applications.

- The application of importance sampling raises questions about the motivation behind estimating Ep and Eq to obtain q/p. Is there an advantage to using Ep (q/p) over Eq, especially when Eq is directly available?

- The relationship between the smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of the ratio q/p could be explored further. Are there any implications for the regulariser enforcing smoothness on the ratio?

- A discussion on the dimension dependence of the convergence rates would be valuable, including the dimensions for which the method is expected to be useful.

- The motivation behind the choice of the family of test functions U could be elaborated upon.

- Finally, it would be interesting to discuss whether the proposed approach is the most suitable for the covariate shift setting, particularly in comparison to other methods such as [1]. The use of test functions is a promising direction, but a more in-depth analysis of the overall approach would be beneficial.

This paper is a timely and strong contribution to the field, providing a thorough convergence study and experimental results for a relevant problem.
