- Proposes a novel density ratio estimation framework by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind.  
- Introduces FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), using RKHS regularization to solve the inverse problem.  
- Provides detailed theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.  
- Presents a new unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) for parameter tuning in density ratio estimation.  
- Connects and extends existing methods (KMM, LSIF) by framing density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem.  
- Leverages spectral theory to provide a unified theoretical foundation and natural regularization mechanism.  
- Demonstrates flexibility of the framework: supports various loss functions/norms and retains computational simplicity via the representer theorem.  
- Thorough theoretical treatment covers both Type I (samples from both distributions) and Type II (known function values at samples) settings.  
- Experimental results show strong empirical performance against state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF).  
- Writing is clear and well-organized, making technical content accessible to readers familiar with kernel methods.  
- Significance is high because density ratio estimation is fundamental to transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift adaptation.  
- Theorem 1 exhibits slow convergence (a (-t/log λ) factor) for fixed t, potentially suboptimal versus standard kernel method rates.  
- Suggestion to discuss whether stronger spectral/operator assumptions (e.g., Caponnetto & De Vito) could yield improved polynomial rates.  
- Relies on strong Sobolev-space assumptions for q/p, which may be unrealistic and require discussion of practical implications and limitations.  
- Notes that the inverse ratio p/q may behave poorly under the Sobolev assumption and should be addressed.  
- Supplementary material contains technical errors (typos in Lemma 5, incorrect expressions at lines 675 and 686, and a domain error in an integral bound) that must be corrected.  
- Main methodological strength is unifying density ratio estimation under an inverse-problem perspective, enabling cross-validation in an unsupervised setting.  
- Comparison with KMM highlights that the L2 formulation enables effective cross-validation where RKHS-based objectives do not.  
- Overall assessment: significant, well-motivated, and technically strong contribution with convincing experiments, but requires fixes to theoretical analysis, discussion of assumptions, and corrections in supplementary material.
