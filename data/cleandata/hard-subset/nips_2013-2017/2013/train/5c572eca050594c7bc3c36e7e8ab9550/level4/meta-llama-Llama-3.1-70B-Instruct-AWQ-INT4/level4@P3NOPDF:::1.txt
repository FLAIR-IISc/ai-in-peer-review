The paper proposes a novel method for estimating the density ratio p/q, a problem that has been addressed by several existing approaches. The key contribution of this work lies in its reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and readability. The authors have done an excellent job in presenting their ideas, making the paper an enjoyable read. Furthermore, the density ratio estimation problem is an important and interesting area of research, and the authors' efforts to tackle it are commendable.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, some details are missing from the presentation. For instance, the statement \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t) is not adequately justified. A proof or a reference to support this claim would be necessary, even if the result is straightforward to establish. This lack of detail is not limited to this specific example, and the authors should ensure that all such statements are properly substantiated.

Another point worth noting is that the proposed method can be applied to density estimation as a special case (q=1). Interestingly, this approach does not require the bandwidth to converge to zero, which is a requirement for standard kernel density estimation (KDE) to be consistent. A more in-depth discussion of this aspect would be beneficial, as it would help readers understand the distinct roles of the bandwidth parameters in this paper compared to those in KDE.

Additionally, a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would be helpful, as this concept may be unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS community. While the proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, it would be reassuring to know that they have been verified for correctness.

The authors should also consider providing a clearer explanation of why a naive plug-in approach, which involves separately estimating densities p and q, is inferior to the proposed method. Furthermore, it is unclear how the proposed method compares to other existing approaches, such as the one presented in [12], which also derives minimax bounds. While Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate, it is uncertain how far these bounds are from the optimal minimax rate.

Moreover, the authors should provide references for the datasets used in the paper, specifically CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS. It would also be beneficial to include a demonstration of the proposed method on toy problems with fixed and known p and q. For instance, a plot of the true p/q and the estimated p/q for 1-dimensional p and q densities would help readers assess the quality of the estimation.

In conclusion, this is a nice paper on density ratio estimation. However, its strengths are somewhat overshadowed by the lack of clarity on how the proposed method compares to other algorithms, such as [12], and the absence of a demonstration on toy problems. Addressing these concerns would significantly enhance the paper's overall impact and value.
