The paper introduces a new method for estimating the density ratio p/q. Although several approaches to this problem already exist, the novelty here is the reformulation of density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

Strong points:
- The paper is well written and engaging.
- The density-ratio estimation problem is important and interesting.

Weak points, comments, and questions:
(i) Some technical details are missing. For instance, why does
∫ k_t(x, y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t)?
Please provide a proof (in the paper or supplementary material) or a precise reference for statements of this kind, even if they are straightforward.

(ii) As a special case (q = 1), the method can be used for density estimation. It is notable that this approach does not require the bandwidth to shrink to zero, whereas standard KDE is inconsistent under fixed bandwidth. Please expand the discussion to clarify this point and to make explicit that the bandwidth parameters in this paper play a different role than in KDE.

(iii) Consider adding a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization, as many in the NIPS community may not be familiar with it.

(iv) All proofs are relegated to the supplementary material. I have not verified their correctness.

(v) Please discuss why a naïve plug-in approach—estimating p and q separately and then taking their ratio—performs worse than the proposed method.

(vi) The comparative performance relative to existing methods is unclear. For example, if I recall correctly, [12] also derives minimax bounds. Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate, but it is not clear how close these are to the optimal minimax rate. Please position your results relative to known optimal rates.

(vii) Please provide references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) I missed a demonstration on simple toy problems with known p and q. For example, in one dimension, plotting the true p/q alongside the estimate would help readers assess estimation quality.

Overall, this is a nice paper on density-ratio estimation. However, it remains unclear how the method compares to other algorithms (e.g., [12]), and a toy example (such as simple one-dimensional p and q) would strengthen the presentation.
