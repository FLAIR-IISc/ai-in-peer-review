Summary
The paper tackles density-ratio estimation, i.e., estimating q(x)/p(x) given samples from p and either samples from q (Type I) or pointwise evaluations of q at p-sample locations (Type II). The key idea is to rewrite density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind using kernel integral operators. The problem is then addressed as an inverse problem with Tikhonov-Phillips regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form kernel methods the authors call FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper further proposes an unsupervised model-selection procedure, CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), based on validating the importance-sampling identity across random test functions. Theoretical contributions include concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth compact submanifolds, and experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (discussion), and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline on covariate-shift classification and regression.

Relation to prior work
The work connects density-ratio estimation with classical inverse problems, complementing direct empirical risk minimization approaches such as LSIF and KLIEP, and moment-matching approaches such as KMM. The Type I formulation with RKHS loss relates closely to KMM but incorporates explicit regularization and an out-of-sample functional representation via the representer theorem. The spectral/operator-theoretic analysis leverages classical theory not typically emphasized in ML density-ratio papers.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation gives a principled framework linking importance sampling, kernel integral operators, and inverse problems.
- Algorithmic simplicity: The FIRE estimators admit closed-form solutions via kernel matrix operations with natural out-of-sample extension.
- Flexibility: The framework supports multiple loss norms (L2 under p, q, mixtures, and RKHS norms) and two data scenarios (Type I and II).
- Theory: Nonasymptotic bounds and convergence rates are provided for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and smooth compact manifolds, which is relatively uncommon in this literature.
- Practical model selection: CD-CV is an unsupervised procedure that exploits the importance-sampling identity with random test functions; the idea is simple and easy to implement.
- Empirical results: The method shows competitive performance and computational efficiency in covariate-shift settings.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Assumptions and dominating measure: The paper should clearly and early state the measurable space, dominating measure, and absolute continuity assumptions. The draft sometimes refers to “densities” without specifying the reference measure (e.g., Lebesgue measure on Rd, Riemannian volume on a manifold, or counting measure). Because q/p is undefined where p=0, the paper must explicitly state whether p>0 on the support of q, how support mismatch is handled, and how measure-zero issues are treated.
- Domain and smoothness: Assumptions are scattered (e.g., later sections restrict to Rd or smooth compact submanifolds and assume Sobolev smoothness). A consolidated “Assumptions” subsection in the introduction would improve rigor and readability: specify the domain (Rd or a given manifold), whether boundaries are allowed, boundedness of p and q, positivity of p, and the smoothness conditions on q/p required by the theory.
- Choice and role of kernels: At times the kernel defining the integral operator and the RKHS kernel coincide, and at other times they differ (k versus k_H). The consequences of these choices for bias, stability, and rates should be clarified, as well as guidance for practitioners on when to set k=k_H.
- Identifiability and constraints: Since q/p need not be nonnegative in Type II when q is not a density, do the algorithms enforce or allow nonnegativity? If the ratio is intended as an importance weight, a discussion of positivity and normalization constraints (or their absence) would be useful.
- CD-CV justification: While CD-CV is intuitive, a brief discussion of its statistical properties (e.g., bias of the validation criterion, dependence on the choice of test-function family, and robustness to sample size imbalance) would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental scope: More details on computational complexity, numerical stability (conditioning with small λ), and sensitivity analyses (kernel width, sample sizes n vs. m, support mismatch) would help practitioners. Comparisons that include KMM with an out-of-sample extension surrogate would also be informative.

Quality
The technical development is promising and, if the assumptions are properly consolidated, appears sound. The bounds and rates are helpful, though the presentation would benefit from clearer statements of conditions (dominating measure, support inclusion, smoothness) alongside each theorem.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written but notation-dense. Suggested improvements:
- Add a dedicated assumptions section early on, defining the measurable space, dominating measure, domain, smoothness, and positivity/support conditions.
- Clearly distinguish the roles of the kernel used in the operator and the RKHS kernel; consider harmonizing notation to reduce cognitive load.
- State explicitly how measure-zero sets and regions where p=0 are handled in both algorithms and theory.
- Provide a short complexity discussion for the closed-form solutions.

Originality
The inverse-problem framing with Fredholm operators and the associated analysis is a fresh angle within the density-ratio literature, even if related identities have been used in KMM. The CD-CV selection idea is practical and, to my knowledge, not standard in this exact form.

Significance
Estimating q/p is central to covariate shift, importance sampling, and off-policy evaluation. A stable, regularized, out-of-sample-capable method with theory and unsupervised model selection is likely to be useful to both researchers and practitioners.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Principled inverse-problem formulation with solid theoretical backing
- Simple, practical algorithms with out-of-sample extension
- Unsupervised model selection procedure
- Empirically competitive results

Cons:
- Insufficiently explicit assumptions about the underlying measure, domain, and support conditions
- Some ambiguity about kernel choices and their implications
- Limited discussion of CD-CV’s statistical properties and broader experimental ablations

Minor comments
- Correct typographical errors (e.g., “TiknonovPhilips” should be “Tikhonov–Phillips”; “a unsupervised” → “an unsupervised”).
- Be consistent with notation for norms and operators; avoid overloading symbols.

Recommendation
Borderline accept, contingent on clarifying the assumptions (dominating measure, domain, support, positivity), tightening notation around kernel choices, and adding brief discussion of CD-CV’s properties and computational aspects.
