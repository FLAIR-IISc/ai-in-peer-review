This paper introduces two complementary approaches for estimating the density ratio q/p. The first is derived directly from the importance sampling identity and is cast as a regularized inverse problem: minimize an L2 discrepancy from the equality together with an RKHS penalty (Type I Fredholm equation of the first kind). The second uses a localized kernel that approximates the identity as its bandwidth shrinks; this yields another inverse formulation that is likewise handled via regularization (Type II Fredholm equation). The authors provide convergence analyses and report empirical results demonstrating practical performance.

Although related to prior work such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), a major advantage here is that the L2-based formulation naturally supports cross-validation (CV) for tuning algorithmic parameters. In contrast to supervised learning, where CV is standard, unsupervised settings rarely admit a generally applicable model selection strategy. The paper addresses this for density ratio estimation through a neat use of an L2 criterion.

It would be helpful to explicitly note that objectives defined in an RKHS norm, as in KMM, are not well-suited for kernel selection via CV, because the objective value depends on the kernel itself and is thus not directly comparable across kernels.

Regarding Theorem 1, the presence of the (−t/log λ) factor implies a slow rate when t is fixed. In much of the kernel literature, with fixed t, one can obtain a polynomial rate in the sample size after specifying how λ decays. Are there reasonable additional assumptions under which the proposed estimator attains a polynomial rate? For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression by imposing spectral conditions on the relevant integral operator K_{p,t}. I suspect that strengthening assumptions on q/p would also yield polynomial rates. It would be valuable to discuss such polynomial-rate results under stronger assumptions with fixed t.

In density ratio estimation, one typically assumes q/p belongs to a regular function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This is a strong requirement, effectively imposing knowledge about the ratio or tail behavior of p and q. Under such an assumption on q/p, the reverse ratio p/q often behaves poorly. It would be important to include a discussion of this limitation of density ratio methods.
