The authors derive two estimators of the Radon-Nikodym derivative f = p/q using two equalities: (i) Ep(k(x,.)f) = Eq(k(x,.)) and (ii) Ep(k(x,.)f) ≈ q(x). They replace integral operators with empirical versions, treating {k(x,.)} as a set of test functions that minimize the difference between the right and left sides. This choice of test functions enables the use of standard kernel methods for optimization.

Convergence guarantees are provided for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. The rate of convergence depends crucially on the dimension of the underlying space, which is expected for a density estimate. Overall, this is a well-executed work with a nice approach.

A few comments are in order:

- For the convergence rates, optimizing over the set {k(x,.)} is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the ratio. While this is intuitive for (ii) due to bandwidth dependence, a short intuitive argument explaining why this also works for (i) would be helpful.

- The authors optimize over L2,p on page 2, but later use a combination of L2,p and L2,q in equation (9). A motivation for the choice of cost function would be beneficial, ideally linking it to specific applications. Ultimately, the cost function choice should stem from the tasks of interest.

- Speaking of applications, the authors mention importance sampling, where q/p is relevant for moving from Eq to Ep. However, since the empirical cost function effectively estimates Ep and Eq, it is unclear why not using Eq directly. Can Ep(q/p) be better than directly using Eq? A clearer motivation for the setup is needed, especially since estimating Ep and Eq is required to estimate q/p. The sentence on page 5 mentions that sampling from Eq is difficult, but it is unclear if this is the primary application in mind.

- The regularizer enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p, while the theorem assumes smoothness on q and p. It would be interesting to explore the relationship between smoothness on q and p and smoothness of the ratio. One possible answer is that smoothness on q and p (possibly bounded away from 0) implies smoothness of the ratio, or that a smooth ratio implies the existence of smooth q and p that produce the ratio.

- A discussion on the dimension dependence of the rates would be beneficial, including the dimensions for which the method is expected to be useful.

- A clear motivation for the choice of the family of test functions U would be helpful.

- A more principled question is whether the approach using {k(x,.)} is the most suitable for the q/p and covariate shift setting. The main difficulty in this setting seems to be defining a generic cost function. While the authors' approach has similarities with [1], it is unclear whether this is the most effective approach. The use of test functions is an improvement over [1], but a more in-depth discussion on the choice of approach would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem, provides a convergence study, and presents experimental results.
