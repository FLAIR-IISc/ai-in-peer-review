This paper proposes two new methods for estimating the density ratio between two probability distributions. The first derives from the importance sampling identity and casts density-ratio estimation as a regularized least-squares problem: it minimizes the L^2 discrepancy from the identity with an RKHS penalty (a Type I Fredholm formulation). The second introduces a kernel with a locality parameter that must shrink to zero for consistency; this approach is also written as a regularized problem (a Type II Fredholm formulation). The paper presents convergence results and empirical evaluations.

Although the approach is related to existing methods such as KMM, a notable advantage here is that the L^2 formulation naturally supports cross-validation (CV) for selecting algorithmic parameters. Unlike in supervised learning—where CV is the de facto standard—there is no generally accepted parameter-selection method for unsupervised problems; using the L^2 loss is a clever way to make CV applicable to density-ratio estimation.

It would be helpful to explicitly note that RKHS objective functions like those used in KMM are not well suited for kernel selection via CV, because their values depend on the kernel itself and are therefore not comparable across kernels.

Regarding Theorem 1, the convergence rate is slowed by the (-t/log λ) factor when t is fixed. In much of the kernel-methods literature, one can obtain a polynomial rate in the sample size for fixed t after specifying how λ scales. Are there reasonable additional assumptions under which the proposed estimator achieves a polynomial rate? For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression under spectral assumptions on the operator K_{p,t}. I suspect that imposing stronger conditions on q/p would also yield a polynomial rate. It would strengthen the paper to discuss such polynomial rates under stronger assumptions with fixed t.

Density-ratio estimation typically assumes that q/p lies in a regular function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This assumption is quite strong, as it requires prior knowledge of the ratio or the tails of p and q. Moreover, under such an assumption on q/p, the inverse ratio p/q often behaves poorly. The paper should include a discussion of this limitation.
