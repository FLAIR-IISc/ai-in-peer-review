The paper tackles estimation of the density ratio f = q/p by deriving two formulations based on the identities (i) Ep[k(x,·) f] = Eq[k(x,·)] and (ii) Ep[k(x,·) f] ≈ q(x) when k is a localized (e.g., Gaussian) kernel. The corresponding integral operators are replaced by their empirical analogues. Interpreting {k(x,·)} as a family of test functions, the method minimizes the discrepancy between the two sides over this family, which enables optimization with standard RKHS machinery via representer theorems and Tikhonov regularization. The authors develop two estimators (Type I and Type II, FIRE), provide explicit closed-form solutions, and analyze their statistical properties through spectral/operator-theoretic arguments. They establish concentration bounds and convergence rates under smoothness and boundedness assumptions, including settings on Rd and on smooth manifolds, and propose an unsupervised model selection scheme (CD-CV) based on cross-density validation. Experiments compare favorably to KMM, LSIF, and a baseline inverse KDE, with additional discussion of covariate shift and importance sampling connections to prior work.

The theoretical results include nonasymptotic error bounds for both Type I and Type II estimators with Gaussian kernels. The rates depend on the intrinsic dimension (as expected in problems tied to density estimation and kernel integral operators), with separate treatments for Euclidean domains and compact manifolds. Overall, the analysis cleanly leverages properties of Fredholm operators and RKHS regularization to justify the proposed procedures.

I find this to be a solid and well-executed contribution.

Comments and suggestions:
- On the convergence rates: optimizing over the span of {k(x,·)} suffices for recovery of the ratio. For the Type II approximation (ii), this is intuitive because of the bandwidth-dependent δ-approximation. It would help to include a brief intuition for why the same choice of test functions also suffices in the exact identity (i) beyond the formal operator-theoretic argument.
- Loss choices: early on you optimize under the L2,p norm, while later (Eq. 9) you consider a mixture γL2,p + (1−γ)L2,q. Some motivation for these specific choices would be welcome, ideally tied to target tasks (e.g., covariate shift risk under q vs. stability under p), and guidance for when each loss is preferable in applications.
- Applications and motivation: for importance sampling, the relevant quantity is q/p to reweight from Ep to Eq. Since the empirical objectives estimate expectations under both p and q, why not directly use the available Eq in downstream tasks? In what regimes does Ep(q/p · ·) offer a practical advantage over directly approximating Eq(·)? You note on p.5 that sampling from q can be difficult; is this the primary motivating scenario?
- Regularization vs. smoothness assumptions: the method regularizes the ratio f, while the theorems assume smoothness of p and q. Is there a principled relationship you can state (e.g., p, q smooth and p bounded away from zero implies f smooth), or conversely that smooth f can arise from smooth p, q? A short discussion clarifying when smoothness of p, q translates into smoothness of f would strengthen the narrative.
- Dimension dependence: given the explicit d-dependence in the rates, for what dimensional ranges (or intrinsic dimensions on manifolds) do you expect FIRE to be practically effective? Some rule-of-thumb guidance based on the theory and experiments would be useful.
- Choice of validation functions U in CD-CV: could you further justify the specific families (random linear and half-space indicators)? For example, do they target stability across a broad class of downstream functionals, or approximate integral probability metrics relevant to covariate shift?
- More broadly: in covariate shift and related ratio-estimation problems, a central challenge is defining a generic objective via expectations over test functions. Do you view the choice {k(x,·)} as the “right” or canonical family here? There are conceptual similarities to “Smooth Operators” [1] in manipulating transformations to reveal target quantities; your Radon–Nikodym approach via test functions is elegant, but a brief comparison clarifying similarities/differences in objectives and guarantees could help position the contribution.

Overall, this is a timely and strong paper addressing an important problem, with clear algorithms, a careful convergence analysis, and supportive empirical results.

[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor
