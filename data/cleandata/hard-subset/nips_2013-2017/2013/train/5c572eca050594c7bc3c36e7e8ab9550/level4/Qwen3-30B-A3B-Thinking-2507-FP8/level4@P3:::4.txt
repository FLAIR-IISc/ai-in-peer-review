

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then regularized using Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) techniques. The authors develop a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) that provides a principled framework for this problem, with detailed theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems in operator theory, offering a fresh perspective on an important problem in transfer learning and importance sampling.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a clear connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm integral equations, a well-studied area in mathematical analysis. This formulation allows the authors to leverage spectral theory and regularization techniques for theoretical analysis. Second, they provide a flexible framework that accommodates various loss functions and norms, including L2,p, L2,q, and combinations thereof, while maintaining computational simplicity. Third, they derive convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings, which is a substantial theoretical contribution. Finally, they introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a completely unsupervised method for model selection that addresses a key challenge in density ratio estimation.

The paper excels in quality, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by concentration bounds and convergence rates. The authors carefully analyze both Type I and Type II settings, providing clear error bounds for different domains (Euclidean space, manifolds with boundary). The experimental results on real datasets demonstrate strong performance compared to existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE), with particular emphasis on computational efficiency. The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-structured arguments and sufficient mathematical detail to reproduce the results. The theoretical development is logically presented, and the connection to previous work is well-motivated.

The originality of the approach is substantial. While related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, the Fredholm equation formulation offers a distinct perspective that enables more straightforward theoretical analysis. The paper explicitly differentiates itself from previous work, noting that KMM does not have a natural out-of-sample extension and lacks a regularizing term, while LSIF uses a parametric family that requires basis approximation. The authors' approach avoids these limitations by framing the problem as a classical inverse problem with regularization.

The significance of this work is evident in its applications to covariate shift and importance sampling. The theoretical guarantees provide a solid foundation for practitioners, and the unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) addresses a critical practical challenge in density ratio estimation. The paper demonstrates how the proposed methods can improve classification performance under covariate shift, showing tangible benefits for real-world applications.

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few constructive suggestions. The motivation for using the specific family of test functions {k(x, ·)} could be more thoroughly explained. The paper states that these functions arise naturally from the integral equation formulation, but a more detailed discussion of why this choice is optimal for the problem would strengthen the contribution. Additionally, while the theoretical analysis provides convergence rates, the practical implications of the dimension dependence (d) could be more explicitly discussed. For instance, the paper could better clarify for which dimensions the method would be most effective, given the dimension-dependent terms in the error bounds.

The paper also briefly mentions the challenge of sampling from q, but this could be expanded to better justify the primary applications of the approach. A more detailed discussion of scenarios where q is known explicitly but sampling from q is computationally prohibitive would strengthen the motivation.

The review criteria are well-addressed:
- Quality: High - Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported, with clear experimental validation.
- Clarity: High - The paper is well-written, logically structured, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.
- Originality: High - The Fredholm equation formulation offers a novel perspective that distinguishes it from existing methods.
- Significance: High - The method addresses a fundamental problem in transfer learning with clear practical applications and theoretical contributions.

Pro for acceptance: The paper provides a principled framework for density ratio estimation that is theoretically grounded, computationally efficient, and practically validated. The connection to classical inverse problems is insightful and leads to novel algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees.

Con for acceptance: The motivation for the specific choice of test functions could be strengthened, and the practical implications of the dimension dependence could be more clearly articulated.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and well-executed, the algorithms are simple and effective, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
