

The authors formulate density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, transforming the problem into a well-studied inverse problem framework. They derive two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative f = q/p: (i) Ep[k(x,·)f] = Eq[k(x,·)] and (ii) Ep[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x), where the integral operators are approximated using empirical versions. The choice of {k(x,·)} as test functions enables the application of standard kernel methods for optimization, providing a principled approach to density ratio estimation within the RKHS framework. The paper delivers comprehensive convergence guarantees for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the underlying densities, with convergence rates that appropriately depend on the ambient dimension.

The work represents a well-executed and timely contribution to the field of density ratio estimation, with clear theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The authors' approach provides a natural connection between classical operator theory and modern kernel methods, yielding both theoretical insights and practical algorithms.

Key strengths include the elegant reformulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, the detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates, and the introduction of CD-CV for unsupervised model selection. The experimental results demonstrate strong performance compared to existing methods.

Some points for consideration:
- The convergence analysis for setting (i) could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why optimizing over {k(x,·)} guarantees convergence to the ratio, particularly given the bandwidth dependence is more apparent in setting (ii).
- The transition from optimizing over L2,p to a combined L2,p + (1-γ)L2,q loss in equation (9) requires clearer motivation. The authors should connect the choice of cost function to specific application scenarios.
- Regarding applications, the paper mentions importance sampling as a key use case. It would be helpful to elaborate on why estimating q/p through the proposed framework is advantageous compared to directly estimating expectations under q, particularly since the method requires estimating both Ep and Eq to compute the empirical loss.
- The relationship between smoothness assumptions on p and q and the smoothness of their ratio merits further discussion. The authors note that many different p and q can yield the same ratio, but a clearer characterization of the conditions under which p and q smoothness implies ratio smoothness would strengthen the theoretical foundation.
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the dimension-dependent convergence rates, particularly for which dimensions the method would be most effective.
- The motivation for the specific family of test functions U used in CD-CV could be strengthened with additional justification.
- The approach of using test functions to extract density information has parallels with [1] (Smooth Operators), and it would be valuable to more explicitly position the work within this broader context while highlighting how the current formulation provides advantages for density ratio estimation.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding and practical implementation of density ratio estimation, with clear connections to related work in kernel methods and inverse problems. The technical quality is high, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims.
