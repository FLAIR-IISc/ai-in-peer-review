The authors have used two equations to find two approximate values of the Radon-Nikodym derivative f= p/q:
(i) Ep ( k(x,.) f) = E_q k(x,.) and (ii) Ep (k(x,.) f ) ~ q(x)
The integral operators have been replaced with empirical versions. {k(x,.)} can be seen as a set of testing functions where the difference between the right and left sides is minimized. The advantage of choosing {k(x,.)} as a family of testing functions is that the standard kernel method can be used for optimization.

Convergence is guaranteed in both cases under the assumption of smoothness and finite limits of the density. The rate of convergence mainly depends on the dimension of the underlying space – which is expected for a density assumption.

I'm thinking here: you are effectively estimating Ep and Eq to get your empirical cost function. So why don't you directly use the Eq you got there? Could Ep (q/p) be better than Eq directly? I think I'm asking for an inspiration for the setup here, since you have to estimate Ep and Eq to estimate q/p. You have a sentence about this on page 5. That is, sampling from Eq is difficult. Do you have such major applications in mind?
- Regularizer: You apply smoothness to the q/p ratio. Later in the theorem, you get a smoothness assumption on q and p (which I would say is normal). So I'm wondering if there is a relationship between the smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of the ratio? I think there is already an answer here that you have many different q and p that create the same ratio; that is, changing the q and p by a countable or uncountable number of points makes it extremely irregular. So it could be smoother p,q (possibly limited to away from 0) => the ratio is smooth (?) or the ratio is smooth => there is a smooth p and q that creates the ratio.
- It would be good to discuss the d dependence of your rate - for what dimension do you expect your method to be useful?
- Is there any good inspiration for your testing function U family?
- More ethical questions: A major difficulty for this q/p ; covariates shift, etc. is creating a generic cost function. One way is to go through expectation operators and use various testing functions to find the underlying density. Do you think you have the "right" method with {k(x,.)}?
