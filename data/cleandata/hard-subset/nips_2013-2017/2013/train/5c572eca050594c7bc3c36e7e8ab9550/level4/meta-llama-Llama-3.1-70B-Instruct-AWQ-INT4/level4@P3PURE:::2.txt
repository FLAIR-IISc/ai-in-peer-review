This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio of two probabilities. The first method leverages the equation of importance sampling, formulating it as a regularization problem that incorporates the L^2 norm of the deviation from the equality and a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) regularization term, resulting in a Type I Fredholm equation. The second method employs a kernel where the locality parameter must approach zero for consistency, also expressed in the form of regularization as a Type II Fredholm equation. The paper presents theoretical results on convergence and demonstrates the practical performance of the methods experimentally.

While the proposed method bears similarities to existing methods such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), a significant advantage of the proposed approach is that the L^2 formulation enables the application of cross-validation (CV) for choosing parameters in the algorithm. This is particularly noteworthy, as CV is a dominant method for choosing parameters in supervised problems, but there is a lack of general methods for unsupervised learning. The paper addresses this issue in the context of density ratio estimation with a clever idea of utilizing the L^2 norm.

It is worth noting that the objective function given by RKHS, such as in KMM, is not suitable for choosing a kernel with CV, as the values of the objective function depend on the kernel, rendering them incomparable.

In Theorem 1, the derived convergence rate is slow due to the (-t/log λ) factor with fixed t. In many kernel method literatures, it is possible to derive a polynomial rate with respect to the sample size after fixing the rate of λ. The question arises whether there are reasonable additional assumptions that can yield a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) make assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to derive the optimal rate for kernel ridge regression. It is likely that making stronger assumptions on q/p will also lead to a polynomial rate. A discussion on such polynomial rates under stronger assumptions with fixed t would be more interesting and provide further insights.

In estimating a density ratio, it is common to assume that q/p belongs to a nice function class, such as Sobolev. However, this assumption is quite strong, requiring some knowledge of the ratio or tails of p and q. Under such an assumption on q/p, the opposite ratio p/q often behaves poorly. It is essential to include a discussion on this limitation of density ratio estimation.

While not a requirement, the supplementary materials would benefit from improvement. There are numerous typos and minor mistakes, making it challenging to verify the correctness of the theoretical results in the main paper. For example, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 \|f\|_2^2. The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, whereas Eq.(31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 must be \| ξ\|^2 ≥. Since the theoretical results on convergence are a primary contribution of this paper, it is advisable for the authors to revise the supplementary material.
