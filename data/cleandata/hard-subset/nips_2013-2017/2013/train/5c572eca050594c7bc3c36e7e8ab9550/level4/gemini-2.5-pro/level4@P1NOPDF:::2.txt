This paper introduces two new methods for estimating the density ratio between two probability distributions. The first method is based on the importance sampling identity and is formulated as a regularization problem that minimizes the L2 norm of the deviation from this identity, penalized by an RKHS norm (a Type I Fredholm equation). The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that must go to zero for consistency, and this is also formulated as a regularization problem (a Type II Fredholm equation). The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate the practical performance of their methods experimentally.

While the proposed methods share similarities with existing approaches such as KMM, a key advantage is that the L2 formulation enables the use of cross-validation (CV) for model selection. This is particularly valuable because, while CV is a standard tool for parameter tuning in supervised learning, general-purpose methods for unsupervised learning problems are scarce. The authors address this challenge for density ratio estimation through their clever L2 formulation.

To further highlight this contribution, it would strengthen the paper to explicitly remark that objective functions based directly on an RKHS norm, such as the one used in KMM, are ill-suited for model selection via CV. This is because the value of such objectives is kernel-dependent, making scores from different kernels incomparable.

Regarding the theoretical analysis, the derived convergence rate in Theorem 1, which includes a `(-t/log λ)` factor, is quite slow for a fixed `t`. In the kernel methods literature, it is often possible to derive a polynomial convergence rate with respect to the sample size by appropriately setting the decay rate of λ. Can a polynomial rate be achieved for the proposed estimator under reasonable additional assumptions? For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) make assumptions on the spectrum of the integral operator to derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression. I suspect that stronger assumptions on the smoothness of the true ratio `q/p` would also yield a polynomial rate. The paper would be more compelling if the authors discussed the possibility of achieving polynomial rates under such stronger, yet reasonable, assumptions for a fixed `t`.

A more general point of discussion relates to the underlying assumptions of density ratio estimation. These methods typically require that the true ratio `q/p` belongs to a well-behaved function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This is a strong assumption, as it implies significant prior knowledge about the relationship between `p` and `q`, particularly concerning their tails. Furthermore, under such an assumption on `q/p`, the inverse ratio, `p/q`, will often be ill-behaved. The authors should include a discussion of this general limitation, as it provides important context for their method's applicability.

Finally, while not a major criticism, the supplementary material would benefit from significant revision. It contains numerous typos and minor errors that hinder verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. For instance:
*   In Lemma 5, `W2^2` should be `Ws^2`.
*   The bound in Lemma 5 should be `D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^(1-α) D2 ||f||_2^2`.
*   The expressions at lines 675 and 686 appear to be incorrect, while Eq. (31) is correct.
*   In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 should specify the lower bound `||ξ||^2 ≥ ...`.

Given that the theoretical convergence results are a central contribution of this work, it is crucial that the authors thoroughly revise and correct the supplementary material.
