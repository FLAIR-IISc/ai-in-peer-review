This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio between two probability distributions. The first method is based on the importance sampling identity and is formulated as a regularization problem minimizing the L2 norm of the deviation from this identity, augmented by an RKHS regularization term (a Type I Fredholm equation). The second method employs a kernel whose locality parameter must approach zero for consistency, and is also formulated as a regularization problem (a Type II Fredholm equation). The authors provide theoretical convergence results and demonstrate the practical performance of their methods through experiments.

A significant advantage of the proposed approach, particularly when compared to similar methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), is that its L2 formulation naturally enables the use of cross-validation (CV) for model selection (e.g., choosing regularization and kernel parameters). In contrast to supervised learning where CV is a standard tool, robust parameter selection remains a major challenge in many unsupervised learning settings. This paper offers a clever and effective solution to this problem for density ratio estimation.

To strengthen this point, the authors should consider explicitly remarking on why their L2-based objective is suitable for CV while other objectives, such as the one used in KMM, are not. Specifically, objective functions whose values are kernel-dependent (e.g., involving RKHS norms) cannot be directly compared across different kernels, making CV for kernel selection problematic. The proposed L2 objective elegantly sidesteps this issue.

The convergence rate derived in Theorem 1 appears to be slow due to the `(-t/log λ)` factor, particularly for a fixed `t`. In the kernel methods literature, it is often possible to establish polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size (for a fixed `t` and an appropriately chosen decay rate for `λ`). Could a polynomial rate be achieved for the proposed estimator under reasonable additional assumptions? For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression by making assumptions on the spectrum of the integral operator. It is plausible that introducing stronger smoothness or regularity assumptions on the true density ratio `q/p` could similarly lead to a polynomial rate. A discussion of such faster rates under stronger, clearly stated assumptions would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

A broader point of discussion is warranted regarding the inherent assumptions in density ratio estimation. The method implicitly assumes that the true ratio `q/p` belongs to a well-behaved function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This is a strong assumption that depends on the tail behavior of the distributions `p` and `q`. It is important to acknowledge that if `q/p` is well-behaved, the inverse ratio `p/q` may not be, which has implications for the symmetric use of such estimators. The paper would benefit from a discussion on this fundamental limitation.

The supplementary material requires significant revision. It contains numerous typographical and minor errors that hinder the verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. Specific examples include:
*   In Lemma 5, `W2^2` should likely be `Ws^2`, and the bound appears to be `D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^(1-α) D2 ||f||_2^2`.
*   The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, though Eq. (31) is correct.
*   In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of the integral at line 1147 should be `||ξ||^2 ≥ ...`.
Given that the convergence analysis is a main contribution of this work, it is crucial that the supplementary material is corrected to allow for a thorough review of the proofs.

In summary, this paper proposes a novel and promising approach to density ratio estimation. Its primary strength lies in providing a principled way to apply cross-validation for parameter selection, a significant contribution to the field.
