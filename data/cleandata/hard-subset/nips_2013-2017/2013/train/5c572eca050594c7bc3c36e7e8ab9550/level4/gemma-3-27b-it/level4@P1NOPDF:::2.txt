This paper introduces two novel methods for density ratio estimation. The first leverages importance sampling and formulates it as a regularization problem employing both an L^2 norm measuring deviation from equality and an RKHS regularization term (resulting in a Type I Fredholm equation). The second method utilizes a kernel with a locality parameter that, for consistency, must approach zero, and is similarly expressed as a regularization problem (a Type II Fredholm equation). The authors support their approach with theoretical convergence results and experimental demonstrations of practical performance.

A key strength of the proposed methods, distinguishing them from existing techniques like KMM, is the L^2 formulation, which enables the application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. While CV is a standard practice in supervised learning for parameter tuning, general methods for unsupervised learning are lacking. This paper addresses this gap for density ratio estimation through a clever use of the L^2 norm.

It’s worth highlighting that the objective function derived from RKHS, as used in KMM, is less suited for CV-based kernel selection, as the objective function’s value is kernel-dependent and therefore not directly comparable across different kernels.

Regarding Theorem 1, the convergence rate derived, incorporating the factor (-t/log λ), appears slow for a fixed *t*. In much of the kernel methods literature, fixing *t* allows for the derivation of a polynomial convergence rate with respect to sample size, after fixing the rate of λ. I wonder if the authors could achieve a polynomial rate by introducing reasonable additional assumptions. For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) leverage assumptions about the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to establish optimal rates for kernel ridge regression. I suspect that strengthening the assumptions on q/p could also yield a polynomial rate. Discussing such a rate under stronger assumptions with fixed *t* would be particularly interesting.

Estimating density ratios typically requires an assumption that q/p lies within a well-behaved function class, such as a Sobolev space. It’s important to acknowledge that this is a strong assumption, demanding prior knowledge about the ratio or the tails of *p* and *q*. Under such an assumption on q/p, the inverse ratio p/q often exhibits poor behavior. A discussion of this limitation within the context of density ratio estimation would be valuable. 

While not essential, the supplementary materials would benefit from improvement. Numerous typos and minor errors hinder verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. For instance, in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-\alpha} D2 ||f||_2^2. The expressions on lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, although Eq. (31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the integral domain on line 1147 should be \| ξ\|^2 ≥. Given that the convergence results constitute a primary contribution of this paper, I strongly recommend the authors revise the supplementary material with careful attention to detail.
