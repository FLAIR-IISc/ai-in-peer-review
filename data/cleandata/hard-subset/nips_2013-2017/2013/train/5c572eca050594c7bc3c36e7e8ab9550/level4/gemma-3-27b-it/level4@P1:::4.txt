The authors address the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, q(x)/p(x), where p is a density and q is another density or an arbitrary function. This estimation is motivated by applications such as importance sampling and covariate shift in transfer learning. The core idea is to reformulate this problem as an inverse problem using Fredholm integral equations and leverage regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to a family of algorithms, FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), with associated theoretical guarantees and promising experimental results.

This work builds upon a significant body of research in density ratio estimation, including [6, 10, 9, 22, 2], and connects it to classical operator theory. The authors present a principled approach that combines the strengths of kernel methods and regularization, offering a flexible and theoretically grounded solution. The introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Formulation:** The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation provides a fresh perspective and allows the application of tools from classical analysis.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel in various settings (Euclidean space and manifolds).
*   **Algorithm Flexibility:** The FIRE algorithms are flexible and easy to implement, allowing for different norms and regularization techniques.
*   **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method offers a practical solution for model selection in unsupervised or semi-supervised settings.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing methods like KMM [9] and LSIF [10].

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Intuition for (i):** While the paper clearly explains the intuition behind the convergence in setting (ii), a short, intuitive argument explaining why the optimization over the kernel functions {k(x,.)} guarantees convergence in setting (i) would be beneficial.
*   **Cost Function Motivation:** The rationale for using a combination of L2,p and L2,q norms (in equation 9) could be strengthened by explicitly linking the choice to specific applications and their associated requirements.
*   **Motivation of Setup:** The paper states that estimating q/p is relevant for importance sampling and acknowledges the need to estimate both Ep and Eq. A more detailed discussion on why estimating q/p through this process is advantageous compared to directly utilizing the estimated Ep and Eq would be valuable, particularly focusing on scenarios where sampling from q is difficult (as mentioned on page 5).
*   **Smoothness Assumptions:** Investigating the relationship between the smoothness of q and p, and the smoothness of their ratio, might offer further insights. It would be helpful to clarify if smoothness of q and p necessarily implies smoothness of the ratio, or vice-versa.
*   **Dimensionality Dependence:**  A discussion regarding the impact of dimensionality (d) on the convergence rates and the practical utility of the method for different values of d would be insightful.
*   **Test Function Family:** Providing a stronger justification for the choice of the test function family U, beyond its convenience, could enhance the paper.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:**  While the authors acknowledge related work, a more detailed comparison of their approach to other methods, such as [1] (Smooth Operators, Grünewälder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor), and a discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of each, would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem with significant practical applications.
*   The detailed theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees provide strong support for the method's effectiveness.
*   The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance and highlight the practical utility of the proposed algorithms.
*   The introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Some aspects of the motivation and intuition behind certain choices (e.g., cost function, test function family) could be further clarified.
*   A more in-depth discussion of the method's limitations and potential areas for improvement would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. While some aspects could be further clarified, the novel formulation, theoretical analysis, and empirical validation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance.
