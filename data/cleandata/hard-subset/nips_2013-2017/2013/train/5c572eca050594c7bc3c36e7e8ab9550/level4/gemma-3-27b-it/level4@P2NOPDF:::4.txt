This paper presents a novel approach to estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative, f = p/q, utilizing two equivalent formulations and deriving corresponding estimators. The core idea involves replacing integral operators with their empirical counterparts and framing the problem as an optimization over a set of test functions, {k(x,.)}, allowing the application of standard kernel methods. The authors provide convergence guarantees under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities p and q, with convergence rates appropriately dependent on the dimensionality of the underlying space. Overall, this is a well-executed and timely contribution to an important problem.

However, several points could benefit from further clarification. Regarding the convergence rates, the sufficiency of optimizing over {k(x,.)} to ensure convergence to the correct ratio is intuitively clear for the second estimator due to its bandwidth dependence. A brief explanation of why this also holds for the first estimator would strengthen the presentation.

The choice of cost function is another area deserving more discussion. The paper initially optimizes over an L2,p norm (page 2), then transitions to a combination of L2,p and L2,q norms in equation (9). Providing motivation for this shift, and ideally linking the cost function selection to specific applications where these norms are particularly relevant, would be beneficial. While the paper mentions importance sampling as an application (q/p is useful for moving from Eq to Ep), the methodology estimates both Ep and Eq to construct the empirical cost function. A justification for this approach, compared to directly utilizing the estimated Eq, would be valuable. Is Ep(q/p) demonstrably superior to simply using Eq? The discussion on page 5 regarding the difficulty of sampling from Eq hints at the primary application; explicitly stating this would enhance clarity.

The paper enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p via regularization, and simultaneously presents smoothness assumptions on the individual densities p and q. Investigating the relationship between the smoothness of p and q and the smoothness of their ratio would be insightful. While it's acknowledged that multiple p and q distributions can yield the same ratio (potentially irregular ones), clarifying whether smooth p and q imply a smooth ratio (or conversely, a smooth ratio implies the existence of smooth p and q) would be helpful.

Further discussion of the dimensional dependence of the reported convergence rates is warranted. Specifying for which dimensions the proposed method is expected to be most effective would aid potential users in assessing its applicability. Additionally, elaborating on the motivation for the specific family of test functions, U, chosen would be appreciated.

Finally, a more foundational question arises in the context of covariate shift and similar problems: identifying a generic cost function remains a major challenge. The authors’ approach of utilizing expectation operators and employing test functions to extract density information is a viable strategy. However, it would be interesting to understand the authors' perspective on whether the chosen {k(x,.)} represents an optimal approach. The work shares some conceptual similarities with [1] (Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor, *Smooth Operators*), which also leverages transformations to address the problem. While this paper’s method of working with test functions for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is an improvement, the overarching strategy exhibits parallels. 

Despite these suggestions, this is a strong paper with a rigorous convergence analysis and supporting experimental results. It addresses a relevant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
