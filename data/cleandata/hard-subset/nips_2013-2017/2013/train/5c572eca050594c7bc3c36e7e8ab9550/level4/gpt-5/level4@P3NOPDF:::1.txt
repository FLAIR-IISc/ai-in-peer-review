Summary:
The paper proposes a new method for density ratio estimation p/q by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. This is a fresh perspective relative to prior approaches and is paired with a Tikhonov regularization scheme. The problem is important and the paper is clearly written and engaging.

Strengths:
- Novel and interesting formulation of density ratio estimation via a Fredholm integral equation.
- The problem setting is significant, with broad applicability in machine learning.
- The exposition is generally clear and pleasant to read.

Weaknesses, questions, and suggestions:
1) Missing technical details and references. The paper uses statements such as ∫ k_t(x, y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) without sufficient justification. Please provide precise assumptions (e.g., properties of k_t, smoothness of f, domain conditions) and include proofs or references. Even if standard, these steps are central to the approach and should be documented, either in the main text or supplementary material.

2) Relation to density estimation (q = 1). In the special case q = 1, your method yields a density estimator. It is intriguing that consistency appears to hold without the bandwidth t → 0, in contrast to standard KDE. Please clarify:
   - Why and under what assumptions your estimator remains consistent with a fixed bandwidth.
   - How the role of the bandwidth and regularization in your method differs from KDE’s bandwidth.
   - Whether there is a bias–variance tradeoff analogous to KDE and how it interacts with the regularization.

3) Brief primer on Tikhonov regularization. Many NIPS readers may be unfamiliar with the functional-analytic perspective. A short explanation (what it is, why it arises in ill-posed inverse problems, and how your choice of regularizer and parameter affects the solution) would improve accessibility. A concise reference list would also help.

4) Proof placement and verification. All proofs are in the supplementary material. Consider moving key lemmas or proof sketches to the main text, especially those that convey the core intuition (e.g., stability of the operator, bias decomposition, and the main rate-driving arguments). I did not independently verify the proofs; clearer signposting of assumptions and intermediate steps would help readers assess correctness.

5) Comparison to the naive plug-in approach (estimate p and q separately, then take their ratio). Please discuss theoretically and empirically why your direct approach is preferable:
   - Error amplification in ratios and instability when q is small.
   - Potential advantages of regularizing the ratio directly (e.g., control over smoothness relative to q).
   - Any results showing improved risk or rates versus plug-in under typical smoothness/low-noise conditions.

6) Rates and optimality. Corollaries 2 and 4 give upper bounds on convergence rates. How do these compare to known minimax rates for density ratio estimation? For example, prior work (e.g., [12]) also derives minimax bounds. Are your rates tight, or is there a gap? If lower bounds are out of scope, a discussion that situates your rates among existing results (including dependence on smoothness, dimension, and kernel bandwidth/regularization) would be valuable.

7) Dataset references. Please provide citations for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS), as well as any preprocessing details. This is important for reproducibility and for readers unfamiliar with these benchmarks.

8) Toy problems and qualitative assessment. A simple 1D experiment with known p and q would greatly aid intuition. Plotting the true p/q alongside estimates for varying bandwidths and regularization would help readers:
   - Assess bias, variance, and stability.
   - Understand sensitivity to hyperparameters.
   - See how your method behaves where q is small or rapidly varying.

Additional minor suggestions:
- Clarify the assumptions on the kernel k_t (positivity, normalization, smoothness, relation to a heat kernel or diffusion operator) and the domain (e.g., R^d vs. manifold) under which your approximation claims hold.
- Explicitly state the dependence of constants in big-O terms on dimension, smoothness, and kernel parameters.
- Provide guidance for hyperparameter selection (bandwidth and regularization), including any data-driven procedures you recommend, and discuss their computational cost.
- Briefly describe computational complexity and scalability, since density ratio estimation often targets large datasets.

Overall assessment:
This is a thoughtful and well-presented paper with a novel formulation of density ratio estimation. The work would be strengthened by tighter theoretical positioning (particularly regarding optimality and comparisons to [12] and related methods), clearer documentation of technical steps and assumptions, and more comprehensive empirical validation, including simple toy problems and discussion of the plug-in baseline. With these additions, the contribution would be both clearer and more compelling to the community.
