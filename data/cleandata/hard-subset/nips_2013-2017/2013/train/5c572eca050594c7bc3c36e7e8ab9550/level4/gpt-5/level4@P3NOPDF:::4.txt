Summary
The paper proposes two kernel-based estimators for the Radon–Nikodym derivative f = p/q using the identities (i) E_p[k(x,·) f] = E_q[k(x,·)] and (ii) E_p[k(x,·) f] ≈ q(x). By choosing test functions from a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), both estimators reduce to tractable kernel methods based on empirical expectations. The authors provide convergence guarantees under standard smoothness and boundedness conditions on p and q, with rates reflecting the ambient dimension. The presentation is clear, and the algorithms are well motivated and supported by experiments.

Strengths
- Elegant formulation that leverages RKHS test functions to obtain practical estimators of density ratios.
- Solid theoretical analysis with finite-sample bounds and clear dependence on smoothness and dimension.
- Methodologically useful: the framework unifies moment-matching and a regression-style approach to q via kernels.
- Experiments appear to substantiate the claims.

Main questions and suggestions
1) Intuition for estimator (i). While the proof is rigorous, a short intuitive explanation would help: if the class {k(x,·)} is rich enough (e.g., a characteristic kernel making mean embeddings injective) then equality of E_p[f φ] and E_q[φ] for all φ in the RKHS pins down f uniquely in L2(p). This mirrors kernel mean matching: matching all linear functionals over a dense test class identifies the target. For (ii), the intuition is clearer via bandwidth/locality; it would be helpful to make the analogous “completeness/identifiability” intuition explicit for (i).

2) Choice of norms/objectives (L2(p) vs L2(q)). Early sections optimize over L2(p), while Eq. (9) mixes L2(p) and L2(q). Please motivate this design choice. What downstream risks do these objectives control? For example, under covariate shift, one often wants to control prediction risk under p while observing samples from q. Can you provide guidance on selecting the training objective based on the target task (e.g., importance-weighted ERM, off-policy evaluation), and whether your bounds translate into guarantees for those tasks?

3) Applications and the value of estimating f. Since empirical objectives already use estimates of E_p and E_q, when is it preferable to estimate f rather than the final target expectation directly? In importance sampling, to estimate E_p[h] from q-samples the weights are p/q; conversely, to estimate E_q[h] from p-samples the weights are q/p. Please clarify the intended direction and main use cases. It would help to emphasize scenarios where a reusable estimate of f is advantageous (e.g., multiple downstream functionals h, covariate shift correction, domain adaptation, off-policy evaluation) versus computing a single expectation.

4) Regularization on f versus assumptions on p and q. You regularize f for smoothness, but the theorems assume smooth, bounded densities. Please discuss the relationship: under what conditions on p and q does f inherit smoothness (e.g., p and q α-smooth and bounded away from zero implies f is α-smooth)? Conversely, highlight failure modes (e.g., when q is small, f can be spiky) and how your regularizer mitigates them. Stating minimal sufficient conditions for f’s smoothness would strengthen the story.

5) Dimensional dependence. The rates depend on dimension, as expected. Please discuss practical regimes of d where the method is effective, and whether additional structure (e.g., low-dimensional manifolds, additive models, product kernels) can alleviate the curse of dimensionality. Any empirical sensitivity analysis versus d and bandwidth would be useful.

6) Test function family U and kernel choice. The choice U = {k(x,·)} is natural computationally. Please provide principled motivation: e.g., identifiability via characteristic kernels, approximation properties of the RKHS, and stability considerations. Discuss sensitivity to kernel type and bandwidth and provide practical guidance for selection (e.g., median heuristic, cross-validation), as the choice can dominate performance.

7) Relation to prior work. There seems to be conceptual overlap with kernel mean matching and related density-ratio estimators (e.g., KLIEP, uLSIF), and also similarity in spirit to “Smooth Operators” (Grünwald, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor) in using operator equations and test functions to recover target quantities. It would help to sharpen the contrast: what can your two estimators guarantee or compute that previous approaches could not, and under what assumptions? Do your bounds improve known rates, or do you provide new identifiability conditions?

8) Practicalities. A few clarifications would improve reproducibility:
- Do you recommend sample-splitting or cross-fitting when f is used in downstream tasks, to avoid overfitting to the same samples used to fit f?
- How are regularization parameters selected in practice?
- What is the computational complexity, and how does it scale with sample size and kernel bandwidth?

Minor comments
- Be consistent with notation: define f once (Radon–Nikodym derivative p/q) and use it throughout. Clearly distinguish L2(p) and L2(q) norms and inner products.
- In Eq. (ii), clarify the approximation E_p[k(x,·) f] ≈ q(x): specify whether this is exact for certain kernels or an approximation governed by bandwidth.
- Provide a brief statement on conditions (e.g., characteristic kernels, density boundedness) under which estimator (i) is identifiable.
- A short discussion on robustness when q has light tails relative to p (large f) would be valuable.

Overall assessment
This is a timely and well-executed contribution on kernel-based estimation of density ratios, with a clean formulation, solid theory, and promising empirical results. Addressing the points above—especially the intuition and identifiability for (i), the objective/norm choice, the relation between smoothness assumptions and regularization, and clearer positioning versus prior work—would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance after revision.
