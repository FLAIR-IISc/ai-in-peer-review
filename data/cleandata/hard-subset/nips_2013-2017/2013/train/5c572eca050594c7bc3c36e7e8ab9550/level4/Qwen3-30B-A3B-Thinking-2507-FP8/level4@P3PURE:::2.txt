

This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio between two probability distributions. The first method leverages the importance sampling equation, reformulating it as a regularization problem incorporating an $L^2$-norm penalty for deviation from equality and an RKHS regularization term (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that must vanish for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrate practical efficacy through experiments.  

A key strength of the proposed approach lies in its compatibility with cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection—a significant advantage over existing methods like KMM. Unlike supervised learning, where CV is standard for hyperparameter tuning, unsupervised learning lacks generalizable methods for this purpose. The $L^2$ formulation enables CV to be applied effectively to density ratio estimation, addressing a critical gap. It would be beneficial to explicitly note that RKHS-based objective functions (e.g., in KMM) are unsuitable for CV, as their values depend on the kernel choice and thus lack comparability across different kernels.  

In Theorem 1, the convergence rate includes a factor of $-t / \log \lambda$, resulting in a suboptimal rate for fixed $t$. In kernel method literature (e.g., Caponnetto and De Vito, 2007), polynomial rates in sample size are typically achievable under fixed $t$ by adjusting $\lambda$'s decay rate and imposing spectral assumptions on the integral operator. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss whether additional assumptions (e.g., on the spectrum of $K_{p,t}$ or stronger conditions on $q/p$) could yield a polynomial rate. Such an analysis would strengthen the theoretical contribution.  

The paper assumes $q/p$ belongs to a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev), a condition that is generally restrictive as it requires prior knowledge of the density ratio or tail behavior of $p$ and $q$. Crucially, under this assumption, the reciprocal ratio $p/q$ often exhibits poor behavior. The authors should address this limitation in the discussion, as it significantly impacts the applicability of density ratio estimation.  

While not a requirement, the supplementary material requires substantial revision. Numerous typos and minor errors impede verification of the theoretical results. For instance:  
- Lemma 5: $W_2^2$ should be $W_s^2$, and the bound should be $D_1 (t / -\log \lambda)^2 + \lambda^{1-\alpha} D_2 \|f\|_2^2$.  
- Expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Equation (31) is correct.  
- In Lemma 5’s proof, line 1147 must specify the integral domain as $\|\xi\|^2 \geq \cdots$.  
Given that the convergence results constitute the paper’s core contribution, the authors should prioritize correcting these issues.  

The paper proposes a new approach to density ratio estimation, with the key strength being the effective application of cross-validation for parameter selection.
