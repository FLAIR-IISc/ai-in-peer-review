This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, *q(x)/p(x)*, where *p(x)* is a density and *q(x)* is either a density or an arbitrary function. This is motivated by applications in importance sampling and covariate shift. The authors reformulate this problem as an inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm equation of the first kind, and leverage regularization techniques from Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop a family of algorithms termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). They provide theoretical guarantees, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, and demonstrate promising experimental results.

This is a well-executed and timely work establishing a connection between density ratio estimation and classical operator theory. The approach of formulating the problem as a Fredholm equation is insightful and allows for the application of powerful theoretical tools. The resulting algorithms are presented clearly and appear straightforward to implement. The theoretical analysis, including convergence rates, is a significant contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Formulation:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation provides a fresh perspective and bridges the gap between statistical learning and classical analysis.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, which is a strong point.
*   **Algorithm Flexibility:** The FIRE algorithms are flexible and easy to implement within the kernel learning framework.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on several datasets and in various settings.
* **Clear Presentation**: The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Intuition for Convergence (Eq. (i)):** While the convergence analysis is strong, a brief intuitive explanation of *why* optimizing over the kernel functions {*k(x,.)*} guarantees convergence to the ratio *q/p* – especially for the first integral equation – would strengthen the paper.
*   **Cost Function Motivation:** The switch from solely using the L2,p norm to incorporating L2,q (Eq. 9) could benefit from more detailed motivation. Linking the cost function choices explicitly to specific applications would be valuable.
*   **Application Justification:** The authors mention that estimating *q/p* is useful even if estimating *Ep* and *Eq* is challenging. However, a more in-depth discussion of scenarios where this approach provides a distinct advantage over directly utilizing *Ep* and *Eq* – potentially focusing on scenarios with difficult sampling from *q* – would be helpful.
* **Smoothness and the Ratio:** It is worth discussing the relationship between the smoothness assumptions on *q* and *p*, and the smoothness of their ratio. Establishing this link could strengthen the theoretical foundation.
*   **Dimensionality Dependence:** A discussion of the dependence of the convergence rates on the dimensionality of the data (*d*) and when the method is expected to be most effective would be valuable.
*   **Test Function Family:** Further justification for the choice of the family of test functions *U* used in the cross-validation procedure would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** While the connection to [1] (Grünewälder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor) is noted, a more nuanced discussion of how the proposed approach differs and complements existing methods for addressing similar problems – particularly in terms of generic cost function design – would be helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to density ratio estimation.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees for the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the practical utility of the method.
*   The work addresses a relevant problem in machine learning and statistics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Some aspects of the motivation and intuition behind certain design choices (e.g., the L2,q norm, the test function family) could be further clarified.
*   A more thorough discussion of the dimensionality dependence of the convergence rates and the limitations of the method in high dimensions would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by providing a principled and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation. While some aspects could benefit from further clarification, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
