### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability densities, q/p, a crucial task in areas like importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The authors introduce a novel and principled approach by reformulating this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. By applying Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm, they derive a flexible family of algorithms named FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator).

The paper proposes two main settings: Type I, where samples from both distributions p and q are available, and Type II, where samples are available from p, and the function q is known at these sample points. The framework leads to simple, closed-form solutions within the standard kernel methods paradigm. The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis, establishing concentration bounds and convergence rates for FIRE using Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. A significant practical contribution is the proposal of a completely unsupervised model selection technique called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). Finally, the paper presents extensive experimental results, demonstrating the strong performance of FIRE in comparison to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF).

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is novel and insightful. It connects a key problem in machine learning to the well-established field of operator theory and inverse problems, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms.

2.  **Simple and Flexible Algorithms:** The regularization framework naturally leads to a family of simple and easy-to-implement kernel-based algorithms (FIRE). The distinction between Type I and Type II settings makes the approach applicable to a variety of practical scenarios, such as when sampling from q is difficult but evaluating it is feasible.

3.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates. Proving these results for distributions on both R^d and on smooth manifolds is a significant strength, adding to the paper's technical depth.

4.  **Practical Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised hyperparameter tuning is a major practical contribution. Model selection is a notoriously difficult aspect of unsupervised and semi-supervised learning, and providing a well-motivated technique specifically for this setting is highly valuable.

5.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing FIRE against several relevant and state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets and tasks (regression and classification). The results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness and computational efficiency of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Assumptions on Densities and the Dominating Measure:** The paper's framework rests on the assumption that the probability measures p and q possess densities. This can be a strong assumption, as it is well-known that measures without densities can exist arbitrarily close to those with them. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion on the robustness of the method to deviations from this assumption.

2.  **Clarity of Foundational Assumptions:** The theoretical setup could be presented more clearly.
    *   The dominating measure (presumably the Lebesgue measure, given the context of integral equations and Gaussian kernels) is not explicitly stated. This should be clarified for rigor.
    *   A critical requirement for the ratio q(x)/p(x) to be well-defined is that the support of q is contained within the support of p (i.e., p(x) > 0 where q(x) > 0). This assumption is not explicitly stated or discussed. Addressing how the algorithm behaves when p(x) is near zero would be valuable, especially since the TIKDE baseline method explicitly handles this via thresholding.

3.  **Organization of Theoretical Assumptions:** While the necessary assumptions for the theoretical analysis are provided in Section 3, the paper would be clearer if these key conditions (e.g., smoothness of densities, bounds on their values, properties of the domain Ω) were summarized more prominently at the beginning of the paper or in Section 2 where the algorithms are introduced.

**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the claims are rigorously supported by both a detailed theoretical analysis and strong empirical evidence. The work appears complete and carefully executed.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core ideas are communicated effectively, and the algorithms are presented with explicit formulas. However, as noted in the "Weaknesses" section, clarity could be improved by explicitly stating foundational assumptions regarding the densities and their underlying measure earlier in the text.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it leverages familiar tools like kernel methods and Tikhonov regularization, the formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a novel and significant conceptual contribution. This approach provides a new perspective that is distinct from prior methods like KMM and LSIF.

*   **Significance:** The results are significant. Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with broad applicability in machine learning. This paper introduces a powerful, theoretically sound, and practical family of algorithms, along with a novel method for model selection. The ideas presented are likely to be used by other researchers and practitioners and could inspire further work at the intersection of machine learning and classical analysis.

**Conclusion**

This is a strong paper that makes several important contributions. It introduces a novel and principled framework for density ratio estimation, provides rigorous theoretical backing, and develops a practical unsupervised model selection technique. Despite some minor issues regarding the clarity of its foundational assumptions, the work is of high quality, originality, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
