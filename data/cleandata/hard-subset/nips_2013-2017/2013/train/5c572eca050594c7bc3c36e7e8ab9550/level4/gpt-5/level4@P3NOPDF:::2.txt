Summary
The paper proposes two RKHS-based methods for density-ratio estimation r = q/p, each cast as a Tikhonov-regularized solution to a Fredholm equation: (i) a Type I formulation based directly on the importance-sampling identity and an L2 fitting loss under p, and (ii) a Type II formulation built from a kernel operator whose locality parameter is sent to zero for consistency. The authors provide convergence guarantees and empirical evaluations. A notable advantage over prior approaches such as KMM is that the L2 objective enables principled cross-validation (CV) for model selection, bringing to density-ratio estimation a tuning methodology that is standard in supervised learning but largely missing in unsupervised settings.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual link between importance sampling and regularized operator inversion, leading to two practical algorithms.
- The use of an L2 loss under p makes CV natural and comparable across kernels and regularization parameters.
- Theoretical results establish consistency and provide explicit rates (albeit conservative).
- Empirical results are encouraging, and the overall presentation highlights the relationship and differences with KMM.

Weaknesses and questions
- Convergence rate. In Theorem 1, the term involving t/(-log λ) leads to slow rates for fixed t. In much of the kernel literature, polynomial rates in n can be obtained by coupling λ with n under source and capacity assumptions on the target and the operator spectrum. Are there additional, reasonable assumptions (e.g., source conditions on r = q/p and spectral decay assumptions on the integral operator Kp,t) under which your estimator achieves polynomial rates for fixed t? See, e.g., Caponnetto and De Vito (2007), who derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression using such assumptions. It would substantially strengthen the theory to add a corollary or discussion giving polynomial rates under stronger yet standard assumptions.
- Assumptions on r = q/p. Successful density-ratio estimation typically assumes r belongs to a smoothness class (e.g., Sobolev or RKHS). This can be strong or unrealistic without prior knowledge of p and q, especially in the tails. Moreover, regularity of r often implies that the inverse ratio p/q is poorly behaved. Please discuss this limitation explicitly, including practical guidance on when the assumptions are reasonable and how violations (e.g., heavy-tail mismatch) affect the methods.
- Comparing objectives across kernels. It would be helpful to explicitly remark that objectives defined as RKHS norms or kernel-dependent moments (as in KMM) are not directly comparable across kernels, making CV ill-posed there. In contrast, an L2(p) loss yields kernel-comparable validation scores. This point is important for practitioners choosing among kernels and bandwidths.
- Practical guidance. Some additional details would improve usability:
  - How should one schedule the locality parameter for the Type II method as n grows in practice, and how sensitive is performance to this schedule?
  - What CV objective do you recommend (e.g., held-out L2(p) risk via importance-weighted samples), and how do you implement splitting when only samples from p and q are available?
  - Any advice on computational complexity and numerical stability of the operator inversions?

Relation to prior work
The relationship to KMM and kernel ridge regression is well motivated. Because your approach uses a least-squares criterion, it also connects to LSIF/KLIEP-type methods; clarifying similarities and differences (e.g., loss, regularization, and CV comparability) would further situate your contribution.

Comments on theory
- Please consider adding results under standard source and capacity assumptions to yield polynomial rates. Even a brief theorem sketch with assumptions on the spectrum of Kp,t and smoothness of r would help readers align your rates with the broader kernel literature.
- Clarify whether t is a tuning parameter or a fixed property of the kernel family, and how it should scale with sample size if at all.

Quality of writing and presentation
The main paper is generally clear. However, the supplementary material contains numerous typographical and minor mathematical errors that make it hard to verify the results. Given that the convergence analysis is a central contribution, I strongly encourage a careful revision. Specific issues I noticed include:
- Lemma 5: “W2^2” should read “Ws^2” (presumably W_s^2).
- Lemma 5 bound: the expression should be D1 (t/(-log λ))^2 + λ^{1−α} D2 ||f||_2^2.
- Lines 675 and 686: the displayed expressions appear incorrect; Eq. (31) is consistent and appears to be the correct form.
- Proof of Lemma 5 (around line 1147): the domain of the integral should be restricted to the region {||ξ||^2 ≥ …} rather than taken over the entire space; please correct the domain and the ensuing bounds accordingly.
- Throughout, check subscripts/superscripts, operator definitions, and the dependence of constants on kernel and distributional quantities.

Minor suggestions
- State explicitly whether the L2 loss is with respect to p or q and ensure consistent notation throughout.
- Provide a brief discussion on when to prefer the Type I versus Type II formulation (e.g., data regime, kernel choice, anticipated smoothness).
- Include a short paragraph on robustness to kernel mis-specification and extreme-density regions, and whether trimming or regularizing r in the tails is advisable.

Overall assessment
This paper introduces a compelling and practically useful approach to density-ratio estimation. The use of an L2 objective that enables CV is a notable and timely contribution, with clear advantages over objectives whose scales are kernel dependent. While some of the theoretical rates are conservative and the supplement needs careful correction, the core ideas are sound and well motivated. With strengthened theoretical discussion (polynomial rates under standard assumptions) and a thoroughly revised supplement, I would view this work favorably.
