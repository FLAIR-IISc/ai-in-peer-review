This paper introduces two novel methods for density ratio estimation, framing them as regularization problems rooted in Fredholm equations of the first and second kind. The first method leverages importance sampling and employs an L² norm to measure deviation from equality, alongside a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) regularization term. The second method utilizes a kernel with a locality parameter designed to approach zero for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem. The authors support their approach with theoretical convergence results and empirical demonstrations.

A key strength of the proposed methods, particularly the L² formulation of the first approach, lies in its compatibility with cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. While CV is a standard practice in supervised learning, its application to unsupervised learning problems like density ratio estimation has been limited. This work addresses that gap with a clever and potentially impactful idea. Notably, the authors correctly point out that the objective functions commonly used in RKHS-based methods, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), are less amenable to CV due to the kernel-dependence of their values, making direct comparison across different kernel choices problematic.

However, the convergence rate presented in Theorem 1 exhibits a slow dependency on *t* due to the (-t/log λ) factor. In the kernel methods literature, it is often possible to achieve polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size when *t* is fixed, following a suitable choice of λ. I encourage the authors to investigate whether stronger assumptions could yield such a polynomial rate for their estimator. For example, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression by imposing assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K<sub>p,t</sub>. Exploring similar assumptions regarding the ratio q/p might lead to improved convergence guarantees and would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution of the paper. 

A crucial consideration in density ratio estimation, which the paper would benefit from discussing, is the inherent difficulty of assuming a well-behaved function class (e.g., Sobolev space) for q/p. Such assumptions, while necessary for theoretical analysis, are strong and require prior knowledge about either the ratio itself or the tails of the underlying probability distributions *p* and *q*. Moreover, if q/p is constrained to a smooth class, the inverse ratio p/q often exhibits poor behavior. Acknowledging this limitation and its implications for practical applications would provide a more balanced and complete presentation of the method.

Finally, the supplementary material requires significant revision. Numerous typos and minor errors impede verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. Specifically, in Lemma 5, W<sub>2</sub><sup>2</sup> should be W<sub>s</sub><sup>2</sup>, and the bound should be D<sub>1</sub>(t/-log λ)<sup>2</sup> + λ<sup>1-α</sup> D<sub>2</sub> ||f||<sub>2</sub><sup>2</sup>. The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq.(31) is correct. In the proof of Lemma 5, the integral domain at line 1147 should be  ||ξ||<sup>2</sup> ≥.  Given that the convergence results form a core contribution of this work, I strongly recommend the authors prioritize a thorough revision of the supplementary material to ensure its accuracy and clarity.

In summary, this paper proposes a valuable new approach to density ratio estimation, with the notable advantage of enabling effective parameter tuning via cross-validation. Addressing the concerns regarding convergence rates and the limitations of underlying assumptions, as well as meticulously revising the supplementary material, would significantly enhance the paper's impact and strengthen its contribution to the field.
