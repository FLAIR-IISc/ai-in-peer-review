This paper proposes a method for estimating the ratio of two probability density functions. The approach formulates the problem as an inverse problem by utilizing a kernel and a Fredholm integral equation.

A fundamental concern with the paper's premise is its reliance on the existence of probability densities. In machine learning, we often deal with empirical distributions or complex data generating processes where the existence of a well-defined density with respect to a dominating measure is not guaranteed. More formally, it is well-established that for any probability measure P that possesses a density with respect to a measure ν, there exist other probability measures arbitrarily close to P (i.e., within any epsilon neighborhood) that do not have such a density. This makes the direct estimation of a density ratio, as proposed here, a potentially ill-posed problem from a practical machine learning perspective, where the true underlying measures may not be absolutely continuous.

Furthermore, the paper's theoretical framework lacks several crucial details, which makes it difficult to assess the rigor of the results. The authors should provide a clear and consolidated statement of their assumptions. Specifically:

1.  **Dominating Measure:** The paper does not explicitly state the dominating measure ν with respect to which the densities are defined. Is it the Lebesgue measure for continuous variables, the counting measure for discrete ones, or another measure? This ambiguity is significant because it affects the interpretation of the results, particularly concerning the handling of sets of ν-measure zero.

2.  **Domain of the Variables:** The domain of the input variable x is not specified. Is it assumed to be R^d, or does the framework apply to more general topological spaces? This information is essential for understanding the scope and applicability of the proposed method.

3.  **Support of the Densities:** The assumptions regarding the support of the densities p(x) and q(x) are unclear. Is it assumed that the densities are strictly positive across their entire domain? How does the method handle regions where p(x) or the denominator q(x) might be zero? This is particularly critical for a method focused on estimating a ratio.

In summary, while the formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is an interesting direction, the paper currently suffers from a disconnect with the practicalities of machine learning problems and a lack of theoretical clarity. Addressing the fundamental issue of assuming density existence and explicitly stating the underlying mathematical assumptions are crucial steps for establishing the method's validity and relevance.
