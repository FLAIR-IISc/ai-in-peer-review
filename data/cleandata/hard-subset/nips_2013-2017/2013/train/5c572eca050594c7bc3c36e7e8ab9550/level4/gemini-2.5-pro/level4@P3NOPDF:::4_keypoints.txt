- Introduces two novel RKHS-based estimators for the Radon–Nikodym derivative using equalities E_p[k(x,·)f]=E_q[k(x,·)] and E_p[k(x,·)f]≈q(x), replacing integral operators with empirical estimates.  
- Uses the RKHS {k(x,·)} as test functions to enable kernel-method optimization.  
- Provides convergence guarantees under standard smoothness and boundedness assumptions; rates degrade with data dimensionality.  
- Overall assessment: elegant methods, sound theory, convincing experiments; recommendation to accept.  

Detailed concerns/suggestions:
1. Request an intuitive argument why minimizing the RKHS objective guarantees convergence to the true ratio for the first estimator.  
2. Ask for clearer motivation and justification for the chosen cost functions (L2,p vs combined L2,p and L2,q) and links to downstream tasks.  
3. Question the importance-sampling motivation: if empirical E_p is available, why learn the ratio; ask for scenarios where learning the ratio is preferable (e.g., when sampling from p is difficult).  
4. Request discussion on the relationship between smoothness regularization on the ratio and smoothness assumptions on p and q (do smooth p,q imply a smooth ratio?).  
5. Ask for practical guidance on the curse-of-dimensionality implications: what dimensional ranges are feasible and how performance scales versus other methods.  
6. Request principled justification for choosing the RKHS as the family of test functions and why it is sufficient.  
7. Suggest situating the work in broader context (e.g., relation to Grünewald et al. “Smooth Operators”) and highlighting specific advantages of the kernel-based test-function approach.  
- Concluding note: addressing these points would improve the paper’s clarity and impact.
