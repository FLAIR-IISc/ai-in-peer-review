Summary and relation to prior work
The paper reformulates density-ratio estimation f(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it with RKHS-based Tikhonov regularization. Two complementary formulations are used:

- Type I: Ep[k(x, ·) f(·)] = Eq[k(x, ·) 1], yielding Kp f = Kq 1 as an operator equation that can be estimated from samples of p and q.
- Type II: For “local” kernels kt (e.g., Gaussian), Ep[kt(x, ·) f(·)] ≈ q(x), i.e., Kt,p f ≈ q, which applies when q is known (possibly up to a constant) or observable at p-sample points.

Empirical versions replace integrals with averages, and the representer theorem yields simple linear-system solutions (the FIRE family). The authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth submanifolds, and propose an unsupervised model-selection scheme, CD-CV, based on cross-density moment matching using test functions. Experiments compare FIRE with KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline under covariate shift and importance-weighted learning.

The approach connects kernel mean matching (KMM) to an explicit inverse problem and adds regularization, out-of-sample estimation, and theoretical analysis. It also relates to LSIF/KLIEP by using test-function moment discrepancies, but without restricting f to a fixed finite basis.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem yields a principled, flexible framework bridging operator theory and kernel learning.
- Algorithmic simplicity: closed-form solutions via RKHS representer theorem; natural out-of-sample predictions (unlike standard KMM).
- Theory: nonasymptotic bounds and convergence rates with explicit dependence on bandwidth, regularization, and intrinsic dimension (including manifold support).
- Practical model selection: CD-CV provides a reasonable, fully unsupervised procedure that aligns with the moment-matching objective.
- Empirical results: competitive or better performance and stability vs. KMM/LSIF/TIKDE in covariate shift and importance weighting.

Weaknesses and questions
- Identifiability via test functions: For Type II, the δ-kernel limit makes the consistency intuitive. For Type I, an intuitive argument for why optimizing over {k(x, ·)} suffices for recovering f would help. For example, under what conditions on k and p is the operator Kp injective on the function class of interest? Can this be linked to universality or characteristic kernels and to the invertibility/spectral properties of Kp?
- Loss choice: The paper optimizes L2,p (and a γ-combination with L2,q). More motivation connecting these losses to downstream tasks would be useful. For instance, minimizing L2,p aligns with importance-weighted risk minimization using samples from p, while L2,q may better control errors where test density mass lies. When should one prefer the mixture, and how should γ be chosen?
- Importance sampling motivation: Since the empirical objectives already estimate Ep and Eq, when is learning f preferable to directly estimating Eq[u] for the target u? Clarify scenarios (e.g., many downstream u, covariate shift classification, Type II with known q but no q-samples, AIS/MCMC contexts) where a reusable f is advantageous or necessary.
- Regularization and smoothness: The algorithm penalizes the RKHS norm of f = q/p, while assumptions are on p and q. Can the authors discuss sufficient conditions under which smooth p and q (bounded away from 0) imply smooth f, and the reverse? Are there practical constraints (nonnegativity, normalization Ep[f]=1) that could be incorporated into FIRE, analogous to KMM’s constraints?
- Dimensional dependence: The rates deteriorate with ambient dimension but improve on a d-dimensional manifold. Guidance on practical regimes (n vs. d, bandwidth scaling) where FIRE is effective would help practitioners.
- Choice of test functions U in CD-CV: The use of random linear and half-space functions is plausible, but a stronger motivation would be welcome. For example, match U to expected downstream function classes (linear predictors, margin-based classifiers), discuss sensitivity to U, and report robustness across choices.
- Positioning vs. related work: The relation to KMM is compelling (Type I with RKHS loss), and the paper argues for better stability and out-of-sample behavior. It would help to elaborate on the connection to “Smooth Operators” (Grünewälder et al.), which also leverages operator identities and test functions, and to clarify what is fundamentally new here (inverse problem setup for f, regularized solution with explicit rates for density ratios).

Quality
Technically sound with clear derivations, appropriate use of RKHS regularization, and meaningful nonasymptotic bounds. Some assumptions (e.g., smoothness, boundedness, kernel choices) are standard; more intuition on operator injectivity and kernel requirements would improve accessibility.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Suggested additions: brief intuitive argument for Type I identifiability; explicit guidance on loss selection; a short discussion on constraints (nonnegativity/normalization). A summarized statement of when to use Type I vs. Type II in practice would also help.

Originality
Recasting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem and solving it with RKHS regularization, along with manifold-aware rates and an unsupervised CD-CV scheme, is a novel and useful synthesis that extends beyond KMM/LSIF.

Significance
The work addresses a central component of transfer learning and importance weighting with a principled, practical method, theoretical guarantees, and competitive performance. The manifold perspective and unsupervised model selection broaden applicability.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled inverse-problem formulation with simple kernel algorithms and out-of-sample capability
- Solid theoretical guarantees, including manifold settings
- Useful unsupervised model selection (CD-CV)
- Strong motivation and relevance to covariate shift and importance weighting
- Empirically competitive and stable

Cons for acceptance
- Limited intuitive explanation of Type I identifiability and kernel/operator conditions
- Sparse guidance on choosing among L2,p, L2,q, and mixtures, and on selecting U
- No discussion of constraints ensuring a valid ratio (nonnegativity, normalization)
- Practical guidance on dimensionality regimes could be strengthened

Overall evaluation
A timely and strong contribution that advances density-ratio estimation through a rigorous and practical framework. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarifications above.
