The authors propose two estimators of the Radon–Nikodym derivative f = p/q based on the identities: (i) E_p[k(x, ·) f] = E_q[k(x, ·)], and (ii) E_p[k(x, ·) f] ≈ q(x). They replace the associated integral operators with empirical counterparts. The set {k(x, ·)} is treated as a family of test functions over which the discrepancy between left- and right-hand sides is minimized; this choice enables the use of standard kernel methods for optimization.

They provide convergence guarantees for both formulations under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. The convergence rates depend critically on the ambient dimension, as is typical for density estimation problems. Overall, I find this a well-executed piece of work.

Comments and questions:

- Convergence intuition. Optimizing over the family {k(x, ·)} appears sufficient to guarantee convergence of the estimator to the true ratio. For (ii), this is intuitive via the kernel bandwidth: as the bandwidth shrinks, k(·, x) approximates a delta function and E_p[k(x, ·) f] recovers q(x). It would be helpful to add a short intuitive argument for why optimizing over {k(x, ·)} also suffices in case (i), beyond the formal convergence proof.

- Objective and norms. On page 2 you optimize over L2,p, whereas in (9) the objective mixes L2,p and L2,q terms. Some motivation for this particular choice of cost would be useful. In the end, the objective should be aligned with downstream tasks; could you link specific choices (e.g., L2,p vs L2,q weighting) to representative applications where each is preferable?

- Applications and motivation. One application you mention is importance sampling, i.e., using weights p/q to translate expectations under q to expectations under p. In your setup, you already estimate E_p and E_q to form the empirical objective used to learn f. Why not directly use the E_q that is already being estimated? In what sense can a reweighted estimate E_q[h(x) p(x)/q(x)] be better than relying on E_q[h(x)] in the scenarios you have in mind? I am essentially asking for a clearer motivation of the overall setup, given that one must estimate both E_p and E_q to estimate p/q. You have a sentence on p. 5 noting that sampling under q may be difficult; is this the main application scenario motivating your approach?

- Regularization and smoothness. You regularize the ratio f = p/q for smoothness, while your theorems assume smoothness of p and q (which is the natural place to impose such assumptions). Is there a principled relation between smoothness of p, q and smoothness of the ratio f? For example, does p and q smooth (and bounded away from 0) imply a smooth f? Conversely, if f is smooth, do there exist smooth p and q generating it? I recognize there are many p, q pairs yielding the same ratio (e.g., by changing values on sets of measure zero or more adversarially), so clarifying the implications you actually need would be helpful.

- Dimensional dependence. It would be useful to discuss the dependence of your rates on d in more concrete terms. For what ranges of dimension (relative to sample sizes and assumed smoothness) do you expect the method to be practically effective?

- Choice of test functions. Is there a principled motivation for the particular family of test functions U = {k(x, ·)} (beyond computational convenience)? Under what conditions would alternative families be preferable?

- Broader perspective. In covariate shift and related density-ratio estimation settings, a central challenge is to define a generic but task-relevant cost. One approach, which you adopt, is to go through expectation operators and probe them with test functions to extract the ratio. Do you feel that using the RKHS kernel family {k(x, ·)} is “the right” approach here? There is some conceptual similarity to [1], which also uses transformations of expectations to access latent quantities. Your approach to the Radon–Nikodym derivative is appealing in its use of test functions, but the overall strategy seems related; a short discussion of these connections would be valuable.

I believe this is a timely and strong paper addressing a relevant problem, with solid convergence analysis and supporting experiments.

[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor
