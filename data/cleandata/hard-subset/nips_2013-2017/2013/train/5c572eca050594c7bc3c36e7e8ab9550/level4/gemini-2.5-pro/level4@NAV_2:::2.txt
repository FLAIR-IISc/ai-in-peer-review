This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the density ratio q/p by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose two main settings. The "Type I" setting leverages the importance sampling identity to construct an integral equation, while the "Type II" setting uses the property of local kernels (e.g., Gaussians) to derive an approximate integral equation. Both inverse problems are then solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, leading to a family of algorithms called FIRE. The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, and introduces a practical, unsupervised model selection technique named Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). The effectiveness of the proposed methods is demonstrated through experiments on several datasets.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength and a significant contribution of this work is its principled approach to unsupervised model selection for density ratio estimation. While the proposed formulation bears some resemblance to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), the use of an L2 objective function is a key differentiator. This choice naturally enables the use of cross-validation (the proposed CD-CV method) to select hyperparameters, a notoriously difficult problem in most unsupervised learning scenarios. The paper provides a clever and practical solution to this challenge.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Model Selection:** To further highlight the novelty of the CD-CV approach, it would be beneficial to explicitly discuss why objective functions based on an RKHS norm, as used in methods like KMM, are ill-suited for cross-validating kernel parameters. The authors could add a remark explaining that since the RKHS norm itself depends on the choice of kernel, the objective values are not directly comparable across different kernels, which reinforces the advantage of their L2-based formulation.

2.  **Theoretical Rates:** The theoretical analysis is a core component of the paper, but the convergence rates presented in Theorem 1 appear somewhat slow for a fixed kernel width `t`. In much of the kernel methods literature, polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size can be obtained under certain assumptions. It would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution to discuss whether faster, polynomial rates could be achieved for the FIRE estimator under stronger, yet reasonable, assumptions. For instance, following work like Caponnetto and De Vito (2007), assumptions on the spectral decay of the integral operator or stronger smoothness assumptions on the target ratio `q/p` might yield tighter bounds. A discussion of such possibilities would be very insightful.

3.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** The problem of density ratio estimation generally relies on the strong assumption that the ratio `q/p` belongs to a well-behaved function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This implies certain constraints on the tail behavior of the densities `p` and `q`. It is important to acknowledge this limitation, as the assumption can be violated in practice. Furthermore, if `q/p` is assumed to be smooth, the inverse ratio `p/q` may not be. Including a discussion on these inherent limitations of density ratio estimation would provide a more complete and nuanced presentation.

4.  **Supplementary Material:** The supplementary material requires careful revision. It contains several typos and minor errors that make it difficult to follow the proofs and verify the correctness of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. For example:
    *   In Lemma 5, `W2^2` should likely be `Ws^2`, and the bound appears to have a typo.
    *   The expressions at lines 675 and 686 seem incorrect, though the final `Eq.(31)` is correct.
    *   In the proof of Lemma 5, the integration domain at line 1147 should probably be `||ξ||^2 ≥ ...`.
    Given that the theoretical analysis is a central contribution, ensuring the clarity and correctness of the supplement is crucial.

In summary, the paper introduces an elegant and well-motivated approach to density ratio estimation. Its main contribution—a practical method for unsupervised cross-validation—is significant. The suggestions above are intended to help the authors further strengthen the clarity and theoretical depth of their work.
