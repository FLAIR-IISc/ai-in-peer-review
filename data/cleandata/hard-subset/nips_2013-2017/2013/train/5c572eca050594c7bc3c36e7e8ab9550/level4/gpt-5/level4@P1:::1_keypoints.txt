- Proposes estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x) by casting it as a Fredholm integral equation and solving via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form FIRE estimators.  
- Considers two settings: Type I (samples from p and q) and Type II (q known or evaluable at p-sampled points).  
- Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on R^d and on smooth submanifolds.  
- Introduces an unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) tailored to density-ratio estimation.  
- Empirically compares FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, with covariate-shift applications.  
- Relates to KMM, LSIF, KLIEP and classical inverse-problem/regularization literature; novelty is the operator-theoretic Fredholm formulation.  
- Strength: clear, well-written presentation of a practically important problem.  
- Strength: conceptual novelty in recasting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.  
- Strength: simple, implementable algorithms with out-of-sample prediction capability.  
- Strength: practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV).  
- Strength: solid theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels, including manifold settings.  
- Weakness: several technical claims lack proofs or precise citations (e.g., ∫ k_t(x,y)f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t)).  
- Weakness: unclear discussion of the bandwidth’s role here versus KDE; why bandwidth need not shrink to zero.  
- Weakness: paper should include a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization for non-expert readers.  
- Weakness: all proofs are deferred to the supplement and were not verified by the reviewer.  
- Weakness: missing discussion and/or empirical comparison to naive plug-in (estimate p and q separately then ratio).  
- Weakness: unclear how the provided rates compare to known minimax bounds (e.g., prior work [12]); need theoretical comparison.  
- Weakness: dataset references (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) are not provided.  
- Weakness: paper lacks a simple 1D toy example plotting true ratio and estimate to aid intuition.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound and principled with theoretical guarantees, but needs clearer comparisons to minimax rates and plug-in baselines.  
- Clarity assessment: generally well written but should add Tikhonov background, bandwidth discussion, and citations for approximation claims.  
- Originality assessment: novel and insightful Fredholm-equation perspective connecting kernel methods to operator theory.  
- Significance assessment: relevant to importance sampling and covariate shift; framework is flexible and practical.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel formulation, solid theory (including manifolds), practical CD-CV, clear writing, and application relevance.  
- Cons against acceptance: missing citations/proofs, insufficient discussion of bandwidth vs KDE and plug-in methods, unclear minimax comparison, lack of toy example and dataset references.  
- Recommended improvements: add proofs/citations for technical claims, clarify bandwidth role and plug-in inferiority, compare rates to minimax results, include dataset refs and a 1D illustrative example, and briefly introduce Tikhonov regularization.
