This paper introduces two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative, f = p/q, derived from two distinct equalities: (i) E_p[k(x,·)f] = E_q[k(x,·)] and (ii) E_p[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x). In practice, the integral operators are replaced by their empirical counterparts. The core idea is to treat the family of functions {k(x,·)} as a set of test functions and to minimize the discrepancy between the left and right sides of these equalities. A key advantage of this formulation is that it allows for the use of standard kernel methods for optimization. The authors provide convergence guarantees for both estimators under standard smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities p and q. As expected for a density estimation problem, the convergence rates exhibit a strong dependence on the dimensionality of the data space. Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution.

I have a few comments and questions for the authors:

- **Convergence Rates:** The paper shows that optimizing over the set of test functions {k(x,·)} is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the true density ratio. While this is intuitive for estimator (ii), particularly due to its dependence on the kernel bandwidth, a brief intuitive argument for why this also holds for estimator (i) would strengthen the paper.

- **Choice of Cost Function:** The authors first optimize over L_2(p) (page 2) but later, in equation (9), use a combination of L_2(p) and L_2(q). The paper would benefit from a more detailed motivation for these choices. Since the suitability of a cost function often depends on the target application, it would be valuable to explicitly link these specific choices to important tasks like importance sampling or covariate shift correction.

- **Motivation for Importance Sampling:** The paper mentions importance sampling as a key application, where the ratio q/p is used to transform an expectation under p to one under q. However, the proposed method requires samples from both p and q to form the empirical objective function. This raises a question: if samples from q are already available to construct an empirical estimate of E_q, what is the advantage of estimating the ratio q/p to compute E_p[q/p] instead of using the direct empirical estimate of E_q? The authors briefly mention on page 5 that sampling from q might be difficult, but a more thorough discussion on the scenarios where this indirect approach is superior would be beneficial. Is this the primary application setting the authors envision?

- **Smoothness Assumptions and Regularization:** The proposed method uses a regularizer that enforces smoothness on the estimated ratio p/q. In contrast, the theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities p and q. It would be helpful to clarify the relationship between these two notions of smoothness. For instance, while smooth p and q (bounded away from zero) likely imply a smooth ratio, the converse is not necessarily true, as many non-smooth densities can produce the same smooth ratio. A discussion on how the regularizer on the ratio relates to the assumptions on the underlying densities would be insightful.

- **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The convergence rates depend on the data dimensionality `d`. A brief discussion on the practical implications of this dependence would be welcome. For what range of dimensions do the authors expect their method to be practically useful?

- **Choice of Test Functions:** Could the authors provide a more detailed motivation for the specific choice of the family of test functions, U?

- **Relation to Other Approaches:** More broadly, a key challenge in problems like density ratio estimation is defining a suitable objective function. The strategy of matching expectations over a class of test functions is a common one. It would be valuable for the authors to position their specific choice of {k(x,·)} within this wider context. For instance, the work of [1] also manipulates operators to estimate quantities of interest. While the proposed approach of using test functions directly for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is elegant, a brief comparison highlighting the similarities and differences with related operator-based methods would further contextualize the contribution.

In conclusion, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem. The proposed methods are well-motivated, and the inclusion of a convergence study and experimental results makes for a solid contribution.
