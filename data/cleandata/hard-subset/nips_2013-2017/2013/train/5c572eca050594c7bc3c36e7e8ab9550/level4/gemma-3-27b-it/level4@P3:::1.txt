This paper addresses the important problem of density ratio estimation, offering a novel formulation based on Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques. The authors connect this problem to classical operator theory, enabling a principled framework with theoretical guarantees. The paper is well-written and clearly presents a sophisticated approach to a challenging problem.

The core idea—reformulating density ratio estimation as an inverse problem—is a significant contribution. This allows the application of tools from regularization theory, specifically Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, leading to a family of algorithms termed FIRE. The inclusion of a novel unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, further strengthens the work. The theoretical analysis, providing concentration and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, is a valuable asset.

However, several aspects could be improved. While the paper is generally clear, some statements regarding mathematical derivations lack sufficient justification. For example, the claim regarding  \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx=f(y)+ O(t) requires either a proof within the paper or a clear citation to a relevant source.  A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would also be beneficial, as it may not be familiar to all NIPS attendees. The proofs being relegated to the supplementary material is acceptable, but reviewers may not have the time to thoroughly verify them.

A crucial element missing is a direct comparison of the proposed method's performance against established benchmarks, particularly concerning minimax optimality. Corollaries 2 and 4 present upper bounds on convergence rates, but it remains unclear how these rates compare to known lower bounds for density ratio estimation, such as those derived in [12]. A discussion addressing this point would significantly strengthen the significance claim. 

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more explicit justification for why the proposed approach is superior to a naive plug-in density estimation method. While implicitly addressed through the formulation, a dedicated section outlining the advantages would be helpful.  The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, could be enhanced by including illustrative examples on simple, toy problems with known p and q. Visualizing the estimated density ratio against the true ratio for 1D distributions would greatly aid understanding and assessment of the method's performance. 

Finally, the paper lacks citations for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS). Including these would enhance reproducibility and provide context for the experimental results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Principled framework based on regularization theory with theoretical guarantees.
*   Introduction of a new unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV).
*   Clear and well-written presentation of complex ideas.
*   Demonstrated empirical performance on multiple datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of justification for certain mathematical claims and derivations.
*   Missing comparison to minimax optimality bounds and established algorithms.
*   Absence of illustrative examples on toy problems.
*   Lack of dataset citations.
*   Relatively heavy reliance on supplementary material for key proofs.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly relating to comparison with existing bounds, illustrative examples, and justification of key claims – would significantly enhance its impact and strengthen the case for acceptance. I would rate this paper as a **weak accept** contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised above.
