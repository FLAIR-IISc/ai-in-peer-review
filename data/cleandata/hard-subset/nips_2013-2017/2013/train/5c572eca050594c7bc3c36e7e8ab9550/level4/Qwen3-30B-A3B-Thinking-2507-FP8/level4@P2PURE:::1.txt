

The paper introduces a novel approach for density ratio estimation \(p(x)/q(x)\), reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. While several existing methods address this problem, this formulation represents a significant theoretical contribution.

Strengths: The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The density ratio estimation problem is both important and well-justified.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
(i) Key technical claims require justification. For instance, the assertion \(\int k_t(x,y)f(x)\,dx = f(y) + O(t)\) lacks proof or reference; this should be provided in the main text or supplementary material.
(ii) The special case \(q(x) = 1\) enables density estimation without requiring bandwidth convergence to zero (unlike standard KDE). The paper should clarify how the bandwidth parameters in this method differ from those in KDE, highlighting this consistency advantage.
(iii) A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would benefit the NIPS audience, as it is not universally familiar.
(iv) All proofs are relegated to the supplementary material; their correctness has not been verified.
(v) A comparison with the naive plug-in approach (estimating \(p\) and \(q\) separately) is needed to justify the proposed method's superiority.
(vi) The performance relative to competing methods (e.g., [12], which establishes minimax bounds) is unclear. Corollaries 2 and 4 provide convergence rate upper bounds, but their proximity to the minimax optimal rate requires explicit discussion.
(vii) References for datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) must be added.
(viii) A toy example demonstrating the method on simple 1D densities (e.g., plotting true vs. estimated \(p(x)/q(x)\)) would help readers assess estimation quality.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated contribution to density ratio estimation. However, the lack of comparative performance analysis against existing methods (e.g., [12]) and the absence of illustrative toy examples hinder clear assessment of its practical utility.
