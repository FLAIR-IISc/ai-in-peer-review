This paper proposes two novel methods for estimating the density ratio between two probability distributions. The first method is derived from the importance sampling equation and is formulated as a regularization problem, minimizing the L2 norm of the deviation from equality, combined with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) regularization term (a Type I Fredholm equation). The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter that must converge to zero for consistency, and this is also framed as a regularization problem (a Type II Fredholm equation). The paper provides theoretical convergence results and demonstrates the empirical performance of the proposed methods.

A key advantage of this work, particularly when compared to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), is that the L2 formulation naturally enables model selection via cross-validation (CV). In unsupervised learning problems such as density ratio estimation, the lack of a universal method for hyperparameter tuning is a significant practical challenge, in stark contrast to supervised learning where CV is standard practice. The authors address this long-standing issue with a clever and effective L2-based objective, which is a strong contribution.

To further highlight the novelty of their contribution, the authors might consider explicitly discussing why conventional RKHS-based objective functions (as seen in KMM) are unsuitable for cross-validation. The values of such objective functions are dependent on the chosen kernel, making them incomparable across different kernel choices and thus precluding effective CV-based model selection.

Regarding the theoretical analysis, Theorem 1 presents a convergence rate that includes a `(-t/log λ)` factor, which is slow for a fixed `t`. In much of the kernel methods literature, it is common to derive a polynomial convergence rate with respect to the sample size for a fixed regularization parameter rate `λ`, often by introducing additional assumptions. For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) make assumptions on the spectrum of the kernel operator to derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression. It would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution if the authors could discuss whether a polynomial rate can be achieved under stronger, yet reasonable, assumptions on the density ratio `q/p` or the underlying distributions.

Furthermore, a broader discussion on the underlying assumptions of density ratio estimation would be valuable. The assumption that the ratio `q/p` belongs to a well-behaved function class (e.g., a Sobolev space) is common but very strong. It often implies that the inverse ratio, `p/q`, is ill-behaved, limiting the applicability of the estimator to cases where the direction of the ratio is known a priori. Acknowledging this inherent limitation of the general problem would provide important context for the proposed methods.

Finally, while not a strict requirement for acceptance, the supplementary material needs considerable revision. It contains numerous typographical errors and minor mistakes that hinder the verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. For example:
*   In Lemma 5, `W2^2` should be `Ws^2`.
*   The bound in Lemma 5 appears to be incorrect; it should likely be `D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^(1-α) D2 ||f||_2^2`.
*   The expressions at lines 675 and 686 seem incorrect, though Eq. (31) is correct.
*   In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of integration at line 1147 should be `||ξ||^2 >= T`.
Given that the theoretical convergence analysis is a central contribution of this paper, the authors are strongly encouraged to proofread and correct the supplementary material thoroughly.

In summary, this paper introduces a promising new approach for density ratio estimation. Its primary strength lies in the formulation that allows for effective hyperparameter selection via cross-validation, a significant practical advantage. The main areas for improvement are in strengthening the theoretical analysis of convergence rates and providing a more detailed discussion of the problem's underlying assumptions.
