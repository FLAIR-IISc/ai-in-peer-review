The paper "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach" presents a novel approach to estimating the ratio of two densities, q/p, by formulating it as an inverse problem using the Fredholm equation of the first kind. The authors derive two estimators based on two equalities involving integral operators, which are then replaced with empirical versions. The choice of kernel functions as test functions allows for the use of standard kernel methods for optimization.

The paper provides convergence guarantees for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. The rate of convergence depends crucially on the dimension of the underlying space, which is expected for a density estimate. The authors also present experimental results comparing their method, FIRE, with other approaches, including Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF).

The paper is well-executed and addresses a relevant problem in the field. However, there are some aspects that could be improved. Firstly, it would be beneficial to provide a more intuitive explanation for why optimizing over the set of kernel functions is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the ratio in both settings. Additionally, the motivation for the choice of cost function, particularly the combination of L2,p and L2,q, could be further elaborated.

The authors' setup, which involves estimating Ep and Eq to get the empirical cost function, raises the question of why not use directly the Eq obtained. The response to this is that sampling from Eq is difficult, but it would be helpful to provide more context or examples to illustrate this point.

The regularizer used in the paper enforces smoothness for the ratio q/p, but it is unclear how this relates to the smoothness of q and p. The authors could discuss this relationship further, particularly in the context of their smoothness assumptions.

The dimension dependence of the rates is also worth discussing, as it would be helpful to understand for which dimensions the method is expected to be useful. Furthermore, the choice of the family of test functions U could be motivated more explicitly.

Finally, the authors could discuss the generality of their approach and whether they believe it is the "right" approach for the q/p and covariate shift setting. They could also elaborate on the similarities and differences between their approach and other methods, such as the one presented in "Smooth Operators" by Grunewalder, Gretton, and Shawe-Taylor.

Overall, the paper is a timely and strong contribution to the field, providing a convergence study and experimental results for a relevant problem.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to estimating the ratio of two densities using the Fredholm equation of the first kind
* Convergence guarantees for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities
* Experimental results comparing the proposed method with other approaches

Weaknesses:

* Lack of intuitive explanation for why optimizing over the set of kernel functions is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the ratio in both settings
* Limited motivation for the choice of cost function
* Unclear relationship between the smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of the ratio
* Limited discussion of the dimension dependence of the rates
* Limited motivation for the choice of the family of test functions U

Acceptance:

* Yes, the paper is a strong contribution to the field and addresses a relevant problem.
* The paper provides a convergence study and experimental results, which are essential for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The authors could improve the paper by addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, but overall, the paper is well-executed and deserves acceptance.
