This paper introduces two methods for estimating the density ratio `q/p`. The first method (Type I) builds upon the importance sampling identity, formulating the estimation problem as a Tikhonov regularization task that minimizes an L2 norm objective with an RKHS penalty. The second method (Type II) uses a delta-kernel, whose locality parameter must tend to zero for consistency, and is also cast within a similar regularization framework. The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees for their methods and demonstrate their practical effectiveness through experiments.

A major strength of the proposed framework is its use of an L2 objective function. While the approach is related to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), the L2 formulation provides a significant advantage: it enables the use of cross-validation (CV) for model selection (i.e., choosing the kernel and regularization parameters). In supervised learning, CV is the standard for parameter tuning, but principled methods for model selection in unsupervised settings are rare. The paper's "Cross-Density Cross-Validation" (CD-CV) scheme, based on the L2 norm, offers a clever and practical solution to this challenging problem in the context of density ratio estimation.

While the paper is promising, there are several areas for improvement:

1.  **Comparison with KMM:** To further highlight the benefits of their approach, the authors could explicitly remark that objective functions based on an RKHS norm, as used in KMM, are not directly comparable across different kernels. This dependency on the choice of kernel makes CV-based model selection problematic for such methods, thereby reinforcing the value of the proposed L2 formulation.

2.  **Convergence Rates:** The convergence rate derived in Theorem 1 appears to be slow for a fixed kernel width `t`, due to the `(-t/log λ)` factor. In the kernel methods literature, it is often possible to achieve polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size for a fixed `t` by selecting an appropriate decay rate for the regularization parameter `λ`. It would strengthen the theoretical contribution if the authors discussed whether polynomial rates are achievable for their estimator under stronger, yet reasonable, assumptions. For instance, assumptions on the spectral decay of the operator `K_{p,t}` or on the smoothness of the true ratio `q/p` (as in Caponnetto and De Vito, 2007) might yield faster, optimal rates for a fixed `t`.

3.  **Assumptions on the Density Ratio:** A common requirement in density ratio estimation is the assumption that `q/p` belongs to a well-behaved function class, such as a Sobolev space. This is a strong assumption that implies significant prior knowledge about the relationship between `p` and `q`, particularly concerning their tail behavior. It is also worth noting that if `q/p` is well-behaved, the inverse ratio `p/q` is often not. The paper would be more complete if it included a discussion of these limitations and the practical implications of this core assumption.

4.  **Supplementary Material:** The supplementary material requires careful revision. It contains numerous typographical and minor errors that make it difficult to verify the correctness of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. For instance:
    *   In Lemma 5, `W2^2` should likely be `Ws^2`, and the bound seems incorrect.
    *   The expressions on lines 675 and 686 appear incorrect, although Eq. (31) is correct.
    *   In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of integration on line 1147 should be `||ξ||^2 ≥ ...`.
    Given that the convergence analysis is a central contribution of this work, it is crucial that the proofs in the supplement are presented clearly and correctly. I strongly advise the authors to revise the supplementary material thoroughly.

In summary, the paper proposes a novel and well-motivated framework for density ratio estimation. Its primary strength lies in developing a method that is amenable to cross-validation for parameter selection, a significant practical advantage. The main weaknesses relate to the potentially slow theoretical convergence rates and a lack of discussion on the strong underlying assumptions. Finally, the supplementary material needs substantial revision to allow for proper verification of the theoretical claims.
