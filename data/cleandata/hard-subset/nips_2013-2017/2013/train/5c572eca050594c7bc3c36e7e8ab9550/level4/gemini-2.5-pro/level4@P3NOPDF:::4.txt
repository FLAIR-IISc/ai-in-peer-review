This paper introduces two novel estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative (or density ratio) f = p/q. The methods are derived from two fundamental equalities: (i) E_p[k(x,·)f] = E_q[k(x,·)] and (ii) E_p[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x). In practice, the integral operators corresponding to the expectations are replaced by their empirical estimates. The function class {k(x,·)}, drawn from a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), serves as a set of test functions over which the discrepancy between the left and right sides of these equalities is minimized. This choice is advantageous as it allows for the application of standard kernel methods for optimization.

The authors provide convergence guarantees for both estimators under standard smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities p and q. As expected for a non-parametric density estimation problem, the rate of convergence depends on the dimensionality of the underlying space.

Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable piece of work. The proposed methods are elegant, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the problem it addresses is of significant importance in machine learning. I recommend acceptance, but I have several comments and questions that could help clarify and strengthen the paper.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

1.  **Intuition for Convergence:** The convergence analysis is a key contribution. For the second estimator, derived from E_p[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x), the convergence is intuitive given the dependence on the kernel bandwidth. However, it would be beneficial for the reader if the authors could provide a short, intuitive argument for why minimizing the objective over the RKHS is also sufficient to guarantee convergence to the true ratio for the first estimator, derived from E_p[k(x,·)f] = E_q[k(x,·)].

2.  **Motivation for the Cost Function:** The paper would benefit from a clearer motivation for the choice of cost functions. On page 2, the optimization objective is based on an L2,p norm, while the objective in equation (9) appears to involve a combination of L2,p and L2,q norms. A more detailed justification for these specific choices would be welcome. Ideally, this motivation could be linked to the requirements of important downstream applications, explaining why a particular norm is more suitable for certain tasks.

3.  **Clarification of Application Scenarios (Importance Sampling):** The paper highlights importance sampling as a key application, where the ratio q/p is used to transform expectations under p to expectations under q (E_p[g] = E_q[(p/q)g]). However, the proposed method requires empirical estimates from both distributions, E_p and E_q, to learn the ratio. This raises a fundamental question: if an empirical estimate of E_p is already available to formulate the learning objective, why not use it directly to estimate E_p[g]? The paper briefly mentions on page 5 that sampling from p might be difficult. Could the authors elaborate on this? A more detailed discussion of the specific scenarios where this indirect approach—learning the ratio first—is more advantageous than direct estimation would significantly strengthen the paper's motivation.

4.  **Relationship Between Smoothness Assumptions:** The proposed method employs a regularizer that encourages smoothness in the estimated ratio q/p. The convergence theorems, on the other hand, rely on smoothness assumptions on the individual densities q and p. It would be valuable if the authors could comment on the relationship between these two notions of smoothness. For example, do smooth densities p and q (perhaps bounded away from zero) guarantee a smooth ratio? While it is clear that a smooth ratio does not imply smooth densities, understanding the connection in the other direction would make the theoretical framework more cohesive.

5.  **Practical Implications of Dimensionality:** The derived convergence rates show a dependency on the data dimension `d`, which is characteristic of the curse of dimensionality. To help practitioners, it would be useful to include a discussion on the practical implications of this dependency. For what range of dimensions do the authors expect this method to be effective, and how does its performance scale with `d` compared to other density ratio estimation techniques?

6.  **Justification for the Choice of Test Functions:** The choice of the RKHS U as the family of test functions is central to the method. While this enables the elegant application of kernel methods, a more principled discussion on why this is a "good" or "sufficient" family for this problem would be insightful.

7.  **Broader Context and Comparison:** A central challenge in related problems like covariate shift is the formulation of a generic and effective cost function. This paper's approach of using expectation operators and a rich class of test functions is a very appealing way to extract the underlying quantity of interest. This general strategy shares some philosophical similarities with the work of Grünewald et al. ("Smooth Operators"), which also uses transformations to estimate properties of distributions. While the proposed approach for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is arguably more direct and elegant, a brief discussion situating this work within this broader context and highlighting the specific advantages of the kernel-based test function approach would be a valuable addition.

In summary, this is a strong and timely paper addressing a relevant problem with novel methods, a solid convergence study, and convincing experimental results. I believe addressing the points above would further improve its clarity and impact.
