The authors use two analogies to obtain two approximations of the Radon-Nikodiev derivative f= p/q:
(i) Ep ( k(x,.) f) = E_q k(x,.) and (ii) Ep (k(x,.) f ) ~ q(x)
The integral operators have been replaced with empirical versions. {k(x,.)} can be viewed as a set of testing functions on which the difference between the right and left sides is minimal. The advantage of choosing {k(x,.)} as a family of testing functions is that standard kernel methods can be used for optimization.

Convergence is guaranteed for both settings under the notions of smoothness and boundedness on the density. The rate of convergence depends significantly on the dimension of the underlying space – as would be expected for any density approximation.

I think this is good and well-executed work.

A few comments:
- For convergence rates. It is sufficient to guarantee convergence proportional to optimization on the set of {k(x,.)}. Given the bandwidth dependence for (ii), this is intuitive. It would be nice to have a slightly intuitive argument as to why this also works in (i).
- You optimize on L2,p on page 2. Later in (9), you have a combination of L2,p and L_2,q. Some motivation for the choice of cost function would be nice. I think ultimately these should arise from functions of interest. So it would be nice to link the choice of cost function to some important applications.
- Speaking of applications, the example you found is the importance sampling. That is, the relevance of going from the equation q/p to the equation. I'm wondering here: do you effectively approximate the equation and the equation so that you can obtain your empirical cost function? So why don't you use the equation you found there directly? Could the equation be better than the equation directly? I think I'm asking for motivation here for the setup because you need to approximate the equation and approximate q/p to approximate the equation. You have a sentence about it on page 5. That is, sampling from the equation is difficult. Is this the kind of main application you have in mind?
- Regularization: You apply smoothness on the ratio q/p. Later in the theorem, you found notions of smoothness on q and p (which I would say is a natural thing). So I'm wondering if there is some kind of relationship between smoothness on q and p and the smoothness of the ratio? I think there is already an answer here that you have several different q and p that generate the same ratio; that is, just make it extremely random by changing q and p at countless or countless points. So it could be more p,q smooth (possibly bounded away from 0) => ratio smooth (?) or ratio smooth => a smooth p and q exists that generates the ratio.
- It would be nice to discuss the d dependence of your rates – for what dimensions would you expect your method to be useful?
- Any good motivation for your family of testing functions U?
- A more theoretical question: for this q/p; one of the main difficulties for the co-variational change etc. setting seems to be coming up with a general cost function. One approach is to go through the expectation operators and throw various testing functions on these to extract the underlying densities. Do you think you have found the "right" approach with {k(x,.)}? There is also some similarity with [1] in terms of playing with the changes to obtain the amount of interest. Of course, your approach to the Radon-Nikodiev derivative is better by working with testing functions. But there is some similarity in the overall approach.
[1] Smooth operator; Gruewaldner, Gretan, Schev-Taylor
