This work presents two novel approaches to the problem of estimating the ratio between two probability densities. The first approach leverages the importance sampling equation, reframing it as a regularization problem that minimizes the L^2 norm of the deviation from equality combined with an RKHS regularization term (a Type I Fredholm equation). The second approach involves a kernel whose locality parameter must converge to zero to ensure consistency; this method is also cast as a regularization problem, resulting in a Type II Fredholm equation. The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees and support their findings with experimental results demonstrating the methods' practical performance.

Although the proposed methodology shares similarities with existing techniques like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), it offers a significant advantage: its L^2 formulation uniquely permits the use of cross-validation (CV) for hyperparameter selection. This is a notable contribution, as robust hyperparameter tuning methods like CV are commonplace in supervised learning but generally absent in unsupervised settings. The paper cleverly addresses this gap for density ratio estimation through its use of the L^2 norm.

The authors could further strengthen their argument by explicitly remarking that conventional RKHS-based objective functions (e.g., in KMM) are ill-suited for kernel selection via CV. This is because the objective function values are dependent on the choice of kernel, making direct comparisons between different kernels meaningless.

Regarding Theorem 1, the convergence rate appears to be slow for a fixed `t` due to the `(-t/log \lambda)` factor. Much of the kernel methods literature establishes polynomial convergence rates with respect to the sample size under a fixed `t` by appropriately setting the rate of `\lambda`. Could the authors achieve a similar polynomial rate for their estimator by introducing reasonable additional assumptions? For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression by making assumptions on the spectrum of the `K_{p,t}` operator. It seems plausible that a stronger assumption on the ratio `q/p` could yield a polynomial rate here as well. A discussion on achieving such rates for a fixed `t` under stronger assumptions would significantly enhance the paper's theoretical contribution.

A general point of discussion that should be included is the strong underlying assumption common in density ratio estimation: that the ratio `q/p` belongs to a well-behaved function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This is a significant constraint, as it implies prior knowledge about the relationship between `p` and `q`. Furthermore, when this assumption holds for `q/p`, the inverse ratio `p/q` is often ill-behaved. The paper would benefit from a discussion on this inherent limitation of the problem domain.

While not a mandatory revision, the supplementary material requires significant improvement. It currently contains numerous typographical and minor errors that hinder the verification of the main paper's theoretical claims. For example: in Lemma 5, W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the stated bound appears incorrect—it should be D1 (t/-log \lambda)^2 + \lambda^{1-\alpha} D2 \|f\|_2^2. The expressions on lines 675 and 686 are also incorrect, although Eq.(31) is correct. Additionally, in the proof of Lemma 5, the integration domain at line 1147 should be \| \xi\|^2 \geq. Given that the convergence guarantees are a central contribution of this work, it is crucial that the authors thoroughly revise and correct the supplementary material to ensure the proofs are sound and verifiable.
