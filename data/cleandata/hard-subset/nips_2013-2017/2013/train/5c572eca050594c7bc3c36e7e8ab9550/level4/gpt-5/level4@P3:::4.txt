Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles density-ratio estimation (estimating w(x)=q(x)/p(x)) by recasting it as a linear inverse problem for an integral operator associated with a kernel (a Fredholm equation of the first kind). Two formulations are developed:

- Type I: Ep[k(x,·)w(·)] = Eq[k(x,·)] (exact identity), leading to a Tikhonov-regularized solution in an RKHS from samples of p and q.
- Type II: For a “local” kernel kt (e.g., Gaussian with small variance), Ep[kt(x,·)w(·)] ≈ q(x), yielding an approximate equation solvable when q is known at p-sampled points but sampling from q is difficult.

Both are solved via RKHS regularization, yielding closed-form representer-theorem solutions (FIRE). The authors analyze concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Rd and on smooth manifolds, and propose an unsupervised model-selection scheme (CD-CV) based on matching expectations of random test functions under p and q via the learned weights. Experiments compare FIRE with LSIF/KLIEP, KMM (discussion), and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, with applications to covariate shift and importance weighting.

This connects to prior work on density-ratio estimation and covariate shift (e.g., KMM, LSIF/KLIEP) and to inverse problems and RKHS regularization (Tikhonov/spectral methods). The Type I formulation is closely related to KMM’s moment-matching in RKHS; here the ratio is itself modeled in an RKHS with explicit regularization and out-of-sample evaluation. The operator-theoretic lens and manifold results strengthen ties to the “smooth operators” literature and kernel spectral analysis.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic reformulation that unifies density-ratio estimation with classical inverse problems, enabling principled regularization and analysis.
- Simple, explicit algorithms with out-of-sample evaluation via the representer theorem; flexibility to choose norms and kernels.
- Nonasymptotic error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, including on manifolds; the separation of approximation (Type II) and estimation errors is transparent.
- CD-CV provides a practical, fully unsupervised model-selection mechanism that leverages access to samples from both p and q.
- Empirical results are encouraging and cover both regression and classification under covariate shift.

Weaknesses and questions
- Identifiability/intuition for Type I: The sufficiency of optimizing over {k(x,·)} (or equivalently over H) for recovering w deserves more intuition. For (ii), the δ-kernel argument is clear. For (i), a short discussion of conditions under which Kp is injective on the chosen function class (e.g., characteristic kernels or properties of the associated integral operator) would greatly help. Relating this explicitly to the identifiability assumptions in KMM (kernel mean embeddings with characteristic kernels) would clarify when matching Ep[k(·,·)w] to Eq[k(·,·)] pins down w.
- Choice of loss (L2,p vs L2,q and mixtures): The paper introduces L2,p, L2,q, and a γ-weighted combination. Some motivation linking loss choice to downstream tasks (e.g., minimizing mean-squared error of importance-weighted risk under p vs moment-matching under q) would guide practitioners. Are there scenarios (classification vs regression under covariate shift) where L2,q or a mixed loss yields better risk bounds for the final predictor?
- Importance sampling motivation: Since the CD-CV objective requires empirical estimates of expectations under both p and q, please clarify when the proposed approach is preferable to directly using Eq if q-samples are available. The Type II setting (q known up to a normalizing constant; sampling from q is hard) is compelling—expanding this use case (e.g., AIS, MCMC diagnostic/weighting scenarios) would anchor the motivation.
- Smoothness assumptions: The theorems assume smoothness and boundedness for p and q, while the algorithm regularizes w. A brief discussion of how smoothness of p and q (and p bounded away from zero) translates to smoothness of w would be helpful. Conversely, if w is smooth but p and q are not, are the current proofs adaptable? Stating whether the theory requires p(x) ≥ c > 0 on the support would clarify edge cases where w may be unbounded.
- Dimensional dependence: The rates explicitly deteriorate with d. A short discussion of practical regimes where FIRE is effective (e.g., moderate ambient d with strong manifold structure and Gaussian smoothing) and how manifold structure mitigates the curse of dimensionality would be valuable.
- Test function families U in CD-CV: The random linear and half-space functions are intuitive and easy to compute, but please provide a rationale (e.g., they probe low-order moments and linear decision boundaries relevant to many tasks). How sensitive is model selection to the choice/number of test functions? Some stability analysis or ablations (varying F, distributions of β) would strengthen the proposal.
- Constraints on w: As presented, w is unconstrained and may be negative. While regularization and kernels may empirically keep estimates reasonable, do the authors recommend nonnegativity constraints or post-hoc clipping when w is used for importance weighting in practice?
- Computational aspects: The closed-form solutions involve inversions of n×n Gram matrices. Please report time/memory complexity and any low-rank/Nyström or iterative solvers used, and compare with LSIF/KMM scaling.
- Kernel choices: The theory assumes the same Gaussian kernel for operator and RKHS. Empirically, do different choices (e.g., linear operator kernel with Gaussian RKHS kernel) affect stability or rates? A brief note on practice would help.

Pros
- Elegant inverse-problem framing with clean algorithms and theory.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) tailored to density-ratio estimation.
- Covers both Euclidean and manifold settings; provides generalization bounds.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines and good calibration for covariate shift.

Cons
- Limited intuition and identifiability conditions for the Type I operator beyond the δ-kernel argument used in Type II.
- Sparse discussion of loss-function choice vis-à-vis downstream tasks.
- Dimensional dependence and computational scaling could be more fully addressed.
- Lack of explicit positivity constraints and guidance in practice.

Quality: The technical development is careful, and the empirical section is solid. It would benefit from clarifying identifiability, loss selection, and computational aspects.

Clarity: Generally well organized, with room for improved intuition for Type I and clearer assumptions (e.g., p bounded away from zero). A brief schematic of the two settings (data available, objective, solution form) would aid readability.

Originality: The Fredholm inverse-problem framing and manifold analysis provide a fresh and principled perspective, distinct from KMM/LSIF/KLIEP while related. The CD-CV selection is a practical contribution.

Significance: The methods and analysis should be useful to researchers working on covariate shift, importance sampling, and operator-based kernel methods.

Minor suggestions
- Explicitly state identifiability conditions (e.g., characteristic kernels) for Type I.
- Discuss when to favor L2,p, L2,q, or mixtures, and connect to risk bounds for importance-weighted learning.
- Clarify whether p is assumed bounded away from zero on the support of q to control w.
- Report computational complexity and any approximations used.
- Add brief discussion relating your approach to “Smooth Operators” (Grünewälder et al.), highlighting similarities (operator equations/test functions) and key differences (target is Radon–Nikodym derivative; regularizing w directly).

Overall assessment
A timely and strong paper offering a principled operator-theoretic approach to density-ratio estimation, with clean algorithms, nontrivial theory (including manifolds), and a practical unsupervised model-selection scheme. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarifications above.
