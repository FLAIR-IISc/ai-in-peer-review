The authors propose a solution to the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability density functions. The approach is based on reformulating the problem as an inverse problem using a kernel and a Fredholm integral equation. This yields a principled framework for constructing and analyzing algorithms for density ratio estimation. From a machine learning perspective, the assumption of densities can be restrictive, as it is known that probability measures may not have a density with respect to a given dominating measure. Therefore, estimating the ratio of two densities requires careful consideration.

As far as I can see, the authors do not explicitly state which dominating measure ν is used (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure), and it is unclear whether their results are fully rigorous in the presence of sets of ν-measure zero. A clearer statement of the underlying assumptions would be beneficial. For example, is the domain x ∈ ℝᵈ, or does it belong to a more general topological space? Is the density p positive for all x?

The paper’s strengths lie in its novel connection between density ratio estimation and the classical theory of Fredholm integral equations. This allows the authors to leverage tools from operator theory, such as regularization techniques, to develop a family of algorithms (FIRE) with strong theoretical guarantees. The detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, is a significant contribution, particularly for densities defined on Rᵈ and smooth d-dimensional sub-manifolds. The proposed unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, is also a valuable addition. The experimental results demonstrate promising performance compared to existing methods like KMM and LSIF.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the connection to Fredholm equations is insightful, the initial motivation – relating the problem to importance sampling and covariate shift – could be more clearly articulated. The discussion of related work is comprehensive but could be more focused on the specific connections to the proposed approach. The reliance on specific kernel choices (particularly the Gaussian kernel) in the theoretical analysis limits the generality of the results. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method and potential avenues for future work would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation.
*   Strong theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
*   Practical algorithm (FIRE) with promising experimental results.
*   Useful unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Assumptions regarding the existence of densities may be restrictive.
*   Lack of explicit specification of the dominating measure.
*   Theoretical analysis primarily focused on the Gaussian kernel.
*   Motivation linking to importance sampling/covariate shift could be strengthened.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem. While some clarifications and extensions would be beneficial, the contributions are significant and warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
