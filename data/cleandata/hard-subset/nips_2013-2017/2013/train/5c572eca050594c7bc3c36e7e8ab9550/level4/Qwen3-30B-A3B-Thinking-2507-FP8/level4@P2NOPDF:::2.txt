

This paper introduces two novel methods for estimating the density ratio between two probability distributions. The first approach formulates the importance sampling equation as a regularization problem (Type I Fredholm equation), incorporating an L² norm penalty for deviation from equality and an RKHS regularization term. The second method, expressed as a Type II Fredholm equation, requires the kernel's locality parameter to vanish asymptotically for consistency. The authors establish theoretical convergence guarantees and validate the methods through empirical experiments.

A key strength is the effective application of cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection—a significant advantage over existing methods like KMM. While CV dominates parameter tuning in supervised learning, unsupervised settings typically lack generalizable techniques. The L² formulation enables CV by ensuring objective function values are comparable across kernel choices, unlike RKHS-based objectives (e.g., KMM), where objective values depend on the kernel and thus cannot be meaningfully compared. The paper would benefit from a brief remark clarifying this distinction.

The convergence rate in Theorem 1 features a suboptimal factor of $-t/\log \lambda$ under fixed $t$, resulting in a slow rate. In standard kernel methods (e.g., Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007), polynomial convergence rates in sample size are achievable with fixed $t$ under spectral assumptions on the kernel operator. The authors should discuss whether stronger assumptions—such as on the spectrum of $K_{p,t}$ or the smoothness of $q/p$—could yield polynomial rates. This would strengthen the theoretical contribution, as the current rate appears suboptimal compared to established literature.

The paper relies on the strong assumption that $q/p$ belongs to a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev space), which necessitates prior knowledge of the density ratio or tail behavior. Crucially, this assumption often implies that the inverse ratio $p/q$ exhibits poor behavior, limiting the applicability of density ratio estimation. The authors should address this asymmetry and its practical implications, as it represents a significant constraint in real-world scenarios.

The supplementary material requires substantial revision. Multiple typographical errors and minor technical inconsistencies hinder verification of the theoretical results. Specifically:  
- Lemma 5 contains a typo: $W_2^2$ should be $W_s^2$, and the bound should read $D_1 (t / -\log \lambda)^2 + \lambda^{1-\alpha} D_2 \|f\|_2^2$.  
- Expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Equation (31) is correct.  
- In Lemma 5’s proof, the integral domain at line 1147 must be $\|\xi\|^2 \geq \cdots$.  
Since the convergence analysis constitutes the paper’s core theoretical contribution, these errors must be corrected to ensure reproducibility and credibility.

The paper’s core contribution—enabling CV for density ratio estimation via L² regularization—is compelling and well-motivated. The methods are clearly presented, and the experimental validation is adequate. However, the theoretical analysis would be significantly strengthened by addressing the convergence rate discussion and the assumption limitations. The supplementary material errors, while not affecting the main claims, undermine confidence in the technical rigor.

**Recommendation:** Major revision. The authors should:  
1. Discuss polynomial convergence rates under stronger assumptions (e.g., spectral conditions on $K_{p,t}$ or smoothness of $q/p$).  
2. Clarify the limitations of the $q/p \in \text{Sobolev}$ assumption and its asymmetry with $p/q$.  
3. Correct all errors in the supplementary material, particularly the typos and proof inaccuracies noted above.  
These revisions would substantially enhance the paper’s theoretical depth and technical soundness.
