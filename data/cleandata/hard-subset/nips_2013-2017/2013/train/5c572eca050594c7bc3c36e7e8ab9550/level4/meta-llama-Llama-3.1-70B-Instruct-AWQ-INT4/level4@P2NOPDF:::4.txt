The authors propose two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative f = p/q, leveraging two equalities: (i) Ep ( k(x,.) f) = E_q k(x,.) and (ii) Ep (k(x,.) f ) ~ q(x). By replacing integral operators with empirical versions, they utilize {k(x,.)} as a set of test functions to minimize the difference between the right and left sides. This choice allows for the application of standard kernel methods in optimization.

Convergence guarantees are established for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. The rate of convergence is crucially dependent on the dimension of the underlying space, which is expected for density estimation.

This work is well-executed and provides a valuable contribution to the field. A few comments and suggestions for improvement are noted below:

1. The convergence rates are influenced by optimizing over the set {k(x,.)}. While this is intuitive for (ii) due to bandwidth dependence, a brief explanation for why this approach also works for (i) would be beneficial.

2. The authors optimize over L2,p on page 2, but later combine L2,p and L_2,q in equation (9). A motivation for the choice of cost function would be helpful, ideally linking it to specific applications. The choice of cost function should stem from the tasks of interest, and a discussion on this connection would strengthen the paper.

3. One of the applications mentioned is importance sampling, where q/p is relevant for moving from Ep to Eq. However, since the empirical cost function is estimated using Ep and Eq, it is unclear why Ep (q/p) would be preferable to directly using Eq. A clearer motivation for the setup, considering the need to estimate Ep and Eq to estimate q/p, would be beneficial. The sentence on page 5 regarding the difficulty of sampling from Eq is a good start, but further clarification is needed.

4. The regularizer enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p, while the theorem assumes smoothness on q and p. It would be interesting to explore the relationship between smoothness on q and p and the smoothness of the ratio. While it is acknowledged that many different q and p can produce the same ratio, a more in-depth discussion on this topic would be valuable.

5. A discussion on the dimension dependence of the rates would be beneficial, as it would provide insight into the dimensions for which the method is expected to be useful.

6. A clear motivation for the choice of the family of test functions U would be helpful, as it is currently unclear why this specific choice was made.

7. A more principled question is whether the approach using {k(x,.)} is the most suitable for estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative in the context of covariate shift. While the method shows promise, a comparison with alternative approaches, such as the one presented in [1], would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.

Overall, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem, provides a convergence study, and presents experimental results.
