This paper introduces a novel method for estimating the density ratio p/q. While several methods exist for this problem, the key contribution here is the reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

**Strong points of the paper:**

The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read. The authors tackle the important and interesting problem of density ratio estimation.

**Weak points of the paper, comments, and questions:**

(i) The manuscript could be strengthened by providing more details for some of the technical claims. For instance, the statement `∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)` is presented without justification. Even if such results are straightforward to derive, including a brief proof (perhaps in the supplementary material) or a reference would be beneficial for the reader.

(ii) The paper notes that the proposed method can be used for density estimation as a special case (by setting q=1). An interesting property of this approach is that it does not require the bandwidth parameter to converge to zero for consistency, unlike standard kernel density estimation (KDE). This is a significant point of departure from classical methods. I would encourage the authors to expand on this, perhaps by explicitly discussing how the role of the bandwidth parameter in their framework differs from its role in traditional KDE. This would help clarify the unique properties of their approach for readers familiar with standard non-parametric estimation.

(iii) Given that Tikhonov regularization is central to the proposed method, it would be helpful to include a brief explanation of it. Many readers in the NIPS community may not be familiar with this technique, and a few sentences of background would make the paper more self-contained and accessible.

(iv) All proofs are deferred to the supplementary material. I have not verified their correctness.

(v) The paper would benefit from a discussion on why the proposed method is preferable to a naive plug-in approach (i.e., estimating p and q separately using a standard density estimator and then taking their ratio). Briefly outlining the theoretical or practical shortcomings of such a baseline would better motivate the need for the authors' more sophisticated approach.

(vi) The paper's positioning relative to existing work could be improved. While Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on the convergence rate, it is unclear how these rates compare to the optimal minimax rates for this problem. For instance, [12] also derives minimax bounds for density ratio estimation. A direct comparison or discussion of how the proposed method's theoretical guarantees relate to these known lower bounds would provide crucial context and strengthen the paper's theoretical contributions.

(vii) Please provide citations or references for the datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) To better illustrate the behavior of the proposed estimator, I would suggest including a simple toy experiment. For example, using 1-dimensional densities for p and q where the true ratio is known would be very insightful. A plot showing the true ratio versus the estimated ratio would provide a clear and intuitive demonstration of the method's performance and help readers build intuition.

**Summary:**

Overall, this is a strong paper on an important topic. The primary weaknesses are the lack of a clear comparison of the method's theoretical and empirical performance against state-of-the-art alternatives (such as [12]) and the absence of a simple, illustrative toy example to help readers visualize the estimator's behavior. Addressing these points would significantly improve the manuscript.
