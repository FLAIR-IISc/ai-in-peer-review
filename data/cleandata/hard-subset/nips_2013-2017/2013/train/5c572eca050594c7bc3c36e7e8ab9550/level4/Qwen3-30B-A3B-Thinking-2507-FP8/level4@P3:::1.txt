

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, leading to the development of the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithms. The authors establish connections between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problem theory, providing a principled framework that leverages regularization techniques from operator theory. This work builds upon recent advances in density ratio estimation for covariate shift adaptation [9, 10, 22, 23], but distinguishes itself by framing the problem within the well-established mathematical context of Fredholm equations rather than focusing solely on algorithmic improvements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory. This perspective offers a fresh lens for understanding existing methods and opens new avenues for theoretical analysis. The authors successfully translate this connection into a flexible framework with multiple variants (Type I and Type II settings) that accommodate different data availability scenarios. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing convergence rates for both Euclidean spaces and d-dimensional sub-manifolds with Gaussian kernels—extending beyond previous work [14, 26] that primarily focused on Euclidean domains.

2. **Theoretical Clarity and Rigor:** The paper provides detailed concentration bounds and convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4) that demonstrate the algorithm's statistical properties. The authors correctly identify the key trade-offs between approximation error (O(t)), estimation error, and regularization, providing a clear theoretical foundation for the method. The introduction of CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for model selection is particularly valuable, addressing a critical challenge in unsupervised learning where labeled data is scarce.

3. **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear logical flow from problem formulation to algorithmic implementation and theoretical analysis. The connections to related work (particularly KMM [9]) are carefully explained, highlighting both similarities and key differences in approach. The algorithmic descriptions are precise, and the use of mathematical notation is consistent and appropriate.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1. **Technical Justification:** The paper makes several claims about approximation errors (e.g., ∫k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)) without sufficient justification. While these are standard results in kernel approximation theory, a brief reference or sketch of the proof would strengthen the paper, especially for readers unfamiliar with this area. The supplementary material should contain these details to support the claims.

2. **Comparison to Baseline Methods:** While the paper compares FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE, it would benefit from a more thorough discussion of why the proposed approach outperforms naive density estimation (i.e., separately estimating p and q via KDE). The paper mentions that this approach "doesn't require the bandwidth to converge to zero" but doesn't elaborate on how this differs from standard KDE or why it leads to better performance. A brief comparison to the minimax rates established in [12] would also strengthen the theoretical contribution.

3. **Practical Demonstration:** A simple 1D toy example would greatly enhance the paper's accessibility and help readers assess the quality of estimation. For instance, showing the true density ratio and estimated ratio for a simple Gaussian mixture model would provide immediate intuition about the method's performance.

4. **Context for NIPS Community:** While the paper is technically sound, a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization (beyond citing [5]) would help the broader NIPS audience understand the theoretical foundation of the approach. The paper would also benefit from citing recent related work on kernel methods for inverse problems [3, 20] to better situate the contribution.

**Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

*For Acceptance:*  
- The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation with clear connections to classical mathematical theory.  
- The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, covering both Euclidean spaces and manifolds with convergence rates.  
- The CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is a significant practical contribution.  
- The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field of density estimation and covariate shift adaptation.

*Against Acceptance:*  
- Some key technical claims lack sufficient justification or reference.  
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to naive density estimation approaches.  
- A simple illustrative example would make the method more accessible to the broader NIPS audience.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel perspective of framing the problem as a Fredholm integral equation provides a principled framework that connects to classical mathematical theory while yielding practical algorithms. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the proposed CD-CV method for model selection addresses a significant challenge in unsupervised learning. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding the suggested clarifications, particularly the justification for key approximation claims and a simple 1D demonstration. Given the quality of the theoretical contributions and the practical relevance of the problem, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation and provides a framework that could inspire future work in related areas of statistical learning.
