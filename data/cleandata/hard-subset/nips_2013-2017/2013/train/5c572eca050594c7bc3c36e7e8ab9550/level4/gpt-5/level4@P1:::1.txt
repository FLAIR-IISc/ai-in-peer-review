Summary
This paper proposes a new approach to estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x) by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors consider two settings: Type I, where samples from both p and q are available, and Type II, where q is known (or can be evaluated) at points sampled from p. They solve the resulting inverse problems via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding simple closed-form estimators (FIRE) through the representer theorem. The paper provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on R^d and on smooth submanifolds, and introduces an unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) tailored to density-ratio estimation. Experiments compare FIRE with KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, with applications to covariate shift.

Relation to prior work
The method is related to kernel mean matching (KMM), least squares importance fitting (LSIF), and KLIEP, as well as to classical inverse problems and regularization in RKHSs. The connection to the Fredholm equation provides a principled operator-theoretic framework for both algorithm design and analysis, which is a novel perspective within the density ratio estimation literature.

Strengths
- Clear, enjoyable presentation of an important problem with practical impact (e.g., covariate shift, importance sampling).
- Conceptual novelty: casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in RKHS.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with out-of-sample predictions.
- Unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) is appealing in this setting.
- Theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and manifolds.

Weaknesses, comments, and questions
(i) Several technical claims need proofs or explicit references. For example, why does ∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t)? Please provide a proof (in the paper or supplement) or a precise citation for this and similar statements, even if standard.

(ii) As a special case (q ≡ 1), the method yields a density estimator for p. It is interesting that bandwidth need not shrink to zero here, whereas standard KDE would be inconsistent in that regime. A clearer discussion would help readers see how the bandwidth in this paper plays a different role than in KDE.

(iii) Please add a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization (and its role in inverse problems) for readers who may be less familiar with it.

(iv) All proofs are in the supplement; I have not verified them.

(v) Please discuss why a naive plug-in approach (separately estimating p and q and taking their ratio) is inferior to the proposed method. A short conceptual and/or empirical comparison would be valuable.

(vi) How does this method compare theoretically with existing approaches? For example, if I recall correctly, [12] derives minimax bounds. Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds; it would be helpful to clarify how close these are to optimal minimax rates and to compare with prior results.

(vii) Please provide references for the datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

(viii) A simple 1D toy example with known p and q would greatly aid intuition. Plotting the true ratio q/p and the estimate would help readers assess estimation quality.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically sound and grounded in a principled inverse-problem formulation with regularization. Theoretical guarantees are provided, though all proofs are deferred to the supplement. Empirical evaluation is encouraging, but clearer comparisons to minimax-optimal rates and a discussion of plug-in baselines would strengthen the case.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Adding brief background on Tikhonov regularization, clarifying the role of bandwidth vs. KDE, and citing proofs/references for key approximation claims would improve accessibility.
- Originality: The Fredholm-equation formulation for density-ratio estimation is novel and insightful, connecting modern kernel methods to classical operator theory.
- Significance: Estimating q/p is central to importance sampling and covariate shift. The proposed framework is flexible, simple to implement, and potentially broadly useful. CD-CV is a practical contribution for unsupervised model selection.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled formulation (inverse problem via Fredholm equation) leading to simple algorithms.
- Solid theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels, including manifold settings.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection (CD-CV).
- Clear writing and relevance to important applications (covariate shift, importance sampling).

Cons against acceptance
- Missing citations/proofs for key technical approximations (e.g., δ-kernel property).
- Limited discussion of how bandwidth differs from KDE and why plug-in density estimation is suboptimal here.
- Unclear relation of the provided rates to known minimax-optimal results; comparison to prior theory (e.g., [12]) should be clarified.
- Lack of illustrative toy examples with known p and q.
- Dataset references not provided.

Overall
This is a nice paper on density ratio estimation with an elegant inverse-problem perspective and promising algorithms. Strengthening the theoretical comparisons to prior minimax results, clarifying the differences from plug-in/KDE approaches, adding references and brief background on Tikhonov regularization, and including a simple toy demonstration would substantially improve the paper.
