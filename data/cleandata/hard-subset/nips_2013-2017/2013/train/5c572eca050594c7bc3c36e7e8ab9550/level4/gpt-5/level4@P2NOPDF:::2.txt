This paper introduces two methods for estimating the density ratio q/p. The first reformulates the importance-sampling identity as a regularized least-squares problem: minimize the L2 discrepancy from the equality with an RKHS penalty (a Fredholm equation of the first kind). The second uses a localized kernel whose bandwidth must shrink to zero for consistency and is likewise cast as a regularized problem (a Fredholm equation of the second kind). The authors provide convergence guarantees and empirical evaluations.

A key advantage over related approaches such as KMM is that the L2 formulation yields a natural, comparable risk across kernels, enabling practical cross-validation (CV) for model selection. In supervised learning, CV is standard; in unsupervised settings, robust parameter selection is much less settled. Leveraging L2 risk to enable CV for density ratio estimation is both clever and practically valuable. It would help to explicitly note that RKHS-norm-based objectives (e.g., in KMM) are not suitable for kernel selection via CV, because their values depend inherently on the kernel and are thus not directly comparable across kernels.

On theory, Theorem 1 delivers a convergence rate that includes the factor t/(-log λ), which is slow when t is fixed. In much of the kernel literature, one can obtain polynomial rates in the sample size with fixed t by appropriately setting λ. Are there additional, reasonable assumptions under which your estimators achieve polynomial rates? For instance, Caponnetto and De Vito (2007) derive optimal rates for kernel ridge regression by placing spectral assumptions on the integral operator; analogous assumptions on the spectrum of K_{p,t} may yield sharper rates here. Stronger smoothness or source conditions on q/p could also lead to polynomial rates. It would strengthen the paper to discuss such results for fixed t under stronger—but interpretable—assumptions.

Assumptions on the target ratio deserve more emphasis. Density ratio estimation typically requires q/p to lie in a regular function class (e.g., Sobolev). This is a strong requirement, effectively imposing nontrivial knowledge about the ratio and the tails of p and q. Moreover, when q/p is well-behaved under such assumptions, the reverse ratio p/q often behaves poorly. The paper would benefit from a clear discussion of this limitation and guidance on when the assumptions are plausible in practice.

The supplementary material needs careful revision. There are many typos and minor errors that make it difficult to verify the main theoretical claims. Examples:
- In Lemma 5, W2^2 should read Ws^2, and the bound should be D1·(t/−log λ)^2 + λ^{1−α}·D2·||f||_2^2.
- The expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, while Eq. (31) is correct.
- In the proof of Lemma 5, the domain of integration at line 1147 should be over ||ξ||^2 ≥ • (not as currently written).
Because the convergence theory is a central contribution, I strongly recommend revising the supplement for accuracy and clarity.

Reference:
Caponnetto, A., and De Vito, E. Optimal Rates for Regularized Least-Squares Algorithm. Foundations of Computational Mathematics 7:331–368 (2007).

Overall, the paper proposes a promising approach to density ratio estimation. Its main strength is the principled use of L2 risk, which enables effective cross-validation for parameter and kernel selection in an otherwise challenging unsupervised setting. Discussion of sharper rates under stronger assumptions and a thoroughly corrected supplement would further enhance the contribution.
