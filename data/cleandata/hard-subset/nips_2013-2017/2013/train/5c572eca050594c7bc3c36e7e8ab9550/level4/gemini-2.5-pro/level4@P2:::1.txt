This paper addresses the important problem of estimating the density ratio q/p, a quantity crucial for applications such as importance sampling and covariate shift correction in transfer learning. The primary contribution is a novel reformulation of this estimation task as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. By leveraging this connection, the authors propose a principled framework for deriving estimators. The resulting family of algorithms, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), is developed by applying Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The paper presents two distinct settings for the problem (Type I and Type II), provides a detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, and introduces a practical, unsupervised method for model selection called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Framework:** The key strength of this work lies in its elegant reformulation of density ratio estimation as a well-studied inverse problem. This approach connects the problem to classical operator theory and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms. This is a significant departure from many existing heuristic or purely optimization-based methods.

2.  **Algorithm Development:** The proposed FIRE algorithms are simple, explicit, and easily implementable within the standard kernel methods framework. The framework is flexible, allowing for different norms and regularization schemes, as demonstrated by the derivations for L2,p, mixed L2,p/L2,q, and RKHS norms.

3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators in both Euclidean and manifold settings. This level of theoretical support is a valuable contribution and strengthens the credibility of the method.

4.  **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for hyperparameter tuning is a significant practical contribution. Model selection is a notoriously difficult problem in unsupervised settings, and providing a data-driven, unsupervised validation criterion is a valuable asset of this work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is well-written and presents a compelling new approach. However, its clarity, context, and experimental validation could be further strengthened in several areas.

1.  **Clarity and Technical Details:**
    *   Several technical statements are made without sufficient justification. For instance, the approximation `∫ k_t(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)` in the introduction (Eq. 3) is central to the Type II formulation. While likely standard in analysis, a specific reference (e.g., to [24]) or a brief explanation in an appendix would be helpful for readers.
    *   The paper relies on Tikhonov regularization, which may not be familiar to the entire NIPS audience. A brief, one- or two-sentence explanation of the technique in the context of ill-posed inverse problems would improve the paper's accessibility.
    *   The datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be properly cited to ensure the reproducibility of the results.

2.  **Context and Comparison with Baselines:**
    *   The paper would benefit from a discussion of why the proposed direct estimation approach is superior to a naive "plug-in" baseline. Specifically, what are the drawbacks of first estimating p and q separately using a standard method like Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) and then simply taking their ratio? A comparison, even if just conceptual, would better motivate the need for specialized algorithms like FIRE.
    *   The role of the kernel bandwidth parameter `t` is intriguing. In the special case of density estimation (e.g., Type II with `q=1`), the analysis does not appear to require `t` to converge to zero, unlike in standard KDE where this is necessary for consistency. A discussion on the different roles of `t` in this framework versus in KDE would provide valuable insight for readers familiar with density estimation.

3.  **Significance of Theoretical Results:**
    *   Corollaries 2 and 4 provide convergence rates for the FIRE estimator. To better assess the significance of these results, it would be highly beneficial to compare them with the known minimax optimal rates for density ratio estimation (e.g., as discussed in Nguyen et al. [14], which the authors cite). This comparison would clarify how close the FIRE estimator is to the theoretical optimum.

4.  **Experimental Validation:**
    *   While the experiments on real-world datasets are valuable, the paper lacks a simple, illustrative toy problem. For example, using 1D Gaussian distributions for p and q would allow for a clear visualization of the true density ratio versus the estimated one. Such a plot would provide readers with a much stronger intuition for the method's performance and behavior.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound method for density ratio estimation by framing it as a Fredholm inverse problem. The resulting framework is elegant, and the derived algorithms and model selection procedure are practical. The primary weaknesses relate to a lack of context against simple baselines, the need for further clarification on some technical points, and an absence of intuitive toy examples in the experiments. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify the practical and theoretical advantages of this promising approach.
