- Proposes FIRE: reformulates density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, enabling principled regularization.  
- Connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory and operator/kernel methods, offering a new perspective.  
- Framework handles both Type I (samples from both distributions) and Type II (q known at p-sampled points) settings; Type I is relevant for covariate shift.  
- Main contributions: (1) theoretical framework casting density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.  
- Main contributions: (2) a flexible FIRE algorithm family within the kernel learning framework.  
- Main contributions: (3) detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds.  
- Main contributions: (4) CD-CV, a novel unsupervised model selection method (Cross-Density Cross-Validation).  
- Strong technical quality: comprehensive concentration bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and II across domains.  
- Concern: Theorem 1’s convergence rate has a slow (-t/log λ) factor for fixed t; polynomial rates might be achievable under stronger spectral assumptions.  
- Suggestion: Discuss whether assumptions on the operator spectrum or q/p (as in Caponnetto & De Vito 2007) could yield polynomial rates.  
- CD-CV is a major practical strength, addressing unsupervised parameter selection where standard cross-validation fails (unlike KMM).  
- Experiments: FIRE outperforms KMM, LSIF, TIKDE on multiple datasets (USPS, CPUsmall, Kin8nm) showing better accuracy, computational efficiency, and robustness to parameter choice.  
- Limitation: FIRE assumes q/p lies in a “nice” function class (e.g., Sobolev); this often requires knowledge of tail behavior of p and q, which may be unavailable.  
- Presentation: Well-written and organized with thorough related-work comparison, but supplementary material has typos/technical errors (e.g., Lemma 5 typo W²₂→Wˢ₂, errors at lines 675/686; Eq.31 is correct).  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — fix supplementary proofs and add discussion on theoretical rates under stronger assumptions.
