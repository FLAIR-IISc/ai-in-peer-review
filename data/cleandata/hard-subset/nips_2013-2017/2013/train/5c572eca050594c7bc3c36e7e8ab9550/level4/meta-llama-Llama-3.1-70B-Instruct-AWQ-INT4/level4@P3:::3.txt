The authors propose a novel approach to estimating the ratio of two probability density functions, leveraging a kernel and a Fredholm integral equation to formulate an inverse problem. This work is situated within the context of machine learning and statistical inference, where density ratio estimation is crucial for various applications, including importance sampling and covariate shift.

The authors' approach is based on reformulating the density ratio estimation problem as an integral equation, known as the Fredholm equation of the first kind. By employing techniques from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), they develop a principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically. The resulting family of algorithms, dubbed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), is flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, for densities defined on Rd and smooth d-dimensional sub-manifolds of the Euclidean space. The authors also introduce a novel unsupervised method for model selection, called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), which is specifically designed for the density ratio estimation setting.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experimental results on several datasets, comparing their method to existing alternatives, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). The results show that FIRE performs competitively with or even outperforms these methods in various settings.

Strengths of the paper include:

1. Novel formulation of the density ratio estimation problem as an inverse problem, leveraging techniques from classical analysis.
2. Development of a principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically.
3. Introduction of a novel unsupervised method for model selection, CD-CV.
4. Thorough theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement:

1. The authors assume the existence of densities, which may not always be the case in practice. It would be beneficial to discuss the implications of this assumption and potential ways to relax it.
2. The paper could benefit from a clearer statement of assumptions, such as the choice of dominating measure and the properties of the density functions.
3. Some of the theoretical results, such as the convergence rates, may be difficult to interpret without additional context or explanations.
4. The experimental results, while encouraging, are limited to a few datasets and could be strengthened by including more comprehensive evaluations.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation, offering a novel approach and a principled framework for constructing algorithms. With some clarifications and extensions, this work has the potential to make a substantial impact in the machine learning and statistical communities.

Arguments pro acceptance:

1. Novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation.
2. Thorough theoretical analysis and convergence rates.
3. Effective experimental results on several datasets.
4. Introduction of a novel unsupervised method for model selection.

Arguments con acceptance:

1. Assumption of densities may not always hold in practice.
2. Lack of clarity on dominating measure and density properties.
3. Limited experimental evaluations.
4. Some theoretical results may be difficult to interpret.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, addressing the areas mentioned above to strengthen the paper's contributions and clarity.
