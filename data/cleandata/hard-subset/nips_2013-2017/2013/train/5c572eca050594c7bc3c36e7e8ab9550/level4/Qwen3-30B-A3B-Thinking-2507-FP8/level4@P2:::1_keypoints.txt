- Addresses density ratio estimation q(x)/p(x), important for importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation.
- Reformulates density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via regularization in an RKHS.
- Introduces the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms as a principled theoretical framework.
- Establishes a novel connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems/operator theory.
- Develops a flexible framework supporting various norms and regularization techniques for different density-ratio tasks.
- Provides detailed theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case, including concentration bounds and convergence rates on Euclidean spaces and submanifolds.
- Proposes CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a new unsupervised method for model selection in density ratio estimation.
- Demonstrates competitive empirical performance on benchmarks (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) supporting practical significance.
- Writing and presentation are clear and accessible, with careful explanations of math and experiments.
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and experimental setup is comprehensive and reproducible.
- Missing justification or reference for the kernel approximation ∫Rd kt(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t); include proof or citation.
- Needs clearer discussion of how the bandwidth parameter t behaves differently here versus standard KDE (bandwidth convergence issues).
- Should compare provided upper bounds on convergence rates with known minimax rates (e.g., from [14]) to contextualize theory.
- Lacks explicit comparison of theoretical results with related work [12]; include to clarify relative contributions.
- Experimental section would benefit from visualizations of estimated density ratios on simple synthetic (e.g., 1D) examples for intuition.
- Provide more specific citations/references for datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).
- Actionable recommendations: add kernel-approximation justification, elaborate bandwidth distinction, include synthetic visualizations, cite datasets, and compare theoretical bounds to minimax rates.
- Overall assessment: novel, significant, well-written, and technically strong paper; reviewer recommends acceptance.
