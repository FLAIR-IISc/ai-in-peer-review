

The authors employ two key equalities to derive estimators of the Radon-Nikodym derivative \(f = p/q\):  
(i) \(\mathbb{E}_p[k(x, \cdot) f] = \mathbb{E}_q[k(x, \cdot)]\) and  
(ii) \(\mathbb{E}_p[k(x, \cdot) f] \approx q(x)\).  
The integral operators are replaced with empirical approximations. The kernel family \(\{k(x, \cdot)\}\) serves as a set of test functions over which the discrepancy between the left- and right-hand sides is minimized. This choice leverages standard kernel methods for optimization. Convergence guarantees are provided for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, with the convergence rate depending critically on the ambient dimension—consistent with typical density estimation behavior.  

The paper presents a well-executed approach to a relevant problem. The following points warrant clarification:  
- **Convergence rates**: Optimizing over \(\{k(x, \cdot)\}\) suffices for convergence to the ratio in (i). While the bandwidth dependence in (ii) offers intuitive justification, a brief argument for (i) would strengthen the presentation.  
- **Cost function**: The optimization on page 2 uses the \(L_2(p)\) norm, but equation (9) combines \(L_2(p)\) and \(L_2(q)\) norms. A motivation for this choice—ideally linking it to specific application-driven objectives—is needed.  
- **Application context**: The importance sampling example (estimating \(q/p\) to shift from \(\mathbb{E}_q\) to \(\mathbb{E}_p\)) is noted. However, the method requires empirical estimates of \(\mathbb{E}_p\) and \(\mathbb{E}_q\) to construct the cost function. Why not directly use the empirical \(\mathbb{E}_q\) estimate? The brief mention on page 5 that sampling from \(\mathbb{E}_q\) is difficult suggests this is the intended application; elaboration would clarify the scope.  
- **Smoothness assumptions**: The regularizer enforces smoothness on \(q/p\), while Theorem 1 assumes smoothness on \(p\) and \(q\). The relationship between smoothness of \(p, q\) and their ratio requires discussion. For instance, smooth \(p, q\) (bounded away from zero) implies a smooth ratio, but the converse may not hold (e.g., \(p, q\) can be irregular on uncountable sets while yielding a smooth ratio).  
- **Dimension dependence**: A discussion of the practical range of dimensions for which the method is effective would be valuable.  
- **Test function family**: The motivation for selecting the specific family \(\mathcal{U}\) merits elaboration.  
- **Methodology perspective**: In covariate shift settings, a key challenge is designing a generic cost function. The approach of using test functions to extract densities is conceptually similar to [1], though the formulation for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is more direct. A brief reflection on whether this constitutes "the right" approach compared to alternatives (e.g., via expectation operators and test functions) would be insightful.  

The paper is timely, makes a strong contribution, and includes a thorough convergence analysis alongside empirical validation.
