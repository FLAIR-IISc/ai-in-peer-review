The paper proposes a novel method for estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x). While several approaches already exist for this problem, the key innovation of this work lies in reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.

The paper is well-written and engaging. The density ratio estimation problem is both important and interesting, with applications in areas like importance sampling and transfer learning. The connection to classical operator theory provides a strong theoretical foundation.

However, several points could be strengthened. First, the paper occasionally lacks sufficient detail. For example, the statement regarding \int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) requires either a proof (in the paper or supplementary material) or a clear citation. Similarly, other such statements, even if intuitively clear, would benefit from supporting references. 

Second, the authors note that this approach avoids the need for bandwidth to converge to zero, unlike standard kernel density estimation (KDE). A more detailed discussion of this advantage, clarifying the differing roles of bandwidth parameters in this method versus KDE, would be valuable for readers.

Third, while Tikhonov regularization is a central technique, many in the NIPS community may be unfamiliar with it. Adding a few sentences explaining the core idea and motivation behind this regularization method would enhance accessibility. 

Fourth, the proofs are relegated to the supplementary material. While acceptable, it would be beneficial to have key insights or summaries of the proofs highlighted within the main text.

Fifth, a brief discussion comparing this method to a naive plug-in approach – separately estimating p and q – would help illustrate the advantages of the proposed framework. Why is solving the integral equation more effective than direct density estimation?

Sixth, the paper provides convergence rate bounds (Corollary 2 and 4). However, it lacks context regarding the optimality of these rates. Comparing them to known minimax bounds, such as those derived in [12], would strengthen the analysis and provide a clearer understanding of the method's performance.

Seventh, please provide citations for the datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

Finally, a demonstration of the method's performance on simple, controlled “toy” problems with known p and q would be highly beneficial. For example, a plot comparing the true q(x)/p(x) with the estimated ratio for one-dimensional densities would provide a visual assessment of the method’s accuracy and limitations.

Overall, this is a solid paper on density ratio estimation. While it lacks a direct comparison to other state-of-the-art methods (like [12]) and a demonstration on toy problems, the formulation as a Fredholm equation and the accompanying theoretical analysis are valuable contributions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence rate bounds.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Addresses an important problem with potential applications in various fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks comparison to existing minimax bounds.
*   Missing demonstration on toy problems.
*   Some details and motivations are not fully explained (e.g., advantages over naive plug-in estimation, Tikhonov regularization).
*   Reliance on supplementary material for proofs.
