

The paper introduces a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. While several existing methods address this task, the authors' theoretical perspective offers a distinct framework. The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in machine learning.

**Strengths:**  
The writing is clear and engaging, and the density ratio estimation problem is both timely and significant.

**Weaknesses and Recommendations:**  

(i) The derivation of $\int k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)$ requires justification. A proof (in the paper or supplementary material) or a reference should be provided for all such technical assertions, regardless of their apparent simplicity.  

(ii) The method's application to density estimation (via $q=1$) is noteworthy, as it avoids the bandwidth convergence requirement common in standard KDE, which is inconsistent in this setting. The paper should explicitly clarify how the bandwidth parameters in this approach differ from those in KDE, emphasizing this key advantage.  

(iii) A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would benefit the NIPS audience, as most readers may not be familiar with this technique.  

(iv) All proofs are confined to the supplementary material; the reviewer has not verified their correctness.  

(v) The paper should contrast its method with a naive plug-in approach (estimating $p$ and $q$ separately), explaining why this alternative yields inferior results.  

(vi) The comparison to existing methods is insufficient. While Corollaries 2 and 4 establish upper bounds on convergence rates, it is unclear how these bounds relate to the minimax optimal rate (e.g., as derived in [12]). A discussion of this gap is essential.  

(vii) The sources for datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be cited.  

(viii) A toy example with known 1D densities (e.g., plotting the true $p/q$ against the estimated ratio) would significantly aid reader understanding of the method's practical performance. This is particularly important given the lack of concrete demonstration for simple cases.  

In summary, while the theoretical contribution is promising, the paper requires stronger technical justification, clearer comparisons to prior work, and illustrative examples to fully demonstrate its value. The core idea is interesting, but the current presentation leaves several critical questions unanswered for the NIPS audience.
