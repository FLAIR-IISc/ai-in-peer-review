The authors derive two estimators of the Radon-Nikodym derivative f = p/q using two equivalent relationships: (i) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] = E<sub>q</sub>[k(x,.)] and (ii) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] ≈ q(x). The integral operators are replaced with their empirical counterparts, and the set {k(x,.)} can be interpreted as a collection of test functions, chosen to minimize the difference between the left- and right-hand sides of these equations. A key advantage of selecting {k(x,.)} as the family of test functions is that it allows for the application of standard kernel methods for optimization.

Convergence guarantees are provided for both settings under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities, with the convergence rate depending significantly on the dimensionality of the underlying space—a result consistent with the challenges inherent in density estimation. 

Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution. However, I have several comments:

- Regarding the convergence rates: optimizing over the set {k(x,.)} is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the correct ratio. While this is intuitive for (ii) given the bandwidth dependence, it would be helpful to have a concise, intuitive explanation of why it also holds for (i).
- The optimization performed on page 2 utilizes an L<sub>2,p</sub> norm, while equation (9) employs a combination of L<sub>2,p</sub> and L<sub>2,q</sub> norms. More motivation for this choice of cost function would be appreciated. Ideally, the cost function selection should be linked to specific applications of interest.
- Focusing on applications, the paper mentions importance sampling, where q/p is relevant for transitioning from E<sub>q</sub> to E<sub>p</sub>. Given that the method estimates both E<sub>p</sub> and E<sub>q</sub> to obtain the empirical cost function, I wonder why not directly utilize the E<sub>q</sub> estimate? Could E<sub>p</sub>(q/p) offer an improvement over directly using E<sub>q</sub>?  A clearer justification of the overall setup would be beneficial, especially given the need to estimate E<sub>p</sub> and E<sub>q</sub> to estimate q/p. The sentence on page 5 mentioning the difficulty of sampling from E<sub>q</sub> hints at this motivation; is this the primary application envisioned?
- The regularizer enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p. Later, the theorem includes smoothness assumptions on q and p themselves, which seems natural. This raises the question of the relationship between the smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of their ratio. Perhaps many different q and p can produce the same ratio, and arbitrarily modifying q or p at a countable (or uncountable) number of points could lead to an irregular ratio. Consequently, it may be the case that smoothness of q and p (possibly bounded away from zero) implies smoothness of the ratio, or alternatively, a smooth ratio implies the existence of smooth q and p that generate it. 
- It would be beneficial to discuss the dependence of the convergence rates on the dimension *d*. For what values of *d* would this method be expected to remain useful?
- Is there a strong justification for the choice of the family of test functions U?
- A broader question: in the context of q/p estimation, covariate shift, and similar problems, a major challenge appears to be defining a suitable, general-purpose cost function. One approach involves working through expectation operators and applying various test functions to extract information about the underlying densities. Do the authors believe the chosen {k(x,.)} approach represents an optimal solution? There is some similarity to the work in [1], which also explores transformations to access quantities of interest. While the presented approach for the Radon-Nikodym derivative is more elegant by leveraging test functions, the overall strategy shares similarities.
[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor 

In conclusion, this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem, provides a convergence analysis, and includes experimental validation.
