- Problem & contribution: Estimates the ratio q(x)/p(x) by reformulating it as a Fredholm equation and proposing FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) methods using RKHS regularization.  
- Theoretical results: Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates (including for the Gaussian kernel).  
- Novel formulation: Casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation bridges statistical learning and classical operator theory.  
- Algorithm flexibility: FIRE algorithms are simple, flexible, and easy to implement within kernel frameworks.  
- Empirical validation: Experiments show promising performance across datasets and settings.  
- Presentation: The paper is well-written and clear.  
- Intuition for convergence: Needs a clearer intuitive explanation of why optimizing over kernel functions {k(x,·)} ensures convergence to q/p, especially for the first integral equation.  
- Cost function motivation: The move from using only L2,p to incorporating L2,q (Eq. 9) requires stronger motivation and connection to applications.  
- Application justification: Should better explain scenarios where estimating q/p is preferable to directly estimating Ep and Eq, e.g., when sampling from q is difficult.  
- Smoothness assumptions: Should discuss how smoothness of q and p relates to smoothness of their ratio to strengthen theory.  
- Dimensionality dependence: Lacks discussion of how convergence rates depend on data dimension d and practical effectiveness in high dimensions.  
- Test function family: Needs further justification for the chosen family of test functions U used in cross-validation.  
- Comparison to prior work: A more nuanced comparison to related methods (e.g., Grünewälder et al.) and clarifying differences in cost-function design is needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated approach with strong theoretical guarantees and promising empirical results addressing a relevant problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Some motivational and intuition gaps (L2,q choice, test functions) and insufficient discussion of dimensionality limitations.  
- Overall assessment: Strong paper with significant potential impact; strengths outweigh weaknesses and recommendation is to accept.
