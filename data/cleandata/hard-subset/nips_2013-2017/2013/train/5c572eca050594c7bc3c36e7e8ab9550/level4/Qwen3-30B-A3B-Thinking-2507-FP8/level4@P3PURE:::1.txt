

The paper introduces a novel approach to density ratio estimation \(p/q\) by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. While several existing methods address this problem, the proposed reformulation constitutes a significant theoretical contribution.  

**Strengths:**  
The paper is well-written and clearly articulates an important and timely problem in density ratio estimation.  

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**  
(i) The derivation of \(\int k_t(x,y)f(x)\,dx = f(y) + O(t)\) lacks justification. A proof (in the main text or supplementary material) or a reference is required for this key approximation.  
(ii) As a special case (\(q=1\)), the method performs density estimation without requiring the bandwidth to converge to zero—unlike standard KDE, which is inconsistent in this regime. The paper should clarify the distinct roles of bandwidth parameters here versus KDE to highlight this advantage.  
(iii) A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would benefit readers unfamiliar with this technique, which is common in the NIPS community.  
(iv) All proofs are relegated to the supplementary material; the reviewer has not verified their correctness.  
(v) The paper should explicitly contrast the proposed method with a naive plug-in approach (estimating \(p\) and \(q\) separately), explaining why the latter is inferior.  
(vi) The comparison to existing methods requires clarification. While Corollaries 2 and 4 provide upper bounds on convergence rates, it is unclear how these rates compare to the minimax optimal rate established in [12].  
(vii) References for datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) must be provided.  
(viii) A toy example with known 1D densities \(p\) and \(q\) (e.g., plots of true vs. estimated \(p/q\)) would help readers assess estimation quality.  

The method is promising, but the paper would benefit from addressing these points to clarify its theoretical advantages and empirical performance relative to existing work.
