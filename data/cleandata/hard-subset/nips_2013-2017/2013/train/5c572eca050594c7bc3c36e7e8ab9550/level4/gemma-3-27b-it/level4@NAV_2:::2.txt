This paper introduces a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as an inverse problem based on Fredholm integral equations. The authors propose two algorithms, FIRE, leveraging regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. A key contribution is the development of a completely unsupervised model selection method, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), addressing a significant challenge in unsupervised learning. The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, and demonstrates encouraging empirical results.

The work builds upon recent advancements in density ratio estimation, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10], and connects them to classical operator theory. While related to KMM, the proposed approach offers advantages due to the L2 formulation, which enables the application of cross-validation for parameter selection – a crucial benefit in unsupervised settings where standard validation techniques are less readily applicable. The authors correctly point out the limitations of using RKHS-based objectives directly for kernel selection via CV, as the resulting values are not inherently comparable across different kernels.

The theoretical analysis is a significant strength, providing valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms. However, the convergence rates presented in Theorem 1 appear relatively slow due to the dependence on `-t/log λ`. It would strengthen the paper to investigate under what additional assumptions a polynomial convergence rate, independent of *t* after fixing the rate of *λ*, could be achieved. Existing literature on kernel methods, such as Caponnetto and De Vito (2007), demonstrates how assumptions on the operator spectrum or smoothness of the target function can yield improved rates. Exploring such conditions within the context of this work would be a valuable addition. 

The paper acknowledges the inherent limitations of density ratio estimation, particularly the strong assumptions required on the ratio *q/p* (e.g., smoothness, boundedness), which can negatively impact the behavior of *p/q*. Further discussion regarding these limitations, and potential strategies for mitigating their effects, would be beneficial.

While the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, the supplementary material requires significant improvement. Numerous typos and minor errors hinder the verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. For instance, in Lemma 5, *W2^2* should be *Ws^2*, and the bound contains inaccuracies. Corrections are needed in the expressions at lines 675 and 686, and the integral domain in the proof of Lemma 5 (line 1147) needs clarification. A thorough revision of the supplementary material is strongly recommended given its importance for validating the core theoretical contributions of the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem.
*   Development of a practical and effective unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV).
*   Detailed theoretical analysis providing convergence guarantees.
*   Competitive empirical performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Clear connections to existing literature and identification of limitations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Relatively slow convergence rates in Theorem 1.
*   Supplementary material contains numerous errors requiring substantial revision.
*   Limited discussion of the strong assumptions required for density ratio estimation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The proposed approach is theoretically sound, practically relevant, and addresses a significant challenge in unsupervised learning. While the convergence rates could be improved, and the supplementary material requires substantial revision, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the concerns raised regarding the supplementary material and exploring potential avenues for achieving faster convergence rates.
