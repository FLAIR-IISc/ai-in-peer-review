This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, where one is a probability density, framed as an inverse problem utilizing Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques. The core idea – reformulating density ratio estimation as solving an integral equation – is interesting and connects the problem to a well-established area of mathematical physics and analysis. This allows the authors to leverage tools from spectral theory and regularization to develop a family of algorithms, termed FIRE, and provide theoretical guarantees. 

The paper is well-motivated, drawing connections to importance sampling, covariate shift, and transfer learning – areas of active research in NIPS. The authors correctly identify related work, including Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), and clearly position their approach relative to these existing methods. The introduction of the Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) technique for unsupervised parameter selection is a valuable contribution. The theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, is a significant strength, demonstrating a rigorous approach to the problem.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the connection to Fredholm equations is presented, the practical implications of this connection aren’t always fully clear. The discussion of the underlying measure (ν) on which the densities are defined is insufficient. The authors implicitly assume the existence of a density with respect to ν, but fail to adequately address the potential issues arising from cases where such a density might not exist, or is not uniquely defined. A clearer statement of assumptions, including the domain of x (Rd or a more general space) and the positivity of the density p, is needed.

The empirical evaluation, while showcasing promising results, lacks sufficient detail. The choice of test functions for the CD-CV method and the specific resampling strategies employed in the experiments are not fully justified. A more comprehensive comparison with existing methods, particularly KMM with a fair out-of-sample extension, is desirable. Furthermore, the experimental section uses a rather large number of references without providing comprehensive analysis of the work done in those references. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The Fredholm equation approach provides a new perspective on density ratio estimation.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The inclusion of concentration bounds and convergence rates adds rigor to the proposed methods.
* **CD-CV Method:** The unsupervised parameter selection technique is a practical contribution.
* **Clear Connections to Related Work:** The paper positions itself effectively within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Insufficient Discussion of Underlying Measure:** The implicit assumption of a density with respect to a dominating measure is not thoroughly addressed.
* **Lack of Clarity in Assumptions:** Key assumptions regarding the domain and density properties are not explicitly stated.
* **Limited Experimental Detail:** The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient justification for choices made and a thorough comparison with alternatives. 
* **Over-referencing:** Many references are listed without significant discussion of prior work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and mathematically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the properties of the proposed algorithms.
* The CD-CV method offers a practical solution for parameter selection.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity regarding the underlying measure and assumptions weakens the theoretical foundation.
* The limited experimental detail and comparative analysis hinder a thorough evaluation of the methods.
* The connection to Fredholm equations does not fully translate into practical benefits.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the paper presents a technically sound and theoretically interesting approach, the lack of clarity in certain areas and the limited empirical evaluation prevent a stronger recommendation. Addressing the concerns regarding the underlying measure, assumptions, and experimental detail would significantly strengthen the paper. The potential for future work building on this novel formulation is significant, making it a worthwhile contribution, albeit one that requires further refinement. I encourage the authors to address the reviewer concerns in a revised version.
