## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission: [Paper Title]

This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation, a problem with existing solutions, by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation. The core idea is compelling and potentially impactful, and the paper is, overall, well-written and engaging. The problem itself is important and of clear interest to the NIPS community. However, several points require clarification and further development to fully assess the method’s strengths and weaknesses.

**Strengths:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for addressing density ratio estimation is clearly articulated, and the presentation of the proposed method is logical. The potential for density estimation as a special case when q=1 is a particularly interesting observation. The fact that this approach doesn’t require the bandwidth parameter to converge to zero, offering a potential advantage over standard kernel density estimation (KDE) in certain scenarios, is a significant highlight.

**Weaknesses, Comments, and Questions:**

(i) The paper occasionally lacks sufficient detail regarding key mathematical justifications. Specifically, the claim that  ∫ *k<sub>t</sub>*(x,y)*f*(x)dx = *f*(y) + O(t) requires either a proof (within the paper or supplementary material) or a clear citation. This applies to similar statements throughout the manuscript; even if derivations appear straightforward, providing justification strengthens the rigor.

(ii) Expanding on the connection between this method and standard KDE would be beneficial. While the observation regarding bandwidth convergence is noted, further discussion clarifying the distinct roles of the bandwidth parameters in the proposed method versus those in KDE would help readers fully understand the nuances of the approach. A more explicit comparison of the parameter interpretations is needed.

(iii) Given that Tikhonov regularization is likely unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS audience, a brief introductory explanation of the technique – its purpose and general properties – would substantially improve accessibility. 

(iv) The reliance on supplementary material for all proofs is a concern. While acceptable, it places a higher burden on the reviewer to verify the correctness of the theoretical foundations. Confirmation of these proofs is beyond the scope of this review.

(v) A discussion of why a naive plug-in density estimation approach (i.e., separately estimating *p* and *q* and then forming their ratio) is suboptimal compared to the proposed method would add valuable context. Highlighting the advantages of this integral equation formulation over direct density estimation would strengthen the justification for the proposed technique.

(vi) While Corollaries 2 and 4 present upper bounds on the convergence rate, the paper lacks a clear assessment of how these bounds relate to known minimax optimality results. Specifically, how do these bounds compare to those established in related work, such as [12]? Understanding the gap between the achieved rates and the theoretical optimum is crucial.

(vii) The datasets used in the experimental evaluation (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) are not fully referenced. Please provide appropriate citations for these datasets, enabling reproducibility and allowing readers to understand their characteristics.

(viii) The absence of a demonstration on simple, well-defined toy problems is a notable omission. For instance, visualizing the true and estimated density ratios *p*/ *q* for 1-dimensional *p* and *q* distributions would provide a readily interpretable assessment of the method’s performance and help readers build intuition about its behavior. This would significantly aid in understanding the practical implications of the theoretical results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper addressing an important problem in density estimation. The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a novel and interesting approach. However, several areas require further clarification and elaboration. Specifically, addressing the points raised concerning mathematical justifications, comparisons to existing methods (particularly regarding minimax optimality), and the inclusion of illustrative examples on toy problems would substantially strengthen the paper. While the current work is a valuable contribution, resolving these issues would be necessary to fully establish its impact.
