The authors derive two estimators for the Radon-Nikodym derivative f = p/q using two equalities: (i) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] = E<sub>q</sub>[k(x,.)] and (ii) E<sub>p</sub>[k(x,.)f] ≈ q(x). The integral operators are replaced with their empirical counterparts. The set {k(x,.)} can be interpreted as a collection of test functions, chosen to minimize the difference between the left- and right-hand sides of these equations. A key advantage of using {k(x,.)} as the family of test functions is that it allows for optimization using standard kernel methods. 

Convergence guarantees are provided for both settings, under smoothness and boundedness assumptions on the densities. As expected for density estimation, the rate of convergence is crucially dependent on the dimension of the underlying space. 

Overall, I find this to be a well-executed and valuable contribution. However, I have a few comments:

- Regarding the convergence rates: Optimizing over the set {k(x,.)} is sufficient to guarantee convergence to the ratio. For (ii), this is intuitive given the bandwidth dependence. It would be helpful to include a brief intuitive explanation of why this also holds for (i).

- On page 2, the optimization is performed over L<sub>2,p</sub>, while equation (9) involves a combination of L<sub>2,p</sub> and L<sub>2,q</sub>. Some motivation for this choice of cost function would be appreciated. Ideally, this choice should be linked to the specific tasks the authors envision this method being used for – connecting the cost function to important applications would strengthen the justification.

- Speaking of applications, importance sampling is presented as one example, where q/p is relevant for moving from E<sub>q</sub> to E<sub>p</sub>. My question is this: you estimate both E<sub>p</sub> and E<sub>q</sub> to obtain your empirical cost function. Why not directly use the E<sub>q</sub> that you already have? Could E<sub>p</sub>(q/p) offer any advantages over directly using E<sub>q</sub>? I'm essentially asking for a clearer motivation for the overall setup, given that estimates of both E<sub>p</sub> and E<sub>q</sub> are needed to estimate q/p. The sentence on page 5 concerning the difficulty of sampling from E<sub>q</sub> hints at this, so is that the primary application you have in mind?

- The regularizer enforces smoothness on the ratio q/p. Later, the theorem includes smoothness assumptions on q and p, which seems more natural. I’m curious about the relationship between the smoothness of q and p and the smoothness of their ratio. Perhaps the answer is that many different q and p can produce the same ratio, and changing q and p on a countable (or uncountable) number of points can create an extremely irregular function. Therefore, smoothness of q and p (possibly bounded away from 0) might imply smoothness of the ratio, or conversely, a smooth ratio might imply the existence of smooth q and p that produce it.

- It would be helpful to discuss the dependence of your convergence rates on the dimension *d*. For what values of *d* would you expect your method to be most useful?

- Is there a strong justification for your choice of the family of test functions U?

- A more fundamental question: In the q/p, covariate shift, etc. setting, a major challenge seems to be defining a generic cost function. One approach, as you’ve done, is to work through expectation operators and apply various test functions to extract the underlying densities. Do you believe you’ve found an optimal approach with the set {k(x,.)}? There is some similarity to the work in [1], which also involves manipulating transformations to estimate quantities of interest. Your approach to the Radon-Nikodym derivative is certainly more elegant by working directly with test functions. However, the overall strategy shares some similarities. 
[1] Smooth Operators; Grunewalder, Gretton, Shawe-Taylor

In conclusion, I believe this is a timely and strong paper that addresses a relevant problem, provides a rigorous convergence study, and includes experimental results.
