This paper introduces two novel methods for density ratio estimation, framing both within a regularization framework inspired by Fredholm equations. The first method leverages importance sampling and formulates the problem as minimizing an L<sup>2</sup>-norm-based deviation from equality, combined with an RKHS regularization term (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method employs a kernel with a locality parameter designed to approach zero for consistency, also expressed as a regularization problem (Type II Fredholm equation). The authors support their approach with theoretical convergence results and experimental validation.

A key strength of this work lies in its ability to utilize cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection. While CV is a standard practice in supervised learning, effective parameter tuning methods for unsupervised tasks like density ratio estimation are less common. This paper addresses this gap by demonstrating how the L<sup>2</sup> formulation enables the application of CV, a significant advantage over methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM). The authors rightly point out that the objective functions used in methods based on RKHS norms, such as KMM, are less suitable for CV-based kernel selection due to the dependence of objective function values on the kernel itself, making meaningful comparisons difficult.

However, the convergence rate presented in Theorem 1 appears slow, scaling with a factor of (-t/log λ) for fixed *t*. Kernel methods literature often demonstrates polynomial convergence rates with respect to sample size when *t* is fixed, after establishing a rate for λ. It would be valuable to investigate whether stronger assumptions could yield a polynomial rate for the proposed estimator. For example, building upon the work of Caponnetto and De Vito (2007), which utilizes assumptions on the spectrum of the operator K<sub>p,t</sub> to achieve optimal rates in kernel ridge regression, could potentially lead to improved convergence bounds. Exploring how a stronger assumption on the ratio *q/p* might facilitate a polynomial rate would also strengthen the theoretical contribution. Discussing such possibilities, even speculatively, would increase the paper’s impact. 

A critical point often overlooked in density ratio estimation is the underlying assumption that *q/p* belongs to a well-behaved function class, such as a Sobolev space. This is a strong assumption, requiring *a priori* knowledge about the ratio or the tails of the underlying distributions *p* and *q*. Furthermore, satisfying this condition for *q/p* often implies poor behavior for the inverse ratio *p/q*. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this inherent limitation of density ratio estimation within the paper.

Finally, the supplementary material requires substantial revision. Numerous typos and minor errors hinder verification of the theoretical results presented in the main paper. Specific examples include: Lemma 5 incorrectly states W<sub>2</sub><sup>2</sup> when it should be W<sub>s</sub><sup>2</sup>, and the bound is inaccurate, should be D<sub>1</sub>(t/-log λ)<sup>2</sup> + λ<sup>1-α</sup> D<sub>2</sub> ||f||<sub>2</sub><sup>2</sup>.  Expressions at lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, despite Eq.(31) being accurate. The domain of the integral in the proof of Lemma 5 (line 1147) is also improperly specified (should be  ||ξ||<sup>2</sup> ≥ ). Given that the convergence results represent core contributions of this paper, a thorough revision of the supplementary material is strongly recommended.

In conclusion, this paper presents a promising new approach to density ratio estimation, with the key benefit of enabling effective parameter selection via cross-validation. Addressing the concerns regarding the convergence rate and refining the supplementary material would significantly enhance the paper's overall quality and impact.
