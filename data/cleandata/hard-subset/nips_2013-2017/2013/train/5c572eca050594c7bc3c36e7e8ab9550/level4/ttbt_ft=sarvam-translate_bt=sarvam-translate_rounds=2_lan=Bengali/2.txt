This paper discusses two new methods for estimating density ratios using probability models. The first method uses the importance sampling equation and expresses it in the form of a regularization problem where the deviation from the mean and the L^2 norm of the RKHS regularization term are used (Type I Fredholm equation). The second method considers a kernel where the locality parameter should go to zero for consistency. The latter is also expressed in the form of regularization (Type II Fredholm equation). This paper shows some theoretical results on convergence and experimentally demonstrates practical performance.

Although the method is similar to existing methods like KMM, a strong advantage of the proposed method is that the L^2 formulation is capable of applying cross-validation (CV) methods to select the algorithm parameters. Unlike supervised problems, where CV is an effective method for selecting parameters in practice, there is no general method for unsupervised learning. This paper solves this problem for density ratio estimation with a clever idea of using the L^2 norm.

It is worth noting that the objective function given by RKHS, such as KMM, is not suitable for selecting a kernel with CV, because the value of the objective function depends on the kernel and is therefore not comparable.

In Theorem 1, the obtained convergence rate is slower than the constant t by a factor (-t/log \lambda). In much of the literature on kernel methods, including the constant t, a polynomial rate can be obtained with respect to the sample size after fixing the rate of \lambda. Is there any reasonable additional assumption that gives a polynomial rate for the proposed estimate? For example, Caponetto and Di Vito (2007) estimate the spectrum of the operator K_{p,t} to get the best rate for kernel ridge regression. I think a polynomial rate would be obtained if a stronger assumption was made on q/p. It would be more interesting if the authors discussed such polynomial rates under a stronger assumption with constant t.

When estimating density ratios, we usually have to make an assumption that q/p is a nice function class such as Sobachev. It is important to remember that this assumption is very strong, which requires some knowledge about the ratio or tail of p and q. Under such assumptions on q/p, the inverse ratio p/q usually behaves poorly. Some discussion should be included somewhere about this limitation of density ratio estimation.
