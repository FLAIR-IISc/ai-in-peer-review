

This paper addresses the important problem of density ratio estimation (q(x)/p(x)), which is crucial for importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The authors propose a novel approach by reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which they solve using regularization techniques within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This formulation leads to a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which provides a principled theoretical foundation for density ratio estimation.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, which has not been previously explored in the machine learning literature. Second, the authors develop a flexible framework that incorporates various norms and regularization techniques, allowing for different types of density ratio estimation problems. Third, they provide detailed theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for distributions on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. Finally, they introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a novel unsupervised method for model selection in density ratio estimation that addresses a critical practical challenge.

The paper excels in several dimensions. **Originality** is strong: the Fredholm equation perspective offers a fresh theoretical foundation that distinguishes this work from previous approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). The connection to classical operator theory is particularly valuable as it provides a bridge between statistical learning and mathematical analysis. **Significance** is well-supported through both theoretical contributions and empirical validation. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) while providing a theoretically grounded framework that addresses a fundamental challenge in transfer learning. **Clarity** is excellent: the paper is well-organized, with careful explanations of the mathematical framework and clear presentation of both theoretical results and experimental findings. The writing is accessible to readers with a basic understanding of machine learning and functional analysis. **Quality** is high: the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental setup is comprehensive, and the authors provide sufficient detail to reproduce their results.

The main weaknesses of the paper include some missing technical details that would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Specifically, the paper states that ∫Rd kt(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) for smooth functions f without providing a proof or reference. While this might be a standard result in kernel methods, including a brief justification or reference would improve the paper's technical completeness. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the bandwidth parameter t functions differently in their approach compared to standard kernel density estimation (KDE). In KDE, bandwidth must converge to zero for consistency, but the authors note that their method doesn't require this, which is an important conceptual distinction that deserves elaboration.

Additionally, while the paper provides upper bounds on convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4), it would be valuable to compare these bounds with known minimax rates (e.g., from [14]) to better contextualize the theoretical contribution. The paper mentions [12] in the related work but doesn't explicitly compare their theoretical results with those of [12], which would help readers understand the relative contribution.

The experimental section would be strengthened by including visualizations of the estimated density ratios on simple synthetic examples (e.g., 1D distributions with known analytical forms). Such visualizations would help readers intuitively assess the quality of the estimation. While the paper provides results on real-world datasets, a simple toy example would complement these findings.

The paper appropriately cites recent work in density ratio estimation, including KMM [9], LSIF [10], and related theoretical analyses [14, 26]. However, it would be helpful to provide more specific references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS), as these are commonly used in the machine learning community but may not be familiar to all readers.

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation by establishing a connection to classical inverse problems. Their theoretical framework provides a principled approach to this important problem, and their empirical results demonstrate practical utility. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant challenge in transfer learning and importance sampling.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide a brief justification or reference for the approximation ∫Rd kt(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)
2. Elaborate on the conceptual difference between the bandwidth parameter in their method versus standard KDE
3. Include visualizations of estimated density ratios on synthetic examples
4. Provide more specific references for the datasets used
5. More explicitly compare their theoretical bounds with known minimax rates

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to density ratio estimation. The theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper is well-written and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance.
