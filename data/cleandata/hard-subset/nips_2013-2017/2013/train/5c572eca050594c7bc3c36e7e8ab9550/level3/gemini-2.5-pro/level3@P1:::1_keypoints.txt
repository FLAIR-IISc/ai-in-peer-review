- Addresses density-ratio estimation q(x)/p(x) from samples (or p samples + evaluable q) — important for importance sampling and covariate shift.  
- Main novel idea: reframes density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind (an inverse problem).  
- Derives two integral-equation settings: Type I (K_p f = K_q 1) when samples from both p and q exist, and Type II (K_{t,p} f ≈ q) when q is known.  
- Solves the ill-posed problems via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, producing the FIRE family of estimators.  
- FIRE yields simple closed-form estimators as linear combinations of kernels centered on data points.  
- Provides detailed theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels: concentration bounds and convergence rates on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.  
- Proposes an unsupervised model-selection method, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).  
- Connects the framework to existing methods: KMM as an RKHS-loss variant and CD-CV related to LSIF’s objective.  
- Strength: original and principled formulation linking density-ratio estimation to operator/inverse-problem theory.  
- Strength: well-written, clearly organized with a clear distinction between Type I and Type II scenarios.  
- Strength: practical — FIRE is easy to implement with standard kernel operations and CD-CV addresses unsupervised model selection.  
- Strength: substantive theoretical backing stated in the main paper (proofs in supplement).  
- Weakness: lacks discussion comparing derived convergence rates to known minimax/optimal rates (e.g., paper [14]); unclear if rates are optimal or incur a price.  
- Weakness: needs stronger motivation versus naive plug-in KDE ratio approaches (explain why direct methods like FIRE/LSIF/KMM are preferable).  
- Weakness: experimental section would benefit from a simple 1D/2D toy figure showing p, q, true ratio, and FIRE estimate to build intuition.  
- Minor clarity/details needed: specify smoothness conditions for the approximation in Eq.3; add a one-sentence intuition for Tikhonov regularization; discuss the q(x)=1 special case and the kernel bandwidth role vs KDE; provide dataset citations for reproducibility.  
- Note: all proofs are deferred to the supplement and were not checked by the reviewer, so theoretical soundness depends on those unverified proofs.
