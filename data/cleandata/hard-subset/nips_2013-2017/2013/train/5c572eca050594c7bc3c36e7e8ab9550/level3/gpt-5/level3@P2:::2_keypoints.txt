- Formulates density-ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem via Fredholm integral equations and proposes two variants (Type I and Type II) solved with RKHS Tikhonov regularization (FIRE).  
- Type I: approximate operator Kp and right-hand side Kq1 from samples and obtain a closed-form RKHS solution; Type II: use localized kt to approximate Kt,p(r)≈q and solve when q known at sample points.  
- Provides representer-theorem algorithms, several loss choices (L2,p, mixed, RKHS norm), and an unsupervised model selection method CD-CV using random test functions for kernel/regularizer tuning.  
- Theoretical contributions: concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and smooth manifolds, with finite-sample bounds separating p and q sample sizes.  
- Empirical evaluation: comparisons against LSIF, KMM, and thresholded inverse KDE under covariate shift show encouraging performance.  
- Connects to prior work on inverse problems, spectral regularization, conditional mean embeddings, and density-ratio methods; Type I RKHS loss links to KMM as a special case.  
- Strength: clean, principled inverse-problem framing that unifies ideas and yields simple closed-form kernel algorithms with out-of-sample extension.  
- Strength: practical, fully unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) addressing a key tuning problem for density-ratio methods.  
- Strength: nontrivial theory with explicit finite-sample bounds and separate sample-size dependence for p and q.  
- Strength: empirical results competitive against strong baselines in regression and classification under covariate shift.  
- Weakness: convergence rates appear conservative/slow due to interaction of kernel localization t and regularization λ; request discussion on stronger source/spectral assumptions for faster/polynomial rates.  
- Weakness: strong smoothness assumption r ∈ W2 2(Ω) may be unrealistic—need discussion of robustness to unbounded or peaked r and support mismatch between p and q.  
- Weakness: FIRE does not enforce r≥0; negativity can harm importance weighting—suggest adding nonnegativity constraints or clipping and discuss effects.  
- Weakness: Type II has O(t) bias and lacks sufficient experiments; ask for a use case (e.g., when q known pointwise but hard to sample) and sensitivity analysis over t and λ.  
- Weakness: computational scalability concerns—solving dense linear systems is O(n^3)/O(n^2) memory; request wall-clock times and discussion of Nyström/Random Features/preconditioning.  
- Clarity issue: supplemental material contains numerous typos and inconsistent notation that impede verification; specific fixes requested for lemmas, equations, integral bounds, and symbol clashes.  
- Clarity suggestion: add a short intuition section explaining roles of t vs λ and guidance for joint selection.  
- Originality: framing via Fredholm equations and RKHS Tikhonov derivations offer a fresh, unified perspective; CD-CV is a novel, useful contribution despite relation to existing validation ideas.  
- Significance: valuable for covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling due to simplicity, stability, out-of-sample extension, and unsupervised model selection; manifold theory adds interest.  
- Pros: principled perspective, closed-form kernel algorithms, effective unsupervised model selection, clear relation to KMM, and theory for Rd and manifolds.  
- Cons: conservative rates, strong smoothness assumptions, no positivity enforcement, limited Type II experiments, and supplemental typos hinder verification.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing theoretical rate discussion, supplement corrections, and additional computational/Type II experiments.
