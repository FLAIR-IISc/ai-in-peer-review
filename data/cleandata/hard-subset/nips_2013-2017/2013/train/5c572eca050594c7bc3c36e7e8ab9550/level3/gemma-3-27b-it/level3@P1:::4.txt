This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the ratio of two functions, relevant to importance sampling and covariate shift problems, by reformulating the task as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm equations and regularization techniques. The approach leverages Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) and provides both theoretical convergence guarantees and experimental validation. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and represents a significant contribution to the field.

The core idea revolves around expressing the density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm integral equation. The authors cleverly utilize kernel methods to translate this into a computationally tractable optimization problem. Two formulations, Type I and Type II, are presented, offering flexibility depending on the availability of samples from both distributions or knowledge of the target function’s values. The theoretical analysis, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, is a strong point, demonstrating a rigorous understanding of the proposed method. The experimental results on both regression and classification tasks further support the efficacy of the “FIRE” algorithm and its competitiveness with existing approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a fresh perspective, connecting it to a well-established area of mathematical analysis.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence rates, which is crucial for establishing the reliability and scalability of the proposed method.
*   **Practicality:** The algorithms are presented as explicit formulas, making them easy to implement.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The experimental section demonstrates the performance of FIRE on various datasets and tasks, comparing it to relevant baselines.
*   **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for parameter selection is a valuable addition, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Intuition Gap:** While the mathematical derivation is clear, the initial justification for representing the solution in terms of the kernel functions {k(x,·)} could benefit from further intuitive explanation. Specifically, how does optimization over this space guarantee convergence, particularly in the Type I setting, similar to the bandwidth selection intuition in the Type II setting?
*   **Cost Function Motivation:** The transition from initial optimization over the L2,p norm to incorporating the L2,q norm (Eq. 9) is not immediately clear. A more thorough discussion of the motivation behind this combination and its relevance to specific application scenarios would be beneficial.
*   **Importance Sampling Application:** The paper frames the problem as estimating q/p, yet the estimated Ep and Eq are used directly in the empirical cost function.  It would be valuable to clarify scenarios where estimating q/p provides an advantage over directly utilizing the estimated Eq.
*   **Smoothness and Regularization:** A discussion on the interplay between the smoothness of the underlying densities p and q, the smoothness of their ratio q/p, and the role of the regularizer in controlling the solution’s smoothness would strengthen the theoretical foundation.  What conditions on p (e.g., being bounded away from zero) are necessary for well-behaved solutions?
*   **Dimensionality Dependence:** The theoretical analysis indicates dependence on the ambient dimension *d*. A discussion of the practical limitations regarding *d* – for what dimensions can this method be realistically applied? – would be insightful.
*   **Test Function Selection:** The choice of test functions *U* for the CD-CV method requires further justification.  Why were linear and half-space functions chosen? Are there theoretical or empirical reasons to believe they are suitable for a broad range of applications?
*   **Connection to Prior Work:** The paper briefly mentions related work, but a more in-depth discussion of how this approach relates to, and potentially generalizes, existing methods like “Smooth Operators” (Grünewälder et al.) would be valuable, positioning the work within the broader context of covariate shift adaptation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in machine learning.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The inclusion of theoretical analysis, including convergence rates, significantly strengthens the contribution.
*   The work is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Certain aspects of the motivation and intuition behind key choices (kernel representation, cost function combination) could be strengthened.
*   The discussion of the method’s limitations, particularly concerning dimensionality, could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that offers a valuable contribution to the field. While some clarifications regarding the motivation and limitations would be beneficial, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and experimental validation make it worthy of acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, expanding the discussion around the intuition behind using the kernel functions, explaining the motivation for the cost function, and providing a more detailed analysis of the dimensionality dependence would elevate the paper further.
