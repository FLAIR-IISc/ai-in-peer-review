## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques from kernel methods. The core idea of leveraging kernel methods to estimate the ratio *f = p/q* by reformulating the problem as solving an integral equation is well-motivated and elegantly presented. The connection to classical operator theory provides a strong theoretical foundation, and the resulting FIRE algorithm appears flexible and readily implementable. The inclusion of theoretical convergence guarantees, particularly with rates dependent on dimension, is a significant strength. The experimental results demonstrate promising performance compared to existing methods.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, building logically from the problem formulation to the algorithmic details and theoretical analysis. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, appropriately citing relevant literature in covariate shift, importance sampling, and kernel methods. The originality lies in the specific formulation as a Fredholm problem and the systematic application of regularization theory, offering a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem. The significance is considerable, as accurate density ratio estimation is crucial for various applications, including transfer learning and importance sampling. 

However, several points warrant further clarification. While the paper establishes that optimization over {k(x,·)} *ensures* a solution, an intuitive explanation of *why* this process leads to convergence, analogous to the bandwidth selection intuition in kernel density estimation, would be beneficial. The choice of cost function, initially L2,p but then mixing with L2,q in (9), feels somewhat arbitrary. A more detailed motivation, linking this choice to specific application scenarios, would strengthen the argument. 

Regarding importance sampling, the authors should clarify when estimating *q/p* is advantageous compared to directly utilizing the estimated *q*. Furthermore, a discussion of the interplay between the smoothness of *p* and *q* and the smoothness of their ratio would be valuable, including conditions ensuring *p* is bounded away from zero. The dependence on dimension *d* is a critical limitation. A more thorough discussion of the practical usability of the method for different dimensions is needed. Finally, the justification for the chosen family of test functions *U* could be expanded, and a comparison to related work like “Smooth Operators” (Grunewalder et al.) would contextualize the approach within the broader landscape of covariate shift adaptation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and convergence guarantees.
*   Novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
*   Promising experimental results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of intuitive explanation for convergence in the core equality.
*   Unclear motivation for the mixed L2,p/L2,q cost function.
*   Limited discussion of the method’s scalability to high dimensions.



Overall, this is a well-executed and timely paper with significant potential. Addressing the raised questions would further enhance its contribution and strengthen its case for acceptance.
