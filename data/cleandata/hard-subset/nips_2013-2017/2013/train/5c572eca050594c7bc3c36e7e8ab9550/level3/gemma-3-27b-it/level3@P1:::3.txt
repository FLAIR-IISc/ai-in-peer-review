This paper addresses the problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem through the lens of Fredholm integral equations. The authors leverage regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, resulting in a family of algorithms termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). They provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, and introduce a novel unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV. The work is motivated by applications in importance sampling and covariate shift, areas receiving significant attention in transfer learning and statistical inference.

The approach is well-grounded in classical operator theory, a connection the authors explicitly highlight. This allows them to draw on existing tools for analysis, a definite strength.  The formulation is elegant and offers a principled way to combine kernel methods with regularization. The resulting algorithms appear relatively straightforward to implement, making them potentially appealing for practical use.  The theoretical analysis, while complex, attempts to provide guarantees on the performance of the FIRE algorithms, which is crucial for establishing their reliability. The inclusion of CD-CV addresses the often-difficult problem of parameter selection in unsupervised learning scenarios.

However, the paper suffers from some notable weaknesses. A fundamental issue lies in the strong assumption of the existence of densities. The authors implicitly assume the existence of densities *p* and *q*, which is a significant restriction.  In many machine learning applications, dealing with arbitrary measures is essential, and measures that are arbitrarily close may lack densities.  Consequently, the problem statement itself might be problematic in a more general machine learning context. Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear specification of the underlying dominating measure ν with respect to which the densities are defined (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure). This omission affects the validity and interpretation of the results, as sets of ν-measure zero can significantly impact the behavior of the estimators.  

The rigor of the theoretical results is also a concern. The analysis relies on assumptions about smoothness and boundedness, but does not thoroughly address potential issues arising from sets of zero measure.  A clearer statement of the modeling assumptions is needed, specifically regarding the domain of *x* (R<sup>d</sup> or a more general topological space) and whether *p* is assumed to be strictly positive.  Without knowing if p > 0 everywhere, the results may be invalid or ambiguous. The paper references recent related work, including Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), but the comparative analysis feels somewhat superficial.  A deeper discussion of the trade-offs between FIRE and these existing methods, beyond computational efficiency, would be beneficial.  

The experimental section, while presenting promising results, could be strengthened.  While comparisons with TIKDE and LSIF are made, the choice of datasets and the specifics of the experimental setup could be elaborated upon. More detailed analysis of the results, including error bars and statistical significance tests, would enhance the credibility of the findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Principled approach based on RKHS and regularization.
*   Theoretical analysis providing convergence rates (albeit with strong assumptions).
*   Practical algorithm (FIRE) and unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong and potentially limiting assumption of density existence.
*   Lack of specification of the dominating measure.
*   Potential ambiguities in the theoretical analysis related to sets of measure zero.
*   Superficial comparative analysis with existing methods.
*   Experimental section lacks detailed statistical analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and mathematically well-developed approach to density ratio estimation.  However, the strong assumptions regarding the existence of densities and the lack of clarity regarding the underlying measure raise significant concerns. While the theoretical analysis and practical algorithm are commendable, the limitations outweigh the strengths in its current form.  I recommend **rejection** with the suggestion that the authors address the concerns regarding the density assumption and clarify the theoretical framework.  Specifically, the authors should explore alternative formulations that do not rely on the existence of densities or explicitly acknowledge and address the limitations imposed by this assumption.
