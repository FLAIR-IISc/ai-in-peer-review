Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by casting it as a linear inverse problem for an integral operator (a Fredholm first-kind equation). Two formulations are given: Type I uses Kp r = Kq 1 and requires samples from both p and q; Type II uses a localized kernel Kt,p r ≈ q with a δ-family kernel and requires samples from p and pointwise evaluation of q at p’s samples (q need not be a density). Both lead to Tikhonov-regularized solutions in RKHSs, yielding simple closed-form algorithms (FIRE) via the representer theorem. The paper provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth compact manifolds, and proposes an unsupervised model-selection scheme (CD-CV) based on importance-weighted moment matching. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (where applicable), and a thresholded ratio of KDEs (TIKDE), and include covariate shift applications.

The work connects to kernel mean matching (KMM) [Gretton et al.], direct ratio estimation (LSIF/KLIEP) [Kanamori et al.; Sugiyama et al.], and learning-as-operator inversion/regularization in RKHS [De Vito et al.; Smola & Schölkopf]. The “approximate identity” property of Gaussian smoothing underlies Type II and relates to heat-kernel/semigroup analyses in manifold learning [Belkin & Niyogi, Shi et al.]. Likelihood-ratio minimax theory [Nguyen et al.] provides a benchmark for the statistical difficulty of ratio estimation.

Quality
- Theory: The formulation as an inverse problem is sound, and the regularized RKHS estimators are well-motivated. Theorems present finite-sample error bounds and rates for Type I/II in Euclidean and manifold settings. However, several key analytic steps are deferred to the supplement; I did not verify them. In particular, statements like ∫ kt(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) should be justified precisely (assumptions on smoothness, boundary effects, and whether O(t) is in L2,p or uniform). It may help to cite standard “approximate identity”/heat kernel references (e.g., heat semigroup expansion f = e^{tΔ}f = f + tΔf + o(t) on manifolds), in addition to [Tsybakov, Ch. 1].
- Empirics: The experimental section is promising but leaves several questions. The fairness and breadth of comparisons (e.g., to KMM where out-of-sample extension is not required, to LSIF with strong tuning) could be strengthened; include variance/error bars. A simple 1D toy example plotting true and estimated q/p would greatly aid intuition.
- Practicalities: The paper usefully discusses when Type I vs Type II applies. It would be valuable to explain why a naive plug-in ratio q̂/p̂ (two KDEs) is inferior: compounded bias/variance, instability in low-density regions, and bandwidth mismatch; perhaps add a small controlled experiment highlighting this.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. Suggestions:
- Add a short, self-contained primer on Tikhonov regularization for inverse problems (why ill-posed, role of λ, link to RKHS penalty).
- Clarify the roles of k and kH (loss vs regularizer); when they coincide vs differ.
- Explain positivity/normalization: r must be nonnegative and, for density ratios, Ep[r]=1. Do you enforce these (as in KMM/LSIF), or post-hoc renormalize/clip? Discuss impact on stability.
- Explicitly state the assumptions under which Kt acts as an approximate identity on domains with/without boundary (and the corresponding O(t) vs O(t^{1−ε}) terms).
- Provide references for CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS datasets and preprocessing.
- Minor editorial: “TiknonovPhilips” → “Tikhonov–Phillips”.

Originality
Framing density-ratio estimation explicitly as solving a Fredholm equation with RKHS Tikhonov regularization is elegant and, to my knowledge, novel in this precise form. The connection clarifies relationships to KMM (as an H-norm formulation without explicit regularization) and delivers analyzable, closed-form algorithms. The CD-CV criterion is a nice, simple unsupervised model-selection device grounded in importance-weighted moment matching.

Significance
Density-ratio estimation is central to covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling. The proposed framework is flexible (Type I/II, multiple norms), simple to implement, and theoretically grounded; the manifold case is a plus. The provided rates are useful, but it would help to position them against known minimax results (e.g., [Nguyen et al. 2007]) and discuss whether Corollaries 2 and 4 are near-optimal under your smoothness assumptions or what gaps remain.

Specific requests and questions
- Theory: (i) Make the approximate-identity/heat-kernel step precise with clear assumptions and references; (ii) relate your rates to minimax-optimality for likelihood/density-ratio estimation; quantify gaps; (iii) discuss bias from the Kt,p r ≈ q approximation in Type II and how t, λ should scale jointly; (iv) clarify dependence on d in constants/exponents; (v) state whether r = q/p is assumed Sobolev-smooth and how this enters λ,t choices.
- Empirics: (i) Add a 1D toy example with ground truth r; (ii) whenever the downstream task only needs weights on training points, include KMM in comparisons; (iii) report time/memory and asymptotic complexity vs LSIF/KMM; (iv) ablate Type I vs Type II when q is known vs only sampled; (v) sensitivity of CD-CV to the choice/number of test functions U (linear vs half-spaces), with held-out U to avoid overfitting.
- Practical constraints: Discuss enforcing nonnegativity and normalization of r, and whether this improves stability in low-density regions (compare to TIKDE’s thresholding).
- Datasets: Cite sources and provide preprocessing details; include seeds and code for reproducibility.

Pros
- Clear, principled inverse-problem formulation; simple closed-form RKHS estimators (FIRE).
- Theoretical bounds in Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV).
- Addresses an important problem with broad applicability.

Cons
- Some crucial analytic steps are only sketched; relation to optimal rates is unclear.
- Empirical comparisons and diagnostics could be more thorough; missing simple illustrative toy plots.
- Treatment of positivity/normalization constraints is not discussed.
- Limited discussion of when FIRE materially outperforms plug-in KDE ratios in practice.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept, conditional on clarifying the theoretical approximations, strengthening the comparison to prior work (rates and experiments), and adding illustrative experiments. The paper is well written, addresses an important problem with a conceptually clean and potentially influential approach.

Reviewer confidence: moderate (the main paper was read carefully; proofs in the supplement not checked).
