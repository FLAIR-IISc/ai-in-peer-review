

# Review for "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors establish a connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, solving them using regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms, which they theoretically analyze and empirically validate. The work builds upon and extends recent NIPS contributions in covariate shift and density ratio estimation, particularly connecting to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), while providing a more principled theoretical foundation.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors derive concentration bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold cases under reasonable smoothness assumptions, providing a clear theoretical foundation for their approach. The experimental validation on multiple datasets and comparison with state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) is thorough and convincing. The paper carefully discusses both strengths and limitations of their approach, including the trade-offs between Type I and Type II settings. The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, with clear statements of assumptions and precise error bounds. The main weakness is the lack of discussion regarding the relationship between the smoothness of p and q and the smoothness of the ratio q/p, which would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The connection between the integral equation formulation and the resulting algorithms is clearly explained, with careful attention to the mathematical details. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why optimizing over the kernel functions {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in the Type I setting, as this is less intuitive than the Type II bandwidth-based approach. The discussion of the CD-CV model selection method is particularly clear and well-motivated.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant contribution by framing density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem, establishing a novel connection to operator theory. While related to prior work on kernel methods for density estimation (e.g., [9, 10]), the Fredholm equation perspective provides a unified theoretical framework that extends previous approaches. The CD-CV model selection method is particularly innovative for unsupervised settings. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work, particularly in clarifying how their approach differs from KMM (which lacks a regularizing term and proper out-of-sample extension).

**Significance:** Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with broad applications in transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift adaptation. The FIRE framework provides a principled approach that is both theoretically sound and practically effective, as demonstrated by the empirical results. The paper advances the state of the art by providing convergence guarantees that depend on ambient dimension in a principled way, and by introducing a novel unsupervised model selection technique. The method's computational efficiency and strong performance across multiple domains suggest significant potential for real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with precise convergence rates
- Practical and well-implemented algorithms (FIRE)
- Innovative CD-CV model selection method
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets and applications
- Clear relationship to previous work (KMM, LSIF)

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient discussion of the relationship between smoothness of p, q and smoothness of q/p
- Limited discussion of practical dimensionality limitations (for which the method is actually useful)
- Need for more motivation for the choice of cost function mixing L2,p and L2,q norms
- Clarification needed on when estimating q/p is preferable to using estimated Eq directly
- Need for better justification of the chosen family of test functions U

**Specific Questions for Authors:**
1. Could you provide an intuitive argument why optimization over {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in the Type I setting (equality (i)), similar to the bandwidth intuition provided for Type II (equality (ii))?
2. What is the motivation for mixing L2,p and L2,q norms in equation (9), and how does this relate to specific application scenarios?
3. When would estimating the density ratio q/p be preferable to directly using the estimated Eq in importance sampling applications?
4. Could you elaborate on the relationship between the smoothness of p, q and the smoothness of q/p, and under what conditions p is bounded away from zero?
5. For what ambient dimensions d would you expect this method to be practically useful, given the dimension-dependent convergence rates?
6. How does your approach relate to the "Smooth Operators" framework (Grunewalder et al.) and what are the key differences?

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical framework is novel and well-supported, the algorithms are well-motivated and implemented, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper meets all the criteria for high-quality NIPS research and would be a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
