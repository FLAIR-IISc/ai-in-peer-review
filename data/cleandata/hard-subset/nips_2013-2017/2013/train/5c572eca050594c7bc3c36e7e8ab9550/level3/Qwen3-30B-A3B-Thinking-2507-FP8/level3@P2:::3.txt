

# Review for "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors leverage regularization techniques from functional analysis to develop the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms. The key insight is to view the density ratio estimation problem as an inverse problem for integral operators, which allows them to apply classical techniques from operator theory and provide theoretical guarantees for their approach.

The paper makes a valuable connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, which is distinct from most prior work in this area. It relates to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10], but provides a more principled framework that unifies these approaches under a single theoretical umbrella. The authors also introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), an unsupervised model selection technique for density ratio estimation, which addresses a significant practical challenge in this domain.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality:** The Fredholm equation formulation represents a novel perspective on density ratio estimation, moving beyond the standard approaches that typically treat this as a direct approximation problem. The connection to classical operator theory is well-motivated and provides a fresh lens for analyzing this problem.

2. **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides detailed convergence rate analyses for both Type I and Type II settings on Euclidean spaces and manifolds. The theoretical results for Gaussian kernels are comprehensive and address an important gap in the literature regarding density ratio estimation on manifolds.

3. **Algorithmic Simplicity:** The FIRE algorithms are well-motivated, simple to implement, and have clear computational advantages. The CD-CV model selection procedure is particularly noteworthy as it provides a principled unsupervised approach to parameter selection.

4. **Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against relevant baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) on both regression and classification tasks, demonstrating the practical utility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation Concerns:** The paper makes strong assumptions about the existence of densities that may not hold in practical machine learning settings. Specifically, the authors assume the existence of a dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue or counting measure) but fail to specify this in the formulation, which is critical for the mathematical validity of their approach. This omission makes the theoretical results ambiguous, as the paper does not address how sets of ν-measure zero might affect their results.

2. **Modeling Assumptions:** The paper lacks clear specification of the domain of x (R^d or general topological space) and fails to state whether p(x) > 0 everywhere, which is a crucial assumption for density ratio estimation. Without these clarifications, the theoretical results may not apply to many practical scenarios.

3. **Practical Limitation:** The core method assumes that the density ratio q(x)/p(x) exists as a function, which is problematic in machine learning where arbitrarily close distributions may not share a common dominating measure (e.g., when one distribution is continuous and the other is discrete). This undermines the practical applicability of the approach, as the authors acknowledge in their key points.

4. **Clarity Issues:** While the paper is generally well-written, the theoretical development would benefit from more explicit statements about the assumptions required for the results to hold, particularly regarding the measure-theoretic foundations.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

*For acceptance:* The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework that unifies and extends existing approaches to density ratio estimation. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, and the practical CD-CV method addresses a significant challenge in the field. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.

*Against acceptance:* The fundamental theoretical assumptions (existence of densities, unspecified dominating measure) severely limit the practical applicability of the approach. The paper fails to adequately address the measure-theoretic challenges that arise in real-world machine learning applications, which are well-documented in the literature (e.g., [14, 26]).

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework with strong technical content, the foundational assumptions significantly undermine its practical relevance. The authors should clarify their measure-theoretic assumptions and demonstrate how their approach handles cases where the density ratio does not exist as a function. Without addressing these concerns, the theoretical results may not apply to many practical machine learning scenarios where density ratio estimation is needed.

I recommend rejection due to the fundamental theoretical limitations that prevent the method from being practically applicable in the machine learning context. The authors should consider revising the paper to either (a) clarify the measure-theoretic assumptions and demonstrate how their approach handles the cases where density ratios don't exist, or (b) frame their work in terms of the Radon-Nikodym derivative with appropriate measure-theoretic foundations.

The paper shows promise but requires significant theoretical refinement to be considered for publication at NIPS. The connection between inverse problems and density ratio estimation is valuable, but the current formulation's theoretical limitations prevent it from making a meaningful contribution to the field.
