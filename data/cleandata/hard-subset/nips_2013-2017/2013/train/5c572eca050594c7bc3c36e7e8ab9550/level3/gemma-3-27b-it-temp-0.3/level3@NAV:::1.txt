## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory. The core idea of framing the problem as an inverse problem is a significant contribution, offering a fresh perspective on a well-studied topic with connections to covariate shift and importance sampling. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and methodology, addressing an important and interesting problem within the machine learning community. 

The strength of the work lies in its theoretical grounding and the potential for analysis through the lens of operator theory. The introduction of the FIRE algorithm and the unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, are promising developments. However, several aspects require further clarification and elaboration. 

Firstly, the justification for the approximation ∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) (stated in the introduction) lacks sufficient detail. A proof sketch or a clear citation to a standard reference is needed. Secondly, the interesting special case of q=1, which suggests density estimation without the need for bandwidth selection approaching zero, deserves a more thorough discussion. How does the bandwidth role in FIRE differ from traditional Kernel Density Estimation (KDE), and what are the practical implications? A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would also benefit readers less familiar with the technique.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear explanation of why a naive plug-in approach (separately estimating p and q) is inferior to the proposed method. A direct comparison, even a simplified one, would strengthen the argument for the FIRE algorithm. While experimental results are presented, the empirical and theoretical comparison to prior methods, particularly [12] as mentioned by the authors, is unclear. A discussion of minimax-optimality and how the convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 relate to optimal rates would be valuable. 

Finally, providing references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) is crucial for reproducibility. Including toy experiments, such as 1D plots of true vs. estimated p/q, would visually illustrate the method’s performance and quality.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Potential for theoretical analysis and generalization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Missing theoretical details and justifications.
*   Unclear comparative performance to existing methods.
*   Lack of illustrative toy experiments.
*   Insufficient discussion of the advantages over naive approaches.



**Overall:** This is a promising paper with a novel approach. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
