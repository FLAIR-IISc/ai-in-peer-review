This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors present a flexible family of algorithms (FIRE) and provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels. The work is well-written, addresses an important problem relevant to areas like importance sampling and transfer learning, and demonstrates a clear connection to classical operator theory.

The core idea of reframing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is a significant contribution. This allows the application of established techniques from regularization and spectral analysis, offering a principled framework for algorithm development and theoretical guarantees. The resulting FIRE algorithms are presented as being simple and easy to implement. The introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is also a valuable addition.

However, while the paper presents a strong conceptual foundation, several areas could benefit from further clarification and elaboration. While the statement regarding the approximation of integrals using δ-kernels (e.g., ∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) dx ≈ f(y) + O(t)) is mentioned, a more explicit proof or a precise citation would strengthen the argument. The role of the kernel bandwidth *t* and its relationship to traditional kernel density estimation (KDE) bandwidth selection isn't fully discussed. A brief explanation of why, in the special case where q=1, this approach differs from standard KDE (and potentially avoids the bandwidth-to-zero limit) would be beneficial.

The paper would also benefit from a more detailed explanation of Tikhonov regularization, particularly for readers unfamiliar with this technique. A concise description of the motivation and underlying principles would enhance accessibility. Furthermore, a clear justification for *why* a direct plug-in approach (estimating *p* and *q* separately and then forming the ratio) is inferior to the proposed method would be valuable. Currently, the benefit of the integral equation formulation isn't entirely clear in terms of practical advantages over more straightforward methods.

The empirical evaluation, while comprehensive in terms of datasets and comparison algorithms, lacks a level of detail that would allow for a definitive assessment of the method's performance. While comparisons to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE are provided, a more rigorous analysis – including statistical significance testing – is warranted. The theoretical results (Corollaries 2 and 4) present convergence rates. It would be valuable to discuss these rates in the context of minimax optimality for density ratio estimation, and to highlight how close the achieved rates are to the theoretical lower bounds.

Minor points include the lack of references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS). Providing these would increase reproducibility and allow readers to easily access the data. Finally, including illustrative toy experiments – for example, 1D plots showing the true and estimated density ratios *p(x)/q(x)* for simple distributions – would provide a more intuitive understanding of the method's behavior and the quality of the estimates.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Principled framework based on regularization theory and RKHS.
*   Theoretically sound with concentration bounds and convergence rates.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Missing justifications for key statements (e.g., integral approximation).
*   Lack of detailed comparison to existing methods (statistical significance).
*   Theoretical results lack discussion of minimax optimality.
*   Insufficient detail regarding the role of the kernel bandwidth.
*   Limited illustrative examples to demonstrate performance.
*   Proofs are relegated to the supplemental material and not verified by this reviewer.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
*   The proposed method has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in density ratio estimation.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented, demonstrating a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient detail and statistical rigor.
*   The connection to existing methods could be further clarified.
*   Some theoretical details require further elaboration and justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would substantially strengthen the contribution and increase confidence in the results. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised regarding theoretical justification, empirical evaluation, and clarity of presentation. A response to the reviewer highlighting how the changes were made would also be valuable.
