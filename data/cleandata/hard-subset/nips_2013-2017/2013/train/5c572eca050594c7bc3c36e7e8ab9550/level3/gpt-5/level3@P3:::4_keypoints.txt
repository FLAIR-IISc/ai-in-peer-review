- Proposes FIRE, kernel-based estimators of the density ratio f = q/p framed as Fredholm integral equations of the first kind.

- Derives two formulations: (i) Kp f = Kq 1 using RKHS representers, and (ii) Kt,p f ≈ q using δ-like kernels with bandwidth t.

- Both formulations use RKHS Tikhonov regularization, admit closed-form representer solutions, and are estimated from samples of p (and optionally q).

- Provides finite-sample concentration and convergence rates under boundedness and Sobolev smoothness for Euclidean domains and manifolds.

- Introduces CD-CV, an unsupervised model-selection scheme that cross-validates by testing importance-sampling identities on random test functions U.

- Connects to prior work: situates KMM as a special case of (i), contrasts FIRE with LSIF/KLIEP (no finite basis) and relates to operator-theoretic views and conditional mean embeddings.

- Technically sound derivations, closed-form algorithms, and meaningful finite-sample bounds; manifold analysis is a valuable addition.

- Empirical study shows practical viability and stability; CD-CV is elegant and useful in practice.

- Overall clarity is good; operator notation, assumptions, and algorithms are reproducible, though some choices need more motivation.

- Novel framing: casting density-ratio estimation as an inverse problem in RKHS is original and fruitful.

- Significant contribution due to importance of density-ratio estimation and the operator-theoretic formulation with convergence guarantees.

- Strength: clean operator formulation yielding two estimators and closed-form kernel algorithms.

- Strength: theoretical guarantees that explicitly show dependence on bandwidth, regularization, and dimension.

- Strength: practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) leveraging test-function identities.

- Strength: clear relation to KMM and LSIF, and natural out-of-sample extension.

- Weakness: insufficient intuition for why optimizing over {k(x,·)} in (i) suffices compared to bandwidth intuition in (ii).

- Weakness: mixed loss in Eq. (9) (mixing L2,p and L2,q) lacks clear, application-driven rationale.

- Weakness: unclear when estimating q/p is preferable to directly estimating Eq[u] (importance-sampling motivation needs clarification).

- Weakness: more discussion needed on how smoothness of p and q implies smoothness of q/p and role of p bounded away from zero.

- Weakness: dimension dependence of rates is concerning; needs practical guidance on regimes where FIRE is effective.

- Weakness: choice and sensitivity of validation test functions U for CD-CV require justification and ablation.

- Weakness: relation to “Smooth Operators” and conditional mean embeddings should be deepened to position FIRE among operator-inversion methods.

- Requests: provide RKHS intuition for (i) via reproducing property, injectivity, and regularization-stability arguments.

- Requests: motivate mixed loss (γ-weighting) with scenarios and guidance for setting γ (e.g., CD-CV).

- Requests: list explicit scenarios where estimating q/p is preferable (multiple u, covariate shift, MCMC/unnormalized densities, out-of-sample weighting).

- Requests: state smoothness/positivity assumptions (e.g., p ≥ c > 0), and how they enter source conditions and rates.

- Requests: give practitioner guidance on dimensionality limits (e.g., useful d ranges, need for manifold structure) and sensitivity plots.

- Requests: justify CD-CV test-function choices, consider alternatives (random Fourier features, localized kernels), and relate to MMD.

- Requests: discuss when operator inversion with RKHS Tikhonov is preferable to finite-basis LSIF/KLIEP and consider other spectral regularizers.

- Practicalities requested: address nonnegativity/normalization enforcement, computational complexity (O(n^3) and Nyström/iterative solvers), and kernel choices for K vs kH.

- Pros summary: elegant inverse-problem framing, solid theory including manifold case, clear algorithms, useful model selection, strong empirical results.

- Cons summary: limited practical guidance on choosing (i) vs (ii), dimensionality and smoothness caveats, and motivation for mixed losses and validation families.

- Overall assessment: strong, well-executed, timely paper likely to influence density-ratio estimation and covariate-shift methods; recommendation to accept.
