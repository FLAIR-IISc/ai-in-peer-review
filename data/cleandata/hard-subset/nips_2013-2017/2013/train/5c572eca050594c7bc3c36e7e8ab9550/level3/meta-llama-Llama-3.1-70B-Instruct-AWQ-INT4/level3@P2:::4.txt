This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, specifically the Radon-Nikodym derivative q/p, where p is given by a sample and q(x) is either a known function or another probability density function given by a sample. The authors reformulate the density ratio estimation as an integral equation, known as the Fredholm equation of the first kind, and solve it using the tools of regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the context of covariate shift assumption and the problem of estimating the density ratio from sampled data. The authors mention several related papers, including [6, 10, 9, 22, 2], and highlight the connections to the classical operator theory setting, which makes it easier to apply the standard tools of spectral and Fourier analysis to obtain theoretical results.

The strengths of the paper include its well-executed and timely contribution to the field, with both theoretical and experimental results. The authors provide a principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically, using the techniques of regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces. The resulting family of algorithms (FIRE) is flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

One of the weaknesses of the paper is the lack of an intuitive argument for why optimizing over the set of kernel functions ensures convergence in equality (i), similar to (ii) where bandwidth intuition helps. The authors should provide more insight into this aspect.

Another concern is the choice of cost function. The authors initially optimize over L2,p, but later mix L2,p and L2,q in equation (9). They should provide more motivation and links to target applications to justify this choice.

The paper raises an application/motivation concern for importance sampling. Since Ep and Eq are estimated to form the empirical cost, it is not entirely clear why not use the estimated Eq directly. The authors should clarify when estimating q/p helps versus using Eq.

The regularizer and smoothness assumptions are also worth discussing. The authors should provide more insight into the relationships between the smoothness of p and q and the smoothness of the ratio q/p, as well as conditions like p being bounded away from zero.

The dependence on dimension d is also an important aspect to discuss. The authors should provide more information on for what dimensions the method is practically useful.

The choice of the family of test functions U is also worth justifying. The authors should provide more motivation and justification for the chosen family of test functions.

Finally, the authors should discuss whether using the set of kernel functions is the "right" general approach for covariate-shift/q/p estimation, and how this relates to prior work like "Smooth Operators" (Grunewalder et al.).

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their ideas. However, addressing the above concerns and providing more insight into the theoretical and practical aspects of the method would strengthen the paper.
