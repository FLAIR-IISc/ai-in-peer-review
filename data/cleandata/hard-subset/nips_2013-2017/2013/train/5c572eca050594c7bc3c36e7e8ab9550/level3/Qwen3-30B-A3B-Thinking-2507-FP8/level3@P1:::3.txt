

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it using regularization techniques within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors introduce the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) framework, which provides a principled way to estimate q(x)/p(x) where p and q are densities. This approach is related to previous work on covariate shift and importance sampling, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), but offers a different theoretical foundation based on classical integral equation theory.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a reformulation of density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem, (2) a flexible framework for constructing algorithms using regularization, (3) theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and (4) a novel unsupervised model selection method called CD-CV.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. The theoretical analysis is thorough for the Gaussian kernel case, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings. The authors carefully distinguish between different formulations and their applications, and the CD-CV model selection method is a practical contribution addressing a key challenge in unsupervised learning. The experimental results on classification and regression tasks show competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods like KMM and LSIF.

However, the paper has significant theoretical concerns that undermine its quality and validity. The core theoretical framework assumes the existence of densities q(x) and p(x) with respect to a dominating measure ν, which is never specified (Lebesgue, counting, etc.). This omission is critical because the existence of densities depends on the choice of ν, and the validity of the entire approach hinges on this specification. In machine learning, we often deal with measures that are not absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure (e.g., discrete distributions), making the density ratio estimation problematic.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear statement of modeling assumptions about the domain of x (R^d or more general space) and makes no assumption about the positivity of p(x) (is p > 0 everywhere?). This is particularly concerning because density ratio estimation typically requires p(x) > 0 whenever q(x) > 0, but the paper does not specify this critical condition. Without these assumptions, the theoretical results may be invalid or ambiguous due to sets of ν-measure zero, which is a fundamental issue in measure theory.

The paper also fails to acknowledge that in many machine learning applications, the underlying distributions may not have densities at all (e.g., discrete distributions or mixtures of discrete and continuous components). The authors claim that their approach "addresses the problem of estimating the ratio q/p where p is a density function and q is another density," but they do not discuss the limitations of this assumption in practical machine learning settings where such densities may not exist.

Regarding clarity, the paper is well-written and logically organized, but the theoretical framework would benefit from greater precision in stating assumptions. The connection to previous work is well-documented, but the paper could better clarify how it differs from KMM and LSIF in terms of both theoretical foundation and practical implementation.

The originality is moderate, as the paper builds on existing work on density ratio estimation but provides a different theoretical perspective based on Fredholm equations. However, the theoretical novelty is somewhat limited by the reliance on standard regularization techniques in RKHS, which have been widely used in machine learning.

The significance of the paper is somewhat constrained by the theoretical limitations mentioned above. While the proposed CD-CV method is practical and useful, the theoretical framework's dependence on strong density assumptions limits the applicability of the results to real-world machine learning problems where such densities may not exist.

The paper would benefit from addressing the following issues: (1) clearly specifying the dominating measure ν and discussing its implications, (2) stating all necessary modeling assumptions about the domain of x and the positivity of p(x), (3) discussing the limitations of the density assumption in practical machine learning contexts, and (4) clarifying how the results extend to cases where densities do not exist.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting theoretical perspective on density ratio estimation with a well-executed experimental evaluation. However, the lack of precise theoretical foundations and critical assumptions undermines the quality of the work. The theoretical results are not sufficiently general for machine learning applications, where the existence of densities is often not guaranteed. While the practical contributions (particularly CD-CV) are valuable, the theoretical framework's limitations prevent this paper from making a strong contribution to the field as it stands. I recommend rejection due to the fundamental theoretical shortcomings that invalidate the main claims.
