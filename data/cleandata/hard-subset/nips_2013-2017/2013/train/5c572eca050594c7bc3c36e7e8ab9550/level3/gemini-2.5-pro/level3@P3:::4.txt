### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio f = q/p, a crucial task in importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and other areas of machine learning. The authors propose a novel and principled framework by reformulating this estimation problem as a classical Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two main formulations are derived. The first (Type I) is based on the equality E_p[k(x,·) f] = E_q[k(x,·)], which is suitable when samples from both distributions p and q are available. The second (Type II) uses the approximation E_p[k_t(x,·) f] ≈ q(x) for a local kernel k_t, which is useful when q is a known function.

To solve these ill-posed inverse problems, the authors apply Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach leads to a family of simple and efficient algorithms, named Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimators (FIRE), which have closed-form solutions involving standard kernel matrix operations.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing concentration bounds and convergence rates for the estimators using Gaussian kernels. The analysis covers distributions on R^d as well as on d-dimensional sub-manifolds. A key practical contribution is a fully unsupervised model selection procedure called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to select hyperparameters. The paper's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on several datasets, showing strong performance compared to related methods like LSIF and a baseline kernel density-based approach. The work positions itself relative to prior methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, arguing that its inverse problem formulation provides a more stable and theoretically transparent foundation.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a highly original and insightful contribution. It connects a core machine learning problem to a well-studied area of functional analysis, providing a clear and principled foundation for the proposed algorithms. This perspective helps unify and clarify the relationships between different approaches.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is technically strong, providing detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates (Theorems 1 & 3). The analysis under smoothness and boundedness assumptions is rigorous and covers both Euclidean and manifold settings, which is a significant strength.

3.  **Practicality and Simplicity:** Despite the sophisticated theoretical underpinnings, the resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, easy to implement, and have non-iterative, closed-form solutions. This makes the method highly accessible to practitioners.

4.  **Novel Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for hyperparameter selection is a clever and important practical contribution. Unsupervised model selection is a notoriously difficult problem, and the proposed solution is intuitive and shown to be effective empirically.

5.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a solid experimental section that not only validates the performance of FIRE against competitors but also thoughtfully evaluates the proposed CD-CV model selection procedure itself.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors

The paper is well-executed, and the following points are primarily suggestions for clarification and further strengthening the work.

1.  **Intuition for the Type I Formulation:** The intuition for the Type II estimator is clear: the operator K_{t,p} acts as a smoother that approximates the identity (times p) as the bandwidth t shrinks. However, the intuition for the Type I formulation (based on E_p[k(x,·) f] = E_q[k(x,·)]) is less immediate. Could the authors provide a more intuitive argument for why minimizing `||K_p f - K_q 1||` forces f to converge to the true ratio q/p? Is this related to the operator K_p being injective or having a dense range under certain conditions on p and the kernel k?

2.  **Motivation for Mixed Norm:** In Equation (9), a cost function mixing L2,p and L2,q norms is introduced. The motivation provided is that it allows using both samples in the loss. Could the authors elaborate on the practical or theoretical benefits of this? Are there specific applications or types of distribution shift where minimizing error with respect to a combination of p and q is demonstrably better than focusing on the p-weighted error alone?

3.  **Fundamental Motivation for Importance Sampling:** The paper frames the problem within importance sampling. However, in the Type I setting where samples from q are available, one can estimate E_q[h(x)] directly via a simple Monte Carlo average over the q-samples. Could the authors clarify the scenarios where estimating the ratio f=q/p provides a distinct advantage? For example, does it lead to variance reduction, or is the main benefit for covariate shift where the goal is to learn a re-weighted model rather than estimate a single expectation?

4.  **Smoothness of the Ratio:** The theoretical analysis assumes the true ratio q/p is in a Sobolev space. This is a crucial assumption that depends on the properties of p and q themselves. It would be beneficial to include a discussion on the conditions under which this assumption holds. Specifically, how do the smoothness of p and q relate to the smoothness of their ratio, and what are the implications of the necessary condition that p must be bounded away from zero on the support of q?

5.  **Dependence on Dimension:** The derived convergence rates (Corollaries 2 & 4) show a strong, polynomial dependence on the ambient dimension `d`. This suggests the method may suffer from the curse of dimensionality. Could the authors provide some discussion on the practical range of dimensions `d` for which the method is expected to be useful?

6.  **Choice of Test Functions for CD-CV:** The CD-CV procedure uses random linear functions and half-spaces as the test family U. What is the justification for this specific choice? How sensitive is the performance of the cross-validation to the choice of U, and would other families (e.g., based on kernels) be more robust or powerful?

7.  **Relation to Other Operator-Based Methods:** The paper does a good job of relating the work to KMM and LSIF. It would be interesting to also position this framework with respect to other operator-based approaches for learning on distributions, such as "Smooth Operators" by Grünewalder et al. (ICML 2012), which also uses integral operators for learning but focuses on a different goal.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions on both theoretical and practical fronts.

*   **Quality:** The work is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The core ideas are communicated effectively.
*   **Originality:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is novel and provides a powerful new perspective on a classic problem.
*   **Significance:** The problem of density ratio estimation is of high importance in modern machine learning. The proposed FIRE method is principled, practical, and effective, making it highly likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners. The CD-CV procedure is also a valuable contribution.

Overall, this is a timely and well-executed paper that represents a strong contribution to the field. I recommend it for acceptance.
