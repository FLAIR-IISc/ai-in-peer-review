## Review of “Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach”

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors derive estimators for the ratio q/p, providing theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrating strong empirical performance on several datasets, including applications to covariate shift. This work builds upon a substantial body of literature in transfer learning and density estimation, notably Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10], but distinguishes itself through a more general operator-theoretic framework.

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The derivation of the estimators is clear, and the theoretical analysis – offering convergence rates depending on the dimension – is a significant contribution. The inclusion of both theoretical results and experimental validation strengthens the work considerably. The CD-CV method for parameter selection appears particularly promising, offering an unsupervised approach to model selection, which is a common bottleneck in these types of problems. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, making the complex mathematical concepts accessible.

However, a more intuitive explanation of why optimizing over the kernel functions {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in the first integral equality (Eq. 2) would be beneficial.  The discussion of bandwidth selection (similar to the intuition behind convergence in Eq. 3) clarifies the second equality well, but a similar insight into the first would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the choice of combining L2,p and L2,q norms in the cost function (Eq. 9) could be motivated more thoroughly.  What application scenarios specifically benefit from this mixed norm, and how does it relate to the target problem's properties?  

Regarding the application to importance sampling, a clearer justification of when estimating q/p is preferable to directly using the estimated q would be valuable.  A discussion regarding the relationship between the smoothness of p and q and the resulting smoothness of their ratio, and any conditions required (like p being bounded away from zero), would also enrich the theoretical analysis. Finally, while the theoretical analysis considers the dimension *d*, a more practical discussion of its impact—for what values of *d* is the method computationally feasible and accurate—would be helpful.  The justification for the chosen family of test functions *U* also feels somewhat lacking.  Is this family particularly well-suited for the problem, or are other choices possible? Lastly, exploring the relationship to prior work on "Smooth Operators" [7] might provide further context and nuance regarding the general approach. 

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The strengths (theoretical rigor, experimental validation, novel framework) clearly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel approach, strong theoretical results, good experimental validation, clear writing.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Minor points regarding justification of design choices and potential connections to related work.
