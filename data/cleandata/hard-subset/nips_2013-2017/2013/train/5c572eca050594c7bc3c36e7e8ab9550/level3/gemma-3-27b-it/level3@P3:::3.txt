This paper addresses the problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques. The approach, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), aims to provide a principled framework with theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms for importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation.

The work builds upon a substantial body of prior research in density ratio estimation, including Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10], and methods addressing covariate shift [19, 27]. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, highlighting the connection to classical operator theory and providing a regularization-based perspective that differs from KMM’s optimization approach. The emphasis on theoretical analysis, providing convergence rates and concentration bounds, is a valuable contribution, differentiating it from many purely empirical approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** Reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem offers a fresh perspective and connects the problem to a well-established area of mathematics.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides theoretical bounds for the Gaussian kernel in Euclidean and manifold settings, which is a significant achievement. These bounds, though potentially loose, offer insight into the algorithm’s behavior.
* **Flexibility:** The FIRE algorithm is flexible, allowing for different norms and loss functions. The inclusion of Type I and Type II formulations expands its applicability.
* **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method offers a practical solution to parameter selection, a crucial aspect often overlooked in density ratio estimation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The mathematical derivations are generally well-presented and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density Existence Assumption:** A major and fundamental flaw is the implicit assumption that densities *exist*. The paper acknowledges this is strong, but doesn't adequately address its implications. Arbitrarily close measures may lack densities, rendering the entire framework invalid in those cases. This significantly limits the scope and applicability of the method.
* **Dominating Measure Specification:** The paper lacks specification of the dominating measure ν used in the integral equations. Whether it’s Lebesgue measure, counting measure, or something else fundamentally alters the validity of the results. This omission is critical.
* **Sets of Zero Measure:** The theoretical results may be invalidated or become ambiguous due to the potential presence of sets with zero measure. The analysis doesn't explicitly address how these sets are handled.
* **Positivity Assumption:**  The paper doesn’t state explicitly whether the density *p* is assumed to be strictly positive everywhere. If *p* is zero in certain regions, the ratio *q/p* becomes undefined, and the analysis breaks down.
* **Modeling Assumptions:** The domain of *x* is not clearly specified. While examples use R^d, the theoretical results should explicitly state whether they hold for general topological spaces or are restricted to Euclidean domains. This lack of generality limits the wider applicability of the work.
* **Experimental Scope:** While experiments demonstrate promising results, the comparison is limited, and the impact of the choice of test functions in CD-CV is not fully investigated.
* **Practicality:** The theoretical complexity, especially regarding the inversion of kernel matrices, may pose practical challenges for high-dimensional data.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The novel formulation and associated theoretical analysis offer a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation.
* The algorithm is relatively simple to implement and offers flexibility in terms of kernel choice and regularization parameters.
* The CD-CV method provides a practical solution for parameter selection.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The fundamental assumption of density existence is a significant limitation.
* The lack of specification of the dominating measure raises serious concerns about the validity of the results.
* The insufficient discussion of sets of zero measure and the positivity assumption of *p* cast doubt on the robustness of the theoretical guarantees.
* The limited experimental evaluation does not fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and mathematically sophisticated approach to density ratio estimation. However, the critical assumptions regarding density existence, the unspecified dominating measure, and potential issues with sets of zero measure undermine the theoretical foundations of the work. While the algorithm itself is well-motivated and the experimental results are encouraging, these fundamental flaws prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. 

**Recommendation:**  Reject with encouragement to address the fundamental theoretical limitations. Specifically, the authors need to: (1) explicitly address the issue of density existence and consider approaches applicable to measures that may not have densities; (2) clearly specify the dominating measure used in their analysis; and (3) discuss how sets of zero measure are handled in their theoretical derivations.  A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, including a wider range of datasets and a detailed analysis of the CD-CV method, would also strengthen the paper.
