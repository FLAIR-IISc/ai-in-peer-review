This paper proposes a novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This reformulation is the core contribution, leading to a family of algorithms called Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimators (FIRE) that are solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The work connects to an extensive body of literature on importance estimation and covariate shift, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, but distinguishes itself by casting the problem as a classical inverse problem, thereby leveraging the tools of operator theory.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning. The central idea of treating density ratio estimation as an inverse problem via an integral equation is elegant, novel, and provides a new theoretical lens through which to view the problem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the derivation of the FIRE algorithms from the integral equation formulation is clearly presented.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Quality:** The theoretical claims require more support within the main text. For instance, the crucial approximation `∫ k_t(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)` should be justified with a proof sketch or a precise reference. Furthermore, while convergence rates are provided (Corollaries 2 and 4), their significance is unclear without a discussion of how they compare to minimax-optimal rates for this problem. A discussion explaining why the proposed method is superior to a naive plug-in approach (e.g., dividing two separate kernel density estimates) would also strengthen the paper's claims. Note that all proofs are in the supplementary material and have not been checked for this review.
*   **Clarity:** To improve accessibility, the authors should add a brief, intuitive explanation of Tikhonov regularization for readers not familiar with inverse problems. The empirical evaluation would also benefit greatly from simple toy experiments, such as 1D plots comparing the true density ratio against the estimated one, which would provide valuable intuition.
*   **Evaluation:** The empirical comparison to prior work is not sufficiently thorough. For example, a direct comparison to related density estimation methods like [12] is missing. The paper should also discuss the interesting special case where q=1, which corresponds to density estimation. How does the role of the bandwidth `t` here differ from traditional KDE, where the bandwidth must tend to zero? Additionally, references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) are needed for reproducibility.

**Overall:** This is a promising paper with a strong, original contribution. However, its claims of superiority are not yet fully substantiated. The manuscript would be significantly improved by strengthening the theoretical justifications, providing clearer context for its convergence rates, and conducting a more comprehensive and reproducible experimental evaluation.
