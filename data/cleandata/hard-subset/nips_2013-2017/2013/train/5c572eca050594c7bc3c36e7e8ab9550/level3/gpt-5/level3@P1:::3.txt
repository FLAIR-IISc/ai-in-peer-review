This paper tackles density ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving the resulting inverse problem with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two variants are developed: (I) “operator matching” Kp r ≈ Kq 1 when samples from both p and q are available, and (II) “deconvolution” Kt,p r ≈ q when q is known pointwise on samples from p via a localized kernel Kt. The representer theorem yields simple closed-form kernel solutions (FIRE), and the authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels both on Rd and on smooth manifolds. They further propose an unsupervised model selection scheme, CD-CV, which selects kernel width and regularization by enforcing importance sampling identities on held-out test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (discussion), and a thresholded KDE baseline under covariate shift.

Relation to prior work: The formulation unifies and clarifies connections to KMM (Gretton et al.), LSIF/KLIEP (Kanamori/Sugiyama), and broader RKHS inverse problem views (e.g., De Vito et al.). Unlike KMM, FIRE has an explicit out-of-sample extension and explicit regularization, and unlike LSIF’s finite basis regression view, FIRE directly solves operator equations with spectral analysis. The CD-CV idea is related to importance-weighted CV but does not require labels.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting density ratio estimation as an inverse problem on integral operators is elegant and connects to classical operator theory and regularization.
- Algorithmic simplicity: Closed-form kernel solutions with standard Gram matrices; straightforward to implement for both Type I and II settings.
- Theory: Nonasymptotic bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings are valuable; the use of concentration plus spectral techniques is appropriate.
- Practical model selection: CD-CV is a simple, unsupervised criterion exploiting the importance sampling identity; it is attractive when labels are scarce.
- Empirical results: Comparisons show competitive performance and improved stability relative to baselines; discussion of KMM’s lack of out-of-sample extension is fair.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dominating measure and “density” assumptions: The paper assumes the existence of densities p and q but does not specify an underlying σ-finite dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue on Ω ⊂ Rd, Riemannian volume on a manifold, or mixed discrete–continuous cases). In ML, distributions frequently lack densities w.r.t. a common ν or are mixtures, making “q/p” ill-defined outside the support of p. The correct object is the Radon–Nikodym derivative dq/dp, which requires q ≪ p. The paper should state this explicitly and restrict statements to L2(p)-equivalence classes.
- Positivity and support: Many expressions implicitly require p(x) > 0 (at least on the domain of interest). Without a lower bound on p or an assumption q ≪ p, r(x) can be undefined/infinite on sets with positive q-measure, and loss terms like ‖Kpf − Kq1‖L2(p) do not penalize errors where p = 0. The theory should clarify that estimation is on supp(p), and experiments should discuss support mismatch.
- Measure-zero and operator domains: Results involving equalities of functions (e.g., Kp r = Kq 1) should be stated as holding p-a.e. (or in H′ if working in RKHS), to avoid ambiguity due to ν-null sets. Similarly, when using Gaussian kernels on manifolds, the integral and normalization should be with respect to the Riemannian measure on the manifold, not ambient Lebesgue; this is hinted at but not formalized.
- Modeling assumptions are scattered: While Section 3 provides assumptions for Rd and compact manifolds, the domain Ω, the measure, and smoothness/regularity (e.g., p, q/p ∈ W2
2) should be clearly consolidated early. The kernel’s normalization and the meaning of “δ-family” should be tied to the chosen ν. For Type II, clarify when q must belong to L2(p) vs RKHS and how bias O(t) depends on regularity.
- Practical scope: Many real ML settings violate q ≪ p (e.g., covariate shift with missing support). The paper should discuss failure modes and whether FIRE can be adapted (e.g., via support detection, clipped ratios, or enforcing r ∈ [0, rmax]).
- Complexity and scalability: The solutions require inverting O(n)×O(n) kernel matrices, but computational/memory costs and approximations (Nyström, random features, conjugate gradients with preconditioning) are not discussed.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The inverse problem formulation, algorithmic derivations, and bounds appear technically sound under the stated (but incompletely centralized) assumptions. The main technical claims are supported with appropriate structure, though measure-theoretic precision should be tightened.
- Clarity: Exposition is generally good, with intuitive operator equalities and explicit formulas. However, clarity would improve by: (1) early, precise statement of domain, dominating measure, support/positivity, and absolute continuity; (2) explicit “p-a.e.” qualifiers; (3) a brief discussion of manifold kernels vs ambient Gaussians.
- Originality: Reframing density ratio estimation directly as a Fredholm inverse problem with RKHS regularization is a fresh and unifying perspective, distinct from but related to KMM/LSIF. The CD-CV criterion is a neat, practical twist.
- Significance: Density ratio estimation is central to covariate shift, OOD weighting, and IS. The theoretical rates and the unsupervised model selection are likely to be useful. Impact depends on addressing the measure-theoretic scope and practical support mismatch.

Suggestions for improvement
- State up front: Fix a σ-finite measure ν on Ω; define p = dp/dν, q = dq/dν when they exist; otherwise define r as dq/dp and assume q ≪ p (or explicitly restrict objectives to L2(p) and interpret all equalities p-a.e.). Clarify where r is defined and how errors are measured outside supp(p).
- Positivity: Either assume p(x) ≥ c > 0 on Ω or restrict attention to supp(p). Discuss regularization/thresholding strategies for small p and their effect on bounds.
- Manifold case: Specify the Riemannian measure, the intrinsic dimension d, and whether kernels approximate the heat kernel on the manifold. Align normalization constants accordingly.
- Losses and CD-CV: Discuss sensitivity of CD-CV to the choice of test function family U and provide guidance (e.g., use MMD features or a broad randomized family). Note moment conditions required for stability.
- Scalability: Add a paragraph on computational cost and practical approximations (Nyström, random features), and how they interact with theory.
- Experiments: Include a controlled case where q is not absolutely continuous w.r.t. p to illustrate limitations or propose a clipped/robust variant.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled framing of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem with accompanying rates.
- Simple, practical algorithms with out-of-sample extension.
- Useful unsupervised model selection mechanism.
- Solid connections to and clarification of prior methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently explicit measure-theoretic foundations (dominating measure, absolute continuity, p-a.e. statements), potentially undermining the generality of claims.
- Limited discussion of support mismatch and small-p pathologies common in practice.
- Missing computational scalability discussion.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and promising contribution. With clearer and more rigorous modeling assumptions and a brief discussion of practical limitations and scalability, it would be a strong paper.
