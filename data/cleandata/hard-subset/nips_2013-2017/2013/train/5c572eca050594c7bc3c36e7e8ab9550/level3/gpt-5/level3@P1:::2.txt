This paper studies density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) through an inverse-problem lens. The key observation is that the importance-sampling identity induces a Fredholm equation of the first kind: Kp r = Kq 1 (Type I). A second route uses a local kernel kt to obtain Kt,p r ≈ q (Type II). Both are solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) by the representer theorem. The authors present several loss choices (L2,p, L2,q, a mixture, and an RKHS loss), discuss out-of-sample prediction, and give concentration/error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels when p, q are supported either on Rd or on smooth compact manifolds. A practical contribution is an unsupervised model-selection criterion, CD-CV, that uses the importance-sampling identity evaluated on random test functions to choose kernel width and regularization. Experiments on USPS, CPUsmall, and Kin8nm compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (where applicable), and a TIKDE baseline, showing improved stability and competitive or better accuracy.

Relation to prior work. The formulation connects density-ratio estimation to classical operator inversion and regularization (De Vito et al.; Engl et al.), and to kernel methods for distribution shift (Shimodaira; Gretton et al. KMM; Kanamori et al. LSIF; Sugiyama et al. KLIEP). Type I with an RKHS loss directly recovers a regularized variant of KMM, but FIRE differs in regularizing the ratio function itself in H, providing a natural out-of-sample estimator and improved stability. Relative to LSIF/KLIEP, FIRE does not require choosing a finite basis; instead it solves the Fredholm equation with RKHS regularization. The CD-CV criterion addresses a well-known pain point: model selection for unsupervised ratio estimation. The authors correctly argue that RKHS objective values (as in KMM) depend on the kernel and are not comparable across kernel choices, making L2-based cross-density validation attractive.

Quality. The inverse-problem formulation is principled and the algorithms are clean. The theoretical section provides finite-sample bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels, covering both Euclidean domains and manifolds. The experimental methodology around CD-CV is thoughtful and shows practical utility.

That said, the rates appear relatively slow. For Type I, the stated corollary yields O(n−1/(3.5+d/2)) in L2,p, which is weak in moderate dimensions. The bounds are driven by the interplay of the kernel bias (t), regularization (λ), and sampling terms; Type II also incurs an O(t) approximation bias from Kt. It would strengthen the paper to (i) clarify whether stronger source/eigen-decay assumptions on the integral operator (e.g., effective-dimension and source conditions in the style of Caponnetto & De Vito) or smoothness assumptions on r = q/p could yield faster, polynomial rates without sending t → 0, and (ii) discuss whether the current rates are minimax-optimal under the assumed smoothness (cf. Nguyen, Wainwright, Jordan on likelihood-ratio estimation). In addition, several assumptions may be strong in practice: bounded densities, Sobolev regularity for r and r/p, and the requirement m > λ1/3 n in the corollary. A brief discussion of when these hold (or fail) in covariate-shift applications would help.

Clarity. The main paper is generally clear and well organized, and the operator notation is helpful once introduced. A few suggestions:
- Make the distinctions between Type I and Type II more explicit up front (data assumptions, available norms, approximation bias in Type II).
- State computational complexity and any use of low-rank approximations (Nyström, CG) for large n.
- For CD-CV, specify the number of test functions, variance across random draws, and sensitivity to the choice of function family.

The supplementary material currently contains numerous typos that hinder verification. Specific corrections:
- Lemma 5: W2^2 should be Ws^2, and the bound should read D1 (t/−log λ)^2 + λ1−α D2 ||f||2^2.
- Lines 675 and 686 are inconsistent; Eq. (31) appears to be the correct form.
- At line 1147 the integral domain should read ||ξ||^2 ≥ … (lower bound is missing in the current text).

Originality. Casting density-ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem and solving it with standard RKHS regularization is a neat conceptual unification that leads to practical, closed-form estimators. The Type II variant based on local kernels is also interesting. The CD-CV criterion is, in my view, the most practically impactful novelty: it enables reliable, unsupervised parameter selection using a kernel-independent objective derived from the importance identity.

Significance. Density-ratio estimation is central to covariate shift, importance sampling, and off-policy evaluation. A stable, out-of-sample estimator with a usable, unsupervised model-selection procedure is valuable. The theory extends existing analyses to manifold domains and clarifies the bias-variance trade-offs in both Type I and II. The empirical evidence is encouraging, though broader comparisons (e.g., to KLIEP, uLSIF variants) and higher-dimensional settings would further support impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean inverse-problem formulation leading to simple, closed-form kernel algorithms.
- Unsupervised, kernel-independent CD-CV model selection with clear motivation and demonstrated utility.
- Theoretical bounds for Euclidean and manifold domains; out-of-sample estimators with regularization.
- Empirical results showing stable performance and competitive accuracy.

Cons for acceptance:
- Convergence rates are relatively slow; limited discussion of whether stronger assumptions could improve them.
- Strong smoothness/boundedness assumptions on r = q/p; practical implications not fully discussed.
- Supplementary typos/errors impede verification.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader, and scalability details are sparse.

Overall, this is a novel and useful contribution. The main strength is a principled estimator coupled with an effective, unsupervised model-selection mechanism. Addressing the theoretical rate discussion and fixing the supplementary material would further solidify the paper.
