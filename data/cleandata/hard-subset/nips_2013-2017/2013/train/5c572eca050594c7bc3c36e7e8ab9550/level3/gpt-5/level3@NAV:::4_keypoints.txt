- Reframes density-ratio estimation f = q/p as a Fredholm inverse problem and derives two RKHS-regularized estimators (Type I and Type II).
- Type I uses the identity Ep[k(x,·) f] = Eq[k(x,·)]; Type II uses local kernels kt to obtain Ep[kt(x,·) f] ≈ q(x).
- Both formulations yield empirical Tikhonov problems with closed-form solutions via the representer theorem (FIRE).
- Proposes an unsupervised model-selection criterion (CD-CV) based on a family of test functions U.
- Theoretical results give concentration and convergence rates under smoothness and boundedness assumptions, both in R^d and on smooth manifolds.
- Rates show critical dependence on ambient dimension.
- Empirical comparisons to LSIF, KMM, and a TIKDE baseline are encouraging.
- Situates work relative to prior literature: tight connection to KMM and conditional mean embeddings; echoes spectral regularization and inverse-problem perspectives; differs from LSIF/KLIEP by not requiring an explicit basis for f.
- Technical quality is strong: careful operator-based derivations and nonasymptotic bounds; experiments support claims; CD-CV is practical.
- Clarification needed on positivity and normalization of the estimated density ratio f.
- Clarification needed on computational scaling and numerical stability for large sample sizes n.
- Recommend ablation studies for choices of U and for CD-CV’s behavior.
- Notation is dense; suggest consolidating assumptions (smoothness, bounds, p bounded away from zero) and more clearly separating H = k vs general H cases in proofs.
- Fredholm framing and the Type II construction are novel; showing KMM as a special case is insightful.
- High significance for covariate shift and importance sampling; operator viewpoint may enable ratio estimation on manifolds.
- Request an intuitive argument why optimizing over {k(x,·)} suffices for consistency in Ep[k f] = Eq[k], analogous to bandwidth intuition for Type II.
- Ask for motivation of the mixed L2,p and L2,q cost in (9), and discussion of its statistical trade-offs and target applications.
- Ask when estimating q/p is preferable to directly using empirical Eq — clarify regimes (variance, reusability across test functions, sample reuse) where ratio estimation helps.
- Ask how smoothness of p and q translates to smoothness/stability of q/p and state required conditions (e.g., p bounded away from zero on support of q).
- Given the dimension dependence of rates, request guidance on ranges of d and sample sizes where FIRE is expected to outperform KDE-based baselines and LSIF.
- Ask for justification of chosen U families (linear, half-space), sensitivity analysis of CD-CV to U, and whether U can be adapted to task-specific function classes.
- Request a broader position on whether using {k(x,·)} is the “right” class for covariate-shift estimation and how FIRE compares to Smooth Operators/conditional mean embeddings in identifiability and function-class richness.
- Pros for acceptance: principled formulation, solid theory, simple algorithms, unsupervised model selection, good experiments.
- Cons for acceptance: unclear assumptions and positivity/normalization issues, strong dimension dependence, and insufficient motivation/analysis for mixed norms and choice of U.
