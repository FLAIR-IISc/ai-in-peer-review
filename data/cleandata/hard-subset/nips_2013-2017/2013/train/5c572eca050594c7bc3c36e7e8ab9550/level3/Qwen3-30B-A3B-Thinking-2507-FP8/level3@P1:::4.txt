

This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors introduce the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms, which provides a principled framework for estimating the ratio q(x)/p(x) where p and q are probability densities. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems in operator theory, enabling the application of spectral analysis for theoretical guarantees.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) establishing a connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm integral equations; (2) developing a flexible family of algorithms within the RKHS framework; (3) providing detailed theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds; and (4) introducing CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for unsupervised model selection. The theoretical results demonstrate convergence rates that depend on dimensionality and smoothness assumptions, with rates of O(n^{-1/(3.5+d/2)}) for Euclidean domains and improved rates for sub-manifolds.

The work builds on and extends previous approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). Unlike KMM, which lacks out-of-sample extension and stability guarantees, FIRE provides a regularized solution with explicit theoretical analysis. The authors also clarify how their approach relates to "Smooth Operators" (Grunewälder et al.) by showing that their test function selection {k(x,·)} is theoretically justified for the integral equation framework.

The paper's strengths are substantial: the theoretical foundation is rigorous, the algorithms are simple to implement, and the experimental results demonstrate strong performance across multiple datasets and applications. The CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is particularly valuable, addressing a significant practical challenge in density ratio estimation. The paper's clarity is excellent, with well-structured theoretical development and clear algorithm descriptions.

However, several points require clarification. First, the paper's motivation for optimizing over the kernel feature space {k(x,·)} needs stronger justification. While the paper notes this is "natural" for the integral equation, a more intuitive argument is needed for why this choice ensures convergence in the equality Ep[k(x,·)f] = Eq[k(x,·)] (as opposed to the more intuitive bandwidth-based intuition in the approximation Ep[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x)). 

Second, the choice of cost function in Eq. (9) mixes L2,p and L2,q norms. The authors should motivate why this combination is preferable to using a single norm, especially since the primary goal is to estimate q/p for importance sampling. The connection to target applications needs clarification - specifically, why estimating q/p helps in importance sampling when one could directly estimate the empirical expectations Ep[k(x,·)] and Eq[k(x,·)].

Third, the paper could better discuss the relationship between the smoothness of p and q and the smoothness of the ratio q/p, as well as conditions under which p is bounded away from zero (critical for practical implementation). The dependence on dimensionality d is also worth deeper exploration - the convergence rates deteriorate with increasing dimension, so the authors should discuss the practical dimensionality limits for which their method is effective.

The experimental section is strong, with thorough comparisons against relevant baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and settings. The CD-CV method shows particular promise for unsupervised parameter selection, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical foundation is sound, the algorithms are practical, and the experimental validation is comprehensive. The paper is well-written and clearly demonstrates how classical inverse problem theory can provide principled solutions to modern machine learning problems.

Pro: Novel formulation connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations; rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates; practical algorithms with strong experimental results; CD-CV for unsupervised model selection; clear connections to prior work.

Con: Needs stronger justification for kernel feature space optimization; requires clearer motivation for mixed norm cost function; should better discuss smoothness conditions and dimensionality limitations; could expand on practical implementation considerations.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of transfer learning and covariate shift adaptation. It advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation by providing a theoretically grounded framework with practical implementation. The work is highly relevant to NIPS' focus on statistical learning theory and its applications, and the results are significant enough to be of interest to both theoretical and applied machine learning researchers. The paper meets all four criteria for quality: it's technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical validation, clearly written with well-organized presentation, original in its approach to density ratio estimation, and significant for its contribution to an important problem in machine learning.
