- Novel approach: Reframes density-ratio estimation q/p as an inverse problem via the Fredholm equation, yielding the FIRE algorithm family that is flexible and easy to implement.  
- Theoretical analysis: Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates (notably for the Gaussian kernel).  
- Experimental validation: Empirical results show effectiveness on multiple datasets, including covariate-shift classification tasks.  
- Relation to prior work: Clear summary and connections to importance sampling, covariate shift, and transfer learning.  
- Strength — novelty: Presents a fresh, principled framework leveraging integral-operator theory.  
- Strength — completeness: Combines theoretical guarantees with practical algorithms and experiments.  
- Weakness — intuition for convergence: Needs a more intuitive argument for why optimizing over {k(x,·)} guarantees convergence in equality (i).  
- Weakness — cost function motivation: Needs stronger motivation and application links for the mixed L2,p / L2,q cost in Eq. (9).  
- Weakness — when to estimate q/p: Should clarify when estimating q/p is preferable to using Eq directly and better contextualize the importance-sampling application.  
- Weakness — regularizer and smoothness: Should discuss how smoothness of p and q affects smoothness of q/p and state conditions (e.g., p bounded away from zero).  
- Weakness — dependence on dimension: Needs discussion of dependence on dimension d and practical guidance on usable dimensionalities.  
- Weakness — test-function choice: Should motivate and justify the chosen family of test functions U.  
- Originality: Novel use of the Fredholm equation for density-ratio estimation, constituting a new principled framework.  
- Significance: Potential to advance density-ratio estimation, importance sampling, covariate shift, and related transfer-learning applications.  
- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance pending revisions to address the listed intuition, motivation, smoothness, and dimensionality issues.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel framework, rigorous theoretical analysis, and strong empirical support.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Lacks some intuitive explanations, application motivation for cost choice, and discussions on smoothness and dimensionality.
