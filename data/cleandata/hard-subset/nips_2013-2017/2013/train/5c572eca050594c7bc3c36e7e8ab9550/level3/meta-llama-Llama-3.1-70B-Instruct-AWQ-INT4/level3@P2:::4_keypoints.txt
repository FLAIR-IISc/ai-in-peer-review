- Problem: Estimate the density ratio q/p (Radon–Nikodym derivative) from samples or known q by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation and solving with regularization in an RKHS.

- Relation to prior work: Connects to covariate-shift and density-ratio literature (e.g., [6,10,9,22,2]) and leverages classical operator theory for spectral/Fourier analysis.

- Theoretical + experimental contribution: Provides a principled, analyzable framework with both theoretical guarantees and empirical results.

- Algorithmic contribution: Introduces the FIRE family of algorithms, which are flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

- Missing intuition: No intuitive argument explaining why optimizing over the set of kernel functions ensures convergence in equality (i).

- Cost function ambiguity: Mixing L2,p and L2,q in eq.(9) is not well-motivated; authors should link the choice to target applications.

- Importance sampling motivation: Unclear when estimating q/p is preferable to directly using an estimated Eq for importance sampling.

- Regularization and smoothness: Need clearer discussion of regularizer choices and how smoothness of p and q relates to smoothness of q/p, including assumptions like p bounded away from zero.

- Dimensionality concerns: Dependence on input dimension d is not addressed; practical limits on dimensionality should be provided.

- Test function family U: Choice of test functions lacks justification and motivation.

- Methodology question: Authors should discuss whether using kernel-function families is the “right” general approach for covariate-shift/q/p estimation and relate to prior work (e.g., "Smooth Operators").

- Writing and presentation: Paper is well-written, clear, and concise but would be strengthened by addressing the above concerns.

- Pro-accept summary: Principled framework, flexible/simple FIRE algorithms, clear presentation, and strong relation to covariate-shift literature.

- Con-accept summary: Lacks intuition for kernel convergence, unclear cost-function choice and application links, weak importance-sampling motivation, unclear regularization/smoothness and dimensionality treatment, and insufficient justification of test functions.

- Scores: Quality 8/10; Clarity 9/10; Originality 8/10; Significance 8/10.
