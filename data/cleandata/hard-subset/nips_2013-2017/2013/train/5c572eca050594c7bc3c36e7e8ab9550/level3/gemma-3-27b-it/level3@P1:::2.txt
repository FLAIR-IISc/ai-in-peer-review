This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation, framing the problem as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors propose two algorithms, Type I and Type II, differing in their assumptions and applicability. Type I leverages a standard importance sampling equation, while Type II utilizes localized kernels to approximate the ratio directly. A key contribution lies in the development of a Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method for unsupervised parameter selection, a significant advantage over existing approaches. The work is grounded in theoretical analysis, offering convergence bounds for Gaussian kernels, and substantiated by experimental results demonstrating competitive performance.

The approach builds upon a substantial body of prior work in density ratio estimation and transfer learning, notably referencing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [Gretton et al., 2006], Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [Kanamori et al., 2010], and Kullback-Leibler Importance Estimation Procedure (KLIEP) [Nguyen et al., 2010]. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, highlighting the advantage of CD-CV for model selection – a feature lacking in KMM due to its non-natural out-of-sample extension. The connection to classical operator theory, specifically Fredholm equations, provides a strong theoretical foundation and opens avenues for applying spectral and Fourier analysis. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its principled formulation, offering a flexible framework for density ratio estimation. The theoretical analysis, while complex, provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms.  The introduction of CD-CV addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning: reliable parameter selection. The experimental results are encouraging, demonstrating the effectiveness of FIRE compared to baseline methods and competing approaches. The clarity of the exposition is generally good, although some sections require careful reading due to the mathematical complexity.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The convergence rates presented in Theorem 1 are somewhat slow, containing a factor dependent on both the kernel width *t* and the regularization parameter *λ*. This raises the question of whether stronger assumptions regarding the smoothness of the densities or the nature of the ratio *q/p* could yield more desirable polynomial convergence rates. Exploring assumptions similar to those found in spectral regularization theory [Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007] might be beneficial.  Furthermore, the paper relies on the strong assumption that *q/p* resides within a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev space). While mathematically convenient, this assumption may be restrictive in practice, and the consequences of this assumption on the behavior of *p/q* should be discussed. The assumption that smooth densities imply the ratio is also smooth deserves more discussion. 

A significant concern is the quality of the supplementary material. The reviewer’s notes detail numerous typos and errors that impede verification of the theoretical results. Specifically, inaccuracies in Lemma 5 and lines 675, 686, and 1147 render the supporting analysis questionable. Correcting these errors is crucial for establishing the credibility of the theoretical claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Development of a practical and effective unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV).
*   Solid theoretical foundation and convergence analysis.
*   Competitive experimental performance.
*   Clear connection to relevant prior work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Slow convergence rates with current theoretical bounds.
*   Strong assumptions regarding the smoothness of *q/p*.
*   Numerous errors and typos in the supplementary material hindering verification.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The proposed algorithms and, in particular, the CD-CV method, offer a practical and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem. However, the slow convergence rates and the errors in the supplementary material raise concerns. Addressing these issues, particularly through a careful review and correction of the supplemental material, would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance contingent upon these revisions.  The potential impact of the CD-CV method for practical applications in unsupervised learning justifies its inclusion in NIPS.
