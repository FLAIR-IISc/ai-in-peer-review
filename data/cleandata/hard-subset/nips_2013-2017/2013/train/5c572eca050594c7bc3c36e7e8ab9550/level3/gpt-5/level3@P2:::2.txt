Summary and relation to prior work
The paper formulates density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem via Fredholm integral equations of the first kind. Two variants are proposed:
- Type I: Use the importance-sampling identity Eq(Φ) = Ep(rΦ) and approximate the operator Kp from samples of p and the right-hand side Kq1 from samples of q. Regularize with an RKHS norm to obtain a closed-form solution (FIRE).
- Type II: Use a δ-kernel kt to localize Kt,pf ≈ f, yielding an approximate equation Kt,p(r) ≈ q; regularize in an RKHS to solve for r when q is known at sample points from p.

The paper provides explicit algorithms via the representer theorem, several loss choices (L2,p, mixed γL2,p + (1−γ)L2,q, and RKHS norm), and a model selection method, CD-CV, that uses cross-density moment matching with random test functions to tune kernel width and regularization without labels. Theoretical analysis gives concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels when densities are supported on Rd or smooth compact manifolds, and experiments compare FIRE against LSIF, KMM (where applicable), and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline under covariate shift.

This work connects naturally to prior NIPS and related literature on inverse problems and kernel methods (e.g., Tikhonov regularization and operator inversion, De Vito et al.; spectral regularization; conditional mean embeddings) and to density-ratio estimation (KMM, LSIF/KLIEP, Nguyen–Wainwright–Jordan). The Type I RKHS-loss variant encompasses KMM as a special case with a different optimization/regularization, clarifying the relationship. The CD-CV criterion is closely related to moment matching and, practically, to the validation criteria used by LSIF/KLIEP, but without requiring the validation functions to form an approximation basis.

Strengths
- A clean, principled inverse-problem formulation that unifies several existing ideas and yields simple, closed-form kernel algorithms with natural out-of-sample extension.
- A compelling, fully unsupervised model selection scheme (CD-CV) that is practical and addresses a key pain point for density-ratio methods; clear argument why RKHS-objective values (as in KMM) are not directly comparable across kernels.
- Nontrivial theory: concentration and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and manifolds; explicit finite-sample bounds that separate sample sizes for p and q.
- Empirical results are encouraging across regression and classification under covariate shift; comparisons include strong baselines.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Rates appear slow due to the interplay of kernel localization parameter t and regularization λ (e.g., bias terms like O(t) and, per the supplement, terms of the form (t/−log λ)^2). Please discuss whether stronger source/spectral assumptions (e.g., effective dimension and source conditions as in Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007) on r = q/p and the operator Kp can yield polynomial rates with fixed t or faster joint choices of (t, λ). As-is, the corollaries yield exponents that are quite conservative.
- Assumptions: r ∈ W2 2(Ω) is strong; in practice r can be highly peaked where p is small. Please discuss robustness when r is unbounded or has limited smoothness, and the impact of support mismatch between p and q.
- Positivity: FIRE does not enforce r(x) ≥ 0. Negativity can harm importance weighting. Consider adding nonnegativity constraints (as in KMM/LSIF) or recommending clipping strategies; discuss theoretical/empirical effects.
- Type II: The approximation Kt,p(r) ≈ q incurs O(t) bias; while this is analyzed, the experimental section largely focuses on Type I. Please include a use case where q is known pointwise but hard to sample (e.g., AIS/MCMC scenarios), and show sensitivity to t and λ.
- Computational aspects: Solving linear systems with matrices like Kp,pKH or Kp,p^2KH is O(n^3) and O(n^2) memory. The paper claims efficiency vs KMM/LSIF; please report wall-clock times, and discuss scalability with Nyström/Random Features or preconditioning.

Clarity
- The main paper is generally clear and well organized. However, the supplemental material contains numerous typos that impede verification. Please fix the following (non-exhaustive):
  - Lemma 5: W2^2 should be Ws^2; the bound should read D1 (t/−log λ)^2 + λ^{1−α} D2 ||f||_2^2.
  - Lines 675 and 686: equations there are inconsistent; Eq. (31) is correct.
  - Line 1147: the integral domain should be ||ξ||^2 ≥ (threshold; please correct the missing bound).
  - Several minor symbol clashes between k, kH, and kt; ensure consistent notation when KH = K.
- Please add a short intuition section on the roles of t vs λ in Type I and Type II and guidance for their joint selection.

Originality
- The framing via Fredholm equations and the explicit Tikhonov–RKHS derivations bring a fresh, unified perspective and lead to practical algorithms with clean formulas. The CD-CV model selection is a particularly novel and useful contribution, despite being related to existing validation ideas (LSIF/KLIEP). The connection to KMM is made precise and insightful.

Significance
- Density-ratio estimation is central to covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling. A method that is simple, stable, with out-of-sample extension, and that admits practical unsupervised model selection is valuable. The theoretical bounds on manifolds are also of interest. With stronger rate results and improved supplement, the impact would increase further.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Principled inverse-problem perspective with simple closed-form kernel algorithms.
- Effective unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) and good empirical performance.
- Clear relationship to KMM; natural out-of-sample extension.
- Theory covering Rd and manifolds.

Cons:
- Conservative convergence rates and limited discussion of source/effective-dimension assumptions.
- Strong smoothness assumptions on r; no positivity constraints.
- Limited Type II experiments; computational scalability not fully addressed.
- Supplemental typos hinder verification.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a novel, useful perspective and a practical contribution (CD-CV) with competitive performance. Addressing the theoretical rate discussion, supplement corrections, and adding computational/Type II experiments would strengthen it further.
