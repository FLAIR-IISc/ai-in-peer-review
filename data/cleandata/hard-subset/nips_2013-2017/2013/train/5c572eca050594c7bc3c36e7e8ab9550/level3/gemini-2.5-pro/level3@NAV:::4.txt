This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio f = q/p, which is crucial for tasks like importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The core contribution is a novel and principled reformulation of this problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two estimators are derived from the equalities Ep[k(x,·)f] = Eq[k(x,·)] and Ep[k(x,·)f] ≈ q(x). Using Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS framework, the authors develop the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithm. This work provides a new perspective on prior methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which relates to an unregularized variant, and LSIF, whose validation ideas are adapted for the proposed unsupervised cross-validation scheme (CD-CV).

The paper’s primary strength is its elegant and principled formulation of density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem. This work is technically strong, offering convergence guarantees under specified smoothness assumptions. The resulting FIRE algorithm is straightforward to implement, and the proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is a significant practical contribution. Overall, this is a well-executed and timely paper presenting a compelling combination of theory and experimental validation.

While the paper is strong, it could be improved by addressing the following points:

1.  **Intuition:** Could the authors provide an intuitive argument for why using the family {k(x,·)} as test functions in the Type I equality ensures convergence of the ratio estimate, similar to the delta-kernel intuition for Type II?
2.  **Cost Function:** Please provide further motivation for the mixed L2,p and L2,q norm in Equation (9) and discuss target applications where this choice is beneficial.
3.  **Applicability:** For importance sampling, when samples from q are available, what is the advantage of estimating f=q/p to compute Ep[h*f] instead of using the empirical estimate of Eq[h] directly?
4.  **Assumptions & Dimensionality:** A discussion on the relationship between the smoothness of p, q, and their ratio f (including the condition that p is bounded away from zero) would be welcome. Additionally, please comment on the practical range of dimension d where the method remains effective, given the explicit dependence in the convergence rates.
5.  **Comparison:** How does using {k(x,·)} as test functions compare to operator-based views like Grünewälder et al. ("Smooth Operators")?

This is a high-quality paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. I strongly recommend acceptance and encourage the authors to incorporate the requested clarifications in the final version.
