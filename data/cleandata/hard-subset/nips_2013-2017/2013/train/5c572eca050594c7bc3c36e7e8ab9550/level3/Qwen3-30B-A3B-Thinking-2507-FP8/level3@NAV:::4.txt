

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation (q/p) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, solved using kernel methods and regularization. The authors develop the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms that provide principled approaches for both Type I (when samples from q are available) and Type II (when q values are known at p-sampled points) settings. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, offering convergence guarantees under smoothness assumptions with rates depending on ambient dimension.

The paper's strengths are substantial: it provides a theoretically sound foundation with rigorous error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and manifolds, introduces the CD-CV model selection method for unsupervised parameter tuning, and demonstrates strong empirical performance compared to state-of-the-art methods like KMM and LSIF. The connection to classical operator theory is particularly compelling and provides a unified perspective on density ratio estimation.

However, several important questions require clarification. First, the paper lacks an intuitive explanation for why optimization over {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in equality (i), unlike the bandwidth intuition provided for equality (ii). Second, the motivation for mixing L2,p and L2,q norms in Equation (9) is unclear—why not consistently use one norm? Third, the paper doesn't sufficiently address when estimating q/p is preferable to directly using an estimated q in importance sampling applications. Fourth, the relationship between smoothness of p, q, and the ratio q/p needs more discussion, as this critically impacts convergence rates. Finally, the choice of test function family U requires justification, and the paper should better position its approach against prior work like "Smooth Operators" (Grunewalder et al.) to clarify its novelty.

While the theoretical analysis is strong and the experimental results are convincing, addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper. The method's practical utility for high-dimensional problems (beyond d=3-4) also requires more explicit discussion. Overall, this is a well-executed, timely contribution that advances the field of density ratio estimation with significant theoretical and practical value.
