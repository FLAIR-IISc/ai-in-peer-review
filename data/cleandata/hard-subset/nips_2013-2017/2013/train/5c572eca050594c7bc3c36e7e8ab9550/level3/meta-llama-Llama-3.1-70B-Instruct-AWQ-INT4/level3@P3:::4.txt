This paper presents a novel approach to estimating the ratio of two densities, p and q, using the Fredholm equation of the first kind. The authors reformulate the problem of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem and apply regularization techniques to solve it. The resulting algorithms, known as FIRE, are flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

The paper is well-executed, and the authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel. The experimental results are also encouraging, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method in various settings.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to leverage kernel methods for optimization. By using test functions, the authors can take advantage of the representer theorem to obtain simple and explicit solutions. The paper also provides a principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically.

The authors address the problem of model selection, which is a challenging task in unsupervised learning. They propose a completely unsupervised technique, CD-CV, for cross-validating the parameters of their algorithm. This technique is based on a performance measure that is unique to their setting and allows for the use of various cross-validation procedures.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The organization of the paper is logical, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of related work.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. One of the concerns is the choice of cost function. The authors initially optimize over L2,p but later mix L2,p and L2,q. It would be helpful to provide more motivation and justification for this choice. Additionally, the authors could discuss the relationships between the smoothness of p and q and the smoothness of the ratio q/p.

Another area of improvement is the discussion of the dependence on dimension d. The authors provide some theoretical results, but it would be helpful to provide more guidance on the practical implications of these results. Specifically, the authors could discuss the dimensions for which the method is practically useful.

The authors could also provide more motivation and justification for the chosen family of test functions U. While the paper provides some theoretical results, it would be helpful to provide more intuition and explanation for the choice of test functions.

Finally, the authors could discuss the principled question of whether using {k(x,·)} is the "right" general approach for covariate-shift / q/p estimation. They could also relate their work to prior work, such as "Smooth Operators" (Grunewalder et al.).
