

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation, a classical inverse problem. The authors develop a principled framework using regularization techniques within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), resulting in a family of algorithms they term FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper establishes theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds, and introduces CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for unsupervised model selection.

**Strengths:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and addresses an important problem in machine learning with clear connections to kernel methods and inverse problems. The integral equation perspective provides a unifying framework for density ratio estimation, which is a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis, while partially relegated to supplementary material, provides valuable convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings. The CD-CV model selection technique is particularly innovative and addresses a critical practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Theoretical gaps:** The paper makes key claims about the approximation property ∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) without proof or sufficient reference (only citing [24], Ch. 1). This is a fundamental component of the approach, and its justification should be provided in the main text or a clear reference. Additionally, the theoretical analysis relies heavily on supplementary material, which the reviewer was unable to verify.

2. **Comparison to prior work:** The paper lacks a clear empirical and theoretical comparison to relevant methods. For instance, the reference to [12] (which appears to be a density estimation method) is not discussed in relation to the authors' special case q=1 (where the method reduces to density estimation without bandwidth→0). The paper should explicitly discuss how bandwidth plays a different role in this approach compared to kernel density estimation (KDE), where bandwidth must tend to zero for consistency.

3. **Methodological clarity:** The paper would benefit from a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization for readers unfamiliar with this technique, particularly in the context of inverse problems. The naive plug-in approach (estimating p and q separately) is mentioned as inferior but not clearly explained—why is this approach problematic for density ratio estimation compared to the proposed method?

4. **Empirical details:** The paper lacks references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS), which is unusual for machine learning papers. Including these would help readers reproduce the experiments. Toy experiments (e.g., 1D plots of true vs estimated p/q) would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation by illustrating the method's performance qualitatively.

5. **Theoretical context:** The paper claims "minimax-optimality" but does not provide a clear discussion of how Corollaries 2 and 4 relate to known optimal rates. The comparison to [12] is unclear, and the paper should address whether the proposed method achieves minimax rates.

**Recommendation:**
This is a well-written paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution by reframing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation problem. The FIRE framework is elegant and the CD-CV model selection technique is particularly valuable. However, the missing theoretical details, unclear comparisons to prior work, and insufficient empirical validation prevent this from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

**Pro:**
- Novel perspective on density ratio estimation
- Strong theoretical framework with convergence rates
- Practical CD-CV model selection technique
- Well-organized and clearly written

**Con:**
- Key theoretical claims lack justification
- Insufficient comparison to prior methods (especially [12])
- Missing dataset references and toy experiments
- Theoretical analysis relies on unverified supplementary material

Overall, this is a promising paper that addresses an important problem with a novel approach. With the addition of the missing theoretical details, clearer comparisons to prior work, and more comprehensive empirical validation, it would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper's current state prevents the reviewer from fully endorsing its acceptance at NIPS.
