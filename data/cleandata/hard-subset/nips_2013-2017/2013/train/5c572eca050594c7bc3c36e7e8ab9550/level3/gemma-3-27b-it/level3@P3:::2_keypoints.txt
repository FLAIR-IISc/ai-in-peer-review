- Proposes a novel density-ratio estimation framework by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and developing two algorithms (FIRE Type I & II).  
- Main practical contribution: enables reliable unsupervised cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection, an advantage over methods like KMM.  
- Well-motivated and connected to importance sampling, covariate shift, and transfer learning; positions and compares appropriately to KMM, LSIF, KLIEP.  
- The Fredholm formulation provides a solid theoretical foundation and concise algorithmic presentation.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive and diverse, with convincing comparisons to relevant baselines.  
- Strength: theoretical convergence analysis is provided, offering insights into algorithm behavior.  
- Weakness: convergence rate in Theorem 1 is very slow (depends on (-t / log λ)); authors should discuss whether polynomial rates are achievable under stronger assumptions.  
- Weakness: reliance on q/p belonging to a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev) is a strong limitation; more discussion and mitigation strategies for failures of this assumption are needed.  
- Weakness: numerous typos and errors in the supplementary material severely hinder verification of theoretical results and must be corrected.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of computational cost (e.g., kernel matrix inversion); include complexity/Scalability analysis.  
- Specific correction: Lemma 5 should use Ws^2 and the bound D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 ||f||_2^2.  
- Specific correction: Lines 675 & 686 contain errors; Eq. 31 is the correct expression.  
- Specific correction: Line 1147 integral domain should read ||ξ||^2 ≥.  
- Practical comment: Theorem 1/Corollary 2 critically depend on kernel width t; provide guidance on selecting t in real applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, practical CV-based tuning, convincing experiments, and relevance to transfer learning/covariate shift.  
- Arguments against acceptance: slow convergence raising scalability concerns, strong smoothness assumption limiting generality, and supplementary errors undermining theoretical confidence.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions conditional on fixing supplementary errors, expanding discussion of convergence rates and smoothness limitations, and adding a brief computational complexity analysis.
