- Paper frames density-ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS (FIRE).
- Two formulations: Type I (Kp r = Kq 1) from importance-sampling identity, and Type II using a local kernel Kt with approximation Kt,p r ≈ q.
- Closed-form kernel algorithms derived via the representer theorem; several loss choices presented (L2,p, L2,q, mixture, RKHS loss).
- The method provides natural out-of-sample prediction and regularizes the ratio function directly in H, differing from KMM and LSIF/KLIEP approaches.
- The paper gives finite-sample concentration/error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and smooth compact manifolds.
- A practical unsupervised model-selection criterion, CD-CV, uses importance-sampling on random test functions to choose kernel width and regularization.
- Experiments on USPS, CPUsmall, and Kin8nm show FIRE is stable and competitive or better than LSIF, KMM (where applicable), and a TIKDE baseline.
- Relation to prior work: connects to inverse-problem/regression theory (De Vito, Engl) and to existing kernel ratio estimators (Shimodaira, Gretton, Kanamori, Sugiyama).
- Type I with an RKHS loss recovers a regularized KMM variant but improves stability and provides an out-of-sample estimator.
- FIRE avoids choosing finite bases required by LSIF/KLIEP by solving the Fredholm equation with RKHS regularization.
- CD-CV addresses a major practical pain point: unsupervised model selection independent of kernel scaling issues.
- Theoretical rates are relatively slow (e.g., Type I O(n−1/(3.5+d/2)) in L2,p), driven by kernel bias t, regularization λ, and sampling terms.
- Reviewer requests clarification whether stronger source/eigen-decay or smoothness assumptions could yield faster polynomial rates and whether current rates are minimax-optimal.
- Several assumptions may be strong in practice (bounded densities, Sobolev regularity for r and r/p, requirement m > λ1/3 n); discuss applicability in covariate-shift settings.
- Clarity suggestions: make Type I vs Type II distinctions explicit, state computational complexity and low-rank approximations, and detail CD-CV hyperparameters (number of test functions, variance, sensitivity).
- Supplementary material contains typos/errors that hinder verification; specific corrections to lemmas, equations, and integral bounds are noted.
- Originality: neat conceptual unification casting ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem and a useful Type II/local kernel variant.
- Significance: valuable for covariate shift, importance sampling, and off-policy evaluation due to stable out-of-sample estimator and unsupervised model selection.
- Pros: principled formulation, closed-form algorithms, CD-CV model selection, theoretical bounds for Euclidean and manifold domains, promising empirical stability.
- Cons: slow convergence rates, strong smoothness/boundedness assumptions, supplementary typos, limited experimental breadth and sparse scalability details.
