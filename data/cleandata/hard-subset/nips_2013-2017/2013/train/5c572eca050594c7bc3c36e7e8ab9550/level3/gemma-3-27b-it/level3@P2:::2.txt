This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation, framing the problem as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors present two formulations, Type I and Type II, offering theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrating strong empirical performance, particularly highlighting the benefit of unsupervised parameter selection using a novel Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly situating the work within the broader context of importance sampling, transfer learning, and covariate shift adaptation. The connection to classical operator theory provides a principled foundation and allows for application of well-established tools for analysis. The formulation leveraging RKHS regularization is elegant and leads to easily implementable algorithms, termed FIRE. The contribution of enabling robust, unsupervised parameter selection via CD-CV is particularly significant, addressing a known challenge in unsupervised/semi-supervised learning. The comparison to related methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) is thorough and appropriately positions the strengths of the proposed approach.

The theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, is a major strength. However, the stated convergence rates in Theorem 1 raise concerns. The dependence on *t* (kernel width) and *λ* (regularization parameter) as (*t* / log *λ*) suggests a slow convergence rate. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss whether stronger assumptions—such as spectral assumptions (e.g., Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007) or more restrictive smoothness conditions on the density ratio *q/p*—could yield polynomial convergence rates with a fixed kernel width *t*. The assumption that *q/p* resides in a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev space) is strong and deserves further discussion. In practice, the function *p/q* may exhibit more irregular behavior, potentially impacting the practical applicability of the theoretical results.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some sections are dense with mathematical notation. The organization is logical, progressing from problem formulation to theoretical analysis and then to experimental validation. However, the experimental section could be strengthened by providing more detailed justifications for the choice of specific datasets and resampling strategies. A clearer discussion of the computational cost of the proposed methods compared to existing alternatives would also be beneficial.

The originality of this work is noteworthy. While the use of kernel methods in density ratio estimation is not new, the Fredholm equation formulation, the specific regularization approach, and the CD-CV method represent novel contributions. The paper diligently references related work, acknowledging the connections to KMM and LSIF while clearly articulating the advantages of the proposed framework.

The significance of the results is substantial. Reliable, unsupervised parameter selection is crucial for the practical application of kernel methods in density ratio estimation. The CD-CV method offers a promising solution to this challenge, and the theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the performance of the algorithms. The potential impact extends to various applications, including transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Effective and practical CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection.
*   Competitive empirical performance compared to existing methods.
*   Clear presentation and connection to relevant prior work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Slow convergence rate indicated by Theorem 1.
*   Strong assumptions on the smoothness of the density ratio.
*   Supplementary material contains numerous errors hindering verification.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and significant potential impact. The main weakness lies in the potentially slow convergence rate and the reliance on strong smoothness assumptions. However, the practical benefit of the CD-CV method and the clear presentation of the framework outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**However, acceptance is contingent on the authors addressing the issues with the supplementary material.** The numerous typos and errors identified (e.g., Lemma 5, lines 675, 686, and 1147) are a significant impediment to verifying the theoretical results and must be corrected before publication. A revised supplementary material that is accurate and clearly presented is essential.
