This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation, reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging regularization techniques from RKHS theory. The core contribution lies in the development of two algorithms, FIRE Type I and Type II, and crucially, demonstrating the applicability of cross-validation (CV) for unsupervised parameter selection – a significant advantage over existing methods like KMM.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting the problem to importance sampling, covariate shift, and transfer learning. It positions itself appropriately within the existing literature, acknowledging related work such as KMM, LSIF, and KLIEP, and clearly articulating its advantage: enabling reliable CV-based parameter tuning. The formulation using Fredholm equations provides a solid theoretical foundation, and the resulting algorithms are presented concisely. The experimental validation appears comprehensive, including comparisons to relevant baselines on various datasets and resampling strategies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a fresh perspective offering a strong theoretical basis.
* **Effective CV-Based Parameter Selection:** This is the paper's most compelling contribution.  The ability to reliably select parameters in an unsupervised setting is a practical and valuable advancement.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence rate analysis, though with limitations discussed below, provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, explaining complex concepts in a relatively accessible manner.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough, encompassing various datasets, resampling methods, and comparison algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Slow Convergence Rate:** The convergence rate in Theorem 1, dependent on (-t/log λ), is considerably slow. The authors correctly identify this and question the possibility of achieving polynomial rates under stronger assumptions. While the exploration of spectral assumptions is a good direction, a more concrete discussion of achievable rates would be beneficial.
* **Assumption on q/p Smoothness:** The reliance on q/p belonging to a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev space) is a significant limitation. The paper acknowledges the potential for poor behavior of p/q, but this practical concern warrants a more in-depth discussion, potentially including scenarios where this assumption fails and mitigation strategies.
* **Supplementary Material Errors:** The numerous typos and errors in the supplementary material severely hinder verification of the theoretical results.  Correcting these is crucial.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the algorithms are described as simple and easy to implement, a discussion of their computational complexity, especially regarding kernel matrix inversion, would be valuable.

**Specific Comments & Corrections Needed:**

* **Lemma 5:** As pointed out by the authors themselves, W2^2 should be Ws^2 and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 ||f||_2^2.
* **Lines 675 & 686:** The authors correctly note errors on these lines, with Eq. 31 being the correct one.
* **Line 1147:** The integral domain should be ||ξ||^2 ≥.
* **Theorem 1/Corollary 2:** The rate of convergence is critically dependent on the choice of 't' (kernel width). A more detailed discussion on practical selection of 't' to achieve reasonable convergence in real applications would be valuable.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to density ratio estimation with a strong theoretical foundation.
* The demonstrated ability to perform unsupervised parameter selection via CV is a significant practical contribution.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate competitive performance.
* The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning with connections to transfer learning and covariate shift.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The slow convergence rate raises concerns about the algorithm's scalability and practical applicability in high-dimensional settings.
* The strong assumption on the smoothness of q/p limits the algorithm's generality.
* The errors in the supplementary material undermine confidence in the theoretical results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The novel formulation, coupled with the demonstrated efficacy of CV-based parameter selection, is a significant advancement. However, the slow convergence rate and strong assumptions require careful consideration. Addressing the errors in the supplementary material is essential. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on correcting the supplementary material and providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations related to the convergence rate and the smoothness assumption.  The authors should also consider including a brief analysis of the algorithm’s computational complexity.
