1. Novel formulation: reframes density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, linking it to classical operator theory.  
2. FIRE algorithms: introduces the FIRE family using regularization from functional analysis to solve the inverse problem.  
3. Unification: provides a principled framework that unifies prior methods like KMM and LSIF.  
4. CD-CV: proposes an unsupervised Cross-Density Cross-Validation procedure for model/parameter selection.  
5. Theoretical contributions: gives convergence rate analyses for Type I/II settings on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, with detailed results for Gaussian kernels.  
6. Algorithmic simplicity: FIRE methods are simple to implement and computationally attractive.  
7. Empirical evaluation: thorough experiments comparing against KMM, LSIF, TIKDE on regression and classification, showing practical gains.  
8. Missing measure specification: fails to specify the dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue/counting), making theoretical claims ambiguous.  
9. Domain and positivity unclear: does not clearly state domain of x (R^d vs general space) or whether p(x)>0 everywhere, weakening applicability.  
10. Existence of density ratio assumed: core method assumes q/p exists as a function, which can fail when distributions lack a common dominating measure (e.g., continuous vs discrete).  
11. Practical applicability limited: foundational measure-theoretic assumptions may not hold in real-world ML settings, undermining usability.  
12. Clarity issues: theoretical development needs more explicit statements of measure-theoretic assumptions and their consequences.  
13. For acceptance: novel theoretical framework, comprehensive analysis, and practical CD-CV address important challenges.  
14. Against acceptance: fundamental unaddressed measure-theoretic assumptions severely limit practical relevance.  
15. Recommendation: reject pending revision; authors should clarify measure-theoretic assumptions and handle cases without a classical density ratio (e.g., use Radon–Nikodym framing).
