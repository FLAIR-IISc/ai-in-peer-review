Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes FIRE, a family of density-ratio estimators that reformulate ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solve it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two variants are analyzed: Type I uses samples from both p and q and matches their kernel-smoothed moments; Type II uses a local kernel kt and leverages the approximate identity property Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q to obtain an inverse problem driven by samples from p and pointwise values of q. The paper provides explicit closed-form solutions via the representer theorem, an unsupervised model-selection procedure (CD-CV), and nonasymptotic bounds/rates for Gaussian kernels on R^d and d-dimensional manifolds.

The approach connects density-ratio estimation to classical inverse problems and spectral theory, complementing prior NIPS and ML work on KMM (Gretton et al.), LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.), and RKHS-based density estimation (e.g., Mukherjee & Vapnik [12]). The operator-theoretic view clarifies when and how one can regularize the ill-posedness intrinsic to inverting kernel operators.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and yields a principled, flexible estimator with clear connections to operator theory.
- Clarity and presentation: the paper is well written, with concrete algorithms and a useful unsupervised model-selection scheme (CD-CV).
- Theory: nonasymptotic bounds and convergence rates are provided for Euclidean and manifold settings, which is relatively rare in this area.
- Practical appeal: simple closed-form expressions and favorable computational profile; sensible baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) and applications to covariate shift.

Weaknesses and questions
- Missing justification of key approximation: statements of the form ∫ kt(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) should be proved or rigorously cited (see suggestions below).
- Comparative theory: the paper does not position its rates versus minimax-optimal results for ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al. [14]) nor versus RKHS density estimators (e.g., [12]).
- Empirical comparisons: while encouraging, comparisons to prior methods (especially [12]) are limited and do not disentangle when FIRE’s operator inversion is preferable to plug-in or parametric bases.
- Special case q=1: the paper hints at density estimation, but the role of bandwidth differs from KDE and needs clarification (see below).
- Proofs relegated to supplement; not checked by the reviewer.
- Missing dataset references; and additional toy experiments would help illustrate estimator behavior.

Detailed comments and requests
1) Justify the smoothing identity. For Gaussian kt with variance t, Kt f(y) = ∫ kt(x,y) f(x) dx equals the heat semigroup e^{(t/2)Δ}f(y) = f(y) + (t/2)Δf(y) + O(t^2) for sufficiently smooth f. This yields L2 or pointwise O(t) bias under Sobolev/Hölder smoothness. Standard references: Evans, Partial Differential Equations (2nd ed., 2010, Sec. 2.3); Stein, Singular Integrals and Differentiability Properties of Functions (1970); for manifolds, heat kernel asymptotics (Varadhan, 1967) and modern treatments in geometric analysis. Please add a precise statement with assumptions and a proof or citations, and specify whether the O(t) is in L2,p or uniform norm.

2) Special case q=1 and relation to KDE. When q=1, one is effectively estimating 1/p (or p via inversion). In FIRE-Type II, the bandwidth t enters the forward operator Kt,p and induces a bias term C1 t (Theorems 1–3). This differs from KDE where bandwidth directly smooths the estimator; here, t controls how ill-posed the inverse problem is and the amount of bias in the approximate identity. Consistency still requires t→0 with n,λ appropriately tuned, but the inversion plus Tikhonov regularization can allow larger bandwidths than KDE without catastrophic variance. Please clarify this distinction and whether fixed t with λ→0 suffices under additional source conditions.

3) Brief primer on Tikhonov regularization. For readers unfamiliar with inverse problems: Tikhonov solves minf ||Af−b||^2 + λ||f||_H^2, trading fidelity to the (noisy) forward model Af≈b against a penalty that enforces smoothness of f in an RKHS H. The parameter λ controls bias–variance: larger λ yields smoother, more stable estimates; smaller λ reduces bias but risks amplifying noise/ill-conditioning. In our setting, A is Kp or Kt,p, and f is the ratio q/p.

4) Why plug-in is inferior. Estimating p and q separately (e.g., KDE) and taking q̂/p̂ compounds bias and inflates variance, especially where p is small (division by a noisy denominator), necessitating ad-hoc thresholding (TIKDE). FIRE avoids explicit density estimation, directly targets the ratio through an integral equation that aggregates information from both samples, and regularizes the ill-posed inversion in a principled way, yielding better stability and out-of-sample generalization.

5) Minimax and distance from optimal rates. Please relate Corollaries 2 and 4 to known lower/upper bounds for likelihood/density-ratio estimation (e.g., [14]) and to classical s-smooth nonparametric rates n^{-2s/(2s+d)}. The exponents 1/(3.5 + d/2) (Type I) and 1/(4 + 5d/6) (Type II) appear conservative, likely due to using Gaussian RKHS without explicit source conditions and to generic concentration bounds. It would help to (i) state the implied smoothness, (ii) compare to minimax-optimal rates under Sobolev or RKHS source conditions, and (iii) discuss whether sharper effective-dimension or spectral decay assumptions could improve rates.

6) Empirical comparisons to [12] and others. Please add experiments comparing to SV density estimation–based plug-in (e.g., [12]) and report compute time and sensitivity to bandwidth/regularization. Clarify how FIRE behaves in low-density regions vis-à-vis KMM/LSIF.

7) Dataset references. Add citations: CPUsmall and Kin8nm from the UCI Machine Learning Repository (Bache & Lichman, 2013; now Dua & Graff, 2017), “Kinematics of an 8-link Robot Arm”; USPS: Hull, A Database for Handwritten Text Recognition Research, PAMI 1994.

8) Toy 1D illustrations. Include simple 1D examples with known p and q showing truth vs. estimates for q/p under varying n, t, λ, and a plot of Kt,p f vs. q to visualize the inverse problem and regularization effect.

9) Reproducibility. Since all proofs are in the supplement and were not checked by the reviewer, please ensure key lemmas and assumptions are stated in the main text, and release code to facilitate verification.

Quality: Technically solid and promising; some theoretical claims (approximate identity, optimality) need stronger justification and positioning.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized; would benefit from a concise primer on Tikhonov and a more explicit discussion of bandwidth roles.

Originality: High; the Fredholm equation reformulation for ratio estimation is novel and insightful.

Significance: High; density-ratio estimation is central to covariate shift and importance sampling, and FIRE appears practical and theoretically grounded.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, novel formulation with principled algorithms and analysis
- Strong writing and practical unsupervised model selection
- Broad relevance to transfer learning and importance sampling

Arguments against acceptance
- Comparative theory and optimality discussion are limited
- Some key approximations lack proof/citation in the main text
- Empirical comparisons could be more comprehensive (incl. [12]) and illustrative (1D toys)

Overall: A nice paper addressing an important problem with a fresh perspective. I am positive about acceptance, contingent on clarifying the approximation justification, positioning the rates, and strengthening comparisons.
