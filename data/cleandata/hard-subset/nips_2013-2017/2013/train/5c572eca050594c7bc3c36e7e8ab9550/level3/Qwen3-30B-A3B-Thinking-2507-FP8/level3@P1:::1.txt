

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors develop a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) that addresses the problem of estimating q(x)/p(x) where p is a known density and q is either another density or an arbitrary function. The key insight is recognizing that density ratio estimation can be viewed as an inverse problem, enabling the application of classical integral equation theory and regularization methods.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a principled framework for density ratio estimation by connecting it to the classical Fredholm problem, which provides theoretical advantages over existing methods. Second, it develops the FIRE algorithm family, which is flexible, simple to implement, and supported by detailed theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. Third, it introduces CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a novel unsupervised model selection technique specifically designed for density ratio estimation. The theoretical analysis includes concentration bounds and convergence rates, which are particularly valuable for understanding the algorithm's behavior.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with kernel methods. The authors effectively relate their work to related approaches, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), highlighting both similarities and key differences. For instance, they note that while KMM can be viewed within their Type I setting, it lacks the regularization term present in FIRE, leading to potential instability, and does not provide a natural out-of-sample extension.

However, several aspects require clarification. The claim that ∫_Rd kt(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) lacks a proper justification or reference, which is crucial for the theoretical foundation of their Type II formulation. The special case when q=1 (density estimation) is mentioned but not adequately explored, particularly regarding how bandwidth selection differs from traditional KDE. The paper would benefit from a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization for readers less familiar with this technique. The comparison with prior methods like [12] is unclear, and the paper does not sufficiently discuss how their convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4) compare to minimax-optimal rates.

The empirical evaluation is generally strong, with comparisons to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE on standard datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS), though the paper would be strengthened by including toy 1D examples showing true vs estimated density ratios. The authors should also explicitly explain why a naive plug-in approach (estimating p and q separately) is inferior to their method, as this is a critical point for understanding the problem's difficulty.

The theoretical analysis is comprehensive but relies heavily on supplementary material that the reviewer has not verified. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the practical implications of their theoretical results, particularly regarding the trade-offs between different norm choices (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS) and their impact on performance.

The significance of the work is clear: density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling, with broad applications. The FIRE framework provides a principled approach with strong theoretical grounding and practical utility, particularly with their novel CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection.

Strengths: Clear exposition, novel theoretical formulation, strong empirical results, well-motivated problem.

Weaknesses: Missing theoretical justification for key approximation, incomplete discussion of special cases, unclear comparison with prior work, lack of toy examples to illustrate method performance.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to density ratio estimation. The integral-equation reformulation provides a fresh perspective and theoretical advantages over existing methods. With minor clarifications and expansions addressing the weaknesses noted, this would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The theoretical results are compelling but would be strengthened by more explicit discussion of how their rates compare to minimax-optimal rates. The empirical evaluation is thorough but would benefit from visualizations of the estimated density ratios in simple settings.

Pro: Novel theoretical framework, strong theoretical analysis, practical algorithm with effective model selection, well-written, addresses an important problem.
Con: Missing justification for key approximation, incomplete special case discussion, unclear comparison with prior work, lacks illustrative toy examples.
