This paper introduces a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory. The core idea is to connect density ratio estimation to the well-studied field of inverse problems, offering a principled framework for algorithm design and theoretical analysis. The authors propose a family of algorithms, FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), and provide theoretical guarantees for convergence rates using Gaussian kernels. A novel unsupervised model selection technique, CD-CV, is also presented and evaluated.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in density ratio estimation, including methods addressing covariate shift [19, 27] and importance sampling [2, 6, 9, 10, 22]. It particularly draws connections to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10], positioning FIRE as a related yet distinct approach.  The novelty stems from the integral equation reformulation, providing a different perspective and analytical tools than existing methods. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Theoretical Foundation:** The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is a significant contribution, grounding the problem in the established theory of inverse problems. This provides a strong theoretical basis for the proposed algorithms.
*   **Principled Framework:** The use of Tikhonov regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) offers a flexible and principled framework, allowing for different norms and regularization techniques.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings.  This is a key strength, demonstrating a rigorous understanding of the method's properties.
*   **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The CD-CV technique addresses the challenging problem of model selection in unsupervised learning, offering a practical solution for parameter tuning.
*   **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the underlying concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Justification of Key Statements:**  Several statements require further justification. For example, the approximation  ∫ k\_t(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t)  should be accompanied by either a proof or a clear citation to support its validity.
*   **Bandwidth Considerations:**  The paper briefly touches upon density estimation, implicitly when q=1. A more thorough discussion of how the bandwidth parameter 't' in the Gaussian kernel differs from standard Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) would be beneficial.  Does the approach avoid the bandwidth-zero limit issues of traditional KDE?
*   **Tikhonov Regularization Explanation:**  While familiar to some, a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would improve accessibility for readers less versed in the topic.
*   **Comparative Performance:** The empirical results show promising performance, but a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art methods (beyond KMM, LSIF and TIKDE) is needed. How does FIRE compare to methods like [12]?  Is there a discussion of minimax optimality rates and how the reported convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 relate to these theoretical bounds?
*   **Dataset Details:** The paper lacks references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS). Providing these references would increase reproducibility and allow readers to further investigate the results.
*   **Illustrative Experiments:**  While extensive experiments are presented, the inclusion of simple toy experiments (e.g., 1D plots of true and estimated p(x)/q(x) for known distributions) would provide a more intuitive understanding of the method’s behavior and quality of the estimates.
*   **Plug-in Approach Discussion:** A clearer explanation of why a straightforward plug-in approach, where p and q are estimated independently and then their ratio calculated, is inferior to the proposed method would strengthen the justification for the integral equation formulation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density ratio estimation.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the method's properties and limitations.
*   The CD-CV technique offers a practical solution for model selection.
*   The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Some theoretical statements lack sufficient justification.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and include a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   The lack of dataset references and illustrative examples hinders reproducibility and understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The novel integral equation formulation and accompanying theoretical analysis represent a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. However, addressing the identified weaknesses – particularly regarding justification of theoretical claims, comparative performance evaluation, and clarity of experimental details – would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
