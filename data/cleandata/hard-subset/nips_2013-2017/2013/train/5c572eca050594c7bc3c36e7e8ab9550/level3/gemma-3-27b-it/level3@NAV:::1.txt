## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by cleverly reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. This allows the authors to leverage tools from operator theory and regularization techniques, specifically Tikhonov regularization, resulting in a flexible family of algorithms (FIRE) for estimating the density ratio. The motivation is strong, as density ratio estimation is crucial in areas like importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation, and the connection to integral equations provides a fresh perspective. The paper is well-written and addresses a significant, interesting problem relevant to the NIPS audience, building upon prior work in kernel methods and transfer learning (e.g., [9, 10, 22]).

The key novelty lies in this integral equation reformulation, and the subsequent application of regularization. However, the theoretical justification for key statements, such as ∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t), lacks sufficient detail and requires either a formal proof or a clear citation to established literature. While mentioned in passing ([24]), a more direct reference would be helpful. 

A particularly interesting observation is the special case of q=1, which suggests a density estimation method avoiding the need for bandwidth selection approaching zero. The authors should expand on how the bandwidth’s role differs in FIRE compared to traditional Kernel Density Estimation (KDE). A brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization would also improve accessibility for readers potentially unfamiliar with the technique.

The paper mentions the superiority of their approach compared to a naive plug-in estimator, but lacks a clear explanation *why* this is the case.  Furthermore, the empirical and theoretical comparisons to prior methods, such as [12], are currently unclear.  Discussion of the minimax-optimality of FIRE and a quantification of how close Corollaries 2 and 4 are to optimal rates would significantly strengthen the contribution.

The datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) lack references, hindering reproducibility.  Finally, the inclusion of toy experiments (e.g., 1D plots comparing true and estimated p/q) would dramatically improve intuition and demonstrate the method's performance visually. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel approach to a fundamental problem, theoretically grounded, well-written, potential for significant impact.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Lack of detailed theoretical justification for key claims, unclear comparative performance, missing dataset references, absence of illustrative toy experiments.

**Overall:** This is a promising paper with a clever idea. However, addressing the weaknesses regarding theoretical detail, comparative analysis, and experimental illustration is crucial for a stronger contribution. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
