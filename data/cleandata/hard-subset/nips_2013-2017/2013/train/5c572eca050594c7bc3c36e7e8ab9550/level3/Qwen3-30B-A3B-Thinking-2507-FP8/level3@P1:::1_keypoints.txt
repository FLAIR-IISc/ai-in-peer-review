- Novel formulation: Recasts density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via RKHS regularization.  
- Algorithmic contribution: Introduces the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family for estimating q(x)/p(x) where p is known and q is a density or arbitrary function.  
- Model selection: Proposes CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a new unsupervised model selection method tailored to density ratio estimation.  
- Theoretical analysis: Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates, with detailed analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and submanifolds.  
- Clarity and exposition: Paper is well-written, organized, and situates the work relative to KMM and LSIF.  
- Relation to KMM/LSIF: Highlights similarities and differences — notes KMM can fit their Type I setting but lacks regularization and out-of-sample extension.  
- Missing justification: The approximation ∫_Rd kt(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) is asserted without proper reference or proof, undermining Type II foundations.  
- Special case q=1: The density-estimation special case is mentioned but not adequately explored, particularly bandwidth selection versus KDE.  
- Regularization background: Paper should briefly explain Tikhonov regularization for readers unfamiliar with it.  
- Prior work comparison: Comparison with prior methods (e.g., reference [12]) is unclear and needs clarification.  
- Minimax comparison: Convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4) are not compared to minimax-optimal rates; this comparison is missing.  
- Empirical evaluation: Strong comparisons to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE on standard datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).  
- Visualization gap: Lacks simple 1D toy examples showing true vs. estimated density ratios to illustrate behavior and failure modes.  
- Plug-in baseline: Authors should explicitly explain why naive plug-in (estimate p and q separately) is inferior to their direct approach.  
- Reliance on supplementary material: Key theoretical claims depend on unverified supplementary proofs; more discussion of practical implications is needed.  
- Norm trade-offs: Paper should discuss practical consequences of choosing different norms (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS) and how they affect performance.  
- Significance and recommendation: Work is significant for transfer learning, covariate shift, and importance sampling; with minor clarifications it is a strong NIPS submission.  
- Strengths vs weaknesses: Strengths — novel theoretical framing, solid analysis, practical algorithm and model selection, clear writing; Weaknesses — missing key theoretical justifications, incomplete special-case discussion, unclear prior-work comparisons, and lack of illustrative toy examples.
