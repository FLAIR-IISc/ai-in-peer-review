## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating it as an inverse problem using Fredholm integral equations and techniques from regularization theory, specifically within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The resulting algorithms, termed FIRE, are presented along with theoretical analysis (concentration bounds and convergence rates) and experimental results demonstrating performance on benchmark datasets, including applications to covariate shift. This work builds on a significant body of research in density ratio estimation [6, 10, 9, 22, 2], particularly those addressing covariate shift [19, 27] and kernel methods [21, 17].  The connection to classical operator theory is a potentially valuable contribution, offering a different perspective than existing approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10].

**Strengths:** The paper’s primary strength lies in its theoretically grounded approach. Framing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem opens up opportunities for applying well-established tools from functional analysis, leading to the derivation of concentration bounds and convergence rates (Theorem 1-3). The unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) is also a positive aspect, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning. The FIRE algorithms themselves appear relatively straightforward to implement, leveraging the representer theorem for efficient computation. The experimental results offer encouraging comparisons to existing methods, demonstrating competitive performance. 

**Weaknesses:** The core theoretical assumption – the existence of densities *p* and *q* – is problematic within a machine learning context. As correctly pointed out by the authors, arbitrary measures may lack densities, making the foundational assumption strong and potentially limiting the applicability of the method. The lack of specification of the dominating measure *ν* (e.g., Lebesgue, counting) significantly impacts the validity of the theoretical results. Results relying on the existence of a density might be invalid or ambiguous for data concentrated on sets of zero measure.  Moreover, the paper lacks clear statements regarding the domain of *x* (e.g., R<sup>d</sup> or a more general topological space) and assumptions about the positivity of the density *p* (i.e., is *p* > 0 everywhere?).  These omissions reduce the rigor and generalizability of the proposed framework. The comparison with KMM seems unfair, as the paper focuses on comparing implementation simplicity but does not discuss the out-of-sample extension limitation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical framing of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Derivation of theoretical guarantees (concentration bounds and convergence rates).
*   Practical algorithm (FIRE) with potential for efficient implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Strong assumption regarding the existence of densities, limiting applicability.
*   Lack of specification of the dominating measure *ν*.
*   Missing key modeling assumptions (domain of *x*, positivity of *p*).
*   Limited discussion of the limitations of the assumptions made.



**Overall:** While the paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound approach to density ratio estimation, the strong assumptions regarding the existence of densities and the lack of clarification on several key modeling details raise significant concerns. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the limitations should be more thoroughly addressed. I recommend **rejection** in its current form, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit, addressing the points raised above, particularly the critical issue of density existence and the necessary assumptions for theoretical validity.
