- Reformulates density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, connecting it to classical inverse problems in operator theory.
- Introduces two algorithms: Type I (samples from both p and q available) and Type II (q(x) known at p samples), both solved via Tikhonov regularization in RKHS.
- Proposes CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for unsupervised model selection, addressing the lack of applicable traditional cross-validation in density ratio estimation.
- Uses L2 norms in the loss (vs. KMM’s RKHS-norm loss), which enables effective cross-validation.
- Provides comprehensive theoretical analysis: convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, with detailed concentration bounds.
- Type I is well-motivated by importance sampling theory; Type II handles cases where q is known but hard to sample.
- Experimental validation is strong: FIRE often outperforms LSIF and TIKDE on regression and classification tasks.
- The framework separates the inverse problem from regularization, yielding flexible and interpretable algorithms.
- Paper is technically sound, original, and connects appropriately to related work (Sugiyama, Kanamori, Gretton, Yu & Szepesvári, Belkin).
- CD-CV is a significant practical contribution enabling parameter selection without labels, increasing applicability in unsupervised settings.
- Main theoretical weakness: slow convergence rate in Theorem 1 due to the (-t / log λ) factor.
- Suggestion: consider stronger spectral assumptions (e.g., Caponnetto & De Vito) to potentially obtain polynomial convergence rates.
- Practical limitation: Sobolev smoothness assumption for q/p may be unrealistic when density ratios are highly non-smooth; needs more discussion.
- Clarity is generally good, but the supplementary material contains numerous typos, incorrect notation/equations (e.g., Ws^2 vs W2^2, lines 675/686) undermining confidence and requiring correction.
- Significance is high for transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift; approach could extend to other statistical inverse problems.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to correct supplementary errors and expand discussion on assumptions and convergence.
