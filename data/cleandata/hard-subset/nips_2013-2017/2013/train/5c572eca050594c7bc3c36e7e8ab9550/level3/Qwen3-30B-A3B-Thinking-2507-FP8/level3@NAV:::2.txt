

This paper proposes a novel framework for density-ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, leading to two new algorithms (Type I and Type II) based on regularization with RKHS norms. The authors establish a principled connection between density-ratio estimation and inverse problems in operator theory, which enables reliable unsupervised parameter selection via their proposed CD-CV method. The key theoretical contribution is demonstrating that the L² formulation allows for meaningful cross-validation, addressing a critical limitation in existing methods like KMM where RKHS objective values are kernel-dependent and thus unsuitable for CV.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical rigor and practical contribution. The authors provide detailed convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and the CD-CV method is well-motivated and experimentally validated across multiple datasets. The comparison to KMM and LSIF demonstrates clear advantages in parameter selection without requiring labeled data, which is a significant practical benefit.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. The convergence rate in Theorem 1 (O(t + λ^{1/2} + ...)) appears slow due to the (-t/log λ) factor, and the authors should investigate whether stronger assumptions (e.g., spectral conditions from Caponnetto & De Vito 2007) could yield polynomial rates with fixed t. The paper also makes a strong assumption that q/p lies in a smooth Sobolev space, but fails to discuss the practical consequence that p/q may behave poorly in applications. The supplementary material contains multiple typos and errors (e.g., Lemma 5 should have W_s^2 instead of W_2^2, and specific line corrections are needed), which significantly hinder verification of theoretical results.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution with clear significance for unsupervised learning applications. The novel CD-CV approach addresses a genuine problem in density-ratio estimation, and the theoretical framework is well-developed. With the requested revisions to the supplementary material and deeper exploration of convergence rate assumptions, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
