This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two densities, q/p, a crucial task in areas like importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The central idea is to reframe this estimation task as a classical inverse problem. Specifically, the authors construct a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind where the density ratio is the unknown function. This inverse problem is then solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, leading to a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). This approach connects the problem to classical analysis and provides a principled way to derive kernel-based algorithms. The work relates to prior methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, but distinguishes itself by its direct inverse problem formulation and the associated regularization scheme.

The primary strength of the paper is its novel and elegant formulation. Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm problem provides a clear and principled foundation, from which simple, kernel-based algorithms naturally emerge. The theoretical analysis, which includes convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, is a significant contribution and adds rigor to the proposed method. Furthermore, the introduction of Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) for unsupervised model selection is a strong practical contribution, addressing a notoriously difficult aspect of this problem.

However, the paper's theoretical foundation rests on several strong and potentially problematic assumptions that are not adequately addressed. The core assumption that the measures admit densities p and q with respect to a dominating measure ν is not always valid in machine learning contexts, where distributions may be supported on lower-dimensional manifolds or be mutually singular. This raises concerns about the well-posedness of estimating the ratio q/p itself. The paper also fails to specify the dominating measure ν, which is critical for the interpretation and validity of the integrals. A further concern is the lack of an explicit assumption on the positivity of the density p. If p(x) can be zero on a set of non-zero measure, the ratio is ill-defined, potentially invalidating the derived results. While the problem formulation is original and the results are promising, these foundational ambiguities weaken the paper's claims of theoretical soundness. Clarifying these modeling assumptions is essential for the work to be a complete and rigorous contribution.
