This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation. The authors introduce a principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically, using techniques from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces. The resulting family of algorithms, called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), is flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

The paper relates to previous work on density ratio estimation, including Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). However, the novelty of the approach lies in the integral-equation reformulation, which allows for a more principled and flexible framework for density ratio estimation.

The paper is well-written and addresses an important and interesting problem. The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel. They also propose a completely unsupervised technique, CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), for model selection and demonstrate its usefulness.

However, there are some missing details and unclear points in the paper. For example, the authors state that ∫ kt(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) without providing a proof or reference. Additionally, the role of bandwidth in the proposed method is not clearly explained, particularly in comparison to traditional kernel density estimation.

The authors also mention that a naive plug-in approach (estimating p and q separately) is inferior to the proposed method, but they do not provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. Furthermore, the empirical and theoretical comparison to prior methods, such as KMM and LSIF, is not clearly explained, and the authors do not provide a minimax-optimality discussion or a comparison of the rates achieved by their method to the optimal rates.

Finally, the authors do not provide references for the datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS) and do not include toy experiments to illustrate the method's performance and quality.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the authors demonstrate that their method is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

However, the originality of the paper is not entirely clear, as the authors build upon existing work on density ratio estimation and regularization. The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses an important problem in machine learning and provides a novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with some revisions to address the missing details and unclear points mentioned above.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation.
* The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis and experimental results.
* The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks some details and explanations, such as the proof for ∫ kt(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) and the role of bandwidth.
* The comparison to prior methods is not clearly explained.
* The authors do not provide references for the datasets used in the experiments.
* The paper could benefit from toy experiments to illustrate the method's performance and quality.
