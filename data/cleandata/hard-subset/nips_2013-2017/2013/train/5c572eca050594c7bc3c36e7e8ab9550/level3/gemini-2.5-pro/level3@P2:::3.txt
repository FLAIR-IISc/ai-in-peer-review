This paper proposes a new framework for estimating the ratio of two densities, q/p, a quantity of significant interest in importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and other areas of statistical inference. The central idea is to reframe this estimation problem as a classical Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. By applying a kernel operator K to the unknown ratio f = q/p, the authors derive two main integral equalities. The first (Type I) relates the application of the operator under measure p to the unknown ratio, Kp(q/p), to an expectation under measure q, Kq(1). The second (Type II) leverages properties of local kernels (e.g., Gaussian) to state that Kt,p(q/p) is approximately equal to q.

Solving these integral equations for q/p is an ill-posed inverse problem. The authors propose using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework to find a stable solution. This approach leads to a family of simple and flexible algorithms, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). The paper presents detailed theoretical analysis for the FIRE algorithms, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates for the case of Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. A key practical contribution is a fully unsupervised model selection procedure called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). The paper benchmarks FIRE against related methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF), demonstrating strong empirical performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Formulation:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is novel and elegant. It establishes a strong connection between a common machine learning task and the classical theory of integral operators and regularization. This principled approach provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms and their analysis.

2.  **Theoretical Depth:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed FIRE algorithm. The derivation of concentration bounds and convergence rates in Section 3 is a significant contribution, lending strong theoretical support to the method. The analysis covers both the standard Euclidean case and the more complex setting of distributions on sub-manifolds.

3.  **Practicality and Algorithm Design:** The regularization framework naturally leads to simple, closed-form solutions within the standard kernel methods paradigm. The resulting FIRE algorithms are easy to implement. Furthermore, the proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is a valuable practical contribution, addressing a critical and often difficult aspect of applying such methods.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It effectively introduces the problem, develops the theoretical framework, derives the algorithms, and presents a thorough experimental evaluation.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

The primary weaknesses of the paper lie in its foundational assumptions, which are not stated with sufficient rigor and may limit the generality of the claims.

1.  **Assumptions on Densities:** The entire framework is predicated on the existence of density functions p and q. This is a strong assumption in machine learning, where probability measures may not be absolutely continuous with respect to a standard reference measure (like the Lebesgue measure). For example, data could lie on a lower-dimensional manifold, or the distributions could have singular components. The validity of the approach in such common scenarios is unclear.

2.  **Ambiguity of the Dominating Measure:** The paper does not explicitly state the dominating measure ν with respect to which p and q are densities. The choice of ν (e.g., Lebesgue measure on R^d, counting measure on a discrete set) is fundamental to the definition of a density and the validity of the integral equations. This omission makes the theoretical setup ambiguous. For instance, statements and results are only defined up to sets of ν-measure zero, a detail that warrants explicit mention and discussion.

3.  **Implicit Assumption on Support:** The estimation of the ratio q(x)/p(x) is ill-defined if p(x) = 0 for some x where q(x) > 0. The paper does not explicitly state the assumption that the support of q must be contained within the support of p. This is a critical prerequisite for the problem to be well-posed. The authors should clarify this assumption and discuss how the algorithm behaves in practice when this condition is violated (e.g., when p(x) is very close to zero).

4.  **Clarity of Initial Setup:** While the paper is generally clear, the initial problem statement could be strengthened by formally stating all modeling assumptions upfront. This includes the domain of x, the choice of dominating measure, and the required properties of p and q (e.g., smoothness, support). Currently, some of these details are only revealed later in the theoretical analysis section.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper introduces a highly novel and principled connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems. This framework is backed by strong theoretical analysis (convergence rates) and leads to simple, effective algorithms. The proposed unsupervised model selection technique is a significant practical advantage.

*   **Arguments Against Acceptance:** The theoretical framework rests on strong assumptions (existence of densities) that are not always met in practical machine learning settings. The lack of explicit statements regarding the dominating measure and the relative supports of the distributions introduces ambiguity and questions the rigor of the formulation.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in terms of its novel formulation, theoretical analysis, and practical algorithm. The strengths comfortably outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the clarity and rigor of the underlying assumptions rather than a flaw in the core methodology. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version of the paper. Explicitly stating and justifying the foundational assumptions would significantly strengthen the work and clarify its scope of applicability.
