### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel framework for estimating the density ratio *q*/*p*, a quantity of significant importance in areas like importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The core idea is to reformulate the density ratio estimation problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This reformulation allows the authors to leverage the classical theory of inverse problems and apply Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework.

The authors propose two main settings. "Type I" applies when samples from both distributions *p* and *q* are available, using the identity `∫ k(x, y) (q(y)/p(y)) p(y) dy = ∫ k(x, y) q(y) dy`. "Type II" applies when samples from *p* are available and the function *q* can be evaluated directly, using the approximation `∫ k_t(x, y) (q(y)/p(y)) p(y) dy ≈ q(x)`. The resulting algorithms are termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). The paper provides theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels and introduces a cross-validation method (CD-CV) for model selection. This work connects to previous methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF) but distinguishes itself through its direct inverse problem formulation.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its elegant and novel reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation. This provides a principled and theoretically grounded perspective on a problem of significant practical importance in machine learning. The connection to the classical theory of inverse problems is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is very well-written and clearly articulates its main ideas. The derivation of the FIRE algorithms from the integral equation formulation is straightforward and easy to follow. The framework is flexible, allowing for different norms and regularization schemes, leading to simple and implementable kernel-based algorithms.

3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for both the Type I and Type II settings. Analyzing performance for distributions on both Euclidean space and sub-manifolds is a strong theoretical contribution.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are several areas where clarity and completeness could be improved.

1.  **Missing Theoretical Details:** The paper makes several key statements without adequate justification. For example, the approximation `∫ k_t(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)`, which is fundamental to the Type II formulation, is presented without proof or a precise reference. The authors should include a brief justification or cite a specific theorem to make the theoretical development self-contained and verifiable.

2.  **Need for Conceptual Discussion (Bandwidth Role):** The Type II setting with *q*=1 presents an interesting special case for estimating 1/*p*, which is a form of density estimation. It would be highly insightful if the authors discussed how the role of the kernel bandwidth `t` in their framework differs from its role in standard Kernel Density Estimation (KDE). In KDE, consistency requires the bandwidth to approach zero, whereas the analysis here seems to treat `t` as a fixed parameter in the rate calculations. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the paper's contribution to density estimation literature.

3.  **Accessibility (Tikhonov Regularization):** For the broader NIPS audience who may not be experts in inverse problems, a brief, one-sentence explanation of Tikhonov regularization would improve the paper's accessibility. Mentioning that it is a standard method for stabilizing the solution of ill-posed problems (like inverting a compact operator) by adding a penalty on the solution's norm would be sufficient.

4.  **Motivation vs. Naive Methods:** The paper would benefit from a more explicit motivation for why a direct ratio estimation method is superior to a naive "plug-in" approach (i.e., estimating *p̂* and *q̂* separately via KDE and computing the ratio *q̂*/*p̂*). The naive method is known to be unstable, particularly where *p*(x) is small, leading to error amplification. Explicitly stating that the proposed regularized inverse formulation is designed to avoid this instability would better position the work.

5.  **Context of Theoretical Rates:** The convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 are a key result, but they are presented without context. A discussion on how these rates compare to the known minimax-optimal rates for density ratio estimation (e.g., as studied in [14]) is needed. This would allow readers to understand how close to optimal the proposed method is and would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical claims.

6.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The experiments demonstrate the method works, but they could be more illustrative. The inclusion of a simple 1D toy experiment, with plots showing the true *p*, *q*, the true ratio, and the FIRE estimate, would provide valuable visual intuition about the method's performance and behavior.
    *   The empirical comparison to prior work is limited. A more direct comparison with KMM (even with the out-of-sample extension caveat) would be informative.
    *   For reproducibility, citations should be provided for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

7.  **Proofs in Supplement:** All proofs are located in the supplementary material and have not been checked by this reviewer.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a very nice paper that introduces a novel and principled approach to the important problem of density ratio estimation. The reformulation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and the resulting algorithms are simple and well-grounded. The main weaknesses are in the lack of some key theoretical justifications and a limited experimental and theoretical comparison to the state-of-the-art. Addressing the points above would significantly strengthen the manuscript. The paper is a solid contribution and is recommended for acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors incorporate the feedback to improve the final version.
