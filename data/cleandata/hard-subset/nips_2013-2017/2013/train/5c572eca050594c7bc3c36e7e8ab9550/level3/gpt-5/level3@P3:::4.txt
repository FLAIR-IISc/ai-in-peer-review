Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes FIRE, a family of kernel-based estimators for the Radon–Nikodym derivative f = q/p framed as solving Fredholm integral equations of the first kind. Two formulations are derived:
(i) Kp f = Kq 1, obtained by setting h(·) = k(·,x) in Eq[h] identities, and
(ii) Kt,p f ≈ q, using δ-like kernels (e.g., Gaussian with bandwidth t) so the integral operator approximates pointwise evaluation.
Both are regularized via RKHS penalties, admit closed-form solutions through representer theorems, and use empirical approximations of integral operators from samples of p (and optionally q). The authors analyze concentration and convergence under boundedness and Sobolev smoothness, giving rates for Euclidean domains and manifolds. They also propose an unsupervised model-selection scheme, CD-CV, that cross-validates by testing importance-sampling identities on random test functions U. Experiments compare to LSIF, KMM (discussion), and TIKDE.

This connects to covariate shift and direct density-ratio estimation (LSIF/KLIEP), kernel mean matching (KMM), and operator-theoretic views of learning (e.g., Tikhonov regularization in RKHS, conditional mean embeddings). The KMM identity Eq[Φ] = Ep[f Φ] is shown to be equivalent to (i), situating KMM as a special case but with different optimization and no explicit regularizer. Compared to LSIF/KLIEP, FIRE does not require the ratio to be represented in a finite basis; instead it solves an inverse problem with RKHS regularization, leveraging spectral/operator tools.

Quality:
Technically sound with careful derivations, closed-form solutions, and finite-sample bounds. The rates clearly expose dependence on bandwidth, regularization, and dimension, and the manifold case is a nice addition. The empirical study demonstrates practical viability and the CD-CV model-selection idea is elegant and useful.

Clarity:
Overall clear and well organized. The operator notation, assumptions, and empirical algorithms are described in sufficient detail to reproduce. A few choices (losses and validation families U) would benefit from more motivation (see questions).

Originality:
Casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem and unifying operator identities (i) and (ii) in an RKHS-regularized framework is a novel and fruitful perspective, distinct yet connected to KMM and LSIF.

Significance:
Density-ratio estimation is central to covariate shift and importance sampling. The operator-theoretic formulation, convergence guarantees on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and the practical CD-CV selection contribute meaningful advances likely to be reused.

Strengths:
- Clean operator formulation leading to two estimators and closed-form kernel algorithms.
- Theoretical guarantees with explicit dimension dependence; includes manifold support.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) leveraging test-function identities.
- Clear relationship to KMM, LSIF; out-of-sample extension comes for free.
- Experiments show strong performance and stability with regularization.

Weaknesses / points needing clarification:
- Intuition for why optimizing over {k(x,·)} suffices in (i) is under-explained relative to the bandwidth intuition in (ii).
- The mixed loss in Eq. (9) blends L2,p and L2,q without a clear application-driven rationale.
- Importance sampling motivation: when both Ep and Eq are empirically estimated to form the cost, in what scenarios is estimating q/p preferable to directly estimating Eq[u]?
- Smoothness/regularizer: more discussion is needed on how smoothness of p and q translates to smoothness of q/p (e.g., p bounded away from 0).
- Practical dimensionality: the rates degrade with d; more guidance on useful regimes would help.
- Choice of validation test functions U for CD-CV needs motivation and sensitivity analysis.
- Relation to “Smooth Operators” (Grünewälder et al.) and conditional mean embeddings could be deepened to position FIRE as a general approach for covariate shift.

Requests and detailed comments:
- Intuition for (i) with test functions {k(x,·)}: Because k is a reproducing kernel, {k(x,·)} spans a dense subset of H. The identity Kp f = Kq 1 means that for all x, ⟨k(x,·), Kp f − Kq 1⟩H = 0, i.e., the residual is orthogonal to the span of all representers. If k is characteristic (mean embedding injective) and if Kp is a compact, injective operator on H with appropriate source conditions, then matching against all k(x,·) pins down Kp f uniquely; with regularization and consistent empirical approximations of Kp and Kq, f converges to q/p. This mirrors (ii): instead of shrinking t→0 to localize, (i) uses the RKHS representers as a rich test set and relies on the injectivity of the kernel mean map and stability from Tikhonov regularization.

- Mixed loss in Eq. (9): Please explain when a γ-weighted mix of L2,p and L2,q is preferable. Potential motivations: (a) downstream risk under q but with weights learned from p-samples—trading variance when q’s sample size m is small; (b) semi-supervised scenarios where labels are under p but target generalization under q; (c) robustness to sample-size imbalance via importance of both domains. Clarify how γ should be set (e.g., by CD-CV) and link to specific applications.

- Importance sampling vs direct Eq estimation: If one only needs Eq[u] for a fixed u and has q-samples, a direct Monte Carlo estimate is natural. Estimating q/p is useful when: (a) one must reweight many functions u (e.g., training losses), including future, unseen u; (b) labels or simulators are available under p but not q (covariate shift); (c) one needs out-of-sample weights for new x; (d) MCMC/unnormalized densities where q is known up to a constant but sampling from q is costly. Please make these scenarios explicit and discuss when the overhead of estimating q/p pays off empirically.

- Regularization and smoothness: Please discuss conditions under which q/p is smooth if p and q are smooth. For example, if p,q ∈ W2
s(Ω), p is bounded away from 0 and ∇p is bounded, then q/p inherits (most of) the smoothness of q moderated by 1/p, with potential loss near small p. Explicitly state assumptions like p ≥ c > 0 on support of q, and how they enter the source conditions in your rates.

- Dimensionality: Your bounds have factors like λ−a t−d/2 /√n, revealing a curse of dimensionality. Provide practitioner guidance: rough ranges of d and n for which FIRE is competitive (e.g., d ≤ 10–20 with manifold or low-intrinsic-d structure; larger d needs strong smoothness/low-noise or manifold structure plus careful t scaling). Empirical sensitivity plots over d would help.

- Test functions U in CD-CV: Please justify the choices (random linear and half-space indicators). Why are these sufficient to detect mismatch in the importance identity, and how sensitive is CD-CV to U? Consider adding ablations with alternative U (e.g., random Fourier features, localized kernels) and a discussion linking CD-CV to MMD: JCD is essentially matching expectations against a test family, akin to using a kernel witness function.

- Is {k(x,·)} the right general approach? Position FIRE relative to “Smooth Operators” and conditional mean embedding regression. Those works also invert smoothed operators in RKHS to recover conditional objects; here you invert Kp to recover q/p. Please discuss when operator inversion with RKHS Tikhonov is preferable to direct finite-basis LSIF/KLIEP, and whether other operator choices or spectral regularizers (e.g., early stopping, spectral filtering) could offer better bias–variance trade-offs.

- Practicalities: (a) Nonnegativity and normalization of f: do you enforce/encourage positivity or integral constraints? (b) Computational complexity: O(n3) linear algebra—any Nyström/iterative solvers used? (c) Choice of kernels for K (integral operator) vs kH (regularizer): guidance and empirical comparison.

Pros:
- Conceptually elegant inverse-problem framing; principled regularization.
- Solid theory including manifold case; clear, implementable algorithms.
- Useful, unsupervised model selection; good empirical results.

Cons:
- Limited discussion of when to prefer (i) vs (ii) in practice.
- Dimension dependence and smoothness assumptions deserve deeper practical guidance.
- Motivation for mixed losses and validation test families could be strengthened.

Overall assessment: Strong paper—well executed, timely, and likely to influence subsequent work on density-ratio estimation and covariate shift. I recommend acceptance.
