- Paper reframes density ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation solved with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form kernel solutions (FIRE) for two settings (operator matching and deconvolution).
- Provides nonasymptotic concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth manifolds.
- Proposes an unsupervised model selection scheme (CD-CV) that chooses kernel width and regularization by enforcing importance sampling identities on held-out test functions.
- Clarifies connections to prior methods (KMM, LSIF/KLIEP) and inverse-problem RKHS views, emphasizing explicit out-of-sample extension and spectral regularization differences.
- Strength: Conceptual clarity—casting density-ratio estimation as an inverse problem connects to operator theory and regularization.
- Strength: Algorithmic simplicity—representer theorem gives simple closed-form solutions using standard Gram matrices, easy to implement for both Type I and II.
- Strength: Theory—provides meaningful finite-sample bounds and rates using concentration and spectral techniques.
- Strength: Practical model selection—CD-CV is a simple unsupervised criterion useful when labels are scarce.
- Strength: Empirical results—competitive performance and improved stability vs baselines; fair discussion of KMM’s out-of-sample limitations.
- Weakness: Lacks explicit specification of a σ-finite dominating measure ν or formal statement that p,q are densities w.r.t. ν; should emphasize Radon–Nikodym derivative dq/dp and q ≪ p.
- Weakness: Positivity/support issues—many expressions implicitly require p(x)>0; paper must clarify estimation domain (supp(p)), small-p pathologies, and when r is undefined/infinite.
- Weakness: Measure-zero/operator-domain ambiguity—equalities and integral expressions should be stated p-a.e. or in the appropriate dual/RKHS sense, and manifold integrals tied to Riemannian measure.
- Weakness: Modeling assumptions scattered—domain, measure, regularity classes, and kernel normalization (δ-family) should be consolidated early and precisely.
- Weakness: Practical scope—many ML settings violate q ≪ p (support mismatch); paper should discuss failure modes and adaptations (support detection, clipping, bounded r).
- Weakness: Scalability—not addressed: inverting n×n kernel matrices is costly; no discussion of Nyström, random features, or iterative solvers and their theoretical impact.
- Suggestions: State a dominating measure ν up front or explicitly restrict to q ≪ p and p-a.e. equalities; clarify where errors are measured and where r is defined.
- Suggestions: Either assume p≥c>0 on domain or restrict to supp(p), and discuss regularization/thresholding for small p and implications for bounds.
- Suggestions: Formalize the manifold case using the Riemannian measure and intrinsic dimension; align kernel normalization with heat-kernel approximations.
- Suggestions: Give guidance on CD-CV’s test function family (e.g., MMD features or randomized families) and required moment conditions for stability.
- Suggestions: Add a paragraph on computational cost and practical approximations (Nyström, random features, conjugate gradients) and how they affect theory.
- Suggestions: Add experiments demonstrating behavior when q is not absolutely continuous w.r.t. p, or propose clipped/robust variants to handle support mismatch.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled inverse-problem framing, simple practical algorithms with out-of-sample extension, solid theoretical rates, and useful unsupervised model selection.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient measure-theoretic rigor (dominating measure, q ≪ p), inadequate treatment of support mismatch and small-p pathologies, and missing scalability discussion.
- Overall: Promising and thoughtful contribution that would be stronger with tightened measure-theoretic assumptions, clearer modeling statements, a discussion of practical limitations, and scalability considerations.
