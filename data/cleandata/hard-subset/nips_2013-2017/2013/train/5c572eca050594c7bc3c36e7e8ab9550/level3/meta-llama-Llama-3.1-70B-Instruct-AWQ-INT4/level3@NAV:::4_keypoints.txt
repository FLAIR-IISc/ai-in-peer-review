Strengths:
1. Clear introduction to density ratio estimation and its importance.
2. Novel, well-motivated reformulation via the Fredholm equation of the first kind.
3. Detailed theoretical analysis including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel.
4. Empirical effectiveness demonstrated on regression and classification tasks.
5. Well-written with clear, concise explanations of methods and results.

Weaknesses:
1. Lacks an intuitive explanation for why optimizing over the test functions {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in equality (i).
2. Choice of cost function (mixing L2,p and L2,q in eq. (9)) is not fully justified.
3. Insufficient discussion of how the smoothness of p and q relates to the smoothness of q/p.
4. Limited insight into dependence on dimension d and practical dimensionality limits.
5. Needs a more detailed comparison with prior work such as "Smooth Operators" (Grunewalder et al.).

Originality:
1. Introduces a novel Fredholm-equation-based approach to density ratio estimation.
2. Combines kernel methods and regularization into a flexible, principled framework.
3. Provides thorough theoretical analysis (bounds and rates) for the proposed family of algorithms.

Significance:
1. Tackles a fundamental problem in statistical inference and machine learning.
2. Approach has potential impact on applications like importance sampling, covariate shift, and transfer learning.
3. Empirical results indicate practical usefulness across varied tasks.

Clarity:
1. Paper is well-written and explanations are clear.
2. Organization is good and the structure is easy to follow.
3. Provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce results.

Quality:
1. Technically sound with clear presentation of methods.
2. Claims are supported by both theoretical analysis and experiments.
3. Paper is complete and presents conclusions and future directions.

Arguments for acceptance:
1. Presents a novel Fredholm-based method for density ratio estimation.
2. Supplies detailed theoretical guarantees including concentration bounds and convergence rates.
3. Demonstrates empirical effectiveness on multiple datasets and tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:
1. Missing intuitive justification for convergence when optimizing over {k(x,·)}.
2. Insufficient motivation for the mixed L2,p and L2,q cost choice.
3. Lacks detailed discussion on smoothness relationships between p, q, and q/p.
