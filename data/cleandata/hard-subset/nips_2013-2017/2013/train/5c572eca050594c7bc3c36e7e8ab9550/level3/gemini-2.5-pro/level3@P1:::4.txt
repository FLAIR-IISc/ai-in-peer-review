This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio `q/p`, also known as the importance function. This is a critical task in settings such as importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation in transfer learning. The authors' central and novel contribution is to reframe this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind.

Two main formulations are derived. The "Type I" setting is based on the identity `Ep[k(x,·) * (q/p)] = Eq[k(x,·)]`, where `k` is a kernel function. This is useful when samples from both distributions `p` and `q` are available. The "Type II" setting uses the approximation `Ep[k_t(x,·) * (q/p)] ≈ q(x)` for a delta-like kernel `k_t`, which is applicable when `q` is a known function but sampling from it may be difficult. To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the authors apply Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to a family of simple and elegant algorithms, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), with closed-form solutions involving kernel matrix inversions.

The paper further provides a detailed theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case, deriving concentration bounds and convergence rates for distributions on R^d and on smooth sub-manifolds. A significant practical contribution is the proposal of a completely unsupervised model selection method, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to evaluate the quality of the estimated ratio. The experimental section demonstrates the effectiveness of FIRE and CD-CV, comparing them favorably to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Framework:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant, novel, and principled. It connects a modern machine learning problem to a well-established area of classical analysis, providing new tools and perspectives. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rates under standard smoothness assumptions. The analysis covers both Euclidean and manifold settings, which is comprehensive. These results provide strong theoretical backing for the proposed algorithms.
3.  **Practicality and Completeness:** The work is a complete package. It introduces a new framework, derives practical algorithms (FIRE), provides theory, addresses the crucial issue of model selection with a novel unsupervised method (CD-CV), and presents solid experimental validation on multiple datasets and tasks. The resulting algorithms are simple to implement.
4.  **Significance:** The problem of covariate shift and importance weighting is of broad interest in the NIPS community. By providing a flexible, well-analyzed, and effective new method, this work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. The CD-CV method, in particular, is a valuable contribution to the difficult problem of hyperparameter tuning in unsupervised settings.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The paper is of high quality, and the weaknesses are primarily areas where clarity or discussion could be improved rather than fundamental flaws.

1.  **Intuition for Type I Convergence:** For the Type II formulation, the intuition is clear: the operator `K_{t,p}` acts as a smoothed version of the identity, so `K_{t,p} f ≈ f`. For the Type I setting, the intuition is less direct. While `Ep[k(x,·) f] = Eq[k(x,·)]` is a valid identity if `f = q/p`, it is not immediately obvious why minimizing a norm of the difference `||K_p f - K_q 1||` should force `f` to converge to `q/p`. An intuitive argument for why matching the distributions in the feature space of the kernel `k` is sufficient would be helpful. Is this guaranteed for any "rich enough" kernel, and what does that mean formally?

2.  **Motivation for Mixed Norm:** In Section 2 (Eq. 9), the authors propose a loss function that is a mixture of the L2,p and L2,q norms. While technically sound, the motivation for this specific combination is not fully elaborated. What are the practical scenarios or theoretical advantages that would lead one to prefer this mixed norm over the simpler L2,p norm?

3.  **Application Context for Importance Sampling:** A primary motivation is importance sampling, where one wants to compute `Eq[h]` using samples from `p`. However, the Type I setting requires samples from `q` to construct the empirical objective function. If samples from `q` are available, one can directly estimate `Eq[h]` via a simple Monte Carlo average. Could the authors clarify the specific applications where estimating the ratio `q/p` is still necessary or advantageous even when samples from `q` are present? (e.g., variance reduction, or cases where the `q` sample is small).

4.  **Smoothness Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions on the ratio `q/p`. It would be beneficial to include a discussion on how these assumptions on the ratio relate to the properties of the individual densities `p` and `q`. For example, what conditions on `p` and `q` (e.g., smoothness, `p` being bounded away from zero on the support of `q`) are sufficient to guarantee that `q/p` is in the required Sobolev space?

5.  **Dependence on Dimension:** The derived convergence rates exhibit a strong, and typical, dependence on the ambient dimension `d`. This is a critical aspect for practitioners. A brief discussion on the practical implications of this dependency would be valuable. For what range of dimensions `d` is the method expected to be practically useful, given a reasonable number of samples?

6.  **Justification for CD-CV Test Functions:** The CD-CV method relies on a set of test functions `U`. The experiments use random linear functions and half-space indicators. This choice seems somewhat arbitrary. Could the authors provide more justification for this choice and comment on how sensitive the performance of CD-CV is to the family of test functions used?

7.  **Relation to "Smooth Operators":** The core idea of using integral operators to characterize properties of a distribution is related to prior work. For instance, Grünewälder et al. ("Smooth Operators," ICML 2012) use a similar philosophy for different ends. A brief discussion situating the Fredholm approach within this broader context of "learning with operators" could help clarify its unique contributions.

In summary, this is a well-executed and timely paper that presents a novel and powerful framework for density ratio estimation. The combination of a principled formulation, solid theory, and practical algorithms with a novel model selection technique makes it a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance. Addressing the points above would further strengthen an already excellent manuscript.
