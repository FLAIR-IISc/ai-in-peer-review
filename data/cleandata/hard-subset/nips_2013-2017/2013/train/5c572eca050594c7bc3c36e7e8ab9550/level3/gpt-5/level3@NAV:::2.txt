Summary: The paper recasts density-ratio estimation r = q/p as a Fredholm first-kind inverse problem and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. It proposes two estimators: Type I, which fits Kp r ≈ Kq 1 in L2(p), and Type II, which uses a localized kernel kt with t → 0 to fit Kt,p r ≈ q in L2(p). This yields simple closed-form kernel methods (FIRE) and a practical, unsupervised model-selection procedure, CD-CV, based on L2(p) criteria computable from samples of p and q. The work is related to KMM (Gretton et al.) and LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.) but differs in its inverse-problem formulation and in advocating L2-based CV, arguing that RKHS-objective values (as in KMM) are kernel-dependent and not comparable across kernels. The analysis provides concentration bounds and convergence rates on Euclidean domains and smooth manifolds.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound, with clear operator-theoretic grounding and representer-theorem derivations. The experiments convincingly support the CD-CV advantage for parameter selection. However, the stated convergence in Theorem 1 appears slow due to the (-t/log λ) coupling (as used in the supplement), raising the question of whether polynomial-in-n rates are attainable with fixed t under stronger source/capacity assumptions. Please clarify the rate-driving terms and consider adopting spectral assumptions à la Caponnetto & De Vito (2007) or stronger smoothness/regularity on q/p to obtain polynomial rates.

Clarity: The main paper is generally clear and well organized. The supplementary material, however, contains numerous typos that impede verification. See specific corrections below.

Originality: Framing density-ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem with two RKHS-regularized variants is a neat synthesis. The key practical novelty is the L2 formulation enabling reliable, fully unsupervised CV for both kernel and regularization parameters—an important improvement over RKHS-objective methods like KMM for model selection.

Significance: Density-ratio estimation underlies importance sampling and covariate shift; a robust, CV-friendly method is valuable and likely to be adopted. The Type II formulation’s ability to handle known q (not necessarily a density) is also practically useful.

Concerns and suggestions:
- Assumptions: The paper assumes q/p lies in a smooth class (e.g., Sobolev). Please discuss when this is realistic and the practical limitation that, under this assumption, p/q may be poorly behaved. Offer guidance or diagnostics for practitioners.
- Rates: Can polynomial rates be derived under standard source and effective-dimension conditions or under additional structure on q/p?
- KMM comparison: Empirically demonstrate that RKHS-objective values are not comparable across kernels for CV, reinforcing the motivation for L2-based CD-CV.

Specific supplementary corrections:
- Lemma 5: W2^2 should be Ws^2; the bound should read D1 (t/−log λ)^2 + λ^{1−α} D2 ||f||_2^2.
- Lines 675 and 686 are incorrect; Eq. (31) is correct.
- Line 1147: the integral domain should be “||ξ||^2 ≥”.

Pros:
- Elegant inverse-problem formulation with two practical variants.
- Strong case for L2-based unsupervised CV; clear empirical benefits.
- Solid, relevant theoretical development.

Cons:
- Conservative convergence dependence; unclear path to faster rates.
- Strong smoothness assumptions with limited discussion of implications.
- Supplement quality issues hinder reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing rate clarification and fixing the supplement.
