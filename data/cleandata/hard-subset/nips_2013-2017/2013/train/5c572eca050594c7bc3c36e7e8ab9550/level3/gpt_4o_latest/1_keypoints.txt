- Proposes FIRE: reformulates density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and solves via Tikhonov regularization in RKHS; introduces CD-CV for unsupervised model selection.
- Strength: Novelty — connects operator theory with ML by a new Fredholm-based formulation for density ratio estimation.
- Strength: Theoretical rigor — provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Strength: Practical contributions — FIRE is simple, flexible, computationally efficient, and CD-CV addresses unsupervised parameter selection.
- Strength: Experimental validation — empirical comparisons with LSIF and TIKDE show strong performance across multiple datasets.
- Weakness: Missing proofs and references — key theoretical proofs are only in the supplement and some mathematical statements lack citations or justification.
- Weakness: Clarity and accessibility — paper assumes advanced background (Tikhonov, Fredholm) and would benefit from brief explanatory material.
- Weakness: Comparative analysis insufficient — lacks detailed comparisons (e.g., minimax bounds) and justification why naive plug-in approaches are inferior.
- Weakness: Practical demonstrations limited — no experiments on toy problems with known p and q to assess accuracy in controlled settings.
- Weakness: Dataset references missing — CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS not adequately referenced, hindering reproducibility.
- Recommendation: Conditional acceptance — promising and relevant but requires revisions to improve clarity, proofs, comparisons, and demos.
- Required revisions: add missing proofs/references; include brief explanations of Tikhonov regularization and Fredholm integral equations.
- Required revisions: clarify limitations of naive plug-in methods and expand comparative performance analysis.
- Required revisions: include experiments on toy problems with known densities and provide dataset references.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded approach with strong empirical results and practical utility for semi-supervised learning.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing proofs, insufficient comparative analysis, limited accessibility for non-experts, and lack of controlled demonstrations.
