- Proposes a kernel-based Fredholm integral equation formulation for density-ratio estimation, solved by Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form FIRE algorithms for two settings and a CD-CV tuning heuristic.  
- Connects and unifies prior work on importance weighting, KMM/LSIF, RKHS inverse problems, and manifold learning via a Fredholm inverse-problem perspective.  
- Technical derivations and RKHS-regularized estimators are standard and well-posed; theory gives finite-sample deviation bounds and convergence rates.  
- Key measure-theoretic and modeling assumptions are underspecified (existence of densities, unspecified dominating measure ν), creating ambiguity in L2/ a.e. statements.  
- The method implicitly assumes absolute continuity Q ≪ P and p>0 on supp(Q); these assumptions are often violated under covariate shift (support mismatch) and are not addressed.  
- Positivity/nonnegativity of estimated weights w is not enforced or discussed, risking instability in importance weighting.  
- Domain specification is inconsistent (Ω ⊂ R^d vs. manifold); topology, compactness, and measure assumptions needed for operator/RKHS results are not stated up front.  
- Proofs use strong regularity assumptions (bounded densities, Sobolev regularity of q/p and q/p^2); source/qualification conditions for compact operators should be articulated.  
- Notation is overloaded (integral kernel k vs. RKHS kernel k_H) and sometimes ambiguous regarding when they coincide and their properties.  
- CD-CV is a clever, practical unsupervised model-selection method and experiments show competitive, stable empirical performance.  
- Experiments do not probe critical failure modes for importance weighting (support mismatch, near-zero p, singular Q) where theoretical assumptions break.  
- Rates and exponents should be compared to known minimax results (e.g., Nguyen et al.), and choices of t and λ justified under smoothness classes.  
- Computational complexity and numerical stability (conditioning of kernel matrices and regularized systems) are not sufficiently discussed or analyzed.  
- Strengths: principled inverse-problem formulation, clear connection to KMM, out-of-sample estimation, effective CD-CV tuning, nontrivial theory on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and promising empirical efficiency.  
- Weaknesses/requests: specify dominating measure ν and measure-theoretic setup; state Q ≪ P and p≥c>0 on supp(Q) or analyze violations; discuss nonnegativity constraints and clipping; clarify k vs k_H and all a.e. equalities; test support-mismatch scenarios experimentally.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel unifying perspective, useful CD-CV method, solid empirical results and accessible algorithms.  
- Cons for acceptance: foundational gaps (ν, absolute continuity, positivity), lack of treatment for support mismatch, and some theoretical assumptions misaligned with experiments.  
- Recommendation: Borderline — support acceptance conditional on revisions that (i) recast development measure-theoretically with explicit assumptions (Q ≪ P, ν, a.e. statements), (ii) add positivity/support conditions and nonnegativity discussion, and (iii) include experiments probing support mismatch and near-zero p.
