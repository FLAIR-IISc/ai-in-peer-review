- Proposes reframing density-ratio estimation q/p as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind (Type I and Type II formulations).  
- Type I: Kp(q/p) expressed as an expectation under q; Type II: for local kernels, Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q.  
- Estimation is an ill-posed inverse problem, addressed via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.  
- Introduces FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms with closed-form kernel solutions.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis: concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.  
- Proposes an unsupervised model-selection method, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).  
- Empirical benchmarks against KMM and LSIF show strong performance.  
- Strength: highly original, principled connection between density-ratio estimation and classical integral-operator theory.  
- Strength: substantial theoretical depth, with meaningful convergence and concentration results.  
- Strength: practical algorithm design—simple, implementable closed-form solutions and useful unsupervised parameter selection.  
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation and thorough experiments.  
- Weakness: foundational assumptions (existence of densities) are strong and not stated rigorously, limiting generality.  
- Weakness: paper omits explicit specification of the dominating measure ν, creating ambiguity in definitions and results.  
- Weakness: fails to state the necessary support assumption (support(q) ⊆ support(p)) and lacks discussion of behavior when p≈0.  
- Weakness: initial setup should more formally state domain, dominating measure, and properties required of p and q.  
- For acceptance: novel formulation, strong theory, simple effective algorithms, and useful unsupervised model selection.  
- Against acceptance: reliance on strong/unstated assumptions (densities, dominating measure, support) raises rigor and applicability concerns.  
- Recommendation: accept, but require the authors to explicitly state and justify foundational assumptions and clarify scope.
