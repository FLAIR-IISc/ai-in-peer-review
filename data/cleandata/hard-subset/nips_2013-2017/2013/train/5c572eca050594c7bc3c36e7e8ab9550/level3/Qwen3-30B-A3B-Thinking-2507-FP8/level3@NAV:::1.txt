

This paper proposes a novel density ratio estimation method by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation, introducing the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) framework. The authors connect density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems in operator theory, using Tikhonov regularization within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces. The approach offers theoretical guarantees for convergence rates and introduces a new unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) for density ratio estimation.

The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem with clear connections to related work, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF. The theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds is comprehensive, though the absence of complete proofs (all in supplementary material) limits the reviewer's ability to fully assess the technical merits. A significant strength is the elegant reformulation that provides a principled framework for density ratio estimation.

However, several critical gaps need addressing. The key theoretical claim ∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) requires proper justification with either proof or citation. The special case q=1 (density estimation) merits deeper discussion regarding bandwidth selection compared to KDE, as the paper suggests bandwidth need not approach zero. Tikhonov regularization should be briefly explained for readers unfamiliar with the technique. The paper fails to clearly explain why a naive plug-in approach (estimating p and q separately) is inferior to the proposed method, which is crucial for understanding the novelty.

The empirical comparison to prior methods (e.g., [12]) lacks minimax-optimality discussion, and the distance of Corollaries 2 and 4 from optimal rates remains unclear. Dataset references for CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS are missing. Including toy experiments (e.g., 1D plots of true vs estimated p/q) would greatly enhance the empirical validation.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with strong theoretical foundations and practical contributions. However, the missing theoretical justifications, incomplete empirical comparisons, and lack of illustrative experiments prevent it from being fully convincing. The paper makes an interesting contribution to density ratio estimation but requires significant revisions to address the theoretical and experimental gaps before acceptance.
