This paper presents a novel framework for density-ratio estimation, reformulating the problem as a classical Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This elegant connection allows the authors to leverage the well-established theory of inverse problems. Two distinct methods are proposed: Type I, which is based on the importance sampling identity `K_p(q/p) = K_q 1`, and Type II, which uses a local kernel approximation `K_{t,p}(q/p) ≈ q`. Both inverse problems are solved using Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm penalty, leading to a family of algorithms called Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimators (FIRE).

The proposed approach is related to prior work such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF). The authors clearly position their work relative to these methods, noting that the KMM objective can be seen as a specific instance of their Type I formulation using an RKHS norm as the loss function. However, the key contribution and distinguishing feature of this work is the development of a principled and effective method for unsupervised parameter selection. By using an L^2 objective, the proposed Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) procedure provides a reliable way to select kernel and regularization parameters, a significant advantage over methods like KMM whose RKHS-based objectives are kernel-dependent and thus not directly comparable across different parameter settings.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** Casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is an original and insightful contribution. It provides a principled and flexible framework that connects a common machine learning problem to classical operator theory.

2.  **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The primary strength of this paper is the introduction of the CD-CV method. Parameter tuning is a major challenge in unsupervised density-ratio estimation. The authors provide a compelling argument for why their L^2 formulation is well-suited for cross-validation, unlike RKHS-norm objectives used in prior work. The experimental results validate that this approach works well in practice. This is a significant practical contribution that other researchers and practitioners are likely to adopt.

3.  **Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis with convergence rates for the proposed estimators under specific assumptions. This is complemented by a thorough experimental evaluation on several datasets, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the FIRE algorithms and the CD-CV procedure compared to alternatives.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Convergence Rates:** The theoretical analysis in Theorem 1 provides convergence rates, but these rates appear to be quite slow. This is partly due to the `(-t / log λ)` factor that arises in the analysis. Could the authors achieve polynomial rates with a fixed `t` under stronger, yet reasonable, assumptions? For instance, imposing spectral assumptions on the integral operator, similar to the work of Caponnetto & De Vito (2007), might yield faster rates. Alternatively, stronger smoothness assumptions on the target ratio `q/p` could be explored. A discussion of these possibilities would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

2.  **Smoothness Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the strong assumption that the true density ratio `q/p` lies in a smooth function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). While such assumptions are common in non-parametric estimation, it would be beneficial to discuss their practical implications. Specifically, if `q/p` is assumed to be smooth, the inverse ratio `p/q` may not be, particularly if `q` has support where `p` is near zero. This could limit the applicability of the method in scenarios where both ratios are of interest. A brief discussion of this potential limitation would make the paper more complete.

3.  **Supplementary Material:** The supplementary material contains numerous typos and errors that significantly hinder the verification of the theoretical results. This is a critical issue that should be addressed. Specific corrections needed include:
    *   In Lemma 5, `W2^2` should be `Ws^2`. The final bound also appears incorrect; it should likely be of the form `D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 ||f||_2^2`.
    *   Lines 675 and 686 are incorrect, though the final statement in Eq. 31 seems correct.
    *   The integral domain at line 1147 should be `||ξ||^2 ≥` instead of what is written.

    I strongly urge the authors to perform a thorough proofreading of the supplement and correct these errors. A clear and correct appendix is essential for the paper's claims to be reproducible and credible.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of density-ratio estimation. Its main novelty lies in the elegant Fredholm inverse problem formulation and, more importantly, the practical and well-justified method for unsupervised cross-validation (CD-CV). This latter contribution addresses a significant open problem and is a clear advantage over existing methods. While the theoretical convergence rates could be improved and the underlying assumptions warrant further discussion, the core ideas are sound and well-supported. The errors in the supplementary material are a notable weakness but are correctable. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and its contributions are significant. I recommend acceptance, contingent on a thorough correction of the supplementary material.
