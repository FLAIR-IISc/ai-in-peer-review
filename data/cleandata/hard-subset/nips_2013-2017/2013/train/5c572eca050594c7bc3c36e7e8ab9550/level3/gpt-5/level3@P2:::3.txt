Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernel-based framework (FIRE) for estimating density ratios w(x) = q(x)/p(x) by recasting the problem as an inverse problem for a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two formulations are given: Type I uses the exact identity Kp w = Kq 1, where Kρ is the integral operator induced by a kernel and empirical samples from p and q are used to estimate both sides; Type II uses an approximate identity Kt,p w ≈ q with δ-like kernels (e.g., Gaussian) so that the right-hand side is available when q is known (possibly up to a constant) at p-sample locations. Both are solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form representer solutions. The paper also proposes an unsupervised hyperparameter selection method (CD-CV) that cross-validates kernel width and regularization by matching moments across p and q using random test functions. Theoretical results include concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth d-dimensional manifolds.

The approach relates closely to prior NIPS work on density ratio estimation and covariate shift: Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) (Gretton et al.), LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.), and operator-theoretic perspectives on kernel methods and inverse problems (De Vito et al., Smola & Schölkopf). The paper clarifies that KMM can be seen as solving the same operator equation in an RKHS norm but without regularization and without a natural out-of-sample extension; FIRE adds explicit regularization and representer solutions. The theory complements analyses such as Nguyen et al. (minimax rates) and Yu & Szepesvári (KMM), by providing FIRE-specific rates under Gaussian kernels and manifold settings.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and unifying, connecting to classical regularization theory and kernel methods.
- Practical algorithms: simple closed-form representer solutions, flexible loss choices (e.g., γ L2(p) + (1−γ) L2(q)), and out-of-sample evaluation.
- Theory: finite-sample concentration and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and compact manifolds; use of spectral tools is appropriate for the operator setting.
- Model selection: CD-CV is simple, fully unsupervised, and leverages cross-density identities; useful in covariate shift where labels may be scarce.
- Empirics: reasonable comparisons to LSIF, KMM, and a baseline TIKDE; applications to regression/classification under covariate shift.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Measure-theoretic foundations are underspecified. The ratio q/p only makes sense p-a.e. and only if q is absolutely continuous w.r.t. p. The paper does not specify a dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue in Rd or Riemannian volume on the manifold), nor does it consistently state “p-a.e.” identities. As written, equalities can be invalid or ambiguous on sets of ν-measure zero.
- Missing positivity/absolute continuity assumptions. Many results implicitly require p(x) > 0 on the support of q (or q ≪ p) and often a lower bound p ≥ c > 0 on the region of interest to avoid unbounded ratios. These assumptions are not clearly stated; without them, rates and bounds involving the ratio can break down.
- Domain and measure not always explicit. While Section 3 mentions Rd and compact manifolds, the measure (Lebesgue vs. Riemannian volume) should be explicit in all statements, and the manifold case should clarify that densities are w.r.t. the volume measure. Boundary cases are only briefly remarked upon.
- Type II interpretation. When q is “an arbitrary function,” w need not be nonnegative nor integrate to one when multiplied by p. Claims and terminology should distinguish density-ratio estimation (q a density) from general L2(p)-regression of q by Kt,p w.
- Lack of constraints. The estimator does not enforce nonnegativity or normalization; negative weights may harm downstream importance weighting. At least discuss constrained variants or post-processing.
- CD-CV theory. The model selection criterion is intuitive but lacks theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency, relation to MMD risk or excess ratio error). A brief analysis would strengthen the contribution.
- Clarity/notation issues. Several typos and ambiguities (e.g., exponents in Corollaries: “56 d”; powers of kernel matrices K^2; when K = KH; dimensions/scaling of Kp,p, Kp,q). The sampling/normalization constants in kernel matrices vary across sections and should be unified.
- Computational aspects. The proposed solutions require solving n×n linear systems with higher-order kernel products; complexity and numerical stability are not discussed, nor are approximations (Nyström, CG, preconditioning).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The operator-theoretic formulation and representer derivations are solid. The finite-sample bounds look plausible but depend on unspoken absolute continuity/positivity assumptions; making these explicit and stating results p-a.e. would improve rigor. The empirical section is reasonable but could include sensitivity analyses and larger-scale experiments or approximations.

Clarity
- Well organized and generally readable, but important modeling assumptions (dominating measure, absolute continuity, support/positivity) and notational consistency need attention. Several typos in the theory section should be corrected.

Originality
- Moderate. The Fredholm formulation is a clean unification and the manifold analysis is a nice extension. The approach is closely related to KMM/LSIF/KLIEP and to prior inverse-problem views of kernel learning; the novelty lies in the specific operator-regularized ratio estimation and CD-CV.

Significance
- Potentially useful to practitioners dealing with covariate shift and importance weighting, provided the foundational assumptions are clarified and, ideally, constraints are incorporated. The manifold treatment and unsupervised tuning are valuable additions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, principled operator view yielding simple, practical algorithms with out-of-sample extension.
- Theoretical bounds (including manifold case) are of interest to the NIPS audience.
- Unsupervised model selection is practical and performs well empirically.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently precise measure-theoretic assumptions; current presentation risks invalid conclusions on sets where p=0.
- Lack of constraints and limited discussion of negative weights and support mismatch.
- Missing theoretical justification for CD-CV and limited clarity/notation in the theory section.
- Computational scalability not addressed.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- State assumptions up front: existence of a σ-finite ν; densities p = dp/dν, q = dq/dν; q ≪ p (or an assumption on the support) and, when needed, p ≥ c > 0 on the region where q > 0. Reformulate w as the Radon–Nikodym derivative dq/dp, and state identities p-a.e.
- Make the underlying measure explicit in both Rd (Lebesgue) and manifold (Riemannian volume) cases, and clarify the boundary case.
- Discuss support mismatch/unbounded ratios; indicate when the rates hold and how to handle tails (e.g., trimming, reweighting, or localized regularization).
- Consider constrained variants enforcing nonnegativity (and optionally normalization) and compare empirically.
- Provide a brief analysis connecting CD-CV to an integral probability metric (e.g., MMD) or show that minimizing JCD correlates with the target risk.
- Clean up notation/typos and detail matrix constructions and computational complexity; suggest approximations for large n.

Overall evaluation
A promising and well-motivated paper whose clarity and rigor would benefit from explicit measure-theoretic assumptions and a more careful discussion of support/positivity issues. With these fixes, the contribution would be solid. As is, I lean toward borderline acceptance.
