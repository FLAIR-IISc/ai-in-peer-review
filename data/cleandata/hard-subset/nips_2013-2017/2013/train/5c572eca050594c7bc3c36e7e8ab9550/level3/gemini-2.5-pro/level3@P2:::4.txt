### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio `q/p`, a fundamental task in importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and other areas of machine learning. The authors propose a novel and principled framework by reformulating this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind.

Two main formulations are derived. The "Type I" setting is based on the equality `E_p[k(x,·) (q/p)] = E_q[k(x,·)]`, which holds for any kernel `k`. The "Type II" setting uses the approximation `E_p[k_t(x,·) (q/p)] ≈ q(x)`, which holds when `k_t` is a delta-like kernel (e.g., a Gaussian with small bandwidth `t`). These integral equations are ill-posed and are solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach leads to a family of simple and efficient algorithms, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), with closed-form solutions involving kernel matrices.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing concentration bounds and convergence rates for the FIRE estimators using Gaussian kernels, considering data on both Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. A key practical contribution is a fully unsupervised model selection method called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which allows for principled selection of the kernel and regularization parameters. The effectiveness of the proposed methods is demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on regression and classification tasks, showing favorable performance against related methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and sound. The theoretical analysis is a significant strength, providing convergence rates under clear assumptions. The claims are well-supported by both this theory and the experimental results, which are thorough and compare against relevant baselines. The proposed CD-CV method for model selection is a strong practical contribution, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised settings.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The derivation of the two main problem settings (Type I and II) from the integral equations is easy to follow. The resulting algorithms are presented with explicit formulas, which should allow for straightforward implementation. The connections to and differences from prior work like KMM and LSIF are well-articulated.

**Originality:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem for an integral operator is highly original. While kernel-based methods for this problem exist (e.g., KMM), this work provides a new and powerful perspective by connecting it to the classical theory of integral equations and regularization. This not only yields a new family of algorithms but also provides a principled framework for their theoretical analysis.

**Significance:** The problem of density ratio estimation is important and has broad applications. This paper offers a flexible, computationally efficient, and theoretically grounded new tool for this task. The strong empirical performance and the inclusion of a practical model selection procedure make it likely that these ideas will be used by other researchers and practitioners. The connection to classical operator theory may also inspire new approaches to other problems in machine learning.

Despite the paper's many strengths, there are several points that could be clarified or discussed further to strengthen the contribution:

1.  **Intuition for Type I Convergence:** The intuition for the Type II estimator is clear: convolving the ratio `q/p` with a narrow kernel should approximate `q`. However, for the Type I estimator, it is less obvious why matching the expectations of the kernel feature map (`E_p[k(x,·)f] = E_q[k(x,·)]`) is sufficient to ensure that `f` converges to `q/p`. A short intuitive argument, perhaps relating to the richness of the RKHS, would be helpful for the reader.

2.  **Motivation for Mixed Norm:** In Equation (9), a loss function combining `L2,p` and `L2,q` norms is proposed. While mathematically sound, the motivation for this specific combination is not fully explained. A discussion on the scenarios or applications where this mixed norm would be preferable to a single norm would be beneficial.

3.  **Application Context for Importance Sampling:** The paper motivates the problem with importance sampling. In the Type I setting, the algorithm requires samples from both `p` and `q` to construct the empirical objective function. A potential question from a practitioner is: if samples from `q` are available to estimate `E_q`, why not use them directly for Monte Carlo estimation instead of undertaking the intermediate step of estimating `q/p`? Clarifying the specific applications where estimating the ratio is necessary despite having samples from `q` would strengthen the motivation.

4.  **Relationship between Smoothness Assumptions:** The use of an RKHS norm as a regularizer imposes a smoothness assumption on the estimated ratio `f = q/p`. It would be insightful to include a discussion on how this relates to the smoothness properties of the individual densities `p` and `q`. For instance, what are the implications if one density is smooth and the other is not? Furthermore, the method implicitly requires that `p(x) > 0` where `q(x) > 0`; a brief mention of this support condition would be appropriate.

5.  **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The derived convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 show a significant dependence on the data dimension `d`. This is expected, but a practical discussion of the "curse of dimensionality" would be valuable. Based on the theory and experiments, for what range of dimensions is this method expected to be practically useful?

6.  **Justification of CD-CV Test Functions:** The choice of random linear functions and half-space indicators for the test set `U` in the CD-CV procedure seems somewhat ad-hoc. Could the authors provide more justification for this choice and comment on how sensitive the performance of CD-CV is to the family of test functions used?

7.  **Relation to Other Operator-Theoretic Work:** The paper positions itself well against KMM and LSIF. It could be further contextualized by briefly discussing its conceptual relationship to other operator-theoretic approaches in machine learning, such as Grünewälder et al. (ICML 2012, "Smooth Operators"), which also analyze integral operators for learning properties of distributions.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem.
*   Strong and rigorous theoretical analysis providing convergence rates.
*   Development of simple, efficient, and flexible kernel-based algorithms (FIRE).
*   Important practical contribution of an unsupervised model selection procedure (CD-CV).
*   Thorough experimental validation showing strong performance.
*   Addresses a significant and widely applicable problem in machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper could benefit from additional discussion to clarify motivation and practical limitations regarding the choice of norms, the curse of dimensionality, and the specific application to importance sampling. (These are minor points that can likely be addressed in a revision).

Overall, this is an excellent, well-executed, and timely paper that makes a significant contribution to an important area. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the core strengths of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
