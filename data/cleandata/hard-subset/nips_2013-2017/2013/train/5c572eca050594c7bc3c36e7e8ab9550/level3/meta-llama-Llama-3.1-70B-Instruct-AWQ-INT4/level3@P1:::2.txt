This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as an inverse problem using the Fredholm equation of the first kind. The authors propose two new methods, Type I and Type II, which utilize regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm penalty. The key advantage of this approach is that it enables reliable cross-validation (CV) for parameter selection in an unsupervised setting.

The paper provides theoretical convergence results and experimental validation of the practical performance of the proposed methods. The authors also discuss the similarity of their approach to existing methods, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), but emphasize the CV applicability as a strong benefit over RKHS-objective methods.

One of the main contributions of this paper is the development of a principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically. The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates, for the Gaussian kernel in the Euclidean case and for distributions supported on a sub-manifold.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in various settings, including regression and classification tasks. The authors also introduce a new technique, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), for model selection, which is shown to be effective in selecting the optimal parameters for the proposed methods.

Overall, this paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation with a strong theoretical foundation and promising experimental results. The main contribution of the paper is the development of a principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically, which enables reliable cross-validation for parameter selection in an unsupervised setting.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to density ratio estimation using the Fredholm equation of the first kind
* Principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically
* Enables reliable cross-validation for parameter selection in an unsupervised setting
* Theoretical convergence results and experimental validation of practical performance
* Effective model selection technique, CD-CV, for selecting optimal parameters

Weaknesses:

* Convergence rate in Theorem 1 is slow due to the (-t / log λ) factor
* Strong assumption that q/p lies in a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev)
* Practical limitation that p/q may behave poorly
* Typos and errors in the supplementary material hinder verification of theoretical results

Suggestions for improvement:

* Investigate stronger assumptions on q/p to obtain polynomial convergence rates
* Discuss the practical implications of the strong assumption that q/p lies in a smooth function class
* Improve the supplementary material to correct typos and errors
* Explore the application of the proposed methods to other problems, such as transfer learning and covariate shift.
