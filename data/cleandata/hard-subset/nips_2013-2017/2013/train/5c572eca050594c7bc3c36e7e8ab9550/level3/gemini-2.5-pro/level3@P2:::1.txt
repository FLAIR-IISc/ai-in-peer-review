This paper addresses the important problem of estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x) given samples from distributions p and q, or samples from p and access to the function q. This problem is fundamental to importance sampling and transfer learning under the covariate shift assumption. The main contribution is a novel reformulation of this problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. By viewing the density ratio as the unknown function in an integral equation, the authors connect the problem to the classical theory of inverse problems.

The paper proposes two specific formulations based on this idea. The Type I formulation, `Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)`, is applicable when samples from both p and q are available. The Type II formulation, `Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`, is useful when q is a known function. To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the authors employ Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm, a standard technique in both inverse problems and machine learning. This principled approach leads to a family of simple, kernel-based algorithms with closed-form solutions, which the authors name FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The work is supported by a theoretical analysis providing convergence rates for Gaussian kernels and a novel, fully unsupervised method for model selection called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). This work relates to previous methods such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, but distinguishes itself through its foundational integral equation perspective, which provides a flexible framework and enables analysis via classical operator theory.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and novel. It provides a principled and unifying framework that connects this machine learning problem to a rich body of classical mathematics. The problem itself is of high significance to the NIPS community, with direct applications in transfer learning, off-policy reinforcement learning, and Bayesian inference.

2.  **Clarity and Methodological Soundness:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The derivation of the FIRE algorithms from the integral equation formulation and Tikhonov regularization is straightforward and technically sound. The resulting algorithms are simple to implement, involving standard kernel matrix operations.

3.  **Practical Contribution:** The proposed Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method is a valuable contribution. Unsupervised model selection is a notoriously difficult problem, and CD-CV offers a practical and intuitive solution specific to the density ratio setting, which appears effective in the experiments.

4.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators in both Euclidean and manifold settings. This provides strong theoretical support for the method. (Note: The proofs, located in the supplementary material, have not been checked).

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, several aspects could be clarified and strengthened to improve its impact.

1.  **Justification and Context for Theory:**
    *   The paper makes statements such as `∫ k_t(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)` for delta-like kernels. While standard, this relies on smoothness assumptions on `f`. For completeness and rigor, the authors should either cite a specific theorem or provide a brief justification outlining the necessary conditions.
    *   The provided convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 are a key theoretical result. However, their significance is unclear without context. It would greatly strengthen the paper to compare these rates to known minimax optimal rates for density ratio estimation (e.g., as discussed in Nguyen et al., [14]) and comment on their sub-optimality, if any.

2.  **Clarity and Motivation:**
    *   A brief, high-level explanation of Tikhonov regularization could make the paper more accessible to readers not specialized in inverse problems.
    *   The paper compares against a baseline TIKDE, which is a naive plug-in estimator. It would be beneficial to include a more explicit discussion on why directly estimating p and q and then taking their ratio is generally an inferior strategy, both theoretically and practically. This would better motivate the need for direct density ratio estimation methods like FIRE.
    *   The paper notes that the special case of `q=1` corresponds to inverse density estimation. This is an interesting connection. A short discussion on how the role of the kernel bandwidth `t` here differs from its role in standard Kernel Density Estimation (where `t` must go to zero) could provide valuable insight into the mechanics of the proposed method.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The empirical evaluation is solid but could be more comprehensive. Given the close connection highlighted in the paper, a direct comparison with Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) is a notable omission. While KMM lacks a natural out-of-sample extension, a comparison could still be made on the training points or by using a standard heuristic for extension.
    *   The experiments would be significantly more illustrative with the inclusion of a simple 1D or 2D toy problem. Visualizing the true distributions `p` and `q`, the true ratio `q/p`, and the estimate from FIRE would provide compelling intuition about the method's performance and failure modes.
    *   For reproducibility, the authors should provide references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS).

In summary, this is a very nice paper presenting an elegant and powerful new perspective on density ratio estimation. The proposed framework is principled and leads to simple, effective algorithms with theoretical backing. The main weaknesses lie in the lack of context for some theoretical claims and a somewhat incomplete experimental comparison. Addressing the points above would substantially strengthen an already compelling submission.
