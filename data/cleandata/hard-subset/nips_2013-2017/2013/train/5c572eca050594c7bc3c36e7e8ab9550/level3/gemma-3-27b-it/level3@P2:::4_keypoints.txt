- Addresses estimation of the density ratio p/q via a Fredholm integral inverse formulation and proposes FIRE algorithms using RKHS regularization, with theoretical analysis and empirical validation.
- Novel formulation: framing density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem provides a fresh, operator-theoretic perspective.
- Theoretical guarantees: concentration bounds and convergence rates are derived, giving insight into algorithm behavior and limitations.
- Principled RKHS regularization: method is grounded in a solid mathematical framework that justifies algorithm design.
- Practical algorithms: FIRE family is relatively simple to implement and shows competitive empirical performance.
- Unsupervised model selection: proposes CD-CV as a practical method for parameter tuning without labeled data.
- Paper is well-written, well-motivated, and well-positioned within related work on density-ratio estimation and kernel methods.
- Experimental results are encouraging and demonstrate competitive performance across datasets.
- Weakness — dimensionality dependence: convergence rates depend on ambient dimension d, potentially limiting high-dimensional applicability; practical limits by dimension should be discussed.
- Weakness — cost function motivation: the combined L2,p and L2,q norm in Eq. (9) is not fully motivated; rationale and effects when data sizes differ need clarification.
- Weakness — importance sampling clarity: unclear when estimating q/p is preferable to directly using empirically estimated Eq in importance sampling scenarios.
- Weakness — smoothness assumptions: paper assumes smoothness and boundedness of p and q but lacks discussion of how these translate to smoothness of the ratio q/p and conditions ensuring p bounded away from zero.
- Weakness — test function choice: choice of test function family U for CD-CV (random linear and half-space functions) lacks detailed justification or exploration of alternatives.
- Argument for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound approach with valuable analysis, clear writing, and competitive experiments.
- Argument against acceptance: dimensionality dependence and insufficient motivation for some algorithmic choices may limit practical impact.
- Request 1: Provide an intuitive argument for why optimization over {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in equality (i), analogous to the bandwidth intuition for (ii).
- Request 2: Elaborate motivation for mixing L2,p and L2,q norms in Eq. (9) and describe performance impacts across application contexts.
- Request 3: Explain specific scenarios where estimating q/p yields advantages over directly using the empirically estimated Eq.
- Request 4: Describe how smoothness requirements on p and q translate to smoothness conditions on the density ratio q/p.
- Request 5: Provide a more detailed discussion of dependence on dimension d and indicate for which dimensionalities the method is practically useful.
- Request 6: Justify the chosen family of test functions U for CD-CV and discuss potentially more effective families.
- Request 7: Compare this linearization/inverse-operator approach to related work like “Smooth Operators” (Grunewalder et al.), clarifying differences in flexibility and assumptions.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel contribution with theoretical and empirical merit; recommended for acceptance pending clarifications on dimensionality and certain methodological motivations.
