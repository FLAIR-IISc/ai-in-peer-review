Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles density-ratio estimation w = q/p by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation and solving the resulting inverse problem with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are proposed:
- Type I (Fredholm I): Kp f = Kq 1, solved via minfвҲҲH ||Kp f вҲ’ Kq 1||^2L2,p + О»||f||^2H. This applies when samples from both p and q are available.
- Type II (local kernel/Fredholm II): Kt,p f вүҲ q as t вҶ’ 0, solved via minfвҲҲH ||Kt,p f вҲ’ q||^2L2,p + О»||f||^2H. This applies when q(x) is known (not necessarily a density) at points drawn from p.

Both have closed-form kernel solutions via the representer theorem. The authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth manifolds, and introduce CD-CV, a fully unsupervised cross-validation procedure that exploits the importance sampling identity with random test function families (e.g., linear, half-space). Empirically, the FIRE estimators are competitive with LSIF and heuristic TIKDE; KMM is discussed and compared conceptually.

This work is closely related to KMM (Gretton et al. 2009), which also enforces Eqq[ОҰ] = Ep[wОҰ] but optimizes an RKHS objective; and to LSIF/KLIEP (Kanamori et al., Sugiyama et al.), which minimize L2,p (or KL) with a parametric basis. FIRE differs by casting the problem explicitly as an inverse problem with RKHS regularization, offering simple closed-form estimators with natural out-of-sample evaluation and, crucially, an L2-based validation objective that enables reliable model selection.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation that unifies and extends prior density-ratio methods, including a principled Type II variant leveraging locality of kernels.
- Simple, closed-form algorithms with natural out-of-sample predictions (unlike KMMвҖҷs empirical weights).
- Fully unsupervised, practical model selection via CD-CV; the L2 formulation yields a kernel-independent validation target, making cross-kernel CV meaningful. This is a notable practical advantage over methods whose objective values depend on the chosen RKHS (e.g., KMM).
- Theoretical analysis covers both Euclidean and manifold settings for Gaussian kernels; bias terms and sample errors are disentangled.
- Experiments indicate robust performance and demonstrate the effectiveness of CD-CV across different test function families.

Weaknesses and questions
- Convergence rates. The main rates are relatively slow; in places the analysis (supplement) introduces terms of the form (t/вҲ’log О»), which yield sub-polynomial convergence unless t and О» are tightly coupled. Can the authors obtain polynomial rates with fixed t under stronger assumptions? For instance, source/capacity (spectral) conditions as in Caponnetto & De Vito (2007) or effective-dimension control could yield rates O(nвҲ’ОІ) for ОІ depending on smoothness and kernel eigen-decay. Similarly, stronger assumptions on w = q/p (e.g., higher-order Sobolev regularity or HГ¶lder smoothness under p) may improve rates.
- Assumptions on smoothness. The analysis assumes q/p вҲҲ W2^s (Sobolev). This is strong in covariate shift problems where w can be unbounded in low-support regions of p. Please discuss the practical implications: (i) what happens when w вҲү L2(p) or violates the assumed smoothness? (ii) Type I optimizes L2,p, which is reasonable statistically, but can underweight rare regions where w is large; how sensitive is CD-CV to support mismatch? Also note that if q/p is smooth, the inverse p/q may be poorly behavedвҖ”clarify how this affects applications that need both directions.
- Kernel dependence and CV. The paper rightly argues that RKHS-objective values (as in KMM) are kernel-dependent and not comparable for CV. This is an important point; it would be useful to quantify empirically how CD-CV behaves as the kernel family varies (e.g., Gaussian vs. Laplace) and to clarify any mismatch between the operator kernel and the RKHS kernel in theory and practice.
- Positivity/normalization constraints. FIRE does not explicitly constrain f вүҘ 0 or Ep[f] = 1. In applications (reweighing), negative weights can be problematic. Please discuss practical fixes (e.g., positivity projection, nonnegativity constraints, or constrained variants) and their effect on theory and CD-CV.
- Scalability. Solutions require solving linear systems in n (Type I) or in the number of p-samples. Please comment on runtime, conditioning, and the use of NystrГ¶m/preconditioned CG for larger n.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with solid derivations and clear links to operator theory. The empirical section convincingly demonstrates CD-CVвҖҷs utility. However, supplemental proofs contain numerous typos that materially hinder verification; see detailed corrections below. The convergence rates could be strengthened under standard spectral/source assumptions.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written, and the operator perspective is pedagogically valuable. The exposition could better separate assumptions specific to Gaussian kernels from those needed for general kernels. More discussion on when Type I vs. Type II is preferable would help practitioners.

Originality
While related to KMM/LSIF, the precise inverse-problem framing (Type I/II), the L2,p-regularized RKHS solutions, and especially the CD-CV mechanism for unsupervised model selection are original and practically important.

Significance
The main practical contribution is enabling reliable, unsupervised hyperparameter selection in density-ratio estimation. This addresses a chronic pain point in covariate shift and importance sampling and is likely to be adopted. The theoretical framework is a useful addition even if the current rates are conservative.

Pros
- Elegant inverse-problem formulation (Type I/II) with simple kernel algorithms.
- Fully unsupervised, robust CV via an L2-based criterion; practically valuable and novel.
- Theoretical grounding in operator theory; manifold extension.
- Empirical evidence supports the utility of CD-CV and competitive accuracy.

Cons
- Conservative convergence rates; reliance on strong smoothness; limited discussion of unbounded ratios.
- No positivity/normalization constraints by default.
- Supplemental material errors impede verification.

Overall assessment
A novel and useful contribution. The key advance is the L2 formulation that makes cross-validation meaningful and effective in an unsupervised setting, a clear advantage over RKHS-objective methods like KMM. I recommend acceptance after the authors (i) clarify rate assumptions and potential for polynomial convergence using spectral/source conditions, (ii) discuss smoothness/positivity issues, and (iii) thoroughly revise the supplement to correct the listed errors.
