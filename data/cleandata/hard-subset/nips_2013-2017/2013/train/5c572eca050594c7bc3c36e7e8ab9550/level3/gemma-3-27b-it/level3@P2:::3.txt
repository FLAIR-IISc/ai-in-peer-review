This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques. The resulting algorithms, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), are presented with theoretical analysis and experimental validation, including a novel unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV).

The core idea of reformulating density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is interesting and connects the problem to a well-studied area of mathematics, potentially enabling the application of established theoretical tools. The authors correctly identify the connection to importance sampling and covariate shift, highlighting the practical relevance of their work. The use of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) provides a flexible framework for implementing the algorithms and facilitating theoretical analysis. The introduction of CD-CV is a valuable contribution, addressing a crucial challenge in unsupervised and semi-supervised learning – model selection.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. The most fundamental is the implicit, and often unstated, assumption of the existence of underlying densities. While mathematically convenient, this is a strong and potentially problematic assumption in many machine learning contexts. Arbitrarily close measures may lack densities with respect to a given dominating measure, rendering the entire framework ill-defined. The paper consistently operates with densities *p* and *q* without specifying the dominating measure *ν* with respect to which these densities are defined (e.g., Lebesgue measure, counting measure). This omission is crucial as the validity of the Fredholm equation and the subsequent analysis fundamentally depend on the choice of *ν*. Further, the presence of sets with zero *ν*-measure can lead to ambiguous or invalid results, especially when dealing with empirical approximations.

The lack of clear statement of modeling assumptions is another weakness. The domain of *x* is not explicitly defined – is it *R<sup>d</sup>* or a more general topological space? This impacts the applicability and interpretation of the theoretical results. Additionally, the assumption of positivity of the density *p* is not stated. If *p* is not strictly positive, the ratio *q/p* is undefined in those regions, and the entire framework breaks down.

The theoretical analysis, while providing concentration bounds and convergence rates, relies heavily on the Gaussian kernel and specific assumptions about the smoothness of the densities. The extent to which these results generalize to other kernels or less smooth densities is unclear. The analysis feels somewhat isolated from the practical implications and doesn’t thoroughly discuss the limitations imposed by the assumptions.

Experimentally, the paper demonstrates competitive performance on several datasets. However, the comparison with existing methods (TIKDE, LSIF, KMM) is not always comprehensive. The choice of test functions for CD-CV appears somewhat arbitrary, and the sensitivity of the results to the choice of these functions is not adequately explored. The comparisons could be strengthened by providing statistical significance tests and a more thorough ablation study to isolate the impact of different components of the FIRE algorithm.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Principled framework based on RKHS and regularization.
*   Introduction of the CD-CV unsupervised model selection method.
*   Theoretical analysis providing convergence rates (though limited in scope).
*   Competitive empirical performance on several datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong and potentially unrealistic assumption of underlying densities.
*   Missing specification of the dominating measure *ν*.
*   Lack of clear statement of modeling assumptions.
*   Theoretical results limited to Gaussian kernel and smooth densities.
*   Incomplete comparison with existing methods.
*   Potential ambiguity and invalidity due to sets of zero measure.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation, the fundamental issues surrounding the assumption of densities and the lack of clarity in modeling assumptions significantly detract from its impact. The theoretical results, while mathematically sound under the stated assumptions, are limited in scope and may not generalize well to real-world applications. The experimental results, while promising, do not fully justify the complexity of the proposed approach.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The issues raised are substantial enough to warrant rejection in its current form. However, the authors are encouraged to address the concerns regarding the existence of densities and the specification of the dominating measure, and to broaden the scope of their theoretical analysis and experimental evaluation. A revised version addressing these points could be a significant contribution.
