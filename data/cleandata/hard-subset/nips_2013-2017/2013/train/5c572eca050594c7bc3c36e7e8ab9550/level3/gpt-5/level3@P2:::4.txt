Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles density-ratio (importance weight) estimation f(x) = q(x)/p(x) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two identities underpin the approach: (i) Ep[k(x,·) f] = Eq[k(x,·)], which yields an exact operator equation when samples from both p and q are available; and (ii) Ep[kt(x,·) f] ≈ q(·) for “local” kernels kt (e.g., Gaussian), which yields an approximate operator equation when function values of q are available at sample points from p. These are solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, leading to closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE). The paper further proposes an unsupervised model-selection criterion, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which uses independent test functions u to check whether the reweighting identity Ep[u f] ≈ Eq[u] holds. Theoretical results provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Rd and on smooth manifolds, together with empirical comparisons against LSIF, KMM (when applicable), and a KDE-based baseline.

The work is closely related to KMM (Gretton et al.), which enforces Eq(Φ) ≈ Ep[f Φ] in an RKHS without explicit regularization and with positivity/normalization constraints; to LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.), which minimize L2,p or KL divergence to the ratio in a parametric RKHS subspace; and to operator-theoretic views of kernel methods (e.g., Smooth Operators, Grünewälder et al.; inverse problems in RKHS by De Vito et al.). The contribution here is to explicitly cast density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem and to exploit classical regularization theory to obtain algorithms and rates.

Quality
- Theory: The operator-theoretic formulation is sound, and the Tikhonov approach is appropriate for ill-posed integral equations. Assumptions (bounded densities; Sobolev smoothness; Gaussian kernels) are standard. The nonasymptotic bounds and rates are clearly stated; the explicit dependence on dimension d and regularization/bandwidth is appreciated. However, the rates degrade quickly with d, and the role of lower bounds on p (to control the ratio) should be discussed more explicitly. The positivity/normalization of f is not enforced; practical implications and possible constraints/regularizers could be addressed.
- Algorithms: The empirical objectives are clear, admit representer solutions, and are easy to implement. The variants mixing L2,p and L2,q are interesting, but the motivation for combining the two losses merits more discussion in terms of downstream tasks.
- Model selection: CD-CV is a practical and elegant idea in this setting; more guidance on the choice of U and its stability would be useful. Any theoretical justification (e.g., consistency of the criterion) would strengthen the contribution.
- Experiments: The setups (covariate shift; regression/classification) are appropriate. Some comparisons exclude KMM due to out-of-sample extension; elsewhere comparisons are provided—please ensure fairness and clarity across all settings. Reporting computational cost (n, m, kernel width grid, λ grid, wall-clock) would help readers gauge scalability. Consider adding Nyström/random features variants.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The connection between the operator equations and the empirical losses is clear, and the matrix formulas are explicit. Suggestions:
- Provide an intuitive explanation for why optimizing with k(x,·) as test functions in identity (i) is sufficient to recover f, analogous to the bandwidth intuition for (ii). For example, argue via RKHS completeness or characteristic kernels and identifiability of f from operator equality Ep[k(·,·) f] = Eq[k(·,·)].
- Clarify when to use L2,p vs L2,q vs mixed loss (Eq. 9), and how these choices align with intended applications (importance sampling vs covariate shift risk minimization).
- Discuss conditions ensuring smoothness/regularity of q/p given assumptions on p and q (e.g., p bounded away from zero on support of q, continuity of log-densities).
- Summarize dimensional dependence of the bounds in a sentence to aid practitioners.

Originality
Recasting density-ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem and solving it with RKHS Tikhonov regularization is a fresh, principled angle that unifies and clarifies connections to KMM/LSIF and operator-theoretic kernel literature. The CD-CV criterion is a neat, problem-specific model selection idea. While individual ingredients are known, their combination and the developed analysis are novel.

Significance
Importance weighting is central to covariate shift and Monte Carlo integration. The framework provides a clean operator view, simple algorithms with out-of-sample predictions, and nontrivial rates on Rd and manifolds. Practical impact is tempered by dimension dependence and the lack of positivity constraints; nevertheless, the approach should influence subsequent work on stable, regularized ratio estimation and model selection under distribution shift.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Intuition for identity (i): Please give a simple argument why restricting to test functions {k(x,·)} (or equivalently Φ(x)) suffices to recover f; e.g., via characteristic kernels and uniqueness of mean embeddings.
- Loss choice: What is the practical motivation for the mixed loss γ L2,p + (1−γ) L2,q in Eq. (9)? How should γ be chosen relative to downstream objectives (minimizing target risk vs accurate reweighting of specific function classes)?
- Importance sampling motivation: Since you empirically estimate both Ep and Eq to form the loss, in what scenarios is estimating f preferable to directly estimating Eq[u] (e.g., via KDE or regression on q)? Please clarify when a reusable estimate of f provides clear advantages.
- Regularity of q/p: What conditions on p and q (e.g., p ≥ c > 0 on support of q) are needed to ensure boundedness/smoothness of q/p compatible with the RKHS penalty? Any guidance on detecting/handling regions where p is small?
- Dimensionality: Given the explicit d-dependence in the rates, for what ranges of d (and sample sizes) do you expect FIRE to be practically competitive? Do the manifold results mitigate the curse of dimensionality in high-dimensional but low-intrinsic-d data?
- CD-CV test functions U: Why random linear and half-space indicators? How sensitive is CD-CV to U? Any recommendations for choosing U to match downstream tasks? Any theory on identifiability or coverage of function classes by U?
- Relation to Smooth Operators (Grünewälder et al.): Please position FIRE relative to their operator regression viewpoint and conditional mean embeddings; is using {k(x,·)} as test functions the “right” general approach for ratio estimation under shift?

Pros
- Clear, principled inverse-problem formulation; simple RKHS algorithms with closed forms.
- Solid theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and rates in Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) well tailored to the problem.
- Empirical results are encouraging and computationally efficient relative to alternatives.

Cons
- Rates degrade with dimension; discussion of practical regimes is limited.
- No positivity/normalization constraints; potential negative weights and instability in tails.
- Limited guidance on choice of loss (L2,p vs L2,q vs mixed) and CD-CV test functions.
- Comparisons with KMM are uneven due to out-of-sample issues; scalability analysis is light.

Overall recommendation
A well-executed, timely, and strong paper that advances density-ratio estimation through an elegant operator-theoretic lens, with both theory and practice. I recommend acceptance after addressing the above clarifications, especially the intuition for identity (i), loss-choice motivation, and discussion of dimensionality and regularity conditions.
