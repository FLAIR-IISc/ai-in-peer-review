Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernel-based formulation of density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are offered: (I) K_P w = K_Q 1 with w = q/p, estimated from samples from both p and q; (II) K_{t,P} w ≈ q with a δ-family kernel, when q is known on the p-sample. The approach yields closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) via the representer theorem. Theoretical results give concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on R^d and smooth compact submanifolds. A model-selection heuristic (CD-CV) uses importance-sampling identities with random test functions to tune bandwidth and regularization without labels. Empirically, FIRE is compared against LSIF, KMM (qualitatively), TIKDE, and baselines on covariate-shift tasks.

The paper connects to a rich literature on importance weighting and covariate shift (Shimodaira; Gretton et al. KMM; Sugiyama et al. LSIF/KLIEP), RKHS-based inverse problems (De Vito et al.), and manifold learning. The “Fredholm inverse problem” perspective unifies and clarifies several existing methods, showing KMM as a special case when cast in RKHS terms and motivating regularization for stability.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The operator-based derivations and RKHS-regularized estimators are standard and well-posed. The theory provides finite-sample deviation bounds and convergence rates that depend on kernel bandwidth, regularization, and dimension. However, key measure-theoretic and modeling assumptions are underspecified, and some are strong for ML settings:
  - The paper assumes densities p, q exist (w.r.t. an unstated dominating measure ν) and uses w = q/p pointwise. In modern ML, absolute continuity Q ≪ P frequently fails (support mismatch under covariate shift), and measures arbitrarily close in common metrics may lack ν-densities. Without Q ≪ P, w is not defined (or is infinite) on sets of positive Q-measure, making the problem ill-posed irrespective of regularization.
  - The dominating measure ν is not specified (Lebesgue on R^d? volume measure on a manifold? counting measure for discrete domains?). Equalities and L2,ρ statements are only meaningful ν-a.e.; missing ν leads to ambiguity and potential invalidity on sets of ν-measure zero.
  - No explicit assumption that p > 0 (or p ≥ c > 0) on the support of q. Without positivity, the ratio may blow up, invalidating risk bounds and practical use (importance weights). The empirical procedures do not enforce nonnegativity of w.
  - The domain of x is inconsistently specified: some sections treat Ω ⊂ R^d or a submanifold; earlier sections appear agnostic. The operator and RKHS results rely on topology and measure (compactness, volume form); these should be stated up front.
- Empirics: CD-CV is clever and practically useful; experiments suggest stability and competitive performance. However, experiments do not probe failure modes central to importance weighting (support mismatch, near-zero p, singular Q), where the theory’s assumptions break.
- Proof rigor: Theorems assume bounded densities and Sobolev regularity of q/p and q/p^2, which are strong. It would help to state all equalities as P-a.e., specify ν, and articulate source/qualification conditions for compact operators.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written, but clarity is impacted by:
- Notation overload between k (integral kernel) and k_H (RKHS kernel). Sometimes they are identified; sometimes not. Explicit assumptions when they differ would help (boundedness, PD, spectral properties).
- Lack of an explicit measure-theoretic setup: measurable space (X, F), probability measures P, Q, dominating measure ν, Radon–Nikodym derivative w = dQ/dP, and operators K_P f(x) = ∫ k(x,y) f(y) dP(y). This would eliminate ambiguity and remove the need to assume “densities.”
- The role of positivity/nonnegativity constraints on w is not discussed; importance weights should be ≥ 0.

Originality
The Fredholm inverse-problem framing is a nice unification that clarifies connections among KMM/LSIF and classical inverse problems. The Type II setting and CD-CV add practical value. While related to prior work, the synthesis and analysis are novel enough for NIPS 2013.

Significance
If the assumptions are clarified and aligned with ML practice (Q ≪ P, positivity, support conditions), FIRE could be a robust alternative for density-ratio estimation with principled regularization and usable model selection. As written, the gap between assumptions and common covariate-shift scenarios (support mismatch) limits applicability.

Strengths
- Clear inverse-problem formulation; principled regularization in RKHS with closed-form solutions.
- Useful perspective connecting KMM to Fredholm equations; out-of-sample estimation is straightforward.
- CD-CV provides an unsupervised, simple, and effective tuning method.
- Nontrivial theory (concentration and rates) on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
- Empirical performance and efficiency look promising.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Specify the dominating measure ν and adopt a measure-theoretic setup:
  - Define (X, F), P, Q; assume Q ≪ P (or state clearly when the method applies).
  - Define w = dQ/dP, and write K_P w = K_Q 1 as an identity in H or L2(P), holding P-a.e.
  - For manifolds, specify ν as the Riemannian volume measure; for subsets of R^d with boundary, state boundary conditions and their effect on K_P.
- State explicit support/positivity conditions:
  - Assume p(x) ≥ c > 0 on supp(Q) (or on the set where q > 0) or discuss how the method behaves otherwise.
  - Discuss nonnegativity constraints for w and whether simple post-processing (clipping) or constrained optimization improves stability.
- Discuss the case Q ⊄ supp(P) (support mismatch). Without Q ≪ P, importance weighting is undefined; explain whether FIRE detects/imposes assumptions, and add experiments probing this.
- Clarify when k = k_H versus k ≠ k_H; list assumptions ensuring compactness, boundedness, and applicability of the representer theorem.
- Make all equalities and norms precise: a.e. statements, spaces for each function (H, L2(P), L2(Q)).
- Compare rates to known minimax results (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2007), and discuss optimal choices of t, λ under smoothness classes; justify exponents (e.g., n^{−1/(3.5+d/2)}).
- Discuss computational complexity and numerical stability (conditioning of K^2 KH + nλI), and give guidance on scaling.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean unification via Fredholm operators with practical algorithms and theory.
- CD-CV is a valuable and general unsupervised model-selection tool.
- Solid empirical performance and simplicity.

Cons for acceptance
- Foundational modeling gaps (dominating measure, absolute continuity, positivity) make some statements ambiguous and limit applicability to key ML scenarios.
- No handling of support mismatch; no nonnegativity constraints.
- Some theoretical assumptions are strong and not aligned with experiments; clarity of assumptions is lacking.

Recommendation
Borderline. I would support acceptance if the authors revise to (i) recast the development in measure-theoretic terms with explicit assumptions (Q ≪ P, ν, a.e. statements), (ii) add positivity/support conditions and discuss nonnegativity constraints, and (iii) include experiments probing support mismatch and near-zero p. These changes would substantially strengthen both rigor and practical relevance.
