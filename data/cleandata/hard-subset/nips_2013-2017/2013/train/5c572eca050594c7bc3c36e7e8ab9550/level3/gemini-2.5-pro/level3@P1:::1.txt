This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x) given samples from distributions p and q, or samples from p and the ability to evaluate q(x). This problem is of significant importance in machine learning, particularly for tasks involving importance sampling and covariate shift correction. The central and novel contribution of this work is to reframe the density ratio estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. By applying a kernel integral operator to the unknown ratio f = q/p, the authors derive two types of integral equations. Type I, `K_p f = K_q 1`, is applicable when samples from both p and q are available. Type II, `K_{t,p} f ≈ q`, is used when samples are available from p and q is a known function.

To solve these ill-posed inverse problems, the authors employ Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, leading to a family of algorithms named FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). This principled approach yields simple, closed-form solutions for the estimator as a linear combination of kernels centered on the data points. The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case, establishing concentration bounds and convergence rates on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Furthermore, a practical and completely unsupervised method for model selection, called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), is proposed. The paper relates this new framework to existing methods, noting that Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) can be viewed as a related formulation using an RKHS norm as the loss function without explicit regularization, and that the CD-CV validation metric is related to the objective function in LSIF.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation. This is an elegant approach that connects a core machine learning problem to the well-established field of inverse problems and operator theory. This new perspective is a significant conceptual contribution and provides a flexible and principled framework for deriving a family of algorithms.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. It effectively introduces the core idea, derives the algorithms, presents the theoretical results, and describes the experimental validation. The distinction between Type I and Type II settings is clear and addresses different practical scenarios.

3.  **Significance and Practicality:** The problem of density ratio estimation is highly relevant to the NIPS community. The proposed FIRE algorithms are simple to implement, involving standard kernel matrix operations. The introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is a crucial practical contribution, as model selection is a major challenge in unsupervised learning settings.

4.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a substantial theoretical analysis, offering convergence rates for the proposed estimators. While the proofs are in the supplement, the statement of these results in the main paper provides strong theoretical backing for the method's soundness.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Context for Theoretical Results:** While the paper provides convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4, it lacks a discussion of how these rates compare to known minimax optimal rates for this problem (e.g., as discussed in [14], which is cited). Situating the theoretical results within the broader context of statistical estimation theory would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of significance. Are the derived rates optimal, or is there a price to be paid for the proposed framework?

2.  **Motivation and Comparison:** The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of why a naive "plug-in" approach—estimating p and q separately with Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) and then taking their ratio (as done with the TIKDE baseline)—is often suboptimal. A brief theoretical or conceptual argument would better motivate the need for direct estimation methods like FIRE, LSIF, and KMM.

3.  **Experimental Visualization:** The experimental results on real-world regression and classification tasks are valuable. However, the paper would be greatly enhanced by including a simple 1D or 2D toy experiment. A figure showing the true densities p and q, the true ratio q/p, and the estimate produced by FIRE would provide powerful visual intuition about the method's performance and the effect of its parameters.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues and Missing Details:**
    *   The crucial approximation in Eq. 3, `∫ k_t(x, y)f(x)dx ≈ q(y)`, is stated to be `q(y) + O(t)`. For clarity and self-containment, the authors should briefly state the required smoothness conditions on the function for this approximation to hold, perhaps with a more specific reference than a book chapter.
    *   A brief, one-sentence intuitive explanation of Tikhonov regularization in the introduction would make the paper more accessible to readers not deeply familiar with inverse problems.
    *   The paper mentions an interesting special case of the Type II setting where `q(x)=1`, which effectively becomes a method for estimating `1/p(x)`. This connects FIRE to density estimation. A brief discussion on how the role of the kernel bandwidth `t` in this context differs from its role in standard KDE (where the bandwidth must go to zero for consistency) would be an insightful addition.
    *   The datasets used in the experiments (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) should be accompanied by citations to ensure reproducibility.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and elegant framework for density ratio estimation. The connection to Fredholm integral equations is a strong conceptual contribution, and the resulting algorithms are practical and theoretically grounded. The main weaknesses are a lack of context for the theoretical rates and a need for more illustrative experiments. These points could be addressed to further strengthen an already impressive piece of work.

(Note: All proofs were deferred to the supplementary material and have not been checked by this reviewer. The soundness of the theoretical claims rests on their correctness.)
