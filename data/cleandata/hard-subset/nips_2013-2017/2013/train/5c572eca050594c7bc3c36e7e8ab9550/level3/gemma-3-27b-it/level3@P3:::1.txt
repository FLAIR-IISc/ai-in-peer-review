This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and solving it using regularization techniques from the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The central idea – viewing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem – is a significant contribution, offering a principled way to apply tools from operator theory. The resulting algorithms, termed FIRE, are presented with detailed theoretical analysis including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels. The paper is well-written and addresses an important and interesting problem with connections to importance sampling and covariate shift.

However, while the paper demonstrates a strong conceptual foundation, several areas require further clarification and strengthening. A key weakness lies in the theoretical justifications. For example, the statement  ∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) (referenced to [24]) requires more explicit explanation or a more detailed justification within the paper itself, perhaps a sketch of the proof or a more specific bound on the error term.  Simply citing a textbook isn’t sufficient for a reader to fully assess the validity of this crucial approximation.

The special case q=1 is particularly interesting, as it suggests a density estimation method that avoids the typical bandwidth-to-zero limit inherent in Kernel Density Estimation (KDE). A detailed discussion comparing and contrasting the role of the bandwidth parameter *t* in FIRE versus the bandwidth in KDE would be beneficial. How does the choice of *t* influence the bias-variance trade-off in FIRE, and how does this differ from standard KDE? Is there a theoretical justification for choosing *t* based on the data, and how does this relate to existing bandwidth selection methods for KDE?

For readers unfamiliar with Tikhonov regularization, a brief, self-contained explanation would improve accessibility. While the reference to [5] is helpful, a couple of sentences outlining the purpose of the regularization term (preventing overfitting, stabilizing the solution) and its connection to the RKHS norm would be valuable.

The paper claims the proposed method is superior to naively estimating *p* and *q* separately. However, the justification for this claim is not sufficiently detailed. Why does estimating the ratio directly, as FIRE does, outperform estimating *p* and *q* independently and then dividing? Is it due to reduced variance, better utilization of the data, or some other factor? A more concrete explanation would strengthen this argument.

The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient comparative analysis. While comparisons are made to TIKDE, LSIF, and KMM, a more thorough comparison to prior state-of-the-art methods, such as [12] mentioned in the related work, is needed. Crucially, the paper should discuss minimax-optimality. What are the known minimax rates for density ratio estimation in the settings considered (Euclidean, manifold)? How do the convergence rates derived in Corollaries 2 and 4 compare to these optimal rates? Quantifying the gap between the theoretical results and the optimal rates would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

The datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) are referenced but lack explicit citations. Adding references (e.g., links to UCI Machine Learning Repository) would improve reproducibility. Furthermore, the experiments lack visual illustrations. Including toy examples – 1D plots showing true *p* and *q*, and the estimated *p/q* ratio using FIRE – would dramatically improve intuition and demonstrate the method’s performance and quality. Similarly, visualizing the estimated density ratio on the more complex datasets would be useful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.
*   Principled approach based on RKHS theory and Tikhonov regularization.
*   Detailed theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and convergence rates.
*   Well-written and clear presentation of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed justification for key theoretical statements.
*   Insufficient comparison to existing methods and discussion of minimax-optimality.
*   Missing dataset citations and visual illustrations.
*   Limited explanation of the bandwidth parameter *t* and its role compared to KDE.



**Overall:** This is a promising paper with a strong conceptual contribution. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly regarding theoretical justification, comparative performance, and experimental details – is crucial for elevating the paper to a level suitable for publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing these concerns.
