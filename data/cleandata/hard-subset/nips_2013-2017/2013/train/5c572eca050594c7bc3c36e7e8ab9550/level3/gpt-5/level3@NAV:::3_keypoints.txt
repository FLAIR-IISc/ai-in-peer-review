- Proposed method: estimate density ratio q/p by casting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.  
- Two formulations: Type I uses samples from P and Q to match Kp(q/p) to Kq1; Type II uses δ-like kernels requiring samples from P and pointwise q values.  
- Closed-form solutions via the representer theorem and an unsupervised model-selection procedure (CD-CV) are provided.  
- Theoretical contributions: concentration and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, with explicit t, λ trade-offs and extension to manifold settings.  
- Empirical results: experiments show favorable performance versus KMM and LSIF on covariate shift tasks.  
- Relation to prior work: connects operator-theoretic inverse-problem and RKHS regularization literature and aligns with existing density-ratio estimators (KMM, LSIF/KLIEP); Fredholm framing and manifold analysis are novel additions.  
- Strength: elegant, implementable inverse-problem formulation with simple closed forms.  
- Strength: meaningful theoretical rates and valuable manifold extension.  
- Strength: practical, unsupervised CD-CV and convincing experiments.  
- Core weakness: missing foundational measure-theoretic assumptions — no dominating measure ν specified and no explicit assumption Q ≪ P (Radon–Nikodym).  
- Modeling ambiguity: domain and measure not crisply stated (is X = R^d, a measurable space, or a manifold?); recommend stating (X, B, ν), p = dP/dν, q = dQ/dν, and Q ≪ P, p > 0 P-a.e.  
- Positivity/integrability concerns: q/p nonnegativity and integrability are not enforced; estimators can be negative or undefined on p = 0 — consider positivity constraints or estimating dQ/dP directly and discuss support mismatch.  
- Identifiability/ill-posedness: no conditions given for injectivity of Kp or when the inverse problem yields an identifiable q/p.  
- CD-CV concern: model selection may depend strongly on the choice of test functions U; robustness and possible degeneracies are not discussed.  
- Scalability concern: computational cost (O(n^3)) and large-scale approximations (Nyström, conjugate gradient, etc.) are not addressed.  
- Clarity: generally well written but notation around q/p, measures, and domains needs tightening.  
- Originality: moderate — solid synthesis of known tools with some novel framing.  
- Significance: potentially high if foundational measure assumptions and practical issues are clarified.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject pending revision to address core measure-theoretic assumptions, positivity/support issues, identifiability, and scalability.
