- Novel formulation: casts density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, connecting it to classical inverse problems and yielding the FIRE estimator family.  
- Theoretical strength: provides rigorous concentration bounds and convergence rates for Euclidean and manifold cases under reasonable smoothness assumptions.  
- Algorithmic contribution: derives regularized RKHS-based algorithms (FIRE) with principled connections to operator theory.  
- Empirical validation: thorough experiments comparing to KMM, LSIF, TIKDE across datasets, with convincing results and reproducible details.  
- Relation to prior work: extends and unifies recent kernel-based density-ratio methods, clarifying differences from KMM (regularization, out-of-sample extension).  
- Clarity and exposition: well-written, well-organized, with clear mathematical derivations and experimental descriptions.  
- Model selection: introduces an innovative CD-CV unsupervised model selection method, clearly motivated.  
- Practicality/significance: method is computationally efficient, broadly applicable (transfer learning, importance sampling, covariate shift) and offers meaningful convergence guarantees.  
- Strength: precise error bounds and careful statement of assumptions strengthen theoretical foundation.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion on how smoothness of p and q translates to smoothness of the ratio q/p.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical dimensionality limits given dimension-dependent convergence rates.  
- Weakness: insufficient motivation for mixing L2,p and L2,q norms in the cost (eq. 9).  
- Weakness: unclear guidance on when estimating q/p is preferable to directly using an estimated expectation Eq.  
- Weakness: need better justification for the chosen family of test functions U.  
- Specific requests to authors: provide intuition why optimizing over {k(x,·)} ensures Type I convergence; justify L2,p/L2,q mixing; clarify when to estimate q/p vs Eq; elaborate on p/q smoothness and p boundedness; state practical ambient-dimension limits; compare to the "Smooth Operators" framework.  
- Overall verdict: excellent, novel, and well-supported work that merits acceptance.
