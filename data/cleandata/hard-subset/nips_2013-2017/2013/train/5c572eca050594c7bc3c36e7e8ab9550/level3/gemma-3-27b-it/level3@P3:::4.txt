This paper addresses the important problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm equations and regularization techniques. The approach is well-motivated, leverages kernel methods effectively, and provides theoretical guarantees alongside encouraging experimental results. It's a strong contribution, particularly timely given the prevalence of covariate shift and importance sampling in machine learning. The connection to classical operator theory is a notable strength, offering a principled framework often lacking in empirical approaches.

The core idea – reformulating density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm integral equation – is clever and allows the application of powerful tools from regularization theory. Using kernel functions as test functions naturally leads to algorithms within the RKHS framework, providing a clear path to implementation and analysis. The introduction of the FIRE algorithm and the CD-CV model selection technique are valuable contributions. The theoretical analysis, covering both Euclidean and manifold settings, is impressive and provides crucial insights into convergence rates. The experimental validation supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates competitive performance against existing methods.

However, some aspects warrant further clarification. While the paper establishes that optimization over {k(x,·)} leads to a solution, a more intuitive argument for *why* this ensures convergence in equality (i) – Ep[k(x,·) f] = Eq[k(x,·)] – similar to the bandwidth intuition driving convergence in equality (ii) (the approximation) would be helpful. The paper effectively shows *that* it works, but less on *why* it works from a deeper intuitive perspective.

The choice of cost function also raises questions. Initially, the optimization focuses on L2 norms, but equation (9) mixes L2,p and L2,q. The motivation for this mixing, and a clearer link to specific target applications where L2,q is particularly beneficial, would strengthen the paper. Is there a scenario where minimizing with respect to q’s distribution is critical?

Regarding the application to importance sampling, the paper estimates Ep and Eq to form the empirical cost. It's not immediately clear why estimating q/p is always necessary. When is estimating q/p superior to directly using the estimated Eq for integration, and vice versa? A more explicit discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial.

The relationship between the smoothness of p and q and the smoothness of their ratio (q/p) deserves further attention. Specifically, under what conditions is the ratio smoother than either p or q individually? The assumption that p is bounded away from zero is crucial; how does this interact with smoothness requirements for theoretical guarantees to hold?

The dependence on dimension *d* is a practical concern. While theoretical convergence rates are provided, for what dimensions is this method realistically useful? The rates appear to degrade significantly with increasing *d*. Providing guidance on practical dimensionality limits would be valuable.

The justification for the chosen family of test functions U, particularly in the context of CD-CV, could be elaborated upon. Why this specific family, and how sensitive are the results to its choice?

Finally, a principled discussion of whether using {k(x,·)} is the *optimal* general approach for covariate-shift/q/p estimation is warranted. How does this work relate to prior work like “Smooth Operators” (Grunewalder et al.), which also leverages kernel methods and operator theory for related problems? Exploring the connections and potential advantages of the FIRE approach over alternatives within this broader landscape would further solidify its contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Practical algorithm (FIRE) with an effective model selection technique (CD-CV).
*   Competitive experimental results.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of intuitive explanation for convergence in equality (i).
*   Motivation for mixing L2 norms in equation (9) is unclear.
*   Discussion of trade-offs between estimating q/p versus directly using Eq is lacking.
*   Practical dimensionality limitations are not clearly addressed.



**Overall Assessment:** Accept. This is a well-executed, timely, and theoretically sound paper. Addressing the points raised above would further strengthen its contribution, but it is already a significant advancement in the field.
