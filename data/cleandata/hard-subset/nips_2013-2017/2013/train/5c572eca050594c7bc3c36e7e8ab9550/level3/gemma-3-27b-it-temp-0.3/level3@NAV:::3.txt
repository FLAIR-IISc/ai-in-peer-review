## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as an inverse problem based on Fredholm integral equations and utilizing tools from regularization theory. The core idea is compelling: leveraging the well-established framework of operator theory to provide a principled approach to a problem central to importance sampling, transfer learning, and covariate shift. The authors present a family of algorithms (FIRE) and provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels. 

The work builds upon existing literature in density ratio estimation (e.g., [6, 9, 10, 22]) and kernel methods ([21, 17]). It explicitly connects to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], positioning FIRE as a related but distinct approach with potential advantages in stability and theoretical analysis. The inclusion of a novel unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) is also a positive contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. The fundamental assumption of the existence of densities *p* and *q* is problematic in a machine learning context, as arbitrarily close measures may lack densities. This severely limits the practical applicability of the method. Furthermore, the paper lacks crucial specification of the dominating measure ν with respect to which densities are defined. The validity of the results hinges on this specification (e.g., Lebesgue, counting measure), and its omission is a major oversight. 

The rigor of the theoretical analysis is also questionable. Results may be invalid or ambiguous due to potential issues with sets of ν-measure zero, which are not adequately addressed. The paper also lacks a clear statement of modeling assumptions. For instance, the domain of *x* is not explicitly defined (R<sup>d</sup> or a more general topological space?), and it's unclear whether the density *p* is assumed to be positive everywhere. These omissions hinder a complete understanding and evaluation of the theoretical claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Connection to established operator theory and regularization techniques.
*   Development of a new unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV).
*   Theoretical analysis, albeit with limitations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong and problematic assumption of density existence.
*   Missing specification of the dominating measure ν.
*   Rigor concerns regarding sets of measure zero.
*   Lack of clarity regarding modeling assumptions (domain, positivity of *p*).



**Overall:** While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable perspective on density ratio estimation, the significant theoretical weaknesses and lack of clarity regarding fundamental assumptions outweigh the strengths. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should address the concerns regarding density existence, the dominating measure, and the rigor of their analysis before resubmission.
