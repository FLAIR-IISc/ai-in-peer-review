This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q/p, a crucial component in importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and other areas. The central idea is to reframe this estimation task as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose two main formulations based on two different integral identities. The "Type I" formulation stems from the importance sampling identity `E_p[ (q/p) k(x,.) ] = E_q[ k(x,.) ]`, while the "Type-II" formulation uses the property that for a local kernel `k_t`, `E_p[ (q/p) k_t(x,.) ] ≈ q(x)`. By treating the ratio `q/p` as the unknown function, these identities become Fredholm equations.

The authors then solve these equations using Tikhonov regularization in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), leading to a family of algorithms they call Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). This framework is elegant and principled, connecting a common machine learning problem to a well-studied area of classical analysis. It also leads to simple, closed-form solutions for the estimator in terms of kernel matrices, making the algorithms easy to implement.

This work relates to several recent methods for density ratio estimation. It is particularly close to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], which can be seen as an instance of the Type I formulation where the loss function is the RKHS norm itself rather than an L2 norm. It also shares similarities with LSIF [10], especially in the model selection phase. The main distinction and a key contribution of this paper is that the L2-based objective function in FIRE allows for a theoretically sound and practically effective method for unsupervised model selection, which the authors call Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Formulation:** Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a novel and insightful contribution. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms and opens up connections to the rich literature on inverse problems and regularization theory.

2.  **Unsupervised Model Selection (CD-CV):** The most significant practical contribution of this paper is the CD-CV method. A major challenge in density ratio estimation is selecting the kernel and regularization parameters without access to labeled data for the target task. The authors correctly argue that minimizing an RKHS-based objective, as in KMM, is not suitable for cross-validation because the norm's value is kernel-dependent and thus not comparable across different parameter settings. By using an L2 norm, the FIRE objective can be reliably approximated on a hold-out set, enabling effective parameter selection. The experiments demonstrate that this approach works well in practice. This is a significant advance for practitioners.

3.  **Flexibility and Simplicity:** The proposed FIRE framework is flexible, allowing for different norms (L2,p, L2,q, or combinations) and settings (Type I where samples from q are available, Type II where function values of q are known). The resulting algorithms are simple linear algebra operations on kernel matrices.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Theoretical Convergence Rates:** While the inclusion of a theoretical analysis is a strength, the derived convergence rates in Theorem 1 and its corollary are quite slow. The rate depends on a term like `(t / -log λ)`, which converges very slowly. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether faster, polynomial rates could be achieved under stronger, yet standard, assumptions. For instance, spectral assumptions on the decay of the eigenvalues of the integral operator, as used in works like Caponnetto & De Vito (2007), often yield polynomial rates for regularized inverse problems. It is possible that with fixed `t` and stronger smoothness assumptions on the true ratio `q/p`, better rates can be obtained.

2.  **Strong Smoothness Assumption:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the true ratio `q/p` lies in a smooth function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This is a strong assumption with practical consequences that are not fully discussed. For example, if `p(x)` is non-zero in a region where `q(x)` is zero, the ratio is not well-behaved. Furthermore, even if `q/p` is smooth, the inverse ratio `p/q` may not be, which could limit the applicability of the method if one wishes to estimate the ratio in the other direction. A brief discussion of these limitations would be welcome.

3.  **Clarity of Supplementary Material:** The supplementary material is essential for verifying the theoretical claims, but in its current state, it contains numerous typos and errors that impede this process. For the paper to be technically sound and reproducible, this section needs significant revision. Specific examples include:
    *   In Lemma 5, the Sobolev space appears to be `W_2^2` but should probably be a general `W_s^2`. The resulting bound `D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 ||f||_2^2` seems incorrect; the power of the first term is likely different.
    *   Lines 675 and 686 contain incorrect expressions, though the reference to Equation 31 is correct.
    *   In the proof on line 1147, the domain of integration should likely be `||ξ||^2 >= ...`.
    Fixing these issues is crucial for the paper's technical quality.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    1.  The paper presents a novel and principled framework for density ratio estimation by connecting it to Fredholm integral equations.
    2.  The main contribution, the CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection, solves a critical practical problem in a sound and effective way, making it a significant advance over methods like KMM.
    3.  The experimental results are encouraging and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed FIRE algorithms and the CD-CV procedure.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    1.  The theoretical convergence rates are very slow, which is a notable limitation of the current analysis.
    2.  The assumptions required for the theory are strong, and their practical implications are not fully explored.
    3.  The supplementary material contains significant errors that prevent a full verification of the theoretical results.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper with a novel formulation and a significant practical contribution in the form of unsupervised model selection. The weaknesses are primarily in the theoretical analysis and the clarity of the supplement. The practical importance of the CD-CV method is high enough to recommend acceptance, provided that the authors thoroughly revise and correct the supplementary material to allow for proper verification of their theoretical claims. Addressing the questions about the convergence rates and the practical impact of the smoothness assumptions would further strengthen the paper.
