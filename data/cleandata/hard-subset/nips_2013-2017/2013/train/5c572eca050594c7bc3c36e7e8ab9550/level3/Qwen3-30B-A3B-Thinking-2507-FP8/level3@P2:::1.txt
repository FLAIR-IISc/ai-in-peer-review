

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors develop a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) that can handle both Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when values of q are known at sample points from p) settings. The paper provides theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, along with a novel unsupervised model selection technique called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation).

The paper builds on recent work in covariate shift adaptation and density ratio estimation, particularly connecting to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10]. The key novelty lies in the integral equation reformulation, which provides a principled framework for density ratio estimation that is distinct from previous approaches. The authors demonstrate how their formulation relates to classical operator theory, allowing for spectral analysis and theoretical guarantees that are not available in many existing methods.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-motivated theoretical analysis. The authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings on Euclidean spaces and manifolds. However, the theoretical justification for the approximation ∫ k_t(x,y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t) is missing, which is a critical component of their Type II formulation. While the proofs are referenced to supplementary material, the reviewer has not verified them. The paper could strengthen its theoretical contribution by explicitly comparing their convergence rates to minimax optimal rates and discussing how close their Corollaries 2 and 4 are to these optimal rates. Additionally, the paper does not sufficiently explain why a naive plug-in approach (estimating p and q separately) is inferior to their method, which would help contextualize their contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experiments. The FIRE algorithms are described in sufficient detail for implementation. However, the paper would benefit from a brief explanation of Tikhonov regularization for readers unfamiliar with this technique, as it is central to their approach. Including simple 1D toy experiments showing true vs. estimated density ratios would greatly improve the clarity of the method's performance.

**Originality:** The core contribution of reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is genuinely novel in the context of density ratio estimation for machine learning. While related to kernel methods in general [16, 21], the specific connection to Fredholm equations and the resulting theoretical framework represent a significant conceptual advance. The CD-CV model selection technique is also a valuable contribution that addresses a common challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Significance:** Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with applications in importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and transfer learning. The authors demonstrate strong empirical performance compared to existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS). However, the paper would be more impactful with a clearer discussion of how their approach advances the state of the art, particularly in terms of computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees. The paper does not provide sufficient references for the datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS), which would help readers contextualize the experimental results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical framework connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations and operator theory
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels
- Practical and well-implemented algorithms (FIRE) with strong empirical results
- Introduces CD-CV, a useful unsupervised model selection technique

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Missing theoretical justification for key approximation (O(t) term)
- Insufficient comparison with minimax optimal rates
- Unclear explanation of why naive plug-in approach is inferior
- Missing toy experiments to illustrate method performance
- Some references for datasets are missing

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel theoretical perspective on density ratio estimation. The main weaknesses are in the theoretical completeness and experimental illustration. With minor revisions addressing these points (particularly the missing theoretical justification, comparison to minimax rates, and inclusion of toy experiments), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. As it stands, it's a solid submission with significant potential but requires clarification on several key theoretical points. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
