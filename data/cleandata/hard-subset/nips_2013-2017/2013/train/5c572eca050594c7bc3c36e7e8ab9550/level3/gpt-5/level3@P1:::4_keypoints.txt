- Problem & approach: Recasts density-ratio estimation w = q/p as a Fredholm integral equation and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.  
- Two estimators: Type I uses Ep[k(x,·)w(x)] = Eq[k(x,·)] for general kernels; Type II uses approximate-identity kernels kt to approximate q(x) directly.  
- Implementation: Both are converted to empirical inverse problems and solved in closed form via the representer theorem.  
- Theory: Provides finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth compact submanifolds.  
- Model selection: Introduces CD-CV, an unsupervised cross-density moment-matching validation scheme using random test functions.  
- Experiments: FIRE is competitive or superior to KMM, LSIF, and thresholded KDE on covariate-shift tasks with good stability and out-of-sample performance.  
- Relation to prior work: Unifies and clarifies KMM, LSIF, KLIEP and connects to operator-theoretic kernel methods, while adding explicit regularization and out-of-sample estimators.  
- Methodological quality: Sound development with clear assumptions (bounded densities, Sobolev smoothness) and transparent dependence on t, λ, d, m, n.  
- Manifold contribution: Extends rates to manifold-supported data, broadening applicability when data lie on low-dimensional structures.  
- Limitations — dimension: Rates degrade with dimension (td/2 terms), limiting performance in very high dimensions without additional structure.  
- Limitations — practical issues: Computational complexity, scalability, and sensitivity to kernel choices need more discussion.  
- Clarity suggestions: Provide an intuitive argument for Type I identifiability/consistency and clarify the relation between the operator kernel k and RKHS kernel kH.  
- Loss choice: Motivate the mixed loss γ L2,p + (1−γ) L2,q, explain how γ should be set in covariate-shift applications, and its effect on guarantees.  
- Regularity & p: Discuss how smoothness of p and q translates to w, require p bounded away from zero on supp(q), and sensitivity to small p regions.  
- When to estimate w: Clarify scenarios where estimating a reusable w(x) is preferable to directly estimating Eq[u] for specific functions u.  
- Dimensional remedies: Advise on regimes (d, n) where the method is practical and whether random features/Nyström or adaptive bandwidths can mitigate curse of dimensionality.  
- CD-CV choices: Justify choice of random linear and half-space validation functions and assess sensitivity or alternatives like kernel/task-aligned u.  
- Relation to operator literature: Discuss whether using {k(x,·)} as test functions is generally optimal and whether conditional-embedding-style operator equations could offer complementary estimators.  
- Pros summary: Principled inverse-problem formulation, closed-form out-of-sample estimators, solid finite-sample theory, practical model selection, strong empirical results.  
- Cons summary: Need more identifiability intuition, limited high-dimensional scalability, mixed-loss motivation unclear, assumptions on p require explicit discussion.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — timely, well-executed, and advances density-ratio estimation theory and practice.
