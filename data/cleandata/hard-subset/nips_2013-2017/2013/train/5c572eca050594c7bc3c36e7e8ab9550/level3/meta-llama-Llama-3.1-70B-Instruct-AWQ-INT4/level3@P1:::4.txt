This paper presents a novel approach to estimating the ratio of two densities, q/p, by reformulating the problem as an inverse problem in terms of an integral operator. The authors leverage the Fredholm equation of the first kind to develop a principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically. The resulting family of algorithms, known as FIRE, is flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

The paper provides a clear and concise summary of the main ideas, relating them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the problem and its connections to other areas of research, such as importance sampling, covariate shift, and transfer learning.

The strengths of the paper include:

1. Novel approach: The authors present a fresh perspective on the problem of density ratio estimation, leveraging the Fredholm equation to develop a new framework for constructing algorithms.
2. Theoretical analysis: The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the algorithms, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel.
3. Experimental results: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on several datasets, including applications to classification within the covariate shift framework.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

1. Intuitive argument: The authors could provide a more intuitive argument for why optimizing over {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in equality (i), similar to (ii) where bandwidth intuition helps.
2. Choice of cost function: The authors could provide more motivation and links to target applications for the choice of cost function, particularly the mix of L2,p and L2,q in equation (9).
3. Application/motivation: The authors could clarify when estimating q/p helps versus using Eq directly, and provide more context for the importance sampling application.
4. Regulariser and smoothness: The authors could discuss the relationships between smoothness of p and q and smoothness of the ratio q/p, and provide conditions like p bounded away from zero.
5. Dependence on dimension: The authors could discuss the dependence on dimension d and provide guidance on for what dimensions the method is practically useful.
6. Test functions: The authors could provide more motivation and justification for the chosen family of test functions U.

The authors also provide a detailed theoretical analysis and experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
