**Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"**

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the ratio of two probability densities, `q/p`, a problem central to importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and other areas of machine learning. The core idea is to reframe the estimation problem as a classical inverse problem governed by a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors derive two main formulations: Type I, where samples from both `p` and `q` are available, and Type II, where samples from `p` are available along with the values of `q` at those sample points. The ill-posed nature of this inverse problem is handled by Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), leading to a family of algorithms called Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). The solutions are elegant, resulting in closed-form kernel-based expressions. The paper supports this framework with a theoretical analysis providing convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds. A significant practical contribution is the introduction of a cross-validation method for unsupervised model selection, termed Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). Experimental results demonstrate the method's effectiveness compared to related approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF). This work connects density ratio estimation to the well-established theory of integral operators and regularization, offering a new and principled perspective.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Elegance of Formulation:** The main strength of the paper is the conceptual shift of viewing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation. This is an elegant and novel approach that connects a common machine learning problem to a rich body of classical mathematics. This perspective provides a principled foundation for algorithm development beyond simple heuristics.

2.  **Principled and Flexible Algorithm Family:** By leveraging the Tikhonov regularization framework in an RKHS, the paper develops a flexible family of algorithms (FIRE). This approach naturally separates the core inverse problem from the regularization method, allowing for various choices of norms (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS) and kernels. The resulting algorithms are simple, direct, and have closed-form solutions, making them easy to implement.

3.  **Strong Practical Contribution in Model Selection:** The proposed Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method is a valuable and practical contribution. Unsupervised model selection is a notoriously difficult problem, and CD-CV provides a well-motivated and effective heuristic for choosing hyperparameters (like kernel width and regularization) without access to labeled data, using the fundamental importance sampling identity.

4.  **Theoretical Support:** The paper provides a non-trivial theoretical analysis of the proposed estimators, including concentration bounds and convergence rates. While there are some concerns about the underlying assumptions (see Weaknesses), this analysis adds significant weight to the proposed method and demonstrates its statistical soundness under the given conditions.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Strong and Potentially Problematic Theoretical Assumptions:** The paper's core theoretical setup relies on the existence of densities `p` and `q` with respect to an unspecified dominating measure (implicitly, the Lebesgue measure `dx`). This is a strong assumption that can be problematic from a modern machine learning perspective. Many practical scenarios involve distributions that may not have densities (e.g., mixtures of continuous and discrete components) or where one measure is not absolutely continuous with respect to the other. The more general and robust object of interest is the Radon-Nikodym derivative `dq/dp`, which exists under weaker conditions. By focusing on a ratio of pre-supposed densities, the paper's theoretical scope is narrower than it could be.

2.  **Missing Assumptions on Density Support:** A critical issue is the lack of discussion regarding sets of measure zero. The ratio `q(x)/p(x)` is ill-defined or infinite if `p(x) = 0` at a point where `q(x) > 0`. For the ratio to be a well-behaved function, one must assume that the support of `q` is contained within the support of `p` (i.e., `q` is absolutely continuous with respect to `p`). Furthermore, for the ratio to be bounded, one typically needs a stronger assumption, such as `p(x) > c > 0` on the support of `q`. The paper makes no explicit statement of these crucial assumptions, which undermines the validity of the theoretical results and the stability of the practical algorithm.

3.  **Lack of Formalism and Clarity in Problem Setup:** The initial problem formulation lacks mathematical rigor. Key modeling assumptions are not clearly stated upfront. For instance:
    *   The dominating measure `ν` is not specified. The notation `dx` suggests the Lebesgue measure, but this is not formally stated, and the validity of the entire framework depends on this choice.
    *   The domain of `x` (e.g., R^d, a general measurable space) is not formally defined in the introduction, although it is clarified in the theory section.
    *   A formal statement of the function space to which the true ratio `q/p` belongs is missing. The analysis assumes it is in a Sobolev space, but this should be part of the initial problem definition.

This lack of precision makes it difficult to assess the exact conditions under which the proposed method is guaranteed to work.

**Questions for the Authors**

-   Could you clarify the precise assumptions made on the densities `p` and `q`? Specifically, what do you assume about the support of `p` relative to the support of `q`? Is `p(x) > 0` assumed on the domain of interest?
-   How would your framework extend to the more general problem of estimating the Radon-Nikodym derivative `dq/dp`, where `p` and `q` are measures that may not have densities with respect to the Lebesgue measure?
-   The CD-CV method relies on a set of test functions `U`. How sensitive is the performance of the chosen model to the choice of this function class `U`?

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a compelling and elegant new perspective on density ratio estimation. The formulation as an inverse problem is novel, and the resulting FIRE algorithms are practical and well-motivated. The CD-CV method for model selection is a particularly strong practical contribution. However, the paper's theoretical foundation is weakened by a lack of rigor in its assumptions, particularly concerning the existence of densities and the behavior of `p` on its support. These are not minor points; they are fundamental to the well-posedness of the problem being solved.

Despite these theoretical shortcomings, the novelty of the approach and its strong empirical performance and practical utility make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the theoretical weaknesses in the final version by explicitly and formally stating all assumptions regarding the measures, their densities, and their supports, and discussing the limitations of their formulation.
