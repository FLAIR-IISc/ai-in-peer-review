

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation (q/p) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, solved using regularization techniques within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors develop the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithm family, providing theoretical guarantees and demonstrating strong empirical performance.

The paper's main contribution lies in its principled reformulation of density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem. The key insight is that for kernel functions k(x,·), the equality Ep[k(x,·)f] = Eq[k(x,·)] holds when f = q/p. The authors leverage this to develop regularized estimators that replace integral operators with empirical versions. This approach differs significantly from prior work like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, which operate within different optimization frameworks without the same theoretical foundation.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a clear theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and classical integral equation theory. Second, it provides detailed convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds, showing that the method achieves rates of O(n^{-1/(3.5+d/2)}) in the Euclidean case. Third, it introduces CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), an unsupervised model selection technique specifically designed for this problem, which addresses a critical practical challenge in density ratio estimation.

The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-motivated, with careful attention to the relationship between the ambient dimension d and convergence rates. The authors demonstrate that for d > 3, the method's practical utility diminishes due to the curse of dimensionality, though they show improved rates on sub-manifolds with lower effective dimension.

Strengths:
- Well-executed theoretical analysis with meaningful convergence rates
- Principled framework connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems
- Novel CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection
- Strong empirical results showing competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods
- Clear exposition of the connection to prior work, particularly KMM

Weaknesses:
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address why the L2,p/L2,q mixed norm (equation 9) is better than using either norm alone. A more detailed justification would strengthen the methodological contribution.
- The relationship between the smoothness of p, q, and q/p requires more discussion, particularly regarding the assumption that p is bounded away from zero.
- The practical dimensionality limit (d > 3) needs more explicit guidance for practitioners.

Regarding the key points raised in the query:

The intuitive argument for why optimization over {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in equality (I) is that these kernel functions form a reproducing kernel Hilbert space, which provides a natural basis for representing smooth functions. The choice of {k(x,·)} as test functions ensures that the solution is smooth and well-behaved, with convergence rates depending on the smoothness of the underlying density ratio. This is analogous to the bandwidth intuition in (II), where the Gaussian kernel's bandwidth controls the approximation quality.

For the cost function mix, the combination of L2,p and L2,q norms efficiently leverages both samples simultaneously, providing better estimation when both are available. This is particularly valuable when samples from p and q are unbalanced.

The paper's approach to density ratio estimation is well-motivated for importance sampling applications where we need to adjust weights for samples from p to match q. Estimating q/p directly is necessary when we don't have function values of q at the p-samples, which is the typical scenario in covariate shift problems.

The method's practical utility is limited to low-dimensional settings (d < 3) or when the data lies on a low-dimensional sub-manifold. For higher dimensions, the convergence rates become prohibitively slow.

The choice of test functions {k(x,·)} is principled, as it forms an RKHS with natural regularization properties. This approach is related to "Smooth Operators" (Grunewalder et al.), but differs by framing the problem as a specific Fredholm equation with a clear regularization strategy and convergence guarantees.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical framework is sound, the algorithms are well-motivated, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a valuable connection between classical inverse problems and modern machine learning techniques.

Pro: Principled theoretical foundation, strong convergence rates, novel CD-CV method, clear connection to prior work.
Con: Limited discussion of smoothness assumptions, insufficient justification for mixed norm, practical dimensionality limitations not fully addressed.

The paper is of high quality, original, and significant for the field. It advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation with both theoretical and practical contributions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
