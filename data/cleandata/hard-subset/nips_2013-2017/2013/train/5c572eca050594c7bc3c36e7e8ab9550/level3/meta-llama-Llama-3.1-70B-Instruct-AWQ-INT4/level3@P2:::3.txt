The paper "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach" proposes a novel method for estimating the ratio of two densities using a kernel and a Fredholm integral equation. The authors frame the problem of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, which is a classical approach in functional analysis.

The main idea is to reformulate the problem of estimating the ratio q/p, where p is a density function and q is another density or an arbitrary function, as an integral equation known as the Fredholm equation of the first kind. The authors then apply regularization techniques, specifically Tikhonov regularization, to solve this inverse problem. The resulting family of algorithms, called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), is flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel. The authors also introduce a completely unsupervised technique, CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), for model selection and demonstrate its usefulness in various experiments.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors provide a novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation, which is a problem of significant interest in machine learning and statistical inference.
* The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the proposed method, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method in various experiments, including regression and classification tasks.

However, there are also some weaknesses and concerns:

* The assumption of the existence of densities is strong and problematic in machine learning, as arbitrarily close measures may lack ν-densities.
* The estimation of a ratio of densities is therefore problematic from the machine learning viewpoint.
* The paper does not specify the dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue, counting), which affects the validity of the results.
* The results may be invalid or ambiguous because of sets of ν-measure zero.
* The paper does not provide a clear statement of modeling assumptions (e.g., domain of x: R^d or a general topological space).
* The paper does not assume the positivity of the density p (is p > 0 everywhere?).

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation and provides a novel and principled approach to this problem. However, the authors should address the concerns and weaknesses mentioned above to strengthen their results and provide a more complete and rigorous treatment of the subject.
