This paper addresses density-ratio estimation w(x) = q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two estimators are derived: Type I uses the identity Ep[k(x,·) w(x)] = Eq[k(x,·)], which holds for a general positive definite kernel k; Type II leverages approximate identities for δ-like kernels kt to obtain Ep[kt(x,·) w(x)] ≈ q(x). Both are turned into empirical inverse problems using samples from p (and from q for Type I), and solved in closed form via the representer theorem. The authors provide finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth compact submanifolds, and introduce an unsupervised model-selection scheme (CD-CV) based on cross-density moment matching with random test functions. Experiments compare FIRE against KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded KDE baseline on covariate shift tasks and show competitive to superior performance with favorable stability and out-of-sample properties.

Relation to prior work. The Fredholm formulation unifies and clarifies several existing approaches. The KMM condition Eq[Φ(x)] = Ep[w(x)Φ(x)] with Φ the RKHS feature map is precisely the Type I identity with an RKHS loss; FIRE adds explicit Tikhonov regularization and obtains an out-of-sample estimator for w, addressing KMM’s stability and extension issues. LSIF and KLIEP minimize L2,p or KL discrepancies to w within a linear family; FIRE instead solves an inverse problem in a nonparametric RKHS with regularization, avoiding basis approximation error. The paper is also connected to operator-theoretic views of kernel methods (e.g., “Smooth Operators,” conditional mean embeddings, spectral regularization), but here the operator equation is used to target the Radon–Nikodym derivative explicitly, with accompanying statistical rates on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.

Quality. The methodology is sound and carefully developed. The theory states clear assumptions (bounded densities, Sobolev smoothness, Gaussian kernels) and yields explicit finite-sample bounds with transparent dependencies on bandwidth t, regularization λ, dimension d, and sample sizes m,n. The rates degrade with d as expected for kernel methods, and the manifold setting is a welcome addition. The empirical section validates CD-CV and compares fairly to strong baselines; leveraging out-of-sample estimators is appropriate. Some practical aspects could be discussed more (computational complexity and scalability; sensitivity to kernel choices), but overall the claims are well-supported.

Clarity. The paper is generally well-organized and readable despite heavy notation. Suggestions:
- Provide an intuitive argument for identifiability and consistency under Type I when using {k(x,·)} as the test family: e.g., the map f ↦ Ep[k(·,x)f(x)] is injective on H under mild conditions (characteristic kernels, integral operator with trivial nullspace), paralleling the bandwidth intuition in Type II.
- Clarify the relationship between the kernel used in the operator (k) and the RKHS used for regularization (kH), and when choosing k = kH is advisable.
- Expand the motivation for the mixed loss γ L2,p + (1−γ) L2,q (Eq. 9): how γ should be set in typical covariate-shift applications (e.g., prioritizing performance under q vs. stability under p), and whether this affects the convergence guarantees.

Originality. Casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem and solving with RKHS Tikhonov regularization is a clean, unifying perspective that yields simple algorithms, stability via regularization, and rigorous rates including manifold support. CD-CV is a practical contribution tailored to the availability of samples from both p and q.

Significance. Estimating w = q/p underpins importance sampling, covariate shift adaptation, and related transfer tasks. The approach is broadly applicable and likely to be adopted, especially given its simplicity and out-of-sample nature. The dimension dependence limits performance in very high dimensions without further structure, but this is a common limitation. The manifold results broaden applicability when data concentrate on low-dimensional structures.

Questions and suggestions to the authors:
- Intuition for Type I consistency: Under what conditions on k and H is the operator Kp: f ↦ Ep[k(·,x)f(x)] injective on H? Is a characteristic kernel sufficient? A brief functional-analytic argument (e.g., no nontrivial f orthogonal to all kernel sections under L2(p)) would aid understanding.
- Loss choice and applications: Please motivate the mixed loss (Eq. 9). In covariate shift, one usually optimizes target risk under q, suggesting L2,q. What are the empirical or theoretical benefits of mixing L2,p and L2,q, and how should γ be chosen?
- When to estimate w versus directly using Eq: Since the empirical objectives estimate Ep and Eq functionals, in what settings does learning a reusable w(x) bring clear advantages (e.g., reweighting many functions u, out-of-sample deployment, classification/regression weighting) compared to directly estimating Eq[u] for specific u?
- Regularity and division by p: Please discuss how smoothness of p and q translates to smoothness of w = q/p, and the need for p bounded away from zero on the support of q. How sensitive are the methods and bounds to regions where p is small?
- Dimensional dependence: The rates involve td/2 and yield n exponents degrading with d. Provide guidance on regimes of d and sample sizes where the method is practically effective, and whether random features/Nyström approximations or adaptive bandwidths mitigate the curse of dimensionality.
- Choice of validation test functions U for CD-CV: Justify random linear and half-space functions. How sensitive is selection to this choice, and would using kernel features (or task-aligned u) improve robustness?
- Relation to “Smooth Operators” and conditional embeddings: Is using {k(x,·)} as test functions the “right” general approach for covariate shift? Could alternative operator equations (e.g., leveraging conditional mean embeddings) yield advantages or complementary estimators?

Pros:
- Clear, principled formulation as an inverse problem with RKHS regularization.
- Simple closed-form algorithms with out-of-sample estimators.
- Solid theory with finite-sample bounds and manifold support.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection (CD-CV).
- Strong and relevant empirical results against established baselines.

Cons:
- Limited intuition for identifiability in Type I; clarity on kernel/operator choices could improve.
- Dimension dependence may limit high-dimensional performance; scalability discussion is brief.
- Mixed loss motivation and guidance on γ need elaboration.
- Assumptions on p bounded away from zero and support alignment merit explicit discussion.

Recommendation: Strong accept. The paper is timely, well-executed, and advances both understanding and practice of density-ratio estimation for covariate shift and importance sampling.
