## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory, specifically within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors propose two algorithms, FIRE Type I and Type II, and demonstrate theoretical convergence results alongside experimental validation. A key contribution is the identification of Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) as an effective, unsupervised method for parameter selection – a significant advantage over existing RKHS-based methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) where principled model selection is challenging due to the kernel-dependence of the objective function.

The work is well-motivated, connecting to the broader areas of importance sampling, transfer learning, and covariate shift. The formulation as an inverse problem provides a fresh perspective and allows for the application of established theoretical tools. The authors correctly point out the limitations of using RKHS objective values for CV in methods like KMM, a crucial observation. The theoretical analysis, while providing convergence rates, reveals a slow convergence dependent on (-t/log λ), prompting a valid critique regarding the potential for stronger assumptions to achieve polynomial rates. 

However, the paper’s strength – the theoretical framework – also highlights a weakness. The assumption that q/p lies in a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev) feels restrictive and the authors should more thoroughly discuss the practical implications when p/q behaves poorly under this constraint. Furthermore, while the theoretical results are presented, the supplementary material suffers from numerous typos and errors, hindering verification – a significant issue for a theoretically focused paper. Specifically, the corrections outlined by the supervisor (Lemma 5, lines 675, 686, and 1147) *must* be addressed. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel approach, strong theoretical grounding, effective unsupervised parameter selection via CD-CV, clear articulation of limitations of existing methods (KMM).
**Arguments against acceptance:** Slow convergence rate, restrictive smoothness assumption, problematic supplementary material requiring substantial revision.

**Overall:** This is a promising paper with a novel contribution. However, the convergence rate and the quality of the supplementary material are significant concerns. Addressing these issues, particularly the errors in the supplementary material, is crucial for acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent on major revisions addressing these points. The clarity of the writing is good, and the paper is well-organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.
