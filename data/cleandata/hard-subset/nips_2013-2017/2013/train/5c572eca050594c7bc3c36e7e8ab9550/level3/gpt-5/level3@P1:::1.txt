This paper proposes FIRE, a family of density-ratio estimators that reformulate r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solve it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two complementary settings are developed: (Type I) when samples from both p and q are available, estimating Kp r = Kq 1, and (Type II) when q is known (not necessarily a density) at the p-sample locations, estimating Kt,p r ≈ q with a δ-kernel. Representer-theorem arguments yield closed-form linear systems for the coefficients, making the approach simple to implement with out-of-sample evaluation. The paper provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on R^d and on compact smooth manifolds, and introduces a practical unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, that cross-validates kernel and regularization parameters by testing the importance-sampling identity on random test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM, and a KDE plug-in baseline, and include covariate-shift applications.

Relation to prior work: FIRE connects density-ratio estimation to classical operator inversion and spectral regularization (e.g., Tikhonov and related inverse-problem views of kernel methods [3, 20]). Compared to KMM [9], FIRE uses explicit regularization and yields a natural out-of-sample estimator in the ambient RKHS; the authors also give an RKHS-loss variant that can be seen as a regularized KMM with a different optimization procedure. Compared to LSIF/KLIEP [10, 23], FIRE does not rely on a finite basis expansion for r but solves an operator equation directly; its CD-CV model selection measure resembles LSIF’s validation objective yet decouples estimation and testing functions. Theoretical rates are discussed in the context of [14], which gives minimax results for likelihood ratio estimation; however, a more explicit comparison to those optimal rates would strengthen the narrative.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The integral-equation formulation is correct and well-motivated; the empirical objectives are standard Tikhonov-regularized least squares in RKHS with clear closed-form solutions. The theoretical section states high-probability error bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds. However:
  - Several key approximations are used without full justification in the main text. In particular, the “approximate identity” statement ∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) dx = f(y) + O(t) should be stated as a lemma with precise conditions on f, the domain (with or without boundary), and the kernel; a specific citation (e.g., to a standard result on Gaussian mollifiers, or a theorem in [24]) would help.
  - For manifolds and domains with boundary, the dependence of the bias on curvature and boundary effects is only remarked upon; clarifying assumptions and constants would aid readers applying the results beyond R^d.
  - The rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 are nonstandard (e.g., n^{-1/(3.5 + d/2)}). Please relate them to known minimax rates in [14] (and any other relevant work), explain the origin of the exponents (bias-variance-regularization trade-off), and quantify the gap to optimality under your smoothness assumptions.
  - Complexity and scalability are not discussed in detail. Providing time/memory complexity for the main closed-form solvers, and whether Nyström or randomized solvers are needed for large n, would be useful.
- Empirical evaluation: The CD-CV idea is clever and practically appealing. Comparisons to LSIF and KMM are relevant, though some choices are restrictive:
  - The first set of experiments omits KMM due to out-of-sample issues; consider including KMM where feasible (e.g., by retraining on full training points or using standard extrapolation) so readers can see a direct performance comparison.
  - Please add simple 1D toy examples with known p, q and ground-truth r to visualize bias/variance, the effect of λ and t, and to compare FIRE against (thresholded) KDE-ratio and LSIF/KLIEP.
  - Provide references and basic descriptions for CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS, and clarify any preprocessing. Report sensitivity of CD-CV to the number and type of test functions (linear vs half-spaces), and compare to importance-weighted CV [22] as a model-selection baseline.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized. To improve accessibility:
  - Add a short primer on Tikhonov regularization in inverse problems, explicitly relating the operator, source conditions, and the role of λ in stabilizing ill-posedness.
  - Expand the discussion of the role of kernel width t versus regularization λ. In particular, in the special case q=1 (density estimation), clarify how FIRE differs from KDE: FIRE leverages operator inversion and regularization to mitigate the need to take t→0, whereas KDE relies primarily on bandwidth for bias-variance control. A side-by-side comparison would be illuminating.
  - Explain why the naive plug-in ratio q̂/p̂ is inferior: ratio estimators amplify denominator estimation error, leading to instability and large variance in regions where p is small; FIRE directly targets r in a single convex problem with explicit regularization and an operator that averages under p, which improves stability.
  - Make explicit which results depend on k = k_H and which allow different analysis/regularization kernels.

Originality:
- The Fredholm-equation reformulation and the systematic inverse-problem viewpoint for density-ratio estimation, together with RKHS Tikhonov solutions and CD-CV, is a novel and coherent package. The link to KMM as a special case is insightful, and the manifold-support analysis is a nice extension.

Significance:
- Importance weighting under covariate shift and related applications are central to ML. FIRE’s simplicity, out-of-sample estimators, and unsupervised model selection are practically valuable. Stronger empirical comparisons and clearer positioning of the rates relative to minimax bounds would further increase impact.

Pros:
- Clear and principled formulation as an inverse problem with RKHS regularization.
- Simple, closed-form algorithms with out-of-sample evaluation.
- Theoretical bounds for Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection via CD-CV.
- Addresses widely relevant scenarios (covariate shift, MCMC with known q up to constant).

Cons:
- Missing or informal justifications for key approximations; boundary/manifold details could be clearer.
- Limited discussion of optimality of rates; unusual exponents not interpreted.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader (more KMM, toy 1D, sensitivity and ablations).
- Lacks dataset references and complexity/scalability discussion.
- Main text assumes familiarity with inverse problems; brief tutorial would help.

Suggestions/questions for authors:
- Provide a precise lemma (and citation) for the approximate identity with Gaussian kernels on R^d and manifolds, including boundary effects.
- Compare your rates to minimax results (e.g., [14]) and discuss the gap and whether it is due to the proof technique or intrinsic to the setting.
- Add toy 1D visualizations of r and FIRE vs plug-in and LSIF/KLIEP; include more direct KMM comparisons where possible.
- Discuss computational complexity and any use of low-rank approximations.
- Elaborate on CD-CV: provide guidance on the number/type of test functions, and compare against IWCV [22].
- Include proper citations for CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS.

Overall evaluation: Strong, well-written paper addressing an important problem with a principled and flexible approach. I lean to accept, contingent on clarifying theoretical justifications and strengthening empirical comparisons.
