

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density-ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, solved using regularization techniques with Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors introduce two main methods: Type I (importance-sampling equation as L^2 regularization with RKHS) and Type II (kernel with locality → 0 as regularization). The key theoretical contribution is the derivation of convergence rates for both methods with Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, while the main practical contribution is the introduction of CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), enabling reliable unsupervised parameter selection.

The paper establishes strong connections to previous work, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). The authors correctly identify that while KMM is related to their Type I formulation, it uses a different optimization procedure that lacks the natural out-of-sample extension of their RKHS-based approach. They also note that their CD-CV method addresses a key limitation of previous approaches: the inability to use cross-validation for kernel selection due to the kernel-dependence of objective values.

## Strengths

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, the theoretical framework provides a principled connection between density-ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, allowing for the application of spectral analysis techniques. The convergence rate results in Theorems 1 and 3 are comprehensive, covering both Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and include explicit error bounds with confidence intervals.

The most valuable practical contribution is CD-CV, which enables effective unsupervised model selection. The authors convincingly demonstrate that traditional CV approaches are problematic for density-ratio estimation because RKHS objective values are kernel-dependent and not comparable across different kernels. The experimental results on regression and classification tasks show that CD-CV significantly outperforms other parameter selection methods.

The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and helpful comparisons to related work. The experimental section provides thorough validation across multiple datasets and problem settings, including a convincing comparison to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE.

## Weaknesses

The main theoretical weakness is the slow convergence rate in Theorem 1, which includes a (-t / log λ) factor. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not explore whether stronger assumptions (e.g., spectral assumptions from Caponnetto & De Vito 2007) could yield polynomial convergence rates with fixed t. This is a significant gap, as the current rate is not optimal for practical applications.

The paper makes a strong assumption that q/p lies in a smooth function class (e.g., Sobolev), which may not hold in practice. The authors should better discuss the practical limitation that if q/p is smooth, p/q may behave poorly, potentially leading to numerical instability in applications.

The supplementary material contains several errors that hinder verification of the theoretical results. Specifically, Lemma 5 should have W_s^2 instead of W_2^2, and the bound should be D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 ||f||_2^2. Additionally, lines 675 and 686 contain errors that contradict the correct Eq. 31, and the integral domain at line 1147 should be ||ξ||^2 ≥.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

The paper demonstrates high technical quality with sound theoretical analysis and comprehensive experimental validation. The theoretical results are well-supported, though the convergence rate limitation requires attention. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with well-structured mathematical formulations and helpful comparisons to related work.

The originality is strong, as the Fredholm equation approach to density-ratio estimation represents a novel perspective that connects to classical analysis while providing practical algorithmic benefits. The key innovation is the CD-CV method, which addresses a critical limitation in unsupervised learning for density-ratio estimation.

The significance is substantial. Density-ratio estimation is a fundamental problem in transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift adaptation. The paper provides a principled framework with strong theoretical guarantees and a practical solution for the challenging problem of unsupervised model selection. The CD-CV approach is particularly valuable as it enables reliable parameter selection without labeled data, which is a major bottleneck in many real-world applications.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of density-ratio estimation with a novel theoretical framework and practical method. The main weakness is the suboptimal convergence rate in Theorem 1, which could be improved with stronger assumptions. The supplementary material requires correction of several technical errors. With these improvements, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the convergence rate discussion and supplementary material corrections. The authors should explicitly address whether polynomial convergence rates are possible under stronger assumptions and provide corrected versions of the supplementary material.
