- Paper reformulates density-ratio estimation f(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation and solves it with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE).
- Two key identities drive the approach: Ep[k(x,·) f] = Eq[k(x,·)] (exact with samples) and Ep[kt(x,·) f] ≈ q(·) for local kernels (approximate when q-values at p-samples available).
- Proposes Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), an unsupervised model-selection criterion that tests Ep[u f] ≈ Eq[u] using independent test functions u.
- Provides nonasymptotic concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Rd and on smooth manifolds, with explicit dependence on dimension, bandwidth, and regularization.
- Connects to prior work: extends and unifies ideas from KMM, LSIF/KLIEP, and operator-theoretic kernel literature by casting ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
- Theoretical formulation is sound and appropriate for ill-posed integral equations; assumptions (bounded densities, Sobolev smoothness, Gaussian kernels) are standard.
- Rates degrade quickly with dimension and the role of lower bounds on p (to control q/p) needs clearer discussion.
- Positivity and normalization of estimated f are not enforced; practical implications and remedies (constraints/regularizers) are not addressed.
- Empirical objectives admit representer solutions and are easy to implement; mixed L2,p and L2,q variants are interesting but need stronger motivation.
- CD-CV is practical and elegant, but more guidance on choosing test functions U and its stability (and theoretical consistency) is needed.
- Experiments cover covariate shift and supervised tasks and show competitive results, but comparisons with KMM are uneven and scalability reporting (n, m, grids, wall-clock) is missing.
- Suggest adding scalable approximations (Nyström/random features) to improve practical applicability.
- Paper is generally well written; matrix formulas and connections between operator equations and empirical losses are clear.
- Recommend adding intuitive explanations for why using k(x,·) as test functions suffices to recover f (e.g., via characteristic kernels or RKHS completeness).
- Clarify when to use L2,p vs L2,q vs mixed loss and how loss choice aligns with downstream objectives (importance sampling vs risk minimization).
- Discuss conditions ensuring smoothness/boundedness of q/p (e.g., p bounded away from zero) and how to detect/handle small-p regions.
- Summarize dimensional dependence of bounds succinctly to help practitioners assess applicability.
- Originality: novel explicit Fredholm inverse-problem framing plus RKHS Tikhonov regularization and CD-CV model selection—ingredients known but combination and analysis are new.
- Significance: provides principled, reusable ratio estimates with out-of-sample prediction and theory, but practical impact limited by dimensionality and lack of positivity constraints.
- Key questions requested: intuition for identity (i), motivation and selection of mixed loss γ, scenarios favoring estimating f vs direct Eq[u], conditions on p/q for regularity, practical ranges of d where method is competitive, and sensitivity/choice of CD-CV test functions U.
- Also asks to position FIRE relative to Smooth Operators/conditional mean embeddings and justify using {k(x,·)} as test functions for ratio estimation.
- Pros: principled inverse-problem formulation, solid theoretical analysis, practical unsupervised model selection, and encouraging empirical efficiency.
- Cons: rates suffer in high dimensions, no positivity/normalization constraints, limited guidance on loss choice and CD-CV test functions, uneven KMM comparisons and light scalability analysis.
- Overall recommendation: strong, timely paper recommended for acceptance after addressing clarifications on identity (i), loss choice, dimensionality, and regularity conditions.
