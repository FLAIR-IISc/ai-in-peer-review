This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q/p, a crucial task in areas like importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation in transfer learning. The authors propose a novel framework by reformulating the problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This is achieved by considering integral operators associated with a kernel function. Two settings are proposed: Type I, which leverages an importance sampling identity `Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)` for when samples from both p and q are available, and Type II, which uses the approximation `Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q` for local kernels, applicable when q is known analytically at sample points from p.

Solving these integral equations via Tikhonov regularization in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) leads to a family of simple and flexible algorithms, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimators (FIRE). This work connects the density ratio problem to a well-established body of literature on inverse problems, allowing for a principled theoretical analysis. The paper provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators using Gaussian kernels. A key contribution is a completely unsupervised method for model selection, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to validate the learned ratio. This approach is contrasted with related methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Ftting (LSIF), with the authors arguing that their L2-norm-based formulation is more amenable to principled cross-validation than RKHS-norm-based objectives like KMM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Framework:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and novel. It provides a new theoretical lens through which to view this problem and connects it to the rich theory of operator inversion.

2.  **Unsupervised Model Selection:** Perhaps the most significant practical contribution is the CD-CV method for parameter selection. Choosing hyperparameters (like kernel width and regularization) is a notoriously difficult problem in unsupervised settings. The paper convincingly argues that its L2-based formulation enables reliable cross-validation, unlike methods like KMM where the RKHS-norm objective is kernel-dependent and not directly comparable across different parameter settings. This is a major practical advantage.

3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for the proposed estimators in both Euclidean and manifold settings. This demonstrates the work's technical soundness and completeness.

4.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The resulting FIRE algorithms are presented with simple, closed-form solutions that are easy to implement within the standard kernel methods framework.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Convergence Rates:** The convergence rates presented in Theorem 1 appear to be quite slow, with a logarithmic dependence on the regularization parameter λ (e.g., a factor of `(t/-log λ)`). Could the authors comment on whether it is possible to achieve polynomial convergence rates with a fixed kernel width `t`? For instance, under stronger spectral assumptions on the integral operator (as in Caponnetto & De Vito, JMLR 2007) or stronger smoothness assumptions on the true ratio `q/p`, one might expect to see improved rates.

2.  **Smoothness Assumption:** The theoretical analysis relies on the strong assumption that the target ratio `q/p` belongs to a smooth function class (e.g., a Sobolev space). This assumption should be discussed more thoroughly. If `q/p` is smooth, it implies that `p` cannot have support where `q` does not. Furthermore, in regions where `q` is small but non-zero, `p` must also be very small for `q/p` to remain smooth, which could lead to `p/q` being highly non-smooth or having singularities. What are the practical implications of this assumption?

3.  **Supplementary Material:** The supplementary material, which is crucial for verifying the theoretical results, contains numerous typos and errors that make it very difficult to follow the proofs. This significantly detracts from the quality of the submission. For the paper to be accepted, the supplement must be thoroughly revised. Specific examples include:
    *   In Lemma 5, the space should likely be Ws^2, not W2^2. The bound appears to be missing a term and should perhaps be of the form `D1 (t/-log λ)^2 + λ^{1-α} D2 ||f||_2^2`.
    *   Lines 675 and 686 contain expressions that appear incorrect, though the final statement in Eq. 31 seems correct.
    *   At line 1147, the domain of integration should be `||ξ||^2 ≥ ...`.
    A careful proofreading and correction of the supplement is essential.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated framework for density ratio estimation. Its main contribution, an effective method for unsupervised parameter selection (CD-CV), addresses a critical practical problem in the field and provides a clear advantage over existing methods like KMM. The work is supported by both theoretical analysis and promising experimental results.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The theoretical convergence rates are slow, and the strong assumptions underpinning them warrant more discussion. Most critically, the supplementary material is not of publishable quality in its current state, preventing a full verification of the theoretical claims.

In conclusion, this paper presents an interesting and significant contribution. The core idea is strong, and the proposed CD-CV method is of high practical value. However, acceptance should be conditional on a major revision of the supplementary material to correct the numerous errors and a more detailed discussion of the theoretical assumptions and the resulting convergence rates.
