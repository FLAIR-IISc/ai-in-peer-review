

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, establishing a connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems in operator theory. The authors introduce two main algorithms: Type I (for when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (for when q(x) is known at sample points from p), both solved using Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norms. The key contribution is the CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) method for unsupervised model selection, which addresses a significant challenge in density ratio estimation where traditional cross-validation methods are inapplicable.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that connects to existing work on covariate shift (e.g., Sugiyama et al., 2007; Huang et al., 2007) and kernel methods (e.g., Gretton et al., 2007; Kanamori et al., 2009). The authors correctly position their work relative to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) by noting that while KMM uses RKHS norms in the loss function, their approach uses L2 norms that enable effective cross-validation. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, with detailed concentration bounds. The Type I setting is particularly well-motivated by importance sampling theory, while the Type II setting extends to cases where q(x) is known explicitly but sampling is difficult.

The quality of the paper is strong, with rigorous theoretical results supported by experimental validation. The CD-CV method is a significant practical contribution that enables effective parameter selection without labeled data, addressing a critical limitation of existing methods. The experimental section demonstrates strong performance on both regression and classification tasks, with FIRE outperforming alternatives like LSIF and TIKDE in most cases. The paper's connection to classical operator theory provides a principled framework that separates the inverse problem from regularization, leading to flexible and interpretable algorithms.

Clarity is generally good, though the supplementary material contains numerous typos and errors that undermine confidence in the theoretical results. Specific issues include incorrect notation (W2^2 should be Ws^2 in Lemma 5), incorrect bounds, and miswritten equations (lines 675 and 686). These should be corrected for the final version. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the practical limitations of the Sobolev smoothness assumption for q/p, which may not hold in many real-world applications where the density ratio can be highly non-smooth.

Originality is a clear strength. While density ratio estimation has been studied extensively (e.g., Kanamori et al., 2009; Sugiyama et al., 2012), the Fredholm equation reformulation and CD-CV method represent novel contributions. The connection to classical inverse problems provides a fresh perspective that distinguishes this work from previous kernel-based approaches. The authors appropriately cite related work, including the recent theoretical analysis of KMM (Yu and Szepesvári, 2012) and manifold regularization (Belkin et al., 2006).

Significance is high for the machine learning community working on transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift. The CD-CV method solves a critical practical problem in unsupervised learning, and the theoretical guarantees provide a solid foundation for future work. The paper's approach could be extended to other inverse problems in statistics and machine learning.

The main weakness is the convergence rate in Theorem 1, which appears slow due to the (-t/log λ) factor. The authors should address whether stronger assumptions (e.g., spectral assumptions like Caponnetto & De Vito, 2007) could yield polynomial convergence rates with fixed t. The paper also needs more discussion of the practical limitations of assuming q/p lies in a smooth Sobolev space, as this may lead to poor behavior when p/q is not well-behaved.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with strong theoretical foundations, novel contributions to model selection for density ratio estimation, and compelling experimental results. The CD-CV method is particularly valuable as it enables effective parameter selection in unsupervised settings where traditional approaches fail. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of transfer learning and importance sampling, advancing the state of the art in density ratio estimation. With corrections to the supplementary material and additional discussion of theoretical assumptions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The main strengths are the novel Fredholm equation formulation, comprehensive theoretical analysis, and the practical CD-CV method. The main weaknesses are the slow convergence rate in Theorem 1 and the need for more discussion of the Sobolev smoothness assumption. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
