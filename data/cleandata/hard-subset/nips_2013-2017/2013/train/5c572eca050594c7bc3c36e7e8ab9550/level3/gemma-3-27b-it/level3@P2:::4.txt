This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, p/q, which is central to importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The authors formulate this as an inverse problem using Fredholm integral equations and leverage regularization techniques from reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to develop a family of algorithms, FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper provides theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates, and demonstrates empirical performance on several datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important problem with a novel and principled approach. The connection to classical operator theory provides a strong theoretical foundation, and the resulting algorithms are relatively simple to implement. The theoretical guarantees, though with dependence on dimension, are a significant contribution. The experimental results are encouraging and demonstrate competitive performance. This work builds upon a substantial body of related work in density ratio estimation, covariate shift adaptation (e.g., [19, 27, 2, 6, 9, 22]), and kernel methods [21, 17, 16]. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape and clearly articulate the differences and advantages of their approach, particularly in comparison to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem provides a fresh perspective and allows the application of powerful tools from integral equation theory.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The provided concentration bounds and convergence rates offer valuable insight into the algorithm's behavior and limitations.
* **Principled Approach:** The use of RKHS regularization provides a solid mathematical framework and justifies the algorithm's design.
* **Practical Algorithms:** The FIRE algorithms are straightforward to implement and demonstrate competitive performance.
* **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method offers a practical solution to the often-challenging problem of parameter selection in unsupervised settings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dimensionality Dependence:** The convergence rates, while present, exhibit a dependence on the ambient dimension *d*, which may limit the practical applicability of the method to high-dimensional spaces.  A discussion of the practical limitations based on dimension would be helpful.
* **Cost Function Motivation:** The introduction of a combined L2,p and L2,q norm in Eq. (9) is not fully motivated. Clarifying the rationale behind this choice and its connection to specific application scenarios would strengthen the paper.  In particular, how does using both norms affect the performance in scenarios where one distribution has significantly more data than the other?
* **Importance Sampling Clarification:** The paper mentions application to importance sampling, but it isn’t immediately clear *why* estimating q/p is beneficial when both Ep and Eq are being estimated empirically. A more explicit discussion of the scenarios where this approach provides an advantage over directly using the estimated Eq would be valuable.
* **Smoothness and Regularity:** The paper assumes smoothness and boundedness of *p* and *q*.  A discussion of how the smoothness of the *ratio* q/p relates to the smoothness of *p* and *q* would be beneficial, as well as conditions ensuring *p* is bounded away from zero.
* **Test Function Choice:** The choice of test functions *U* for the CD-CV method lacks detailed justification.  Why were random linear and half-space functions chosen?  Are there other families of functions that might be more effective or informative?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in machine learning.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The dimensionality dependence of the convergence rates may limit the practical applicability of the method.
* Some aspects of the algorithm design (e.g., the combined L2 norm in Eq. 9) lack sufficient motivation.



**Specific Questions/Requests:**

1.  The paper states that the optimization over {k(x,·)} ensures convergence in equality (i) - could the authors provide a more intuitive argument for this, similar to the bandwidth intuition provided for equation (ii)?
2.  Could the authors elaborate on the motivation for mixing the L2,p and L2,q norms in Eq. (9) and describe how this impacts performance in different application contexts?
3.  Given the empirical estimation of both Ep and Eq, what scenarios specifically benefit from estimating q/p instead of directly utilizing the estimated Eq?
4.  How do the smoothness requirements on *p* and *q* translate to conditions on the smoothness of the density ratio q/p?
5.  Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the dependence on dimension *d* and for what dimensionalities the method remains practically useful?
6.  What is the justification for the specific family of test functions *U* used in the CD-CV method?
7.  How does this “linearization” approach (framing as an inverse problem involving an integral operator) compare to other related work, such as “Smooth Operators” [Grunewalder et al.] and how does this differ in terms of flexibility and assumptions?



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. While there are a few areas where further clarification and discussion would be beneficial, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and experimental results justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2013.
