

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by framing it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, leading to the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithm. The authors reformulate the density ratio estimation problem as an inverse problem solvable via regularization techniques in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). This approach builds upon and extends previous work on density ratio estimation, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), by providing a more principled theoretical foundation based on classical inverse problem theory.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical foundation using Fredholm equations and regularization is novel and well-motivated.
- The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
- The proposed CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) method for unsupervised model selection is a significant practical contribution.
- The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing methods like KMM and LSIF.

**Weaknesses:**

**Quality:** The paper contains serious theoretical shortcomings that undermine its main claims. The authors assume the existence of densities p and q with respect to a dominating measure ν, which is a strong assumption in machine learning contexts. Arbitrarily close probability measures may not have densities with respect to a common dominating measure (e.g., one measure could be absolutely continuous while the other is singular). This assumption is not justified, and the paper lacks a discussion of this critical limitation.

The paper fails to specify the dominating measure ν (e.g., Lebesgue, counting), which is essential for the validity of the density ratio. Without this specification, the entire formulation is ambiguous. For instance, if ν is Lebesgue measure, the paper must assume p(x) > 0 everywhere for q(x)/p(x) to be well-defined, but this assumption is not explicitly stated or justified.

The theoretical analysis contains potential rigor issues due to sets of ν-measure zero. The paper's concentration bounds and convergence rates assume properties that may not hold on sets of measure zero, which is a common pitfall in measure-theoretic analysis that can invalidate the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, but the missing specification of modeling assumptions significantly reduces clarity. The domain of x (R^d vs. a general topological space) is not clearly stated, which affects the interpretation of the theoretical results. The paper also fails to explicitly state whether p(x) > 0 everywhere, which is crucial for the density ratio to be well-defined.

**Originality:** The connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm integral equations is novel and represents a significant theoretical contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work like KMM and LSIF, though the authors could better highlight the theoretical advantages of their approach.

**Significance:** The density ratio estimation problem is important in transfer learning and importance sampling, so the paper addresses a relevant problem. However, the theoretical limitations significantly reduce the significance of the contribution. The practical results are promising, but the theoretical foundation is weakened by the unaddressed assumptions.

**Specific Concerns:**
1. The paper assumes the existence of densities p and q but does not discuss the implications of this assumption in machine learning contexts where such densities may not exist.
2. The dominating measure ν is never specified, making the entire formulation ambiguous.
3. The paper does not state whether p(x) > 0 everywhere, which is necessary for q(x)/p(x) to be well-defined.
4. The theoretical analysis assumes properties that may fail on sets of measure zero, potentially invalidating the convergence rates.
5. The paper does not discuss how their method handles cases where the density ratio is not well-defined (e.g., when p(x) = 0 for some x).

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, which could be valuable if the theoretical assumptions were properly addressed. However, the critical omissions in the theoretical foundation significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The authors should explicitly state their modeling assumptions, specify the dominating measure, and address the limitations of assuming the existence of densities.

Without addressing these fundamental theoretical issues, the paper's main claims about the validity of the density ratio estimation approach are questionable. The practical results are promising, but the theoretical foundation is too weak to support the paper's significance claims.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Explicitly stating all modeling assumptions (domain of x, positivity of p, dominating measure)
2. Discussing the limitations of assuming density existence
3. Addressing how the method handles cases where the density ratio is not well-defined
4. Providing a more rigorous treatment of measure-theoretic issues related to sets of measure zero

Given these critical theoretical limitations, I recommend rejection. The paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance if the authors could address these fundamental concerns in a revised version.
