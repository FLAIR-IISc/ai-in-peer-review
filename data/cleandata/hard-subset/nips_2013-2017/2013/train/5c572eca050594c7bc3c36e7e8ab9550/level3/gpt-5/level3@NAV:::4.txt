This paper recasts density-ratio estimation f = q/p as a Fredholm inverse problem and derives two RKHS-regularized estimators. Type I exploits the identity Ep[k(x,·) f] = Eq[k(x,·)] and Type II uses local kernels kt to obtain Ep[kt(x,·) f] ≈ q(x). Both lead to empirical Tikhonov problems with closed-form solutions via the representer theorem (FIRE), and the authors propose an unsupervised model-selection criterion (CD-CV) based on test functions U. Theoretical results provide concentration and convergence rates under smoothness and boundedness assumptions, both in Rd and on smooth manifolds, highlighting a critical dependence on ambient dimension. Experiments against LSIF, KMM, and a TIKDE baseline are encouraging.

Relation to prior work is appropriate: Type I connects tightly to Kernel Mean Matching (Gretton et al., 2009) and to the RKHS operator view underpinning “Smooth Operators” and conditional mean embeddings. The inverse-problem lens echoes De Vito et al. and spectral regularization lines of work, while differing from LSIF/KLIEP by not requiring a basis that approximates f.

Quality: The paper is technically solid, with careful operator-based derivations and nonasymptotic bounds. The empirical section supports the claims and the CD-CV idea is practical. Some points would benefit from clarification: positivity/normalization of f, computational scaling and numerical stability for large n, and ablations for U and CD-CV.

Clarity: Generally well-organized, but notation is dense. It would help to consolidate assumptions (smoothness, bounds, p bounded away from zero) and to more clearly separate the cases H = k versus general H, especially where proofs rely on equality of kernels.

Originality: The Fredholm framing and Type II construction are novel and timely. The link to KMM as a special case is insightful and positions the contribution well.

Significance: Importance for covariate shift and importance sampling is high; the operator viewpoint may spur further work on ratio estimation on manifolds.

Questions and suggestions:
- Provide an intuitive argument for why optimizing over {k(x,·)} is sufficient for consistency in Ep[k f] = Eq[k], analogous to the bandwidth intuition for Type II.
- Cost function: you start in L2,p, but (9) mixes L2,p and L2,q. Please motivate this choice, relate it to target applications, and discuss its statistical advantages/trade-offs.
- Importance sampling: since Eq and Ep are empirically estimated to form the loss, when is estimating q/p preferable to directly using the empirical Eq? Clarify regimes (variance, reusability across test functions, sample reuse) where ratio estimation helps.
- Regularization and smoothness: discuss how smoothness of p and q translates to smoothness of q/p, and state conditions (e.g., p bounded away from zero on support of q) needed for stability.
- Dimension: given rates’ dependence on d, for what ranges of d and sample sizes do you expect practical gains over KDE-based baselines and LSIF?
- U selection: justify the chosen families (linear, half-space); how sensitive is CD-CV to U, and can U be adapted to task-specific function classes?
- Broader perspective: is using {k(x,·)} the “right” class for covariate-shift estimation? Please position FIRE relative to “Smooth Operators” and conditional mean embeddings, especially in terms of identifiability and function-class richness.

Pros for acceptance: principled formulation, solid theory, simple algorithms, unsupervised model selection, good experiments. Cons: clarity on assumptions and positivity, dimension dependence, and motivation for mixed norms and U needs strengthening.
