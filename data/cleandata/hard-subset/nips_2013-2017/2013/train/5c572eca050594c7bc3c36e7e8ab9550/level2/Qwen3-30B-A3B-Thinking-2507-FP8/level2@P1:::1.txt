

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem in mathematical analysis. The authors develop a framework called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) that uses regularization within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to solve this inverse problem. The key contribution is establishing a connection between density ratio estimation and classical operator theory, which enables a principled theoretical analysis and provides a flexible framework for constructing algorithms.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it offers a fresh perspective by framing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, which connects to a rich body of work in mathematical analysis. This theoretical foundation allows the authors to provide detailed concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and d-dimensional sub-manifolds, which is a significant advancement over previous work that typically lacks such comprehensive theoretical analysis. Second, the authors introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), a completely unsupervised model selection method that addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning. Third, the resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, flexible, and easy to implement, as evidenced by the clear mathematical formulations and empirical results.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and establishes the connection to related work, particularly KMM and LSIF. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is thorough, with careful statements of assumptions and precise error bounds. The experimental section is well-designed, comparing FIRE with relevant baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and tasks (regression, classification). The results demonstrate that FIRE achieves competitive performance while offering computational advantages.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical novelty and the practical utility of the proposed algorithms. The connection to Fredholm equations provides a principled framework that is both mathematically rigorous and practically implementable. The CD-CV method is particularly valuable as it solves a persistent challenge in unsupervised learning. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and addresses the convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings, which is an important contribution to the field.

A few areas for improvement could enhance the paper further. While the connection to classical inverse problem theory is well-established, the paper could better emphasize the practical insights gained from this perspective beyond the theoretical analysis. For instance, it would be helpful to explain how the Fredholm equation approach specifically enables the development of the CD-CV method. The paper also could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the Type I and Type II settings have different theoretical properties, particularly regarding the error terms. Additionally, while the theoretical analysis is strong, a brief discussion of the practical implications of the convergence rates (e.g., how they translate to sample complexity in real applications) would make the results more accessible to practitioners.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning (density ratio estimation) with a novel theoretical approach that connects to classical mathematical analysis. The significance of the work is evident in its potential to advance both the theoretical understanding and practical implementation of density ratio estimation methods, which are crucial for transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful approach to density ratio estimation. The novel connection to Fredholm integral equations provides a principled framework that enables comprehensive theoretical analysis and leads to simple, effective algorithms. The CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is particularly valuable. The paper's strengths in theoretical contribution, algorithmic simplicity, and experimental validation make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the practical insights from the theoretical framework and to provide a bit more intuition about the relationship between the two settings.
