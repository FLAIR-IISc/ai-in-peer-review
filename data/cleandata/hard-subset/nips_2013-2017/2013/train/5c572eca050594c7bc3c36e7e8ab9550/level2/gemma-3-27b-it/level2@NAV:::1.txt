## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory, specifically within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors propose a family of algorithms, FIRE, and provide theoretical analysis including concentration bounds and convergence rates, alongside a novel model selection technique, CD-CV.

The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is insightful and connects the problem to a well-established area of mathematical analysis. This allows for a principled approach to algorithm design and theoretical guarantees. The authors correctly identify connections to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), clearly differentiating their approach. The theoretical analysis, offering convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in various settings (Euclidean, manifolds) is a significant contribution. The introduction of CD-CV as an unsupervised model selection method is also a valuable addition.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is comprehensive, the practical implications of the derived bounds are not thoroughly discussed. It’s unclear how the convergence rates translate to actual performance gains in realistic scenarios, especially concerning the choice of regularization parameter λ. The experimental section, while demonstrating competitive performance, lacks a detailed ablation study to isolate the impact of each component of the FIRE algorithm and the benefits of CD-CV. Furthermore, the presentation can be dense, with a significant amount of mathematical notation that may hinder accessibility for readers less familiar with functional analysis. A more intuitive explanation of the core concepts would improve clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Introduction of a practical unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV).
*   Competitive experimental results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense mathematical presentation potentially limiting accessibility.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications of theoretical results.
*   Experimental section lacks detailed ablation studies.



**Overall:** This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. While the presentation could be improved and the experimental validation expanded, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions, specifically focusing on improving clarity and providing a more practical interpretation of the theoretical findings. The authors should also consider expanding the experimental section with a more thorough analysis of the algorithm’s components.
