This paper addresses the problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques. The central contribution is the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithm, along with a novel unsupervised model selection procedure (CD-CV). The work provides theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates, and demonstrates empirical performance on several datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting to important problems like importance sampling and covariate shift. The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is a novel and potentially powerful approach, allowing the application of tools from operator theory. The introduction clearly lays out the problem, related work, and the paper’s contributions. The derivation of the FIRE algorithms appears sound, and the use of RKHS regularization is a natural fit. The theoretical results, while potentially complex, are a significant strength, offering guarantees on the algorithm's performance. The empirical evaluation, including comparison to existing methods like KMM and LSIF, is thorough. The introduction of CD-CV for model selection is particularly noteworthy, as unsupervised model selection is often challenging.

However, several areas could benefit from improvement. The notation, while generally clear, could be more consistent. For instance, the use of Kp and Kt sometimes feels arbitrary, and a more systematic naming convention would enhance readability. The presentation of the theoretical results feels dense and could be more accessible to a broader audience. While the authors mention convergence rates, a clearer discussion of the practical implications of these rates – i.e., how many samples are needed to achieve a certain level of accuracy – would be valuable. The experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks details on hyperparameter tuning beyond the range of values used for *t* and *λ*. A more in-depth analysis of the sensitivity of performance to these parameters would strengthen the conclusions. Furthermore, the justification for choosing particular test functions *u<sub>i</sub>* in CD-CV is not fully explained.  Why are linear and half-space functions chosen, and how do they represent relevant scenarios? A discussion on the limitations of these choices would be helpful. The connection to related work, particularly KMM, is well-explained, but a more detailed comparison of computational complexity between FIRE and KMM would be beneficial.  The statement that KMM lacks an out-of-sample extension is not entirely accurate, as methods exist to approximate KMM's output for unseen data, even if it isn't "natural."  This should be clarified. Finally, a discussion of the computational cost of the matrix inversions involved in the FIRE algorithms, particularly for large datasets, is missing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to density ratio estimation via Fredholm equations.
*   Strong theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
*   Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating competitive performance.
*   Introduction of a novel unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV).
*   Clear connection to existing literature and relevant applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense and potentially inaccessible theoretical presentation.
*   Lack of detailed hyperparameter tuning analysis in experiments.
*   Insufficient justification for the choice of test functions in CD-CV.
*   Minor inaccuracies in the discussion of related work (KMM).
*   Missing discussion of computational complexity.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation and theoretical analysis are particularly strong. While the presentation could be improved for clarity and the experimental section could be more detailed, the overall quality of the work is high.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.  It is recommended for acceptance, pending minor revisions to address the points raised. A focus on clarifying the theoretical results and providing more detailed experimental analysis would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
