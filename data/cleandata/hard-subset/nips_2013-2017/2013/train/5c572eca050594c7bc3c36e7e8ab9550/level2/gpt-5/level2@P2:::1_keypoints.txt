- Proposes estimating density ratios w(x)=q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem (Fredholm equation of the first kind) with two formulations (Type I and Type II).
- Type I: uses identity Kp w = Kq 1 and Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS; Type II: uses δ-like kernel Kt to recover w when q is known pointwise.
- Both formulations yield closed-form linear systems (FIRE) via representer theorems.
- Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact smooth manifolds.
- Introduces an unsupervised model-selection criterion (CD-CV) based on importance sampling identities evaluated on random test functions.
- Experimental comparisons against LSIF and a plug-in ratio (TIKDE) with applications to covariate shift in regression and classification.
- Connects closely to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), showing KMM as an instance of the same integral identity with different loss/optimization.
- Differs from LSIF/KLIEP by regularizing the inverse operator in RKHS and offering a Type II setting suited to MCMC/unnormalized models.
- Builds on existing inverse-problem/Tikhonov literature but is novel in applying it to density ratio estimation and providing manifold-aware rates.
- CD-CV relates to importance-weighted CV in uLSIF/KLIEP but is estimator-agnostic and uses random test functions.
- Strength: principled operator-theoretic reformulation yielding simple explicit estimators with out-of-sample evaluation.
- Strength: nontrivial theoretical contributions with high-probability error bounds and clear decomposition of error terms.
- Strength: practical, easy-to-implement unsupervised model selection (CD-CV).
- Strength: algorithms are straightforward linear solves and likely more stable than unconstrained moment-matching.
- Weakness: experiments are modest in scope (small datasets, limited comparisons) and exclude head-to-head KMM comparisons.
- Weakness: no enforcement of nonnegativity or normalization on estimated weights, which can harm importance weighting.
- Weakness: theoretical rates have unusual exponents, depend on t and λ, include fudge factors on manifolds, and lack explicit tuning schedules or minimax comparisons.
- Weakness: assumptions on smoothness and requirements linking m and λ (e.g., m > λ1/3 n) need more critical discussion for practicality and identifiability.
- Weakness: cubic computational complexity (O(n3)) with no discussion of scalable solvers or approximations.
- Clarity: generally well written but dense notation; empirical derivations are compact and would benefit from pseudocode and complexity/memory analysis.
- Clarity: interplay among kernels (k, kH, δ-kernels) and experimental setup (splits, grids, seeds) need clearer exposition; runtime claims need wall-clock evidence.
- Originality: moderate-to-high—Fredholm framing, Type II, and manifold analysis are novel contributions circa 2013; CD-CV is a neat, estimator-agnostic idea.
- Significance: estimating density ratios is central to many tasks; a stable closed-form estimator with unsupervised model selection is valuable but would benefit from broader validation and positivity/normalization constraints.
- Suggestions: enforce nonnegativity/normalization (projection or penalty), provide explicit tuning schedules, compare against KMM/uLSIF/RuLSIF including runtimes, add synthetic L2,p error experiments, discuss p lower-bounds and identifiability, analyze CD-CV test-function family and sample size, and address scalability (Nyström, RFF, iterative solvers).
- Pros for acceptance: principled flexible estimator, meaningful theoretical bounds including manifolds, practical model selection, and simple implementable algorithms.
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical validation and comparisons, lack of positivity/normalization, seemingly suboptimal/unclear rates and missing schedules, and absent scalability discussion.
- Overall: solid and interesting contribution that could have substantial impact with stronger empirical validation and practical enhancements.
