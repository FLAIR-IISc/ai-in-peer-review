Summary
This paper proposes a principled formulation of density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem for an integral operator, leading to Fredholm equations of the first kind. Two settings are considered: Type I, where samples from both p and q are available and one estimates r by aligning Kp r with Kq 1; and Type II, where q(x) is known (not necessarily a density) at the p-sample points and one aligns Kt,p r with q. The solution is obtained by Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding simple closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) via the representer theorem. The authors provide finite-sample error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels for distributions on Rd and smooth submanifolds, and introduce an unsupervised model-selection heuristic, CD-CV, based on testing the importance sampling identity against random test functions. Empirical results compare FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and a baseline TIKDE on regression/classification under covariate shift and on proxy error measures.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which can be seen as an instance of the Type I identity in RKHS; the paper clarifies this connection and offers a regularized inverse-operator viewpoint. It also relates to LSIF/KLIEP (minimizing L2,p or KL divergence over a parametric class), to inverse-problem regularization in RKHS, and to kernel integral operator analyses including manifold settings. The novelty lies in casting density-ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem, providing closed-form regularized solutions with theory (including manifold results), and proposing a practical unsupervised model-selection procedure.

Quality
- Theory: The operator formulation is sound, and the regularized estimators are well-defined. Theorems provide high-probability bounds with explicit dependence on t (kernel scale), λ, and sample sizes (n from p, m from q). The rates (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2) in Type I) reflect the ill-posedness and smoothing bias; assumptions (Sobolev smoothness, bounded densities) are standard. The manifold extension is a nice addition. Some terms (e.g., λ−1/6, λ−1/3) and the O(t) bias in Type II underscore the delicate bias–variance tradeoff; a clearer discussion of the optimal scaling of t and λ as functions of n,m would aid practitioners.
- Algorithms: The derivations are clear and lead to explicit linear systems in kernel matrices. However, unconstrained regression may produce negative ratios; this is important for importance weighting and could cause instability in practice. Complexity is O(n3) without approximation; scalability is not discussed.
- Experiments: The covariate shift applications are appropriate. CD-CV is a reasonable and simple criterion and is shown to correlate with performance on independent test function classes. Comparisons to LSIF are included; KMM is omitted in some cases citing out-of-sample issues, though simple regression post-processing could provide OOS predictions. Baselines could be strengthened with KLIEP and synthetic cases where the true ratio is known to directly evaluate L2 error. Runtime and memory are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well-written and organized. The distinction between the operator kernel k and the RKHS kernel kH is sometimes blurred and could be made more explicit throughout. A brief algorithmic summary (steps, complexity, and recommended parameter ranges) would help. The CD-CV procedure is easy to follow; a note on the number of test functions and its variance would be useful. English and notation are generally clear.

Originality
Moderate to good. The inverse-problem framing is insightful and unifies several existing ideas, with new closed-form regularized estimators and generalization to manifold domains. The CD-CV validation is a practical contribution, albeit conceptually related to LSIF/KLIEP objective surrogates.

Significance
Density-ratio estimation is central in covariate shift, importance sampling, and transfer. The proposed framework is flexible, theoretically grounded, and easy to implement. The manifold results broaden applicability. Empirical gains appear promising, though broader and more rigorous comparisons and scalability analysis would strengthen the impact.

Pros
- Clear and principled operator formulation (Fredholm first-kind) leading to simple kernel estimators.
- Theoretical bounds and convergence rates on Rd and manifolds.
- Unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) that is practical and task-agnostic.
- Closed-form solutions with standard kernel machinery; extensible to different norms.

Cons
- No positivity or normalization constraints on the ratio; negative weights can be problematic for importance weighting and learning.
- Scalability not addressed; cubic complexity limits large-scale use.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader (e.g., KLIEP, KMM with OOS extension) and include synthetic datasets with known ground truth and statistical significance.
- Limited guidance on choosing t and λ beyond CV; theory-derived prescriptions would be helpful.
- Type II assumes access to q(xi), which is strong in many applications; demonstrations in realistic scenarios (e.g., AIS/MCMC) would clarify its utility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate nonnegativity (and optionally normalization) via constraints or link functions (e.g., exponentiated RKHS, or quadratic programs with α ≥ 0), and study the impact on theory and practice.
- Provide complexity analysis and scalable variants (Nyström/Random Features, iterative solvers, early stopping).
- Add synthetic experiments with known r to report L2,p error, and include KLIEP and KMM+regression OOS baselines. Report runtimes and memory.
- Offer practical guidelines for setting t and λ (e.g., from balancing the terms in the bounds), and analyze the stability of CD-CV (variance vs. number/type of test functions).
- Clarify kernel choices (k vs kH) and unify notation. Discuss sensitivity to mismatched kernels.
- Demonstrate Type II on a setting where q is known up to a constant (e.g., annealed importance sampling), and assess robustness to normalization constants.

Questions for the authors
1) How often do negative estimated ratios occur, and how do they affect downstream importance-weighted learning? Have you tried constrained or transformed variants?
2) Can you provide a rule-of-thumb for choosing t and λ that tracks the theoretical optimal rates?
3) How sensitive is CD-CV to the choice and number of test functions? Is there evidence that it is consistent for selecting t,λ as n,m grow?
4) What are the computational costs relative to LSIF and KMM on your datasets? Any results with Nyström/Random Features?
5) For Type I, how does FIRE perform when m ≪ n (or vice versa), and how does that align with the λ−1/6 and 1/√m terms in the bounds?

Overall
A solid, principled contribution that unifies density-ratio estimation with inverse problems, provides new theoretical insights (including manifold results), and offers a simple and effective unsupervised model-selection approach. With stronger empirical evaluation, attention to positivity/scalability, and clearer practical guidance, this work would make a valuable addition to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
