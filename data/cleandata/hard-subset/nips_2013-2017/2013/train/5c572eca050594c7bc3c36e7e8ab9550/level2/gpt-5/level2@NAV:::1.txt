Summary and relation to prior work: The paper recasts density-ratio estimation q/p as an inverse problem via Fredholm equations of the first kind, solved with Tikhonov regularization in RKHS, yielding a family of algorithms (FIRE). Two formulations are covered: Type I uses samples from both p and q and fits Kp(q/p) ≈ Kq1; Type II exploits δ-like kernels to obtain Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q, requiring only samples from p and function values of q at those points. The authors derive explicit representer-theorem solutions, propose an unsupervised model-selection criterion (CD-CV) based on cross-density validation functionals, and provide concentration and convergence-rate guarantees for Gaussian kernels on R^d and smooth submanifolds. Experiments compare against LSIF and a thresholded KDE baseline, with additional discussion of KMM. This connects to NIPS work on kernel mean matching, LSIF/KLIEP, spectral regularization, and minimax analysis for likelihood ratios.

Quality: The formulation is technically sound, with clear operator-theoretic grounding and careful regularization. Theoretical support includes nonasymptotic bounds and explicit dependence on t, λ, n, m, and d. The empirical section demonstrates utility on covariate-shifted variants of USPS/CPUsmall/Kin8nm and shows a practical unsupervised model-selection mechanism. However, experiments are limited in breadth and do not include statistical significance analyses; some important baselines (e.g., uLSIF/KLIEP proper, importance-weighted CV) and real-world shift scenarios are missing. Positivity and normalization of q/p are not enforced, which can matter in downstream use.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written with explicit formulas, but notation is heavy and key choices (e.g., kernel width t in Type II and its bias O(t)) could be more prominently discussed. Pseudocode and a brief computational complexity section would aid reproducibility.

Originality: Framing density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel synthesis that unifies and clarifies relations to KMM and LSIF. The CD-CV criterion is pragmatic and interesting.

Significance: The approach is practically useful for covariate shift and importance sampling (including settings where q is known up to a constant). The theory broadens understanding on manifolds. Impact could be higher with stronger empirical evidence and scaling strategies.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Elegant inverse-problem formulation leading to simple, closed-form kernel algorithms.
- Nontrivial theory (rates, manifolds) and clear relations to prior kernel methods.
- Unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) addresses a common pain point.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited empirical evaluation: few datasets, synthetic shifts, missing uLSIF/KLIEP and IWCV baselines, no significance tests.
- No constraints ensuring nonnegativity/normalization of ratios.
- Scalability and conditioning not addressed (large kernel matrices; no Nyström/RFF).

Constructive suggestions:
- Add comparisons to uLSIF/KLIEP and importance-weighted cross-validation; include significance tests.
- Enforce or post-process nonnegativity/normalization (e.g., constrained RKHS or calibrated clipping) and study its effect.
- Provide complexity analysis and scalable approximations (Nyström, random features), plus ablations for γ in mixed norms.
- Evaluate on real covariate-shift benchmarks and analyze support-mismatch sensitivity.
