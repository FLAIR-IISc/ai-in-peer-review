Summary
The paper proposes to estimate density ratios w(x) = q(x)/p(x) by casting the task as an inverse problem for an integral operator, yielding a Fredholm equation of the first kind. Two formulations are considered: Type I uses the identity Kp w = Kq 1 and applies Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, while Type II uses a δ-like kernel Kt and the approximation Kt,p w ≈ q to recover w when q is known pointwise on samples from p. By invoking representer theorems, both lead to closed-form linear systems (FIRE). The authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact smooth manifolds, and introduce an unsupervised model-selection criterion, CD-CV, based on the importance sampling identity evaluated on random test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF and a plug-in ratio (TIKDE), with applications to covariate shift in regression and classification.

Relation to prior work
The operator equation in Type I is closely related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which enforces Eq[Φ(x)] = Ep[w(x)Φ(x)] in an RKHS; the paper makes this connection explicit and views KMM as an instance of the same integral identity with a different loss/optimization. LSIF/KLIEP minimize L2,p or KL discrepancy to the true ratio using linear function classes; FIRE differs by directly regularizing the inverse operator solution in RKHS and by offering a Type II setting where q is known pointwise (e.g., MCMC/unnormalized models). The inverse problems and Tikhonov perspective build on established work (e.g., De Vito et al.) but are novel in their application to density ratio estimation and in the accompanying rates on manifolds. The CD-CV idea is related to the importance-weighted CV in uLSIF/KLIEP; here it is decoupled from the estimator and uses random test functions.

Quality
Strengths:
- Principled reformulation with a clear operator-theoretic foundation; the use of RKHS Tikhonov yields simple, explicit estimators with out-of-sample evaluation.
- Nontrivial theory: high-probability error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, with clear decomposition of approximation (t), regularization (λ), and statistical errors (n, m).
- A practical, unsupervised model-selection criterion (CD-CV) that matches the target identity; empirically useful and easy to implement.
- Algorithms are straightforward linear solves and likely more stable than unconstrained moment-matching (KMM), with natural out-of-sample predictions of w(x).

Weaknesses:
- The practical section is relatively modest in scope (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS; small n), and head-to-head comparisons with KMM are limited. The justification for omitting KMM due to out-of-sample extension is debatable for covariate shift classification where weights on training points suffice.
- No positivity or normalization constraints on ŵ are enforced; negative weights can occur and Ep[ŵ] ≠ 1, which is known to hurt importance weighting. This contrasts with bounds in KMM and nonnegativity in (u)LSIF variants.
- Theoretical rates involve unusual exponents (e.g., n−1/(3.5 + d/2)), depend on choices of t, λ, and have fudge factors (ε, η) on manifolds. The paper does not provide explicit schedules for (t, λ) attaining the stated rates nor compare them to known minimax results (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2008).
- Assumptions on smoothness (w ∈ W2 2, bounded q, p) and the requirement on m relative to λ (e.g., m > λ1/3 n) could be discussed more critically in terms of practicality and identifiability (e.g., lower bounds on p to control q/p2).
- Computational complexity is still O(n3) without approximation; no discussion of scalable solvers (Nyström, random features, conjugate gradients, preconditioning) is provided.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written, yet notation is dense. Derivations for the empirical solutions are compact; pseudocode and a brief complexity/memory analysis would help readers implement FIRE. The interplay among k, kH, and δ-kernels (t) could be better signposted; sometimes k = kH is assumed and sometimes not. The experimental setup could be described more transparently (exact train/validation/test splits, parameter grids, and seeds). Some claims (e.g., better computational efficiency) would benefit from wall-clock/runtime comparisons.

Originality
Moderate to high. While KMM already embodies the core moment identity, the Fredholm inverse problem framing, the Type II formulation, and the manifold-aware analysis are, to my knowledge, new in the density ratio literature circa 2013. CD-CV is a neat twist on importance-weighted validation that is estimator-agnostic.

Significance
Estimating density ratios is central to covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling. A stable, closed-form, out-of-sample estimator with unsupervised model selection is practically valuable. The rates on manifolds broaden applicability. However, broader empirical validation and constraints ensuring positivity/normalization would strengthen adoption.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Enforce nonnegativity and approximate normalization. Simple post-processing (projection onto the nonnegative cone and rescaling to satisfy Ep[ŵ] ≈ 1) or a penalized objective (add μ(Ep[w] − 1)2) could improve stability and importance sampling performance.
- Provide explicit tuning schedules (t(n), λ(n)) that achieve the stated rates and discuss whether CD-CV recovers near-optimal choices in practice.
- Compare against KMM and uLSIF/KLIEP across all tasks (including classification under covariate shift) with runtime and memory footprints. Consider RuLSIF or related robust variants as additional baselines.
- Add synthetic experiments with known w(x) to report L2,p errors directly and to probe sensitivity to dimension and support mismatch.
- Discuss conditions on p (e.g., lower bounded away from 0 on the support of q) needed for controlling q/p2 and for the operator inversion to be well-posed.
- Analyze the impact of the test-function family U in CD-CV. How many functions are needed? Are results robust to linear vs half-space vs kernel functions? Provide ablations and guidance.
- Address scalability: suggest Nyström/landmark approximations, random Fourier features, or iterative solvers, and assess their effect on accuracy and CD-CV.
- Clarify the fairness of excluding KMM due to out-of-sample issues; for covariate shift learning, KMM’s sample weights are sufficient.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, flexible estimator with clear operator-theoretic grounding and out-of-sample capability.
- Meaningful theoretical bounds, including manifold settings.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection that aligns with the target identity.
- Simple, implementable algorithms.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical scope and incomplete comparisons (notably with KMM/uLSIF across tasks and runtimes).
- Lack of positivity/normalization constraints.
- Rates appear suboptimal/unusual; parameter schedules and minimax comparisons are missing.
- Scalability considerations are not addressed.

Overall, this is a solid and interesting contribution that could have substantial impact with stronger empirical validation and a few practical enhancements.
