This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x) given samples from p and q (Type I setting), or samples from p and access to the function q(x) (Type II setting). This is a well-studied problem with applications in importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The main contribution of this work is to provide a novel and principled framework by reformulating the estimation problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem. By applying Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm, the authors derive a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The solutions have simple, closed-form expressions based on kernel matrices, making them easy to implement. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, deriving concentration bounds and convergence rates for the estimators using Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. A significant practical contribution is the proposal of a completely unsupervised model selection technique called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to evaluate model parameters. The paper's approach connects the density ratio problem to a rich body of work on inverse problems and regularization theory, providing a unifying perspective that encompasses and relates to prior methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Formulation:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and novel. It provides a strong theoretical foundation for the problem, connecting it to classical operator theory. This "linearization" of the problem via integral operators is a powerful conceptual contribution and allows the authors to draw on the well-developed machinery of regularization theory.

2.  **Flexibility and Simplicity:** The proposed FIRE framework is highly flexible. It naturally handles two distinct and important settings (Type I and Type II) and allows for various loss functions (L2,p, a mix of L2,p and L2,q, and the RKHS norm). Despite this flexibility, the resulting algorithms are simple, non-iterative, and have closed-form solutions, which is a major advantage for practical implementation and computational efficiency.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Support:** A major strength of the paper is its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide convergence rates for their estimators in both the Euclidean and manifold settings (Theorems 1 & 3, Corollaries 2 & 4). This level of theoretical detail provides confidence in the method's soundness and characterizes its behavior as a function of sample size and model parameters.

4.  **Practical Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method is an excellent practical contribution. Unsupervised model selection is a notoriously difficult problem, and CD-CV provides a well-motivated and effective way to select hyperparameters (like kernel width and regularization) without access to labels. The idea of using a set of test functions to check the importance sampling identity is clever and appears to work well empirically.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **Comparison with KMM:** The paper rightly points out the close connection between the FIRE formulation with an RKHS loss and the KMM algorithm. It is stated that FIRE can be seen as a regularized version of KMM. However, the experimental comparison with KMM seems to be missing from the main results (Table 1), with the authors noting KMM's lack of a natural out-of-sample extension. While this is a valid point, a more direct empirical comparison demonstrating the benefits of FIRE (e.g., stability due to regularization, or the utility of the out-of-sample extension) would have strengthened the paper's claims. Could the authors elaborate on the computational differences between solving their regularized closed-form solution versus the constrained QP of KMM?

2.  **CD-CV Test Functions:** The performance of the CD-CV method for model selection likely depends on the choice of the test function family `U`. The paper explores random linear and half-space functions. It would be beneficial to include a discussion on the sensitivity of the parameter selection to this choice. Are there guidelines for selecting an appropriate family of test functions for a given problem?

3.  **Assumptions for Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical results rely on the assumption that the true ratio `q/p` (and its square) belongs to a Sobolev space. This is a standard assumption in such analyses, but it would be helpful for the reader if the authors could briefly comment on the practical implications of this assumption and if there are common scenarios in which it might be violated.

4.  **Approximation Error in Type II:** In the Type II setting, the integral equation itself is an approximation (`Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`), introducing an error term on the order of O(t) for the Gaussian kernel. This seems to create a fundamental trade-off, as the kernel width `t` is a parameter that needs to be chosen. The theory handles this by having `t` shrink with `n`, but a more explicit discussion of the practical challenge this poses would be valuable. Does this inherent approximation error limit the performance of the Type II estimator compared to the Type I estimator in practice?

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes significant contributions on both theoretical and practical fronts. The novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a valuable conceptual advance. This framework leads to simple and flexible algorithms, which are supported by rigorous convergence rate analysis. The proposed unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, is a key practical result that addresses a major challenge in this area. While there are minor points that could be clarified, the strengths of the paper are substantial. The work is technically sound, original, clearly presented, and the results are significant for researchers working on transfer learning, importance sampling, and kernel methods. I recommend acceptance.
