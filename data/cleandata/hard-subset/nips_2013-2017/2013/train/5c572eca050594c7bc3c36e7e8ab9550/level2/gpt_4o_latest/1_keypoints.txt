- Paper: Formulates density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and proposes FIRE (RKHS-regularized estimators) with theoretical guarantees and CD-CV model selection, validated empirically against KMM/LSIF.

Strengths:
- Novelty & theory: Connects density-ratio estimation to inverse problems and provides rigorous convergence rates and concentration bounds.
- Algorithmic simplicity & flexibility: FIRE yields explicit, computationally efficient solutions via the representer theorem and supports varied norms/regularizers.
- Unsupervised model selection: Introduces CD-CV to tune parameters in unsupervised/semi-supervised settings.
- Empirical validation: Demonstrates strong performance, computational efficiency, and robustness in experiments.
- Related-work positioning: Clearly situates contributions relative to KMM, LSIF, and kernel methods.

Weaknesses:
- Clarity & accessibility: Dense theoretical exposition may be hard for readers unfamiliar with integral equations or RKHS.
- Limited experimental scope: Experiments are mostly synthetic/small-scale; lacks large-scale or diverse real-world evaluations.
- KMM comparison gap: Criticizes KMM's limitations but lacks direct experimental comparisons or a practical workaround.
- Smoothness assumptions: Theoretical results rely on smoothness (Sobolev) assumptions that may not hold in high-dimensional/noisy data; limited discussion of remedies.

Recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance, with revisions to improve clarity and address experimental and theoretical limitations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides strong, detailed theoretical contributions.
- Offers a novel, flexible algorithmic framework with explicit solutions.
- Addresses practical model selection via unsupervised CD-CV.
- Empirical results show competitive and robust performance.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Presentation is dense and may limit broader accessibility.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks direct KMM comparison.

Summary:
- Valuable theoretical and algorithmic contribution to density-ratio estimation and kernel methods; acceptance would benefit the community if clarity and evaluation gaps are addressed.
