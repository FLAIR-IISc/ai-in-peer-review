- Recasts density-ratio estimation q/p as a Fredholm inverse problem solved with Tikhonov regularization in RKHS, yielding FIRE algorithms.  
- Type I formulation: uses samples from p and q to fit Kp(q/p) ≈ Kq1.  
- Type II formulation: uses δ-like kernels and samples from p plus q values to fit Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q.  
- Provides representer-theorem closed-form solutions for the proposed estimators.  
- Proposes an unsupervised model-selection criterion (CD-CV) based on cross-density validation functionals.  
- Derives nonasymptotic concentration and convergence-rate guarantees for Gaussian kernels on R^d and smooth submanifolds.  
- Empirical comparisons include LSIF and a thresholded KDE baseline, with discussion of KMM; experiments on covariate-shifted USPS/CPUsmall/Kin8nm.  
- Technical formulation and regularization are sound with clear operator-theoretic grounding.  
- Theoretical results explicitly track dependence on t, λ, n, m, and d.  
- Experiments demonstrate practical utility and the unsupervised model-selection mechanism, but are limited in breadth.  
- Missing important baselines (proper uLSIF/KLIEP, importance-weighted CV) and lack statistical significance analyses.  
- The method does not enforce positivity or normalization of estimated q/p, which can affect downstream use.  
- Notation is heavy and some key choices (e.g., kernel width t and its bias O(t)) need more prominence.  
- Pseudocode and computational-complexity discussion are absent but would aid reproducibility.  
- Framing as a Fredholm inverse problem is a novel, unifying contribution connecting KMM and LSIF.  
- Approach is practically useful for covariate shift and settings where q known up to a constant; theory extends to manifold settings.  
- Impact limited by empirical scope and lack of scalability strategies for large kernel matrices.  
- Suggested improvements: add uLSIF/KLIEP and IWCV comparisons with significance tests, enforce or post-process nonnegativity/normalization, provide complexity analysis and scalable approximations (Nyström/RFF), and evaluate on real covariate-shift benchmarks and support-mismatch scenarios.
