

The paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces. This formulation establishes a connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, leading to a principled framework for constructing algorithms (FIRE: Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) and providing theoretical analysis. The authors provide detailed error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on manifolds, and introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for unsupervised model selection.

The quality of the work is strong, with technically sound theoretical analysis that addresses both Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when q values are known at sample points) settings. The theoretical results are well-justified with appropriate assumptions and detailed concentration bounds. The experimental validation demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods (LSIF, KMM, TIKDE) across multiple datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) with careful consideration of different test functions for model selection. The paper effectively addresses the challenging problem of unsupervised model selection through CD-CV, which represents a significant practical contribution.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The mathematical formulation is precise and well-organized, with clear connections to classical analysis and machine learning. The relationship to existing methods (particularly KMM) is thoroughly explained, highlighting both conceptual similarities and methodological differences. The algorithms are described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the notation is consistently applied throughout.

The originality of the work is significant. While density ratio estimation has been studied extensively, the Fredholm equation formulation provides a fresh theoretical perspective that unifies and extends previous approaches. The paper demonstrates how KMM can be viewed within this framework, offering a more principled approach with better stability properties. The CD-CV method for model selection is particularly innovative, addressing a critical challenge in unsupervised learning that has received limited attention in prior work.

The significance of the work is high. Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with broad applications in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling. The FIRE framework provides a flexible, theoretically grounded approach that achieves competitive performance with better computational efficiency than alternatives. The connection to classical inverse problems opens new avenues for theoretical analysis and algorithm design in this important area.

One area for improvement would be to provide more concrete guidance on the practical implementation of the theoretical results. While the paper provides convergence rates, it could better explain how these translate to practical choices of parameters (like t and λ) for different problem sizes and data distributions. A brief discussion of computational complexity comparisons with existing methods would also strengthen the practical relevance.

The paper is well-motivated and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The theoretical insights are rigorous, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the practical algorithms have clear utility. The connection to classical inverse problems provides a novel perspective on a well-studied problem, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical implementation of density ratio estimation.

The work is well within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel mathematical approach that combines classical analysis with modern machine learning techniques. The paper's theoretical contributions and practical algorithms make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
