## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory. The core idea – connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems – is a significant and potentially fruitful direction. The authors propose a family of algorithms, FIRE, and provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, alongside a practical model selection technique, CD-CV.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the connections to importance sampling, covariate shift, and existing literature like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). The authors accurately position their work within the broader context of transfer learning and kernel methods. The theoretical analysis is a strong point, providing rigorous bounds under reasonable assumptions. The introduction of CD-CV is a valuable contribution, offering an unsupervised approach to parameter selection, a notoriously difficult problem.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is present, the convergence rates, particularly for manifolds, involve a somewhat restrictive epsilon dependence, diminishing their practical appeal. The experimental section, while demonstrating promising results, lacks a detailed ablation study. It's unclear how sensitive the performance is to the choice of test functions in CD-CV, and a more thorough comparison with strong baselines (beyond TIKDE and LSIF) would strengthen the claims. The presentation could be more concise; the paper feels somewhat repetitive in its explanations of the mathematical framework. Furthermore, the discussion of related work, while extensive, could be more focused on highlighting the *specific* advantages of FIRE over existing methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and convergence rates.
*   Practical contribution of the CD-CV model selection method.
*   Clear connection to existing literature and well-motivated problem setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Convergence rates have restrictive dependencies.
*   Experimental evaluation lacks depth and a comprehensive baseline comparison.
*   Presentation could be more concise.
*   Limited discussion of practical considerations and computational complexity.

**Overall:**

Despite some shortcomings, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The novel formulation and theoretical analysis are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed experimental evaluation and a more focused discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results. The authors should also address the restrictive epsilon dependence in the convergence rates if possible.
