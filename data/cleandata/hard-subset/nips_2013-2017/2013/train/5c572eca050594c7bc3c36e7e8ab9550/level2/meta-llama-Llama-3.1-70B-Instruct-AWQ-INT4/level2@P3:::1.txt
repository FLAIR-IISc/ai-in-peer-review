**Summary of the Paper**

The paper "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach" addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two density functions, p and q, where p is given by a sample and q is either a known function or another probability density function given by a sample. The authors reformulate the problem as an inverse problem using the Fredholm equation of the first kind and solve it using regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). They develop a family of algorithms, called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which are flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel formulation**: The authors provide a new and interesting formulation of the density ratio estimation problem as an inverse problem using the Fredholm equation of the first kind.
2. **Theoretical analysis**: The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the algorithms, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel.
3. **Simple and flexible algorithms**: The FIRE algorithms are easy to implement and can be used in various settings, including unsupervised and semi-supervised learning.
4. **Experimental results**: The authors provide encouraging experimental results, including applications to classification within the covariate shift framework.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited experimental evaluation**: The experimental evaluation is limited to a few datasets and settings, and more extensive experiments would be needed to fully assess the performance of the algorithms.
2. **Comparison to other methods**: While the authors compare their method to some other algorithms, a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art methods would be helpful.
3. **Assumptions on the data**: The theoretical analysis assumes that the data is sampled from a distribution with certain properties (e.g., smoothness), and it is not clear how the algorithms would perform in more general settings.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper provides a novel formulation of the density ratio estimation problem and develops a family of algorithms that are simple, flexible, and easy to implement. The theoretical analysis and experimental results demonstrate the potential of the approach. The paper is significant because it provides a new perspective on the density ratio estimation problem and offers a promising solution for various applications, including covariate shift and importance sampling.

**Clarity and Organization**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear introduction to the problem and the proposed approach, and the theoretical analysis and experimental results are well-organized and easy to understand.

**Recommendation**

I recommend accepting the paper, subject to minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above.
