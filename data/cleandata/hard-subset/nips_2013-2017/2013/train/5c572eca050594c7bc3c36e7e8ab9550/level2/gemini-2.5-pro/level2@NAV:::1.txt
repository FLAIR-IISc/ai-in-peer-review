This paper presents a novel and principled framework for estimating the density ratio q/p, a fundamental problem in areas like importance sampling and covariate shift. The core contribution is to reformulate density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This perspective allows the authors to leverage the well-established theory of Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to a flexible family of algorithms, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which possess simple, closed-form solutions. The work clearly relates this formulation to prior methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF), positioning FIRE as a well-grounded and regularized alternative.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its originality and theoretical quality. Framing the problem in terms of integral operators is an elegant and powerful idea that connects machine learning to classical analysis. This foundation is used to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, for data on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. This level of theoretical support is a significant contribution. Another major strength is the introduction of Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), a practical and fully unsupervised method for model selection. This addresses a critical challenge in applying such estimators, substantially increasing the practical significance of the work. The paper is well-written, clear, and logically structured.

The main weaknesses are minor but worth noting. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating strong performance, could be more comprehensive. For instance, a direct empirical comparison to KMM is omitted from the main results, though it is discussed as the most closely related algorithm. Acknowledging KMM’s limitations while still providing a comparison would have strengthened the paper’s claims. Furthermore, the paper lacks a discussion on limitations, most notably the computational complexity. The proposed algorithms require inverting an n x n kernel matrix, which scales cubically with the number of samples from p, making it potentially challenging for very large datasets.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a significant and elegant framework for an important problem. The novel formulation, strong theoretical guarantees, and practical model selection technique make a compelling case for acceptance. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contributions.
