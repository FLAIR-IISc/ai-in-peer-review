This paper tackles the problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm equations and Tikhonov regularization. The core idea – leveraging operator theory for a well-studied statistical problem – is appealing and offers a principled framework. The authors provide theoretical guarantees (concentration bounds and convergence rates) for the Gaussian kernel and introduce a novel unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV). The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and addresses a relevant problem in machine learning, particularly in the context of transfer learning and covariate shift.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  The Fredholm equation approach provides a fresh perspective on density ratio estimation.  Relating this to classical operator theory is a significant contribution, allowing application of established analysis tools.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The derivation of concentration bounds and convergence rates, even with assumptions, is valuable. The extension to manifolds is a nice addition.  While the rates are somewhat standard for kernel methods, having them formalized in this context is important.
* **CD-CV Method:** The Cross-Density Cross-Validation method for model selection is a clever and practical contribution. The unsupervised nature is particularly attractive.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanation of the algorithms is easy to follow.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors do a good job relating their work to existing literature, particularly KMM and LSIF, and highlighting the differences and advantages of their approach.  The extensive list of references demonstrates familiarity with the field.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Impact of Theoretical Results:** The convergence rates obtained, while mathematically sound, appear to depend on rather strong assumptions and may not translate to highly competitive performance in practical applications, particularly in high dimensions. The dependence on `n` (sample size) is relatively high (e.g., `n^-3.5+d/2`).  A discussion of the practical implications of these rates would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While experiments are presented, they are somewhat limited in scope.  The comparison with TIKDE and LSIF is good, but a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets and with more diverse settings (e.g., higher dimensionality, more complex distributions) would strengthen the claims. More details about the hyperparameter tuning for baseline methods would also be useful.  The choice of test functions `U` in CD-CV is not thoroughly justified.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn’t explicitly address the computational complexity of the FIRE algorithms, especially the matrix inversions involved. For large datasets, this could be a significant bottleneck. A discussion of computational considerations would be valuable.
* **Redundancy & Detail:** Some sections, particularly the detailed explanation of algorithms with different norm choices, feel a bit repetitive. The level of detail in some derivations might be reduced without sacrificing clarity.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption that `qp` is in a Sobolev space is somewhat strong and might not hold in all practical scenarios. It would be good to discuss the robustness of the method to violations of this assumption.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in machine learning.
* The theoretical guarantees, while possibly conservative, provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms.
* The CD-CV method is a valuable contribution to unsupervised model selection.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical impact of the theoretical results is uncertain.
* The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited.
* The computational complexity is not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-motivated paper with a novel contribution. While the theoretical results might be somewhat conservative and the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the overall quality of the work is high. The Fredholm equation formulation is a significant conceptual advance, and the CD-CV method is a practical addition. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, primarily focusing on a more detailed discussion of practical implications and computational complexity, and expanding the empirical evaluation. Specifically, I suggest including a discussion of runtime, memory requirements, and a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity.
