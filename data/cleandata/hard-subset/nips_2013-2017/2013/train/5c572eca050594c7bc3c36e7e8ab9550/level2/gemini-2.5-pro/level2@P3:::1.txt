This paper presents a novel framework for the problem of density ratio estimation, q/p. The core idea is to reformulate this estimation task as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This is achieved by considering the action of an integral operator with a kernel on the density ratio. Two primary settings are developed. The "Type I" setting, applicable when samples from both distributions p and q are available, is based on the identity Kp(q/p) = Kq(1). The "Type II" setting, for cases where q is a known function and samples are drawn from p, relies on the approximation Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q for a local kernel Kt.

Recognizing that these Fredholm equations are ill-posed, the work proposes solving them using Tikhonov regularization with a penalty defined by a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm. This principled approach leads to a family of simple and flexible algorithms, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The solutions are derived in closed form as linear combinations of kernel functions centered at the data points, making them easy to implement.

The paper provides significant theoretical contributions, establishing concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators using Gaussian kernels. The analysis covers data defined on both Euclidean spaces and smooth sub-manifolds. A key practical contribution is an unsupervised model selection method called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to tune hyperparameters without labeled data. Experimental results on several datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the FIRE algorithms and the CD-CV procedure in comparison to existing methods like LSIF and a kernel density-based baseline.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm problem is elegant and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms. The derivations are clear and the resulting algorithms are well-specified. A major strength is the detailed theoretical analysis in Section 3, which provides convergence rates for the FIRE estimators. This level of theoretical rigor is often missing in purely empirical papers and lends significant credibility to the method. The work is presented as a complete contribution, spanning formulation, algorithm derivation, theoretical guarantees, a model selection procedure, and experimental validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the main ideas. The distinction between the Type I and Type II settings is clear, as are the scenarios in which each is applicable. The derivation of the algorithms from the regularized objective functions is straightforward. The description of the CD-CV method for model selection is also easy to understand.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to clarify the notation in Section 2. The use of multiple similar-looking kernel matrices (e.g., Kp,p, Kq,p, KH) can be slightly confusing on first read. A small table summarizing these matrices and their definitions could enhance readability. Additionally, while the theoretical results are stated clearly, a brief discussion contextualizing the convergence rates—for example, by comparing them to known minimax rates for this problem—would be beneficial for the reader.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While kernel methods and Tikhonov regularization are standard tools, their application to the density ratio problem via the Fredholm integral equation framework is novel. This perspective provides new insights and distinguishes the work from prior art. For instance, while related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), the proposed framework naturally incorporates regularization, leading to a more stable problem, and provides a clear out-of-sample extension. The proposed CD-CV procedure is also a novel and practical contribution, addressing the critical and often difficult problem of hyperparameter tuning in an unsupervised setting. The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself relative to existing literature, clearly articulating how its formulation differs from and improves upon methods like KMM and LSIF.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have a considerable impact. Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem in machine learning with direct applications to important areas like covariate shift adaptation, transfer learning, and importance sampling. The FIRE algorithms are not only theoretically well-founded but also simple to implement, involving standard kernel matrix operations. The development of the CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is a particularly important practical contribution, as it makes the overall method far more accessible and usable for practitioners. Researchers working on covariate shift and related problems are very likely to use these ideas and build upon this framework.

The experimental section demonstrates strong performance, but a more direct comparison to KMM in the supervised learning tasks would be valuable. While the paper notes the difficulty of out-of-sample extension for KMM, addressing this (e.g., by fitting a regressor to the KMM weights) would provide a more complete comparison for practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The novel and principled formulation of density ratio estimation as a regularized inverse problem provides a new and powerful perspective on a fundamental machine learning task.
2.  The resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, flexible, and presented in easily implementable closed forms.
3.  The work is supported by a strong theoretical analysis, including explicit convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings.
4.  The introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection is a significant practical contribution that addresses a key challenge in the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
This reviewer sees no major flaws that would warrant rejection. The suggestions for improving clarity and expanding the experimental comparison are minor and could be addressed in a final version. The overall contribution is strong, novel, and significant.
