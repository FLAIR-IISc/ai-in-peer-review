- Proposes formulating density-ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind with two settings (Type I and Type II).  
- Solves the inverse problem via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) through the representer theorem.  
- Provides finite-sample error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and smooth submanifolds.  
- Introduces an unsupervised model-selection heuristic (CD-CV) that tests the importance-sampling identity against random test functions.  
- Clarifies connections to prior methods: KMM (as a Type I RKHS instance), LSIF/KLIEP, and inverse-problem regularization analyses.  
- Novelty lies in the explicit Fredholm inverse-problem framing, closed-form regularized estimators, manifold extensions, and practical CD-CV procedure.  
- Theoretical analysis is sound with high-probability bounds depending on kernel scale t, regularization λ, and sample sizes; rates reflect ill-posedness and smoothing bias.  
- Manifold extension is a valuable addition; some bias–variance tradeoff terms (e.g., λ powers, O(t) bias) need clearer practical guidance on choosing t and λ.  
- Algorithms lead to explicit linear systems in kernel matrices, but unconstrained regression can produce negative ratio estimates, problematic for importance weighting.  
- Computational complexity is O(n^3) without approximations; scalability and approximate/efficient solvers are not discussed.  
- Experiments on covariate shift are appropriate and show CD-CV correlates with performance, but some baselines (KLIEP, KMM OOS) and synthetic ground-truth comparisons are missing.  
- Runtime and memory usage are not reported, limiting assessment of practical scalability.  
- Paper is mostly well-written; distinction between operator kernel k and RKHS kernel kH should be made more explicit throughout.  
- A brief algorithmic summary (steps, complexity, recommended parameter ranges) would aid practitioners.  
- CD-CV is simple and practical, but discussion of number of test functions and variance would be helpful.  
- Originality is moderate–good: the framing unifies existing ideas and contributes new theoretical and practical elements.  
- Significance: method is flexible, theoretically grounded, easy to implement, and broadened by manifold results, but impact would increase with stronger empirical and scalability analyses.  
- Pros: principled operator formulation, theoretical bounds on Rd and manifolds, practical unsupervised model selection, closed-form kernel solutions.  
- Cons: no positivity/normalization constraints (negative weights possible), scalability not addressed, experimental baselines and synthetic evaluations limited, limited practical guidance on t and λ, strong Type II assumptions.  
- Suggested improvements: enforce nonnegativity or normalization, add scalable variants (Nyström/Random Features, iterative solvers), include synthetic ground-truth experiments and more baselines, report runtimes/memory, and provide practical rules for t and λ.  
- Suggested clarity fixes: unify notation for kernels, discuss sensitivity to kernel mismatch, and provide algorithmic recommendations.  
- Recommend demonstrating Type II in realistic settings (e.g., AIS/MCMC) and assessing robustness to unknown normalization constants.  
- Key questions asked: frequency and impact of negative ratios; rule-of-thumb for t and λ; CD-CV sensitivity and consistency; computational costs vs LSIF/KMM and any Nyström/RF results; performance when m ≪ n or vice versa.  
- Overall: a solid, principled contribution with useful theory and practical ideas; acceptance favored pending stronger empirical evaluation, positivity handling, scalability, and clearer practical guidance.
