

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques from the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors introduce the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms, which includes both Type I and Type II settings, and provide a detailed theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. They also propose a novel unsupervised model selection method called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) to address the challenging problem of parameter selection in unsupervised density ratio estimation.

The paper's main strengths are its principled connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problem theory, the theoretical rigor with which they analyze their approach (providing error bounds and convergence rates), and the introduction of CD-CV which solves a significant practical challenge. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) are comprehensive and cover both Euclidean and manifold settings. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance against existing methods like KMM and LSIF on standard datasets, with the added benefit of computational efficiency.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with related work. While the connection to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) is mentioned, the paper does not fully articulate how their approach differs in terms of implementation, theoretical guarantees, or practical advantages. The KMM method also uses an RKHS framework but has a different optimization procedure, and it would be valuable to explicitly contrast these aspects. Additionally, the theoretical analysis claims "detailed theoretical analysis" but the convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4) appear to be similar to those established in prior work on density ratio estimation (e.g., [14] establishes minimax rates for likelihood ratio estimation). The paper should better highlight how their analysis advances beyond existing theoretical results.

The paper's claim that "q does not have to be a density function" in the Type II setting is interesting but lacks concrete examples of practical applications where this is advantageous. Providing such examples would strengthen the motivation for the Type II formulation. Furthermore, the theoretical analysis assumes that both p and q are bounded (q(x) < Γ, p(x) < Γ), which might be restrictive in some real-world applications where density functions can have unbounded support or heavy tails.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations is clearly explained, and the algorithms are presented with sufficient mathematical detail. The experimental section is thorough, with multiple datasets and performance metrics, though it would be beneficial to include more recent baselines beyond LSIF and KMM.

The significance of the work is clear: density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem in transfer learning and importance sampling, and the FIRE framework provides a theoretically grounded, flexible, and practical approach. The CD-CV method is particularly noteworthy as it addresses a persistent challenge in unsupervised learning.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a more detailed comparison with KMM and other related methods, explicitly discussing implementation differences and theoretical advantages.
2. Providing concrete examples where the Type II setting (q not necessarily a density) is practically useful.
3. Clarifying how the theoretical results advance beyond existing minimax rates for density ratio estimation.
4. Considering whether the boundedness assumption on p and q can be relaxed or if there are natural ways to handle unbounded densities.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on density ratio estimation. The connection to inverse problems is novel, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are promising. With the suggested improvements, the paper would be even more impactful and well-positioned for publication at NIPS.
