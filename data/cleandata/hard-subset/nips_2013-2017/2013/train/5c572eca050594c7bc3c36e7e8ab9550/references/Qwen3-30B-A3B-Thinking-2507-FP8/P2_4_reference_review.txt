

# Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation (q(x)/p(x)) by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem. The authors develop two algorithms, FIRE Type I (when samples from both p and q are available) and FIRE Type II (when q is known at sample points from p), using Tikhonov regularization in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The paper provides theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and introduces a novel unsupervised parameter selection method called CD-CV. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical operator theory, offering a unifying perspective that differs from existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-motivated error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings (Theorems 1-4). The authors carefully distinguish between the two settings and provide precise conditions under which their bounds hold. The experimental section demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and tasks (regression, classification). The CD-CV parameter selection method is particularly valuable for unsupervised learning, addressing a significant practical challenge.

**Originality:** The core contribution—formulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation—is genuinely novel. While density ratio estimation has been studied extensively (e.g., [6, 9, 10, 22]), the authors establish a fresh connection to classical inverse problems and spectral theory, which is not present in existing work. The FIRE algorithms are new, though related to KMM (as noted in the paper), and the CD-CV method for parameter selection is original. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly by emphasizing the RKHS regularization framework and the theoretical machinery from operator theory.

**Significance:** Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with broad applications in transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift adaptation. This paper provides a principled theoretical foundation that unifies existing approaches and offers new insights. The theoretical guarantees for Gaussian kernels on manifolds (Theorem 3) are particularly significant for high-dimensional data. The CD-CV method directly addresses a major practical challenge in unsupervised learning, making the work immediately applicable to real-world problems.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity:** While well-organized, the paper could improve in several areas. The notation for L2,p norm is inconsistent (sometimes written as ‖·‖2,p, sometimes as ‖·‖2,p). The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation could be clearer. For instance, the paper mentions "for small enough t" in Theorem 1 but doesn't provide guidance on how to select t in practice. The experimental section would benefit from more detailed descriptions of parameter ranges and computational complexity.

**Theoretical Scope:** The theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels, which might restrict the generality of the results. While the authors justify this choice for the theoretical analysis, it would be valuable to discuss how their approach might extend to other kernels. The paper also doesn't thoroughly compare the computational complexity of FIRE with existing methods, which is important for practical adoption.

**Experimental Depth:** The experiments are thorough but could be more comprehensive. The comparison with KMM is limited (the authors note they don't compare due to out-of-sample extension requirements), which is a missed opportunity to directly contrast their RKHS-based approach with KMM's. The paper could also include more datasets or more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical framework is novel, rigorous, and well-motivated, providing a fresh perspective on an important problem. The algorithms are well-designed, with strong theoretical guarantees and competitive empirical performance. The CD-CV method is particularly noteworthy for addressing a practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

The paper meets NIPS standards in quality, originality, and significance. While there are minor clarity issues and some limitations in the theoretical scope, these do not undermine the paper's overall contribution. The work advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation by providing a principled framework that bridges classical analysis and modern machine learning.

## Recommendation
**Accept**

**Justification for Acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for density ratio estimation that connects it to classical inverse problems. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the algorithms are well-motivated and implemented, and the empirical results are strong. The CD-CV parameter selection method is a particularly valuable contribution. While the theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels, the paper provides a solid foundation that could be extended to other kernels. The work is clearly distinct from previous approaches, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field that will be of interest to NIPS readers.
