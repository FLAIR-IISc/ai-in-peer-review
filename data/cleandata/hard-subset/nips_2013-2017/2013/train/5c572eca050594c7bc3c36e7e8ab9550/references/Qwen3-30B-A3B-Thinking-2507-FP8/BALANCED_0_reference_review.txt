

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors develop two main algorithms (Type I and Type II), collectively called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), and provide theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds. The paper also introduces CD-CV, an unsupervised cross-validation technique for parameter selection.

The work builds on the classical mathematical theory of inverse problems while connecting to recent machine learning approaches for density ratio estimation. It relates to KMM [9] and LSIF [10], but presents a fundamentally different perspective by framing the problem as an inverse problem rather than a direct optimization problem. The authors establish connections to spectral theory and classical analysis, which is a notable conceptual contribution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Novelty:** The paper's main contribution is reframing density ratio estimation as a classical Fredholm equation, providing a new theoretical perspective that connects machine learning to mathematical analysis. This is a significant conceptual advance.

2. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides rigorous error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean space and on sub-manifolds, which is a substantial theoretical contribution.

3. **Practical Algorithm Design:** The FIRE algorithms are simple, explicit, and easily implementable. The CD-CV parameter selection method addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning.

4. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate clear advantages of FIRE over existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) in both regression and classification tasks, with good computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical analysis focuses exclusively on Gaussian kernels, which is a common choice but limits the generalizability of the results. The paper could benefit from discussing how the framework might extend to other kernel functions.

2. **Type II Setting Limitations:** The Type II setting requires knowing q(x) at the data points from p, which is a significant constraint in many practical applications. The paper doesn't sufficiently address this limitation or provide concrete examples where this setting is applicable.

3. **Comparison with Recent Methods:** While the paper compares with KMM and LSIF, it doesn't include comparisons with more recent density ratio estimation methods like those proposed by Sugiyama, Kanamori, or others in the past few years.

4. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on strong assumptions (e.g., qp and q/p² in Sobolev space W²₂(Ω)). The paper doesn't discuss how restrictive these assumptions are in practice or provide evidence that real-world data satisfy them.

5. **Lack of Discussion on Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the FIRE algorithms, which would be important for assessing their practical utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a novel theoretical framework that connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable convergence rates.
- The proposed FIRE algorithms are simple and show strong empirical performance.
- The CD-CV parameter selection method is a useful practical contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels, which restricts the general applicability of the results.
- The Type II setting has significant practical limitations that aren't adequately addressed.
- The comparison with the most recent methods is insufficient.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the practical limitations of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a significant conceptual contribution by reframing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the experimental results demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed approach. The main weakness is the limited scope of the theoretical analysis (Gaussian kernels only) and the lack of discussion about the practical limitations of the Type II setting.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a new theoretical perspective on density ratio estimation. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Extend the theoretical analysis to discuss how the approach might generalize to other kernel functions.
2. Provide a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the Type II setting and concrete examples where it is applicable.
3. Include comparisons with more recent density ratio estimation methods in the experimental section.
4. Add a brief discussion of computational complexity.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it provides both theoretical depth and practical algorithms for an important problem in machine learning. The novel perspective on density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is particularly valuable for the NIPS community.
