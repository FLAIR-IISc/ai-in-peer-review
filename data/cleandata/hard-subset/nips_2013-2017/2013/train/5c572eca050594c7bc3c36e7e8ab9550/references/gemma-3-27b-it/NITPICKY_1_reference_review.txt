This paper tackles the density ratio estimation problem by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). While the idea of connecting density ratio estimation to inverse problems is intriguing and potentially fruitful, the execution and presentation contain numerous flaws, ranging from notational inconsistencies to a lack of sufficient justification for several claims. Despite the potential significance, I recommend rejection due to a multitude of concerns.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The core idea is sound: viewing density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm equation opens up possibilities for applying well-established techniques from operator theory. The connection to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) is appropriately highlighted, acknowledging its relationship as a special case of the proposed Type I formulation. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of covariate shift adaptation and transfer learning. However, the literature review, while extensive, feels somewhat haphazard, listing numerous related works without a clear synthesis of how this work differentiates itself beyond the Fredholm formulation. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The framing of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is a fresh perspective.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The attempt to provide theoretical guarantees (error bounds and convergence rates) is commendable, though the results are presented without adequate explanation of assumptions or limitations.
* **RKHS Framework:** The use of RKHS provides a principled and flexible approach, facilitating the application of regularization techniques.
* **Unsupervised Parameter Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method offers a potential solution to the crucial problem of hyperparameter tuning in unsupervised settings.

**Weaknesses (Numerous & Significant):**

* **Notational Inconsistency:** Throughout the paper, notation is inconsistent and often ill-defined. For example, the meaning of `q` (a density function vs. a function evaluated at data points) is frequently ambiguous.  The subscript 'p' in operators like Kp is sometimes used to denote weighting by density 'p', and other times seems to denote operating on samples drawn from 'p' - this is not clearly delineated.
* **Lack of Justification:** Several crucial steps lack sufficient justification.  The approximation in Equation (3) –  `Kt,p qp (y) ≈ q(y)` – is stated without a clear discussion of the conditions under which it holds beyond a citation to a textbook. The impact of the kernel choice on the accuracy of this approximation isn't discussed.
* **Overly Optimistic Claims:**  The claim that "all of these involve only function evaluations at the sample points" is misleading.  While the *empirical* approximations do, the theoretical analysis (and the RKHS formulation itself) relies on integration with respect to densities.  This distinction is glossed over.
* **Unclear Loss Functions:**  The introduction of various norms (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norm) feels ad hoc, and the rationale for choosing one over another isn’t sufficiently explained.  The combination of norms (γL2,p +(1−γ)L2,q) is presented without clear guidance on how to select the parameter γ.
* **Theoretical Results:** The convergence rates in Theorems 1 and 3 are presented with seemingly arbitrary constants (C1, C2, C3, C4) and a reliance on conditions (e.g., smoothness assumptions) that are rarely checked in practice. The assumptions themselves are stated briefly. The conditions for the theorems to hold are not well discussed.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experimental setup includes comparisons to relevant baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE), the results are presented in a confusing table (Table 1) with unclear labeling. The impact of the choice of test functions (U1 vs U2) on the selection of hyperparameters is not adequately addressed. The resampling using PCA and labels seems reasonable, but the parameter choices for resampling are not justified.
* **Missing Details:** The description of the algorithms lacks sufficient detail to allow for easy replication.  For instance, the implementation of the matrix inversions in Equation (8) (and analogous equations for Type II and combined norms) could be numerically unstable and should be addressed.
* **Minor Errors:**  Several minor typographical errors and inconsistencies are present throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts, incorrect referencing).

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* The Fredholm formulation offers a novel perspective on a well-studied problem.
* The theoretical analysis, despite its flaws, represents an attempt to provide rigorous guarantees.
* The CD-CV method is a valuable contribution to unsupervised parameter selection.

**Against:**

* The numerous notational inconsistencies and lack of justification undermine the clarity and rigor of the presentation.
* The theoretical results are difficult to interpret due to the unexplained constants and limited discussion of assumptions.
* The experimental evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods.
* The overall quality of the writing and presentation is below the standards expected for NIPS.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting idea, its execution is significantly flawed. The lack of clarity, the numerous unjustified claims, and the insufficient experimental validation render this work unsuitable for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to address the aforementioned weaknesses – particularly focusing on notational consistency, rigorous justification of their approximations, and a more thorough experimental evaluation – before resubmitting.  The potential of the Fredholm formulation is overshadowed by the numerous technical and presentation issues.
