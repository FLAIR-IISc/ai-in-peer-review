Summary
The paper reframes density-ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and tackles it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two operator identities are used: (I) Kp r ≡ Kq 1 (exact, estimable from samples of p and q) and (II) Kt,p r ≈ q for δ-like kernels (estimable when q(x) is known at p-sample points). By replacing the continuous problem with empirical operators and using the representer theorem, the authors derive closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) for several choices of loss (L2,p; mixed L2,p/L2,q; RKHS norm). They provide nonasymptotic error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact manifolds, and propose an unsupervised model-selection procedure (CD-CV) based on checking importance-sampling identities against random test functions. Experiments on covariate shift (regression/classification) compare FIRE to LSIF and a KDE-based baseline; KMM is discussed and compared conceptually and in some experiments.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself with KMM, LSIF/KLIEP, and RKHS/inverse-problem literature (e.g., De Vito et al.). The equivalence between KMM’s moment-matching and an operator equation is noted, and the proposed formulations differ by putting the density-ratio function itself in RKHS with explicit Tikhonov regularization and out-of-sample extension. The Type II formulation (when q is evaluable but not samplable) is particularly distinctive. The theoretical analysis leverages kernel operator approximations and concentration on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, linking to work on kernel integral operators and diffusion-type approximations.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and connects transfer-learning practice to classical operator theory.
- Algorithmic simplicity: Closed-form kernel solutions via standard linear algebra; flexibility to choose losses and kernels; natural out-of-sample extension.
- Theory: Nonasymptotic bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, with coverage of both Euclidean and manifold-supported distributions. The separation of approximation, sample, and regularization errors is explicit.
- Practical consideration: CD-CV gives an unsupervised way to tune t and λ by testing preserved expectation identities without labels, applicable in covariate-shift.
- Relevance: Density-ratio estimation is central to domain adaptation, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited: small datasets, moderate n (≤1000), and primarily proxy metrics (JCD). Claims of improved computational efficiency are not supported by runtime/memory analyses; kernel methods here are cubic without approximation.
- Comparison to strong baselines is incomplete: KMM is not consistently evaluated due to out-of-sample issues; modern density-ratio estimators (e.g., uLSIF variants, probabilistic classification approaches) are not comprehensively compared. TIKDE is a weak baseline.
- Positivity/normalization: The unconstrained RKHS regression can yield negative ratios and does not enforce Ep[r]=1. Practical implications (variance of importance sampling, downstream instability) are not addressed experimentally; simple remedies (nonnegativity projection, normalization) are not discussed.
- Theory presentation: Rates (e.g., n^−1/(3.5+d/2)) and conditions (e.g., m > λ1/3 n) are unusual and under-explained. Guidance on joint choice of t, λ as functions of n,m to attain the stated rates is not made explicit. Some notation/typos hinder readability.
- CD-CV lacks theory: No guarantees that minimizing JCD leads to near-optimal predictive risk; the choice and number of test functions and their sensitivity are not analyzed.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The methodology is technically sound, grounded in well-established regularization theory. The bounds appear reasonable given smoothing and sampling errors, though detailed assumptions (Sobolev smoothness, boundary effects) and parameter-selection prescriptions could be clearer. Empirical validation supports feasibility but is not extensive or stress-tested (no high-d, no large-n, no robustness/variance analysis).
- Clarity: Generally readable, with concrete formulas and algorithm descriptions, but marred by typos, notational overload (K, KH, Kp,p, etc.), and occasional inconsistencies. The roles of k vs kH and when they coincide should be streamlined. Explicit guidelines for choosing t, λ and for implementing CD-CV would improve reproducibility.
- Originality: Moderate to good. The Fredholm framing and Type II setting are novel and insightful in the density-ratio context; relating KMM to the operator identity from an RKHS perspective is a useful unification. The theoretical treatment on manifolds adds originality.
- Significance: Moderate. The approach may influence how density-ratio estimation is taught and analyzed, and the algorithms are practical for small-to-medium problems. Broad impact would be strengthened by stronger empirical evidence, constraints handling, and scalability (e.g., Nyström/random features).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide guidance (or theory) for selecting t and λ to achieve the stated rates; include sensitivity plots.
- Enforce or post-process nonnegativity and normalization; evaluate the impact on importance sampling variance and downstream tasks.
- Add runtime and memory comparisons; explore scalable approximations (Nyström, random features).
- Expand experiments: larger datasets, higher d, ablations on CD-CV choices, comparisons to additional baselines (e.g., uLSIF variants, probabilistic classification ratio estimation).
- Improve exposition: unify notation, fix typos, clearly separate operator kernels from RKHS kernels and state when they coincide.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide theoretical or empirical evidence that minimizing JCD correlates with downstream risk under covariate shift?
- How sensitive is FIRE to negative estimated ratios? Have you tried nonnegativity constraints or clipping?
- What are recommended schedules for t and λ as functions of n,m and d?
- Can the framework handle normalization Ep[r]=1 explicitly (e.g., via a linear constraint)?

Reasons to accept
- Elegant and principled framing that unifies and extends prior methods.
- Solid theoretical analysis, including manifold-supported distributions.
- Simple, implementable algorithms with unsupervised model selection.

Reasons to reject
- Limited and partly inconsistent empirical validation and lack of scalability analysis.
- Practical issues (nonnegativity, normalization) not addressed.
- Clarity issues and under-explained rates/assumptions.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (borderline). The conceptual contribution and theory merit publication; strengthening experiments and clarity would make it a clear accept.
