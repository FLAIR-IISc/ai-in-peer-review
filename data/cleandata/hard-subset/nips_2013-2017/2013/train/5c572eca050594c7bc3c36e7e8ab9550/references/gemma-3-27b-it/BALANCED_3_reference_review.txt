This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques from Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is compelling: leveraging tools from operator theory to provide a principled and theoretically grounded framework for a problem frequently encountered in machine learning, particularly in transfer learning and importance sampling. The authors present two formulations (Type I and Type II) and develop corresponding algorithms (FIRE) with explicit solutions facilitated by the representer theorem. Theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds for the Gaussian kernel, is provided, alongside an unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) and empirical validation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Theoretical Grounding:** The paper provides a fresh perspective on density ratio estimation, connecting it to classical integral equation theory. This connection enables the application of well-established mathematical tools for analysis and algorithm design.
* **Principled Framework:** The RKHS regularization approach provides a flexible and theoretically sound framework, allowing for different norms and regularization techniques.
* **Explicit Solutions:** The use of the representer theorem leads to easily implementable algorithms with explicit solutions, a significant practical advantage.
* **Theoretical Analysis:**  The paper presents a non-trivial theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds, which is crucial for understanding the behavior and performance of the proposed algorithms.  The extension to manifolds is also notable.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing FIRE to several state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE, and importance sampling) on various datasets and tasks. The CD-CV approach for parameter selection is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper meticulously relates the proposed methods to existing literature, specifically KMM and LSIF, clearly highlighting the differences and advantages of the FIRE framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Notation & Presentation:**  The paper is densely written and utilizes a significant amount of mathematical notation. While mathematically precise, this can hinder readability and make it difficult for readers unfamiliar with the intricacies of integral equations and RKHS theory to grasp the key ideas quickly. The introduction of numerous equations and notations in the initial sections may be overwhelming.
* **Limited Practical Advantage in Some Cases:**  While theoretically sound, the empirical results don't always demonstrate a significant performance advantage over simpler or existing methods.  The performance improvements are not always consistent across different datasets and experimental settings. Further analysis is needed to pinpoint scenarios where FIRE excels.
* **Computational Cost:** The inversion of kernel matrices in the FIRE algorithms (Eqs. 8 and 10) can be computationally expensive, especially for large datasets. The paper does not adequately address the scalability of the method or discuss potential approximation techniques to mitigate this issue.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of the algorithms is likely sensitive to the choice of kernel parameters (e.g., bandwidth *t* in the Gaussian kernel) and the regularization parameter λ. While CD-CV is proposed for parameter selection, its robustness and efficiency could be further investigated.
* **Assumptions on Smoothness:** The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions regarding the smoothness of the densities (Sobolev spaces). The practical implications of violating these assumptions are not fully explored.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-grounded approach to a challenging problem in machine learning.
* The framework is flexible and provides explicit algorithms that can be readily implemented.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the algorithms.
* The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates the potential of the proposed methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and mathematically challenging, potentially limiting its accessibility.
* The practical advantages of FIRE over existing methods are not always clear.
* The computational cost of the algorithms may be prohibitive for large datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. It presents a novel and theoretically well-supported approach to density ratio estimation. However, the density of the presentation and the limited practical advantage in some cases raise concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize improving the clarity and readability of the paper, especially in the initial sections. They should also investigate strategies to mitigate the computational cost of the algorithms and provide a more thorough analysis of the parameter sensitivity.  Further investigation into the conditions under which FIRE provides a significant advantage over existing methods is also warranted.  Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
