## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and mathematically grounded framework for density ratio estimation by recasting it as a classical inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques from RKHS theory. The connection to operator theory provides a solid theoretical foundation and leads to well-defined algorithms (FIRE) with clear practical implementations. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the empirical evaluation, while extensive, feels somewhat incremental compared to existing methods. I lean towards **Accept with Minor Revisions**, contingent on clarification of the practical advantages and potential limitations of FIRE compared to state-of-the-art methods like KMM and LSIF in specific scenarios.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper cleverly reframes density ratio estimation, a common problem in areas like transfer learning and importance sampling, as solving a Fredholm integral equation. This allows the authors to leverage the rich toolkit of regularization methods developed for inverse problems, particularly Tikhonov regularization within the framework of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). They propose two formulations (Type I and Type II) based on different availability of samples from the target distribution.  The resulting algorithms (FIRE) are explicit, implementable via linear combinations of kernel functions, and amenable to theoretical analysis.  A novel unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) is also introduced.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing body of work on density ratio estimation (citing [6, 10, 9, 22, 2]).  It clearly distinguishes itself by shifting the perspective from direct estimation to solving an inverse problem. The connection to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] is well explained, and the authors accurately point out the differences – particularly the regularization term in FIRE and its implication for stability and out-of-sample extension.  The comparison to LSIF [10] is also relevant and appropriately contextualized.  The authors demonstrate a good awareness of the broader literature on kernel methods and inverse problems.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:**  The formulation as a Fredholm equation is elegant and provides a rigorous theoretical basis for analysis. The theoretical results (convergence rates, error bounds) for Gaussian kernels are valuable, though limited to specific domains (Euclidean space and manifolds).
* **Principled Approach:** Using Tikhonov regularization and RKHS provides a well-understood and flexible framework with various regularization options.
* **Explicit Algorithms:** FIRE algorithms are well-defined and have efficient implementations due to the representer theorem, allowing for straightforward computation.
* **Novel CD-CV method:** The cross-density cross-validation approach for parameter selection is a valuable contribution, particularly in unsupervised settings.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation covers various datasets and settings, including regression, classification, and comparisons to established baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Empirical Gains:** While the experiments demonstrate that FIRE performs competitively with existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE), the improvements are often marginal. The paper doesn't clearly highlight scenarios where FIRE *significantly* outperforms the alternatives.  The performance gains of CD-CV over standard cross-validation are also not overwhelmingly convincing.
* **Theoretical Limitations:** The theoretical analysis is currently limited to Gaussian kernels and specific domain assumptions (Euclidean and manifolds). Expanding this to other kernels or more general settings would strengthen the work.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn’t provide much guidance on choosing the appropriate kernel, regularization parameter, or bandwidth for practical applications. While CD-CV helps with parameter tuning, some further insights would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Presentation:**  The paper is quite dense and mathematically involved.  While the technical rigor is appreciated,  some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations and simplification for a broader audience.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density ratio estimation.
* The framework connects a practical problem to a well-established area of mathematics (inverse problems and operator theory).
* The algorithms are explicitly defined and implementable.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates competitive performance.
* The CD-CV method is a valuable contribution to unsupervised parameter selection.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical gains over existing methods are relatively modest.
* The theoretical analysis is limited in scope.
* The paper is mathematically dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.




**Specific Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3 (Theoretical Analysis):** The convergence rates seem to depend heavily on the smoothness of the densities. How sensitive are the results to violations of these smoothness assumptions?
* **CD-CV:** Is the choice of test functions in CD-CV (linear, half-space) crucial?  Would other function classes lead to different parameter choices and performance?
* **Computational Cost:**  How does the computational cost of FIRE compare to KMM and LSIF, particularly for large datasets?  The matrix inversions in Eq. 8 and Eq. 10 could be computationally expensive.
* **Type I vs. Type II:** What are the practical guidelines for choosing between Type I and Type II formulations?  Which one is more robust in scenarios with limited data or noisy samples?
* **Error Analysis:**  A more detailed error analysis, breaking down the contributions of different terms in the error bounds (e.g., regularization term, approximation error), would be valuable.



**Overall, I believe this is a solid contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and make it even more impactful.**
