This paper presents a novel framework for the problem of density ratio estimation, q(x)/p(x), by reformulating it as an inverse problem. The authors derive two types of Fredholm integral equations of the first kind. The "Type I" formulation, `Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)`, is applicable when samples from both densities p and q are available. The "Type II" formulation, `Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`, is used when samples from p are available and the function q is known. These ill-posed integral equations are solved using Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm penalty. This approach, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), leads to simple, closed-form solutions within the standard kernel learning paradigm. The paper provides a theoretical analysis with error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, and proposes an unsupervised model selection procedure called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). The work is positioned in relation to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF).

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel Formulation:** The central contribution of casting density ratio estimation as a classical Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and principled. It provides a unifying perspective that connects this problem to a rich literature in functional analysis and operator theory.
2.  **Flexibility:** The framework naturally accommodates different loss functions (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norm) and settings (Type I and Type II), leading to a family of related algorithms.
3.  **Practical Contribution:** The proposed unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, is well-motivated and provides a practical solution to the challenging problem of hyperparameter tuning in this unsupervised setting.
4.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper attempts a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithms, providing convergence rates on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, despite its promising conceptual foundation, suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its execution, ranging from critical notational ambiguities to unsupported experimental claims and formatting violations.

**1. Lack of Clarity and Notational Issues:**
*   The very first sentence of the introduction contains a critical typo, stating the problem is estimating "q(x)p(x)" instead of the ratio q(x)/p(x).
*   The notation for the empirical kernel matrices is highly confusing and likely erroneous. For instance, in Section 2, the matrix `Kp,p` is defined with elements `(Kp,p)ij = 1/n k(xi, xj)`. Including the `1/n` factor within the matrix definition is unconventional and makes the derivation of the final solution in Eq. (8), `v = ( K^2_p,p KH + nλI )−1 Kp,pKp,q 1_zq`, extremely difficult to follow or verify. Standard practice would be to define the Gram matrix `K_ij = k(xi, xj)` and absorb scaling factors into the objective function. This ambiguity permeates all derived algorithms, severely hindering the paper's clarity and reproducibility.
*   The language is imprecise at times. For example, "If KH and Kp,p are the same kernel" should be "if the kernel functions kH and k are identical."
*   In Section 4, the resampling formula for `Pi` appears to have a typo (`+e(...)` in the denominator instead of being part of a sum for normalization), making it unintelligible.

**2. Concerns with Theoretical Analysis:**
*   The assumptions for the theoretical results are quite strong (e.g., `q/p` and `(q/p)^2` in the Sobolev space `W^2_2`) without sufficient discussion of their practical implications.
*   The stated error bounds in Theorem 1 contain terms that are counter-intuitive. Specifically, the variance term has `λ` and `λ^(1/6)` in the denominator. This implies that decreasing the regularization parameter `λ` causes the error bound to diverge, which is the opposite of the expected behavior where less regularization increases variance. This suggests either a typo or a non-standard result that requires significant explanation, which is absent.
*   The derived convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 are exceptionally slow, with a strong negative dependence on the data dimension `d`. For instance, the rate `O(n^(-1/(3.5+d/2)))` is practically unusable in even moderate dimensions. The authors make no attempt to compare these rates to existing minimax rates for this problem or to the rates of competing algorithms, making it impossible to judge the quality of these theoretical results.

**3. Incomplete and Unverifiable Experiments:**
*   The entire experimental section is built around results presented in tables which are completely missing from the manuscript. The text refers to "Table 1" and subsequent comparisons for regression and classification tasks, but no such tables exist. This makes the empirical evaluation section entirely unsubstantiated and impossible to assess.
*   The description of the supervised learning experiments is vague, failing to specify which "several other methods" FIRE is being compared against.
*   Without the experimental results, the practical utility of the proposed FIRE algorithm and the CD-CV selection method remains an open question.

**4. Formatting and Submission Guideline Violations:**
*   The manuscript includes an "Acknowledgements" section, which is a clear violation of the double-blind reviewing policy for NIPS. This alone is often grounds for rejection.

**Summary Evaluation:**
*   **Quality:** The paper is not technically sound in its current state. The notational confusion makes the algorithms questionable, the theoretical bounds seem paradoxical, and the experimental validation is entirely absent. The work feels incomplete.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is poorly written. Key mathematical derivations are obscured by confusing notation, and multiple typographical errors impede understanding.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of using Fredholm equations is novel and interesting. The connection to classical inverse problems is a valuable contribution.
*   **Significance:** The potential significance of the work is high due to its principled and flexible framework. However, this potential is completely undermined by the severe flaws in the execution. As it stands, other researchers cannot build upon this work, as they can neither confidently reproduce the algorithms nor trust the theoretical or empirical results.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the fundamental idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is commendable, the manuscript is marred by a pervasive lack of attention to detail. The combination of critical notational ambiguities, questionable theoretical bounds, a complete absence of experimental results, and a violation of the anonymity policy makes the paper unacceptable for publication at NIPS in its current form. The work requires a thorough and meticulous revision before it can be reconsidered.
