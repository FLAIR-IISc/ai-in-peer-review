Summary
The paper proposes to estimate the density ratio r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by casting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving a Tikhonov-regularized inverse problem in an RKHS. Two formulations are considered: Type I uses samples from both p and q and exploits the identity ∫ k(x,y) r(y) dp(y) = ∫ k(x,y) dq(y); Type II uses a “local” kernel kt and data from p together with values of q at the p-sample, leveraging the approximate identity property ∫ kt(x,y) r(x) dp(x) ≈ q(y). Representer-theorem-based solutions yield closed-form estimators (FIRE). The paper provides concentration/error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and an unsupervised parameter selection scheme (CD-CV) based on matching empirical expectations of test functions under q to reweighted expectations under p. Experiments on CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (partially), TIKDE, and importance sampling.

Relation to prior work
The work connects density-ratio estimation to operator-theoretic inverse problems. It is closely related to KMM (matching kernel means) and LSIF/KLIEP (L2,p or KL matching), as acknowledged. The Type I RKHS-loss variant is essentially a regularized KMM objective with a different solver; Type II resembles Nadaraya–Watson/approximate identity arguments used in nonparametrics. The spectral/inverse-problem framing has appeared in learning-as-inverse-problem and spectral regularization literatures.

Strengths
- Clean operator-theoretic derivation that unifies several density-ratio estimators under a Fredholm/Tikhonov lens.
- Simple closed-form solutions via RKHS representer theorems; clear linear algebra recipes for multiple loss choices (L2,p; γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q; RKHS norm).
- Theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean/manifold cases; finite-sample bounds are stated.
- CD-CV offers an unsupervised, practitioner-friendly model selection mechanism independent of using a basis for r.
- The distinction between Type I and II is practically relevant (e.g., when q is known up to a constant but sampling from q is hard).

Weaknesses (many minor but consequential issues; some major)
Technical correctness/clarity
- Pervasive notation/typographical issues that obscure meaning: the target ratio is repeatedly written as “qp” instead of q/p; “TiknonovPhilips” vs. Tikhonov–Phillips; “δ-family”; “q p2” likely means q/p^2; “Kt,q f ≈ f p” (Type II paragraph) appears incorrect (should be ≈ f q). These are not cosmetic—some directly contradict earlier identities (Kt,q f should approximate f·q, not f·p).
- Support and normalization are not discussed. If q is known only up to a multiplicative constant (common in MCMC), the Type II loss is not scale-invariant, and the estimator will be scaled. For importance weighting, normalization E_p[r]=1 and nonnegativity are crucial; FIRE does not enforce either and may produce negative weights.
- Assumptions and rates are insufficiently specified. Results state “for sufficiently small t” but omit precise schedules relating t, λ, n, m required to achieve the claimed rates. Corollaries include unusual exponents (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) and conditions like “m > λ1/3 n” that may be unrealistic for small λ. Some exponents in Cor. 4 appear mangled (e.g., “56 d” likely 5/6·d), making the results hard to interpret or verify.
- Boundary and manifold remarks are cursory; the impact of boundary bias for δ-kernels is not treated, yet appears in the claims.
- The RKHS-loss formulation requires q∈H′, a strong assumption that is not examined empirically or theoretically (approximation error if q∉H′).

Algorithmic/practical issues
- Computational complexity and scalability are not discussed. All solutions require inverting n×n systems (O(n^3)). No mention of Nyström/iterative solvers, memory footprint, or runtime comparisons with LSIF/KMM.
- Stability/conditioning of (K^2KH + nλI) is not analyzed. As t→0, kernels become ill-conditioned; the paper provides no guidance beyond λ grids.
- Type II claims that only L2,p is available, but in practice one can incorporate other empirical norms; this claim is left unexamined.
- No guarantee of out-of-sample extension is needed for FIRE (a plus), yet the paper uses this to exclude KMM from some comparisons; KMM weights live on the p-sample, which suffices for reweighting—this choice disadvantages a key baseline.

Experiments
- Dataset splits: “first 500 or 1000 points” from the original set are used for Xp—this introduces ordering bias. Randomization and multiple seeds should be used.
- Resampling schemes (PCA(a,b), label-based) are ad hoc and can conflate covariate and label shift. Overlap of supports (p>0 where q>0) is not ensured.
- Evaluation relies heavily on the JCD metric (matching test-function expectations) rather than ground-truth ratio error or target-domain risk; sensitivity to the choice/number of test functions U is not reported. No confidence intervals/variance estimates.
- Comparisons: KMM is excluded “in these experiments” due to out-of-sample extension, yet later the abstract/intro claim competitive performance vs. KMM; this is inconsistent. Details on TIKDE threshold tuning and LSIF model selection are insufficient for fair comparison. No runtime or memory comparisons.
- Weighted SVM/regression with potentially negative weights is not explained; many implementations assume nonnegative weights.

Quality
Mixed. The core idea is sound and interesting, but the manuscript has numerous technical inaccuracies, missing conditions, and notational errors that impede verification. Empirical evaluation does not convincingly establish superiority.

Clarity
Below NeurIPS standards. Notational inconsistency, typos, and some incorrect identities (e.g., Kt,q f ≈ f p) must be corrected. Proofs are deferred; key assumptions/rate schedules are missing.

Originality
Moderate. The operator-theoretic unification and Type II framing are nice, but much overlaps with KMM/LSIF/KLIEP and learning-as-inverse-problem. The RKHS-loss Type I is essentially regularized KMM.

Significance
Potentially useful if cleaned up, but current theory/practice gaps and experimental limitations reduce impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat operator viewpoint with simple closed-form estimators.
- Attempts a unified theory and covers manifold settings.
- Practical unsupervised hyperparameter selection (CD-CV).

Cons for acceptance
- Many technical/notation errors; some contradictions.
- Incomplete theory (unclear regimes; odd exponents).
- No positivity/normalization constraints; unclear handling of unnormalized q.
- Limited/scant experimental rigor; questionable fairness to baselines; no scalability discussion.

Questions for authors
1. Clarify Type II identities: should Kt,q f ≈ f q? Please fix all occurrences and provide conditions and rates with explicit schedules for t(n), λ(n), m(n).
2. How do you enforce or handle nonnegativity and normalization of r in practice? What happens if q is known only up to scale?
3. Provide runtime/memory comparisons and discuss scalability (Nyström, CG, preconditioning).
4. Report sensitivity to U in CD-CV and provide standard errors across randomized splits.
5. Ensure fair KMM comparisons (weights on p-sample suffice) and detail tuning for all baselines.
6. Correct references (years, venues) and clean up notation throughout.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper has promise, but the accumulation of technical inaccuracies, unclear assumptions, and experimental shortcomings outweigh the contributions in its current form. I encourage a thorough revision addressing the above points.
