## Peer Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and mathematically grounded approach to density ratio estimation, framing it as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The connection to classical operator theory is a significant strength, enabling theoretical analysis and offering a principled framework for algorithm design. While the technical details are solid and the theoretical results are promising, the paper suffers from a lack of truly compelling empirical results that decisively demonstrate a significant advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods, especially given the increased complexity. The contribution is valuable, but falls slightly short of a strong accept.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The authors reformulate density ratio estimation (q(x)/p(x)) as solving a Fredholm integral equation. This allows them to utilize regularization techniques within an RKHS framework to estimate the ratio. Two formulations (Type I and Type II) are presented, catering to different scenarios regarding availability of samples from q and knowledge of q’s values. They develop algorithms (“FIRE”) based on these formulations and propose a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for parameter selection. The work bridges the gap between density ratio estimation techniques commonly seen in machine learning and classical integral equation theory.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Foundation:** The Fredholm equation formulation is a genuinely novel perspective on density ratio estimation. The rigorous mathematical treatment, drawing on operator theory and RKHS, is a significant strength and allows for theoretical analysis.
* **Generality & Flexibility:** The framework is flexible, accommodating different kernels, norms, and loss functions. The two types of formulations offer solutions for different data availability scenarios.
* **Theoretical Analysis:**  The paper presents convergence rates and error bounds for the Gaussian kernel, a valuable contribution to understanding the theoretical properties of the proposed algorithms.  The extension to manifolds is also appreciated.
* **CD-CV for Parameter Selection:** The unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) is a practical contribution, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The authors clearly position their work relative to KMM, LSIF, and other relevant approaches, acknowledging their connections and differentiating their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Results are Not Convincing:** While experiments are presented, the performance gains over strong baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) are modest and not consistently demonstrated across all datasets and settings. The justification for some experimental choices (e.g., PCA resampling) isn't fully clear.  The limited performance improvement doesn’t fully justify the added theoretical complexity.
* **Complexity:** The framework, while flexible, is more complex than some existing methods (e.g., KMM). A clear articulation of scenarios where the added complexity is justified by performance benefits is missing.
* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results:** The theoretical analysis is currently limited to the Gaussian kernel.  Extending these results to other commonly used kernels would strengthen the paper.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t adequately address the computational cost of the FIRE algorithms compared to existing methods, particularly in terms of kernel matrix inversions.



**Review of Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations. The theoretical analysis is a strong point. However, the empirical validation doesn't fully support the claimed benefits.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the mathematical notation can be dense at times. A more intuitive explanation of the connection between the Fredholm equation formulation and density ratio estimation could improve clarity for a broader audience.
* **Originality:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is genuinely original. The application of regularization techniques within this framework is also novel.
* **Significance:** The potential significance is high if the method can deliver substantial performance improvements in challenging scenarios. However, the current empirical results don't fully demonstrate this.  The theoretical results are valuable, but their practical impact needs to be better illustrated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically rigorous approach to a challenging problem.
* The connection to classical operator theory offers a valuable new perspective.
* The theoretical analysis provides insights into the behavior of the algorithms.
* The CD-CV method is a practical contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The empirical results are not consistently superior to existing methods.
* The increased complexity of the framework isn't always justified by performance gains.
* The limited scope of the theoretical analysis.




**Recommendation:**

**Borderline Accept / Weak Reject.** I recommend a borderline accept with a strong request for the authors to provide more compelling empirical results. Specifically, they should:

1. **Conduct more extensive experiments:**  Evaluate FIRE on a wider range of datasets and with more thorough hyperparameter tuning.
2. **Focus on scenarios where FIRE excels:**  Identify specific datasets or scenarios where FIRE consistently outperforms existing methods and highlight those results.
3. **Analyze computational cost:** Provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of FIRE compared to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE.
4. **Consider additional kernels:** Explore the performance of FIRE with kernels beyond the Gaussian kernel.




I believe the paper has merit and the ideas are valuable. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly in the empirical evaluation, would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more compelling contribution to NIPS.
