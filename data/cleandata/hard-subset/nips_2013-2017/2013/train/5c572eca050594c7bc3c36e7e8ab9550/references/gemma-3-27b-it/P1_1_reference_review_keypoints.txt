- Paper frames density ratio estimation as an inverse problem via Fredholm integral equations, proposing the FIRE family of algorithms.
- Two formulations (Type I and Type II) are introduced to handle different data availability scenarios.
- The approach leverages operator theory, RKHS regularization, and provides theoretical analysis (concentration/error bounds) for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
- Authors propose CD-CV, an unsupervised cross-density cross-validation method for parameter selection.
- Experiments show competitive performance against baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across several datasets.
- Relation to prior work is well-situated, highlighting connections to KMM and viewing FIRE as a generalization with more stable regularization.
- Novelty: casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is a significant and original contribution.
- Generality/flexibility: FIRE supports different kernels, norms, and regularization choices, and both Type I/II formulations.
- Theoretical strengths: provides convergence bounds and extends analysis to manifolds, strengthening theoretical foundations.
- Practical addition: CD-CV addresses unsupervised parameter tuning, a common challenge in density estimation.
- Clear presentation: paper is well-written, mathematically clear, and logically organized.
- Comprehensive experiments: includes a good range of datasets and baseline comparisons.
- Limitation: theoretical analysis is restricted to Gaussian kernels and relatively simple domains.
- Limitation: Type II formulation assumes knowledge of q(x_i) at samples, which may be unrealistic in practice.
- Limitation: paper lacks discussion of computational complexity and scalability for large datasets.
- Limitation: empirical analysis could be deeper—no statistical significance tests or thorough sensitivity analysis reported.
- Minor clarity issues: some sections (e.g., norms in the introduction) could be more concise and focused.
- Quality assessment: technically sound with correct derivations and supported claims.
- Originality assessment: combines operator theory, RKHS regularization, and CD-CV in an original way.
- Significance assessment: provides a new framework useful for transfer learning and domain adaptation.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical framing, flexible framework, solid analysis, effective CD-CV, and competitive experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited theoretical scope, practical issues with Type II, missing complexity discussion, and shallower empirical analysis.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with weaknesses outweighed by strengths.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
- Requested revisions: discuss practical limitations of Type II, add computational complexity/scalability discussion, include statistical significance and sensitivity analyses, consider extending theory to other kernels/domains, and improve clarity in certain sections.
