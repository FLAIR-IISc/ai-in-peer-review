Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes to estimate density ratios q/p by casting the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two complementary formulations are presented: Type I uses the identity Kp(q/p) = Kq1 and fits f ≈ q/p from samples drawn from both p and q; Type II uses a δ-kernel Kt,p to approximate q ≈ Kt,p(q/p) and fits f from samples drawn from p when pointwise values of q are available (q need not be a density). By the representer theorem, both lead to closed-form kernel solutions (linear systems) that the authors call FIRE. The paper also proposes an unsupervised cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) criterion that matches empirical expectations of test functions under p and q to select bandwidth and regularization. Theoretically, for Gaussian kernels the authors provide concentration bounds and convergence rates in Euclidean space and on compact manifolds, with explicit dependence on bandwidth t, regularization λ, sample sizes (n from p, m from q), and dimension.

The approach connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems and operator theory, and relates to kernel mean matching (KMM) and least-squares importance fitting (LSIF/KLIEP). The Type I RKHS-loss variant can be seen as a regularized KMM with a different objective, while Type II leverages kernel integral approximations. The unsupervised validation criterion is related to moment matching and, implicitly, to IPMs/MMD.

Quality
- Theory: The formulation is technically sound and the operator-theoretic view is insightful. The bounds for Gaussian kernels in Rd and on manifolds are nontrivial. The rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 are explicit, though somewhat unusual (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) and likely suboptimal relative to known minimax rates for likelihood ratio estimation. Assumptions (q/p and q/p^2 in W2
2, bounded densities, small-t expansions) are strong but standard in this line of analysis. The theory mostly treats the case where the operator kernel and RKHS kernel coincide; it would help to discuss the general case suggested by the algorithms.
- Algorithms: The derivations of the empirical solutions and matrix forms are clear. FIRE has a natural out-of-sample extension (unlike the original KMM) and includes a regularizer, which likely improves stability.
- Model selection: CD-CV is a practical criterion, but the paper does not offer guarantees that minimizing JCD leads to consistent hyperparameter selection or improved risk; nor is its connection to established IPM/MMD criteria made explicit. The choice of random linear and half-space test functions is reasonable, but coverage and sensitivity are not analyzed.
- Experiments: The settings (USPS, CPUsmall, Kin8nm) are reasonable, and the resampling protocols follow prior work. However, the empirical section is short on details in the provided text: precise quantitative results, error bars, and computational costs are not fully reported here. Some comparisons are omitted (e.g., KMM), ostensibly due to out-of-sample issues, but KMM can be compared on training-sample weights and via standard tricks; a regularized KMM baseline would be appropriate given Eq. 10.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear introduction, algorithmic derivations, and statement of main theorems. That said:
- Notation is heavy and sometimes overloaded (k vs kH, K vs KH; Kp,p, Kp,q, etc.). A concise notation table and consistent subscripts would help.
- The role of t (approximation bias) versus λ (regularization bias) could be better explained, with guidance on practical tradeoffs.
- Some references are incomplete (years/pages) and there are typos and minor grammatical issues. Pseudocode for FIRE variants and a brief complexity analysis would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem with RKHS Tikhonov regularization is a novel and clean unification of ideas that have appeared in different guises (e.g., KMM’s feature-moment equation, LSIF’s regression view). The Type II setting that uses known q values without needing q-sampling is a useful extension not emphasized in prior work.
- The CD-CV model selection is a simple, potentially impactful idea; relating it to MMD/IPM would clarify novelty versus existing moment-matching validation ideas.

Significance
- Methodologically, the operator-theoretic formulation and the resulting closed-form kernel estimators are of interest to the community, offering a principled alternative to KMM/LSIF with better stability and out-of-sample capability.
- Theoretical results with manifold support are a plus and may spur follow-up work.
- Practically, if the experimental advantages over LSIF/KMM hold broadly and CD-CV works reliably, practitioners may adopt FIRE. The paper would benefit from stronger, more comprehensive empirical evidence and scalability discussion.

Pros
- Clear, principled operator-theoretic formulation (Type I/II) with simple kernel algorithms and out-of-sample predictions.
- Regularized estimators likely more stable than KMM; explicit matrix solutions.
- Nontrivial theory (rates, concentration) including manifold case.
- Unsupervised hyperparameter selection (CD-CV) addressing a common pain point.

Cons
- Positivity and normalization of the ratio are not enforced; the estimator can be negative or mis-normalized (contrast with KMM’s constraints).
- Limited empirical detail; omission of a fair KMM baseline and scalability analysis; small datasets.
- Strong assumptions and possibly suboptimal rates; theory focuses on matched kernels.
- Clarity/notation and references need polishing.
- No complexity analysis or large-scale approximations (Nyström, random features).

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce nonnegativity and (optionally) Ep[f]=1 via constraints or post-hoc projection; discuss impact on theory and practice.
- Provide a complexity/conditioning analysis and explore scalable solvers (Nyström/rand features).
- Strengthen experiments: include synthetic benchmarks with known ratios; ablate t vs λ; compare to KMM (with regularization), LSIF, KLIEP; report run times and conditioning.
- Analyze CD-CV theoretically or relate it explicitly to MMD/IPM; consider using RKHS-based test functions to recover MMD as a special case.
- Clarify the effect of using different kernels for K and H; improve notation and complete citations.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a compelling and well-founded viewpoint with practical algorithms and meaningful theory. With stronger empirical validation and minor presentation improvements, it would make a solid NIPS contribution.
