Summary
The paper reframes density-ratio (importance weight) estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem for integral operators: (I) Kp r = Kq 1 and (II) Kt,p r ≈ q, where Kρ is an integral operator with kernel k and Kt,· is a “δ-like” smoothing operator (e.g., Gaussian). It then applies Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS to recover r from samples of p (and either samples of q for Type I or pointwise values of q for Type II). The Representer Theorem yields closed-form kernel ridge regression-like solutions (FIRE) with different choices of loss: L2,p, mixtures γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q, and an RKHS norm that connects to KMM. The analysis provides finite-sample error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and compact manifolds. The paper also proposes an unsupervised model selection procedure (CD-CV) based on importance-sampling identities tested against random function families, and presents experiments against LSIF and a thresholded KDE baseline on regression and classification tasks under covariate shift.

Relation to prior work
The Kp r = Kq 1 identity is the operator-theoretic restatement of moment-matching used by KMM (Gretton et al.) and is closely connected to kernel mean embeddings and conditional mean embeddings. The use of Tikhonov regularization in RKHS for inverse problems and operator learning is well-trodden (Smola & Schölkopf 1998; De Vito et al. 2005; Steinwart & Christmann). LSIF/KLIEP estimate r by minimizing divergences in parametric RKHS expansions. This work’s main novelty lies in explicitly treating density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation, unifying derivations for several empirical losses, providing direct closed-form linear solvers, and giving bounds on manifolds.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation that unifies several existing approaches and yields simple closed-form solutions.
- Inclusion of multiple loss choices (L2,p, γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q, RKHS norm) within a single framework; insightfully positions KMM as a special case.
- Theory: finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, including manifold support; clean bias-variance decomposition with dependence on t and λ.
- CD-CV model selection is simple, fully unsupervised, and practically useful when no labels are available.
- Algorithms are straightforward to implement and compatible with standard kernel machinery.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty relative to KMM and mean embedding literature feels incremental. The identity Kp r = Kq 1 under RKHS embeddings is the core of KMM; the main difference is the Tikhonov solution and loss choice, but this has been considered in the inverse-problem view of kernel methods. The paper should sharpen what is genuinely new (e.g., Type II; manifold bounds; particular finite-sample rates; CD-CV).
- Constraints: The proposed estimators do not enforce non-negativity or normalization of r, unlike KMM/LSIF/KLIEP. Negative or poorly scaled weights can degrade downstream performance; this is neither analyzed nor addressed.
- Theoretical rates are unusual (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)); there is little discussion of optimality or comparison to known minimax results (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2008). Regularity assumptions (Sobolev W2
2) and the m > λ1/3 n condition deserve interpretation.
- Type II practicality: scenarios with known q(x) values but only samples from p exist (e.g., unnormalized targets in MCMC), but the experiments are focused on covariate shift (Type I). The paper would benefit from a demonstration where Type II is the right model.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical justification. Minimizing JCD over random linear or half-space functions does not come with guarantees about L2,p error of r; it is related to moment matching/MMD, but this connection is not formalized. Also, the choice of function family can induce high variance.
- Experimental scope: datasets are small/modest, and KMM is omitted due to “out-of-sample extension,” which is not a compelling reason—KMM yields weights for p-sample points, which suffice for importance-weighted ERM. Comparisons to uLSIF/RuLSIF/KLIEP on standard covariate shift benchmarks, synthetic data with known ratios, and larger n are needed. Computational complexity and scalability (O(n3)) are not addressed; no low-rank approximations are explored.
- Clarity issues: typos (“TiknonovPhilips”), occasional ambiguous notation (when k = kH vs different), and the interplay between the approximating δ-kernel and the RKHS kernel could be clearer.

Quality
Technically sound in formulation and derivation; closed-form solvers follow from standard RKHS calculus. The bounds appear reasonable for Gaussian kernels and manifold settings, though proof sketches are deferred and optimality is unclear. Experimental validation is limited and omits key baselines; the usefulness of CD-CV is promising but under-theorized.

Clarity
Generally readable, but polishing is needed. Be explicit about when k = kH in analysis; collect assumptions in one place; discuss normalization/non-negativity. A schematic comparing FIRE variants to KMM/LSIF would help.

Originality
Moderate. The Fredholm/inverse-problem perspective is a clean unifier, and Type II + manifold rates are useful. However, the core identity and RKHS solution philosophy are closely related to existing work on KMM/mean embeddings and kernel inverse problems.

Significance
Potentially useful as a simple, regularized alternative to KMM/LSIF with a principled operator view. Impact depends on stronger empirical evidence (including larger-scale, more diverse shifts) and handling of constraints.

Questions for authors
- Can you enforce non-negativity and normalization (e.g., via projection or constrained ridge) and report the impact?
- How do your rates compare to minimax lower bounds for likelihood ratio estimation?
- Can you formalize CD-CV’s consistency, perhaps relating JCD to an IPM/MMD over a characteristic function class?
- Why not compare with KMM by using its weights on the p-sample directly for weighted ERM? Please add uLSIF/RuLSIF.
- Any scalable approximations (Nyström, random features) and their effect on bounds?
- Provide a Type II experiment where q is known up to a constant (e.g., AIS-like settings).

Pros
- Unifying operator-theoretic view with simple closed-form estimators.
- Multiple losses and connections to prior methods.
- Nontrivial theory including manifold support.
- Practical, unsupervised hyperparameter selection idea.

Cons
- Incremental novelty versus KMM/mean-embedding literature.
- Missing key constraints on the ratio; limited experiments and baselines.
- CD-CV lacks theory; scalability unaddressed.
- Clarity/polish issues.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. With strengthened empirical comparisons (including KMM/uLSIF), handling of positivity/normalization, and clearer positioning relative to prior art, this could be a solid contribution; as is, it is interesting but not fully convincing. Confidence: medium.
