- Summary: Reframes density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm integral equation, proposes FIRE algorithms using RKHS/operator-theory, gives theoretical convergence/error bounds, introduces CD-CV for parameter selection, and shows competitive empirical results.

- Novel perspective: Viewing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem with Fredholm equations is fresh and enables use of established mathematical tools.

- Theoretical foundation: Provides rigorous convergence rates and error bounds (including manifold extension), a major strength relative to many empirical works.

- Algorithmic simplicity: RKHS formulation yields explicit, easily implementable kernel-based solutions.

- Practical contribution: CD-CV offers a useful method for parameter selection; empirical results compare favorably to KMM and LSIF.

- Related work: Clearly positions contributions and connections to KMM, LSIF, and other literature.

- Limited empirical scope: Evaluation uses standard datasets and lacks exhaustive baseline comparisons and more challenging scenarios.

- Dense theory: The rigorous theoretical analysis is complex and may be inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with functional analysis/operator theory.

- Kernel choice concerns: Heavy focus on Gaussian kernels with limited discussion of practical kernel selection for different applications.

- Regularization sensitivity: Potential sensitivity to the regularization parameter λ; robustness analysis of CD-CV is limited.

- Type I vs Type II clarity: Distinction is mathematically clear but needs more practical clarification and use-case explanation.

- Assessment scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 7/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 8/10 — technically strong and novel but somewhat dense and empirically limited.

- For acceptance: Novel, theoretically grounded approach with strong results, practical algorithm and parameter selection, and competitive empirical performance.

- Against acceptance: Needs broader empirical evaluation, clearer/less dense exposition, and deeper discussion of kernel choice and regularization robustness.

- Overall recommendation: Accept with Minor Revisions.
