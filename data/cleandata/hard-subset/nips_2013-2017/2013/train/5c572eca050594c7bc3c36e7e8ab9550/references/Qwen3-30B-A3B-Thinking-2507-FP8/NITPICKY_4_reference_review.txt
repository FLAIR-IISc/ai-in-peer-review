

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which can be solved using regularization in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors introduce two types of formulations: Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when values of q(x) are known at points from p). They develop algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) and provide theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings.

## Strengths
- The connection to Fredholm equations provides a novel theoretical perspective on density ratio estimation.
- The paper presents a unified framework that encompasses several existing methods (like KMM and LSIF) within the RKHS regularization paradigm.
- The proposed CD-CV model selection technique for unsupervised parameter tuning is interesting and practical.
- The theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case is comprehensive for both Euclidean and manifold settings.

## Major Technical Flaws

### 1. Critical Notational Errors
The paper contains fundamental notational errors that undermine the mathematical validity of the core claims:

- In equation (1), the authors write "Ep(h(x) q(x) p(x))" when they mean "Ep(h(x) q(x)/p(x))". This is a serious error that would confuse readers and invalidate the mathematical foundation.

- In equation (3), the author writes "∫ Rd kt(x, y) q(x) p(x) p(x)dx" when it should be "∫ Rd kt(x, y) q(x)/p(x) p(x)dx" (the p(x) should be in the denominator, not multiplied).

- The paper uses inconsistent notation "q(x)p(x)" to mean both the product q(x)·p(x) and the ratio q(x)/p(x), which is highly confusing and problematic for mathematical rigor. This should be clearly distinguished as either "q(x)/p(x)" or "r(x)" where r is the density ratio.

### 2. Theoretical Analysis Issues
- The proofs of Theorems 1 and 3 are said to be in supplemental material, but no supplemental material is provided in the submission. This violates NIPS submission guidelines and prevents proper evaluation of the theoretical claims.

- The constants C1, C2, C3 in the error bounds (equations 12-15) are not defined or justified. The paper states "for some constants C1, C2, C3 independent of t, λ" without specifying their dependence on problem parameters, making the bounds practically unusable.

- The claim "‖Kt,q1− q‖2,p = O(t)" (in Theorem 3) lacks a proof or citation. The justification provided ("see [24], Ch. 1") is insufficient as [24] is not specified in the references.

### 3. Algorithmic Implementation Issues
- Equation (8) contains the ambiguous notation "Kp,q1zq" which is not defined. Is "1zq" a vector of ones multiplied by the sample from q, or is it a typo for "Kp,q" followed by "zq"? The notation is unclear and would prevent reproduction of the algorithm.

- In equation (10), the authors write "v = (Kp,pKH + nλI) −1 Kp,q1zq" with the same ambiguous notation "1zq" as in equation (8), further compounding the confusion.

- The claim that "the solution is still a linear combination of kernel functions centered on the sample from p" (in the Type I setting) is not actually novel. This is a standard consequence of the representer theorem for RKHS, not a distinctive contribution of the paper.

## Minor Issues

### 1. Reference and Citation Problems
- The reference to [24] for the approximation "∫ Rd kt(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)" lacks a specific page number or section, making verification difficult.

- The paper states "the so-called selection bias [27]" but reference [27] is actually about covariate shift, not selection bias. This misattribution reflects a lack of careful literature review.

### 2. Formatting and Presentation
- The paper uses inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions (e.g., "q(x) p(x)" vs "q(x)/p(x)").

- The reference list has a typo: "The effect of the input density distribution on kernel-based classifiers, C. Williams, M. Seeger, ICML, , " has an extra comma and missing information.

- The paper states "a δ-family" without defining what this means, which is confusing for readers unfamiliar with the terminology.

### 3. Experimental Section
- The paper mentions "Table 1 shows the average errors" but does not include the actual table in the submission, making it impossible to evaluate the experimental results.

- The paper claims "we evaluate and compare our methods on several different data sets" but provides no details about the specific datasets or experimental conditions.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm integral equations, the fundamental notational errors in the mathematical formulation are severe enough to undermine the entire paper's credibility. The theoretical claims are not properly supported by the provided material, and the experimental results are not presented in a way that allows for meaningful evaluation.

The paper's main contribution—reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation—is interesting but not sufficiently novel. The connection to existing work (like KMM) is not clearly differentiated, and the theoretical analysis fails to provide sufficiently precise bounds due to undefined constants and missing proofs.

The paper's technical flaws are substantial, and the lack of properly defined notation and mathematical formulation would prevent other researchers from building upon this work. The paper does not meet NIPS's standards for technical rigor and clarity.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper contains critical mathematical errors that invalidate its core claims, and the theoretical analysis lacks the necessary precision and completeness expected at NIPS. The notational inconsistencies and formatting issues further undermine the paper's credibility. While the problem addressed is relevant, the paper's execution is insufficient to warrant acceptance.
