## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as solving a Fredholm integral equation. The authors leverage tools from operator theory, specifically Tikhonov regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), to develop a family of algorithms ("FIRE") for estimating the density ratio. They provide theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds for the Gaussian kernel, and propose a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for parameter selection. The work is well-motivated, connects density ratio estimation to a rich mathematical theory, and offers a flexible and theoretically grounded approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a significant and insightful contribution. This connection unlocks a powerful toolkit from operator theory for analysis and algorithm design.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a commendable level of theoretical analysis, including convergence rates and error bounds for the Gaussian kernel, which is a substantial achievement. The extension to manifolds is also appreciated.
* **Flexibility and Generality:** The FIRE algorithms are highly flexible, accommodating different norms, loss functions, and kernel choices. The explicit formulas for the solutions, leveraging the representer theorem, are a major benefit.
* **Unsupervised Parameter Selection:** The CD-CV method addresses a crucial practical challenge in unsupervised learning and demonstrates good performance in experiments.
* **Clear Connection to Related Work:** The authors position their work well within the existing literature, clearly differentiating FIRE from methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) while highlighting the advantages of their approach.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides thorough empirical validation on various datasets and tasks, comparing FIRE against several baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the theoretical formulations are elegant, the computational cost of inverting the kernel matrices in the FIRE algorithms (specifically, Eq. 8 and 10) could be prohibitive for large datasets. The paper doesn't deeply address scalability concerns, and it's unclear how well the method performs on high-dimensional problems with limited data. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity would be beneficial.
* **Practical Relevance of Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions about smoothness (Sobolev spaces) and bounded densities. While common in theoretical work, it’s important to discuss the practical implications of these assumptions and how sensitive the performance is to violations.  
* **Limited Novelty in Experimental Setup:** While the experiments are thorough, they largely follow established protocols in the density ratio estimation literature. While this is reasonable for comparison, it would have been interesting to see FIRE applied to a novel problem where its advantages are particularly pronounced.
* **Type II Algorithm Limitations:** The paper acknowledges that the Type II setting lacks the flexibility of the Type I setting (only L2,p norm available). It would be helpful to provide more guidance on when Type II is truly advantageous, considering its limitations.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, the numerous equations and subscripts can be dense and occasionally difficult to follow without careful attention. 



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3 (Theoretical Analysis):**  The error bounds presented are excellent.  However, it would be valuable to discuss the implications of the constants C1, C2, C3, and C4. Are they dependent on data dimensions or other key parameters?  How do they impact the practical applicability of the bounds?
* **CD-CV:** The performance of CD-CV is promising.  A further investigation of its sensitivity to the choice of test functions (U1 and U2) could be worthwhile.
* **Scalability:**  Given the matrix inversions, how does the performance of FIRE scale with the number of samples (n and m)? Are there any strategies (e.g., Nyström methods, approximations) to mitigate the computational cost?
* **Connection to KMM:**  The authors correctly point out the connection to KMM. However, a more in-depth discussion of the differences in regularization properties – and why the FIRE approach might be more stable – could be beneficial.
* **Type II setting** Can the Type II setting be generalized to other norms or loss functions, even approximately? What are the trade-offs of using Type II compared to Type I when q(x) is known?




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound formulation of a challenging problem.
* Strong theoretical guarantees and rigorous analysis.
* Flexible and general algorithmic framework with explicit solutions.
* Effective unsupervised parameter selection method.
* Comprehensive experimental validation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Potential computational limitations for large datasets.
*  Dependence on assumptions that might not always hold in practice.
*  Experimental setup doesn't fully showcase the unique strengths of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation, theoretical rigor, and practical algorithms are commendable. While the computational cost and assumptions warrant further investigation, the potential benefits of this approach are substantial.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding computational complexity, discussing the sensitivity to theoretical assumptions, and potentially clarifying the notation in certain sections. A brief discussion of potential future work addressing scalability would also be valuable.  This paper has the potential to become a foundational work in this area.
