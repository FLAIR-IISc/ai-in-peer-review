## Peer Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation by framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm equation techniques and regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The connection to classical operator theory is a significant strength, and the resulting algorithms (FIRE) appear well-motivated and potentially practical. However, the experimental section, while comprehensive, doesn't entirely showcase a decisive advantage over existing methods, and some aspects of the theoretical analysis could benefit from further clarification.  I lean towards **Accept** with minor revisions, acknowledging the novelty and theoretical depth, but urging the authors to strengthen the empirical demonstration.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper tackles the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability densities, q(x)/p(x), a crucial task in importance sampling and covariate shift correction. Instead of directly estimating the ratio, the authors cleverly recast this as solving a Fredholm integral equation. By leveraging the properties of RKHS and Tikhonov regularization, they derive explicit algorithms (FIRE) that are conceptually simple and amenable to efficient implementation. Two main formulations are presented (Type I and Type II) depending on the availability of samples from both distributions or knowledge of q(x) values. The paper further introduces a novel, unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) and provides theoretical guarantees for convergence rates.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing body of work on density ratio estimation, referencing key contributions like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [Gretton et al., 2009], Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [Kanamori et al., 2010], and others. The authors highlight the connection to KMM, demonstrating that it can be viewed as a specific instance of their framework. They also clearly differentiate their approach, emphasizing the advantages of their regularization-based framework in terms of stability and theoretical analysis. The connection to broader inverse problem theory is also well-established.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Foundation:** The core idea of formulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is novel and offers a fresh perspective. The grounding in operator theory and RKHS provides a strong theoretical basis.
* **Generality & Flexibility:** The framework is general, allowing for different kernels, norms, and loss functions. This flexibility is a significant advantage. The Type I and Type II formulations cater to different data availability scenarios.
* **Explicit Algorithms (FIRE):** The derivation of explicit, implementable algorithms (FIRE) is a major contribution. The algorithms leverage standard kernel methods and are computationally feasible.
* **Unsupervised Parameter Selection (CD-CV):**  The CD-CV method is a valuable addition, addressing the crucial challenge of parameter tuning in unsupervised learning.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The provided concentration and error bounds, even if limited to the Gaussian kernel, are a substantial accomplishment, offering insights into the algorithm's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Results:** While the experiments are extensive, the performance gains of FIRE over existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) are often marginal. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage in terms of accuracy or efficiency.  The parameter sensitivity might be playing a role, and further investigation is needed.
* **Theoretical Scope:** The theoretical analysis is currently limited to the Gaussian kernel. Extending the analysis to other commonly used kernels (e.g., polynomial, sigmoid) would significantly strengthen the paper.  The assumptions regarding smoothness and boundedness of q and p are also somewhat restrictive.
* **Clarity of Theoretical Sections:** Some sections of the theoretical analysis (e.g., the discussion of convergence rates) could benefit from more detailed explanations and intuition. A clearer discussion of the constants involved in the error bounds would also be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:** While the algorithm is stated as efficient, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity (especially concerning kernel matrix inversion) would be beneficial.
* **Connection to Existing Work on Regularized Inverse Problems:** The paper mentions connections to the broader field of regularized inverse problems, but a more in-depth discussion of how this work fits within that context would be valuable.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-founded approach to density ratio estimation.
* The FIRE algorithms are conceptually simple, implementable, and offer a flexible framework.
* The introduction of CD-CV is a valuable contribution to unsupervised parameter selection.
* The theoretical analysis, while limited, provides important insights into the algorithm's behavior.
* The paper addresses a significant problem with potential applications in various machine learning domains.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
* The theoretical analysis is currently limited in scope.
* Some sections of the paper could benefit from improved clarity and detail.




**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Empirical Evaluation:**  Conduct more extensive experiments, focusing on datasets where FIRE is expected to excel.  Investigate the sensitivity of FIRE to parameter settings and compare its robustness to that of other methods.  Include runtime comparisons.
* **Theoretical Generalization:** Explore the possibility of extending the theoretical analysis to other kernels.
* **Clarity & Detail:**  Provide more intuitive explanations of the theoretical results and clarify the assumptions made.
* **Computational Complexity:** Add a section discussing the computational complexity of the FIRE algorithms.
* **Related Work:** Elaborate on the connections to the broader field of regularized inverse problems.



**Overall, I believe this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field.  With minor revisions to strengthen the empirical validation and theoretical scope, I recommend acceptance to NIPS.**
