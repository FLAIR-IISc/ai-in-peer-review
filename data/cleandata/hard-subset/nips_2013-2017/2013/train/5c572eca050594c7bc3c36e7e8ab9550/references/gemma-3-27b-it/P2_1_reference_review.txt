## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel formulation of density ratio estimation (importance weighting) as an inverse problem, specifically framed as solving a Fredholm equation of the first kind. The authors leverage tools from regularization theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop algorithms, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), for this task. They provide theoretical guarantees (convergence rates and error bounds) for the Gaussian kernel and propose a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for parameter selection. The work aims to provide a principled and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation, offering advantages in stability and analysis compared to existing methods like KMM and LSIF.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Perspective:** Framing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem provides a fresh and mathematically rigorous perspective. The connection to Fredholm equations opens up the possibility of applying a well-established toolkit from operator theory.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper delivers substantial theoretical analysis, including error bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, a significant contribution to the field.  This is a clear strength, distinguishing it from many empirical works.
* **Principled Framework:** The use of regularization and RKHS provides a well-defined and flexible framework, allowing for the incorporation of different norms and loss functions.  The explicit formulas for the FIRE algorithms are a valuable outcome.
* **CD-CV Method:** The unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) addresses a critical practical challenge in unsupervised/semi-supervised learning and appears well-motivated.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The authors thoroughly discuss the relationship to KMM and LSIF, explicitly pointing out the advantages and disadvantages of their approach compared to these methods.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental section covers multiple datasets, compares with a variety of baseline methods (including KMM, LSIF, TIKDE, and importance sampling), and explores different resampling strategies.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theoretical Results:** While the theoretical analysis is a strength, the presentation is dense and requires a solid background in functional analysis to fully grasp.  More intuitive explanations or simplifications could make it more accessible.
* **Limited Practical Advantage Demonstrated:** While the authors demonstrate comparable or better performance in experiments, the magnitude of the improvement over strong baselines like KMM and LSIF is not always substantial. A more compelling demonstration of practical benefit would strengthen the paper.
* **Focus on Gaussian Kernel:** The theoretical analysis is primarily focused on the Gaussian kernel. While justified, it limits the broader applicability of the theoretical results and may leave readers wondering about the performance of the method with other kernels.  Brief discussion or preliminary results with other kernels would be beneficial.
* **Repetitive Explanations:** The introduction and section 2 repeat some concepts and explanations, potentially lengthening the paper without adding significant new information.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. Claims are supported by theoretical analysis and experiments. The framework appears complete and well-defined, though the theoretical analysis is complex. The authors are careful in acknowledging the limitations and potential issues (e.g., the dependence on kernel choice). **Strong.**
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written, but the mathematical notation and explanations can be challenging for readers without a strong background in functional analysis. The structure is logical, but some sections could be more concise.  **Good.**
* **Originality:** The framing of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is novel and represents a significant contribution. The use of Fredholm equations in this context is a novel approach.  **Strong.**
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant.  A theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation is valuable, and the FIRE algorithms could be useful in a variety of applications (sampling, transfer learning, covariate shift). However, a more substantial demonstration of practical impact would further enhance the significance. **Good.**

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and mathematically rigorous framework for density ratio estimation.
* Strong theoretical analysis with convergence rates and error bounds.
* Principled approach using regularization and RKHS.
* Comprehensive experimental evaluation.
* Clear connections to related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Complexity of theoretical results might limit accessibility.
* Limited practical advantage demonstrated over strong baselines.
* Focus on Gaussian kernel limits the generality of theoretical results.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS. While the theoretical complexity and modest practical improvements are areas for potential refinement, the novelty of the approach, the rigorous theoretical analysis, and the clear presentation of the FIRE algorithms make it a valuable contribution to the field. The work establishes a promising new direction for research in density ratio estimation and provides a solid foundation for future exploration. I would recommend the authors consider simplifying some of the theoretical explanations and exploring the performance of their method with other kernels to further strengthen the paper.
