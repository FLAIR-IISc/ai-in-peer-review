### Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper presents a novel and principled framework for estimating the density ratio $q(x)/p(x)$, also known as the importance function. The core idea is to reframe this estimation task as a classical inverse problem, specifically, solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors derive two main integral equations based on fundamental properties of kernels. The first (Type I) leverages the identity $E_p[k(x, \cdot) \frac{q}{p}] = E_q[k(x, \cdot)]$, which is suitable when samples from both $p$ and $q$ are available. The second (Type II) uses the approximation $E_p[k_t(x, \cdot) \frac{q}{p}] \approx q(x)$ for a localizing kernel $k_t$, applicable when samples are drawn from $p$ but $q(x)$ is known analytically.

To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the authors employ Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm penalty, a standard and powerful technique in machine learning. This formulation, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), leads to simple, closed-form solutions for the density ratio in terms of kernel matrices, making the algorithms easy to implement. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving concentration and error bounds with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. A key practical contribution is the introduction of an unsupervised model selection method, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which uses an auxiliary set of test functions to evaluate the learned ratio. The proposed methods are experimentally compared against existing approaches like LSIF and a baseline TIKDE.

This work connects well to the existing literature on density ratio estimation. It explicitly relates its formulation to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], correctly identifying KMM as an instance of its Type I framework using an RKHS norm as the loss function, but without regularization and with a different optimization scheme. The proposed FIRE can thus be seen as a regularized and more stable alternative to KMM. The paper also acknowledges similarities between its CD-CV model selection criterion and the objective functions used in LSIF [10] and KLIEP [23], but astutely points out the key difference: FIRE uses this criterion for *validation*, while LSIF/KLIEP use it for *estimation*, which constrains their choice of test functions to be a good basis for the ratio itself.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The central contribution—casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation—is highly original and theoretically appealing. It provides a solid mathematical foundation for the problem, connecting it to the rich literature on inverse problems and operator theory. This "linearization" approach is powerful and provides a unifying perspective on different problem settings (Type I and II).

2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is a significant strength. Providing explicit error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings is non-trivial and substantially elevates the paper's contribution beyond a simple algorithmic proposal. The analysis demonstrates a deep understanding of the interplay between the regularization parameter $\lambda$, the kernel bandwidth $t$, and the sample sizes $n$ and $m$.

3.  **Practical Algorithms and Model Selection:** The paper successfully translates its theoretical framework into practical algorithms with closed-form solutions. The introduction of the CD-CV method for unsupervised hyperparameter tuning is a critical and valuable contribution, as model selection is a major challenge in unsupervised learning settings like covariate shift. The method is well-motivated and appears effective in the experiments.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The distinction between Type I and Type II settings is clearly articulated, the algorithms are derived step-by-step, and the connection to related work is explained with nuance and precision.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability:** The proposed algorithms require the inversion of an $n \times n$ matrix (where $n$ is the number of samples from $p$), which has a computational complexity of $O(n^3)$. This is a significant limitation for large datasets. The paper does not discuss this issue or mention potential avenues for scaling up, such as using Nyström approximations or random features, which are common practice for kernel methods. This is a crucial omission for a NIPS audience.

2.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened. While the comparison to LSIF is valuable, the exclusion of KMM from the supervised learning experiments is a notable gap. The reason provided—that KMM lacks a natural out-of-sample extension—is a known limitation, but standard techniques (e.g., kernel regression on the learned importance weights) could have been used to create a fair comparison. Given the close conceptual relationship highlighted in the paper, a direct comparison is essential to demonstrate the practical benefits of FIRE's regularization.

3.  **Discussion on Kernel Choices:** The framework uses two kernels: one for the integral operator, $k(x, y)$, and one for the RKHS regularization penalty, $k_H(x, y)$. While the theory section assumes they are the same Gaussian kernel for simplicity, the practical implications of this choice are not discussed. Is it always optimal for them to be the same? What are the trade-offs in choosing different kernels or different bandwidths for each? More guidance on this would improve the paper's practical utility.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   In the theoretical analysis, the convergence rates are presented but not compared to known minimax optimal rates for density ratio estimation (e.g., from [14]). Placing the derived rates in this context would help assess their tightness.
*   The CD-CV method's performance likely depends on the choice of the test function family $U$. How sensitive is the method to this choice? The paper demonstrates it works for linear and half-space functions, but some discussion on the properties a "good" family $U$ should have would be beneficial. For example, does $U$ need to be diverse enough to detect errors across the domain?
*   The matrix definitions in Section 2 are quite dense. A little more prose walking the reader through the components and their dimensions would improve readability, especially for the combined norm case.
*   In the Type II setting, the approximation error is of order $O(t)$, creating a bias-variance trade-off with the kernel bandwidth $t$. How is $t$ selected in practice for Type II problems? Is it cross-validated along with $\lambda$? The experiments seem to focus on the Type I setting.

**Conclusion**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. Its primary strength lies in the novel and elegant formulation of the problem as a Fredholm inverse problem, which is backed by rigorous theoretical analysis and leads to simple, practical algorithms. The proposed unsupervised model selection technique is another key strength. The main weaknesses are the lack of discussion on scalability and the somewhat limited experimental comparison, particularly the omission of KMM. Despite these points, the originality and significance of the core ideas are substantial. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the scalability issue and bolster their experimental comparison in the final version.
