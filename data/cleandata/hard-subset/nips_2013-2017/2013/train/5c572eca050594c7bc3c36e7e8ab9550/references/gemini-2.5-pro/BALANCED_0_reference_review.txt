**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel framework for estimating the density ratio `q(x)/p(x)`, a fundamental problem in machine learning with applications in covariate shift correction and importance sampling. The core idea is to reformulate the estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors derive two types of integral equations: Type I, `Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)`, which is suitable when samples from both distributions p and q are available, and Type II, `Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`, for cases where samples from p are available and q can be evaluated at those points.

To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the paper employs Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), yields a family of algorithms with simple, closed-form solutions derived via the representer theorem. The framework is flexible, allowing for different loss functions (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norm) and settings.

A significant part of the paper is dedicated to a rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide error bounds and convergence rates for FIRE with Gaussian kernels, considering data distributed in Euclidean space as well as on compact manifolds. To address the practical challenge of hyperparameter tuning (e.g., kernel width and regularization parameter), a novel unsupervised model selection method called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) is introduced. Finally, empirical results on several datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of FIRE and the CD-CV procedure, showing competitive performance against established methods like LSIF and a kernel density-based baseline.

The work connects well with previous research on density ratio estimation, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF). The authors explicitly relate their formulation to KMM, framing their RKHS-norm variant as a regularized version, and note the conceptual similarity of their CD-CV criterion to the objective functions in LSIF and KLIEP.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is elegant and insightful. It connects a core machine learning problem to the well-established field of inverse problems and operator theory, providing a principled foundation for the proposed algorithms. This "linearization" of the problem via integral operators is a strong conceptual contribution.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is supported by a thorough theoretical analysis. The derivation of concentration and error bounds with explicit convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings is a major strength. This level of rigor is not always present in papers proposing new estimation algorithms and provides strong guarantees on the method's behavior.

3.  **Practicality and Completeness:** The work goes beyond just proposing an algorithm by also tackling the crucial problem of unsupervised model selection. The proposed CD-CV method is well-motivated, simple to implement, and shown to be effective empirically. This makes the overall contribution much more complete and useful for practitioners.

4.  **Flexibility and Simplicity:** The FIRE framework is versatile, leading to a family of algorithms for different scenarios (Type I vs. Type II) and with different loss functions. The resulting algorithms have closed-form solutions that are straightforward to implement, depending only on standard kernel matrix computations.

5.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, the derivations are easy to follow, and the connection to prior art is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The proposed algorithms rely on inverting an `n x n` kernel matrix, where `n` is the number of samples from `p`. This results in `O(n^3)` computational complexity, which can be prohibitive for large datasets. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and could suggest potential avenues for scaling up the method, such as using low-rank kernel approximations (e.g., Nyström method) or iterative solvers.

2.  **Clarity of Comparison with KMM:** The paper's justification for not directly comparing with KMM in the experiments ("out-of-sample extension is necessary for fair comparison") is unclear. KMM does have a standard out-of-sample extension. The primary distinctions are that FIRE is explicitly regularized and the solution is guaranteed to be in the RKHS, whereas KMM performs unregularized quadratic programming on the sample weights. A direct empirical comparison would be highly valuable to substantiate the claims of improved stability and performance. The discussion could also be sharpened to more precisely delineate the differences beyond the regularization term.

3.  **Guidance on Kernel Choices:** The framework involves two potential kernels: `k` for the integral operator and `kH` for the RKHS regularization. The theory simplifies this by setting them to be the same Gaussian kernel. However, in practice, this is a choice. Some discussion on the implications of this choice would be helpful. For instance, what is the effect if `k` and `kH` are different? Is there a principled way to select the kernel families themselves, beyond tuning their parameters?

4.  **Evaluation of CD-CV:** The CD-CV method is a nice contribution, but its sensitivity to the choice of the test function family `U` and the number of functions `F` is not explored. A brief ablation study or discussion on how these choices affect the final performance would strengthen this part of the paper and provide better guidance for users.

5.  **Minor Clarifications:**
    *   In the algorithms section (e.g., Eq. 8), the notation for matrix-vector products like `Kp,q1zq` could be made more explicit (e.g., by defining `1zq` as a vector of ones of length `m`).
    *   An experiment directly comparing the performance of Type I and Type II FIRE on a synthetic dataset (where `q` is known and can be both sampled from and evaluated) would provide valuable insight into the practical impact of the `O(t)` approximation error in the Type II formulation.

**Decision**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on density ratio estimation. The novel formulation as an inverse problem is elegant and powerful, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed method is shown to be effective in practice. The inclusion of a practical unsupervised model selection procedure makes the work a complete and valuable contribution. While there are some minor weaknesses regarding computational scaling and experimental comparisons, they do not detract from the core merits of the paper. The work is likely to be of high interest to the NeurIPS community and to inspire further research at the intersection of kernel methods and inverse problems.

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper.
