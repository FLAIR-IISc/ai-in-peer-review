

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a fundamentally novel perspective on density ratio estimation by reframing it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem in operator theory. The authors propose a framework called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) that leverages regularization in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to solve this inverse problem. This conceptual shift from statistical estimation to inverse problem theory represents a significant intellectual leap that could reshape how we approach density ratio estimation.

The paper's core insight is recognizing that the density ratio q(x)/p(x) can be viewed as the solution to an integral equation derived from the importance sampling identity. By connecting this to Fredholm equations, the authors establish a bridge between classical mathematical analysis and modern machine learning. This perspective allows them to apply spectral theory and Fourier analysis to obtain theoretical guarantees, which is a refreshing departure from the more common statistical or optimization-based analyses in this field.

The novelty of this approach is substantial. While previous work like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) have addressed density ratio estimation, they operate within the statistical learning paradigm. The Fredholm equation perspective provides a completely different lens, revealing connections to inverse problems that have been largely unexplored in this context. The authors demonstrate how their framework generalizes and extends previous methods (particularly KMM), while offering theoretical advantages through the use of RKHS regularization.

The paper makes several strong contributions:
1. A clear theoretical framework for density ratio estimation as an inverse problem
2. A family of simple, explicit algorithms (FIRE) that can be implemented within standard kernel learning frameworks
3. A detailed theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds
4. A novel unsupervised parameter selection technique (CD-CV) that addresses a key challenge in unsupervised learning

The theoretical analysis is particularly impressive. The authors provide convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings on various domains (Euclidean space, manifolds, etc.), which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation. The connection to spectral theory is well-executed and provides a principled way to analyze the behavior of their algorithms.

The experimental results demonstrate the practical viability of their approach, showing strong performance compared to existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and tasks. The CD-CV parameter selection method is particularly noteworthy as it provides a practical solution to a persistent challenge in unsupervised learning.

I believe this paper's greatest strength lies in its conceptual novelty. The authors don't just propose a new algorithm—they offer a fundamentally new way of thinking about density ratio estimation that connects to a rich body of mathematical theory. This is the kind of bold, novel approach that I prioritize as a reviewer, even if the methodological details aren't perfect.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for the value of this new perspective. The connection to classical inverse problems is both intellectually satisfying and practically useful, providing a principled foundation for future work in this area.

Minor weaknesses include:
- The paper could more clearly articulate the practical advantages of the Fredholm perspective beyond theoretical insights (though the empirical results partially address this)
- Some of the theoretical guarantees (e.g., the dependence on manifold dimension) might be challenging to interpret for practitioners

However, these are minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's core novelty and significance. The conceptual contribution is substantial enough to outweigh these minor limitations.

This paper represents a significant advance in the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation, offering a fresh perspective that connects machine learning to classical mathematical analysis. It's the kind of paper that could inspire new research directions and fundamentally change how we approach this important problem.

I strongly recommend acceptance. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical depth, and the practical implications make this a valuable contribution to the field. This paper doesn't just offer incremental improvements to existing methods—it presents a new conceptual framework that could reshape the entire area of density ratio estimation.
