- Reformulates density-ratio estimation q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation solved via RKHS regularization.
- Frames density-ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem, distinct from direct optimization approaches.
- Extends and connects to KMM, providing a more principled regularization and theoretical foundation.
- Proposes two algorithms (Type I and Type II) to handle different practical scenarios (q known vs. sampled).
- Provides unified theoretical analysis with convergence rates for both Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
- Theorems separate approximation (kernel width), estimation (sample size), and regularization errors, clarifying trade-offs.
- Analysis uses appropriate concentration inequalities and spectral techniques, showing strong technical rigor.
- Experiments are comprehensive, comparing FIRE to KMM, LSIF, TIKDE across regression and classification tasks.
- FIRE performs competitively in experiments and is supported by practical implementation details.
- Introduces CD-CV, a novel unsupervised cross-validation approach for model selection.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, with precise math, clear pseudocode, and accessible exposition.
- Clearly distinguishes when to use Type I vs. Type II methods, aiding practical application.
- Original contribution: new perspective linking density-ratio estimation to integral-equation theory, unifying prior methods.
- Manifold-domain theoretical guarantees and CD-CV are notable original contributions.
- Significant impact for transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling applications.
- Strengths: novel mathematical view, comprehensive theory, practical algorithms, CD-CV, and clear Type I/II distinction.
- Weaknesses: needs clearer articulation of practical advantages beyond theoretical elegance.
- Weaknesses: experimental comparisons could include more recent methods (e.g., KLIEP) and additional baselines.
- Weaknesses: Type II setting would benefit from concrete examples where q is known but sampling is difficult.
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, original, clearly written, and significant for the field.
