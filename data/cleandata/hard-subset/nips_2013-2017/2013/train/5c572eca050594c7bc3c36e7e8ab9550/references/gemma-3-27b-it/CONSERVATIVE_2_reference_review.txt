This paper proposes a Fredholm equation approach to density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via regularization techniques within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. While the connection to classical operator theory is interesting, and the presentation is mathematically sound, I find the overall contribution incremental and lacking substantial novelty to warrant acceptance to NIPS at this time. The approach largely re-frames existing kernel methods, specifically relating closely to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), and the theoretical analysis, while present, doesn't offer groundbreaking insights.

The core idea – representing density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm integral equation – feels like a re-interpretation rather than a fundamentally new technique. The authors acknowledge this connection to KMM, but downplay the fact that KMM already provides a practical and well-studied solution to a similar problem. The FIRE algorithms proposed are essentially regularized RKHS solutions, and the gains over established methods aren't convincingly demonstrated.  The two formulations (Type I and Type II) offer flexibility, but Type II's reliance on knowing function values of *q* limits its applicability in scenarios where only samples from *q* are available, a common situation in density ratio estimation.

A significant weakness lies in the limited empirical evaluation. The experiments, while comprehensive in comparing against baseline methods like TIKDE and LSIF, lack a compelling demonstration of FIRE’s superiority. The results appear competitive but not decisively better, and the benefits of the theoretical analysis are not clearly translated into practical performance gains. The unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) is a positive aspect, but doesn’t fundamentally alter the evaluation. The extensive theoretical analysis, while appreciated, feels somewhat detached from the empirical results. The conditions for convergence, especially the restrictions on smoothness and manifold properties, are restrictive and might limit real-world applicability. The error bounds presented, while mathematically rigorous, are expressed in terms of complex parameters that are difficult to interpret in practice.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear mathematical notation and a logical flow of ideas. However, the connection between the theoretical foundations and practical implementation could be strengthened.  The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the practical implications of the theoretical results.  The reference list is comprehensive, demonstrating awareness of related work, but could benefit from more detailed discussion of the limitations of existing methods, further justifying the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Mathematically sound and well-motivated formulation.
*   Connection to established operator theory offers a potentially useful perspective.
*   Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates.
*   Unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – primarily a re-framing of existing techniques.
*   Limited empirical evidence of substantial performance gains.
*   Theoretical analysis is somewhat detached from practical implementation.
*   Strong connection to KMM without demonstrating significant advantages.
*   Restrictive conditions in the theoretical analysis limiting applicability.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper presents a technically sound approach, its lack of substantial novelty and limited empirical advantage over existing methods make it unsuitable for NIPS. The work feels like a mathematically rigorous re-interpretation of existing ideas rather than a breakthrough contribution. A major revision with a more compelling empirical evaluation demonstrating a clear advantage over established techniques, particularly KMM, would be needed for reconsideration. The authors should also focus on clarifying the practical implications of their theoretical results and addressing the limitations of their approach.
