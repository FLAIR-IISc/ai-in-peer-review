Summary
The paper proposes to estimate density ratios r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by recasting either Kp r = Kq 1 (Type I: samples from p and q) or Kt,p r ≈ q (Type II: values of q available on samples from p) as Fredholm integral equations of the first kind, and solving them via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. By applying the representer theorem, the authors derive closed-form kernel ridge–type solutions (FIRE). They provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and introduce an unsupervised model selection criterion (CD-CV) based on matching empirical expectations of random test functions under p and q. Experiments compare FIRE to KMM, LSIF, a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and importance sampling on several datasets.

Relation to prior work
The Type I equality Eq(Φ) = Ep(rΦ) is the starting point of Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), and the paper correctly situates KMM as an instance of the same operator identity with a different loss/constraints. LSIF/KLIEP also minimize L2,p/KL risks for r via linear models in RKHS features. There is a long-standing literature on inverse problems/Tikhonov in RKHS and on operators induced by kernels. The paper’s value-add is to make the Fredholm framing explicit and to derive a family of regularized, closed-form estimators, with some new bounds and a practical unsupervised selection heuristic.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean operator-theoretic formulation that unifies and clarifies connections among KMM/LSIF and RKHS inverse problems.
- Simple, closed-form estimators via kernel ridge regression–like systems; the framework flexibly accommodates different norms.
- Theoretical results: finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and manifolds; explicit decomposition of bias (t), regularization (λ), and sampling terms.
- Type II setting covers practical cases where q is known (possibly up to a constant) but sampling from q is expensive.
- CD-CV is a reasonable, easy-to-implement unsupervised criterion when no labels are available.

Weaknesses
- Novelty/significance: The core Type I equation and RKHS treatment are well known from KMM/mean embeddings; the paper largely repackages these ideas as a Fredholm inverse problem with Tikhonov regularization. The advantages over established baselines (KMM with regularization, LSIF/uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF) are not convincingly demonstrated.
- Constraints: The estimator does not enforce r ≥ 0 or Ep[r] = 1. Negative weights can be problematic for downstream importance-weighted learners and invalidate importance sampling. This is a notable advantage of KMM/LSIF-style formulations with positivity (and often normalization) constraints; the paper should discuss and, ideally, address this.
- Theory-practice mismatch: The bounds assume the same Gaussian kernel for operator and RKHS and fairly strong smoothness (Sobolev W2_2). Experiments use multiple variants and kernels; it is unclear which variants match the theory. Reported rates are relatively slow and degrade steeply with dimension, and no comparison to known minimax rates for likelihood ratio estimation is provided.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical justification. There is no analysis that minimizing JCD leads to consistent tuning of t, λ for the target risk (e.g., L2,p error of r or target-domain predictive risk). As constructed, it can be sensitive to the choice of test function class U and may be gamed by estimators that fit those functions rather than the ratio.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and omits strong baselines or fair settings:
  - KMM is excluded in some experiments due to out-of-sample issues, but simple out-of-sample extensions (e.g., kernel regression of KMM weights) are standard.
  - Recent LSIF variants known for stability/efficiency (uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF) are not included.
  - No synthetic experiments with known ground-truth r to report L2 or KL errors of the ratio itself, nor diagnostics on negativity or normalization violations.
  - Minimal detail on hyperparameter grids, kernel choices for k vs kH, and runtime/memory; matrices involve K^2 or K^3 terms—computational claims should be backed by timings versus QP-based KMM and LSIF with reduced bases.
- Support mismatch is not discussed. Many covariate shift scenarios violate supp(q) ⊆ supp(p); the Fredholm approach using L2,p loss and Kp may perform poorly there. The paper should state assumptions and provide guidance/robustness checks.
- Writing/clarity: Several notational inconsistencies/typos and dense derivations hinder reproducibility. It is not always clear which algorithmic variant is used per experiment.

Questions and requests for the authors
1. Do you ever enforce r ≥ 0 and Ep[r] = 1? If not, how often do negative weights occur in practice, and what is the impact on downstream learners?
2. Please add experiments on synthetic data with known r to report L2,p error of r and calibration (negativity rate, normalization error).
3. Include stronger baselines (uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF) and KMM with standard out-of-sample extension or a regularized KMM variant; report accuracy and training/inference time.
4. Provide ablations for Type I vs Type II, L2,p vs mixed norms vs RKHS-loss variants, and sensitivity to U in CD-CV. Can you justify CD-CV consistency theoretically?
5. Clarify assumptions about support overlap and discuss behavior when supp(q) is outside supp(p).
6. Align theory and practice: specify kernels used in each component (k vs kH), and indicate which theorem applies to which experiment. Provide guidance on choosing t and λ beyond fixed grids.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, unifying operator-theoretic perspective and closed-form RKHS estimators.
- Nontrivial bounds including manifold settings.
- Practical unsupervised tuning heuristic that may help in label-scarce scenarios.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established methods (KMM/LSIF and RKHS inverse problems).
- Missing constraints critical for importance weighting; potential instability/negativity.
- Weak empirical evidence against strong, modern baselines; no ratio-error evaluation.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical guarantees and may be sensitive to test function choice.
- Theory-practice gap and sparse reproducibility details.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject). While the operator framing is elegant and the theoretical development is solid within its assumptions, the practical advantages over established, well-tested methods are not convincingly demonstrated, and important methodological issues (non-negativity/normalization, support mismatch, validation consistency) remain unaddressed.

Confidence: 4/5.
