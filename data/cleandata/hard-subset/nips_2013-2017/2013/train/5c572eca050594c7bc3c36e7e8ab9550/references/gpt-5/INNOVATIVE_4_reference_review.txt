Summary
The paper reframes density ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via Tikhonov-Phillips regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are treated:

- Type I: With samples from both p and q, exploit the identity Kp r = Kq 1 where Kp is the integral operator with kernel k over measure p. The loss can be L2,p, L2,q, or a mixture, and even an RKHS norm version. Representer theorem yields closed-form linear systems (FIRE) for coefficients.

- Type II: When q is known (or values of q are known only at the p-sample), use a δ-kernel Kt,p to get Kt,p r ≈ q with O(t) bias; again solve a regularized inverse problem in RKHS.

The authors give explicit algorithms, emphasize out-of-sample evaluability, and propose an unsupervised model-selection criterion, cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), based on matching moments Eq[u] ≈ Ep[u r̂]. The theory provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, in Euclidean space and when p is supported on a smooth manifold. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, a thresholded KDE ratio baseline, and (partially) KMM, and illustrate CD-CV.

Relation to prior work: Connects closely to KMM (moment matching in RKHS) and LSIF/KLIEP (direct ratio fitting). The novelty is the explicit operator-theoretic inverse-problem formulation, use of general RKHS Tikhonov regularization for the ratio itself (not only for moment matching), the Type II “q-known” variant, manifold-aware analysis, and the unsupervised CD-CV.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Poses density-ratio estimation squarely as a classical inverse problem (Fredholm equation), unlocking standard tools from operator theory and regularization. This is a clean and influential perspective that unifies several strands (KMM, LSIF) and suggests broader variants (loss choices, spectral filters).

- Algorithmic simplicity: Clear representer-theorem solutions with explicit linear systems. Out-of-sample extension is natural. The framework accommodates multiple losses, including a mixed L2,p and L2,q, and an RKHS loss.

- Theoretical analysis: Nontrivial concentration/error bounds and rates under Gaussian kernels, for both Euclidean and manifold support. The manifold case is particularly welcome and uncommon in ratio-estimation papers.

- Practical model selection: CD-CV is an elegant unsupervised criterion leveraging the same identities that motivate the method, and it appears flexible (works with linear and half-space test functions).

- Breadth of applicability: Type II setting (q known, p sampled) is practically relevant (e.g., MCMC, unnormalized models) and underexplored in prior ratio methods.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Positivity and normalization: The ratio r is nonnegative with Ep[r]=1 for densities, but FIRE imposes neither constraint. This can yield negative weights. Please discuss whether simple projections (e.g., clipping at 0) or constrained variants are beneficial, the effect on theory, and whether mixed losses or RKHS-loss mitigate this. A comparison against constrained versions (akin to KMM constraints) would strengthen the empirical story.

- Empirical baselines: While LSIF and a KDE baseline are included, KMM is omitted in some settings due to “out-of-sample” concerns. For many evaluation metrics (e.g., weighted losses on the p-sample, CD-CV, or downstream weighted ERM on the p-sample), KMM’s in-sample weights suffice. Please add KMM results where appropriate. Also consider kernelized uLSIF/KuLSIF and IWCV-based model selection (Sugiyama et al., 2007) as relevant baselines/criteria.

- Rates and interpretation: The corollary rates (e.g., n^{-1/(3.5 + d/2)}) are unusual. How do these compare to known minimax rates for ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2010)? Provide guidance on optimal scaling of t and λ with n,m and a practical interpretation of the d-dependence (especially in the manifold case). A short discussion on tightness and potential improvements via spectral filtering (e.g., Landweber, TSVD) would be valuable.

- Assumptions and small-p regions: The analysis assumes bounded p and q and Sobolev regularity of r and r^2. In practice, density ratios often blow up in low-p regions. Please clarify whether the theory requires p bounded away from 0, or how L2,p integrability suffices, and discuss robustness (e.g., via mixed L2,p + L2,q losses, or thresholding). An experiment probing performance when q/p is large on thin sets would be instructive.

- Computational aspects: FIRE solves n×n systems (often with K^2 terms); this is O(n^3) in general. Claims of “better computational efficiency” should be supported with runtimes and memory vs LSIF/KMM across n,m and kernel widths. Any use of preconditioning, low-rank approximations, or Nyström would be useful to mention.

- Clarity/typos: The paper is generally clear but dense. Consider tightening Section 2 by first presenting the simplest Type I/II cases with kH=k and then listing extensions. Fix typos (e.g., “TiknonovPhilips”), and unify notation (H vs H′, K vs KH). A high-level diagram linking identities → operators → empirical losses → linear systems would aid readability.

Quality
Technically solid overall: the derivations are standard RKHS inverse-problem machinery applied thoughtfully to ratio estimation. The theory is substantial and addresses both sampling and operator approximation error. Empirical evaluation supports the claims but could be broader and more transparent on runtimes and constraint handling.

Clarity
Good, with room for polish as noted. The algorithms are explicit and reproducible given the formulas.

Originality
High. The operator-theoretic framing and Type II setting, plus manifold-aware analysis and CD-CV, represent a meaningful step beyond prior ratio-estimation formulations.

Significance
High. Density-ratio estimation underpins covariate shift, OOD reweighting, and Monte Carlo. A unifying inverse-problem lens can influence both algorithms and theory, and the practical CD-CV is likely to be adopted.

Questions for the authors
- How do you handle negativity of r̂ in practice? Any effect on CD-CV or downstream tasks?
- Can you provide KMM results where only in-sample weights are needed?
- What are recommended defaults or heuristics for choosing t and λ jointly?
- Any insights on using spectral regularization beyond Tikhonov in your framework?

Recommendation
Accept (with minor revisions). Novel and bold conceptual contribution with solid theory and practical algorithms; empirical coverage can be improved but does not detract from the paper’s value.

Reviewer confidence: High.
