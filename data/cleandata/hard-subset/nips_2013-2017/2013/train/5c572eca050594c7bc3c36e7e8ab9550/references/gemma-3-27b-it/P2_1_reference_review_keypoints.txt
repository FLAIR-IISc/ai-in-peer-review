- Novel formulation: casts density ratio estimation as an inverse problem via the Fredholm equation of the first kind.  
- Method: develops FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) using RKHS and regularization.  
- Theory: provides convergence rates and error bounds (focused on the Gaussian kernel).  
- Model selection: proposes an unsupervised cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for parameter tuning.  
- Comparisons: explicitly relates to and contrasts with KMM and LSIF, claiming stability and analytical advantages.  
- Experiments: comprehensive empirical evaluation across datasets and baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE, importance sampling).  
- Strength — novelty: introduces a fresh, mathematically rigorous perspective linking operator theory to density ratio estimation.  
- Strength — theoretical contribution: substantial theoretical analysis distinguishes the work from largely empirical papers.  
- Strength — principled framework: flexible RKHS + regularization approach with explicit FIRE formulas.  
- Strength — CD-CV: addresses unsupervised parameter selection, a practical challenge.  
- Strength — clarity of related work: thorough discussion of connections and trade-offs with existing methods.  
- Weakness — presentation: theoretical results are dense and require strong functional analysis background.  
- Weakness — practical impact: empirical improvements over strong baselines are not consistently substantial.  
- Weakness — kernel focus: theory centers on Gaussian kernel, limiting generality to other kernels.  
- Weakness — repetition: some concepts are repeated in the introduction and section 2.  
- Quality: technically sound and well-supported by analysis and experiments.  
- Clarity: generally well-written but mathematically challenging for non-experts.  
- Originality: framing as an inverse problem/Fredholm approach is novel and significant.  
- Significance: potentially important, though practical impact could be demonstrated more strongly.  
- Acceptance pros: novel framework, strong theory, principled methods, extensive experiments, clear related-work links.  
- Acceptance cons: accessibility of theory, limited practical gains, theoretical scope limited to Gaussian kernel.  
- Overall: reviewer recommends “strong accept” with suggestions to simplify theoretical exposition and test other kernels.
