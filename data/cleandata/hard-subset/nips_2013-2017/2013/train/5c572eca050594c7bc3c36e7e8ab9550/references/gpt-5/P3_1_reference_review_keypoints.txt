- Paper recasts density-ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation and proposes two formulations (Type I and Type II) solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form FIRE estimators.  
- Type I: use a positive-definite kernel to form Kp r = Kq 1 and invert the compact operator Kp; Type II: use a δ-like kernel Kt to approximate q when q(x) is known at p-sampled points.  
- Multiple losses are considered (L2,p, mixed L2,p/L2,q, RKHS norm) and variants depending on whether the RKHS kernel equals the integral kernel; representer theorem gives explicit solutions.  
- Theoretical contribution: nonasymptotic error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and compact submanifolds, with explicit dependence on bandwidth t, regularization λ, sample sizes, and dimension.  
- Introduces unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) using importance-sampling identity validated with random test functions.  
- Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (where applicable), inverse KDE baseline, and standard importance sampling on regression/classification under covariate shift.  
- Relation to prior work: connects closely to KMM (Type I with RKHS-loss is a regularized KMM) and to LSIF; paper frames these as inverse problems and situates work within spectral regularization literature.  
- Strength: conceptual unification — operator-theoretic framing clarifies need for regularization and yields simple closed-form RKHS estimators.  
- Strength: practical flexibility with multiple loss choices and a Type II option for settings with known q values.  
- Strength: unsupervised hyperparameter tuning (CD-CV) and simpler computation with natural out-of-sample extension compared to KMM QP.  
- Weakness: novelty is incremental — Type I RKHS-loss reduces to regularized KMM and L2,p-loss is a smoothed moment-matching objective; relations to KuLSIF/uLSIF and Bregman methods need deeper discussion.  
- Weakness: statistical rates are slow (e.g., n−1/(3.5 + d/2)) and lack comparison to known minimax lower bounds or discussion if inverse framing makes the problem intrinsically harder.  
- Weakness: identifiability/stability concerns — Kp is compact and inverse is ill-posed; conditions for identifiability, spectral role, and bias from kernel choices are not fully articulated.  
- Weakness: unconstrained regression may yield negative ratios; paper lacks discussion of positivity/normalization constraints or post-processing effects on theory and practice.  
- Weakness: Type II practicality underexplored — no MCMC/unnormalized-density experiments and limited guidance on tuning t vs λ in finite samples.  
- Weakness: experimental gaps — missing strong baselines (KuLSIF/RuLSIF, logistic-based estimators), limited dataset sizes/scalability discussion (Nyström, random features), sparse reporting (effect sizes, CIs, ablations).  
- Clarity: generally readable but notation is sometimes overloaded and assumptions/theorems should be centralized; manifold boundary case is handled inconsistently.  
- Significance: operator viewpoint and closed-form estimators are useful; unsupervised model selection is practical, but incremental novelty and limited empirical scope temper impact.  
- Quality: technically plausible but proofs deferred; bounds seem conservative and would benefit from tighter analyses or comparisons to minimax benchmarks.  
- Suggestions: clarify identifiability via spectral/Mercer analysis, discuss positivity/normalization, compare to KuLSIF/uLSIF and classification-based estimators, provide large-scale variants, include a Type II real-world application, and analyze CD-CV robustness/statistics.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear unifying formulation, solid if conservative theoretical bounds including manifold support, practical unsupervised model selection, and natural out-of-sample extension.  
- Cons for acceptance: incremental novelty vs KMM/LSIF, slow rates without optimality discussion, missing strong baselines and Type II use case, and under-discussed positivity/identifiability issues.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject pending stronger empirical comparisons (KuLSIF/classification-based), a Type II application, and clearer identifiability/positivity treatment.
