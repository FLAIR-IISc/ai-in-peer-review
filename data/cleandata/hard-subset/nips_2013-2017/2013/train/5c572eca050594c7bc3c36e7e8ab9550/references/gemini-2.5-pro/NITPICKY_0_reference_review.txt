### Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel framework for estimating the density ratio (or importance function) `q(x)/p(x)`. The central idea is to rephrase the estimation problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is a classical inverse problem. The authors derive two primary formulations: Type I, where samples from both distributions `p` and `q` are available (`Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)`), and Type II, where samples from `p` are available along with the values of the function `q` at those sample points (`Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`). To solve these ill-posed problems, they apply Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm penalty. This principled approach leads to a family of algorithms, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which have simple, closed-form solutions within the kernel learning paradigm. The paper provides a theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels and introduces an unsupervised model selection procedure called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). The proposed method is experimentally compared against related techniques like LSIF and a baseline kernel density-based estimator.

The work connects to and builds upon a significant body of recent literature on importance estimation, notably Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF) [10]. The authors correctly position their RKHS-norm-based formulation as a regularized version of KMM and adapt ideas from LSIF for their model selection criterion.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of the paper is the elegant formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation. This provides a strong theoretical underpinning for the problem and connects it to the well-established field of inverse problems and operator theory. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
2.  **Algorithmic Simplicity and Flexibility:** The application of RKHS-based Tikhonov regularization leads to algorithms with simple, analytical solutions (requiring only matrix inversion). The framework is also flexible, capable of accommodating different loss functions (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norms) and settings (Type I and Type II).
3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper includes a non-trivial theoretical analysis, providing error bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators under different geometric assumptions (Euclidean space and manifolds). This adds considerable rigor to the work.
4.  **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposal of the CD-CV method addresses the critical and often-overlooked problem of hyperparameter selection in an unsupervised setting. This is a valuable practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical errors in key equations to significant clarity issues that impede reproducibility and cast doubt on the experimental results.

1.  **Critical Errors and Inconsistencies:**
    *   The very first sentence of the introduction contains a fundamental typo, stating the problem is to estimate "q(x)p(x)" when it should be "q(x)/p(x)".
    *   Equation (16), which defines the core performance measure `J_CD` for the CD-CV model selection, is mathematically incorrect. It compares `sum u(x)f(x)` to `sum u(x)`. It should be comparing the empirical expectations, i.e., `(1/n) * sum u(x)f(x)` vs. `(1/m) * sum u(x)`. As written, the metric is heavily dependent on sample sizes `n` and `m` in a way that does not approximate the desired population-level equality. Since the experimental model selection relies on this metric, this error undermines the validity of the presented results.
    *   In the "Settings and objects" subsection of Section 2, the empirical approximation of the L2,p norm is given as `‖f‖^2_{L2,p} ≈ (1/n) * sum f(xi)`. This is incorrect; it should be `(1/n) * sum |f(xi)|^2`.
    *   There is an inconsistency in the description of the Type II approximation error: the introduction states it is `O(t)`, while Equation (4) describes it as `o(1)`. While technically `O(t)` implies `o(1)` as `t -> 0`, using the more precise rate `O(t)` consistently would be better.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The derivation of the final algorithms is opaque. The paper presents the final solutions in Equations (8), (10), etc., with the brief justification "From Representer Theorem and matrix manipulation".
    *   The notation used for the kernel matrices is highly ambiguous and non-standard. The definitions `(Kp,p)ij = 1/n k(xi, xj)` and `(Kp,q)ij = 1/m k(xi, x'j)` embed the sample size normalization within the matrix definition itself. This makes expressions like `K^2_{p,p} K_H` in Equation (8) extremely difficult to interpret. Does `K^2_{p,p}` mean matrix multiplication of the normalized matrix or an element-wise square? This lack of clarity makes it nearly impossible to independently reproduce the algorithms from the description provided. A clear, step-by-step derivation is required.

3.  **Methodological and Experimental Concerns:**
    *   The CD-CV method for model selection depends on a user-specified family of test functions `U`. The paper uses random linear functions and half-spaces but provides no analysis of how sensitive the parameter selection is to this choice. This leaves a crucial component of the method under-specified and potentially non-robust.
    *   The experimental comparison is incomplete. KMM, a method the paper repeatedly compares itself to conceptually, is omitted from the main experiments because "out-of-sample extension is necessary". This is an insufficient reason. Given that FIRE is proposed as a more stable, regularized alternative, a direct comparison of stability and performance is essential.
    *   The paper claims "better computational efficiency" in the contributions summary but provides no supporting evidence, such as a complexity analysis or runtime comparisons in the experiments section.

**Decision:**
While the paper is founded on a creative and theoretically appealing idea, the execution is unfortunately not up to the standards of NIPS. The presence of multiple critical errors in fundamental equations, combined with a severe lack of notational clarity that obstructs reproducibility, makes the work technically unsound in its current form. These are not minor cosmetic issues; they are deep flaws that affect the core claims and results of the paper. I cannot recommend this paper for acceptance. The authors should be encouraged to perform a thorough revision to correct the errors, clarify the derivations and notation, and conduct a more complete experimental evaluation before resubmitting to a future venue.
