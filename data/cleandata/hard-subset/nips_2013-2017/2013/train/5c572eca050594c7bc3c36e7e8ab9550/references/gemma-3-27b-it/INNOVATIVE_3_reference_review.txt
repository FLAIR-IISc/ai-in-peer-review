This paper presents a compelling and novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, specifically framing it within the theory of Fredholm integral equations and leveraging tools from RKHS and regularization. The connection to classical operator theory is a significant strength, opening avenues for theoretical analysis and potentially bridging the gap between kernel methods and a deeper understanding of their properties. The introduction of the FIRE algorithms (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) is a welcome contribution, and the theoretical results, while potentially dense, represent a serious effort to ground these methods in solid mathematical foundations.

The core idea – linearizing density estimation via integral operators – is genuinely insightful. It’s not merely another incremental improvement on existing techniques like KMM or LSIF; it offers a fundamentally different perspective. The paper successfully demonstrates how standard regularization techniques can be applied within this framework, leading to easily implementable algorithms. The exploration of different norms and loss functions, and the discussion of Type I and Type II formulations, highlight the flexibility of the proposed approach. I particularly appreciate the inclusion of unsupervised parameter selection (CD-CV), addressing a crucial challenge in this area.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is ambitious, the conditions required for convergence and the complexity of the bounds may limit their practical applicability. The connection to existing work, particularly KMM, is well discussed, but the practical advantages of FIRE over KMM aren't always clearly articulated beyond the potential for better stability (which requires further experimental justification). The experimental section, while comprehensive, feels somewhat overwhelming with tables and lacks a truly insightful discussion of the results. The choice of datasets feels standard, and it isn't immediately clear whether FIRE consistently outperforms baselines across a wider variety of scenarios. The lack of detailed ablation studies (e.g., sensitivity to regularization parameter) is also a drawback. The manuscript suffers from a bit of density; streamlining the presentation, particularly in the theoretical sections, would significantly improve clarity. The use of multiple subscripts and indices can be confusing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The Fredholm equation formulation is a genuinely new and interesting approach to density ratio estimation.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis, including convergence rates, which is rare in this field.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The connection to operator theory could lead to new insights and algorithms.
*   **Flexibility:** The framework allows for the incorporation of different norms and regularization techniques.
*   **Unsupervised Parameter Selection:** The CD-CV method is a valuable addition.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The theoretical analysis is dense and may be inaccessible to many readers.
*   **Experimental Limitations:** The experimental section lacks a clear and insightful discussion of the results, and the choice of datasets is fairly standard.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript could benefit from more streamlined presentation and better use of notation.
*   **Practical Advantages:** The practical benefits of FIRE over existing methods aren’t fully demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach and the theoretical depth outweigh the shortcomings in clarity and experimental analysis. The authors have identified a promising new direction for research, and while further work is needed to fully realize its potential, the paper is sufficiently impactful to warrant acceptance. I lean towards acceptance, recognizing that improvements in presentation and further experimental validation would strengthen the work considerably. The authors should be encouraged to address the clarity issues and expand the experimental evaluation in future work.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
