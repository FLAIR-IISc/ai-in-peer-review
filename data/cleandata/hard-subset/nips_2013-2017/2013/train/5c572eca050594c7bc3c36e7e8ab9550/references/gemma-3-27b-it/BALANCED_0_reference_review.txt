This paper proposes a novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm equation, and leverages tools from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop a new family of algorithms termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The core idea is compelling: recasting a common machine learning problem within the well-studied framework of integral equations allows for application of existing theoretical results and efficient algorithmic solutions. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds for Gaussian kernels, and present experimental results demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Insightful Formulation:**  Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is a genuinely novel and insightful contribution. This perspective unlocks the potential to apply a wealth of tools from classical analysis to a problem that is usually tackled with more ad-hoc methods.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rates and error bounds for Gaussian kernels. This level of mathematical detail is commendable and sets this work apart from many empirical contributions in this area. The extension to manifolds is also a notable strength.
* **Algorithmic Simplicity and Efficiency:**  The resulting FIRE algorithms are described as straightforward to implement, leveraging standard kernel methods. The explicit formulas derived for the solutions are a practical benefit.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:**  The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing FIRE with several state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) on multiple datasets and with different resampling strategies.  The inclusion of a novel unsupervised parameter selection technique (CD-CV) is also positive.
* **Clear Connections to Related Work:** The paper does a good job of situating its contributions within the broader landscape of density ratio estimation, covariate shift adaptation, and kernel methods.  The detailed discussion of relationships to KMM and LSIF is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theoretical Details:** While the theoretical rigor is a strength, the density of mathematical notation and proofs may make the paper inaccessible to some readers.  A more intuitive explanation of the key theoretical results would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Guarantees:** The theoretical analysis primarily focuses on the Gaussian kernel.  While understandable given the complexity, extending these results to other commonly used kernels would significantly enhance the paper's impact. The assumptions about smoothness (Sobolev spaces) could also be more clearly discussed in terms of practical implications.
* **Experimental Setup Details:** While the experiments are comprehensive, the description of the experimental setup could be more detailed. Specific parameter settings for competing methods, hardware used, and statistical significance testing results would strengthen the conclusions. It’s unclear how sensitive performance is to the choices made in the resampling procedure (PCA parameters, label subsets).
* **Practical Significance of Improvements:**  The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, but the improvements over existing methods aren't always dramatic.  A more thorough discussion of the scenarios where FIRE offers a substantial advantage would be helpful. It would be beneficial to highlight the specific situations where FIRE outperforms KMM/LSIF.
* **CD-CV lacks detailed justification:** The motivations and theoretical underpinnings of the CD-CV method, while empirically effective, are not thoroughly explored. A more in-depth analysis of *why* this cross-validation strategy works well would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the properties of the proposed algorithms.
* The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates competitive performance.
* The paper has the potential to influence future research in this area by providing a new perspective on a well-studied problem.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The theoretical complexity may limit the paper's accessibility to a wider audience.
* The experimental improvements over existing methods are not always substantial.
* The scope of theoretical guarantees is currently limited to the Gaussian kernel.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation.  The novel formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis, and comprehensive experimental evaluation are all commendable. While the theoretical complexity and limited scope of guarantees are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the paper's strengths. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide a more intuitive explanation of the key theoretical results.
* Consider extending the theoretical analysis to other commonly used kernels.
* Provide more detailed information about the experimental setup, including parameter settings and statistical significance testing.
* Discuss the scenarios where FIRE offers a substantial advantage over existing methods.
* Elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings and justification of the CD-CV method.
* Address the limitations of the smoothness assumptions in the theoretical analysis.
