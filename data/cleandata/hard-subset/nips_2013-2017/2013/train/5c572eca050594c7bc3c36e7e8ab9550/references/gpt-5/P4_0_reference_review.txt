Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies density ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) through an inverse-problem lens. It rewrites basic importance sampling identities as Fredholm integral equations of the first kind and solves them via Tikhonov–Phillips regularization in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Two settings are defined: Type I uses the identity Kp r = Kq 1, appropriate when samples from both p and q are available; Type II uses an approximate δ-kernel identity Kt,p r ≈ q, suited to cases where q is known pointwise at sample locations from p (e.g., unnormalized models or MCMC contexts). Empirical versions replace integrals by sample averages, yielding closed-form solutions via the representer theorem (FIRE: Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper also proposes an unsupervised model selection criterion, cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), based on checking importance sampling equalities on random test functions. Theoretical results provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and compact submanifolds. Experiments compare FIRE to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], LSIF [10], and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline.

This work is closely related to prior density ratio estimation methods developed for covariate shift and sample selection bias, including KMM [9], LSIF [10], and KLIEP [23], as well as operator-theoretic and inverse-problem perspectives in RKHS [3, 20]. The Type I formulation encompasses the KMM moment-matching identity Eq(KH(x,·)) = Ep(rKH(x,·)) and can be seen as a regularized variant with a different loss. Unlike KMM’s constrained QP over weights at training points, FIRE optimizes an RKHS function with explicit closed-form solutions and out-of-sample evaluations. The use of δ-kernels to obtain Kt,p r ≈ q connects to classical kernel smoothing bias analyses [24], enabling operator-based error decompositions. The manifold setting leverages spectral analysis similar in spirit to work on density-dependent operators [18, 25].

Strengths:
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation that unifies density ratio estimation with classical inverse problems and RKHS regularization.
- Simple, explicit algorithms with out-of-sample evaluation, derived via the representer theorem for several choices of norms (L2,p, mixtures of L2,p and L2,q, RKHS norm).
- Theoretical analysis provides finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, including separation of approximation (bias in t), regularization (λ), and sample errors.
- Type II setting is practically relevant when q is known pointwise but sampling from q is difficult.
- CD-CV offers a plausible unsupervised model selection procedure aligned with the problem structure.
- Empirical comparisons to KMM, LSIF, and a KDE-based baseline, with discussion of computational aspects.

Weaknesses:
- The rates obtained (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2) in L2,p) are relatively slow and their optimality relative to known minimax rates for likelihood ratio estimation [14] is not discussed; guidance on tuning t and λ to attain these rates is limited.
- The δ-kernel approximation introduces bias terms O(t) (or t1−ε on manifolds); practical sensitivity to t is not thoroughly explored, and Type II inherits this approximation error.
- No constraints enforce nonnegativity or normalization of r, which can be important for importance sampling stability; the impact of negative estimates is not analyzed.
- Computational complexity involves inverting n×n systems; comparisons to KMM’s QP and scalable approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features, iterative solvers) are not provided.
- Experimental details are relatively sparse (dataset sizes, feature preprocessing, hyperparameter ranges are briefly listed but ablations, sensitivity, and runtime comparisons are limited). Fairness of comparisons (e.g., out-of-sample extension for KMM variants) is only partially addressed.
- CD-CV, while intuitive, lacks theoretical justification (e.g., consistency) and its dependence on the choice of test function family is only empirically probed.

Quality:
The methodological derivations are sound, and the empirical risk formulations and closed-form solutions follow standard RKHS machinery. The concentration bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels are plausible, with clear separation of approximation, regularization, and estimation errors. Assumptions (bounded densities, Sobolev smoothness) are standard but restrictive; the results depend on t and λ regimes that are not fully operationalized for practitioners. The CD-CV criterion is reasonable but lacks formal guarantees.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear delineation of Type I/II settings and corresponding algorithms. However, notation is heavy and sometimes overloaded (K, KH, Kp,p, etc.); a concise notation table and pseudocode for each algorithm would improve readability. Some typographical issues (e.g., Tikhonov–Phillips spelling) and incomplete references should be corrected. The experimental section would benefit from more detail (dataset sizes, splits, standard errors) and clarity on evaluation metrics.

Originality:
Casting density ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem with Tikhonov regularization and deriving multiple RKHS-based estimators is a useful unifying perspective. While related to KMM/LSIF/KLIEP and RKHS inverse-problem literature, the combination (especially the Type II setting and manifold-rate results) appears novel. The CD-CV criterion is a practical contribution.

Significance:
Density ratio estimation is central to covariate shift adaptation, OOD reweighting, and importance sampling. The proposed framework offers a principled alternative with theoretical support and straightforward implementation. The impact would be strengthened by scalability solutions and broader empirical validation, but the work is likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Discuss how the obtained rates compare with minimax lower bounds [14] and whether faster rates are attainable under stronger assumptions.
- Add positivity/normalization mechanisms (e.g., nonnegativity constraints, exponentiated parametrization) and analyze their effects.
- Provide scalable implementations (Nyström, random features, iterative solvers with early stopping) and runtime/memory comparisons to KMM/LSIF.
- Expand experiments with more benchmarks, ablations over t and λ, sensitivity of CD-CV to test function families, and downstream task performance under covariate shift.
- Clarify when Type II is advantageous in practice (e.g., unnormalized models) and quantify the bias from Kt,p approximations.
- Improve clarity with a notation table, algorithm pseudocode, and polished references.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled and unifying inverse-problem formulation with explicit, practical algorithms.
- Theoretical bounds including manifold settings.
- Out-of-sample estimators and flexible loss choices.
- Practical unsupervised model selection tailored to the task.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Relatively slow rates without discussion of optimality; limited guidance on tuning.
- Lack of positivity/normalization constraints for ratios.
- No scalability strategy; empirical evaluation is limited and lacks comprehensive baselines and ablations.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical guarantees and depends on arbitrary test function choices.
