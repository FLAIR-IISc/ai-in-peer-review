Summary
The paper reframes density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding simple closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) with out-of-sample evaluation. Two formulations are proposed: Type I uses the identity Kp r = Kq 1, where both p and q are available via samples; Type II uses a δ-kernel approximation Kt,p r ≈ q when q is known pointwise on samples from p (e.g., in MCMC contexts). Empirical versions produce linear systems over kernel matrices and admit representer-theorem solutions. The paper also develops an unsupervised model-selection strategy, cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), based on matching empirical expectations of random test functions under q and reweighted p. Theoretical analysis (for Gaussian kernels, with RKHS identical to the integral kernel) provides concentration bounds and convergence rates on Euclidean domains and manifolds. Experiments compare FIRE with LSIF and a thresholded KDE ratio baseline, and study the CD-CV procedure.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects classical inverse problems (Engl et al.) and RKHS regularization (Schölkopf & Smola; Steinwart & Christmann) to density-ratio estimation under covariate shift. It clarifies that Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) corresponds to the same operator identity Eq(Φ) = Ep(r Φ), and shows a regularized alternative with an out-of-sample function representation. It relates to LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.), highlighting a different use of test functions (for validation rather than as a basis). The work also cites manifold learning/operator viewpoints and recent analyses of KMM and minimax likelihood ratio estimation (Nguyen et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual: A clean operator-theoretic viewpoint that unifies and clarifies the density-ratio problem as an inverse problem. The Fredholm formulation and Tikhonov regularization are natural and principled.
- Algorithms: Closed-form solutions via representer theorem; out-of-sample evaluation; multiple loss choices (L2,p, γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q, and RKHS loss) with straightforward matrix expressions.
- Theory: Nontrivial generalization bounds with explicit dependencies on kernel bandwidth t, regularization λ, sample sizes n,m, and domain dimension d; manifold extensions are valuable.
- Practicality: CD-CV offers an unsupervised parameter-selection mechanism, a recognized pain point in covariate shift.
- Breadth: Discusses both settings where q is a density sampled via data (Type I) and where q is known pointwise but difficult to sample (Type II).

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Positivity and normalization: The estimated ratio f can be negative and need not satisfy Ep[f]=1. This can be problematic in importance weighting and covariate-shift applications. KMM imposes constraints (β ≥ 0, Ep[β]=1); FIRE does not. At least a post-hoc projection or constrained version should be discussed and evaluated.
- Clarity/notation: The paper uses multiple kernels (integral kernel k and RKHS kernel kH) and sometimes sets them equal only for theory. The roles of Kp, Kq, Kt,p and their empirical counterparts are heavy; some equations (e.g., Kt,p q/p ≈ q) could benefit from a more explicit variable-of-integration and approximation justification. A small self-contained derivation clarifying Eq. (2–4) would help.
- Assumptions vs practice: Theory assumes Gaussian kernels and identical kernels for operator and RKHS, plus smoothness (q/p ∈ W2
2), boundedness, small t. Experiments appear to use Gaussian kernels but do not analyze sensitivity to violating these assumptions or to the choice k ≠ kH (which sections 2 allow). The condition m > λ1/3 n appearing in Corollary 2 deserves discussion and practical guidance.
- Rates: The exponents (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) are somewhat unusual and not directly compared to known minimax rates for likelihood ratio estimation or to rates in Nguyen et al. A brief comparison would contextualize significance.
- Computational aspects: The linear systems involve Kp,p squared or cubed times KH (e.g., K^2 KH + nλI, or K^3 + λI). For large n, cubic complexity and potential ill-conditioning are concerns. No runtime, memory, or scalability experiments are presented; no discussion of Nyström or random-feature approximations or preconditioning is included.
- Baselines: The experimental baselines are limited. TIKDE is weak and sensitive to thresholding; KMM is omitted due to “out-of-sample” concerns, but it can be fairly compared by evaluating on the same test points via weights on the training set. More recent/stronger baselines (uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF) are not reported. The fairness and breadth of comparisons could be improved.
- CD-CV: While appealing, the choice and number of test functions U, and robustness to their distribution, is not deeply analyzed. There is a risk of mismatch between validation functions and downstream tasks; the experiment partially addresses this but more systematic analysis would help. Also, variance/bias trade-offs in JCD and its consistency are not discussed.
- Experimental scope: Datasets are modest in size; it would be useful to include larger-scale and higher-dimensional covariate-shift benchmarks, and applications like importance sampling or off-policy evaluation where Type II might shine. Claims of “better computational efficiency” are not substantiated with timing or complexity comparisons.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the operator formulation and RKHS regularization are correct, and the empirical solutions follow from the representer theorem. Theoretical results are substantial but rest on strong assumptions and specialized settings; some constants/conditions (e.g., λ dependencies) deserve more intuition. Experiments demonstrate feasibility but do not fully validate advantages against strong baselines or at scale.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, but notation is dense. Consider simplifying the presentation: separate clearly the operator kernel vs RKHS kernel; provide a concise table of symbols; add a short proof sketch in the main text explaining how the main terms in the bounds arise. Clarify the δ-kernel approximation and its error.

Originality
Moderate to high. While closely related to KMM/LSIF, the explicit Fredholm inverse-problem viewpoint, Type II formulation, and CD-CV selection are interesting contributions. The regularized RKHS solution akin to a regularized KMM with out-of-sample function is a useful twist.

Significance
Potentially significant: a principled, flexible framework with theory and practical procedures. Impact depends on demonstrating robustness and advantages over strong baselines and on scalability.

Questions for the authors
- Can you enforce non-negativity and Ep[f]=1 without losing the closed-form solution (e.g., via projection or a simple constrained variant)? Any empirical impact?
- How sensitive are results to choosing k ≠ kH in practice? Any guidance?
- Can you provide runtime/memory comparisons to LSIF/KMM and report scalability with n?
- How does CD-CV perform when validation functions differ significantly from downstream test functions? Any theoretical guarantees?
- Why exclude KMM in experiments? A reasonable comparison using weights on training data seems possible.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add positivity/normalization handling and report its effect.
- Compare against uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF and include KMM with a fair protocol.
- Provide timing/memory and large-n experiments; consider Nyström/random features.
- Expand sensitivity analyses for t, λ, and γ; discuss practical selection ranges.
- Clarify notation and add a compact symbol glossary; include a brief bound derivation sketch.

Pros
- Elegant operator-theoretic framing with simple closed-form RKHS solutions.
- Solid theoretical analysis including manifold case.
- Useful unsupervised model-selection idea (CD-CV).
- Out-of-sample function estimate unlike KMM.

Cons
- No positivity/normalization for ratios; potential practical issues.
- Limited baselines and empirical scope; no scalability evidence.
- Dense presentation; some assumptions mismatch practice.
- Unusual rates not compared to existing theory; limited intuition.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The conceptual contribution and theory are compelling, and the methods are simple and potentially impactful. Strengthen the empirical section (baselines, scalability, constraints) and clarify presentation to solidify the case.
