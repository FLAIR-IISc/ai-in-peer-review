## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper reformulates density ratio estimation (a common problem in transfer learning and importance sampling) as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations. The authors leverage tools from regularization theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop algorithms (FIRE) for estimating the density ratio, providing a principled framework with strong theoretical foundations. They propose two types of formulations (Type I and Type II) depending on the availability of samples from both densities or just values of the target density, and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation.  The paper also introduces a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for unsupervised parameter selection.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a significant and novel contribution. It allows the application of a rich set of tools from operator theory and functional analysis to a problem traditionally tackled with more ad-hoc methods.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides comprehensive theoretical analysis, including concentration inequalities and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in various settings (Euclidean space and manifolds). This is a major strength and sets it apart from many empirical approaches in this area.
* **Principled Framework:** The use of RKHS and Tikhonov regularization provides a well-founded and flexible framework for solving the inverse problem. The authors explore different norms and loss functions, broadening the applicability of their approach.
* **Practical Algorithm (FIRE):** The resulting algorithms (FIRE) are presented clearly and are computationally tractable, with explicit formulas derived based on the Representer Theorem.
* **Unsupervised Parameter Selection (CD-CV):** The proposed CD-CV method addresses a crucial challenge in unsupervised learning – parameter tuning – and demonstrates good performance in experiments.
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  The paper explicitly connects to related work like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), highlighting both similarities and differences, and positioning their contribution effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The paper is dense and requires a strong background in functional analysis and kernel methods to fully grasp. This might limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis heavily relies on the Gaussian kernel and smoothness assumptions on the densities. The generalization to other kernels and less regular densities is not fully explored.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, it focuses primarily on a few datasets. Expanding the evaluation to more diverse and challenging scenarios would strengthen the claims.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to KMM is present, however more extensive comparisons with modern density ratio estimation techniques beyond LSIF and TIKDE would be valuable.  For instance, recent neural network-based approaches have shown strong performance in this area.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the algorithms are presented as tractable, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost, particularly the matrix inversions involved in solving the regularized problems, would be beneficial, especially for high-dimensional data.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound, with strong theoretical backing and a well-defined methodology. The analysis is rigorous, and the authors acknowledge potential limitations.
* **Clarity (7/10):**  While the writing is generally clear, the technical depth and density of the material make it challenging to follow for those not already familiar with the underlying concepts.  More intuitive explanations of the Fredholm equation formulation and its connection to density ratio estimation would improve clarity.
* **Originality (9/10):** The formulation of the problem as a Fredholm equation and the application of regularization theory in this context are highly original. The CD-CV method also adds to the novelty of the work.
* **Significance (8/10):** The work addresses an important problem with broad applicability, and the proposed approach has the potential to advance the state of the art in density ratio estimation.  The theoretical results and principled framework are particularly significant.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density ratio estimation.
* The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithms.
* The algorithms are well-defined and practically implementable.
* The CD-CV method offers a principled way to address parameter selection in unsupervised settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper’s complexity and technical depth may limit its accessibility.
* The theoretical analysis is currently limited to Gaussian kernels and smooth densities.
* The experimental evaluation could be broadened to include more diverse datasets and state-of-the-art comparison methods.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. Addressing the concerns regarding clarity and expanding the experimental evaluation would further strengthen the work and increase its impact. Specifically, the authors should consider including a more accessible explanation of the core idea for a broader audience, and broaden the comparison with recent state-of-the-art density ratio estimation methods (e.g. neural network based techniques).
