This paper presents a novel and principled framework for density ratio estimation, q(x)/p(x), by reformulating it as a classical inverse problem. The core idea is to leverage the importance sampling identity, Ep[h(x)q(x)/p(x)] = Eq[h(x)], by instantiating the test function h(x) with a kernel k(x,y). This transforms the problem into a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose solving this ill-posed problem using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), leading to a family of algorithms they name FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator).

The paper develops two main settings: Type I, where samples from both p and q are available, and Type II, where samples from p are available along with functional access to q(x). The RKHS regularization framework naturally leads to simple, closed-form solutions involving kernel matrix operations, analogous to other kernel-based learning algorithms. This work connects density ratio estimation to the rich literature on inverse problems and operator theory, providing a new perspective that is distinct from, yet related to, prior methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Factorization (LSIF). Key contributions include this novel formulation, the resulting flexible algorithms, a rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean and manifold domains, and a practical unsupervised model selection method called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The central contribution of casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is both novel and powerful. It provides a principled foundation rooted in classical analysis and operator theory. This "linearization" of the problem via integral operators is an insightful conceptual leap that separates the core estimation problem from the subsequent regularization step, offering great clarity and flexibility.

2.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** A major strength of the paper is the detailed theoretical analysis in Section 3. Providing explicit error bounds and convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4) adds significant weight and credibility to the proposed method. The analysis is modern, considering not only the standard Euclidean case but also data lying on a manifold, which is highly relevant to contemporary machine learning. These guarantees provide a strong theoretical justification for the FIRE algorithms.

3.  **Practical and Principled Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for hyperparameter selection is a significant practical contribution. Model selection is a notoriously difficult problem in unsupervised learning. CD-CV is well-motivated by the underlying importance sampling identity and provides a fully unsupervised way to tune the kernel width and regularization parameter. The experimental validation of this approach is a valuable part of the paper.

4.  **Clear Connection to and Distinction from Prior Work:** The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to existing methods. They clearly articulate the relationship to KMM, correctly identifying it as being analogous to a specific instance of their framework (Type I, RKHS norm loss) but without regularization and using a different optimization scheme. The comparison with LSIF is also insightful, noting the similarity in the validation measure but the difference in its use (validation vs. constructing the estimator).

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The proposed algorithms rely on inverting an n x n matrix, where n is the number of samples from p. This leads to an O(n^3) computational complexity, which is prohibitive for large datasets. While this is a common limitation of many kernel methods, the paper would be strengthened by acknowledging this and briefly discussing potential avenues for scaling up, such as using Nyström approximation or random features.

2.  **Comparison of Theoretical Rates:** The convergence rates derived are a key contribution. However, their significance would be clearer if they were contextualized by comparing them to existing minimax rates for density ratio estimation (e.g., from Nguyen et al., [14]) or the rates known for related methods. This would help the reader better appreciate the theoretical quality of the proposed estimator.

3.  **Exploration of Kernel Choices:** The framework allows for the kernel `k` defining the integral operator and the kernel `k_H` defining the RKHS for regularization to be different. This is a potentially powerful source of flexibility. However, the paper does not explore the implications of this choice in depth, and the theory section assumes they are the same Gaussian kernel. A short discussion on the intuition or principles for choosing `k` and `k_H` differently would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Clarity on Experimental Comparisons:** The experiments are generally well-designed. However, the comparison to KMM could be more direct. While the authors correctly note the out-of-sample extension issue for KMM, in the context of covariate shift for a supervised task (regression/classification), KMM can be directly used to compute importance weights for the training points from `p`. A direct comparison in this setting would be very informative, as KMM is arguably the closest state-of-the-art competitor.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant contribution to the field. The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a beautiful and powerful idea that opens up new connections to classical mathematics and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms. This conceptual novelty is backed by strong theoretical guarantees and a practical solution to the challenging problem of model selection. The weaknesses are relatively minor and mostly relate to opportunities for extending the work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and the results are likely to be of great interest to both theorists and practitioners in machine learning. It clearly advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation.
