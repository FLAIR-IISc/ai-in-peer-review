### **Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"**

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper tackles the problem of estimating the density ratio (or importance function) `q(x)/p(x)`. The authors' central contribution is to reframe this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. They derive two main formulations based on different integral equalities:
1.  **Type I:** `Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)`, where `Kp` is the integral operator with kernel `k` under measure `p`. This setting is suitable when samples from both distributions `p` and `q` are available.
2.  **Type II:** `Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`, where `Kt,p` is an operator with a local "delta-like" kernel (e.g., a Gaussian with small variance `t`). This is applicable when samples from `p` are available, and the function `q` can be evaluated at those sample points (even if `q` is not a density).

To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the authors employ a standard and well-principled technique: Tikhonov regularization in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This approach leads to a family of algorithms, dubbed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which have simple, closed-form solutions derived via the representer theorem. The framework is flexible, allowing for different loss functions (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norm).

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing concentration and error bounds with convergence rates for the FIRE estimators using Gaussian kernels, considering data on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. A significant practical contribution is the proposal of a fully unsupervised method for hyperparameter selection called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to create a validation metric. The authors conduct experiments comparing FIRE to other methods like LSIF and a baseline TIKDE on several datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.

This work connects conceptually to prior art in density ratio estimation, most notably Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF). The authors clearly articulate the relationship, positioning their RKHS-norm-based FIRE variant as a regularized version of KMM and noting that their CD-CV validation metric is inspired by the objective in LSIF, but used for model selection rather than for fitting.

### **Evaluation**

**Quality: (High)**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical foundation is strong. Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is an elegant and principled approach that grounds the problem in a well-understood mathematical framework. The application of Tikhonov regularization is a natural and appropriate method for stabilizing the solution to such an ill-posed problem. The theoretical analysis in Section 3, providing convergence rates on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds, is a substantial contribution that adds significant rigor and credibility to the proposed method. The derivations for the algorithms appear correct, and the resulting closed-form solutions are a major advantage.
*   **Weaknesses:** The assumptions for the theoretical results (e.g., densities bounded, ratio function in a Sobolev space) are stated but not discussed in terms of their practical implications. A brief justification of why these are reasonable assumptions for many real-world problems would be beneficial for the reader.

**Clarity: (High)**
The paper is very well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation and core idea are introduced clearly in the first section. The distinction between Type I and Type II settings is well-explained and motivated by different practical scenarios. The algorithms are presented with explicit formulas (Eq. 8, 10, etc.), which should make implementation straightforward and the results reproducible. The connection to and distinction from related work (KMM, LSIF) is explained exceptionally well, which is very helpful for positioning the contribution.
*   **Suggestions for Improvement:** Section 3 is dense with theoretical results. While the main theorems are clearly stated, a bit more intuition behind the error decomposition (e.g., separating approximation error, regularization bias, and sample variance) could make the results more accessible to a broader audience.

**Originality: (High)**
The paper presents a novel and insightful perspective on a familiar problem.
*   **Strengths:** While the components used (RKHS, Tikhonov regularization, kernel methods) are standard tools in machine learning, their application to solve the density ratio problem via a Fredholm equation framework is, to my knowledge, novel. This new formulation is the paper's key original contribution. It provides a new lens through which to view the problem, connecting it to a rich literature in classical analysis and inverse problems. The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised hyperparameter selection is also a novel and valuable contribution, as this is a notoriously difficult problem in this domain.
*   **Weaknesses:** None to note. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior contributions like KMM and LSIF by providing a more general, regularized framework.

**Significance: (High)**
The results are important and likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Strengths:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with applications in covariate shift, transfer learning, and importance sampling. The FIRE framework is appealing because it is principled, flexible (multiple norms and settings), and leads to simple algorithms. The provision of a practical, unsupervised hyperparameter selection method (CD-CV) is a major step towards making density ratio estimation more accessible and reliable in practice. The strong theoretical guarantees further increase the method's appeal. Researchers are likely to build upon this operator-theoretic view of density ratio estimation.
*   **Weaknesses:** The experimental comparison is solid but could be strengthened. KMM is a major competitor, and its omission from the experimental tables is a notable gap. The authors state this is due to out-of-sample extension issues, but this is a well-known aspect of KMM, and strategies exist to handle it (e.g., fitting a separate model to the learned importance weights). A direct comparison, even with caveats, would have made the empirical evaluation more comprehensive.

---

### **Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is original, insightful, and provides a new theoretical foundation for the problem.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous convergence analysis, which is a significant strength for a NIPS paper.
3.  **Practical and Implementable Algorithms:** The proposed FIRE algorithms are derived with simple, closed-form solutions, making them easy to implement.
4.  **Addresses a Critical Practical Problem:** The CD-CV method for hyperparameter selection is a highly valuable contribution that addresses a key bottleneck in using density ratio estimators.
5.  **Excellent Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly situates its contributions within the existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The lack of a direct empirical comparison against Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), a very closely related and widely cited method, is a weakness.

**Recommendation:**
The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The novel formulation, strong theoretical backing, and practical contributions (especially the CD-CV method) make it a high-quality and significant contribution to the field. I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
