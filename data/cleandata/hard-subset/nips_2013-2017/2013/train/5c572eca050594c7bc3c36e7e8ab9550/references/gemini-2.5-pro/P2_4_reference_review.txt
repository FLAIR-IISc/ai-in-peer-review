### **Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"**

**Summary**

This paper recasts the problem of density ratio estimation, q(x)/p(x), as a classical inverse problem. The core idea is to formulate an identity involving the unknown ratio function as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose two main settings: Type I, where samples from both p and q are available, leading to the equation `K_p(q/p) = K_q(1)`; and Type II, where samples are available only from p but the function q(x) is known, leading to the approximate equation `K_{t,p}(q/p) ≈ q`. To solve these ill-posed inverse problems, they employ Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach, which they name Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), yields simple, closed-form solutions for the density ratio in terms of linear combinations of kernel functions. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving convergence rates for the estimator with Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Furthermore, it introduces a novel and practical unsupervised method for model selection, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), and presents experimental results comparing FIRE to other methods like LSIF and a KDE-based baseline.

**Critique**

This is a high-quality paper that makes several significant contributions. The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is both elegant and powerful, connecting a popular machine learning problem to a rich body of literature in classical analysis and operator theory.

**Quality: (Excellent)**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation of the problem is clear and the derivations of the algorithms in Section 2 are correct. The application of the Representer Theorem is standard but appropriately used. The main theoretical contribution lies in Section 3, where the authors provide concentration and error bounds, along with convergence rates. These results are non-trivial, covering both Euclidean and manifold settings, which adds to their generality. The analysis appears rigorous, and the assumptions are clearly stated.
*   **Completeness:** The work is comprehensive. It introduces a new framework, derives multiple algorithmic variants (for different norms), provides strong theoretical backing, addresses the crucial practical problem of hyperparameter tuning with a novel method (CD-CV), and validates the approach experimentally.
*   **Strengths and Weaknesses Evaluation:** The authors are generally careful. They correctly identify the connection to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), noting that their RKHS-norm formulation can be seen as a regularized version of KMM. They also astutely point out a key weakness of KMM (lack of a natural out-of-sample extension) and a key benefit of their Type II setting (q does not need to be a density and sampling from it is not required). One area for improvement would be a more direct discussion of the "curse of dimensionality" apparent in their theoretical bounds (e.g., the `d` in the exponent of the convergence rate). While this is a common issue, a brief discussion of its implications and a comparison to minimax optimal rates (e.g., from [14]) would strengthen the theoretical section.

**Clarity: (Very Good)**

The paper is well-written and well-organized.

*   The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The core idea is presented intuitively before the formal derivations.
*   The distinction between Type I and Type II settings is well-explained and motivated by different practical scenarios.
*   The algorithms are presented with explicit formulas, which would make them easy to implement.
*   The experimental setup and the proposed CD-CV method are described with sufficient detail for understanding.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to perhaps add a small summary table for the different FIRE algorithm variants (e.g., based on the L2,p norm, combined norm, RKHS norm), as the matrix equations can become dense. This would further enhance readability for practitioners looking to implement the methods.

**Originality: (Excellent)**

The paper is highly original. While density ratio estimation is a well-studied problem, and kernel methods are a standard tool, the central contribution here is the novel perspective.

*   **Novel Formulation:** Framing the problem as the solution to a Fredholm equation is, to my knowledge, a new and insightful approach in this context. This "linearization" of the problem via integral operators is a powerful idea that separates the core estimation problem from the choice of regularization, allowing for principled flexibility.
*   **Relationship to Prior Work:** The authors do an excellent job of relating their work to prior art, especially KMM and LSIF. They show that KMM can be viewed as a specific instance within their broader framework (using an RKHS norm loss and no regularization), thereby providing a more general and arguably more stable foundation. The CD-CV method is also novel and cleverly uses the same underlying integral identity for validation without requiring the test functions to form a good approximation basis, distinguishing it from the objective function of LSIF.
*   **New Contributions:** The combination of the Fredholm framework, the explicit RKHS-regularized solutions, the convergence rate analysis on manifolds, and the unsupervised CD-CV model selection method constitutes a significant and original contribution to the field.

**Significance: (Excellent)**

The results in this paper are important and likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners.

*   **Theoretical Impact:** The connection to classical inverse problems and operator theory opens the door for new theoretical tools to be applied to density ratio estimation and related problems. Researchers can build upon this framework by exploring different regularizers, kernels, and loss functions.
*   **Practical Impact:** The resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, non-parametric, and have closed-form solutions (involving a single matrix inversion), making them computationally appealing compared to iterative or quadratic programming-based methods. The Type II setting is particularly valuable for applications where one distribution is defined analytically but is hard to sample from (e.g., in Bayesian inference).
*   **Advancing the State of the Art:** The most significant practical advance is arguably the CD-CV method. Hyperparameter selection is a major bottleneck in unsupervised learning. CD-CV provides a principled, data-driven way to select both the kernel width and the regularization parameter, which is a major advantage over methods that rely on heuristics or supervised validation. This alone makes the paper's contributions highly valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Framework:** The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a fundamental and insightful contribution.
2.  **Principled and Flexible Algorithms:** The regularization framework leads to a family of simple, closed-form kernel-based algorithms (FIRE).
3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous convergence rates for its estimators in general settings (Euclidean space and manifolds).
4.  **Important Practical Contribution:** The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection addresses a critical and challenging problem in the field.
5.  **Clear Relation to Existing Work:** The paper thoughtfully positions itself relative to key methods like KMM and LSIF, unifying and extending previous ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experiments do not include a comparison to KMM, a very closely related and widely used baseline. While the authors provide a valid reason (lack of out-of-sample extension), a more thorough comparison could have been attempted, for instance, by fitting a kernel regressor to the KMM weights to create an out-of-sample extension.
2.  **Lack of Discussion on Rate Optimality:** The theoretical analysis is strong, but it would be even stronger with a discussion of how the derived convergence rates relate to known minimax lower bounds for this problem.

**Conclusion:**

The arguments for acceptance far outweigh the minor weaknesses. This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, principled, and powerful framework for an important problem. It combines theoretical depth with practical relevance, and its contributions are likely to have a lasting impact. I strongly recommend acceptance.
