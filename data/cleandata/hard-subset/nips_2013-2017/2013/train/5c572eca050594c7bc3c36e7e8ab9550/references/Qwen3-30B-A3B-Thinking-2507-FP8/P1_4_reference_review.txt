

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation (q(x)/p(x)) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors introduce two formulations: Type I (when samples from both p and q are available) and Type II (when q(x) values are known at sample points from p but q may not be a density). They develop the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithms based on these formulations, provide theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds, and propose a novel unsupervised parameter selection method called CD-CV.

The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems, which is a fresh perspective compared to existing approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF. The paper builds on and extends the connection between kernel methods and integral equations that has been explored in previous work (e.g., [3, 20]), but makes a specific contribution by framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm integral equations, which provides a new perspective on this well-studied problem. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-4) is thorough, providing convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold domains with Gaussian kernels. The error bounds are carefully derived and include terms for both approximation and estimation errors.

2. **Algorithmic Simplicity and Practicality**: The FIRE algorithms are remarkably simple to implement, with explicit closed-form solutions derived from the Representer Theorem. The paper clearly explains how the theoretical formulation translates to practical algorithms, making it accessible to practitioners.

3. **Novel Model Selection Technique**: CD-CV is a clever unsupervised approach for parameter selection that leverages the importance sampling property directly, avoiding the need for labeled data. This addresses a significant practical challenge in density ratio estimation.

4. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The experiments cover multiple datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS), multiple performance measures, and comparisons with relevant baselines (LSIF, TIKDE, and KMM). The results demonstrate FIRE's competitive performance with better computational efficiency.

5. **Clear Distinction Between Settings**: The paper effectively differentiates between Type I and Type II settings, highlighting when each is applicable and their theoretical differences.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Algorithm**: While the Fredholm equation framing is novel, the resulting optimization problem (Type I with L2,p norm) is very similar to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], differing mainly in the optimization procedure. The connection to KMM is acknowledged, but the paper doesn't sufficiently emphasize how the FIRE approach offers advantages over KMM beyond just the optimization method. The theoretical analysis of FIRE could have been more directly compared to existing theoretical results for KMM.

2. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The theoretical results require strong assumptions about the smoothness of q/p and q/p² (Sobolev space membership). While these are standard in kernel methods, the paper doesn't discuss how restrictive these assumptions are in practice or provide empirical evidence that the data used in experiments satisfies these conditions.

3. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity**: While the paper mentions computational efficiency, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity between FIRE and existing methods. This is particularly important for the Type I setting where the algorithm involves matrix inversion of size n×n.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address limitations of the approach, particularly for high-dimensional data where the Gaussian kernel may suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The Type II setting has a known error term O(t), but the paper doesn't discuss how this affects practical implementation choices.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a novel connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems. The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis and clear algorithmic development. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, demonstrating competitive performance with existing methods.

The main weakness is that the core algorithmic approach, while framed in a novel way, isn't substantially different from existing methods like KMM in terms of the optimization problem solved. However, the theoretical framework provided (Fredholm equations) is valuable as it allows for a more systematic analysis of the problem.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a new theoretical perspective on density ratio estimation, which may lead to further theoretical developments and algorithmic improvements. The CD-CV model selection technique is particularly valuable as it addresses a practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with practical implications, and the experiments support the claims. While the core algorithmic approach has similarities to existing methods, the novel theoretical framing and the CD-CV technique provide significant value to the field. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and addresses a problem of significant interest in transfer learning, domain adaptation, and covariate shift.

The paper advances the state of the art by providing a unified theoretical framework for density ratio estimation that connects to classical operator theory, and by introducing a principled unsupervised parameter selection method. These contributions are significant for both theoretical and applied machine learning research.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the theoretical section, explicitly compare the error bounds of FIRE with those of KMM and LSIF to better highlight the advantages of the proposed framework.

2. Add a brief discussion of computational complexity for the FIRE algorithms, especially for large-scale datasets.

3. Include a short discussion of the limitations of the Gaussian kernel approach and potential extensions to other kernel functions.

4. Consider adding a brief example showing how the Type II setting is applied in a real-world scenario where sampling from q is difficult but q(x) is known explicitly (e.g., in MCMC applications as mentioned in the paper).

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field that would be of interest to NIPS readers, with strong theoretical foundations and practical relevance to current machine learning challenges.
