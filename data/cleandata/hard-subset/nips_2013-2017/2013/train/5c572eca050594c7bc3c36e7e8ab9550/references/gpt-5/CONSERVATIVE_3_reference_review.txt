Summary
The paper tackles density ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are given: Type I uses the identity K_p r = K_q 1, estimable from samples from p and q; Type II uses a “local” kernel K_t,p r ≈ q, estimable when q(x) is known on a sample from p. Using the representer theorem, the authors derive closed-form kernel solutions (FIRE). They provide concentration/error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on R^d and on compact manifolds, and propose an unsupervised model selection scheme (CD-CV) based on checking the importance-sampling equality with random test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded KDE ratio baseline, and apply the weights to regression/classification under covariate shift.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to KMM (Gretton et al., 2007) and LSIF/KLIEP (Kanamori/Sugiyama et al.). Indeed, the paper notes that KMM corresponds to the same Fredholm identity in H, and one of the FIRE variants (RKHS-loss with K=K_H) becomes a regularized KMM. LSIF minimizes L2,q or L2,p errors over a linear family; FIRE uses the full RKHS with Tikhonov regularization, but the loss choices and cross-validation ideas are similar in spirit. The operator-theoretic view connects to inverse problems and prior RKHS regularization work.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation that unifies several density-ratio estimators and yields straightforward algorithms via the representer theorem.
- Multiple variants (Type I/II, L2,p vs mixed norms vs RKHS norm) presented in a common framework.
- Theoretical analysis provides finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
- The CD-CV criterion is a practical, unsupervised tuning approach leveraging the importance-sampling identity.
- Closed-form linear system solvers make the method easy to implement.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: Type I (RKHS loss) is essentially regularized KMM; Type I (L2,p) resembles least-squares matching of kernel means; Type II amounts to inverting a smoothed operator and inherits Gaussian-kernel bias. The “Fredholm” framing is elegant but largely a rephrasing of known identities used by KMM.
- Theoretical results: rates have unusual exponents (e.g., n^{-1/(3.5+d/2)}), depend intricately on t and λ, and are not contrasted with known minimax rates (e.g., Nguyen et al.). Assumptions (bounded p,q and r∈W^2_2) are relatively strong, and optimality/tightness is not discussed. The condition m > λ^{1/3} n in Cor. 2 is somewhat ad hoc. Proofs are deferred to the supplement.
- Practical constraints: Unlike KMM/LSIF, FIRE does not enforce non-negativity or normalization of the ratio; use of negative weights for importance weighting, regression, and SVM is not addressed and can be problematic.
- Empirical validation is limited and, at times, unfair. In several experiments KMM is excluded due to “no out-of-sample extension,” yet KMM is commonly evaluated for covariate shift with downstream tasks; if an out-of-sample function is required, this should be engineered consistently across methods. Important baselines are missing (uLSIF/KuLSIF, RuLSIF, KLIEP). No synthetic datasets with known ground-truth ratios to directly assess ratio error. No ablation comparing Type I vs Type II vs RKHS-loss variants, or the impact of positivity/normalization constraints.
- Tuning and robustness: hyperparameter ranges (t via 10-NN heuristic; λ∈{1e−5,…,1e−10}) seem arbitrary; no sensitivity analyses. CD-CV may align with the estimator’s own bias (choice of test functions), but its stability and bias are not thoroughly analyzed.
- Scalability: All methods require solving n×n systems; no runtime/memory measurements or large-scale experiments to support claims of “better computational efficiency.”
- Clarity: Notation is heavy with occasional typos (q/p vs “qp”), and several matrix expressions (Eqs. 8–10) are presented without derivation. The role of K vs K_H and when they coincide could be clarified further.

Quality
Technically competent in the RKHS/inverse-problem setup, but the theoretical section leaves key questions (optimality, constants, practical implications) unanswered. Experimental methodology does not convincingly demonstrate superiority over strong, established baselines and omits important sanity checks (positivity, normalization, sensitivity, synthetic ground truth).

Clarity
Generally understandable but dense. Improve notation consistency, fix typos, and provide derivations and algorithmic details (complexity, numerical stability, regularization choices). Clarify when K=K_H, and conditions under which Type II bias is acceptable.

Originality
Moderate. The operator viewpoint is insightful but overlaps substantially with prior work (KMM, LSIF, inverse problems in RKHS). The manifold extension and CD-CV are incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful unification and some theory, but current empirical evidence falls short of establishing clear advantages over strong existing methods. Without stronger validation and constraints, practical impact is uncertain.

Questions for authors
- How do you handle non-negativity and normalization of ratios in downstream tasks? Do negative weights occur and how do they affect SVM/regression?
- Can you compare directly to uLSIF/KuLSIF/KLIEP/RuLSIF on synthetic data with known r(x)?
- Provide ablations across Type I/II and loss choices, and sensitivity to t, λ, and kernel choice.
- Are the rates near-minimax under your smoothness assumptions? How do they compare to [14]?
- Report runtimes and memory usage, and consider low-rank approximations for scalability.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, unified operator-theoretic framing with simple closed-form algorithms.
- New bounds for Gaussian kernels on manifolds.
- Practical unsupervised tuning idea (CD-CV).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to KMM/LSIF; one variant reduces to regularized KMM.
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient and omits key baselines; fairness concerns about KMM exclusion.
- No handling of positivity/normalization constraints; potential practical issues.
- Theory not positioned against known optimal rates; assumptions strong.

Recommendation
Reject (leaning). The paper presents an elegant rephrasing with some theory, but lacks compelling novelty and rigorous empirical validation against established baselines. Strengthening experiments, addressing constraints, and clarifying theory would substantially improve the work.
