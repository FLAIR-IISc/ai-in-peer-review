

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a new framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using Tikhonov regularization in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors introduce two formulations (Type I and Type II) and propose an algorithm called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), with theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds. The paper also introduces a novel unsupervised parameter selection method called CD-CV.

The paper's main contribution is the theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, which provides an interesting perspective on an established problem. The authors correctly identify the relationship between their Type I formulation and Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), noting that their approach uses a different optimization procedure but is related. However, the paper doesn't convincingly establish why this theoretical reformulation leads to practical advantages over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis provides meaningful convergence rates for the specific case of Gaussian kernels, which is valuable.
- The paper demonstrates a clear mathematical connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems.
- The proposed CD-CV method for parameter selection is interesting and potentially useful in unsupervised settings.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of substantial empirical validation:** The paper only compares FIRE with KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE on three small datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS). The experiments lack statistical significance testing, and the authors don't demonstrate consistent superiority across multiple problem types. The paper claims "strong performance" but provides limited evidence to support this assertion. In particular, the comparison with KMM is problematic because the paper states "we do not compare with KMM as out-of-sample extension is necessary for fair comparison," yet KMM is explicitly mentioned as a related method.

2. **Limited theoretical scope:** The theoretical analysis is restricted to Gaussian kernels and specific domains (Euclidean space and manifolds), which is common in kernel methods but doesn't justify a novel approach. The convergence rates provided are comparable to those established for other kernel-based density ratio estimation methods, without demonstrating clear improvements.

3. **Insufficient differentiation from existing work:** The paper acknowledges the relationship to KMM but doesn't clearly articulate the practical advantages of their approach. The Type I formulation with L2,p norm is essentially a variant of KMM with a different optimization procedure, but the paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that this variant leads to better performance in practice. The theoretical analysis doesn't demonstrate that their approach achieves better convergence rates than established methods.

4. **Unsubstantiated claims:** The paper claims "better computational efficiency" compared to alternatives, but provides no evidence of this. The computational complexity analysis is minimal, and the paper doesn't report runtime comparisons.

5. **Theoretical gaps:** The paper mentions "a significant potential benefit" of using γL2,p + (1-γ)L2,q norms, but provides no empirical evidence that this improves performance. The Type II setting has an error term O(t) that depends on the kernel parameter t, but the paper doesn't analyze how this affects the overall error when t is chosen optimally.

**Comparison to existing work:** The paper correctly cites relevant work, including KMM and LSIF, but the comparison is limited. The authors note that KMM uses the RKHS norm but doesn't regularize the weight function, which is a valid point. However, they don't demonstrate that this lack of regularization causes significant practical problems in their experiments.

**Overall assessment:** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical perspective on density ratio estimation, it fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to justify its claims of superior performance. The proposed method doesn't convincingly outperform established baselines across multiple datasets and problem settings. The theoretical results are limited in scope, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate the practical advantages of the proposed framework over existing methods. The field of density ratio estimation is well-established, and this paper doesn't provide substantial new insights or significant improvements that would justify its novelty claim.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical connection but lacks the rigorous empirical validation and clear practical advantages required to justify its novelty. The existing methods (KMM, LSIF) are well-established and have been extensively validated on diverse datasets, while the proposed FIRE method lacks sufficient evidence of superior performance. The authors should either provide more extensive empirical validation across diverse datasets or clearly articulate the theoretical advantages that translate to practical benefits. Without this, the paper doesn't meet the high standards for acceptance at NIPS, particularly given the reviewer's preference for established methods with thorough validation.
