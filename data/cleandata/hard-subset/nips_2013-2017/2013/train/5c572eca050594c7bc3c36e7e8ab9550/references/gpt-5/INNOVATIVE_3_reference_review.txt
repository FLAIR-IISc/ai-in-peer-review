Summary
The paper reframes density-ratio estimation q/p as an inverse problem for a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two complementary formulations are proposed:
- Type I (two-sample): Kp(q/p) = Kq 1, solvable from samples from p and q.
- Type II (known q on p-sample): Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q for a δ-kernel (e.g., Gaussian), enabling cases where q is known pointwise (e.g., unnormalized posteriors in MCMC) but sampling from q is hard.

Both are solved via Tikhonov–Phillips regularization in an RKHS, yielding simple closed-form solutions using the representer theorem. The approach, dubbed FIRE, includes variants with L2,p, γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q, and RKHS losses. The operator viewpoint connects FIRE to KMM and LSIF, clarifying similarities and differences.

Theoretical analysis (Gaussian kernels) provides concentration and excess-risk bounds and convergence rates for Euclidean spaces and manifolds. An unsupervised model-selection procedure, cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), is proposed, leveraging the importance-sampling identity with random test function families. Experiments on USPS, CPUsmall, and Kin8nm compare FIRE to KMM/LSIF/TIKDE and show competitive accuracy and efficiency.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual bridge: casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and unifying. It ties a core ML task to classical operator theory, opening the door to spectral tools and regularization theory.
- Simple, explicit algorithms: closed-form solutions with clear kernel-matrix expressions; natural out-of-sample extension unlike KMM’s in-sample weights.
- Breadth of formulations: Type I and Type II cover both two-sample covariate-shift settings and “known q” scenarios relevant to MCMC and unnormalized models.
- Theory beyond Euclidean: rates for manifold-supported distributions with Gaussian kernels are rare in this literature.
- Unsupervised model selection (CD-CV): a practical lever for hyperparameters in an unsupervised task; the use of random linear and half-space test functions is appealingly simple.
- Relation to prior work is thoughtful, situating FIRE with respect to KMM, LSIF, and kernel mean embeddings.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is underdeveloped. Results are summarized but not deeply analyzed; details on data preprocessing, variance across trials, and computational costs are scarce. Comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF, IWCV-based selection) should be added.
- No positivity/normalization constraints on q/p. Negative weights can harm downstream weighted losses. At least a discussion or a simple monotone post-processing would help.
- The Type II approximation introduces a kernel-bias term O(t); guidance for the t–λ trade-off (beyond asymptotics) is limited. Practical heuristics or sensitivity plots would be useful.
- Complexity/scalability not analyzed. Matrix inversions at O(n^3) limit large-scale applicability; no mention of Nyström/CG/preconditioning or mini-batch approximations.
- Some notation and exposition could be clearer (e.g., when k = kH vs. distinct; precise assumptions for operator invertibility; consistent use of empirical vs. population norms).
- The rates (odd exponents like 3.5+d/2) are non-intuitive; comparison to known minimax rates (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2007) is brief.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The operator derivations and RKHS regularization are standard but well adapted to the ratio setting. The generalization bounds appear reasonable under the stated assumptions (bounded p,q and Sobolev smoothness of q/p). However, important practical issues (positivity, normalization, robustness to kernel choice, and algorithmic complexity) are not addressed experimentally or theoretically. The experimental section supports feasibility but is too light to fully validate the claimed advantages and is missing some strong baselines and ablations (e.g., CD-CV vs. IWCV).

Clarity
Readable and generally well organized. The operator background and representer-theorem solutions are clearly derived. Still, the paper would benefit from:
- A dedicated paragraph comparing Type I/II assumptions and when each should be used in practice.
- Explicit complexity analysis and memory footprint for all variants.
- Clearer exposition on choosing k versus kH, and the implications for stability and bias.
- A brief guide to selecting λ and t (beyond CD-CV), with practical defaults.

Originality
High. The Fredholm-equation framing for density-ratio estimation, together with manifold-aware rates and an unsupervised CD-CV criterion, constitute a fresh, compelling perspective. While related to KMM and LSIF, FIRE’s operator-theoretic lens and the explicit closed-form regularized solutions are a distinct contribution.

Significance
Promising. Many tasks (covariate shift, domain adaptation, off-policy evaluation, MCMC) hinge on robust ratio estimation. Providing a unifying operator framework plus usable algorithms and rates is valuable. The CD-CV procedure could be widely adopted if shown to be robust across problems. With stronger empirical validation and scalability improvements, this line could influence both theory and practice.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you enforce non-negativity and approximate normalization of q/p within the RKHS framework (e.g., via constrained quadratic programs or soft penalties)? Any impact on rates?
- Provide an analysis or experiments on computational scaling, and consider Nyström/Random Features or iterative solvers.
- Compare CD-CV to importance-weighted cross-validation (IWCV) and to LSIF’s model-selection strategies.
- Add experiments on higher-dimensional synthetic benchmarks with known ground-truth ratios, and report NLL/KL divergence to assess calibration of weights.
- Clarify the dependence of rates on d versus intrinsic manifold dimension and kernel bandwidth; include a brief discussion comparing to minimax lower bounds.
- Improve citation formatting and complete missing years.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, original conceptual contribution.
- Clean algorithms with closed forms and out-of-sample extension.
- Theoretical guarantees including manifold settings.
- Practical unsupervised model selection idea.

Cons against acceptance
- Empirical section is comparatively weak (baselines, ablations, scalability).
- Lack of constraints on positivity/normalization.
- Limited guidance on hyperparameter sensitivity and kernel choices.

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept to accept). The novelty and unifying operator perspective outweigh the empirical limitations. With modest revisions focusing on experiments and practicalities, this paper can make a meaningful contribution to the NeurIPS community.
