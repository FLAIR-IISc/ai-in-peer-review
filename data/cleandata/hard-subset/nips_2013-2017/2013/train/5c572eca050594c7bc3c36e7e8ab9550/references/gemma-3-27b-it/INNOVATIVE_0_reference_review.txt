This paper presents a fascinating and novel perspective on density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable through Fredholm equations and regularization techniques. The connection to classical operator theory and the potential for leveraging spectral analysis is a significant strength. The authors successfully tie together concepts from kernel methods, regularization, and functional analysis, opening avenues for theoretical investigation that have been largely unexplored in this area. The introduction of FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) and the discussion of different norms (L2,p, L2,q, and RKHS) provides a flexible framework. The unsupervised model selection technique, CD-CV, is also a welcome addition, addressing a practical challenge in the field.

The paper's greatest strength lies in its originality. While density ratio estimation is a well-studied area, this Fredholm equation approach is a genuinely novel contribution. It’s a bold move to re-frame the problem within the context of inverse problems, and the potential theoretical benefits are substantial. The authors correctly identify the connection to KMM and highlight the differences, particularly regarding stability and out-of-sample extension. 

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is present, the convergence rates (Corollary 2 & 4) appear somewhat pessimistic, scaling with powers of *n* that might limit practical applicability, especially in higher dimensions. While the theory is a strong point, it feels somewhat detached from the empirical validation. The experimental section, while demonstrating competitive performance, doesn't fully capitalize on the theoretical insights.  A deeper investigation into the conditions under which the theoretical guarantees hold in practice would be valuable. The tables presented are a bit difficult to parse, and a clearer comparison focusing on the specific advantages of FIRE (e.g., in situations where q is not a density) would be helpful.  The connection to spectral analysis is mentioned but not elaborated upon – a brief illustration of how spectral properties could be utilized for analysis or algorithm design would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the discussion of the boundary case in Theorem 3 feels a little hand-wavy and warrants further clarification.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The Fredholm equation formulation is a genuinely innovative approach to density ratio estimation.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper provides a solid theoretical framework grounded in operator theory and regularization.
* **Flexibility:** The FIRE algorithm offers a flexible framework with different norms and kernel choices.
* **Potential Impact:**  The work opens up new avenues for theoretical analysis and algorithm development in this field.
* **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The CD-CV method is a practical contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Pessimistic Convergence Rates:** The theoretical convergence rates might be too slow for practical applications.
* **Limited Empirical Exploration:** The experimental section could be more focused on showcasing the advantages of the proposed approach and its theoretical underpinnings.
* **Lack of Elaboration on Spectral Analysis:** The connection to spectral analysis remains largely unexplored.
* **Boundary Case Clarity:** The theoretical discussion of manifolds with boundaries could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper's novelty and boldness outweigh the minor flaws in the experimental validation and theoretical detail. The potential for future research based on this framework is significant. While the convergence rates might be a concern, the theoretical contribution is valuable, and the experimental results are promising. This work represents a significant step forward in understanding density ratio estimation and offers a fresh perspective that is likely to inspire further research. I believe this paper is well-suited for NIPS, prioritizing innovative thinking over absolute methodological perfection.
