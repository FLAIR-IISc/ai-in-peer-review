This paper proposes a novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm equation, and develops algorithms (FIRE) based on regularization in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to solve it. The approach offers a theoretical framework grounded in operator theory and provides explicit algorithms with potential for efficient computation. The authors also introduce a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for parameter selection and demonstrate empirical performance on several datasets.

The core idea of reframing density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm equation is insightful and connects the problem to a well-studied area of mathematics. This allows for leveraging tools from spectral and Fourier analysis for theoretical analysis, which is a significant contribution. The FIRE algorithms derived from this formulation are well-motivated and appear computationally tractable, especially given the representer theorem. The exploration of different norms and loss functions, including connections to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), is commendable. The theoretical analysis, providing concentration and error bounds for the Gaussian kernel, is a strong point, and extending this to other kernels would be valuable. The introduction of the CD-CV method for parameter selection is practical and addresses a crucial aspect of applying these algorithms in real-world scenarios.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the connection to existing work is acknowledged, the novelty beyond the re-formulation as a Fredholm equation isn't always clear. The empirical results, while demonstrating competitive performance, don't convincingly showcase a substantial advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods like KMM and LSIF across *all* tested scenarios. The presentation in Section 2, detailing the algorithms, is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples. The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, is highly technical and may be inaccessible to a broader audience; a more high-level summary of the key insights would be helpful. The discussion of the limitations of the approach, beyond mentioning the need for smoothness conditions, could be more comprehensive.  For instance, the computational cost of inverting the kernel matrices, particularly for large datasets, is not thoroughly addressed. The comparison to TIKDE feels somewhat cursory; a more in-depth analysis of why FIRE outperforms or underperforms TIKDE would be valuable.  The use of different kernel functions (beyond Gaussian) and their impact on performance isn’t explored. Finally, while the paper claims a simplification in the framework, the notation is occasionally cumbersome, hindering readability.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous analysis (Gaussian kernel case).
*   Explicit and computationally tractable algorithms (FIRE).
*   Practical CD-CV method for parameter selection.
*   Connections to existing methods (KMM, LSIF).

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental novelty beyond the re-formulation.
*   Empirical results don’t consistently demonstrate a significant advantage.
*   Dense and occasionally difficult-to-follow presentation.
*   Limited exploration of kernel choices.
*   Computational cost analysis is lacking.
*   Theoretical analysis is highly technical.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical perspective on a well-studied problem.
*   The FIRE algorithms are well-motivated and offer a principled approach to density ratio estimation.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the algorithms.
*   The CD-CV method addresses a practical challenge in applying these algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical results, while promising, don’t definitively demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The paper's presentation is dense and could be improved for clarity.
*   The computational complexity analysis needs more attention.
*   The theoretical analysis is quite specialized and may limit the broader impact of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation and theoretical analysis are commendable. However, the incremental novelty and the lack of a compelling empirical advantage over existing methods raise concerns. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. 

**Suggestions for improvement:**

*   Provide a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including comparisons to a wider range of state-of-the-art methods and a more detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of FIRE in different scenarios.
*   Improve the clarity of the presentation, particularly in Section 2, with more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples.
*   Address the computational complexity concerns and discuss potential strategies for scaling the algorithms to larger datasets.
*   Explore the use of different kernel functions and their impact on performance.
*   Provide a more accessible summary of the key insights from the theoretical analysis.
*   Streamline the notation to improve readability.
*   More thoroughly address the limitations of the approach.
